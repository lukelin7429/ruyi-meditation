Session 5 · When you're provoked

The Simile of the Saw

MN 21 — Kakacūpama Sutta
Before class: Read MN 21. Fair warning — its central image is deliberately extreme, and it may unsettle you. Read it anyway, notice your resistance, and bring that resistance to class. It's part of the lesson.
Part 1

Lecture Notes

Patience you only find out about when tested

The discourse opens with a small, sharp story. There is a housewife named Vedehikā, known all over town as gentle, mild, and kind. Her maid Kāḷī begins to wonder: is her mistress actually good-natured, or has she simply never been provoked? So Kāḷī runs a test — she starts sleeping in, getting lazy. And Vedehikā's famous gentleness evaporates: first a scowl, then harsh words, and at last she strikes the maid on the head. Her reputation for kindness, it turns out, held only as long as no one crossed her.

The Buddha's point is immediate and uncomfortable: a calm you have only in calm conditions is not yet a virtue — it's just circumstances. The real measure of patience isn't how peaceful you are on the cushion or on a good day. It's what happens the moment someone provokes you.

Five ways speech arrives — and one training

He then names the ways others' words can land on us: they come timely or untimely, true or false, gentle or harsh, beneficial or harmful, from a mind of love or of hate. Whichever way they come, he says, train like this:

"Our minds will remain unaffected, and we shall utter no evil words; we shall abide compassionate for their welfare, with a mind of loving-kindness, without inner hate."

Notice the aim carefully. It is not to control what others say or do — that's beyond you. It is to keep your own heart from being conscripted into hatred by their behavior. Their words are their business; the state of your mind is yours.

The image that gives the discourse its name

Then the Buddha sets the bar as high as it can possibly go:

"Even if bandits were to sever you limb from limb with a two-handled saw, whoever gave rise to hate in his heart toward them would not be following my teaching."

It is meant to shock, and it does. But read what it actually says. The Buddha is not talking about your body — he grants the body may be destroyed. He is talking about your mind: that there is no provocation extreme enough to make abandoning loving-kindness the right response, because hatred harms the one who holds it first. The saw is the outer limit precisely so that every smaller provocation — the rude email, the cutting remark, the driver who cut you off — is obviously included underneath it. If not even that justifies hate, then certainly not this.

A caution before we go further

This teaching is about the inner state of hatred, not about tolerating harm. The Buddha is not telling you to stay in an abusive situation, accept mistreatment, or refuse to protect yourself and others. You can act firmly — leave, set a boundary, seek justice — without hatred in the heart. "No inner hate" is about your freedom, not about becoming a doormat. We'll draw this line clearly in discussion.

Three ideas to carry out of today
  • Patience is only real when tested. Calm in easy conditions is circumstance; calm under provocation is practice.
  • You can't govern others' words — only your response. The teaching moves the whole battleground inside, where you actually have power.
  • No provocation justifies abandoning loving-kindness. Not because others deserve it, but because hatred wounds its holder first. This is freedom, not submission.

Part 3

Discussion Questions

  1. Be Vedehikā for a moment. Where in your own life is your "kindness" mostly a product of not-yet-being-provoked? Who or what is your Kāḷī?
  2. The saw image probably provoked you. What exactly did you object to — and does the caution above (hatred vs. tolerating harm) change your objection, or not?
  3. Where's the line between "keeping loving-kindness without inner hate" and "letting people walk over you"? Try to say it in your own words.
  4. Think of a recent, small provocation. What would it have looked like to keep your own heart free in that moment — without pretending it didn't happen?
  5. The Buddha claims hatred harms its holder first. From your experience — is that true? When has your own anger cost you more than its target?
Part 4

AssignmentChoose one · bring it next session

Option A — The provocation log

For the days until we meet, keep a small log: each time you feel provoked — irritated, slighted, angered — jot the trigger, what you did, and (honestly) what it cost you. At week's end, write a few lines on any pattern you see. No need to succeed at patience; just watch yourself accurately.

Option B — Drawing the line

Write half a page on a real situation where someone treated you badly. Describe two versions of a firm response — one carried out with inner hatred, one without — and be specific about how they'd differ in what you say, do, and feel. What does "no inner hate" actually change?

Part 5

Self-Check

Take the short quiz for MN 21 on its reading page. Then notice: does anything in you still resist this teaching after the quiz? Good. Bring the resistance — it's more useful to us than easy agreement.

Looking ahead

Session 6 — Working with a distracted mind

This week asked what to do when others provoke you. Next week turns to a quieter struggle: what to do when your own mind won't settle — the intrusive thought, the loop you can't stop. In MN 20 the Buddha offers five very practical methods, like a toolkit for the restless mind. After the intensity of the saw, it's a welcome return to the workbench.

Session 3 · Training the mind

What Mindfulness Actually Is

MN 10 — Mindfulness Meditation (Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta), excerpt
Before class: Read the opening of MN 10 and the section on mindfulness of the body (breathing, the four postures, and full awareness of everyday actions). The later sections on feelings, mind, and phenomena you can skim — we're starting with the body. Bring your notes.
Part 1

Lecture Notes

A word that's been worn smooth

"Mindfulness" is now on cereal boxes and corporate away-days. Most people take it to mean relax, or clear your mind, or calm down. When we go back to the discourse where the Buddha actually lays it out, we find something more precise — and more interesting. He does not call it a relaxation technique. He calls it something much bigger:

"This is the direct path for the purification of beings, for the surmounting of sorrow and lamentation, for the ending of pain and grief… for the realization of nibbāna — namely, the four establishments of mindfulness."

The four establishments are mindfulness of the body, of feelings, of the mind, and of phenomena. That's the whole map. As beginners we take the first and most concrete: the body — and inside it, the simplest anchor there is, the breath.

What mindfulness is — and isn't

Across the whole discourse a single refrain repeats: one dwells "ardent, fully aware, and mindful, having put aside desire and aversion for the world," watching things arise and watching them pass. Unpack that and you have a working definition. Mindfulness is sustained, clear, non-reactive knowing of what is actually happening right now.

Notice what that rules out. It is not blanking the mind — you're knowing, vividly. It is not forcing calm — calm may come, but the job is to see, not to feel a particular way. And it is not judging — "desire and aversion put aside" means you're neither grabbing the pleasant nor shoving away the unpleasant; you're letting it be seen. Mindfulness is knowing, not numbing. You end up more awake, not zoned out.

The breath as the doorway

Here is the Buddha's actual instruction — strikingly plain:

"Breathing in long, one knows: 'I breathe in long.' Breathing out long, one knows: 'I breathe out long.' Breathing in short, one knows: 'I breathe in short.'…"

That's it. You don't control the breath, count it, or fix it. You know it — long when long, short when short. The breath is the chosen anchor for a simple reason: it is always here and always now. You can't breathe yesterday's breath. So whenever the mind has wandered into past or future — which it will, constantly — the breath is a doorway that only ever opens onto the present. Noticing you've wandered and coming back is the practice. That return, done a thousand times without self-criticism, is what trains the mind.

Off the cushion, too

Crucially, MN 10 does not leave mindfulness on a meditation seat. It extends to the four postures — "when walking, one knows 'I am walking'" — and to ordinary actions: eating, dressing, working, even falling silent. The point is portable: the same clear knowing you practice with the breath can be brought to washing a dish or crossing a room. Meditation is the gym; life is the sport.

Three ideas to carry out of today
  • Mindfulness is knowing, not numbing. The aim is clear awareness of what's here — not a blank, not a mood, not forced calm.
  • The breath is the anchor because it's always now. Every time you notice you've drifted and return, that return is the actual training.
  • "Aware… free of desire and aversion" is the whole flavor. Not grabbing the pleasant, not fighting the unpleasant — an unclutching relationship to experience.

Part 3

Discussion Questions

  1. Before this reading, what did you think "mindfulness" meant? Did MN 10 confirm that, or challenge it?
  2. Try it now: three breaths, just knowing in and out. How quickly did the mind wander — and what did it wander to? (No wrong answer; the wandering is the point of the exercise.)
  3. The text keeps having you watch things "arise and pass away." Why might simply seeing that everything passes begin to loosen suffering?
  4. "Having put aside desire and aversion for the world" — does that sound like cold detachment to you, or like freedom? Trust your first reaction, then question it.
  5. Where in an ordinary day could you practice "when walking, I know I am walking"? Pick one real, specific moment you'll actually use this week.
Part 4

AssignmentChoose one · bring it next session

Option A — A week of the breath

Each day until we next meet, sit for five minutes and simply know the breath in and out. Keep a one-line log per day — describe what actually happened (restless, sleepy, three good breaths then lost), not whether it was "good" or "bad." Bring the log; the honest mess is exactly what we'll talk about.

Option B — One mindful action

Choose a single everyday action — washing dishes, walking to the car, drinking your morning tea — and do it each day with full, clear awareness, as MN 10 describes. Write half a page on what, if anything, changed when you actually paid attention to something you normally do on autopilot.

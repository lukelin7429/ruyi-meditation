Session 4 · Breath and concentration

Mindfulness of Breathing

MN 118 — Ānāpānasati Sutta
Before class: Read MN 118. Don't try to master the sixteen steps — just get the shape of them, and notice how the whole path is built on one ordinary breath. We'll sit together this session, so come somewhere you can close your eyes for ten minutes.
Part 1

Lecture Notes

One breath, the whole path

Last week you met the breath as a doorway to the present. This week the Buddha shows just how far that one doorway leads. MN 118 is his fullest instruction on ānāpānasati — mindfulness of breathing — and its quiet claim is astonishing: a single, utterly ordinary object, the breath you are breathing right now, can be developed all the way from first settling the body to full liberating insight. You don't need sixteen techniques. You need one breath, stayed with.

Sixteen steps, in four groups of four

The Buddha lays the practice out in sixteen steps, and they fall into four natural groups (the tradition calls them tetrads). You don't do them all at once; they describe a deepening you grow into over time:

  1. The body — knowing the breath long and short; sensitive to the whole body of breath; calming the body. (Settling in.)
  2. Feeling — becoming sensitive to joy, then to ease; noticing the felt tone of the mind and calming it. (Working with how it feels.)
  3. The mind — knowing the mind's state; gladdening it; steadying it; freeing it. (Working with the mind itself.)
  4. Insight — contemplating impermanence, fading away, cessation, and letting go. (Seeing clearly, releasing.)

Read the arc from top to bottom: it moves from the body, inward to feeling, inward again to the mind, and finally to insight and release. This is the same map as the four foundations of mindfulness from Session 3 — only here it is threaded entirely onto the breath. And the Buddha adds that developed fully, this one practice fulfills all seven factors of awakening. One thread; the whole cloth.

How it begins — plainly

The opening instruction is nearly identical to what you met last week, and just as unadorned:

"Gone to the forest, to the root of a tree, or to an empty hut, one sits down. Having crossed the legs and set the body erect, one establishes mindfulness. Ever mindful one breathes in; mindful one breathes out."

Notice what beginners most need to hear: the depth is not in doing something special to the breath. It is in staying. The first tetrad — long, short, whole body, calming — is where you'll live for a long time, and that is exactly right. Concentration grows not from switching objects but from returning to one, gently, again and again, until the mind stops thrashing and settles like water left undisturbed.

Three ideas to carry out of today
  • One simple object can carry the entire path. You don't collect techniques; you deepen one.
  • Depth comes from staying, not switching. The practice is the return — to the same breath, without self-criticism.
  • The arc runs body → feeling → mind → insight. First you settle; only then, and naturally, does clear seeing arise. Don't rush to the end of the map.
We'll sit together — the first tetrad, about 10 minutes
  1. Sit upright but not stiff — chair or cushion, both fine. Let the hands rest, the eyes close.
  2. Don't change the breath. Just find it — wherever it's clearest (nostrils, chest, or belly) — and let attention rest there.
  3. Breathing in, know you're breathing in. Breathing out, know you're breathing out. Long when long, short when short.
  4. When you notice the mind has wandered — and it will, many times — that noticing is the practice. Simply come back. No scolding.
  5. Near the end, let the breath soften, and with it let the body settle. Then open the eyes slowly.
Part 2

Primary TextRead & annotate

Read MN 118 — first the setting (the Buddha praising the community on a full-moon night), then the sixteen steps. As you read, mark up your copy:

!surprises you / hits hard
?you don't understand
★want to remember
~want to push back on

Don't get tangled trying to understand all sixteen steps precisely — that's a lifetime's work. Instead, in the margin beside each tetrad write one plain-English phrase for what it's about (e.g. "settling the body," "working with mood"). You're learning the shape, not memorizing the list.

Part 3

Discussion Questions

  1. How did the ten-minute sit actually go? Describe what happened honestly — restlessness, sleepiness, a few clear breaths — rather than whether it was "good."
  2. The Buddha says one ordinary breath, stayed with, can carry the whole path. Does that reassure you, or does part of you distrust something so simple? Why?
  3. The practice deepens body → feeling → mind → insight. In your own life, does calming the body actually change how you feel and think? Have you noticed that link?
  4. "Depth comes from staying, not switching." Where else in your life do you tend to switch — apps, plans, projects — the moment things get dull? What might staying teach?
  5. The final tetrad is about letting go. Why do you think the Buddha places release, not effort or achievement, at the very end of the breath?
Part 4

AssignmentChoose one · bring it next session

Option A — Ten minutes, the first tetrad

Each day until we meet, sit ten minutes with just the first tetrad: long/short breath, the whole body of the breath, letting the body calm. Keep a one-line daily log — and this time, notice specifically whether returning to the breath got any easier over the week, or not. Bring the log.

Option B — Where you refuse to stay

Write half a page on one area of your life where you habitually "switch" instead of staying — and what you suspect you'd find if you stayed with it the way this practice asks you to stay with the breath.

Part 5

Self-Check

Take the short quiz for MN 118 on its reading page. The exact order of the sixteen steps is the kind of detail you'll absorb over years — don't worry if you can't recite it. Note anything that puzzled you and bring it.

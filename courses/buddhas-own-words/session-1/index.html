Session 1 · The problem Buddhism is trying to solve

The Simile of the Arrow

MN 63 — The Shorter Discourse with Māluṅkya
Before class: Read MN 63 in full (about 10 minutes) and bring your notes. Don't worry if parts feel confusing — that's what today is for.
Part 1

Lecture Notes

Setting the scene

Imagine a student who has studied with a great teacher for years, and one day marches up, frustrated, and issues an ultimatum: "Either you answer my biggest questions about the universe — right now — or I quit."

That is exactly how this discourse opens. A monk named Māluṅkyaputta comes to the Buddha with a list of ten enormous questions he feels the Buddha has been dodging:

  • Is the universe eternal, or not?
  • Is it infinite, or finite?
  • Is the soul the same thing as the body, or different?
  • Does an enlightened being continue to exist after death — or not, or both, or neither?

He tells the Buddha: answer these, or I'm leaving. These are real questions — people have argued about them for thousands of years. And here is the surprising thing: the Buddha refuses to answer them. Not because he's hiding something, but because he thinks asking them first is a mistake. To show why, he tells one of the most famous stories in all of Buddhism.

The simile of the arrow

Suppose a man is shot with an arrow thickly smeared with poison. His friends rush to bring a surgeon. But the man says:

"I won't let this arrow be pulled out until I know who shot me — his name, his family, whether he was tall or short; until I know what the bow was made of, the bowstring, the kind of feathers on the shaft…"

What happens to this man? He dies. He dies with all his questions still unanswered — because the questions, however interesting, had nothing to do with the thing that was actually killing him.

The Buddha's point lands like a hammer: you are the man with the arrow. The "arrow" is suffering — the dissatisfaction, anxiety, grief, and restlessness woven into ordinary human life. The speculative questions are real, but they will not pull the arrow out. Life is short; the wound is real; get to work on what actually heals.

What the Buddha did choose to teach

So if he set those ten questions aside, what did he teach instead? He was precise about this. He said he taught four things — and only things like them — because they are useful, they lead to peace, they actually pull the arrow out. These four are the foundation of everything else in the course. They are called the Four Noble Truths:

  1. There is suffering (dukkha) — a pervasive unsatisfactoriness in life. (This is the arrow.)
  2. Suffering has a cause — it arises from craving and clinging. (This is the poison.)
  3. Suffering can end — the cause can be let go. (This is the possibility of healing.)
  4. There is a path that leads to its ending. (This is the treatment.)

Notice the shape of this. It is not a creed. It is a diagnosis and a treatment plan — the structure a doctor uses: here is the illness, here is its cause, here is the good news that it can be cured, here is the medicine. The Buddha, in the early texts, is far more like a physician than a prophet.

Three ideas to carry out of today
  • Buddhism begins with a problem, not a belief. The starting point is honest observation of suffering, not faith in a doctrine.
  • The Buddha valued usefulness over speculation. A question is worth answering if answering it helps free you; otherwise he set it aside — not as forbidden, but as a distraction from the wound.
  • The Four Noble Truths are a template, not a dogma. Diagnosis → cause → cure → treatment. The whole path hangs on this shape.

Part 3

Discussion Questions

We'll talk through these together. There are no wrong answers — bring your honest thinking.

  1. The Buddha refuses to answer Māluṅkyaputta's big questions. When you first read that, did it feel wise, or did it feel like a dodge? Be honest.
  2. Think of a time you were "the man with the arrow" — stuck demanding answers or certainty, while the actual problem in front of you went unaddressed. What was your arrow?
  3. The Buddha frames his teaching like a doctor (diagnosis, cause, cure, treatment) rather than a priest (believe this). Does that framing make Buddhism more approachable for you, or less? Why?
  4. Is it really possible to set aside the "big questions" — about death, the universe, the soul — and still live a full life? Or do we need answers to them?
  5. The first noble truth is "there is suffering." Some hear that and call Buddhism pessimistic. After reading this discourse, do you think that's fair?
Part 4

AssignmentChoose one · half to one page · bring it next session

Option A — Your own arrow

Write about a real situation where, like the wounded man, you got stuck on a question that didn't actually help you — while the thing that needed attention went unattended. What was the "arrow"? What were the "who made the bow?" questions you fixated on instead? Looking back, what would "pulling the arrow out" have meant?

Option B — Rewrite the simile

The arrow was a vivid image for its time. Write a modern version — a short parable set in today's world that makes the Buddha's point (stop demanding irrelevant certainty; treat the real wound) to a friend who has never heard of Buddhism. Keep it under one page.

## Restoring the Lost Art

The author describes frustration with skepticism toward breath meditation without scientific validation. They discovered James Nestor's book *Breath: The New Science of a Lost Art* as a bridge between ancient practice and modern science, hoping it would encourage more people to explore meditation.

## The Science of Breathing: Key Takeaways

James Nestor's research presents breathing as a controllable biological function rather than merely automatic. The key principles include:

**Nose Breathing vs. Mouth Breathing:** The nose filters and conditions air while producing nitric oxide, enhancing oxygen absorption. "Mouth breathing, conversely, keeps the body in a state of low-level stress."

**The Power of Slow Breathing:** The optimal rhythm involves inhaling and exhaling for 5.5 seconds each, creating systemic coherence at approximately 5.5 breaths per minute.

**The CO2 Paradox (The Bohr Effect):** Carbon dioxide functions beyond waste removal—it enables oxygen release from hemoglobin. Slower breathing builds healthy CO2 tolerance.

**The Vagus Nerve Connection:** Deliberate slow breathing activates the vagus nerve, engaging the parasympathetic nervous system's "rest and digest" response while reducing stress.

## Where Practice Meets Science

The author observes that Buddhist Anapanasati teaches non-forced focus on breath. When attention settles, breathing naturally becomes "fine" and "long," creating tranquility. Nestor's findings validate this experience scientifically.

## Opening the Nectar Gates

Ancient tradition describes breath meditation as one of the "Two Nectar Gates"—entry points to Nirvana. The Abhidharma texts establish mindfulness of breathing as essential for transcending physical existence toward wisdom.

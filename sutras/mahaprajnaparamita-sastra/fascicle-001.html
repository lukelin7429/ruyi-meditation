Fascicle 1 · 大智度論卷第一

Why the Buddha Teaches Great Wisdom

This page begins Ru-Yi's own English translation of the Mahāprajñāpāramitā Śāstra. We translate slowly, section by section, with the Chinese source beside the English so readers can study the meaning, not merely read around it.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source base: Taishō T25, no. 1509 Begins with the treatise proper

Translation Method

Our aim is not to produce a decorative English paraphrase. We will keep the structure of Kumārajīva's Chinese, preserve important Buddhist terms, and then add plain-English explanations where a modern reader needs a handrail.

This English version begins with the treatise proper. We do not translate Sengrui's Chang'an preface here, since that preface belongs more to Chinese Buddhist literary and translation history than to a first reading of the treatise for modern English readers.

  • Key terms are translated consistently, with the Chinese term visible when useful.
  • Long arguments are divided into readable paragraphs without changing the reasoning.
  • Every completed section can be revised after review by Dharma teachers and readers familiar with the Chinese text.

Opening Homage

The treatise proper begins with homage to the Three Jewels: the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha. It then honors Maitreya, Śāriputra, and Subhūti before announcing the purpose of the work.

Chinese Source

智度大道佛從來，智度大海佛窮盡，智度相義佛無礙，稽首智度無等佛。

有無二見滅無餘，諸法實相佛所說，常住不壞淨煩惱，稽首佛所尊重法。

聖眾大海行福田，學無學人以莊嚴，後有愛種永已盡，我所既滅根亦除；已捨世間諸事業，種種功德所住處，一切眾中最為上，稽首真淨大德僧。

一心恭敬三寶已，及諸救世彌勒等；智慧第一舍利弗，無諍空行須菩提。我今如力欲演說，大智彼岸實相義，願諸大德聖智人，一心善順聽我說！

Ru-Yi Translation

By the great path of wisdom that carries beings across, the Buddha has come. The Buddha has fully sounded the great ocean of this wisdom. In the characteristics and meaning of this wisdom, the Buddha is unobstructed. I bow to the unequaled Buddha of wisdom that carries beings across.

The two views of existence and nonexistence are completely ended. The Buddha teaches the true character of all dharmas: abiding, unbroken, and able to purify afflictions. I bow to the Dharma honored by the Buddha.

The noble Sangha is like a great ocean, practicing as a field of merit. It is adorned by those still in training and those beyond training. The seed of craving for future existence has been forever exhausted; the sense of “mine” has ceased, and its root has been removed.

They have left behind the affairs of the world and become a dwelling place for many virtues. Foremost among all assemblies, this Sangha is true, pure, and greatly virtuous. I bow to it.

Having single-mindedly honored the Three Jewels, and also Maitreya and the other saviors of the world, Śāriputra, foremost in wisdom, and Subhūti, who practices emptiness without conflict, I now wish, according to my ability, to explain the true meaning of the far shore of Great Wisdom. May all noble and wise ones listen with one mind, receptive and well-disposed.

Reading note: The opening presents wisdom as a crossing-over, not as cleverness. Prajñā cuts through fixed views, especially the tendency to grasp either “it truly exists” or “it does not exist at all.”

The First Question

The first question asks why the Buddha taught the Great Perfection of Wisdom Sutra. The treatise answers that Buddhas do not speak casually: a teaching of this scale must have a great cause.

Chinese Source

問曰：佛以何因緣故說《摩訶般若波羅蜜經》？諸佛法不以無事及小因緣而自發言，譬如須彌山王不以無事及小因緣而動。今有何等大因緣故，佛說《摩訶般若波羅蜜經》？

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: For what causes and conditions did the Buddha teach the Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra?

The Dharma of all Buddhas is not spoken without purpose, nor is it spoken for some small cause or condition. It is like Mount Sumeru, king of mountains: it does not move without purpose or for some small cause. What great causes and conditions, then, led the Buddha to teach the Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra?

First Answer: To Reveal the Bodhisattva Path

Chinese Source

答曰：佛於三藏中，廣引種種諸喻，為聲聞說法，不說菩薩道。唯《中阿含．本末經》中，佛記彌勒菩薩「汝當來世，當得作佛，號字彌勒」，亦不說種種菩薩行。佛今欲為彌勒等廣說諸菩薩行，是故說《摩訶般若波羅蜜經》。

Ru-Yi Translation

Answer: In the Three Baskets, the Buddha used many kinds of examples and taught the Dharma for the śrāvakas, but he did not teach the bodhisattva path.

Only in the Beginning and End Sutra of the Middle Āgama did the Buddha give a prediction to the bodhisattva Maitreya, saying, “In a future age, you will become a Buddha, and your name will be Maitreya.” Even there, however, he did not explain the many practices of the bodhisattvas.

Now the Buddha wishes to explain the bodhisattva practices broadly for Maitreya and the others. For this reason, he teaches the Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra.

Reading note: The first reason is simple and important: the Perfection of Wisdom is taught because the bodhisattva path needs a fuller explanation. This sets the tone for the whole treatise. It is not only philosophy; it is a map for people who vow to awaken for the sake of all beings.

Second Answer: To Deepen Recollection of the Buddha

Chinese Source

復次，有菩薩修念佛三昧，佛為彼等欲令於此三昧得增益故說《般若波羅蜜經》。

諸菩薩見佛神變，於念佛三昧倍復增益，以是事故說《摩訶般若波羅蜜經》。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, there are bodhisattvas who cultivate the samādhi of recollecting the Buddha. In order to help them increase and deepen this samādhi, the Buddha teaches the Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra.

As the first chapter of the Prajñāpāramitā says, the Buddha displays spiritual power, releases golden light, and illuminates worlds in the ten directions as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. He manifests a great body of pure radiance, filled with many wondrous colors, filling empty space. In the midst of the assembly, the Buddha is upright, beautiful, and beyond compare, like Mount Sumeru, king of mountains, standing in the great ocean.

When the bodhisattvas see this transformation of the Buddha, their samādhi of recollecting the Buddha increases even more. For this reason, the Buddha teaches the Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra.

Third Answer: Because Profound Dharma Was Part of the Original Turning

Chinese Source

菩薩初生時，放大光明，普遍十方，行至七步，四顧觀察，作師子吼而說偈言：「我生胎分盡，是最末後身；我已得解脫，當復度眾生。」

諸法甚深者，般若波羅蜜是。以是故佛說《摩訶般若波羅蜜經》。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, when the Bodhisattva was first born, he emitted great light that filled the ten directions. He walked seven steps, looked around in the four directions, and uttered a lion's roar in verse:

“My share in the womb is finished. This is my final body. I have already attained liberation, and I will also liberate beings.”

After making this vow, he gradually grew up. Wanting to leave his relatives and go forth to cultivate the unsurpassed path, he rose in the middle of the night and saw the entertainers, queens, and palace women as if they were foul corpses. He ordered Chandaka to saddle his white horse. At midnight he crossed the city wall, traveled twelve yojanas, and reached the forest where the sage Bhārgava lived. There he cut off his hair with a knife and exchanged his fine jeweled garments for a coarse saṅghāṭī robe.

Beside the Nairañjanā River, he practiced austerities for six years, eating only one sesame seed or one grain of rice each day. Then he reflected, “This is not the path.” Giving up the place of austerity, the Bodhisattva went to the bodhi tree and sat on the vajra seat. Māra, king of demons, brought eighteen koṭis of followers to disturb him. By the power of wisdom and merit, the Bodhisattva subdued Māra's army and attained unsurpassed, complete, perfect awakening.

At that time Brahmā, lord of the three-thousand-great-thousand world-system, named Śikhin, together with the gods of the form realm, Śakra Devānām Indra, the gods of the desire realm, and the Four Heavenly Kings, all came to the Buddha and requested the Blessed One to first turn the wheel of Dharma. Remembering his original vow, and moved by great loving-kindness and great compassion, the Bodhisattva accepted their request and taught the Dharma.

Among the dharmas that are extremely profound, the perfection of wisdom is foremost. For this reason, the Buddha teaches the Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra.

Fourth Answer: To Remove Doubt and Guide Those Who Can Be Liberated

Chinese Source

復次，有人疑佛不得一切智。所以者何？諸法無量無數，云何一人能知一切法？

佛從三昧起，欲宣示一切諸法實相，斷一切眾生疑結故，說《般若波羅蜜經》。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, some people doubt that the Buddha has attained all-knowledge. Why? They think, “Dharmas are immeasurable and countless. How could one person know all dharmas?”

The Buddha abides in the true character of the perfection of wisdom, pure like empty space. Among immeasurable and countless dharmas, he sincerely declares, “I am a person of all-knowledge.” Wishing to cut off the doubts of all beings, he teaches the Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra.

Furthermore, there are beings who are ready to be liberated, but because the Buddha's great merit and wisdom are immeasurable, hard to know, and hard to understand, they are misled by bad teachers. Their minds sink into wrong teachings and do not enter the right path.

For such people, the Buddha gives rise to great loving-kindness. With the hand of great compassion, he takes hold of them and leads them into the Buddha path. Therefore he manifests the most wondrous virtues and displays great spiritual power.

As the first chapter of the Prajñāpāramitā says, the Buddha enters the samādhi called King of Samādhis. Emerging from that samādhi, he observes the worlds of the ten directions with his divine eye. From all the pores of his body he smiles. From the thousand-spoked wheel marks on the soles of his feet, he releases six hundred thousand ten-thousands of koṭis of rays of many colors. From his toes up to the crown of his head, every place releases rays of many colors, illuminating immeasurable and countless buddha-worlds in the ten directions, as numerous as the sands of the Ganges.

Emerging from samādhi, the Buddha wishes to reveal the true character of all dharmas and cut through the knots of doubt in all beings. For this reason, he teaches the Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra.

Fifth Answer: To Inspire Faith, Joy, and Healing

Chinese Source

復次，有惡邪人，懷嫉妬意，誹謗言：「佛智慧不出於人，但以幻術惑世。」

我今出世為大醫王，集諸法藥，汝等當服。是故佛說《摩訶般若波羅蜜經》。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, there are crooked and harmful people who, out of jealousy, slander the Buddha, saying, “The Buddha's wisdom does not surpass ordinary human wisdom. He merely uses magical arts to deceive the world.”

To cut off their arrogance and wrong pride, the Buddha displays immeasurable spiritual power and immeasurable wisdom. In the Prajñāpāramitā he says, “My spiritual virtue is immeasurable. I am specially honored in the three realms and protect all beings. If someone gives rise to even one evil thought toward this, that person gains immeasurable fault. If someone gives rise to even one pure faith, that person receives the happiness of humans and devas and will certainly attain the fruit of nirvāṇa.”

Furthermore, in order to lead people to trust and accept the Dharma, the Buddha says, “I am the Great Teacher. I possess the ten powers and the four fearlessnesses. I abide in the dwelling place of the noble ones. My mind is free and sovereign. I utter the lion's roar and turn the wondrous wheel of Dharma. In all worlds, I am most honored and supreme.”

Furthermore, the Blessed Buddha teaches the Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra in order to bring beings joy. He says, “You should give rise to great joy. Why? All beings have entered the net of wrong views and are confused by bad teachers of other paths. I have escaped from all the wrong nets of bad teachers. The Great Teacher who possesses the ten powers is hard to encounter. Now you have met him. At the proper time, I open up the treasury of profound dharmas, such as the thirty-seven factors of awakening, so that you may gather from it as you wish.”

Furthermore, all beings are troubled by the sickness of the fetters. Since beginningless birth and death, no one has been able to cure this sickness, and they have constantly been misled by bad teachers of other paths. “Now I appear in the world as the great king of physicians. I have gathered the medicines of the Dharma. You should take them.” For this reason, the Buddha teaches the Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra.

Sixth Answer: To Show the Buddha's Inconceivable Body and Skillful Means

Chinese Source

復次，有人念言：「佛與人同，亦有生死，實受飢渴、寒熱、老病苦。」

但為此故，以方便力，現行人法，如人威儀，令諸眾生信於深法。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, some people think, “The Buddha is the same as ordinary people. He too has birth and death and truly experiences the sufferings of hunger, thirst, cold, heat, aging, and sickness.” The Buddha wishes to cut off this thought, and so he teaches the Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra, showing them: “My body is inconceivable.”

Even if Brahmā kings and the ancestor-gods of the devas tried, for kalpas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, to measure my body and investigate my voice, they could not fathom them. How much less could they fathom my wisdom and samādhi?

As the verse says: “In the true character of all dharmas, the Brahmā kings and all rulers of heaven and earth are confused and cannot understand. This Dharma is extremely deep and wondrous. No one can measure it. When the Buddha appears, he opens and explains it all, and its brightness shines like the sun.”

When the Buddha first turned the wheel of Dharma, a bodhisattva named Ying-shi came from another direction to measure the Buddha's body. He went upward through empty space, passing immeasurable buddha-lands, until he reached the world of Flower-Superior Buddha. There he saw that the Buddha's body was still the same. The bodhisattva said in verse, “Empty space has no boundary, and the Buddha's virtues are the same. If one tries to measure his body, one labors in vain and can never reach the end.”

Just as the Buddha's body is immeasurable, his light and voice are also immeasurable. The virtues of the Buddhas, such as discipline, samādhi, and wisdom, are all immeasurable.

Furthermore, when the Buddha was first born, he fell to the ground, walked seven steps, and spoke by himself. After speaking, he became silent. Like other infants, he did not walk or speak. He was nursed and fed for three years by his mothers and was gradually raised. Yet the Buddha's body is countless and surpasses all worlds. For the sake of beings, he manifests as an ordinary human.

Ordinary people, when born, have not yet developed the body, the faculties, or consciousness. They do not yet understand the four postures of sitting, lying down, walking, and standing, nor speech, silence, and the many human ways. As days, months, and years pass, they gradually learn and become complete in human conduct. How then could the Buddha speak and walk at birth, but afterward no longer be able to do so? People might find this strange.

For this reason alone, by the power of skillful means, the Buddha manifests human conduct and ordinary human dignity, so that beings may have faith in the profound Dharma. If the Bodhisattva, at birth, could immediately walk and speak, people in the world would think, “This person is unlike anyone ever seen in the world. He must be a deva, nāga, spirit, or god. The Dharma he studies must be beyond our reach. We are fleshly bodies in birth and death, pulled along by fetters and karma, without freedom. Who could reach such profound Dharma?” In this way they would cut themselves off and would not become vessels for the Dharma of sages.

For the sake of such people, he was born in Lumbinī Garden. Although he could have gone directly to the bodhi tree and become a Buddha, by the power of skillful means he manifested as a child, a youth, and an adult. In each stage he experienced play, arts, skills, clothing, conveyances, the five desires, and the full range of human life. Later, gradually seeing the sufferings of aging, sickness, and death, he gave rise to disenchantment. At midnight he crossed the city wall and went forth. He went to the sage Udraka Rāmaputra and appeared as a disciple, though he did not practice that teacher's Dharma.

Although he constantly used spiritual powers and remembered past lives, including how he upheld discipline and practiced the path in the time of Kāśyapa Buddha, he now manifested six years of austerity in search of the path. Although the Bodhisattva is lord of the three-thousand-great-thousand world-system, he manifested the defeat of Māra's army and the attainment of the unsurpassed path. In accord with worldly Dharma, he displayed all these transformations.

Now, in the Prajñāpāramitā, he manifests great spiritual power and the power of wisdom so that people will know: the Buddha's body is countless and surpasses all worlds.

Seventh Answer: To Free Beings from Extremes and Plant Future Causes

Chinese Source

復次，有人應可度者，或墮二邊；或以無智故，但求身樂；或有為道故，修著苦行。

如是等觀諸品中因緣事故，說《般若波羅蜜經》。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, among those who can be liberated, some fall into two extremes. Some, because of ignorance, seek only bodily pleasure. Others, taking that as the path, cling to the practice of painful austerities. Such people lose the right path of nirvāṇa within ultimate meaning. The Buddha wishes to pull them out of these two extremes and lead them into the middle way. Therefore he teaches the Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra.

Furthermore, he teaches the Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra in order to explain the karmic results of making offerings to the birth-body and the Dharma-body, as described in the chapter on the reliquary stūpa. He also teaches it to explain non-retrogression and the marks of non-retrogression, and to address the illusions and activities of Māra.

Furthermore, he teaches the Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra so that people in future generations may create causes and conditions by honoring the perfection of wisdom, and so that he may bestow predictions upon the three vehicles.

As the Buddha told Ānanda: “After my nirvāṇa, this perfection of wisdom will go to the south. From the south it will go to the west. In the latter five-hundred-year period, it will go to the north. There, many faithful good men and good women will honor it with many kinds of flowers, incense, necklaces, banners, music, lamps, jewels, and material gifts. Some will copy it themselves, teach others to copy it, read and recite it, listen to it being explained, remember it correctly, and practice it. In this way they make offerings through the Dharma. Because of these causes and conditions, they will receive many kinds of worldly happiness, and in the end they will attain one of the three vehicles and enter nirvāṇa without remainder.”

For such causes and conditions, as seen throughout the chapters, the Buddha teaches the Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra.

Eighth Answer: To Teach the Four Siddhāntas

Chinese Source

復次，佛欲說第一義悉檀相故，說是《般若波羅蜜經》。有四種悉檀：一者、世界悉檀，二者、各各為人悉檀，三者、對治悉檀，四者、第一義悉檀。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, the Buddha teaches the Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra because he wishes to explain the mark of the siddhānta of ultimate meaning. There are four kinds of siddhānta: first, the worldly siddhānta; second, the siddhānta taught for each individual person; third, the siddhānta of counteractive treatment; and fourth, the siddhānta of ultimate meaning.

Within these four siddhāntas, all the twelve divisions of scripture and the eighty-four thousand Dharma treasuries are true and do not contradict one another. In the Buddha Dharma, some things are true by worldly siddhānta; some are true by the siddhānta taught for individual persons; some are true by the siddhānta of counteractive treatment; and some are true by the siddhānta of ultimate meaning.

Worldly Siddhānta

What is the worldly siddhānta? A thing exists because causes and conditions come together. It has no separate nature of its own. For example, a cart exists because the pole, axle, spokes, rim, and other parts come together. Apart from these, there is no separate cart. A person is the same: because the five aggregates come together, there is a person. Apart from the five aggregates, there is no separate person.

If there were no worldly siddhānta, then since the Buddha is a speaker of truth, how could he say, “With my pure divine eye, I see beings die here and be born there according to their good and bad karma, receiving their results. Those with good karma are born among devas and humans; those with bad karma fall into the three bad destinations”?

The sutras also say, “When one person appears in the world, many people receive celebration, blessing, happiness, and benefit. This is the Blessed Buddha.” The Dhammapada says, “One's own self can save oneself. How can another save one? When one practices good with wise understanding, this is the best way to save oneself.” Other sutras say, “Ordinary people who do not hear the Dharma cling to self,” and “From the night the Buddha first attained the path until the night of his parinirvāṇa, all the scriptures he taught between those two nights are true and not inverted.”

If there were truly no person at all, how could the Buddha say, “With my divine eye I see beings”? Therefore, when we say there is a person, this is by worldly siddhānta. It is not by the siddhānta of ultimate meaning.

Someone asks: “The first siddhānta, ultimate meaning, is what is truly real. Because it is real, it is called first. The others should not be called real.” The answer is: Not so. Each of the four siddhāntas has its own reality. Suchness, Dharma-nature, and the real limit are nonexistent by worldly siddhānta, but existent by ultimate siddhānta. Persons and other such things are the same: they exist by worldly siddhānta, but do not exist by ultimate siddhānta.

Why? Because a person exists through the causes and conditions of the five aggregates. It is like milk: because the causes and conditions of color, smell, taste, and touch exist, there is milk. If milk truly did not exist, the causes and conditions of milk should also not exist. But since the causes and conditions of milk truly exist, milk too should exist. It is not like a second head or third hand on one person, which is only a false name with no causes and conditions. Such marks are called worldly siddhānta.

Siddhānta Taught for Each Person

What is the siddhānta taught for each individual person? The Buddha observes a person's mental tendencies and then teaches the Dharma accordingly. In regard to one matter, he may permit it for one person and not permit it for another.

For example, one sutra says, “Because of mixed-result karma, beings are born into the mixed world and receive mixed contacts and mixed feelings.” Yet the Phagguna Sutra says, “There is no person who obtains contact, and no person who obtains feeling.”

Question: How can these two sutras be reconciled? Answer: Some people doubt future lives, do not believe in fault and merit, commit unwholesome actions, and fall into the view of annihilation. To cut off their doubt, make them abandon bad conduct, and pull out their annihilationist view, the Buddha says that beings are born into the mixed world and receive mixed contacts and mixed feelings.

Phagguna, however, held the view that there is a self, a spirit, and he fell into eternalism. Phagguna asked the Buddha, “Great Virtuous One, who receives feeling?” If the Buddha had said, “So-and-so receives it,” Phagguna would have fallen further into eternalism, and his view of self and person would have become even more solid and immovable. Therefore the Buddha did not say that there is a receiver of feeling or a receiver of contact. Such marks are called the siddhānta taught for each individual person.

Counteractive Siddhānta

What is counteractive siddhānta? A Dharma may exist as a remedy, while by real nature it does not exist. For example, heavy, hot, oily, sour, and salty medicines, herbs, foods, and drinks are called medicine for wind illness, but not for other illnesses. Light, cool, sweet, bitter, and astringent medicines, herbs, foods, and drinks are called medicine for heat illness, but not for other illnesses. Light, pungent, bitter, astringent, and hot medicines, herbs, foods, and drinks are called medicine for cold illness, but not for other illnesses.

The treatment of mental illness in the Buddha Dharma is the same. Reflection on impurity is a good counteractive Dharma for the illness of lust, but it is not good for the illness of anger. Why? Reflection on impurity observes the faults of the body. If an angry person observes faults, this increases the fire of anger.

Reflection on loving-kindness is a good counteractive Dharma for the illness of anger, but it is not good for the illness of lust. Why? Loving-kindness seeks what is good in beings and observes their virtues. If a lustful person seeks what is good and observes virtues, this increases lust.

Contemplation of causes and conditions is a good counteractive Dharma for the illness of delusion, but it is not good for the illnesses of lust and anger. Why? Because wrong views first arise from wrong contemplation, and wrong view is delusion.

Someone asks: “The Buddha Dharma says the twelve links of dependent arising are extremely profound. The Buddha told Ānanda, ‘This Dharma of causes and conditions is extremely profound, hard to see, hard to understand, hard to awaken to, and hard to contemplate. Only people with subtle minds and skillful wisdom can understand it.’ Deluded people have difficulty understanding even shallow and nearby dharmas. How much more difficult is profound dependent arising? How can you now say that deluded people should contemplate causes and conditions?”

The answer is: Deluded people here does not mean deluded like cattle or sheep. It refers to people who seek the true path but, because they contemplate with a wrong mind, give rise to many wrong views. For such deluded people, contemplation of causes and conditions is a good counteractive Dharma.

For people ruled by anger or lust, who seek pleasure or wish to trouble others, this is not a good counteractive Dharma. For those two kinds of people, reflection on impurity and loving-kindness are good counteractive Dharmas, because these two contemplations pull out the poisonous thorns of anger and lust.

Furthermore, beings who are attached to the inverted view of permanence do not understand that dharmas exist in a similar and continuous succession. For such people, contemplation of impermanence is counteractive siddhānta, but not ultimate meaning. Why? Because the nature of all dharmas is empty.

As the verse says: “Seeing permanence in what is impermanent is called inversion. In emptiness, there is no impermanence. Where then could permanence be seen?”

Someone asks: “All conditioned dharmas are connected with the mark of impermanence. This is ultimate meaning. Why do you say impermanence is not real? All conditioned dharmas have the marks of arising, abiding, and ceasing: first arising, next abiding, and later ceasing. How can you say impermanence is not real?”

The answer is: Conditioned dharmas should not have three marks. Why? Because the three marks are not real. If arising, abiding, and ceasing are the marks of conditioned dharmas, then arising itself should also have these three marks, since arising is a conditioned mark. Each and every place would likewise have three marks, and this would lead to infinite regress. The same is true of abiding and ceasing. If each arising, abiding, and ceasing does not itself have arising, abiding, and ceasing, then they should not be called conditioned dharmas. Why? Because the marks of conditioned dharmas would be absent.

Therefore, the impermanence of dharmas is not ultimate meaning. Furthermore, if all real natures were impermanent, then there would be no karmic action and result. Why? Impermanence means loss through arising and ceasing. It is like a rotten seed that cannot produce fruit. If this were so, there would be no action. Without action, how could there be result? Yet all dharmas of sages have results, and wise people can trust and accept this. It should not be denied.

Therefore, dharmas do not have impermanence as their real nature. For countless reasons such as these, one cannot say that dharmas have impermanence as their nature. What is said about all conditioned dharmas being impermanent is also true of suffering, no-self, and so on. Such marks are called counteractive siddhānta.

Ultimate Siddhānta

What is the siddhānta of ultimate meaning? The nature of all dharmas, all debates and language, all claims that something is Dharma or not Dharma, can each be analyzed, broken apart, and dispersed. But the true Dharma practiced by the Buddhas, pratyekabuddhas, and arhats cannot be broken apart and cannot be dispersed. Whatever could not be reconciled in the previous three siddhāntas is reconciled here.

Question: What does “reconciled” mean? Answer: It means being free from every fault, unable to be changed, and unable to be overcome. Why? Apart from the siddhānta of ultimate meaning, all other debates and all other siddhāntas can be broken apart.

As the Sutra of Many Meanings says in verse: “Each relying on one's own view, conceptual elaboration gives rise to dispute. If one can know that this is not so, this is to know right view. Refusing to accept another's Dharma is called being deluded. One who argues in this way is truly a deluded person. If one relies on one's own view as right and gives rise to conceptual elaborations, if this were pure wisdom, then there would be nothing that is not pure wisdom.”

In these three verses, the Buddha teaches the mark of the siddhānta of ultimate meaning. Beings in the world rely on their own views, their own dharmas, and their own arguments, and from these arise disputes. Conceptual elaboration is the root of dispute, and conceptual elaboration arises depending on views.

As another verse says: “Because there is grasping of dharmas, there are arguments. If there were no grasping, what would there be to argue about? The views of grasping and non-grasping, and all such views, have been removed in this person.”

If practitioners can know this as it really is, then with regard to all dharmas and all conceptual elaborations, they do not receive them, do not cling to them, do not see them as real, and do not enter into dispute. They are able to know the sweet nectar taste of the Buddha Dharma. If they do not know this, they slander the Dharma.

If someone refuses another's Dharma, does not know it, and does not accept it, that person lacks wisdom. If so, then all people engaged in debate would lack wisdom. Why? Because each refuses to accept the Dharma of the others. Some people claim that their own Dharma is the pure ultimate meaning, while others speak falsely and impurely. But in worldly laws, those who administer punishment, execution, and many impure acts are trusted and followed by people of the world as genuinely pure. Among renunciants and good noble people, however, such things are most impure.

Among non-Buddhist renunciants, some stand on one foot amid five fires, pull out their hair, and practice other austerities. The Nirgrantha followers consider these to be wondrous wisdom, while others call them deluded practices. In this way, many kinds of non-Buddhist renunciants, householders, and brahmins each consider their own practices good and all others false.

Even within the Buddha Dharma there are Vātsīputrīya monks who say, “Just as the eye-dharma exists from the combination of the four great elements, so too the person-dharma exists from the combination of the five aggregates.” In the Vātsīputrīya Abhidharma it is said, “The five aggregates are not separate from the person, and the person is not separate from the five aggregates. It cannot be said that the five aggregates are the person, nor that the person is apart from the five aggregates. The person is included in the fifth, inexpressible Dharma treasury.”

Those who follow the path that says all exists say, “A spirit-person cannot be found in any mode, at any time, or in any Dharma-door. It is like rabbit horns or turtle hair, always nonexistent. Moreover, the eighteen elements, twelve sense bases, and five aggregates truly exist, but there is no person within them.” Others within the Buddha Dharma, followers of the Vaipulya path, say, “All dharmas are unborn and unceasing, empty, and without anything at all, like rabbit horns or turtle hair, always nonexistent.”

All such debaters guard their own Dharma and do not accept other Dharmas. They say, “This is real; the rest is false.” If they accept only their own Dharma, honor only their own Dharma, practice only their own Dharma, and neither accept nor honor others, this becomes a fault. If this were purity and the benefit of ultimate meaning, then nothing would fail to be pure. Why? Because all of them love their own Dharma.

Question: If all views have faults, which one is the siddhānta of ultimate meaning? Answer: It is beyond every path of language. The range of mental activity has ceased. It depends on nothing anywhere and does not display any dharma. The true character of all dharmas has no beginning, no middle, and no end. It is not exhausted and not destroyed. This is called the siddhānta of ultimate meaning.

As the verses on the meaning of the Mahāyāna say: “Language has come to an end; mental activity also has ceased. Unborn and unceasing, dharmas are like nirvāṇa. To speak of places where action moves is called worldly Dharma; to speak of the place where action does not move is called ultimate meaning. All is real, all is not real; all is both real and not real; all is neither real nor not real. This is called the true character of all dharmas.”

In passages such as these, the scriptures teach the siddhānta of ultimate meaning. This meaning is extremely deep, hard to see, and hard to understand. Because the Buddha wishes to teach this meaning, he teaches the Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra.

Key Terms in This Opening

般若波羅蜜 · prajñāpāramitāThe perfection of wisdom; wisdom that sees all dharmas without fixed self-nature.
諸法實相 · true character of all dharmasThe way things truly are when not distorted by grasping, projection, or fixed views.
有無二見 · the two views of existence and nonexistenceThe extremes of clinging to “it exists” or “it does not exist.” The middle way releases both.
因緣 · causes and conditionsThe network of conditions through which teachings, experiences, and dharmas arise.
三藏 · Three BasketsThe Buddhist canon of discourses, discipline, and analytical teachings.
菩薩道 · bodhisattva pathThe path of awakening practiced for the liberation and benefit of all beings.
念佛三昧 · samādhi of recollecting the BuddhaA concentrated practice in which the mind is gathered through mindful recollection of the Buddha.
方便 · skillful meansThe compassionate ability to teach or appear in ways that suit the capacities of beings.
四悉檀 · four siddhāntasFour modes in which the Buddha's teachings are true: worldly, individual, counteractive, and ultimate.
第一義悉檀 · siddhānta of ultimate meaningThe truth beyond the reach of conceptual elaboration, where the true character of dharmas is not grasped through language or dispute.

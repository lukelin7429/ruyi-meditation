Fascicle 5 · 瑜伽師地論卷第五 · Continuing Directly from Fascicle 4

The Four Dhyānas' Own Flavor of Bliss

Fascicle 4 ended mid-sentence, partway through its survey of pleasure across the three realms — not a natural stopping point, simply where the physical scroll divides. This page picks up exactly there: the classic qualitative signature of each of the four dhyānas' pleasure and the formless realm's pleasure of release, a six-part scale showing why suffering and pleasure both intensify the further one moves from the human middle, and the treatise's own distinction between ordinary and noble sources of pleasure — continuing the Three Grounds of Applied and Sustained Thought's exhaustive survey of the three realms.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 5 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. The Form Realm's Pleasure, Dhyāna by Dhyāna

Fascicle 4 closed mid-survey of pleasure across the three realms; fascicle 5 opens exactly there, with the classic qualitative signature of each of the form realm's four dhyānas.

Chinese Source

復次，於色界中初靜慮地受生諸天，即受彼地離生喜樂。第二靜慮地諸天，受定生喜樂。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, the gods reborn in the first dhyāna of the form realm experience that very ground's joy and bliss born of seclusion. The gods of the second dhyāna experience joy and bliss born of concentration.

第三靜慮地諸天，受離喜妙樂。第四靜慮地諸天，受捨念清淨寂靜無動之樂。

The gods of the third dhyāna experience the sublime bliss free of joy. The gods of the fourth dhyāna experience the still, unmoving bliss of purified equanimity and mindfulness.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads these four short lines as naming the classic qualitative signature of each dhyāna. The first dhyāna's joy and bliss come specifically from having left the desire realm's turmoil behind — "seclusion" names withdrawal from sensory craving itself — while still depending on applied and sustained thought to hold that seclusion in place; a meditator arrives there by cultivating revulsion toward the coarseness of desire-realm existence and appreciation for the stillness above it, then ascends through access concentration to the dhyāna itself, with weak, middling, or superior mastery determining rebirth into Brahmā's Retinue, Ministers, or Great Brahmā respectively.

The second dhyāna's joy and bliss instead come from concentration itself, once applied and sustained thought — which the first dhyāna still needed, and which itself generates a certain restlessness — finally drop away; he calls this joy and bliss more refined precisely because it is undisturbed by that residual mental activity. The third dhyāna abandons joy itself as still too agitating — he compares the second dhyāna's joy to a swimmer bobbing at the surface, alternately breaking through and sinking back under, against the third dhyāna's uninterrupted immersion, an even, unbroken bliss felt by body and mind together, unmoved by any wave. And the fourth dhyāna abandons pleasant feeling altogether: its "bliss" is not actual pleasant sensation at all, but simply the name given to a level of profound, unmoving stillness — free of the three lower dhyānas' agitations, of the eight kinds of ordinary mental disturbance, and of the cosmic conflagrations of fire, water, and wind that periodically destroy everything beneath it.

2. The Formless Realm's Pleasure of Release

Chinese Source

無色界諸天，受極寂靜解脫之樂。

Ru-Yi Translation

The gods of the formless realm experience the most utterly still pleasure of release.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads each of the four formless spheres as releasing the mind from one further layer of what the sphere below it still depended on: infinite space releases the bondage of the physical body itself, infinite consciousness releases the bondage of even that spatial object, nothingness releases the bondage of consciousness as an object, and neither-perception-nor-non-perception releases the bondage of both having a perception and having none — no trace of the coarser states below remains, yet a single, exceedingly subtle thread of mind persists, which is why it is named neither one nor the other. None of these four counts as pleasant feeling in the ordinary sense; each is simply the profoundly still, neutral feeling of a mind released from one further layer of dependency, "pleasure" here naming that stillness itself rather than any positive sensation.

He draws the wider lesson directly from this survey spanning hell to the formless summit: every realm, high or low, is nothing but mind's own thinking made manifest as a world — the entire three realms are mind-only, all phenomena are consciousness-only, and whatever the mind conceives, a corresponding karma follows and a corresponding result ripens. The one who has not yet seen this still struggles against outer circumstances while leaving the mind itself untouched; the wise instead work on the mind directly, since only uprooting affliction there brings ultimate freedom.

3. Six Ways Suffering and Pleasure Scale Across the Realms

Chinese Source

又由六種殊勝故，苦樂殊勝應知。一、形量殊勝，二、柔軟殊勝，三、緣殊勝，四、時殊勝，五、心殊勝，六、所依殊勝。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, it should be understood that suffering and pleasure themselves become more intense by reason of six distinguishing factors: first, the distinction of bodily size; second, the distinction of bodily softness; third, the distinction of conditions; fourth, the distinction of duration; fifth, the distinction of mind; sixth, the distinction of underlying basis.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads a single sixfold scale as running in both directions across all three realms at once: the higher a realm, the larger and softer its beings' bodies, the more numerous and powerful the conditions supporting their pleasure, the longer their lifespan lasts, the sharper their mind's own discernment and the lighter their affliction, and the more excellent the underlying resultant body itself — every one of these six intensifying pleasure as a realm rises. The same six run in exact reverse for suffering: the lower the realm, the larger and softer the body, meaning more surface area and more sensitivity for pain to work on; the more numerous and severe the conditions of suffering; the longer the torment lasts; the less discernment the mind retains, so worse actions keep getting committed; and the more degraded the underlying body itself — the very same six-part scale, simply read from the opposite end.

4. Why Each of the Six Makes Suffering — and Pleasure — More Intense

Chinese Source

何以故？如如身量漸增廣大，如是如是苦轉殊勝；如如依止漸更柔軟，如是如是苦轉殊勝；如如苦緣漸更猛盛眾多差別，如是如是苦轉殊勝；

Ru-Yi Translation

Why is this? The more a being's bodily size gradually increases, the more intense its suffering becomes. The more its bodily basis grows gradually softer, the more intense its suffering becomes. The more the conditions of its suffering grow gradually more intense and more numerous in kind, the more intense its suffering becomes.

如如時分漸遠無間，如是如是苦轉殊勝；如如內心無簡擇力漸漸增廣，如是如是苦轉殊勝；如如所依苦器漸增，如是如是苦轉殊勝。

The more the duration of its suffering gradually extends without interruption, the more intense its suffering becomes. The more its mind's power of discernment gradually diminishes, the more intense its suffering becomes. The more its underlying vessel of suffering gradually increases, the more intense its suffering becomes.

如苦殊勝如是，樂殊勝義隨其所應，廣說應知。

As with the intensification of suffering, so, in due correspondence, should the intensification of pleasure be understood at length.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through the causal logic behind each of the six factors as applied to suffering, one clause at a time, then simply notes that pleasure intensifies by the identical logic running the opposite direction, without needing to spell it out separately. He glosses the fifth factor, diminished discernment, as the deepest of the six: a being whose mind cannot tell benefit from harm keeps committing ever-worse unwholesome action without any capacity for self-correction, which is precisely why the very worst suffering belongs to beings furthest from any wisdom at all. He notes the five aggregates of clinging themselves count as the underlying "vessel" of suffering in the sixth factor, in four overlapping senses: they are what suffering itself arises from, what a wide range of further sufferings depend on, what ordinary painful feeling directly attaches to, and what pleasant feeling itself eventually turns painful within, once it changes and passes.

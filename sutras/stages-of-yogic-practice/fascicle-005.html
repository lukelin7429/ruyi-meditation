Fascicle 5 · 瑜伽師地論卷第五 · Continuing Directly from Fascicle 4

The Four Dhyānas' Own Flavor of Bliss

Fascicle 4 ended mid-sentence, partway through its survey of pleasure across the three realms — not a natural stopping point, simply where the physical scroll divides. This page picks up exactly there: the classic qualitative signature of each of the four dhyānas' pleasure and the formless realm's pleasure of release, a six-part scale showing why suffering and pleasure both intensify the further one moves from the human middle, and the treatise's own distinction between ordinary and noble sources of pleasure — continuing the Three Grounds of Applied and Sustained Thought's exhaustive survey of the three realms.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 5 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. The Form Realm's Pleasure, Dhyāna by Dhyāna

Fascicle 4 closed mid-survey of pleasure across the three realms; fascicle 5 opens exactly there, with the classic qualitative signature of each of the form realm's four dhyānas.

Chinese Source

復次，於色界中初靜慮地受生諸天，即受彼地離生喜樂。第二靜慮地諸天，受定生喜樂。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, the gods reborn in the first dhyāna of the form realm experience that very ground's joy and bliss born of seclusion. The gods of the second dhyāna experience joy and bliss born of concentration.

第三靜慮地諸天，受離喜妙樂。第四靜慮地諸天，受捨念清淨寂靜無動之樂。

The gods of the third dhyāna experience the sublime bliss free of joy. The gods of the fourth dhyāna experience the still, unmoving bliss of purified equanimity and mindfulness.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads these four short lines as naming the classic qualitative signature of each dhyāna. The first dhyāna's joy and bliss come specifically from having left the desire realm's turmoil behind — "seclusion" names withdrawal from sensory craving itself — while still depending on applied and sustained thought to hold that seclusion in place; a meditator arrives there by cultivating revulsion toward the coarseness of desire-realm existence and appreciation for the stillness above it, then ascends through access concentration to the dhyāna itself, with weak, middling, or superior mastery determining rebirth into Brahmā's Retinue, Ministers, or Great Brahmā respectively.

The second dhyāna's joy and bliss instead come from concentration itself, once applied and sustained thought — which the first dhyāna still needed, and which itself generates a certain restlessness — finally drop away; he calls this joy and bliss more refined precisely because it is undisturbed by that residual mental activity. The third dhyāna abandons joy itself as still too agitating — he compares the second dhyāna's joy to a swimmer bobbing at the surface, alternately breaking through and sinking back under, against the third dhyāna's uninterrupted immersion, an even, unbroken bliss felt by body and mind together, unmoved by any wave. And the fourth dhyāna abandons pleasant feeling altogether: its "bliss" is not actual pleasant sensation at all, but simply the name given to a level of profound, unmoving stillness — free of the three lower dhyānas' agitations, of the eight kinds of ordinary mental disturbance, and of the cosmic conflagrations of fire, water, and wind that periodically destroy everything beneath it.

2. The Formless Realm's Pleasure of Release

Chinese Source

無色界諸天，受極寂靜解脫之樂。

Ru-Yi Translation

The gods of the formless realm experience the most utterly still pleasure of release.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads each of the four formless spheres as releasing the mind from one further layer of what the sphere below it still depended on: infinite space releases the bondage of the physical body itself, infinite consciousness releases the bondage of even that spatial object, nothingness releases the bondage of consciousness as an object, and neither-perception-nor-non-perception releases the bondage of both having a perception and having none — no trace of the coarser states below remains, yet a single, exceedingly subtle thread of mind persists, which is why it is named neither one nor the other. None of these four counts as pleasant feeling in the ordinary sense; each is simply the profoundly still, neutral feeling of a mind released from one further layer of dependency, "pleasure" here naming that stillness itself rather than any positive sensation.

He draws the wider lesson directly from this survey spanning hell to the formless summit: every realm, high or low, is nothing but mind's own thinking made manifest as a world — the entire three realms are mind-only, all phenomena are consciousness-only, and whatever the mind conceives, a corresponding karma follows and a corresponding result ripens. The one who has not yet seen this still struggles against outer circumstances while leaving the mind itself untouched; the wise instead work on the mind directly, since only uprooting affliction there brings ultimate freedom.

3. Six Ways Suffering and Pleasure Scale Across the Realms

Chinese Source

又由六種殊勝故，苦樂殊勝應知。一、形量殊勝，二、柔軟殊勝，三、緣殊勝，四、時殊勝，五、心殊勝，六、所依殊勝。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, it should be understood that suffering and pleasure themselves become more intense by reason of six distinguishing factors: first, the distinction of bodily size; second, the distinction of bodily softness; third, the distinction of conditions; fourth, the distinction of duration; fifth, the distinction of mind; sixth, the distinction of underlying basis.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads a single sixfold scale as running in both directions across all three realms at once: the higher a realm, the larger and softer its beings' bodies, the more numerous and powerful the conditions supporting their pleasure, the longer their lifespan lasts, the sharper their mind's own discernment and the lighter their affliction, and the more excellent the underlying resultant body itself — every one of these six intensifying pleasure as a realm rises. The same six run in exact reverse for suffering: the lower the realm, the larger and softer the body, meaning more surface area and more sensitivity for pain to work on; the more numerous and severe the conditions of suffering; the longer the torment lasts; the less discernment the mind retains, so worse actions keep getting committed; and the more degraded the underlying body itself — the very same six-part scale, simply read from the opposite end.

4. Why Each of the Six Makes Suffering — and Pleasure — More Intense

Chinese Source

何以故？如如身量漸增廣大，如是如是苦轉殊勝；如如依止漸更柔軟，如是如是苦轉殊勝；如如苦緣漸更猛盛眾多差別，如是如是苦轉殊勝；

Ru-Yi Translation

Why is this? The more a being's bodily size gradually increases, the more intense its suffering becomes. The more its bodily basis grows gradually softer, the more intense its suffering becomes. The more the conditions of its suffering grow gradually more intense and more numerous in kind, the more intense its suffering becomes.

如如時分漸遠無間，如是如是苦轉殊勝；如如內心無簡擇力漸漸增廣，如是如是苦轉殊勝；如如所依苦器漸增，如是如是苦轉殊勝。

The more the duration of its suffering gradually extends without interruption, the more intense its suffering becomes. The more its mind's power of discernment gradually diminishes, the more intense its suffering becomes. The more its underlying vessel of suffering gradually increases, the more intense its suffering becomes.

如苦殊勝如是，樂殊勝義隨其所應，廣說應知。

As with the intensification of suffering, so, in due correspondence, should the intensification of pleasure be understood at length.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through the causal logic behind each of the six factors as applied to suffering, one clause at a time, then simply notes that pleasure intensifies by the identical logic running the opposite direction, without needing to spell it out separately. He glosses the fifth factor, diminished discernment, as the deepest of the six: a being whose mind cannot tell benefit from harm keeps committing ever-worse unwholesome action without any capacity for self-correction, which is precisely why the very worst suffering belongs to beings furthest from any wisdom at all. He notes the five aggregates of clinging themselves count as the underlying "vessel" of suffering in the sixth factor, in four overlapping senses: they are what suffering itself arises from, what a wide range of further sufferings depend on, what ordinary painful feeling directly attaches to, and what pleasant feeling itself eventually turns painful within, once it changes and passes.

5. Four Ordinary Resources That Produce Pleasure

The treatise now distinguishes two sources of pleasure: what ordinary resources produce, and what noble wealth produces.

Chinese Source

非聖財所生樂者，謂四種資具為緣得生。一、適悅資具，二、滋長資具，三、清淨資具，四、任持資具。

Ru-Yi Translation

Pleasure not born of noble wealth arises in dependence on four kinds of resource: first, resources of delight; second, resources of nourishment; third, resources of good omen; fourth, resources of sustenance.

適悅資具者，謂車乘、衣服、諸莊嚴具、歌笑舞樂、塗香華鬘、種種上妙珍玩樂具、光明照曜、男女侍衛、種種庫藏。

Resources of delight are: conveyances, clothing, every kind of ornament, song and laughter and dance and music, scented oils and garlands, every manner of fine and wondrous plaything, bright illumination, male and female attendants, and every kind of storehouse of goods.

滋長資具者，謂無尋思輪石搥打、築蹋、按摩等事。清淨資具者，謂吉祥草、頻螺果、螺貝滿甕等事。任持資具者，謂飲及食。

Resources of nourishment are: being struck with a mindless roller or stone, being pounded, being trodden upon, being massaged, and the like. Resources of good omen are: auspicious grass, the bilva fruit, jars filled with conch shells, and the like. Resources of sustenance are drink and food.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi catalogues ordinary pleasure by what actually produces it: fine possessions and entertainment, direct bodily therapies such as massage, culturally-designated lucky objects, and simple nourishment. He flags the third category as frankly conventional rather than intrinsically meaningful — auspicious grass or a jar of shells brought good fortune to an ancient Indian household purely because that culture agreed it did, the same way modern customs disagree on which color of gift envelope is lucky; there is nothing objectively special about the objects themselves, only a shared social designation.

6. Seven Kinds of Noble Wealth

Chinese Source

聖財所生樂者，謂七聖財為緣得生。何等為七？一、信，二、戒，三、慚，四、愧，五、聞，六、捨，七、慧。

Ru-Yi Translation

Pleasure born of noble wealth arises in dependence on the seven kinds of noble wealth. What are these seven? Faith, moral discipline, conscientiousness, a sense of shame, learning, generosity, and wisdom.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats these seven as the actual provisions for the path to awakening, each generating its own distinct pleasure. Faith, rooted deeply enough that no rival teacher, no worldly power, not even Māra himself can uproot it, yields the quiet pleasure of simply trusting the Three Jewels. Moral discipline, freedom from the ten unwholesome courses of action, yields the pleasure of a fortunate rebirth. Conscientiousness — refusing wrongdoing purely out of self-respect, whether or not anyone is watching — and its companion, a sense of shame before others, together yield freedom from remorse. He tells the classical story of a poor father caught stealing a neighbor's turnip by moonlight with his young son, who warns him "someone can see you" — meaning the moon itself — at which the father, overcome with shame, abandons the theft and takes his son home empty-handed: a small act of conscience, he notes, worth more than the turnip ever could have been.

Learning yields the pleasure of correct understanding; generosity, freely given without any expectation of return, yields future freedom from want; and wisdom — genuine insight into ultimate truth — yields a liberation no ordinary pleasure can match. Practitioners should regularly examine which of these seven they still lack, he says, since worldly wealth can always be seized by five hostile forces — rulers, thieves, fire, flood, and an unworthy heir — while noble wealth cannot be touched by any of them.

7. Fifteen Ways Noble Wealth Outshines Ordinary Wealth, Part One

Chinese Source

復次，由十五種相，聖非聖財所生樂差別。謂非聖財所生樂能起惡行，聖財所生樂能起妙行。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, noble and non-noble wealth's pleasure differ by fifteen distinguishing features. Non-noble wealth's pleasure can give rise to unwholesome conduct; noble wealth's pleasure can give rise to excellent conduct.

又非聖財所生樂，有罪喜樂相應；聖財所生樂，無罪喜樂相應。又非聖財所生樂，微小不遍所依；聖財所生樂，廣大遍滿所依。

Non-noble wealth's pleasure is associated with blameworthy delight; noble wealth's pleasure is associated with blameless delight. Non-noble wealth's pleasure is slight, never pervading its basis fully; noble wealth's pleasure is vast, pervading its basis completely.

又非聖財所生樂，非一切時有，以依外緣故；聖財所生樂，一切時有，以依內緣故。又非聖財所生樂，非一切地有，唯欲界故；聖財所生樂，一切地有，通三界繫及不繫故。

Non-noble wealth's pleasure is not present at all times, since it depends on external conditions; noble wealth's pleasure is present at all times, since it depends on internal conditions. Non-noble wealth's pleasure is not present in every ground, existing only in the desire realm; noble wealth's pleasure is present in every ground, extending through all three realms, whether one is still bound by them or already free.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi works through the first five of fifteen contrasts. Ordinary pleasure, chased through possessions and sensation, tends toward unwholesome conduct and comes tangled with the very craving, aversion, and delusion that make it blameworthy in the first place; noble pleasure arises from genuinely wholesome conduct and carries no such taint. Ordinary pleasure touches only part of a person at once — good food might please the body while the mind stays unsettled, or an exhilarating hike leaves the body exhausted even as the mind feels good — while noble pleasure, arising from a settled, clarified mind, saturates body and mind together; someone with real conviction in the practice can remain at ease even through real physical discomfort, since the mind's own peace isn't hostage to the body's condition.

Ordinary pleasure depends on the right external circumstances lining up — someone to give a massage, a shop open to sell the right car, a favorable season — and simply isn't available when those circumstances aren't; noble pleasure depends only on the mind's own state, so a single moment of genuine mindfulness produces it on the spot, with nothing external required. And ordinary pleasure exists only within the desire realm, tied to physical sense-objects the higher realms have already outgrown, while noble pleasure runs through the entire nine-ground structure of all three realms at once — available equally to an ordinary being still bound within them and to a noble one already free, since a mind rightly disposed can access it wherever it happens to be.

8. Fifteen Ways Noble Wealth Outshines Ordinary Wealth, Part Two

Chinese Source

又非聖財所生樂，不能引發後世聖非聖財；聖財所生樂，能引發後世聖非聖財。

Ru-Yi Translation

Non-noble wealth's pleasure cannot give rise to noble or non-noble wealth in a future life; noble wealth's pleasure can give rise to both noble and non-noble wealth in a future life.

又非聖財所生樂，若受用時，有盡有邊；聖財所生樂，若受用時，轉更充盛，增長廣大。

Non-noble wealth's pleasure, when enjoyed, is exhaustible and has a limit; noble wealth's pleasure, when enjoyed, only grows fuller and more abundant, expanding without bound.

又非聖財所生樂，為他劫奪，若王、若賊、怨，及水、火；聖財所生樂，無能侵奪。

Non-noble wealth's pleasure can be seized by others — by a king, a thief, an enemy, by flood or by fire; noble wealth's pleasure can be seized by nothing at all.

又非聖財所生樂，不可從今世持往後世；聖財所生樂，可從今世持往後世。又非聖財所生樂，受用之時不可充足；聖財所生樂，受用之時究竟充滿。

Non-noble wealth's pleasure cannot be carried from this life into the next; noble wealth's pleasure can be carried from this life into the next. Non-noble wealth's pleasure, however much it is enjoyed, can never be fully satisfied; noble wealth's pleasure, when enjoyed, is completely and utterly fulfilling.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi continues the contrast through five more dimensions. Ordinary pleasure, chased purely for its own momentary sake, produces no lasting fund of wealth at all for a future life, noble or otherwise; noble pleasure, by contrast, actually deepens the very faculties that produced it, so each act of genuine faith or generosity compounds into more of both spiritual and worldly fortune down the line. Ordinary pleasure is inherently exhaustible — food runs out, entertainment tires, no wardrobe can be worn all at once — while noble pleasure only intensifies with use, the way genuine engagement with a difficult teaching turns frustration into growing delight the more one actually studies it.

Ordinary wealth can be seized outright by a ruler's decree, a thief, an enemy, or simple disaster, while noble wealth, having no physical form at all, cannot be stolen or destroyed by any external force whatsoever. Ordinary wealth, being purely material, cannot follow a person past death, while noble wealth, being a quality of mind itself, carries forward into the very next life, strengthening the same faculties life after life. And ordinary pleasure, however much is consumed, never actually satisfies — a house, clothes, a car, always leaves room for more — while noble pleasure, arising from contentment itself rather than accumulation, is completely fulfilling the moment it is present: real faith, real ethical restraint, real generosity, and real wisdom each bring their own immediate, sufficient sense of ease, nothing further required.

9. The Last Five of Fifteen Contrasts

Chinese Source

有怖畏者，謂懼當生苦所依處故。有怨對者，謂鬥訟違諍所依處故。有災橫者，謂老病死所依處故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Carrying dread means it is the basis for fearing a future birth into suffering. Carrying enmity means it is the basis for quarrels, litigation, and conflict. Carrying calamity means it is the basis for old age, sickness, and death.

有燒惱者，謂由此樂性不真實，如疥癩病，虛妄顛倒所依處故，愁歎憂苦種種熱惱所依處故。不能斷後世大苦者，謂貪瞋等本隨二惑所依處故。

Carrying affliction means that, its very nature being unreal, like a case of scabies, it is the basis for delusion and distortion, and the basis for sorrow, lamentation, and every kind of burning distress. Being unable to sever the great suffering of a future life means it is the basis for the root afflictions and their secondary forms alike, such as craving and hatred.

聖財所生樂，無怖畏、無怨對、無災橫、無燒惱、能斷後世大苦。

Noble wealth's pleasure carries no dread, no enmity, no calamity, no affliction, and is capable of severing the great suffering of a future life.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi closes the fifteen-point comparison with its final five, all describing ordinary pleasure's hidden cost. It carries the seed of future rebirth into suffering, breeds conflict with others over its pursuit, and cannot shield anyone from old age, sickness, and death — he cites the Buddha's own teaching that these three are universally unwelcome, yet universally unavoidable, so that a lifetime spent chasing pleasure while ignoring this fact ends up as wasted effort against an outcome nothing can prevent.

He compares ordinary pleasure to a case of scabies: someone with the itch mistakes the itching for enjoyment and scratches with delight, oblivious that the sensation is actually a symptom of disease — deluded beings likewise mistake what is really unstable and precarious for something worth pursuing. He cites a classical Ming-dynasty folk song on this same theme, cataloguing a single person's wants escalating without end — food, then clothes, then a house, a wife, a horse, servants, rank, the throne itself, even flight to the heavens — never once arriving at satisfaction, interrupted only by death. And because ordinary pleasure is inseparable from the root and secondary afflictions that produce it, it can never sever the great suffering still to come; noble wealth's pleasure, arising instead from a mind already working to uproot those very afflictions, is free of all five of these costs at once.

10. Two Kinds of Consumption: Desire's Objects, and the Dharma

Chinese Source

又外有欲者，受用欲塵。聖慧命者，受用正法。由五種相故有差別。由此因緣，說聖慧命者以無上慧命清淨自活。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, one whose desire is directed outward consumes the objects of desire; one whose life is sustained by noble wisdom consumes the true Dharma. These differ by five distinguishing marks; for this reason, it is said that one whose life is sustained by noble wisdom lives on this unsurpassed, pure wisdom-life.

何等為五？一、受用正法者，不染汙故；二、受用正法者，極畢竟故；三、受用正法者，一向定故；四、受用正法者，與餘慧命者不共故；五、受用正法者，有真實樂故，摧伏魔怨故。

What are the five? First, consuming the true Dharma is undefiled; second, it is utterly final; third, it is one-pointedly certain; fourth, it is not shared with other so-called wisdom-lives; fifth, it carries genuine pleasure, and overcomes the enemy that is Māra.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as a deliberate pun: just as an ordinary being "lives on" whatever food sustains the body, a practitioner whose life is genuinely sustained by wisdom "lives on" the Dharma itself, consuming teaching the way others consume sense-pleasure. He glosses the true Dharma here as the canon's traditional twelvefold division of scripture by literary genre — direct discourse, verse, prophecy, spontaneous utterance, narrative of causes, parable, past lives of disciples, past lives of the Buddha, extended teaching, marvels, and doctrinal analysis — covering both the "gate of understanding," the actual text and teaching, and the "gate of practice," putting it into action through body, speech, and mind. The five marks that follow show exactly how this second kind of nourishment differs from the first.

11. Undefiled, and Utterly Final

Chinese Source

此中諸受欲者所有欲樂是隨順喜處，貪愛所隨故；是隨順憂處，瞋恚所隨故；是隨順捨處，無揀擇捨之所隨故。聖慧命者受用正法則不如是。

Ru-Yi Translation

Here, the pleasure of desire experienced by those who consume desire's objects is, when it leans toward joy, followed by craving; when it leans toward sorrow, followed by hatred; and when it leans toward equanimity, followed by an equanimity devoid of any real discernment. One whose life is sustained by noble wisdom, consuming the true Dharma, is not like this.

又諸有欲者受用欲塵，從不可知本際以來，以無常故，捨餘欲塵得餘欲塵；或於一時都無所得。聖慧命者受用正法則不如是。

Further, those who consume the objects of desire, since beginningless time, forever impermanent, abandon one object of desire only to seize another — or, at times, gain nothing at all. One whose life is sustained by noble wisdom, consuming the true Dharma, is not like this.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains the first mark, undefiled: every pleasant, painful, or neutral feeling an ordinary desire-driven consumption produces automatically drags along its matching affliction — craving trailing pleasure, aversion trailing pain, and a dull, undiscerning equanimity trailing neutral feeling — so that desire's pleasure is never actually separable from the affliction it breeds. Consuming the Dharma carries no such automatic contamination.

The second mark, utterly final, points to how ordinary desire never actually concludes: satisfied in one direction, it simply redirects toward the next object, endlessly, since beginningless time — he again invokes the classical folk song already cited earlier in this fascicle, its escalating list of wants each achieved only to reveal a further want beneath it, ending only at death rather than at satisfaction. Consuming the Dharma, by contrast, has an actual terminus: complete, unsurpassed awakening, a genuine finishing point ordinary desire can never reach.

12. Unwavering, and Uncommon With Other Paths

Chinese Source

又受欲者受用欲時，即於此事一起喜愛，一起憂恚；復即於彼或時生喜，或時生憂。聖慧命者受用正法則不如是。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, when one who consumes desire's objects consumes them, joy and delight arise together over this very matter, and sorrow and resentment arise together as well; and toward that same object, joy arises at one time and sorrow at another. One whose life is sustained by noble wisdom, consuming the true Dharma, is not like this.

又諸離欲外慧命者，於種種見趣、自分別所起邪勝解處，其心猛利，種種取著，恆為欲染之所隨逐；雖已離欲，復還退起。聖慧命者受用正法則不如是。

Further, non-Buddhist practitioners who claim wisdom yet have merely become free of desire, caught up in various views and in the distorted convictions their own conceptual thinking has generated, hold to these with a fierce and manifold attachment, and remain forever pursued by the very taint of desire — though already free of desire, they fall back into it once again. One whose life is sustained by noble wisdom, consuming the true Dharma, is not like this.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the third mark, one-pointedly certain, as naming the sheer instability of ordinary emotional life: the very same object provokes delight one moment and resentment the next, entirely at the mercy of shifting circumstance, while Dharma-consumption stays fixed in a settled equanimity that shifting circumstance cannot move.

The fourth mark, uncommon with other paths, is aimed specifically at non-Buddhist meditators who have genuinely attained freedom from desire through worldly concentration, yet still cling to a view of self at the root of their practice — he traces this to the traditional catalogue of sixty-two wrong views, all ultimately variations on either eternalism or annihilationism built around a real self. Because that underlying self-love is never actually uprooted, even a meditator who has climbed all the way to the peak of the formless realm eventually exhausts that state's karmic support and falls back into desire-realm rebirth, desire's grip reasserting itself once the meditative state that suppressed it runs out. He cites Venerable Yin Shun's observation that craving directed at meditative bliss itself, in the higher realms, is actually a subtler and more dangerous form of desire than ordinary desire-realm craving, precisely because it looks, on the surface, like liberation — and warns that even a bodhisattva should not linger too long in deep absorption for its own sake, but return to the desire realm to actively benefit other beings, since any residual attachment to the pleasure of concentration is itself still a form of self-grasping that blocks genuine freedom.

13. Genuine Joy That Conquers the Four Māras

Chinese Source

又受欲者及諸世間已離欲者，所有欲樂及離欲樂，皆非真實，皆為魔怨之所隨逐；如幻、如響、如影、如焰、如夢所見、猶如幻作諸莊嚴具。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, the pleasure of desire enjoyed by those who consume desire's objects, and the pleasure of freedom from desire enjoyed by those in the world already free of it, are both alike unreal, and both alike remain pursued by the enemy that is Māra — like an illusion, like an echo, like a shadow, like a mirage, like what is seen in a dream, like ornaments conjured by a magician.

又著樂愚夫諸受欲者，及諸世間已離欲者，凡所受用，猶如癲狂、如醉亂等；未制魔軍而有受用。是故彼樂為非真實，亦不能制所有魔事。聖慧命者受用正法則不如是。

Further, the foolish, pleasure-attached consumers of desire, and those in the world already free of desire, both consume it as though deranged, as though drunk and disordered — enjoying it without ever having subdued Māra's own army. For this reason, that pleasure is unreal, and cannot overcome any of Māra's works. One whose life is sustained by noble wisdom, consuming the true Dharma, is not like this.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the fifth and final mark as the culmination of all the others: even the refined bliss of a non-Buddhist meditator's deep absorption is, on this reckoning, no more real than a mirage or a dream — vivid in the moment, but built on nothing that actually lasts, and still fully within reach of what the tradition calls the four Māras. The first is the aggregates themselves, the conditioned body and mind that constitute the very battlefield death and rebirth are fought over; the second, and most fundamental, is affliction itself, the actual root of all suffering, since removing it removes suffering entirely; the third is death, which can end a practice at any moment regardless of how close it stood to success; and the fourth is a literal tempter-deity, ruler of the highest desire-realm heaven, who actively conjures obstacles and distractions for anyone approaching genuine freedom from desire.

Because none of these four is even addressed by ordinary pleasure or by desire-free meditative bliss alone, both remain, in the treatise's own words, "consumed as though deranged" — pursued without any real victory over what actually holds a being in bondage. Only consuming the true Dharma, aimed at uprooting affliction itself, actually defeats all four Māras at once, which is why it alone counts as genuinely, not merely apparently, joyful. With this, the treatise's whole account of pleasure — running from the four ordinary resources through the seven kinds of noble wealth, the fifteen contrasts, and now these five marks of true Dharma-consumption — comes to a close.

14. Observing the Body as a Boil, and the Three Feelings as Its Symptoms

The treatise now teaches how to correctly observe the three kinds of feeling, using a single sustained image throughout.

Chinese Source

復次，三界有情所依之身，當云何觀？謂如毒熱癰，麤重所隨故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, how should the body that beings of the three realms depend on be observed? As a poisonous, feverish boil, followed everywhere by its own underlying affliction.

即於此身樂受生時，當云何觀？謂如毒熱癰暫遇冷觸。即於此身苦受生時，當云何觀？謂如毒熱癰為熱灰所觸。

When a pleasant feeling arises within this very body, how should it be observed? As when a poisonous, feverish boil is briefly touched by something cool. When a painful feeling arises within this very body, how should it be observed? As when a poisonous, feverish boil is touched by hot ash.

即於此身不苦不樂受生時，當云何觀？謂如毒熱癰離冷熱等觸，自性毒熱而本住故。

When a neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling arises within this very body, how should it be observed? As when a poisonous, feverish boil, touched by neither cold nor heat, simply rests in its own underlying feverish, poisoned nature.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this whole passage as a single sustained image: the body itself is a festering boil, carrying its own affliction-seeds within it regardless of what touches it from outside. A pleasant feeling is nothing more than a cool compress laid briefly over that same boil — genuinely soothing in the moment, but never actually curing anything underneath. A painful feeling is hot ash dropped onto it, pain piled directly onto pain. And a neutral feeling is simply the boil left untouched by either — not actually healthy, only quiet, its own festering nature still there the whole time, merely unmasked by any outside stimulus loud enough to disguise it.

He connects this to the classical contemplation of the body's impurity from conception through gestation to its constant discharge and eventual decay, and to the four elements' own internal conflict, each one undermining the others like venomous snakes sealed in a single box. He reads the ultimate lesson as the classical teaching that all feeling, without exception, is suffering: not because pleasure never actually feels good, but because it is never anything more than temporary relief laid over a condition that never actually resolves — recognizing this is what lets a practitioner meet pain without resentment, pleasure without craving, and neutral feeling without the dull confusion that otherwise passes unnoticed.

15. Three Kinds of Suffering Hidden in the Three Feelings

Chinese Source

薄伽梵說：當知樂受，壞苦故苦；苦受，苦苦故苦；不苦不樂受，行苦故苦。

Ru-Yi Translation

The Blessed One said: it should be understood that pleasant feeling is suffering through the suffering of change; painful feeling is suffering through the suffering of pain itself; and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling is suffering through the suffering inherent in all conditioned formations.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains why the Buddha singled out neutral feeling for this particular label: the suffering of pain and the suffering of pleasant feeling's own eventual collapse are both obvious enough on their own, but the third, subtlest kind of suffering — simply being a conditioned, ever-shifting formation, never resting for even a single moment — hides equally within all three feelings, yet is masked in the other two by the noise of active craving or active aversion. Only in neutral feeling, where neither craving nor aversion is loudly distracting the mind, does this baseline restlessness actually become visible — which is exactly why the boil analogy works: cold relief and hot pain are both distracting sensations layered on top, while the boil's own constant, underlying condition is only clearly felt once both are removed.

16. Three Kinds of Joy, and the Deepest Bliss of All

Chinese Source

復說有有愛味喜、有離愛味喜、有勝離愛味喜。應知墮二界攝。

Ru-Yi Translation

It is further said that there is joy with a savor of craving, joy free of that savor, and a superior joy still more free of it. These should be understood as comprised within two realms.

又薄伽梵建立想受滅樂為樂中第一。此依住樂，非謂受樂。

Further, the Blessed One established the bliss of the cessation of ideation and feeling as foremost among all forms of bliss. This is the bliss of abiding, not the bliss of feeling.

又說有三種樂。謂離貪、離瞋、離癡等欲。此三種樂唯無漏界中可得，是故此樂名為常樂，無漏界攝。

It is further said that there are three kinds of bliss: freedom from the craving of desire, freedom from the craving of hatred, and freedom from the craving of delusion. These three kinds of bliss can be found only within the uncontaminated realm; for this reason, this bliss is called constant bliss, comprised within the uncontaminated realm.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes three grades of joy: joy still flavored by craving belongs to the desire realm's ordinary sensory pleasure; joy free of that craving belongs to the first and second dhyānas' meditative joy, genuinely free of desire-realm attachment but still capable of a subtler backsliding; and a superior, uncontaminated joy has actually eliminated the underlying seed of attachment to meditative absorption itself. He notes the formless realm has no joy at all in this technical sense, which is why the whole scheme is said to belong to only two realms.

He then singles out a joy that isn't ordinary feeling at all: the cessation of ideation and feeling, the deepest meditative attainment available to a noble one at the third fruit of the path or above, in which the sixth consciousness's usual activity of feeling actually stops. Because feeling itself has ceased, this "bliss" cannot be pleasant feeling in the ordinary sense — it is instead the bliss of a mind simply, deeply at rest, free of the fatigue and obstruction that would otherwise trouble it. Beyond even this sits a final, genuinely permanent bliss: freedom from craving, hatred, and delusion themselves, available only in the uncontaminated realm and hence alone worthy of being called constant, since it alone is untouched by the marks of impermanence that unravel every other kind of pleasure eventually — the ceaseless arising and passing of conditioned things, the fixed limit on any lifespan, the eventual collapse of any world-system, the ever-present risk of an early death, and the simple fact that no state of ordinary happiness can be guaranteed to last even for one lifetime.

17. Four Kinds of Food That Sustain the Three Realms

The treatise's account of enjoyment now turns from feeling itself to the second of its original three topics: how beings across the three realms are actually nourished.

Chinese Source

復次，飲食受用者，謂三界將生、已生有情壽命安住。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, as for the consumption of food: it is what sustains the lifespan of beings across the three realms, whether about to be born or already born.

此中當知觸、意思、識三種食故，一切三界有情壽命安住。段食一種，唯令欲界有情壽命安住。

Here it should be understood that it is by reason of three kinds of food — contact, mental intention, and consciousness — that all beings throughout the three realms have their lifespan sustained. The one remaining kind, edible food, sustains only the lifespan of beings within the desire realm.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains the traditional fourfold food: ordinary edible food, taken in and broken down to nourish the body; contact, the mere meeting of a sense-faculty with its object, pleasant or soothing enough in itself to sustain a being; mental intention, the hopeful anticipation of something desired, which can itself keep a starving being alive a little longer through sheer expectation; and consciousness, the ālayavijñāna's own act of sustaining the body, the deepest and most fundamental of the four, since the other three all ultimately nourish it. Three of these four — contact, intention, and consciousness — operate throughout all three realms, since they are purely mental; edible food alone belongs to the desire realm, tied to physical digestion no higher realm still needs.

He distinguishes two moments where this matters: a being about to be born relies on the ālayavijñāna's own seed of continued existence, freshly moistened by craving and clinging, to actually take hold of and complete a new body — this alone is what "sustains" a life not yet begun; a being already born needs all four kinds of food together to keep weakened faculties and elements in good repair and to recover from illness, and a being who goes without edible food for too long, however otherwise long-lived, will die before its natural term.

18. Edible Food, Destiny by Destiny

Chinese Source

復於那落迦受生有情，有微細段食。謂腑藏中有微動風，由此因緣，彼得久住。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, beings reborn in the hells have a subtle kind of edible food: a faintly stirring wind within their internal organs, by reason of which they continue to endure.

餓鬼、傍生、人中，有麤段食。謂作分段而噉食之。

Among hungry ghosts, animals, and humans, there is coarse edible food: food taken in discrete portions and actually eaten.

復有微細食。謂住羯羅藍等位有情，及欲界諸天。由彼食已，所有段食流入一切身分支節，尋即銷化，無有便穢。

There is, further, a subtle food: that of beings dwelling at the kalala and similar early embryonic stages, and of the desire realm's gods. Once such a being has eaten, this edible food flows into every limb and joint of the body and is immediately dissolved, leaving no waste behind.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes even hell-beings, whose suffering was described at such length earlier in this fascicle's account of the desire realm, still require a kind of food — the faintest stirring of wind within the body — simply to keep existing at all. Hungry ghosts, animals, and humans eat in the ordinary, recognizable sense, though even here he notes a customary pattern: animals mostly eating in the afternoon, hungry ghosts at night, humans across all three regular meals, gods in the morning, and buddhas at midday. The subtlest tier belongs to two very different kinds of beings for the same underlying reason: an embryo in its earliest weeks, absorbing nourishment through the mother's own subtle bodily fluids before any digestive system exists to process coarser food, and the desire-realm gods, whose refined bodies absorb their food directly into every limb with nothing left over to excrete — the same principle, mild version in the womb and full version in heaven, of nourishment too refined to produce any waste at all. He adds, in closing, that the form realm needs no food whatsoever, sustained instead by the joy of meditative absorption itself, and that the formless realm, having no body, has no occasion for the question to even arise.

19. Sexual Activity, Realm by Realm

The treatise's third and final aspect of enjoyment traces sexual activity through a single, deliberately graded sequence across the realms.

Chinese Source

復次，婬欲受用者，諸那落迦中所有有情皆無婬事。所以者何？由彼有情長時無間多受種種極猛利苦。由此因緣，彼諸有情，若男於女不起女欲，若女於男不起男欲，何況展轉二二交會。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, as for the consumption of sexual activity: beings throughout every hell engage in no such act at all. Why is this? Because those beings, for a long, unbroken span of time, undergo suffering of every kind, extremely intense throughout. Because of this, among them, no man ever conceives desire toward a woman, nor any woman toward a man — much less do the two sexual organs ever actually come together.

若鬼、傍生、人中，所有依身苦樂相雜，故有婬欲，男女展轉二二交會，不淨流出。欲界諸天雖行婬欲，無此不淨；然於根門有風氣出，煩惱便息。

Among hungry ghosts, animals, and humans, the body they depend on mixes pain and pleasure together, and so sexual desire is present: male and female come together, and impure fluid is released. The desire realm's gods, though they too engage in sexual activity, produce no such impurity; rather, a breath of air simply passes through the sexual organ, and the affliction is thereby put to rest.

四大王眾天，二二交會，熱惱方息。如四大王眾天，三十三天亦爾。時分天唯互相抱，熱惱便息。知足天唯相執手，熱惱便息。樂化天相顧而笑，熱惱便息。他化自在天眼相顧視，熱惱便息。

Among the Four Great Kings, it is the actual union of the two organs that lays the affliction to rest. As with the Four Great Kings, so too with the Thirty-Three. The Time-Divisions Heaven's beings need only embrace one another for the affliction to be put to rest. The Contentment Heaven's beings need only hold hands. The Heaven of Delighting in Transformation's beings need only exchange a smiling glance. The Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations' beings need only look at one another with their eyes.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads hell-beings as entirely exempt not through any special virtue but simply because unrelenting suffering leaves no room for desire to even begin forming — he cites the classical eightfold sequence by which sexual craving is said to actually build, from an initial spark of attraction all the way to physical union, and notes that hell-beings never even reach the first stage. Hungry ghosts, animals, and humans engage in full physical union with actual bodily fluid released, since their bodies mix pleasure and pain in the ordinary way.

From the desire-realm heavens upward, the same underlying craving persists, but its resolution grows steadily more refined at each tier — the lower two heavens still through bodily union, the four higher heavens successively through a mere embrace, joined hands, a shared smile, and finally nothing more than an exchanged glance — the craving itself never disappearing, only requiring less and less contact to be satisfied the more refined the birth becomes.

20. Marriage, and Uttarakuru's Different Way

Chinese Source

又三洲人攝受妻妾，施設嫁娶。北拘盧洲無我所故，無攝受故，一切有情無攝受妻妾，亦無嫁娶。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, the people of the three continents take wives and concubines, and establish the institutions of marriage. In Uttarakuru, since there is no notion of "mine," and hence no possessive attachment, no being there takes a wife or concubine, nor is there any marriage.

如三洲人，如是大力鬼及欲界諸天亦爾。唯除樂化天及他化自在天。

As with the people of the three continents, so too with powerful ghosts and the desire realm's gods — with the sole exception of the Heaven of Delighting in Transformation and the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi contrasts the three ordinary continents' formal institution of marriage, built on the very idea of possession — a spouse who is "mine" — with Uttarakuru's complete absence of that concept, already noted earlier in this fascicle's account of the northern continent's wish-fulfilling paradise: no one there recognizes even their own parents as "belonging" to them, so there is nothing resembling courtship or a wedding to arrange. He describes the alternative custom instead: a couple simply goes together beneath a large tree, and if they are unknowingly related, the tree's guardian spirit refuses to let its branches lower to shelter them; if unrelated, the branches lower on their own and the union proceeds, with any resulting child simply left along the road for passersby to feed by touching a finger to its mouth until it can fend for itself. The same institution of marriage found on the three ordinary continents extends to powerful ghosts and most of the desire realm's heavens as well, with the two most refined heavens — where mere eye contact or a shared smile already resolves any craving — having no need of marriage's formal structure at all.

21. How Desire-Realm Gods Are Born

Chinese Source

又一切欲界天眾，無有處女胎藏。然四大王眾天於父母肩上，或於懷中，如五歲小兒欻然化出；三十三天如六歲；時分天如七歲；知足天如八歲；樂化天如九歲；他化自在天如十歲。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, none among the desire realm's heavenly assemblies undergo pregnancy in a womb. Rather, among the Four Great Kings, a being suddenly appears, fully formed, upon its parents' shoulder or within their embrace, resembling a five-year-old child; among the Thirty-Three, resembling a six-year-old; the Time-Divisions Heaven, a seven-year-old; the Contentment Heaven, an eight-year-old; the Heaven of Delighting in Transformation, a nine-year-old; and the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations, a ten-year-old.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes that desire-realm gods, being spontaneously born, skip pregnancy, infancy, and childhood entirely, simply appearing already at a specific apparent age on their new parents' shoulder or in their arms — and, in a small but telling detail, that apparent age climbs by exactly one year at each successive heaven, from five at the lowest up to ten at the highest, mirroring the same steady refinement already seen in every other measure across these six heavens.

22. Birth Establishment: the Three Kinds of Desire-Birth

Having finished enjoyment, the treatise opens its sixth aspect of the three realms' establishment: birth itself, beginning with three kinds of birth defined by desire.

Chinese Source

復次，生建立者，謂三種欲生。或有眾生現住欲塵，由此現住欲塵故，富貴自在。彼復云何？謂一切人及四大王眾天，乃至知足天。是名第一欲生。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, as for birth establishment: there are three kinds of desire-birth. There are beings who dwell directly in present desire-objects, and by reason of these present desire-objects, are wealthy, noble, and unhindered. What are these? All humans, and the Four Great Kings up through the Contentment Heaven. This is called the first desire-birth.

或有眾生變化欲塵，由此變化欲塵故，富貴自在。彼復云何？謂樂化天。由彼諸天為自己故，化為欲塵，非為他故；唯自變化諸欲塵故，富貴自在。是名第二欲生。

There are beings who transform desire-objects, and by reason of these transformed desire-objects, are wealthy, noble, and unhindered. What are these? The Heaven of Delighting in Transformation. Those gods transform desire-objects for their own sake, not for the sake of others; solely by transforming desire-objects for themselves are they wealthy, noble, and unhindered. This is called the second desire-birth.

或有眾生他化欲塵，由他所化諸欲塵故，富貴自在。彼復云何？謂他化自在天。由彼諸天為自因緣亦能變化，為他因緣亦能變化，故於自化非為希奇；用他所化欲塵為富貴自在，故說此天為他化自在。非彼諸天唯受用他所化欲塵，亦有受用自所化欲塵者。是名第三欲生。

There are beings for whom others transform desire-objects, and by reason of these desire-objects transformed by others, are wealthy, noble, and unhindered. What are these? The Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations. Those gods can transform objects for their own causes and conditions just as well as for others' — self-transformation holds nothing remarkable for them — and it is specifically their use of what others transform for them that constitutes their wealth, nobility, and freedom, hence this heaven is named "mastery over others' transformations." Yet it is not that these gods consume only what others transform for them; some also consume what they themselves transform. This is called the third desire-birth.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this triad as a ladder of steadily increasing mastery over the very source of one's pleasures. The first and largest tier — all humans and gods up through the Contentment Heaven — simply enjoys desire-objects already present, taking the world as it is given. The second tier, the Heaven of Delighting in Transformation, no longer needs to take the world as given: he distinguishes transforming an existing object into another (an apple one no longer wants becoming a pear one does) from manifesting an object from nothing at all (wishing for a peach and having one simply appear) — either way, this heaven's gods do the transforming themselves, purely for their own use.

The third and highest tier, the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations, could do the same self-transforming just as easily — the text is careful to say self-transformation is "nothing remarkable" for them — but their defining mark is instead that others transform desire-objects for their enjoyment, a still more refined form of "wealth and mastery" than doing the work oneself. This threefold scheme of present-object, self-transformed, and other-transformed enjoyment gives the treatise's technical account of why desire-realm existence is called a matter of desire-birth at all — three distinct ways craving for sense-pleasure takes hold of a life.

23. The Three Kinds of Joy-Birth

Chinese Source

復有三種樂生。或有眾生用離生喜樂灌灑其身，謂初靜慮地諸天。是名第一樂生。或有眾生由定生喜樂灌灑其身，謂第二靜慮地諸天。是名第二樂生。或有眾生以離喜樂灌灑其身，謂第三靜慮地諸天。是名第三樂生。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are, further, three kinds of joy-birth. There are beings whose entire body is, as it were, drenched in the joy and bliss born of seclusion — the gods of the first dhyāna ground. This is called the first joy-birth. There are beings whose entire body is drenched in the joy and bliss born of concentration — the gods of the second dhyāna ground. This is called the second joy-birth. There are beings whose entire body is drenched in the bliss free of joy — the gods of the third dhyāna ground. This is called the third joy-birth.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi contrasts this triad with the desire-birth just given: where desire-birth is a mixture of pleasure and pain, joy-birth names genuine, unmixed happiness, the very qualities of the first three dhyānas already described earlier in this fascicle — seclusion's joy and bliss, concentration's joy and bliss, and the sublime bliss free of joy — now redescribed as so pervasive that they are said to "drench the entire body," the way water poured over the head runs down and soaks every limb. He notes what the list conspicuously omits: the fourth dhyāna and the four formless spheres are not named among the three joy-births at all, a gap the treatise itself goes on to explain in the very next passage.

24. Why Establish These Six Kinds of Birth — the Three Seekings

The treatise now explains why exactly three desire-births and three joy-births are singled out for classification, tracing the answer to what beings are actually seeking.

Chinese Source

問：何故建立三種欲生、三種樂生耶？答：由三種求故。一、欲求，二、有求，三、梵行求。謂若諸沙門或婆羅門墮欲求者，一切皆為三種欲生，更無增過。若諸沙門或婆羅門墮有求者，多分求樂；由貪樂故，一切皆為三種樂生。由諸世間為不苦不樂寂靜生處起追求者，極為尠少，故此以上不立為生。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: why establish three kinds of desire-birth and three kinds of joy-birth? Answer: because of three kinds of seeking. First, the seeking of desire; second, the seeking of existence; third, the seeking of pure conduct. Any renunciant or brahmin who falls into the seeking of desire falls entirely within these three desire-births, and reaches no further than this. Any renunciant or brahmin who falls into the seeking of existence mostly seeks bliss; because of craving for bliss, all such seeking falls entirely within these three joy-births. Because those in the world who seek out a birthplace of neither pain nor pleasure, utterly still, are extremely few, what lies above this is not established as a "birth" at all.

若諸沙門或婆羅門墮梵行求者，一切皆為求無漏界。或復有一墮邪梵行求者，為求不動空無邊處、識無邊處、無所有處、非想非非想處，起邪分別，謂為解脫，當知此是有上梵行求。無上梵行求者，謂求無漏界。

Any renunciant or brahmin who falls into the seeking of pure conduct seeks, in every case, the uncontaminated realm. Yet there is a further kind who falls into a mistaken seeking of pure conduct: one who seeks the unmoving fourth dhyāna, the sphere of infinite space, the sphere of infinite consciousness, the sphere of nothingness, and the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, giving rise to a mistaken judgment that this is liberation — this should be known as a seeking of pure conduct that still has something above it. The seeking of pure conduct that has nothing above it is precisely the seeking of the uncontaminated realm.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the three seekings as the master key to this entire birth-establishment scheme: the seeking of desire produces exactly the three desire-births already given (up through the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations, with no further tier since desire itself has no subtler grade left to seek); the seeking of existence — his gloss for craving the bliss of meditative absorption itself — produces exactly the three joy-births of the first three dhyānas. He explains the list's stopping point directly: almost no one in saṃsāra actually seeks out the fourth dhyāna's and the formless spheres' neither-painful-nor-pleasant stillness for its own sake, since that stillness offers nothing for craving to fasten onto, so the treatise simply declines to name it a "birth" category at all — not because such beings don't exist, but because they are too rare to warrant classification.

The third seeking, of pure conduct, is where he places the treatise's sharpest edge: it distinguishes those who correctly seek the uncontaminated realm — true nirvāṇa, free of all afflictive seeds — from those who mistake the fourth dhyāna or any of the four formless attainments for liberation itself. He explains this second group as non-Buddhist meditators who, through genuine and often very advanced practice, temporarily suppress the desire realm's afflictions and rest in profound stillness, then wrongly conclude they have attained final liberation; because the seeds of affliction were only suppressed rather than uprooted, that craving for even the most refined meditative bliss eventually reasserts itself, and they fall back into desire-realm rebirth after their allotted formless lifespan — famously illustrated, he notes, by Devadatta's uncle Udraka Rāmaputra, said to have reached the peak of the formless realm only to be reborn afterward as a flying fox. A genuine noble disciple, by contrast, cultivates the very same dhyānas and formless attainments as a path rather than a destination, without ever mistaking the bliss along the way for the goal, and so passes all the way through to actual uncontaminated liberation. This distinction — that even the subtlest craving for meditative bliss is still a form of self-grasping — closes the treatise's account of why three desire-births and three joy-births, no more and no fewer, are established.

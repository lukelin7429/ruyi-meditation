Fascicle 14 · 瑜伽師地論卷第十四 · The Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing, Continued

A Numeric Catalogue of Everything a Buddhist Should Know

Fascicle 14 picks up exactly where fascicle 13 left off, mid-sentence in inner wisdom's fourth aspect: a vast catalogue of Buddhist teaching organized by number, climbing from three things through — eventually — ten, each numbered set naming a distinct facet of the path, from the ten unwholesome courses of action to the subtlest marks of a liberated mind.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 14 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. The Third Numeric Category Opens: The Ten Unwholesome Courses of Action, Analyzed Four Ways

The Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing continues directly from fascicle 13, mid-catalogue: having finished "two things," the treatise now turns to "three things" — a category so vast the treatise organizes it into forty distinct numbered items. The first of these forty takes up the ten classical unwholesome courses of action and analyzes them through four different threefold lenses at once.

Chinese Source

本地分中聞所成地第十之二。已說二種佛教所應知處。次說三種。謂依十相、三門、三種及與三根，於諸有情發起邪行，能令有情墮諸惡趣。言十相者，謂壞生命、財物、妻妾，若壞實義、善友、讚美所為事業，若意三濁。謂執受他財欲為己有，欲令他遭所不愛事，誹謗真實所有惡見。言三門者，一、作業毀壞門，二、意樂毀壞門，三、方便毀壞門。於十相中，前之七種作業毀壞；其次二種意樂毀壞；最後一種方便毀壞，所謂惡見。由惡見故，羞恥、慈悲、離諸惡行悉皆毀壞；無有羞恥、無有慈悲，廣造眾惡。言三種者，一、身所作，二、語所作，三、意所作。言三根者，一、為自饒益相，二、為損害他相，三、於他顛倒相。謂於非法而作法想，於不應作作應作想，堅執現行。

Ru-Yi Translation

The Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing, the tenth, part two, in the Basic Section. The two-thing category of what a Buddhist should know has now been explained. Next, the three-thing category. By way of ten marks, three gateways, three kinds, and three roots, one gives rise to wrongdoing toward sentient beings that causes them to fall into the evil destinies. The ten marks: destroying life, property, or another's spouse; destroying truth, good friendship, or praiseworthy conduct; and the mind's three defilements. Namely: seizing another's wealth, wishing to make it one's own; wishing to bring about what another does not desire; and the wrong view that slanders what is genuinely real. The three gateways: first, the gateway of ruinous action; second, the gateway of ruinous intention; third, the gateway of ruinous means. Among the ten marks, the first seven are ruinous action; the next two are ruinous intention; and the last, wrong view, is ruinous means. Because of wrong view, shame, compassion, and the capacity to abandon unwholesome conduct are all destroyed; lacking shame and lacking compassion, one commits evil extensively. The three kinds: first, what is done by the body; second, what is done by speech; third, what is done by the mind. The three roots: first, the mark of benefiting oneself; second, the mark of harming another; third, the mark of inverted [understanding] toward another. Namely: taking what is not the Dharma to be the Dharma, taking what should not be done to be what should be done, and firmly clinging to and carrying it out.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes this first of forty items simply re-sorts the classical ten unwholesome courses of action — killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying, divisive speech, harsh speech, idle chatter, covetousness, ill will, and wrong view — through four independent threefold grids rather than introducing new content. By gateway, the first seven (all bodily and verbal) ruin through the deed itself; covetousness and ill will ruin through corrupted intention; and wrong view alone ruins through corrupted means, since it destroys the very capacity for shame and compassion that would otherwise check the other nine — a single distorted view, he stresses, is more dangerous than any single bad act, because it removes the internal brakes on everything that follows. By simple kind, the ten sort into bodily, verbal, and mental action. By root, the three mental factors driving all ten reduce to self-benefit (craving), harm to another (hostility), and inverted understanding (delusion — mistaking the forbidden for permissible and then clinging stubbornly to that mistake, his examples being ritual animal sacrifice or the conviction that certain animals exist purely to be eaten). One act of wrongdoing, seen through all four lenses at once, is how this section reads as "three things" four separate ways rather than one.

2. What Leaves the Senses Unguarded, and the Four Seeds Underlying Every Phenomenon

The second of the forty items turns from outward unwholesome action to its inward root: three specific factors that leave a being's six sense-faculties unguarded, breeding desire, hostility, and the wish to harm — followed by a fourfold classification of every karmic seed stored in the mind, wholesome and unwholesome alike.

Chinese Source

復有三法，能令有情不護諸根。一、於依止中邪法種子，二、於諸境界取不正相，三、於私隱處不正思惟。如是三種，當知即是欲貪、瞋恚及與害品。謂於自己利等四種白品法處為欲獲得，或為不失，生欲尋思。於能障彼怨、中二品有情處所，生恚尋思。於親友品有情處所，生害尋思。與此相違所有白品，如應當知。略有四種內法種子，遍攝一切諸法種子。一、世間種子，二、出世種子，三、不清淨種子，四、清淨種子。世間種子者，謂欲、色、無色界繫諸行種子。出世種子者，謂能證三乘及三乘果八聖道等清淨種子。不清淨種子者，謂欲界繫諸行種子。清淨種子復有二種。一、世間淨，二、出世間淨。色無色繫諸行種子，名世間淨；能證三乘及三乘果八聖道等所有種子，名出世淨。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three phenomena that cause a being not to guard the sense-faculties: first, the wrong-dharma seeds within one's own support; second, taking a wrong mark with respect to sense-objects; third, unwholesome reflection in private. These three should be known as desire-craving, hostility, and the class of harmful intent. Namely: with respect to the four bright objects — benefit and the rest — belonging to oneself, wishing to attain them or not to lose them, one gives rise to a thought of desire. Toward beings of the hostile or neutral class who obstruct these, one gives rise to a thought of hostility. Toward beings of the friendly class, one gives rise to a thought of harm. The reverse of this is the corresponding bright side, to be understood accordingly. There are, briefly, four inner-dharma seeds that comprehend the seeds of all phenomena: first, worldly seeds; second, supramundane seeds; third, impure seeds; fourth, pure seeds. Worldly seeds are the seeds of formations bound to the desire, form, and formless realms. Supramundane seeds are the pure seeds capable of realizing the three vehicles and their fruits, the noble eightfold path and the rest. Impure seeds are the seeds of formations bound to the desire realm. Pure seeds are, further, of two kinds: worldly purity and supramundane purity. The seeds of formations bound to the form and formless realms are called worldly purity; the seeds capable of realizing the three vehicles and their fruits, the noble eightfold path and the rest, are called supramundane purity.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the three causes of unguarded senses as a simple chain: a dormant seed for desire, hostility, or the wish to harm sits in the storehouse-consciousness; contact with an object then gets wrongly marked — pleasant, threatening, or worth resenting — which activates that seed; and private, unmonitored reflection lets it multiply through repetition into settled habit. He unpacks the three resulting thoughts through the "eight worldly winds" (gain/loss, praise/blame, fame/disgrace, pleasure/pain): craving arises toward the four favorable ones when they are sought or feared lost; hostility arises toward whoever — enemy or mere stranger — blocks access to them; and, distinctively, only a mild wish to harm (not outright hostility) arises toward someone dear who causes the same obstruction, since natural affection tempers the response into wanting merely to correct rather than destroy — the difference, in his example, between a parent's urge to lightly discipline a disobedient child and genuine hatred toward a stranger.

The four seeds he treats as a complete map of everything the ālaya-vijñāna stores: worldly seeds (bound to any of the three realms) versus supramundane seeds (capable of yielding the three vehicles' path and fruit); and, cutting across that same ground differently, impure seeds (desire-realm only) versus pure seeds, which further split into worldly purity (form- and formless-realm seeds, pure only relative to the coarser desire realm) and genuine supramundane purity (the seeds of the noble eightfold path and its fruit). He notes the commentarial gloss's observation that "eightfold path" here stands in, by its own preeminence, for the entire thirty-seven factors of enlightenment — the most complete of the path factors mentioned in place of the whole set it comprehends.

3. The Three Cravings and the Three Seekings, Named Three Ways

Items three through five all circle back to a single teaching already met in fascicle 13's account of dependent origination: the three cravings that keep beings bound to the three realms, here named and renamed under three slightly different headings — as the cause of suffering, as what actually keeps beings wandering through saṃsāra, and as the root motive behind everything any being ever does.

Chinese Source

復有三種從因所生有漏法因，若於此中不如正理修方便者，能生諸苦；若能如理修方便者，於苦於因，能知能斷。謂於欲界繫法染汙希求，於色無色界繫法亦爾。又有三種諸煩惱趣，令諸有情流轉生死。謂於勝欲發意希求，名初煩惱趣。於色無色界勝自體中發意希求，名第二煩惱趣。於邪解脫發意希求，名第三煩惱趣。又有三種諸有情類欲為根本作業方便。一、為得勝欲，二、為得勝自體，三、為證勝解脫道。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three causes of phenomena with outflow arising from a cause: if one does not cultivate the means correctly with respect to these, suffering results; if one cultivates the means correctly, one is able to know the suffering and able to sever its cause. Namely: defiled craving for phenomena bound to the desire realm, and likewise for phenomena bound to the form and formless realms. There are also three destinies of affliction that cause sentient beings to wander through birth and death. Giving rise to the intention to seek superior desires is the first destiny of affliction. Giving rise to the intention to seek a superior existence within the form or formless realm is the second destiny of affliction. Giving rise to the intention to seek a false liberation is the third destiny of affliction. There are also three means by which desire serves as the root of action for sentient beings: first, to attain superior desires; second, to attain a superior existence; third, to realize the supreme path of liberation.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies all three items as the same underlying teaching — desire for sense-pleasure (欲愛), desire for continued existence (色愛/無色愛, craving a form- or formless-realm existence), and, in its third guise, a corrupted craving for liberation itself, as when non-Buddhist ascetics mistake absorption for nirvāṇa or believe imitating an animal's behavior earns rebirth in heaven — one root confusion producing three different destinations. Named as "seekings" (求) rather than "cravings" (愛) or "destinies of affliction" (煩惱趣), the same three become desire-seeking, existence-seeking, and — when corrected into its genuine form rather than its corrupted counterfeit — the unsurpassed seeking of the pure life itself, the only one of the three actually capable of producing liberation rather than merely another turn of the wheel. He notes the treatise's own diagnosis: suffering results only when these cravings go unexamined; correctly investigated, the very same energy that drives ordinary craving can be redirected toward its cure.

4. Three Powers That Draw a Being Into Rebirth, and Three Kinds of Ignorance Across the Three Times

The sixth item names three successive powers by which karma actually delivers a being into a new existence — including a detailed account of the intermediate-state being between death and rebirth — while the seventh traces how ignorance operating across past, present, and future keeps generating one life after another.

Chinese Source

又有三種諸有情類於三界中攝受自體諸行威勢。一、牽引威勢，二、能得威勢，三、成滿威勢。牽引威勢者，謂能引之業。能得威勢者，謂健達縛正現在前。成滿威勢者，謂住於此受淨不淨諸業異熟。又有三種無明蘊。諸有情類住無明者，由此因緣，能生三世自體差別。謂於過去世前際等無知，能生現在自體；於現在世前際等無知，能生未來自體；於未來世前際等無知，即於未來能生後後當來自體。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three powers by which sentient beings, within the three realms, take up the formations of their own existence: first, the power of drawing forth; second, the power of attaining; third, the power of bringing to completion. The power of drawing forth is the karma that draws one on. The power of attaining is the intermediate-state being actually present. The power of bringing to completion is dwelling within this existence, experiencing the ripened result of pure and impure karma. There are also three aggregates of ignorance. Sentient beings who dwell in ignorance, by this cause, generate the distinct existences of the three times. Namely: ignorance of the prior limit of the past generates the existence of the present; ignorance of the prior limit of the present generates the existence of the future; and ignorance of the prior limit of the future generates, within the future itself, existence after existence to come.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through rebirth's three powers in strict sequence: drawing-forth power is simply the karma itself — the "projecting karma" that determines which of the five destinies a being's next life falls into, as distinct from the subtler "completing karma" that fills in that life's particular details (beauty, status, intelligence) once the destination is set. Attaining power is the intermediate-state being (健達縛, gandharva, literally "scent-eater," since this subtle form is said to be sustained by fragrance alone) — a mind-made body invisible to ordinary sight, possessed of considerable but time-limited power, that actually carries the drawing-forth karma into a new womb or birthplace, its own duration classically capped at forty-nine days. And completing power is simply dwelling in and experiencing the resulting life's pure and impure karmic ripening once rebirth is complete — the three together accounting for the entire mechanism connecting one death to the next birth, without gap or exception.

The three aggregates of ignorance he reads as showing how ignorance never stays confined to a single moment: not knowing the prior limit of the past (how one's present existence actually arose) generates further ignorance-driven existence now; not knowing the prior limit of the present generates the same pattern into the future; and that future ignorance, in turn, generates existence after existence beyond it — a self-perpetuating chain in which each unexamined moment plants the ignorance that produces the next, precisely the same structural insight already drawn from dependent origination in fascicle 13's account of the twelve links.

A Numeric Catalogue of Everything a Buddhist Should Know

Fascicle 14 picks up exactly where fascicle 13 left off, mid-sentence in inner wisdom's fourth aspect: a vast catalogue of Buddhist teaching organized by number, climbing from three things through — eventually — ten, each numbered set naming a distinct facet of the path, from the ten unwholesome courses of action to the subtlest marks of a liberated mind.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 14 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. The Third Numeric Category Opens: The Ten Unwholesome Courses of Action, Analyzed Four Ways

The Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing continues directly from fascicle 13, mid-catalogue: having finished "two things," the treatise now turns to "three things" — a category so vast the treatise organizes it into forty distinct numbered items. The first of these forty takes up the ten classical unwholesome courses of action and analyzes them through four different threefold lenses at once.

Chinese Source

本地分中聞所成地第十之二。已說二種佛教所應知處。次說三種。謂依十相、三門、三種及與三根，於諸有情發起邪行，能令有情墮諸惡趣。言十相者，謂壞生命、財物、妻妾，若壞實義、善友、讚美所為事業，若意三濁。謂執受他財欲為己有，欲令他遭所不愛事，誹謗真實所有惡見。言三門者，一、作業毀壞門，二、意樂毀壞門，三、方便毀壞門。於十相中，前之七種作業毀壞；其次二種意樂毀壞；最後一種方便毀壞，所謂惡見。由惡見故，羞恥、慈悲、離諸惡行悉皆毀壞；無有羞恥、無有慈悲，廣造眾惡。言三種者，一、身所作，二、語所作，三、意所作。言三根者，一、為自饒益相，二、為損害他相，三、於他顛倒相。謂於非法而作法想，於不應作作應作想，堅執現行。

Ru-Yi Translation

The Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing, the tenth, part two, in the Basic Section. The two-thing category of what a Buddhist should know has now been explained. Next, the three-thing category. By way of ten marks, three gateways, three kinds, and three roots, one gives rise to wrongdoing toward sentient beings that causes them to fall into the evil destinies. The ten marks: destroying life, property, or another's spouse; destroying truth, good friendship, or praiseworthy conduct; and the mind's three defilements. Namely: seizing another's wealth, wishing to make it one's own; wishing to bring about what another does not desire; and the wrong view that slanders what is genuinely real. The three gateways: first, the gateway of ruinous action; second, the gateway of ruinous intention; third, the gateway of ruinous means. Among the ten marks, the first seven are ruinous action; the next two are ruinous intention; and the last, wrong view, is ruinous means. Because of wrong view, shame, compassion, and the capacity to abandon unwholesome conduct are all destroyed; lacking shame and lacking compassion, one commits evil extensively. The three kinds: first, what is done by the body; second, what is done by speech; third, what is done by the mind. The three roots: first, the mark of benefiting oneself; second, the mark of harming another; third, the mark of inverted [understanding] toward another. Namely: taking what is not the Dharma to be the Dharma, taking what should not be done to be what should be done, and firmly clinging to and carrying it out.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes this first of forty items simply re-sorts the classical ten unwholesome courses of action — killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying, divisive speech, harsh speech, idle chatter, covetousness, ill will, and wrong view — through four independent threefold grids rather than introducing new content. By gateway, the first seven (all bodily and verbal) ruin through the deed itself; covetousness and ill will ruin through corrupted intention; and wrong view alone ruins through corrupted means, since it destroys the very capacity for shame and compassion that would otherwise check the other nine — a single distorted view, he stresses, is more dangerous than any single bad act, because it removes the internal brakes on everything that follows. By simple kind, the ten sort into bodily, verbal, and mental action. By root, the three mental factors driving all ten reduce to self-benefit (craving), harm to another (hostility), and inverted understanding (delusion — mistaking the forbidden for permissible and then clinging stubbornly to that mistake, his examples being ritual animal sacrifice or the conviction that certain animals exist purely to be eaten). One act of wrongdoing, seen through all four lenses at once, is how this section reads as "three things" four separate ways rather than one.

2. What Leaves the Senses Unguarded, and the Four Seeds Underlying Every Phenomenon

The second of the forty items turns from outward unwholesome action to its inward root: three specific factors that leave a being's six sense-faculties unguarded, breeding desire, hostility, and the wish to harm — followed by a fourfold classification of every karmic seed stored in the mind, wholesome and unwholesome alike.

Chinese Source

復有三法，能令有情不護諸根。一、於依止中邪法種子，二、於諸境界取不正相，三、於私隱處不正思惟。如是三種，當知即是欲貪、瞋恚及與害品。謂於自己利等四種白品法處為欲獲得，或為不失，生欲尋思。於能障彼怨、中二品有情處所，生恚尋思。於親友品有情處所，生害尋思。與此相違所有白品，如應當知。略有四種內法種子，遍攝一切諸法種子。一、世間種子，二、出世種子，三、不清淨種子，四、清淨種子。世間種子者，謂欲、色、無色界繫諸行種子。出世種子者，謂能證三乘及三乘果八聖道等清淨種子。不清淨種子者，謂欲界繫諸行種子。清淨種子復有二種。一、世間淨，二、出世間淨。色無色繫諸行種子，名世間淨；能證三乘及三乘果八聖道等所有種子，名出世淨。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three phenomena that cause a being not to guard the sense-faculties: first, the wrong-dharma seeds within one's own support; second, taking a wrong mark with respect to sense-objects; third, unwholesome reflection in private. These three should be known as desire-craving, hostility, and the class of harmful intent. Namely: with respect to the four bright objects — benefit and the rest — belonging to oneself, wishing to attain them or not to lose them, one gives rise to a thought of desire. Toward beings of the hostile or neutral class who obstruct these, one gives rise to a thought of hostility. Toward beings of the friendly class, one gives rise to a thought of harm. The reverse of this is the corresponding bright side, to be understood accordingly. There are, briefly, four inner-dharma seeds that comprehend the seeds of all phenomena: first, worldly seeds; second, supramundane seeds; third, impure seeds; fourth, pure seeds. Worldly seeds are the seeds of formations bound to the desire, form, and formless realms. Supramundane seeds are the pure seeds capable of realizing the three vehicles and their fruits, the noble eightfold path and the rest. Impure seeds are the seeds of formations bound to the desire realm. Pure seeds are, further, of two kinds: worldly purity and supramundane purity. The seeds of formations bound to the form and formless realms are called worldly purity; the seeds capable of realizing the three vehicles and their fruits, the noble eightfold path and the rest, are called supramundane purity.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the three causes of unguarded senses as a simple chain: a dormant seed for desire, hostility, or the wish to harm sits in the storehouse-consciousness; contact with an object then gets wrongly marked — pleasant, threatening, or worth resenting — which activates that seed; and private, unmonitored reflection lets it multiply through repetition into settled habit. He unpacks the three resulting thoughts through the "eight worldly winds" (gain/loss, praise/blame, fame/disgrace, pleasure/pain): craving arises toward the four favorable ones when they are sought or feared lost; hostility arises toward whoever — enemy or mere stranger — blocks access to them; and, distinctively, only a mild wish to harm (not outright hostility) arises toward someone dear who causes the same obstruction, since natural affection tempers the response into wanting merely to correct rather than destroy — the difference, in his example, between a parent's urge to lightly discipline a disobedient child and genuine hatred toward a stranger.

The four seeds he treats as a complete map of everything the ālaya-vijñāna stores: worldly seeds (bound to any of the three realms) versus supramundane seeds (capable of yielding the three vehicles' path and fruit); and, cutting across that same ground differently, impure seeds (desire-realm only) versus pure seeds, which further split into worldly purity (form- and formless-realm seeds, pure only relative to the coarser desire realm) and genuine supramundane purity (the seeds of the noble eightfold path and its fruit). He notes the commentarial gloss's observation that "eightfold path" here stands in, by its own preeminence, for the entire thirty-seven factors of enlightenment — the most complete of the path factors mentioned in place of the whole set it comprehends.

3. The Three Cravings and the Three Seekings, Named Three Ways

Items three through five all circle back to a single teaching already met in fascicle 13's account of dependent origination: the three cravings that keep beings bound to the three realms, here named and renamed under three slightly different headings — as the cause of suffering, as what actually keeps beings wandering through saṃsāra, and as the root motive behind everything any being ever does.

Chinese Source

復有三種從因所生有漏法因，若於此中不如正理修方便者，能生諸苦；若能如理修方便者，於苦於因，能知能斷。謂於欲界繫法染汙希求，於色無色界繫法亦爾。又有三種諸煩惱趣，令諸有情流轉生死。謂於勝欲發意希求，名初煩惱趣。於色無色界勝自體中發意希求，名第二煩惱趣。於邪解脫發意希求，名第三煩惱趣。又有三種諸有情類欲為根本作業方便。一、為得勝欲，二、為得勝自體，三、為證勝解脫道。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three causes of phenomena with outflow arising from a cause: if one does not cultivate the means correctly with respect to these, suffering results; if one cultivates the means correctly, one is able to know the suffering and able to sever its cause. Namely: defiled craving for phenomena bound to the desire realm, and likewise for phenomena bound to the form and formless realms. There are also three destinies of affliction that cause sentient beings to wander through birth and death. Giving rise to the intention to seek superior desires is the first destiny of affliction. Giving rise to the intention to seek a superior existence within the form or formless realm is the second destiny of affliction. Giving rise to the intention to seek a false liberation is the third destiny of affliction. There are also three means by which desire serves as the root of action for sentient beings: first, to attain superior desires; second, to attain a superior existence; third, to realize the supreme path of liberation.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies all three items as the same underlying teaching — desire for sense-pleasure (欲愛), desire for continued existence (色愛/無色愛, craving a form- or formless-realm existence), and, in its third guise, a corrupted craving for liberation itself, as when non-Buddhist ascetics mistake absorption for nirvāṇa or believe imitating an animal's behavior earns rebirth in heaven — one root confusion producing three different destinations. Named as "seekings" (求) rather than "cravings" (愛) or "destinies of affliction" (煩惱趣), the same three become desire-seeking, existence-seeking, and — when corrected into its genuine form rather than its corrupted counterfeit — the unsurpassed seeking of the pure life itself, the only one of the three actually capable of producing liberation rather than merely another turn of the wheel. He notes the treatise's own diagnosis: suffering results only when these cravings go unexamined; correctly investigated, the very same energy that drives ordinary craving can be redirected toward its cure.

4. Three Powers That Draw a Being Into Rebirth, and Three Kinds of Ignorance Across the Three Times

The sixth item names three successive powers by which karma actually delivers a being into a new existence — including a detailed account of the intermediate-state being between death and rebirth — while the seventh traces how ignorance operating across past, present, and future keeps generating one life after another.

Chinese Source

又有三種諸有情類於三界中攝受自體諸行威勢。一、牽引威勢，二、能得威勢，三、成滿威勢。牽引威勢者，謂能引之業。能得威勢者，謂健達縛正現在前。成滿威勢者，謂住於此受淨不淨諸業異熟。又有三種無明蘊。諸有情類住無明者，由此因緣，能生三世自體差別。謂於過去世前際等無知，能生現在自體；於現在世前際等無知，能生未來自體；於未來世前際等無知，即於未來能生後後當來自體。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three powers by which sentient beings, within the three realms, take up the formations of their own existence: first, the power of drawing forth; second, the power of attaining; third, the power of bringing to completion. The power of drawing forth is the karma that draws one on. The power of attaining is the intermediate-state being actually present. The power of bringing to completion is dwelling within this existence, experiencing the ripened result of pure and impure karma. There are also three aggregates of ignorance. Sentient beings who dwell in ignorance, by this cause, generate the distinct existences of the three times. Namely: ignorance of the prior limit of the past generates the existence of the present; ignorance of the prior limit of the present generates the existence of the future; and ignorance of the prior limit of the future generates, within the future itself, existence after existence to come.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through rebirth's three powers in strict sequence: drawing-forth power is simply the karma itself — the "projecting karma" that determines which of the five destinies a being's next life falls into, as distinct from the subtler "completing karma" that fills in that life's particular details (beauty, status, intelligence) once the destination is set. Attaining power is the intermediate-state being (健達縛, gandharva, literally "scent-eater," since this subtle form is said to be sustained by fragrance alone) — a mind-made body invisible to ordinary sight, possessed of considerable but time-limited power, that actually carries the drawing-forth karma into a new womb or birthplace, its own duration classically capped at forty-nine days. And completing power is simply dwelling in and experiencing the resulting life's pure and impure karmic ripening once rebirth is complete — the three together accounting for the entire mechanism connecting one death to the next birth, without gap or exception.

The three aggregates of ignorance he reads as showing how ignorance never stays confined to a single moment: not knowing the prior limit of the past (how one's present existence actually arose) generates further ignorance-driven existence now; not knowing the prior limit of the present generates the same pattern into the future; and that future ignorance, in turn, generates existence after existence beyond it — a self-perpetuating chain in which each unexamined moment plants the ignorance that produces the next, precisely the same structural insight already drawn from dependent origination in fascicle 13's account of the twelve links.

5. Three Vanities Even a Trainee Must Still Overcome, Three Places Where Craving Takes Hold, and the Three Basic Sufferings

Three more items follow in quick succession: the three specific vanities that even a noble one still short of arhatship shares with ordinary beings and must urgently overcome, the three feeling-states in which greed, hatred, and delusion each take root, and a compact threefold reduction of every kind of suffering a being can experience.

Chinese Source

又有三種未究竟聖，共諸異生生死災患。若有於彼深厭怖者，當速斷除三種憍逸，修習現法涅槃方便。一、無病衰退，二、少年衰退，三、壽命衰退。其有智者，應觀未來如是三事定當隨逐。又有三種有情之類，貪瞋癡縛所依處所身分差別，能急繫縛諸有情類，閉在大苦生死牢獄。一、能饒益，二、能損害，三者、平等二種俱離。又有三處所生諸苦，遍攝有情所有眾苦。一、合會所生苦，二、乖離所生苦，三、平等相續苦。初由損害位和合故；第二由饒益位變壞故；第三於一切位相續而轉，麤重所攝，諸行所生，唯眾賢聖覺之為苦，非諸異生。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three [vanities of a] noble one not yet ultimate, shared with ordinary beings' calamities of birth and death. One who is deeply weary and fearful of these should swiftly sever three kinds of vanity and cultivate the means to nirvāṇa in this very life. First, the decline of freedom from illness; second, the decline of youth; third, the decline of life itself. One who is wise should contemplate that these three things will, in the future, certainly follow. There are also three distinct bodily states, the bases on which the bonds of greed, hatred, and delusion depend, that tightly bind sentient beings and confine them in the great prison of suffering birth-and-death: first, what benefits; second, what harms; third, what is equally free of both. There are also three sources from which all suffering arises, comprehending the entirety of what sentient beings suffer: first, suffering born of union; second, suffering born of separation; third, suffering of even, continuous succession. The first is through the union of a harmful state; the second, through the ruin of a beneficial state; the third turns on continuously through every state, comprised of coarse weight, arisen from formations themselves — perceived as suffering only by the various noble ones, not by ordinary beings.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the three vanities are drawn from a longer classical list of seven (adding pride in noble birth, physical vigor, wealth, and learning), narrowed here to the three every being, however advanced short of full arhatship, remains vulnerable to: pride in present freedom from illness, in present youth, and in present longevity — each a form of intoxicated self-satisfaction that breeds carelessness. His counsel is blunt: since aging, sickness, and death will certainly overtake anyone still short of full liberation, a wise practitioner contemplates this future certainty now, precisely to dissolve the vanity before circumstance forces the lesson.

The three feeling-states he reads as showing exactly where each root affliction gets its foothold: pleasant feeling breeds greed (since beings instinctively cling to what feels good), painful feeling breeds hatred (since beings instinctively resent what hurts), and neutral feeling — neither obviously pleasant nor painful — breeds delusion specifically, because its very blandness lets the four inversions slip in unnoticed, unlike the other two feelings whose sharper character makes the underlying affliction easier to catch. The three sufferings he maps onto the classical threefold distinction (苦苦/壞苦/行苦): suffering-of-suffering (ordinary pain, uniting with what harms), suffering-of-change (pleasure's own inevitable collapse, separating from what benefits), and suffering-of-formations — the subtlest of the three, the mere fact of being a conditioned, karmically-loaded process even when no obvious pleasure or pain is present — visible only to noble ones whose insight can detect affliction's dormant weight beneath an outwardly neutral moment, invisible to anyone without that trained perception.

6. Three Kinds of Conceit That Block Stream-Entry, and the Three Seeds Spanning Past, Present, and Future

Two more items follow: three specific forms of self-comparison that obstruct even the very first fruit of the noble path, and a simple threefold classification of every karmic seed by whether it has already ripened, not yet ripened, or is ripening right now.

Chinese Source

又有三種心高舉法，違害欲求沙門果證修方便者預流果支，能障沙門令不得證。一者、以己校量於他，謂我為勝，心生高舉；二者、以己校量於他，謂我相似，心生高舉；三者、以己校量於他，謂我為劣，心生高舉。復有三種種子，當知能生一切諸行。一、已與果，二、未與果，三、果正現前。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three ways the mind rises up in conceit, which injure the very supports of stream-entry for one who seeks and cultivates the means toward the fruits of the ascetic path, and obstruct that path's realization: first, comparing oneself to another and thinking "I am superior," the mind rises up; second, comparing oneself to another and thinking "I am equal," the mind rises up; third, comparing oneself to another and thinking "I am inferior," the mind rises up. There are also three kinds of seed, which should be known to give rise to all conditioned formations: first, that which has already yielded its fruit; second, that which has not yet yielded its fruit; third, that whose fruit is presently manifesting.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes all three forms of conceit — thinking oneself superior, equal, or even just slightly inferior to another — obstruct the same underlying capacity: the willingness to genuinely receive instruction. Even the third, seemingly modest form still carries enough residual self-regard to prevent full, undistorted listening and reflection, since a mind still measuring the gap, however small, has not yet fully set comparison aside. Because the four supports of stream-entry (approaching a genuine teacher, hearing the true teaching, correct reflection, and practicing in accordance with the Dharma) all depend on this same receptive humility, any of the three conceits — not merely the crudest — is enough to block progress.

The three seeds he treats as simply naming a seed's three possible temporal relations to its own fruit: already having produced its result (past), not yet having done so (future), and presently ripening into visible result (present) — the same three-fold division of past, present, and future already applied to karmic seeds more generally back in fascicle 13's account of the aggregates, here stated as the general principle by which literally every conditioned phenomenon, without exception, comes to be.

7. The Three Times as the Basis of All Speech, and Matter Analyzed Three Ways — Including the Rare Form Only Deep Meditation Can Perceive

Item thirteen states plainly that all speech, without exception, concerns only the past, future, or present. Item fourteen then turns to matter itself, analyzing every physical phenomenon through three characteristics — and closing with an unusual category of subtle matter visible only to a mind in deep meditative concentration.

Chinese Source

又有三種諸行言說所依處。所謂去來今。又有三相，能攝一切色法自相。謂顯、形、作用，安立眼識所取之色。於自處所，障礙餘色行住，安立根色，若一切境界色；當知一切總有十色。及定地色。若得淨定，為引變化修方便者所有諸色，當知是內化心境界，亦是未滿變化心果。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three bases on which speech concerning conditioned formations depends: namely, the past, future, and present. There are also three characteristics that comprehend the particular nature of all matter: color, shape, and function, by which the matter grasped by eye-consciousness is established. Whatever obstructs, in its own location, the movement and abiding of other matter establishes the matter of the faculties and the matter of every object; know that, altogether, there are ten [such] forms of matter. And there is matter belonging to the meditative realm. Matter possessed by one who, having attained pure concentration, cultivates the means to elicit transformation, should be known as the domain of an inwardly transforming mind, and also as the not-yet-complete fruit of a transforming mind.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes item thirteen simply states the obvious once seen clearly: every utterance concerns what has already ceased, what has not yet arisen, or what is presently arising and not yet passed — and because all such talk is, strictly, discursive proliferation (already defined at fascicle 13's §51), naming this threefold basis exposes exactly how limited ordinary speech's actual scope is.

He reads the fourteen-fold matter classification as building from the visible up to the exceptional. Color (顯色: blue, yellow, light, shadow, cloud, mist), shape (形色: long, short, round, square), and function (作用/表色: reaching, withdrawing, bending, stretching — the body's gestures reflecting inner intention) together account for everything the eye can actually see. A second criterion — obstructing other matter from occupying the same space — establishes the five sense-faculties and their five corresponding objects, ten forms of matter in total. But he gives real attention to the final category: matter belonging to the meditative realm (定地色, also called mind-generated matter of the dharma-sphere), invisible to ordinary sight and non-obstructive, perceptible only to a mind possessed of the great meditative power of at least the first dhyāna and above — the same subtle matter, he notes, that underlies a genuine practitioner's power of miraculous transformation, still incomplete and merely internal to the meditating mind until that power reaches full mastery. This closes inner wisdom's excursion into what matter itself, seen at every level from the crudest to the most rarefied, actually consists of.

8. Three Minds Still Trailed by Affliction, Three Grades of How Students Retain Teaching, and Three Doorways Into Seeing Impermanence

Three more items follow: a threefold ranking of minds by how much affliction still clings to them (from ordinary beings up to the very edge of arhatship), a threefold ranking of how well a listener actually retains what they hear, and three concrete meditative doorways — breath, discursive thought, and mental intention — through which impermanence itself first becomes visible.

Chinese Source

又有三種為諸煩惱所隨逐心。一、諸異生心，二、未滿學心，三、已滿學心。又有三種聽聞法者。一、於法於義不能受持；二、唯能領受，不能任持；三、能受能持。又有三法，是修行者觀身語意無常性觀趣入上首。一者、入出息，二者、尋伺，三者、想思。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three minds still trailed by the afflictions: first, the minds of ordinary beings; second, the mind of the trainee not yet complete; third, the mind of the trainee just complete. There are also three kinds of one who hears the teaching: first, one who can retain neither the words nor their meaning; second, one who can only receive them, but cannot sustain them; third, one who can both receive and sustain them. There are also three phenomena that lead the practitioner's contemplation of the impermanent nature of body, speech, and mind: first, the in-and-out breath; second, applied and sustained thought; third, perception and intention.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the three affliction-trailed minds as a steep gradient rather than an even scale: ordinary beings carry the full weight of unrestrained affliction; a trainee anywhere from stream-entry up through the very threshold of arhatship (the "not-yet-complete" trainee) carries progressively less; and even the "just-complete" trainee — in the single instant of the vajra-like concentration first met in fascicle 12 — still carries the very last, subtlest trace of innate self-grasping, not yet severed until that instant passes into full arhatship. The three listeners he maps directly onto dull, middling, and sharp faculties: the dull neither believe nor retain what they hear; the middling receive and believe it but cannot actually apply it; and only the sharp both take it in and put it to work — a caution that hearing correctly (§41's foundation for the entire Ground of Hearing) is itself already a skill unevenly distributed.

The three doorways into impermanence he reads as covering the entirety of what a person actually does: the breath (bodily activity, observed through mindfulness of breathing — its constant change in length, temperature, and rhythm exposing the body's own impermanence); discursive thought (verbal activity, since every utterance is preceded by some coarser or finer thinking that impermanence pervades just as thoroughly); and perception paired with intention (mental activity, the momentary rising and falling of thought itself). He adds a practical aside: without genuinely absorbing this same impermanence at the level of the teaching itself — not just the body — a student clings rigidly to one fixed form of the Dharma exactly as milk insists on staying milk, never becoming curd or clarified butter, and so never reaching its own finest expression.

9. Three Causes That Fix a Being's Destiny, How the Community Flags Wrongdoing in Good Faith, and Why a Buddha Alone Needs No Guarding

Three more items: the causes that sort every being into a fixed destiny of ruin, a fixed destiny of liberation, or an unsettled destiny still undetermined; the threefold basis on which fellow practitioners rightly raise a concern about another's conduct; and why a fully awakened buddha's body, speech, and mind require no vigilant guarding at all, unlike everyone else's.

Chinese Source

又有三種住定不定因。二是定因，一不定因。一、惡趣定因，謂無間業。二、善趣涅槃定因，謂無漏有為法。三、不定因，謂所餘法。又有三法，為令聖教得久住故，展轉舉罪。一者、現見身語現行違犯學處；二、從他聞；三、以餘相比度了知。又諸如來自說具足三不護德。為顯外道諸師內懷眾惡，自稱一切智者，實非一切智者。又欲令彼於如來所發起真實一切智信。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three causes of fixed and unfixed destiny: two are causes of fixation, one is a cause of non-fixation. First, the cause fixing one to the evil destinies: the acts of immediate retribution. Second, the cause fixing one to a favorable destiny and nirvāṇa: the uncontaminated conditioned dharmas. Third, the cause of non-fixation: the remaining phenomena. There are also three things by which, so that the sacred teaching may long endure, wrongdoing is raised from one to another: first, directly witnessing bodily or verbal conduct that violates the precepts; second, hearing it from another; third, inferring it through other signs. The Tathāgatas themselves declare that they possess the three unguarded virtues in full. This is to show that the various non-Buddhist teachers, harboring much evil within, falsely call themselves omniscient though they are not. And it is also to give rise, in those who hear it, to genuine faith in the Tathāgata's true omniscience.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the three destinies as sorting every being by what actually determines their trajectory: committing any of the five acts of immediate retribution (killing a parent or an arhat, wounding a buddha, causing schism in the community) fixes one irrevocably toward the evil destinies; possessing the uncontaminated qualities of a trainee or arhat fixes one irrevocably toward a favorable rebirth or nirvāṇa itself; and everything else — the ordinary condition of most beings, carrying the seeds of affliction but having committed no act severe enough to force either extreme — remains genuinely undetermined, free to move either way depending on future conduct. He treats the three grounds for raising a concern about a fellow practitioner (direct witness, credible report, or reasonable inference) as requiring, per the treatise's fuller account elsewhere, that the accusation always be delivered gently, compassionately, at the right moment, truthfully, and for the other's actual benefit — never merely to embarrass or punish.

The three unguarded virtues he reads as the sharpest possible line between a buddha and everyone else, including arhats: an arhat's three actions are genuinely pure but still require sustained vigilance to keep them that way, since subtle habitual traces of past affliction can otherwise resurface; a buddha's actions are so completely and inherently pure that no such vigilance is needed at all — nothing remains that could ever need concealing or correcting. The Buddha states this about himself for two reasons at once: to expose non-Buddhist teachers who claim omniscience while still harboring inner faults their own unguarded conduct would immediately reveal, and to give listeners genuine grounds — not mere assertion — for trusting that his own omniscience is real.

10. The Three Fires That Burn the Mind, and the Three Genuine "Fires" Worth Honoring Instead

Two more items, linked by a single image: the three afflictions that burn and torment ordinary beings from within, and — addressed specifically to fire-worshipping ascetics of the Buddha's own day — three things genuinely worth honoring as "fire," offered as a corrective to the literal flame such ascetics tended instead.

Chinese Source

復有三種邪執所生大火所起有情燒惱。一、貪愛燒惱，二、愁憂燒惱，三、顛倒燒惱。又有三火，為化樂福邪事外火勝解有情，示無虛誑所應事火；雖實非火，假立火名。一者、父母，二者、妻子，三者、真實應供福田。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three great fires, born of wrong grasping, by which sentient beings are burned and tormented: first, the burning torment of craving; second, the burning torment of grief; third, the burning torment of delusion. There are also three fires, taught in order to transform those beings who delight in merit but wrongly hold to tending an external fire, showing the genuine, non-deceptive objects that should be tended as fire: though not literally fire, the name "fire" is provisionally given to them. First, one's parents; second, one's spouse and children; third, the genuine field of merit worthy of offerings.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the three burning torments simply as greed, hatred, and delusion under a vivid new image — affliction as literal fire, consuming whoever harbors it from the inside out, echoing a passage he cites from the Ratnakūṭa in which a bodhisattva regards all beings as perpetually ill, feverish with these same three heats, and practices diligence precisely as applying the cooling medicine of the true Dharma to relieve them.

The second item he places in its historical setting: ascetics who worshipped fire literally, offering grain and butter into flames in the belief that the fire-gods would return the favor as future merit, were a significant presence in the Buddha's own India — the Buddha's own conversion of the fire-worshipping Kāśyapa brothers at the Nairañjanā River being the most famous encounter. Rather than simply condemning the practice, the Buddha redirects its underlying good intention: genuine merit is tended not in literal flame but in three real relationships — one's parents, without whom one would not exist at all; one's spouse and family, partners in building a household together; and the three jewels, the one field of merit whose worth is genuinely, not merely ritually, supreme. Cháng Bǎi reads this as characteristic of the Buddha's teaching method throughout — not simply telling seekers of merit they are wrong, but showing them where the merit they already rightly seek can actually be found.

11. The Three Genuine Grounds of a Favorable Rebirth, and Pleasure Graded Across the Desire and Form Realms

Three more items chart pleasure and merit across increasingly refined territory: the three genuinely reliable causes of a favorable rebirth, the graded ease with which desire-realm beings enjoy their pleasures depending on which heaven they occupy, and the three qualitatively distinct joys of the first three form-realm dhyānas.

Chinese Source

又有三種，為諸樂欲增上生者，所說真實增上生道。一者、布施，得大財富；二者、持戒，得往善趣；三者、修定，遠離苦受，得生一向無有惱害樂世界中。又有三種諸受欲者劣、中、勝欲，觀待諸欲所生樂故。一、多用功力依緣諸欲，謂現前住所有諸欲。二、少用功力依心諸欲，謂樂化天所有諸欲。三、極少功力依心諸欲，謂他化天所有諸欲。又有三種超過諸欲劣、中、勝樂。一、有尋伺喜，二、無尋伺喜，三、離喜之樂。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three genuine paths to a favorable rebirth, taught for those who delight in and wish for such a rebirth: first, giving, which yields great wealth; second, upholding precepts, which yields rebirth in a favorable destiny; third, cultivating concentration, which frees one from painful feeling and yields rebirth in a world of exclusively untroubled pleasure. There are also three grades — inferior, middling, and superior — of desire among those who enjoy the desires, relative to the pleasure those desires produce: first, desires depending on much exertion and external conditions, namely the desires possessed by those presently dwelling [in the lower heavens]; second, desires depending on the mind with little exertion, namely the desires possessed by the Heaven of Delighting in Transformation; third, desires depending on the mind with the least exertion, namely the desires possessed by the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations. There are also three grades — inferior, middling, and superior — of pleasure that surpass the desires: first, joy accompanied by applied and sustained thought; second, joy free of applied and sustained thought; third, pleasure free even of joy.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the first triad as the classical "three grounds of merit" (giving, precepts, cultivation), cross-referencing the *Abhidharma-saṃgītiparyāya*'s exhaustive fourfold breakdown of each (the act itself, the accompanying restraint, the volition driving it, and its concrete basis) but summarizing simply: giving reliably yields wealth, precepts reliably yield a favorable rebirth, and meditative cultivation reliably yields freedom from painful feeling — the Buddha's own verse that the wise, relying on the Dharma, diligently train in these three and thereby reap a world genuinely free of the desire realm's characteristic suffering.

The second and third items trace pleasure's increasing refinement outward from ordinary effort toward increasingly effortless enjoyment. Among desire-realm beings, those in the lower heavens must exert real effort and depend on external conditions to enjoy anything at all; the Heaven of Delighting in Transformation need only wish and their pleasures appear; and the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations, highest of the six desire heavens, enjoys pleasures either self-generated or borrowed effortlessly from others' creations — refinement measured by how little exertion pleasure now requires. The three form-realm joys chart the same refinement one register higher: the first dhyāna's joy still depends on applied and sustained thought (the coarser mental activity that first disengages from desire); the second dhyāna's joy arises once that thinking itself drops away, born purely of concentration; and the third dhyāna abandons joy's characteristic excitement altogether for a pure, all-pervading pleasure — traditionally named, Cháng Bǎi notes, as the single most refined pleasure found anywhere within the three realms.

12. Three Ways Understanding Actually Dawns, and Three Grades of Bondage from Ordinary Being to Arhat

Two more items: the three successive means by which genuine understanding of one's own body and mind actually develops, common to all three vehicles, and a simple threefold ranking of how thoroughly affliction still binds a being — from the ordinary person bound on every side to the arhat bound not at all.

Chinese Source

又有三種覺悟所知，能令三乘出離眾苦。一、從他聞音種類，二、內正思惟種類，三、長時修習止觀種類。又有三種覺悟所知。一者、具縛，二、不具縛，三、全無縛。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three kinds of awakening to what is to be known, capable of freeing the three vehicles from the multitude of sufferings: first, the kind that comes from hearing another's voice; second, the kind that comes from correct inner reflection; third, the kind that comes from long cultivation of calm and insight. There are also three [degrees of] awakening to what is to be known: first, fully bound; second, not fully bound; third, entirely unbound.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the first triad simply as hearing-wisdom, reflection-wisdom, and cultivation-wisdom — the three-stage architecture the entire Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing has been building toward since §41, restated here as the common path shared by śrāvaka, pratyekabuddha, and bodhisattva alike, none of whom can bypass any of the three stages regardless of which fruit they ultimately seek. The second triad then sorts practitioners by how far that same threefold wisdom has actually progressed: an ordinary being, however much hearing-wisdom or reflection-wisdom (or even a merely worldly cultivation-wisdom) they possess, remains fully bound by affliction in every respect; a trainee from stream-entry through the threshold of arhatship has severed some affliction but not all, remaining not fully bound; and only an arhat, whose hearing, reflection, and cultivation wisdom are all fully consummated, is entirely unbound — the same threefold wisdom, at three different stages of completion, accounting for the entire distance between an ordinary being and full liberation.

13. Three Milestones on the Path, the Three Eyes That See Reality, and Three Ways to Defuse Affliction Before It Manifests

Three more items follow: the three great milestones — severing what is cut by seeing, severing what is cut by cultivation, and abiding in the ease of full realization — that mark real progress along the path; the three distinct "eyes" by which a being can perceive reality at increasingly subtle depths; and three concrete practices that starve affliction of the conditions it needs to actually arise.

Chinese Source

又有三種所應作事，修觀行者由此三事增上力故，修習信等一切善法。一者、永斷見道所斷諸煩惱已，證預流果；二者、永斷修道所斷諸煩惱已，漸次證得一來、不還、阿羅漢果；三者、證得阿羅漢已，現法樂住。又由三分照了一切所知境界增上力故，建立三眼。一者、肉眼，能照顯露無有障礙有見諸色；二者、天眼，能照顯露、不顯露、有障、無障有見諸色；三者、慧眼，照一切種若色非色所有諸法。又有三法，能害現行煩惱怨敵。一者、信順善友；二者、不與在家出家諸眾雜住；三者、內正作意，覺悟所知真實道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three things to be accomplished, by whose power one who cultivates contemplation cultivates faith and all other wholesome qualities: first, having permanently severed the afflictions cut by the path of seeing, realizing the fruit of stream-entry; second, having permanently severed the afflictions cut by the path of cultivation, gradually realizing the fruits of once-return, non-return, and arhatship; third, having realized arhatship, abiding in the ease of the present life. By the power of three divisions illuminating every knowable object, three eyes are established: first, the physical eye, which illuminates visible matter that is exposed and unobstructed; second, the divine eye, which illuminates visible matter whether exposed or hidden, obstructed or unobstructed; third, the eye of wisdom, which illuminates all phenomena of every kind, whether possessing form or not. There are also three things capable of destroying the manifest enemy of affliction: first, trusting and following good friends; second, not dwelling intermixed with crowds of householders or renunciants; third, correct inner attention that awakens to the genuine principle of what is to be known.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps the first triad onto the classic 112 afflictions cut by the path of seeing (the intellectually-held views severed in a single breakthrough to stream-entry) and the 16 cut by the path of cultivation (the innate, habitual afflictions worn away gradually through the higher three fruits) — with a third, distinct milestone: even after full arhatship, repeatedly entering the ease of present-life abiding lets a dull-natured arhat's faculties sharpen further, showing that practice does not simply stop once liberation is reached.

He explains the three eyes as a ladder of increasing subtlety: the physical eye (inherited from one's parents) sees only what is nearby, in front, and unobstructed; the divine eye, gained through meditative attainment, sees matter at any distance and through any obstruction, near or far, past or present; and the eye of wisdom alone sees emptiness itself — not just matter, but the true nature of every phenomenon, material or immaterial, without exception. And he reads the three defenses against affliction as mutually reinforcing: trusting a genuine teacher establishes right view in the first place; avoiding entangled, undisciplined company protects the conditions needed for calm, undistracted practice — he cites the treatise's own repeated warnings that mixing freely with householders and unruly renunciants alike breeds gossip, distraction, and unresolved doubt; and correct inner attention actually applies right view to one's own body and mind. Together, the three simply remove, one by one, the specific conditions affliction depends on to arise at all.

14. Three Domains of Right Instruction, Three Ways to Rest Secure in Practice, and Three Grades of Mental One-Pointedness

Three richly practical items follow: the three domains — precepts, concentration, and wisdom — in which correct instruction and its repeated practice lead progressively toward realization; the three specific supports (the noble abidings, the heavenly abidings, and the divine abidings) that let a practitioner rest genuinely secure while training; and three successive grades of one-pointed concentration mapped directly onto the seven attentions and the three vehicles' meditators.

Chinese Source

復有三種正教誡方便，能展轉證後後所證及得涅槃。一、於尸羅正教誡方便，二、於心住正教誡方便，三、於覺悟所知真實道理正教誡方便。如正教誡方便有三種，當知數習正教誡方便亦爾。又於正教誡方便現修習時，由三種法得安隱住。一者、空、無願、無相、滅盡等至，二者、四靜慮，三者、四無量。又略有三種心一境性，能令證得如實智見。一、於意言中，種種差別所緣行相；二、意言無間，種種差別所緣行相；三、超度意言，專注一境，無種種無差別所緣行相。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three kinds of correct instruction and admonition, by which one progressively realizes each subsequent realization and finally nirvāṇa: first, correct instruction and admonition regarding precepts; second, regarding settling the mind; third, regarding awakening to the genuine principle of what is to be known. As correct instruction and admonition has three kinds, know that its repeated cultivation likewise has three. Further, while actually cultivating correct instruction and admonition, one rests secure by three things: first, the attainments of emptiness, wishlessness, signlessness, and cessation; second, the four dhyānas; third, the four immeasurables. There are also, briefly, three kinds of one-pointedness of mind, capable of bringing about genuine knowledge and vision: first, the manifold and distinct aspect of the object within mental discourse; second, the manifold and distinct aspect of the object with mental discourse uninterrupted; third, the aspect of the object, without manifoldness or distinction, transcending mental discourse and focused single-pointedly.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the three domains of instruction simply as the three higher trainings — precepts, concentration, and wisdom — each requiring both correct initial teaching and its patient, repeated practice before the next domain can be built on it. He then names the three supports that keep a practitioner genuinely at ease during that training: the "noble abiding" (the four attainments of emptiness, wishlessness, signlessness, and cessation, already surveyed in fascicles 11–13, distinguished between their śrāvaka and bodhisattva versions); the "heavenly abiding" (the four dhyānas themselves, each with its characteristic feeling); and the "divine abiding" (the four immeasurables — loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity — cultivated for others' benefit and traditionally said to lead toward rebirth in the Brahmā heavens). Genuine ease in practice, he stresses, is never a matter of external circumstance but of inward joy in the Dharma itself; lacking that joy, no outward comfort can substitute for it.

The three grades of one-pointedness he maps directly onto three types of meditator already named in the Śrāvaka Ground: a beginning practitioner still needs mental discourse (the sixth consciousness's own inner language) to hold the meditation object in mind at all — noticing "this is the breath," for instance, and only then observing its actual coming and going; a practiced meditator sustains that same object without interruption, whether because the concept and object no longer feel separated, because successive moments of attention flow together seamlessly, or because reflection and hearing-wisdom no longer need to be separately consulted mid-session; and a meditator who has gone beyond mental discourse entirely rests single-pointedly on the object itself, corresponding — on the meditative side — to the first dhyāna actually attained, and — on the path side — to stream-entry itself, or, in the Mahāyāna, the first ground. Cháng Bǎi treats this progression as tracing the entire arc from a rank beginner's effortful noticing to a settled meditator's genuinely wordless absorption.

15. Three Skillful Means for Reaching the Undecided, and What Genuine Purity and Genuine Sagehood Actually Consist In

Three more items follow: the three skillful means by which the Buddha draws both wrongly-convinced and simply undecided beings into the teaching; a correction of ascetics who mistake ritual bathing for genuine purity, redirecting "purity" to where it actually belongs; and a parallel correction of ascetics who mistake vows of silence for genuine sagehood.

Chinese Source

又有三處，能善攝受於惡邪處妄計尊勝及處中庸所化有情，引入聖教。一、現己所有最勝神通，二、於他所有染淨諸行遮止開許，三、宣說妙法正教正誡。又有三淨，為欲斷除樂淨外道，以外事水暫除外垢，自謂已得第一清淨所起邪慢故；建立此三第一義淨。不淨處生超越因故。又有三種牟尼，為欲斷除持牟尼戒諸外道等暫息語言，自謂已得真實寂靜所起邪執故；又為顯說無倒牟尼故；建立三種真實牟尼。即是聖所愛戒所攝身語二業，及無漏心。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three means capable of skillfully drawing in beings who falsely regard some evil, wrong position as supreme, as well as those of a genuinely undecided disposition, leading them into the sacred teaching: first, manifesting one's own supreme superknowledges; second, restraining or permitting others' defiled or pure conduct as appropriate; third, expounding the sublime Dharma through correct teaching and correct admonition. There are also three purities, established in order to eliminate the wrong conceit of ascetics who delight in purity through external means — using water to temporarily wash away outer defilement and claiming thereby to have attained the highest purity — these three ultimate purities are established because they are the actual cause of transcending rebirth in impure realms. There are also three [kinds of] sagely silence, established in order to eliminate the wrong grasping of ascetics who observe vows of silence and claim thereby to have attained genuine tranquility, and in order to reveal an undistorted sagely silence: these are precisely the bodily and verbal actions comprised within the precepts a noble one loves, together with the uncontaminated mind.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the three skillful means as the three miraculous transformations already named at §65 of fascicle 13: manifesting superknowledge to win respect, reading and appropriately affirming or restraining others' conduct, and directly teaching — together the Buddha's complete toolkit for reaching beings whose views range from actively mistaken to simply unformed.

He reads the next two items as a matched pair, each correcting the same underlying error from a different angle: certain ascetics believed literal bathing in sacred rivers washed away moral impurity, so the Buddha redirects "purity" to where it actually resides — pure body, speech, and mind, the only purity capable of freeing a being from further rebirth in the impure desire realm; and other ascetics believed that simply keeping silent (a formal vow, "muni precepts," from which the very word "sage," muni, derives) constituted genuine sagely stillness, so the Buddha redirects "sagely silence" to what it actually means — not the absence of speech, but the noble precepts governing genuinely pure bodily and verbal conduct, joined with an uncontaminated mind. In both cases, he notes, the correction follows the same pattern seen already with the fire-worshippers at item 23: not simply rejecting the seeker's aspiration, but showing them where what they are actually looking for can genuinely be found.

16. Overcoming Distracting Thought, the Three Unsurpassed Excellences, and the Three Knowledges That Cure Ignorance — the "Three Things" Category Closes

The final three items close the vast "three things" category — forty items in all. First: three specific feelings of shame and reverence that let a secluded practitioner overcome unwholesome discursive thought. Second: the three unsurpassed excellences (wisdom, practice, and fruit) that structure the entire path from beginning to end. Third and last: the three knowledges — of past lives, of future rebirths, and of the exhaustion of outflows — that directly cure the three specific forms of ignorance underlying wrong views about the past, future, and present alike.

Chinese Source

又有三法，能令處遠離者斷除現行不正尋思。謂由他所誹毀、自所誹毀、退失大利增上力故，所起慚、愧及與愛敬。又依道及道果，當知有三種最勝無上。謂無常智、苦智、無我智；樂速通等四種行跡；一切世間、出世間，有學、無學，時解脫、不動心解脫；最勝無上。修觀行者先得其智；由此智故，為斷煩惱，次修行跡；修行跡已，心得解脫。又有三明。當知為顯於前後中際，斷常二邊邪執、現法涅槃愚癡、沙門婆羅門無明性故，建立三明。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three things that let one dwelling in seclusion sever manifest, unwholesome discursive thought: shame arisen from the power of others' censure, shame arisen from the power of one's own self-censure, and reverent love arisen from the power of [seeing] the loss of great benefit. In dependence on the path and the fruit of the path, know there are three unsurpassed excellences: the knowledge of impermanence, of suffering, and of selflessness; the four courses of practice culminating in swift, blissful penetration; and every worldly and supramundane [attainment], the trainee's and the adept's, time-liberation and unshakeable-mind liberation. One who cultivates contemplation first attains this knowledge; by means of this knowledge, in order to sever affliction, one next cultivates the courses of practice; having cultivated the courses of practice, the mind attains liberation. There are also three knowledges. Know that these are established to reveal, with respect to the prior, later, and middle periods, the wrong grasping at the two extremes of permanence and annihilation, the delusion of a present-life nirvāṇa, and the very nature of ignorance among ascetics and brāhmins.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the three antidotes to distracting thought as covering every angle from which conscience can operate: fear of others' censure (shame before the world), fear of one's own self-censure (shame before oneself), and reverent love born from clearly seeing what carelessness costs — the loss of both worldly and ultimate benefit — motivating cherishing of the three jewels and every wholesome quality precisely because losing them would be so costly. Any one of the three, properly felt, is enough to interrupt an unwholesome train of thought before it consolidates into habit.

He reads the three unsurpassed excellences as structuring the entire path in miniature: unsurpassed wisdom (the three knowledges of impermanence, suffering, and selflessness) is the starting insight; unsurpassed practice (the four courses already introduced at fascicle 13's §66 — slow-or-swift penetration achieved with-or-without prior meditative attainment) is what that insight sets in motion; and unsurpassed fruit (every meditative attainment, every fruit from stream-entry to arhatship, and the distinction between a dull-natured arhat's "time-liberation," dependent on the right conditions being present, and a sharp-natured arhat's unshakeable liberation, secure regardless of circumstance) is what practice yields — knowledge first, then practice to sever affliction, then liberation as the settled result, a clean three-stage arc closing the entire numeric catalogue's fortieth and final item on the very structure of the path itself.

The three knowledges he reads as directly curing the three-part ignorance already named at item 7 (§4): not knowing the prior limit breeds the eternalist extreme (a permanent self persisting from the past), cured by the knowledge of past lives, which sees the true chain of causes rather than a fixed, changeless self; not knowing the later limit breeds the annihilationist extreme (nothing continuing after death), cured by the knowledge of future rebirths, which sees actual continuation without denying change; and not knowing the present breeds the delusion that some worldly pleasure or meditative attainment already constitutes nirvāṇa, cured by the knowledge of the exhaustion of outflows, which alone confirms genuine liberation has actually occurred. With these three knowledges established, the "three things" category — forty items spanning roughly a third of this fascicle's source text so far — comes to its close.

17. The Fourth Numeric Category Opens: The Four Foundations of Mindfulness, Comprehending All Knowledge and Countering All Bondage

With "three things" fully explained, the treatise turns to "four things" — a category of thirty-nine planned items. The first takes up the four foundations of mindfulness (body, feeling, mind, and mental objects) from two angles at once: how they comprehend absolutely everything a being can know together with the wisdom that knows it, and how each one specifically counters one of four distinct ways the mind becomes bound.

Chinese Source

已說三種佛教所應知處。次說四種。謂有四法，能攝一切所知及智。謂身，及聞思修增上念住以為依止緣身境慧。如身及緣身境慧，當知受、心、法，及緣受、心、法境慧亦爾。復有差別，謂四種縛。一、執取縛，二、領受縛，三、了別縛，四、執著縛。當知心於身，由執取縛所縛；於受，由內領受縛所縛；於色等境界相，由了別縛所縛；即於所說身等，由貪瞋等大小煩惱執著縛所縛。對治如是四種縛故，立四念住。

Ru-Yi Translation

The three-thing category of what a Buddhist should know has now been explained. Next, the four-thing category. There are four phenomena that comprehend all that is knowable together with the knowing wisdom itself: namely, the body, and the wisdom born of hearing, reflection, and cultivation that, taking the foundation of mindfulness as its basis, takes the body as its object. As with the body and the wisdom that takes it as object, know that feeling, mind, and mental objects, and the wisdom that takes each as object, are likewise so. There is, further, a distinction: the four bonds. First, the bond of grasping; second, the bond of receiving; third, the bond of discriminating; fourth, the bond of attachment. Know that the mind is bound to the body by the bond of grasping; to feeling, by the bond of inward receiving; to the characteristic marks of form and other objects, by the bond of discriminating; and to the very body, feeling, and so forth just described, by the bond of attachment through greed, hatred, and the greater and lesser secondary afflictions. The four foundations of mindfulness are established precisely to counter these four bonds.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the first angle as a claim about completeness: every object any being can ever know reduces to body, feeling, mind, or mental phenomena, and every grade of knowing wisdom — from initially hearing the teaching, through reflecting on its meaning, to the fully cultivated wisdom that directly realizes it (itself splitting into a still-contaminated and, ultimately, an uncontaminated form capable of severing affliction outright) — applies identically across all four. Nothing a practitioner could ever come to know, and no depth at which they might know it, falls outside this fourfold grid.

The second angle he reads as explaining exactly why these four objects, specifically, were chosen: each corresponds to one of the four classical inversions already met at fascicle 13's §37. The mind binds itself to the body by grasping it as clean when it is not (the grasping-bond, countered by contemplating the body's impurity); to feeling by mistaking painful or neutral experience for pleasant (the receiving-bond, countered by contemplating feeling as suffering); to its own discriminating awareness of objects by mistaking the impermanent for permanent (the discriminating-bond, countered by contemplating mind's impermanence); and to the whole body-feeling-mind complex by mistaking what is selfless for a self, breeding the full range of greed, hatred, and their attendant secondary afflictions (the attachment-bond, countered by contemplating phenomena as selfless). Each foundation of mindfulness, on this reading, is not an arbitrary meditation topic but a precisely engineered antidote, cut to the exact shape of the specific distortion it exists to dissolve.

18. The Four Right Efforts, the Four Bases of Supernormal Power, and the Four Dhyānas as States of Settled Mind

Three more items follow, each already familiar in outline from earlier fascicles but here given fresh technical precision: the four right efforts that structure diligent practice, the four bases from which genuine meditative power is drawn, and the four dhyānas analyzed strictly by what settles the mind at each stage.

Chinese Source

又有四種欲勤為先，觀察過患及與對治以為依止，能斷現行諸不善法，及斷彼繫；能得善法，及能增長。又有四種為欲住心為得勝定修方便者，心住如意能生長門。一、樂出離欲；二、受持讀誦，悔過精進；三、能取賢善定相之心；四、住空閑處，觀察諸法。又有四種心定心住。一、有尋有伺有喜心住；二、無尋無伺有喜心住；三、無尋無伺離喜心住；四、捨念清淨，超度一切苦樂心住。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also four things, aspiration and diligence coming first, taking as their basis the observation of faults and their antidotes, capable of severing manifest unwholesome phenomena and severing their bondage, and capable of attaining wholesome phenomena and increasing them. There are also four things, for one who wishes to settle the mind and cultivates the means to attain superior concentration, that are the gateway through which the mind, settled, is able to grow at will: first, delighting in renouncing desire; second, receiving, retaining, reciting, confessing wrongdoing, and diligence; third, a mind able to grasp the excellent sign of concentration; fourth, dwelling in a secluded place and observing phenomena. There are also four settlings of a concentrated mind: first, the settling accompanied by applied and sustained thought and by joy; second, the settling free of applied and sustained thought yet still accompanied by joy; third, the settling free of applied and sustained thought and free of joy; fourth, the settling of purified equanimity and mindfulness, transcending both pleasure and pain entirely.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the four right efforts (四正勤/四正斷) with their classical four functions: restraint (preventing unarisen unwholesome states), severance (cutting the seeds of what has already arisen), cultivation (generating unarisen wholesome states), and protection (guarding and increasing wholesome states already present) — the same diligence already central to fascicle 13's account of the path, now given its most technical four-part name.

The four bases of supernormal power (四神足) he reads as four different entry points into the identical concentrated state, each named for whichever factor does the main work of getting there: aspiration-concentration (roused by a genuine wish to leave unwholesome states behind); diligence-concentration (roused by sustained study and confession of past wrongdoing); mind-concentration (roused by skillfully applying calming, uplifting, and equanimity exactly as fascicle 13's §30 described, to hold the "excellent sign" — classically the impurity-contemplation itself); and investigation-concentration (roused by retreating to solitude and directly examining whether affliction has actually been severed or merely suppressed, guarding against the false confidence of premature conceit). All four converge on the same one-pointed state; they differ only in which door a given practitioner happens to enter through.

The four settlings of mind he confirms as simply the four dhyānas, redescribed purely by their defining mental factors rather than by realm or number: the first, still accompanied by applied and sustained thought (the coarser and finer mental activity that first disengages from desire) together with joy and bliss; the second, once that thinking itself has been abandoned through disgust with its own agitation, settling into an "inward equipoise" purified of even the need for such thought; the third, abandoning joy's own excitement for a deeper, mindfully guarded equanimity and bliss; and the fourth, abandoning bliss itself for a purified equanimity and mindfulness that transcends pleasant and painful feeling altogether — the same four-stage ladder from fascicle 13, restated here purely in terms of what settles the mind at each rung.

19. The Four Noble Truths as Cause and Effect, Four Kinds of Bondage Through Perception, and the Four Immeasurables

Three more items: the four noble truths reorganized explicitly as two pairs of cause and effect (defiled and pure), four specific ways ordinary perception — however refined, up through the loftiest formless attainments — still binds a being within saṃsāra, and the four immeasurable minds that directly counter hostility, cruelty, envy, and craving alike.

Chinese Source

又有四種所知真實。染汙、清淨二品別故，建立四種。若能了知、善了知者，能斷見、修所斷一切煩惱。一、染汙品果真實，二、彼品因真實，三、清淨品果真實，四、彼品因真實。又有四種想為先戲論縛。一、於小欲中想為先戲論縛，二、於大色中想為先戲論縛，三、於無量空、識無邊處想為先戲論縛，四、於無所有處想為先戲論縛。又有四法，於諸有情對治恚害不樂欲貪。善修習時能生大福，能趣離欲。一、慈，二、悲，三、喜，四、捨。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also four kinds of reality to be known, established by the distinction between the defiled and pure classes: knowing them, and knowing them well, one is able to sever all afflictions cut by the paths of seeing and cultivation. First, the reality of the defiled class's fruit; second, the reality of that class's cause; third, the reality of the pure class's fruit; fourth, the reality of that class's cause. There are also four bonds of discursive proliferation with perception coming first: first, the bond in small desire; second, the bond in vast form; third, the bond in the boundless spheres of space and consciousness; fourth, the bond in the sphere of nothingness. There are also four things that counter hostility, cruelty, displeasure, and craving toward sentient beings; cultivated well, they generate great merit and lead toward freedom from desire: first, loving-kindness; second, compassion; third, sympathetic joy; fourth, equanimity.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps the four realities directly onto the four noble truths reorganized as cause and effect: suffering is the defiled class's fruit, origination is its cause; cessation is the pure class's fruit, the path is its cause. "Knowing" corresponds to the path of seeing's breakthrough (severing the 112 intellectually-held afflictions in a single stroke); "knowing well" corresponds to the path of cultivation's gradual work (wearing away the 16 innate afflictions over time) — the same two-stage structure already met at item 29.

The four perception-bonds he reads as showing that proliferation's grip does not loosen just because a mind's scope grows vaster: desire-realm beings bind themselves through "small" perception, absorbed in narrow sensory craving; form-realm beings bind themselves through "vast" perception, their scope far greater yet still not free of self-referential discrimination; and even the formless attainments — boundless space, boundless consciousness, and finally nothingness itself — remain, however expansive or subtle, still perception, still discursive proliferation, still binding a being within the three realms, since none of these states actually severs affliction, only outgrows or suppresses it temporarily. Vastness of mind, Cháng Bǎi stresses, is not the same thing as freedom from bondage; only genuine insight accomplishes that.

The four immeasurables he pairs precisely with what each corrects: loving-kindness counters hostile intent by actively wishing others well; compassion counters the wish to harm by actively wishing to relieve others' suffering; sympathetic joy counters envy at another's good fortune by genuinely rejoicing in it; and equanimity counters craving itself by releasing hatred toward the hostile, attachment toward the beloved, and indifference toward the neutral alike. Cultivated as mere worldly practice, he notes, citing the Buddha's own teaching to the monks, these four still yield great merit and rebirth in the Brahmā heavens; but cultivated as the basis for the seven factors of enlightenment, they become a direct vehicle toward liberation itself — the same four minds serving, depending on how they are held, either worldly or supramundane ends.

20. The Four Formless Attainments, and the Four Noble Lineages of Contentment That Sustain Genuine Practice

Two more items: the four increasingly rarefied formless attainments that carry a meditator furthest from the form realm, and the four qualities — contentment with clothing, food, and shelter, joined by a genuine delight in severing affliction — that the treatise calls the seeds from which every noble one, without exception, actually grows.

Chinese Source

又有四法超過色界，令成遠分。謂空處、識處、無所有處、非想非非想處。又有四種，為令解脫速得圓滿，勤修行者聖解脫欲勝任持法，為斷四愛增上力故。謂為衣服、飲食、臥具，少有所求，無作無亂，時無虛度，勤修方便；心離散亂，樂斷煩惱、樂修正道。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also four things that transcend the form realm, making it a distant thing: the sphere of infinite space, the sphere of infinite consciousness, the sphere of nothingness, and the sphere of neither perception nor non-perception. There are also four things, sought by the diligent practitioner with the noble aspiration for liberation, that powerfully sustain that liberation and bring it swiftly to fulfillment, by the power of severing the four cravings: having little desire regarding clothing, food, and bedding, free of contrivance and disorder, never idling away the time, diligently cultivating the means; the mind free of distraction, delighting in severing affliction, delighting in cultivating the correct path.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi confirms the four formless attainments as simply the four spheres already surveyed at length in fascicles 11–12 (infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness, and neither-perception-nor-non-perception, the highest point of saṃsāra) — restated here purely to make explicit how each, successively, distances a meditator further from the form realm's own subtle materiality, even while — as item 6 above already cautioned — none of them alone severs affliction outright.

He identifies the four sustaining qualities as the classical "four noble lineages" (四聖種): contentment with whatever clothing, food, and dwelling one happens to have — never scheming to acquire better, never grieving over having less, simply using what is needed to sustain the body for practice and no more — joined by a fourth and decisive quality, genuine delight in severing affliction and cultivating the path itself. The first three, he notes, only protect a practitioner's time and attention from being consumed by acquisition; it is the fourth alone that actually does the work of severing the deepest cravings — for continued existence and for its cessation — that keep saṃsāra turning. Without that fourth delight, contentment with material things is merely comfortable poverty, not yet the seed of anything noble.

21. The Four Fruits Mapped to Exactly Which Fetters They Sever, and the Four Confirmed Purities of a Stream-Enterer

Two more items: a precise mapping of the four classical fruits of the path onto exactly which of the ten fetters each one severs, and the four specific, unshakeable confidences — in the Buddha, the Dharma, the Saṅgha, and one's own precepts — that a stream-enterer gains the moment that first fruit is realized.

Chinese Source

又有四種修習道果諸煩惱斷。一、見所斷煩惱斷，二、修所斷下分結上中品斷，三、即此無餘斷，四、上分結無餘斷。復有四種證預流支，能令行者於佛聖教及善趣中畢竟不動。謂於大師所真覺所生無動心淨。如於大師所，當知於所證法及為證法修正行者所亦爾。如是三種名心清淨。第四一種名色清淨，聖所愛戒所攝故。前之三種，令於聖教無有動搖；最後一種，令於善趣無有動搖。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also four severances of affliction through cultivating the fruits of the path: first, severing what is cut by the path of seeing; second, severing the middle and upper grades of the lower fetters cut by the path of cultivation; third, severing the very remainder of those same lower fetters entirely; fourth, severing the upper fetters entirely. There are also four supports of stream-entry, which cause the practitioner to remain utterly unshaken with regard to the Buddha's sacred teaching and with regard to a favorable rebirth. Namely: toward the Great Teacher, an unshaken purity of mind born of genuine realization. As toward the Great Teacher, know it is likewise so toward the Dharma realized, and toward those who practice rightly in order to realize that Dharma. These three are called purity of mind. The fourth alone is called purity of form, since it is comprised within the precepts a noble one loves. The first three cause one to remain unshaken with regard to the sacred teaching; the last causes one to remain unshaken with regard to a favorable rebirth.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps the four fruits precisely onto the ten fetters (already partly introduced in earlier fascicles): stream-entry severs the intellectually-held afflictions cut by the path of seeing, including self-view, attachment to wrong rules and rites, and doubt — three of the five "lower" fetters gone at once; once-return severs the middle and coarser grades of the remaining two lower fetters, desire and ill will (each graded into nine degrees of intensity, of which the coarsest six are now cut); non-return severs what remains of those same two entirely, the last three finer grades; and arhatship severs the five "upper" fetters outright — craving for form-realm and formless-realm existence, restlessness, conceit, and the last trace of ignorance. Each fruit, on this reckoning, is defined by nothing but a precise, cumulative subtraction of specific afflictions from the ten.

The four confirmed purities he reads as the concrete inward experience of having just become a noble one: an unshakeable confidence in the Buddha, in the Dharma he taught, and in the Saṅgha who practice it — three purities of mind, born directly from having realized the truth oneself rather than merely believed it on report — joined by a fourth, purity of conduct: the noble precepts that arise simultaneously with that same realization, expressed through body and speech. The first three secure a stream-enterer's confidence in the teaching itself; the fourth secures against ever again committing the kind of unwholesome action that would lead to a lower rebirth — together guaranteeing, as item 19's "fixed cause" already established, that a stream-enterer's course toward final liberation, however many lifetimes it may still take, can never again be derailed.

22. The Four Causes of Stream-Entry Restated, and Four Kinds of Knowledge Comprehending All Wisdom

Two more items: the four causes of stream-entry named again, this time under their own specific headings rather than the "four reliances" vocabulary of earlier fascicles, and a fourfold classification comprehending every kind of knowledge a being can possess, from the purest uncontaminated insight down to ordinary worldly know-how.

Chinese Source

又有四種證預流支。一、於說法師及教授者能善承事，無所違犯；二、無倒聽聞師所說法及教授法；三、於所聞法能正思惟及善通達；四、成辦所修。又有四智攝一切智。一、唯無漏，於諸法中能現見智；二、一向無漏，於諸法中非現見智；三、一向有漏，或如理所引、或不如理所引、或非如理非不如理所引世間智；四、通有漏、無漏他心差別智。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also four causes of stream-entry: first, skillfully attending upon the teacher and instructor without transgression; second, hearing the teacher's teaching and instruction without distortion; third, correctly reflecting on and thoroughly penetrating what has been heard; fourth, accomplishing what is thereby cultivated. There are also four kinds of knowledge comprehending all knowledge: first, purely uncontaminated knowledge that directly perceives phenomena; second, uniformly uncontaminated knowledge of phenomena that does not directly perceive them; third, uniformly contaminated worldly knowledge, whether rightly guided, wrongly guided, or neither; fourth, knowledge of others' minds, common to both the contaminated and the uncontaminated.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the four causes as the same four supports of stream-entry named across earlier fascicles (approaching a genuine teacher, hearing the true teaching, correct reflection, and practice in accordance with the Dharma), restated here under headings emphasizing the teacher-student relationship specifically: sincere service to one's teacher, undistorted listening, genuine reflective penetration, and finally the actual accomplishment those first three make possible.

The four kinds of knowledge he maps onto a classical distinction between "dharma-knowledge" and "subsequent knowledge": direct, uncontaminated insight into the desire realm's own four truths (dharma-knowledge proper, since the desire realm is directly present to the practitioner observing it); the parallel but indirect uncontaminated insight into the form and formless realms' four truths (subsequent knowledge, since those realms are not directly present to a desire-realm observer and must be inferred by extension); ordinary worldly knowledge — a farmer's, a lawyer's, a doctor's — which may be skillfully applied toward good ends, misapplied toward exploiting technicalities, or simply neutral busywork; and knowledge of others' minds, which can be either an ordinary meditator's contaminated psychic skill or a noble one's fully purified telepathic insight, depending entirely on who possesses it. Together, he notes, these four exhaust every variety of knowing a being can have, from the most rarefied direct realization down to a craftsman's ordinary competence.

23. Applying the Four Truths to Every Link of Dependent Origination, and the Four Practical Means to Actually Sever Affliction

Two more items: a demonstration that the four truths' cause-and-effect structure can be run through each individual link of the twelve-fold chain of dependent origination, not just applied to suffering in the abstract, and four concrete practical means — sustained diligence, correct self-monitoring, calm, and insight — by which affliction is actually severed once genuine faith is in place.

Chinese Source

又有四種於轉還品真實能取智，能盡諸漏。一、轉品果真實智，二、轉品因真實智，三、還品果真實智，四、還品因真實智。又有四法，能令信者為斷煩惱修正方便。一、相續殷重作用精進，二、正知行念，三、奢摩他，四、毗鉢舍那。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also four kinds of genuine, apprehending knowledge regarding the flux and return classes, capable of exhausting all outflows: first, genuine knowledge of the fruit of the flux class; second, genuine knowledge of the cause of the flux class; third, genuine knowledge of the fruit of the return class; fourth, genuine knowledge of the cause of the return class. There are also four things that let one of genuine faith cultivate the correct means to sever affliction: first, sustained, earnest, diligent effort; second, correct knowledge, conduct, and mindfulness; third, calm; fourth, insight.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the four "flux and return" knowledges as simply the four noble truths applied with unusual rigor: not just to suffering and its cause in general, but link by link through the entire twelve-fold chain of dependent origination, citing the *Sūtra on the Noble Path of Dependent Origination*'s own worked example — seeing aging-and-death (the flux class's fruit), seeing its arising from birth (the flux class's cause), seeing its cessation once birth ceases (the return class's fruit), and seeing the actual path that leads to that cessation (the return class's cause) — then repeating the identical fourfold analysis at every other link back through formations. The method, he notes, is completely general: apply it to any single link, and the whole architecture of suffering, its cause, its cessation, and the path to that cessation becomes visible at that one point, not merely in the abstract.

The four practical means to sever affliction he reads as a complete toolkit building on genuine faith: sustained, earnest diligence (practice that never lapses into carelessness or half-heartedness); correct knowledge, conduct, and mindfulness together (knowing whether affliction is presently active, conducting oneself by the four causes of stream-entry, and holding the teaching in mind so thought stays aligned with it); calm (settling the mind on any suitable object — breath, the body's impurity, or emptiness itself — until the five hindrances subside and at least threshold concentration is reached); and insight (reflecting deeply on the teaching, then, resting on that same concentration, directly observing reality until affliction is actually severed rather than merely calmed). He stresses that calm alone only suppresses affliction temporarily; only calm paired with insight actually uproots it.

24. The Four Formless Aggregates Working as One, and Four Concrete Protections That Keep a Practitioner Loving the Teaching

Two more items, moving toward practical concerns: how the four non-material aggregates (feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness) always operate together as a single coordinated act of cognition, and four concrete forms of self-protection — over livelihood, bodily strength, distracting thought, and sustained effort — that keep a new practitioner's love for the teaching from eroding.

Chinese Source

又有四法展轉相應，有行、有緣，和合而轉、同一緣轉。謂受、想、行、識。又有四護，能令已入佛聖教者愛樂聖教。一者、命護，二者、力護，三者、心煩惱護，四者、正方便護。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also four phenomena mutually corresponding, each with its own activity and object, arising together and operating on a single shared object: namely, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness. There are also four protections that let one who has entered the Buddha's sacred teaching continue to love it: first, protection of livelihood; second, protection of strength; third, protection from mental affliction; fourth, protection of correct effort.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the four formless aggregates always arise together on one shared object in a single moment, each contributing its own distinctive function — feeling registers the object as pleasant, painful, or neutral; perception grasps its identifying mark; formations (represented by volition) drives some response to it; and consciousness simply discerns it — a single act of cognition that only analysis, never actual experience, ever separates into four.

He reads the four protections as covering everything that could otherwise erode a sincere new practitioner's commitment: protection of livelihood means acquiring food, clothing, shelter, and medicine only by legitimate means, never through livelihoods (like killing) that violate precepts even where no civil law forbids them; protection of strength means eating in right measure — not for indulgence or vanity but simply to sustain the body for practice; protection from mental affliction means catching an unwholesome thought the instant it arises and dropping it at once, the way one would drop a venomous snake; and protection of correct effort means bearing whatever hardship sustained practice requires without ever growing slack. Together, he notes, the four amount to nothing more exotic than clean living, disciplined eating, watchful attention, and persistence — ordinary virtues that nonetheless make or break whether love for the Dharma actually survives contact with daily life.

25. Four Supports for Correct View in Both Activity and Stillness, the Three Knowledges and Two Liberations, and What Grounds All Physical Matter

Four more items, moving quickly: the four supports — equanimity, mindfulness, and clear comprehension while active, one-pointedness while still — that keep a practitioner's view undistorted in every circumstance; the specific factors producing the three knowledges and two kinds of liberation already introduced earlier in this category; the four elemental properties (solidity, moisture, heat, mobility) underlying every form of physical matter whatsoever; and the four kinds of food that sustain any already-existing being and draw ordinary beings toward yet further rebirth.

Chinese Source

又有四種能得正見無倒義行所依處所。由前三種，行時清淨；由後一種，住時清淨。謂守根門者，於諸境界不順不違，為守根門念增上力，正知而行。住遠離者，心無染汙，專注一緣。又由四行，當知能證明及解脫。由念、眼、慧能證於明；又由身故，能證不動及時解脫。復有四法，能為廣大種種差別諸所造色生起依止。一者、堅性，二者、濕性，三者，煖性，四者、輕等動性。又有四法，能持已生諸有情類令得久住，及能攝益尋求有者。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also four bases for attaining correct, undistorted view and conduct: the first three purify one while active, the last purifies one while abiding still. Namely: guarding the sense-doors, neither favoring nor opposing any object; the power of mindfulness that sustains this guarding; and acting with clear comprehension. One who dwells in seclusion keeps the mind free of defilement, focused single-pointedly on one object. By four factors, know that the [three] knowledges and [two] liberations are realized: by mindfulness, by the [divine] eye, and by wisdom, the three knowledges are realized; and by the body, unshakeable liberation and time-liberation are realized. There are also four things that give rise to the great variety of matter that arises from them: first, solidity; second, moisture; third, heat; fourth, lightness and mobility. There are also four things that sustain already-arisen sentient beings in continued existence and draw those still seeking further existence toward it.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the four view-supports as covering active and still moments alike: equanimity toward whatever the senses meet, mindfulness that keeps this equanimity from lapsing, and clear comprehension of one's own state moment to moment together purify ordinary activity, while single-pointed absorption purifies formal sitting practice. The three knowledges (already named at item 40 of "three things") he traces to their specific causes: mindfulness of past lives yields knowledge of the past, the divine eye yields knowledge of future rebirths, and decisive wisdom yields knowledge of outflows' exhaustion — while an arhat's remaining body (the "residue" of a final life not yet passed into nirvāṇa without remainder) is what makes the difference between a sharp-natured arhat's unshakeable liberation and a dull-natured arhat's liberation dependent on favorable conditions, already distinguished at item 39.

The four elemental properties he confirms as simply earth, water, fire, and wind under their defining characteristics rather than their common names — solidity, moisture, heat, and mobility — the four properties from which every form of matter whatsoever, without exception, is built. And the four foods (edible food, contact, mental volition, and consciousness, already surveyed at length in fascicle 5) are recalled here specifically for their double function: sustaining beings already alive, and simultaneously fueling the craving that draws unliberated beings toward their next rebirth — the same nourishment that keeps this life going also keeps the next one coming.

26. Four Ways Consciousness Clings to the Aggregates, and Four Kinds of Craving Even a Sincere Monk Must Watch For

Two more items: a detailed account of exactly how consciousness attaches itself to form and, by the same pattern, to feeling, perception, and formations — the mechanism keeping any being's stream of experience bound to the aggregates at all — and the four specific cravings, three ordinary and one subtly mistaken for spiritual aspiration, that even a well-intentioned renunciant must watch for.

Chinese Source

又有四種於生死中諸識流轉所依足跡。謂於諸色見已趣向；由貪愛故，取為所緣；所依、境界俱有建立。如於諸色，於受、想、行，當知亦爾。又諸苾芻，顧戀現法身命為依止故，而於衣服、飲食、臥具生希求愛；顧戀後法身命為依止故，而於後有生希求愛；愚於涅槃為依止故，而於無有生希求愛。如是略有四種希求之愛。謂衣服愛、飲食愛、臥具愛、有無有愛。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also four footprints on which consciousness's flux through birth and death depends. Namely: having seen form, one turns toward it; through craving, one grasps it as an object; and the support and the object are both thereby established together. As with form, know that it is likewise so with feeling, perception, and formations. Further, monks, cherishing the body and life of the present existence as their basis, give rise to craving desire for clothing, food, and bedding; cherishing the body and life of a future existence as their basis, they give rise to craving for further existence; and, deluded with respect to nirvāṇa as their basis, they give rise to craving for non-existence. Thus, briefly, there are four kinds of craving desire: craving for clothing, for food, for bedding, and for existence or non-existence.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads consciousness's four-fold attachment to each aggregate as three linked steps: consciousness follows an object, takes it as its object through craving, and then rests dependent on it — faculty, object, and consciousness co-arising in one bound moment. Desire-realm and form-realm beings attach primarily to form itself; the formless attainments of space and consciousness attach primarily to feeling; nothingness attaches primarily to perception; and the peak of existence attaches primarily to formations — even the most rarefied meditative absorption, he stresses, has not escaped this same structure of attachment, only refined which aggregate it clings to.

The four cravings he treats as a warning aimed specifically at sincere renunciants, not merely worldly people: clinging to present bodily life breeds craving for clothing, food, and bedding — three cravings born from simple attachment to staying comfortably alive; but the fourth, craving for further existence or its opposite, non-existence, is subtler and more dangerous, since it can masquerade as spiritual aspiration. Craving for continued existence hopes for a favorable future rebirth without actually severing the underlying attachment producing it; craving for non-existence, worse still, mistakes the body's mere absence for liberation itself — the confused hope, sometimes even tempting suicide, that simply ending this life ends suffering, when in fact only genuine realization, not mere cessation of the body, brings the freedom sought. Cháng Bǎi treats this fourth craving as the most consequential item in this pairing precisely because it is so easily mistaken, by an earnest but confused practitioner, for the very renunciation meant to cure it.

27. Four Roots Driving Unwholesome Action, and the Four Correct Ways to Answer a Question

Two final items in this stretch: the four underlying mental roots — craving, hostility, fear-driven wrong view, and simple ignorance of cause and effect — that drive a being to act wrongly even when they know better, and a genuinely useful fourfold framework, still cited in classical Abhidharma logic, for correctly answering any question at all: directly, analytically, by counter-question, or by setting the question aside.

Chinese Source

又有四法，能令有情現行造作所不應作。謂隨順可愛事，違逆不可愛事，怖畏彊敵其心顛倒，愚於現法及後法果。又有四種請問記論，能斷所疑，能悟未悟，又能任持勝決擇力。謂於法實相應一向記，於諸有情業果異熟應分別記，於隱密說非一向問應詰問記，於不如理應當置記。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also four things that cause sentient beings to act in ways they should not: yielding to what is agreeable; resisting what is disagreeable; fearing a formidable opponent, the mind thereby inverted; and being deluded regarding the fruit of both this life and the next. There are also four kinds of response to a question, capable of severing doubt, awakening the unawakened, and sustaining the power of superior discernment: with respect to the true nature of phenomena, one should answer directly; with respect to sentient beings' karmic ripening, one should answer analytically; with respect to a question that is ambiguous or not single in intent, one should answer by counter-question; and with respect to what is unreasonable, one should set the question aside.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the four roots of wrongdoing simply as craving, hostility, wrong view, and ignorance under new descriptions: craving is yielding to what is pleasant, hostility is resisting what is unpleasant, wrong view appears specifically as fear of a stronger debate opponent (his example: non-Buddhist "eel-wriggling" sophists who, dreading refutation, answer every question with evasive, ingratiating vagueness rather than a real position), and ignorance is simply not understanding karmic cause and effect across this life and the next. Any action gone wrong, he suggests, traces back to one of these four roots operating unchecked.

He treats the fourfold answering scheme as a genuinely durable piece of classical logic, still cited centuries later in the *Abhidharmakośa*: a straightforward factual claim (will every being die?) gets a direct, one-sided answer; a claim depending on unstated conditions (will every dead being be reborn?) gets an analytical answer distinguishing the cases; an ambiguous question (is a human superior?) gets a clarifying counter-question (superior to what?) before any answer is given; and a question resting on a false premise (are the aggregates the same as, or different from, a person?) gets set aside entirely, exactly as one would decline to debate the color of a barren woman's child, since the very question assumes something that was never real to begin with. Mastering all four, Cháng Bǎi notes, is what actually lets a teacher resolve doubt, awaken genuine understanding, and model discerning wisdom, rather than simply answering everything the same way regardless of what was actually asked.

28. Four Grades of Giving by Giver and Recipient's Purity, the Four Means of Gathering a Following, and Four Modes of Rebirth

Three more items, moving quickly: giving classified by whether giver and recipient both, either, or neither keep pure precepts and right view; the four classical means — generosity, kind speech, beneficial conduct, and shared work — by which a bodhisattva actually gathers and guides a community; and the four modes by which any being enters a new existence at all.

Chinese Source

又有四種惠捨，或清淨，或不清淨。三種清淨，謂唯自身戒見具足，或復唯他戒見具足，或自及他戒見具足；一不清淨，謂自及他戒見二種俱不具足。其清淨者，當生善趣，資產豐饒；不清淨者，當生惡趣，資產無匱。又有四種攝眾方便，能正攝化一切大眾。一、饒益方便，二、攝受方便，三、引導方便，四、修治方便。又有四種從業所生諸有情類，於彼彼趣生依止門。一、由業及卵㲉，二、由業及胎膜，三、由業及潤汙，四、唯由業。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also four kinds of giving, either pure or impure. Three are pure: where only the giver's own precepts and view are complete, where only the recipient's are complete, or where both are complete. One is impure: where neither the giver's nor the recipient's precepts and view are complete. The pure kinds lead to a favorable rebirth abundant in resources; the impure kind leads to an unfavorable rebirth, yet not lacking in resources. There are also four skillful means of gathering a following, capable of rightly drawing in and transforming the entire community: first, the means of benefiting; second, the means of gathering in; third, the means of guiding; fourth, the means of cultivating together. There are also four gateways by which sentient beings, born from karma, take up existence in this or that destiny: first, by karma together with an eggshell; second, by karma together with a womb-membrane; third, by karma together with moisture; fourth, by karma alone.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the four grades of giving as showing that an act's fruit depends on both parties, not the giver alone: full purity on both sides yields the richest favorable result, purity on just one side still yields a favorable if lesser result, and impurity on both sides still ripens materially (an elephant of great strength, or a powerful spirit, can be wealthy despite an unfavorable rebirth) even while leading to an unfavorable destiny overall — merit and destiny, he stresses, are tracked separately, not automatically bundled together. The four means of gathering a community he identifies as the classical four means of a bodhisattva's outreach — giving to relieve material and spiritual want, kind and encouraging speech, guiding others into wholesome conduct through both praise and firm correction, and finally sharing the actual work and hardship of practice alongside those being guided — the same fourfold pattern named slightly differently in the Bodhisattva Ground as "attracting," "capturing," "leading in," and "following along with" skillful means.

The four modes of rebirth he confirms as the classical four: egg-born (birds), womb-born (humans and large mammals), moisture-born (insects generated from damp conditions), and spontaneously born (gods and hungry ghosts, appearing complete with no gestation at all) — noting that spontaneous birth is, by sheer numbers, actually the most common mode across the six destinies, since every intermediate-state being and the entirety of hell and heaven rebirths take this form; birth in a lotus in a pure land, he adds, is itself a kind of spontaneous birth, sparing a being the sufferings of an ordinary womb.

29. Four Kinds of Death by Cause, and Two Frameworks for the Graded Purity of the Path to Realization

Three more items: the four causes by which death actually occurs, from self-inflicted to entirely outside anyone's control; a fourfold grading of the path by whether a practitioner has attained deep concentration, sharp faculties, both, or neither; and a second, complementary fourfold grading — turning away from wrong teaching, turning toward right teaching, gathering provisions, and the path itself — tracing the same purification from a different angle.

Chinese Source

又彼彼處受生有情有四種死。一者、由自故死，謂於戲忘、意忿天中而受生者。二、由他故死，謂於羯羅藍、頞部曇、閉尸、鍵南母腹中者。三、俱由故死，謂在欲界所餘有情。四、俱不由故死，謂色無色界有頂為後所有有情。復有四清淨道。一、非功用根圓滿，亦非喜樂圓滿；二、功用根圓滿，非喜樂圓滿；三、喜樂圓滿，非功用根圓滿；四、喜樂圓滿，亦功用根圓滿。又有四清淨道。一、背惡說法及毗奈耶，二、向善說法及毗奈耶，三、資糧道，四、清淨道。

Ru-Yi Translation

Sentient beings reborn in various places die by four causes: first, by their own cause — those reborn among the gods of frivolous play or of malicious anger; second, by another's cause — those in the womb's five early embryonic stages; third, by both causes — the remaining beings of the desire realm; fourth, by neither cause — beings of the form and formless realms up through the peak of existence. There are also four purities of the path: first, neither effort of the faculties nor bliss and joy is complete; second, effort of the faculties is complete, but not bliss and joy; third, bliss and joy is complete, but not effort of the faculties; fourth, both bliss and joy and effort of the faculties are complete. There are also [another] four purities of the path: first, turning away from wrongly taught doctrine and discipline; second, turning toward rightly taught doctrine and discipline; third, the path of gathering provisions; fourth, the path itself.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the four causes of death as spanning the full range of how a life actually ends: entirely self-caused (gods who die from excessive play, having lost all track of time, or excessive rage, glaring each other to death — vivid illustrations that even heavenly rebirth is no protection against a poorly regulated mind); entirely other-caused (an embryo in its earliest five stages, unable to harm itself, dependent entirely on external causes such as the mother's condition); both self- and other-caused (ordinary desire-realm beings once born, vulnerable to either); and neither (form- and formless-realm beings, whose death comes only when the karma sustaining that rebirth is simply exhausted, with no self-harm or external violence involved at all).

He reads the first fourfold "purity of the path" as directly restating the four courses of practice already introduced at fascicle 13's §66 and item 39 of "three things" — slow-or-fast realization crossed with whether the practitioner has attained the form realm's actual bliss — and the second, complementary fourfold scheme as tracing the same purification narratively: first rejecting wrong teaching and discipline (non-Buddhist views and pointless austerities), then turning toward the genuinely correct teaching, then diligently gathering the supporting provisions (merit, learning, favorable conditions) the path requires, and finally walking the path itself to its conclusion — two different lenses on one and the same movement from confusion to realization.

30. Four Motives for a Trainee to Cultivate Deep Concentration, and Karma Reduced to Just Four Colors

Two more items: the four distinct motives — none of them mutually exclusive — that lead a trainee still short of arhatship to cultivate deep meditative concentration, and every possible karmic act reduced to a simple, memorable scheme of black, white, mixed, and the fourth "color" that is really the absence of color altogether — the noble path that ends karma's cycle rather than merely adding another shade to it.

Chinese Source

又有四種學增上心方便。謂未離欲者，為得不還果；或不還果，依未至定求現法樂住；又為令他斷諸惡法，及往善趣；又為自己斷諸煩惱，得勝決擇。又有二業四相差別。謂轉所攝業差別有三，還所攝業總立一種。當知初業，一向能感不可愛果惡趣異熟；第二業，一向能感可愛樂果色無色界異熟；第三業，能感愛非愛果欲界天人異熟；第四業，能斷前三業。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also four means by which a trainee cultivates the higher mind: one not yet free of desire does so in order to attain the fruit of non-return; or, having attained non-return, one relies on threshold concentration seeking the ease of the present life; or one does so in order to lead others to sever unwholesome states and reach a favorable destiny; or one does so in order to sever one's own afflictions and attain superior discernment. There are also two kinds of karma with four distinguishing marks: three belong to the class comprised within flux, and the class comprised within return is established as a single kind. Know that the first kind of karma uniformly ripens into the disagreeable fruit of the evil destinies; the second uniformly ripens into the agreeable fruit of existence in the form or formless realm; the third ripens into a mixed fruit, agreeable or disagreeable, in the desire realm among gods and humans; the fourth is able to sever all three of the preceding kinds.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the four motives as showing that meditative cultivation, at every stage short of arhatship, always serves some further purpose rather than being an end in itself: a stream-enterer or once-returner cultivates concentration specifically to reach non-return; a non-returner cultivates it, resting on threshold concentration alone, simply to enjoy the ease of the present life; any trainee might cultivate the higher knowledges (specifically the divine eye) purely to demonstrate the Dharma convincingly to beings who respond only to visible power, not argument; and any trainee might cultivate it purely for their own final breakthrough to arhatship. None of the four excludes the others — a single trainee's practice often serves several of these purposes across different stages of the path.

The four karmic colors he reads as the simplest possible summary of all karmic action: purely black karma (the ten unwholesome courses) ripens purely into the lower destinies; purely white karma (form- and formless-realm meditative cultivation) ripens purely into their correspondingly refined heavens; mixed black-and-white karma (ordinary wholesome conduct like the five precepts and ten virtues, imperfectly kept) ripens into the mottled fortune of desire-realm gods and humans, some aspects fortunate and others not; and the noble path alone is "neither black nor white," since it does not add one more shade to the cycle but ends the cycle's coloring altogether — the only one of the four kinds capable of severing the other three rather than merely producing another result within their same system.

31. Pleasure and Benefit in Four Combinations, and the Four Yokes That Bind Beings to Suffering

Two more items: a simple but useful fourfold cross-tabulation of whether an action feels pleasant and whether it is actually beneficial — the two are far more often mismatched than aligned — and the four "yokes" (desire, existence, view, and ignorance) that harness beings to the endless stream of suffering, together with the assurance that the noble path alone can free a being from all four at once.

Chinese Source

又有四種諸有情類增上勤務。一、樂而非利益，二、利益而非樂，三、亦樂亦利益，四、非樂非利益。又有四門起諸煩惱，能令有情與生等苦和合不離。一、染著諸欲門，二、染著色無色等至門，三、外道諸見門，四、住此法中未得眼者無智門。又修聖道，令此四門所生眾苦速得離繫。如能令有情與苦和合，能令順流取後有業難可解脫，當知亦爾。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also four kinds of diligent striving among sentient beings: first, pleasant but not beneficial; second, beneficial but not pleasant; third, both pleasant and beneficial; fourth, neither pleasant nor beneficial. There are also four gateways giving rise to affliction, which cause sentient beings to remain bound together with the suffering of birth and the rest: first, the gateway of attachment to sensory desires; second, the gateway of attachment to the attainments of the form and formless realms; third, the gateway of non-Buddhist views; fourth, the gateway of ignorance in one who, though dwelling within this very teaching, has not yet attained the eye [of wisdom]. By cultivating the noble path, the multitude of sufferings born from these four gateways are swiftly released from their bondage. As these cause sentient beings to remain bound together with suffering, know it is likewise so that they cause the karma that grasps at further existence, following the stream, to be difficult to escape.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the pleasure-and-benefit grid as a practical corrective to how easily the two are conflated: pursuing sensory pleasure and unwholesome action feels good now but ripens into future suffering (pleasant, not beneficial); disciplined precept-keeping or early meditation practice, still difficult before real facility develops, brings no immediate pleasure but genuinely secures future benefit (beneficial, not pleasant); joyful, willing practice of giving or the path brings both at once, the ideal case; and pointless non-Buddhist austerity brings neither, all hardship and no genuine result. He treats the third combination — genuine joy in wholesome practice — as what a mature practitioner should actually be cultivating, not mere grim endurance mistaken for virtue.

The four yokes (欲/有/見/無明枙) and their equivalent four "floods" (same four, described as an overwhelming current rather than a harness) he reads as the complete inventory of what keeps any being bound to birth-and-death: attachment to sensory desire, attachment even to refined form- and formless-realm meditative attainment (affliction merely suppressed, not uprooted, as already warned at item 6), non-Buddhist wrong views, and — notably — even a sincere Buddhist practitioner's own residual ignorance, for one who has entered the teaching but not yet attained genuine wisdom-eyes remains, in this respect, no freer than anyone else. Only the noble path, cultivated in full, releases a being from all four gateways and their corresponding floods at once — a note of realism that formal membership in the teaching is not itself the liberation the teaching points toward.

32. One Being, Sorted Four Different Ways: By Bondage, By Precepts and View, By Birth and Conduct, and By Who Actually Suffers

Item thirty-seven takes up a single question — how should beings be classified? — and answers it four separate times, each sort cutting across the others: by degree of affliction still binding them, by whether they keep precepts and hold right view, by the interplay of birth status and present conduct, and, most pointedly, by who actually bears the cost of an action's "merit."

Chinese Source

復有四種補特伽羅，當知遍攝一切補特伽羅。一者、異生，二者、未離欲有學，三者、已離欲有學，四者、超薩迦耶見一切無學。又有四種補特伽羅。一、自住律儀，不能為他宣說正法；二、自不住律儀，而能為他宣說正法；三、俱能作；四、俱不能作。又有四種補特伽羅。一、以苦自任不任於他而生非福，謂受外道自苦戒者。二、以苦任他不任於自而生非福，謂隨有一不律儀者。三、以苦俱任而生非福，謂諸國王及祠祀主馬祠祀等。四、不以苦任於自他而生大福，謂住靜慮者及離諸惡補特伽羅。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also four kinds of persons, comprehending all persons without exception: first, the ordinary being; second, the trainee not yet free of desire; third, the trainee already free of desire; fourth, the adept who has entirely transcended the view of a substantial self. There are also four kinds of persons: first, one who upholds precepts personally but cannot teach the true Dharma to others; second, one who does not personally uphold precepts but can teach the true Dharma to others; third, one who can do both; fourth, one who can do neither. There are also four kinds of persons: first, one who imposes suffering only on oneself and generates no merit thereby — one who undertakes a non-Buddhist ascetic's self-mortifying vows; second, one who imposes suffering only on others and generates no merit thereby — one who follows any unrestrained livelihood; third, one who imposes suffering on both self and others and generates no merit thereby — kings and those who preside over sacrificial rites such as the horse sacrifice; fourth, one who imposes suffering on neither self nor others and generates great merit thereby — one who dwells in meditative absorption and one who has abandoned all evil.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the first sort (fully bound / partly bound before freedom from desire / partly bound after / entirely unbound, the last defined specifically by transcending self-view) as exhaustive of every stage from ordinary being to arhat. He reads the second sort — precepts without teaching ability, teaching ability without full precepts, both, or neither — as a frank acknowledgment that moral discipline and doctrinal skill do not automatically come together in one person, and that a community needs people who genuinely have both, not merely people who look impressive on one axis alone.

The third sort (low birth with wholesome conduct, high birth with unwholesome conduct, low birth with unwholesome conduct, high birth with wholesome conduct, mapped respectively onto present-life suffering only / future-life suffering only / suffering in both / ease in both) he reads as directly undercutting any assumption that social standing predicts moral or karmic outcome — present circumstance and future consequence are tracked separately, and conduct, not birth, is what actually determines the latter.

The fourth sort he treats as the sharpest of the four, since it exposes "merit" claimed by those who inflict suffering on themselves alone (pointless non-Buddhist austerities, already criticized at item 26 of "three things"), on others alone (any livelihood built on harming other beings), or on both (his examples: kings whose restless ambition drags entire populations into war, and ritual sacrifice inflicting suffering on both the officiant's exhausting labor and the sacrificed animal) as no merit at all — every one of these three generates only further demerit, whatever its practitioners believe. Only conduct that burdens neither oneself nor others — meditative cultivation, or simply a thoroughly ethical life — genuinely produces the great merit its practitioners hope for. Cháng Bǎi reads this fourth classification as the whole item's real point: merit is not measured by how much anyone suffers for it, in either direction.

33. Four Faults of Speech, and the Four Marks of an Ordinary Person's Lie — the "Four Things" Category Closes

The final two items of "four things" both concern speech. The thirty-eighth names the four basic faults any utterance can have, and their four bright opposites. The thirty-ninth analyzes lying itself with unusual precision — the exact four-part structure by which an ordinary, unenlightened person's false speech operates — before naming its noble opposite. With this, the vast "four things" category, thirty-nine items in all, comes to its close.

Chinese Source

又略有四種語失。一、不實，二、乖離，三、毀德，四、無義。與此相違，當知即是四種語德。又略有四種非聖妄語。謂於見不見，顛倒而說。於聞不聞、於覺不覺、於知不知，當知亦爾。與此相違，當知即是賢聖諦語。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also, briefly, four faults of speech: first, untruthfulness; second, divisiveness; third, defaming another's virtue; fourth, meaninglessness. The reverse of these should be known as the four virtues of speech. There are also, briefly, four kinds of an ordinary person's false speech: speaking in reversal of what was seen or not seen. Know that it is likewise so with what was heard or not heard, sensed or not sensed, and known or not known. The reverse of this should be known as the truthful speech of the noble and the wise.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps the four speech-faults directly onto four of the ten unwholesome courses of action already named at fascicle 14's very first item: lying (untruthfulness), divisive speech (breaking apart others' relationships), harsh speech (defaming another's character, costing the speaker others' trust), and idle chatter (speech that serves no purpose and stirs only affliction) — with their bright reverses being simply truthful, harmonizing, gentle, and genuinely beneficial speech.

He treats the thirty-ninth item's finer analysis as worth real attention: any utterance, honest or false, is grounded in one of four sources — what was directly seen, what was heard from another, what was worked out through one's own reflection, or what was directly experienced through smell, taste, touch, or attainment (including, notably, one's own meditative or spiritual accomplishment). An ordinary person's lie is precisely the reversal of any one of these four: claiming not to have seen what was seen, or to have seen what was not; claiming not to have heard, reflected, or experienced what genuinely was, or the reverse. The noble ones' truthful speech is simply the undistorted mirror of the same four sources — always reporting seeing, hearing, reflecting, and experiencing exactly as they occurred, neither adding nor concealing. With this precise a diagnosis of what a lie actually consists of — a specific one of four possible reversals — the "four things" category, spanning fascicle 14's first thirty-nine numbered items, comes to its close.

34. The Fifth Numeric Category Opens: The Five Aggregates, With and Without Clinging

With "four things" complete, the treatise turns to "five things" — a category of twenty planned items. The first restates the five aggregates one final time, this time explicitly named by their function within the cycle of affliction: the five aggregates as they appear bound up with craving in an unliberated being, and their exact mirror-opposite in one whose craving has been entirely released.

Chinese Source

已說四種佛教所應知處。次說五種。謂有五種諸欲貪品麤重隨逐，流轉雜染所攝行聚。一、所依所緣自性行聚，二、能領納自性行聚，三、能分別言說分位取諸法相自性行聚，四、能作用自性行聚，五、能了別自性行聚。此五相違，當知即是離欲貪品麤重還滅清淨所攝自性行聚。

Ru-Yi Translation

The four-thing category of what a Buddhist should know has now been explained. Next, the five-thing category. There are five groupings of formations, trailed by the coarse weight of sensory craving, comprised within the defiled class of flux: first, the grouping whose intrinsic nature is [the body's] support and object; second, the grouping whose intrinsic nature is receiving; third, the grouping whose intrinsic nature is discriminating, speaking, and grasping the marks of phenomena according to circumstance; fourth, the grouping whose intrinsic nature is functioning; fifth, the grouping whose intrinsic nature is discerning. The reverse of these five should be known as the grouping whose intrinsic nature is comprised within the pure class of return, free of the coarse weight of sensory craving.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi confirms this is simply form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness — the five aggregates already analyzed from every conceivable angle since fascicle 3 — named here specifically as the "aggregates subject to clinging" (有取蘊), since it is precisely their entanglement with sensory craving's dormant seeds that makes them the substrate of an unliberated being's continued flux through saṃsāra. Form is the body's own support and the objects its faculties take; feeling registers experience as pleasant, painful, or neutral; perception discriminates, names, and grasps the marks of things according to circumstance; formations (represented by volition) drives action; and consciousness discerns. An arhat's aggregates — the exact same five functions, form through consciousness — are simply no longer trailed by that craving's coarse weight at all, making them, in the precise technical sense, "aggregates free of clinging" (無取蘊): identical in function, utterly different in what still clings to them. Cháng Bǎi treats this opening item as a fitting threshold into "five things," since it restates, one final time and with maximum precision, the single most fundamental classification this entire numeric catalogue has kept returning to since its account of the aggregates began.

35. The Five Sense Pleasures and Four Grades of Longing for Them, and the Five Destinies as Fruit

Two more items: the five classical sense pleasures pursued by those who love them and steadily observed by those turning away from them, cross-referenced with a fourfold analysis of exactly how longing for them differs across past, future, and present; and the five destinies into which any being's karmic fruit, agreeable or disagreeable, actually ripens.

Chinese Source

又有五種受用欲者所愛境界，諸樂欲者常所追求、常所受用；諸背欲者恆正觀察。謂色聲香味觸。當知此中，依所追求、所尋思、所染著事，有四種愛樂。謂未來所愛樂事，即所追求；過去所愛樂事，即所尋思；現在所愛樂事，即所染著。此復二種。一、所愛樂事，二、從彼所生所愛樂受。又有五種有情所得受愛非愛業果異熟自體。謂天、人、那落迦、傍生、鬼趣。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also five objects loved by those who partake of sensory desire, constantly pursued and constantly enjoyed by those who delight in desire, and constantly, correctly observed by those who have turned away from desire: namely, form, sound, smell, taste, and touch. Know that herein, depending on what is pursued, what is dwelt on in thought, and what is presently defiled by attachment, there are four kinds of delight: what is delighted in for the future is what is pursued; what is delighted in from the past is what is dwelt on in thought; what is delighted in for the present is what is presently attached to — and this last is itself twofold, the delighted-in object itself, and the delighted-in feeling arising from it. There are also five kinds of embodiment that sentient beings receive as the ripened fruit of agreeable or disagreeable karma: the gods, humans, hell-beings, animals, and hungry ghosts.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the five sense objects as simply what any desire-realm being's whole attention orbits, whether chased by those who love them or steadily watched, without indulgence, by those training to let them go. The fourfold delight he distinguishes by time and object: longing for a future pleasure not yet had, dwelling in thought on a past pleasure already gone, and — richest of the four — present attachment, which itself splits into clinging to the object (the food itself) and clinging to the feeling that object produces (the pleasure of eating it), a distinction he notes matters practically, since a practitioner can sometimes release attachment to an object while still needing to separately address the subtler attachment to the feeling it once produced.

He reads the five destinies simply as where all such craving, indulged or restrained, actually deposits a being: gods and humans receiving agreeable karmic fruit, hell-beings, animals, and hungry ghosts receiving disagreeable fruit — the entire landscape within which every pursuit or renunciation of the five sense pleasures ultimately plays out.

36. Five Kinds of Stinginess That Cost a Practitioner Both Merit and Liberation, and the Five Hindrances That Ruin Precepts, Learning, and Meditation in Sequence

Two more items, both practical warnings: the five specific kinds of stinginess — over territory, patrons, gifts received, praise, and even the Dharma itself — that quietly undermine a practitioner's own merit and block the very generosity liberation requires; and the five hindrances, working in a strict two-stage sequence, that first ruin precepts and learning, then ruin whatever calm and insight might otherwise have been built on them.

Chinese Source

又有五種失利養因行，亦是背涅槃因行。謂若於是處受用利養，若從彼得，若所得物，若所為得，若如是得，於此諸處心生吝惜。又有五法，令修行者先毀淨戒、多聞，後虧止觀善軛。謂於諸欲中心生愛染，於能覺發憶念教授教誡者所心生瞋恚，未受尸羅令其不受；雖先受得後令棄捨，或使穿穴。耽著惛睡，恆不寂靜，染汙追悔，常懷疑惑，於所聞法不能領受；雖初領受，尋即忘失；雖不忘失，不證決定。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also five causes leading to the loss of one's own gains, which are likewise causes turning one away from nirvāṇa: begrudging the place where offerings are received, the patron from whom they come, the object received, the praise thereby gained, or the teaching thereby attained — feeling stingy in any of these five respects. There are also five things that first cause a practitioner to violate pure precepts and learning, and thereafter impair the good yoke of calm and insight: giving rise to defiled craving toward sensory desires; giving rise to hatred toward those who awaken correct mindfulness through instruction and admonition — preventing one not yet ordained from being ordained, and causing one already ordained to abandon or breach the precepts; indulging in torpor and drowsiness; being constantly unsettled; harboring defiled regret; and constantly holding doubt — such that the teaching once heard cannot be absorbed, or, if absorbed, is soon forgotten, or, if not forgotten, never yields settled certainty.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the five stinginesses, citing the *Tattvasiddhi-śāstra*'s classic list, as five subtly possessive habits that undermine generosity from within: wanting a good dwelling-place, a generous patron, an offering, or praise entirely to oneself, resenting having to share any of them with fellow practitioners, and, worst of all, hoarding Dharma knowledge rather than teaching it freely. He cites the same text's blunt verdict: such stinginess leads directly to rebirth among hungry ghosts, and — since even the willingness to share ordinary, shared possessions is already lacking — such a person has no real prospect of ever practicing the far greater generosity liberation demands, letting go of the five aggregates themselves.

He reads the five hindrances as operating in exactly two stages, not five independent problems: desire and hatred (resenting a teacher's correction) directly damage precepts, either preventing ordination outright or causing an already-ordained practitioner to abandon or compromise them; and torpor, restlessness, and doubt then damage learning itself, preventing the teaching from ever being absorbed, retained, or settled into genuine certainty even when technically remembered. Since precepts and learning are themselves the foundation calm and insight are built upon, damage at this first stage inevitably shows up later as damaged meditation — the five hindrances working as a single cascading failure, not five unrelated obstacles.

Fascicle 8 · 瑜伽師地論卷第八 · A New Topic Begins

What Restlessness Actually Is

With the sixteen-doctrine survey of fascicles 6–7 complete, the treatise opens genuinely new territory: defilement establishment, the fifth and final of the three grounds' five great lenses first promised back in fascicle 4. This page begins with affliction itself — its restless nature, and the many ways it can be classified, from a single root cause up through a hundred and twenty-eight distinct kinds.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 8 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. A New Topic Opens: Defilement Establishment, and Its Three Kinds

The treatise now opens the fifth and final of the three grounds' great lenses, promised all the way back in fascicle 4: how defilement itself arises. It names three kinds at the outset — affliction, karma, and rebirth — the very engine that keeps ordinary beings turning through birth and death.

Chinese Source

復次，云何雜染施設建立？謂由三種雜染應知。何等為三？一、煩惱雜染，二、業雜染，三、生雜染。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, how is defilement establishment to be understood? It should be understood through three kinds of defilement. What are the three? First, afflictive defilement; second, karmic defilement; third, rebirth defilement.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi frames this opening simply: every contaminated phenomenon in an ordinary being's life counts as "defiled" in this technical sense, and the entire mechanism by which beings keep circling through birth and death, life after life, breaks down into exactly these three interlocking pieces. Affliction is the root — the restless mental poisons (craving, aversion, delusion, and their many relatives) that first stir the mind out of peace. Karma is what affliction produces — the deliberate action, wholesome or not, that affliction motivates a being to actually perform. And rebirth is karma's own fruit — the new body and circumstance that karma, once ripened, delivers a being into, which in turn provides fresh occasion for affliction to stir all over again, closing the loop. He notes the treatise's own aside that even an advanced bodhisattva, from the eighth ground onward, can appear to perform action and take rebirth in this same impure world purely out of compassion, without ever being counted as "defiled" in this sense — since without any affliction left to drive it, their action and rebirth are simply skillful means, not the compulsive cycle these three terms describe for ordinary beings.

2. Affliction's Nine-Part Outline, and Its Basic Nature: Restlessness Itself

The treatise takes up the first and deepest of the three defilements — affliction — announcing a ninefold outline it will work through in turn, then opening with the single simplest thing that can be said about affliction: whenever it arises, the mind stops being still.

Chinese Source

煩惱雜染云何？嗢柁南曰：自性若分別，因位及與門，上品顛倒攝，差別諸過患。當知煩惱雜染，由自性故，分別故，因故，位故，門故，上品故，顛倒攝故，差別故，過患故，解釋應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is afflictive defilement? A summary verse states: nature, and classification, cause and stage, and gateway, superior grade, what is subsumed under distortion, differences, and faults. It should be understood that afflictive defilement is to be explained through nature, through classification, through cause, through stage, through gateway, through superior grade, through what is subsumed under distortion, through differences, and through faults.

煩惱自性者，謂若法生時，其相自然不寂靜起；由彼起故，不寂靜行相續而轉，是名略說煩惱自性。

As for affliction's own nature: whenever such a phenomenon arises, its characteristic is to arise, of its own accord, as a state of restlessness — and because it has thus arisen, a continuous current of restless activity carries on from it. This, stated briefly, is affliction's own nature.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this nine-part outline as the treatise's most systematic single treatment anywhere of what affliction actually is, promising in turn its bare nature, how many kinds it can be sorted into, what causes it, what stages it passes through, the two gateways it operates through, what its most intense form looks like, how it maps onto three kinds of fundamental distortion, its many further differences, and finally the concrete faults it produces — a far more exhaustive analysis than the piecemeal mentions of individual afflictions scattered through earlier fascicles.

He reads the opening definition of affliction's nature as deceptively simple but exact: affliction just is restlessness arising in the mind — not a restlessness caused by something else first, but restlessness as affliction's own defining character, needing no further explanation for why it disturbs. And critically, that restlessness does not end the instant it arises; it sets off a continuing current of disturbed mental and physical activity that carries on afterward, which is why a single flash of anger or craving so often leaves a person agitated long after the original trigger has passed. He cites the Abhidharma-samuccaya's own sixfold gloss on this same "restless" quality — distraction, distortion, agitation, torpor, heedlessness, and shamelessness — each a different flavor of the same basic disturbance: a scattered, unsettled mind; thought, view, or perception twisted out of true; body, speech, or mind stirred into agitation; a dulled, sunken heaviness; a failure to cultivate the wholesome and abandon the unwholesome; and an absence of any inner sense of shame — six faces of the one restlessness that affliction, by its very nature, always brings.

3. Classifying Affliction: One Root, Two Paths of Abandonment, Three Realms, Four Moral Grades

The treatise begins working through affliction's many possible classifications, starting from the simplest — treating all affliction as a single undifferentiated root of defilement — and building up through finer and finer distinctions.

Chinese Source

煩惱分別者，或立一種。謂由煩惱雜染義故。或分二種。謂見道所斷、修道所斷。或分三種。謂欲繫、色繫、無色繫。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for the classification of affliction: it may be posited as a single kind, in the sense that all affliction alike constitutes defilement. It may be divided into two kinds: what is abandoned through the path of seeing, and what is abandoned through the path of cultivation. It may be divided into three kinds: what is bound to the desire realm, what is bound to the form realm, and what is bound to the formless realm.

或分四種。謂欲繫記、無記，色繫無記，無色繫無記。

It may be divided into four kinds: what is bound to the desire realm and morally determinate, what is bound to the desire realm and morally indeterminate, what is bound to the form realm and morally indeterminate, and what is bound to the formless realm and morally indeterminate.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through each classification as a progressively finer lens on the same body of affliction. Taken as one kind, every affliction whatsoever simply shares the single property of defiling the mind — the most basic thing that can be said about any of them. Split into two, the distinction tracks how each is actually removed: the intellectually-adopted views — self-view, extreme views, view of superiority, attachment to wrong precepts, and wrong view itself, along with the craving, hatred, and conceit that ride on those views, and the ignorance bound up with them — are all severed in a single decisive stroke the moment genuine insight into the four noble truths arises, called the path of seeing; the remaining innate afflictions, present since birth without needing any view to prop them up, require the sustained, repeated effort of the path of cultivation to gradually wear away.

Split into three, affliction is sorted by which realm's craving it belongs to — desire-realm afflictions tied to sensory craving, form-realm and formless-realm afflictions tied to subtler cravings for meditative states, present even in beings who have never touched an ordinary sense-object. And split into four, the desire realm's afflictions are further divided by moral weight: the intellectually-adopted views just described count as morally "determinate" — clearly, activity unwholesome — while the desire realm's innate afflictions are comparatively milder and count as "indeterminate," alongside all of the form and formless realms' remaining afflictions (already lacking hatred entirely, since no dhyāna-attainer carries anger), which Cháng Bǎi notes still obstruct the path and so count as "obscuring" indeterminate — mild in moral flavor, yet still a real impediment to liberation.

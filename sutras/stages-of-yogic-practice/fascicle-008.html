Fascicle 8 · 瑜伽師地論卷第八 · A New Topic Begins

What Restlessness Actually Is

With the sixteen-doctrine survey of fascicles 6–7 complete, the treatise opens genuinely new territory: defilement establishment, the fifth and final of the three grounds' five great lenses first promised back in fascicle 4. This page begins with affliction itself — its restless nature, and the many ways it can be classified, from a single root cause up through a hundred and twenty-eight distinct kinds.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 8 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. A New Topic Opens: Defilement Establishment, and Its Three Kinds

The treatise now opens the fifth and final of the three grounds' great lenses, promised all the way back in fascicle 4: how defilement itself arises. It names three kinds at the outset — affliction, karma, and rebirth — the very engine that keeps ordinary beings turning through birth and death.

Chinese Source

復次，云何雜染施設建立？謂由三種雜染應知。何等為三？一、煩惱雜染，二、業雜染，三、生雜染。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, how is defilement establishment to be understood? It should be understood through three kinds of defilement. What are the three? First, afflictive defilement; second, karmic defilement; third, rebirth defilement.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi frames this opening simply: every contaminated phenomenon in an ordinary being's life counts as "defiled" in this technical sense, and the entire mechanism by which beings keep circling through birth and death, life after life, breaks down into exactly these three interlocking pieces. Affliction is the root — the restless mental poisons (craving, aversion, delusion, and their many relatives) that first stir the mind out of peace. Karma is what affliction produces — the deliberate action, wholesome or not, that affliction motivates a being to actually perform. And rebirth is karma's own fruit — the new body and circumstance that karma, once ripened, delivers a being into, which in turn provides fresh occasion for affliction to stir all over again, closing the loop. He notes the treatise's own aside that even an advanced bodhisattva, from the eighth ground onward, can appear to perform action and take rebirth in this same impure world purely out of compassion, without ever being counted as "defiled" in this sense — since without any affliction left to drive it, their action and rebirth are simply skillful means, not the compulsive cycle these three terms describe for ordinary beings.

2. Affliction's Nine-Part Outline, and Its Basic Nature: Restlessness Itself

The treatise takes up the first and deepest of the three defilements — affliction — announcing a ninefold outline it will work through in turn, then opening with the single simplest thing that can be said about affliction: whenever it arises, the mind stops being still.

Chinese Source

煩惱雜染云何？嗢柁南曰：自性若分別，因位及與門，上品顛倒攝，差別諸過患。當知煩惱雜染，由自性故，分別故，因故，位故，門故，上品故，顛倒攝故，差別故，過患故，解釋應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is afflictive defilement? A summary verse states: nature, and classification, cause and stage, and gateway, superior grade, what is subsumed under distortion, differences, and faults. It should be understood that afflictive defilement is to be explained through nature, through classification, through cause, through stage, through gateway, through superior grade, through what is subsumed under distortion, through differences, and through faults.

煩惱自性者，謂若法生時，其相自然不寂靜起；由彼起故，不寂靜行相續而轉，是名略說煩惱自性。

As for affliction's own nature: whenever such a phenomenon arises, its characteristic is to arise, of its own accord, as a state of restlessness — and because it has thus arisen, a continuous current of restless activity carries on from it. This, stated briefly, is affliction's own nature.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this nine-part outline as the treatise's most systematic single treatment anywhere of what affliction actually is, promising in turn its bare nature, how many kinds it can be sorted into, what causes it, what stages it passes through, the two gateways it operates through, what its most intense form looks like, how it maps onto three kinds of fundamental distortion, its many further differences, and finally the concrete faults it produces — a far more exhaustive analysis than the piecemeal mentions of individual afflictions scattered through earlier fascicles.

He reads the opening definition of affliction's nature as deceptively simple but exact: affliction just is restlessness arising in the mind — not a restlessness caused by something else first, but restlessness as affliction's own defining character, needing no further explanation for why it disturbs. And critically, that restlessness does not end the instant it arises; it sets off a continuing current of disturbed mental and physical activity that carries on afterward, which is why a single flash of anger or craving so often leaves a person agitated long after the original trigger has passed. He cites the Abhidharma-samuccaya's own sixfold gloss on this same "restless" quality — distraction, distortion, agitation, torpor, heedlessness, and shamelessness — each a different flavor of the same basic disturbance: a scattered, unsettled mind; thought, view, or perception twisted out of true; body, speech, or mind stirred into agitation; a dulled, sunken heaviness; a failure to cultivate the wholesome and abandon the unwholesome; and an absence of any inner sense of shame — six faces of the one restlessness that affliction, by its very nature, always brings.

3. Classifying Affliction: One Root, Two Paths of Abandonment, Three Realms, Four Moral Grades

The treatise begins working through affliction's many possible classifications, starting from the simplest — treating all affliction as a single undifferentiated root of defilement — and building up through finer and finer distinctions.

Chinese Source

煩惱分別者，或立一種。謂由煩惱雜染義故。或分二種。謂見道所斷、修道所斷。或分三種。謂欲繫、色繫、無色繫。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for the classification of affliction: it may be posited as a single kind, in the sense that all affliction alike constitutes defilement. It may be divided into two kinds: what is abandoned through the path of seeing, and what is abandoned through the path of cultivation. It may be divided into three kinds: what is bound to the desire realm, what is bound to the form realm, and what is bound to the formless realm.

或分四種。謂欲繫記、無記，色繫無記，無色繫無記。

It may be divided into four kinds: what is bound to the desire realm and morally determinate, what is bound to the desire realm and morally indeterminate, what is bound to the form realm and morally indeterminate, and what is bound to the formless realm and morally indeterminate.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through each classification as a progressively finer lens on the same body of affliction. Taken as one kind, every affliction whatsoever simply shares the single property of defiling the mind — the most basic thing that can be said about any of them. Split into two, the distinction tracks how each is actually removed: the intellectually-adopted views — self-view, extreme views, view of superiority, attachment to wrong precepts, and wrong view itself, along with the craving, hatred, and conceit that ride on those views, and the ignorance bound up with them — are all severed in a single decisive stroke the moment genuine insight into the four noble truths arises, called the path of seeing; the remaining innate afflictions, present since birth without needing any view to prop them up, require the sustained, repeated effort of the path of cultivation to gradually wear away.

Split into three, affliction is sorted by which realm's craving it belongs to — desire-realm afflictions tied to sensory craving, form-realm and formless-realm afflictions tied to subtler cravings for meditative states, present even in beings who have never touched an ordinary sense-object. And split into four, the desire realm's afflictions are further divided by moral weight: the intellectually-adopted views just described count as morally "determinate" — clearly, activity unwholesome — while the desire realm's innate afflictions are comparatively milder and count as "indeterminate," alongside all of the form and formless realms' remaining afflictions (already lacking hatred entirely, since no dhyāna-attainer carries anger), which Cháng Bǎi notes still obstruct the path and so count as "obscuring" indeterminate — mild in moral flavor, yet still a real impediment to liberation.

4. Five Truths Misunderstood, Six Basic Kinds, Seven Latent Tendencies, Eight With the Two Graspings

Chinese Source

或分五種。謂見苦所斷、見集所斷、見滅所斷、見道所斷、修道所斷。或分六種。謂貪、恚、慢、無明、見、疑。

Ru-Yi Translation

It may be divided into five kinds: what is abandoned by seeing suffering, what is abandoned by seeing its origin, what is abandoned by seeing its cessation, what is abandoned by seeing the path, and what is abandoned through cultivation. It may be divided into six kinds: craving, hatred, conceit, ignorance, view, and doubt.

或分七種。謂七種隨眠，一、欲貪隨眠，二、瞋恚隨眠，三、有貪隨眠，四、慢隨眠、五、無明隨眠，六、見隨眠，七、疑隨眠。或分八種。謂貪、恚、慢、無明、疑、見，及二種取。

It may be divided into seven kinds — seven latent tendencies: craving for desire, hatred, craving for existence, conceit, ignorance, view, and doubt. It may be divided into eight kinds: craving, hatred, conceit, ignorance, doubt, view, and the two kinds of grasping.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the fivefold division as sorting affliction by which of the four noble truths a practitioner's confusion actually centers on when the path of seeing severs it in one decisive stroke — misunderstanding suffering itself, its origin, its cessation, or the path to that cessation — with a fifth category catching everything left over for the slower work of cultivation. The sixfold division instead sorts affliction by its own basic emotional or cognitive flavor: craving, hatred, and conceit as the three classic "poisons" alongside pride; ignorance as the root delusion underlying all the others; view as the general category of every mistaken doctrinal position; and doubt as simple uncertainty about the truth of the path itself.

The sevenfold division of "latent tendencies" (anuśaya) describes the same afflictions in their dormant, seed-like state — present in the mind's underlying stream even when not actively manifesting, the way a person asleep is still capable of waking and acting, rather than simply absent. Cháng Bǎi notes the division splits craving in two here (craving for desire, tied to the desire realm, and craving for existence, tied to the subtler meditative bliss of the form and formless realms) precisely to cover all three realms within a single sevenfold scheme, rather than adding a genuinely new category. The eightfold division builds on the six basic kinds by further splitting "view" into its own three sub-kinds (self-view, extreme views, and wrong view) and adding the two forms of grasping — clinging to a view as supreme, and clinging to a particular ascetic vow or precept as itself the path to purity, the very attachment doctrine 15's animal-imitation ascetics were shown to display back in fascicle 7.

5. Nine Fetters That Bind Beings to Suffering, and the Ten Fundamental Afflictions

Chinese Source

或分九種。謂九結，一、愛結，二、恚結，三、慢結，四、無明結，五、見結，六、取結，七、疑結，八、嫉結，九、慳結。

Ru-Yi Translation

It may be divided into nine kinds — nine fetters: the fetter of craving, the fetter of hatred, the fetter of conceit, the fetter of ignorance, the fetter of view, the fetter of grasping, the fetter of doubt, the fetter of envy, and the fetter of stinginess.

或分十種。一、薩迦耶見，二、邊執見，三、邪見，四、見取，五、戒禁取，六、貪，七、恚，八、慢，九、無明，十、疑。

It may be divided into ten kinds: self-view, extreme views, wrong view, view-grasping, precept-and-vow grasping, craving, hatred, conceit, ignorance, and doubt.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses "fetter" as whatever binds a being together with suffering, and reads this ninefold list as tracking specifically social and lay-life afflictions the earlier lists left less explicit: craving toward what is liked and hatred toward what is disliked, both especially tied to household life; conceit arising whenever beings compare themselves to one another; ignorance, wrong view, and grasping arising specifically from keeping bad company and absorbing false teachings; doubt arising even toward the genuine teaching, from insufficient understanding; and — the two genuinely new additions here — envy, arising specifically among renunciants comparing their own wisdom unfavorably to a companion's, and stinginess, arising specifically among householders reluctant to part with their own possessions.

The tenfold division, he notes, is the classical and most frequently cited scheme in later Abhidharma and Yogācāra literature, splitting into two groups by how readily each can be severed: the first five — self-view, extreme views, wrong view, view-grasping, and precept-grasping — are called the "five sharp fetters," mostly arising through mistaken intellectual reflection and hence swiftly cut the moment genuine insight arises on the path of seeing; the remaining five — craving, hatred, conceit, ignorance, and doubt — are called the "five dull fetters," mostly innate rather than reasoned into, arising automatically from simple deprivation, ego, or bewilderment, and requiring the far slower, more gradual work of the path of cultivation to finally wear away.

6. The Full Tally: One Hundred and Twenty-Eight Kinds of Affliction

The treatise now builds its most exhaustive classification of all: taking the ten fundamental afflictions just given and cross-multiplying them against twelve specific "truths" spread across the three realms, arriving at the classical Abhidharma total of one hundred twenty-eight distinct afflictions a practitioner must ultimately overcome.

Chinese Source

或分一百二十八煩惱。謂即上十煩惱，由迷執十二種諦建立應知。何等名為十二種諦？謂欲界苦諦、集諦，色界苦諦、集諦，無色界苦諦、集諦。欲界增上彼遍智果，彼遍智所顯滅諦、道諦；色界增上彼遍智果，彼遍智所顯滅諦、道諦；無色界增上彼遍智果，彼遍智所顯滅諦、道諦。

Ru-Yi Translation

It may be divided into one hundred twenty-eight afflictions: the ten afflictions just given, established through confusion regarding twelve specific truths, as should be understood. What are these twelve truths? The desire realm's truth of suffering and truth of origin; the form realm's truth of suffering and truth of origin; the formless realm's truth of suffering and truth of origin; the desire realm's truth of cessation and truth of the path, manifested as the result and the very activity of complete understanding; the form realm's truth of cessation and truth of the path, likewise manifested; and the formless realm's truth of cessation and truth of the path, likewise manifested.

此中於欲界苦集諦，及於欲界增上滅道諦，具有十煩惱迷執。於色界苦集諦，及於彼增上滅道諦，除瞋，有餘煩惱迷執。如於色界，於無色界亦爾。於欲界對治修中，有六煩惱迷執。謂除邪見、見取、戒禁取、疑。

Here, with respect to the desire realm's truths of suffering and origin, and its truths of cessation and the path, all ten afflictions arise in confusion. With respect to the form realm's truths of suffering and origin, and its truths of cessation and the path, all afflictions but hatred arise in confusion. As with the form realm, so too with the formless realm. Within the desire realm's cultivation of the antidote, six afflictions arise in confusion — namely, all but wrong view, view-grasping, precept-grasping, and doubt.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through the arithmetic behind this famous total. For the path of seeing: the desire realm's four noble truths (suffering, origin, cessation, and the path) each carry the full set of ten fundamental afflictions, giving 4 × 10 = 40; the form and formless realms each likewise have four truths, but since dhyāna-attainers carry no hatred at all, each truth carries only nine afflictions, giving 4 × 9 = 36 per realm, or 72 for the two combined — bringing the path-of-seeing total to 40 + 72 = 112, the single most commonly cited number for "afflictions cut by insight" in later Buddhist literature. For the path of cultivation that follows: only the innate, non-view-based afflictions remain to be gradually worn away — self-view, extreme views, craving, hatred, conceit, and ignorance in the desire realm (six), and the same set minus hatred in each of the form and formless realms (five apiece) — giving 6 + 5 + 5 = 16. Adding the path of seeing's 112 to the path of cultivation's 16 yields the full 128, the complete inventory of affliction a practitioner must ultimately sever to reach liberation. He notes this scheme presupposes the four requisites for actually entering the path of seeing already named in the source material — keeping good company, hearing the true Dharma, reflecting on it soundly, and practicing in accordance with it — since it is precisely the absence of these four that lets all ten fundamental afflictions take root across all twelve truths in the first place.

7. The Root of All Views: Self-View, and Its Twenty Forms

Having classified affliction by number, the treatise now turns to the fourth item of its ninefold outline — the causes of each of the ten fundamental afflictions — beginning with the deepest and most consequential of all: self-view, the mistaken grasping of the five aggregates as "I" or "mine."

Chinese Source

薩迦耶見者，謂由親近不善丈夫、聞非正法、不如理作意故，及由任運失念故，等隨觀執五種取蘊，若分別、不分別染汙慧為體。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for self-view: it arises through keeping company with unwholesome persons, hearing false teachings, and unsound reasoning attention, as well as through a spontaneous lapse of mindfulness — and consists in observing and grasping, wholly or partially, the five aggregates of clinging as a self, taking as its own nature a defiled discernment, whether deliberately reasoned or unreflectively spontaneous.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes self-view carries two entirely different origins folded into one definition: a "deliberately reasoned" form, produced fresh in this life by keeping bad company, absorbing false doctrine, and reasoning unsoundly about it — precisely the process by which doctrine 4's self-positing theorists back in fascicle 6 talked themselves into their position — and a "spontaneous" form, innate and automatic, arising the moment mindfulness lapses even slightly, needing no teaching or reasoning at all, present in every unenlightened being from birth. He cites the classical count of twenty distinct forms this grasping can take: applied to each of the five aggregates in turn (form, feeling, perception, formations, consciousness), a being can hold any of four positions — that the aggregate itself is the self, that the self possesses that aggregate, that the aggregate belongs to the self, or that the self dwells within that aggregate — five aggregates times four relations yielding twenty distinct varieties of the same underlying error.

He treats the practical implication as crucial for how to actually practice: the deliberately-reasoned form, being grosser and view-based, is what the path of seeing severs in a single decisive stroke of insight; the spontaneous, innate form, far subtler and free of any dependence on doctrine, requires the sustained, repeated work of the path of cultivation to finally wear away. Citing the Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra's own guidance, he stresses that genuine practice must begin with deliberate analysis — consciously observing the five aggregates as not-self through reasoned contemplation — before any hope of reaching the deeper, non-conceptual wisdom that can uproot the innate form; a practitioner who tries to skip straight to "non-conceptual" meditation without this analytical grounding risks instead mistaking the meditating mind itself for a permanent self, a trap Venerable Miào Jìng is said to have warned his own students against repeatedly.

8. Extreme Views (Eternalism and Annihilationism), and Wrong View That Denies Cause and Effect

Chinese Source

邊執見者，謂由親近不善丈夫、聞非正法、不如理作意故，及由任運失念故，執五取蘊為我性已，等隨觀執為斷為常，若分別、不分別染汙慧為體。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for extreme views: having, through keeping bad company, hearing false teachings, unsound attention, or a spontaneous lapse of mindfulness, already grasped the five aggregates of clinging as a self's own nature, one goes on to observe and grasp that self, wholly or partially, as either annihilated or permanent — again a defiled discernment, whether deliberately reasoned or spontaneous, as its own nature.

邪見者，謂由親近不善丈夫、聞非正法、不如理作意故，撥因撥果，或撥作用，壞真實事，唯用分別染汙慧為體。

As for wrong view: it arises through keeping bad company, hearing false teachings, and unsound attention, and consists in denying cause, denying effect, or denying causal function, and undermining what is genuinely real — a defiled discernment that is always deliberately reasoned, never spontaneous, as its own nature.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats extreme views as self-view's direct offspring: having already mistaken the aggregates for a self, one then further insists that self is either permanent (never actually ending) or subject to total annihilation at death — the two positions the treatise's earlier survey worked through in exhaustive detail as doctrine 5 (permanence) and doctrine 12 (annihilation) back in fascicles 6 and 7. He notes both are simply named here as the technical, catch-all category "extreme views" (falling to one edge or the other of the middle path), with their full development left to that earlier, dedicated treatment.

Wrong view, by contrast, he identifies as the treatise's technical term specifically for doctrine 13's nihilism from fascicles 6–7: denying cause is denying that giving, honoring elders, or offerings produce any merit; denying effect is denying that wholesome or unwholesome conduct ripens into any corresponding result; denying causal function is denying real parents, real continuity between one life and the next, or any spontaneously arising intermediate-state being; and undermining what is genuinely real is specifically denying that any authentic arhat has ever actually existed. Unlike self-view and extreme views, which can arise either through reasoning or spontaneously from birth, Cháng Bǎi stresses wrong view in this strict sense is always and only produced by deliberate, if mistaken, reasoning — no infant is born already denying karma; it takes actively absorbed bad teaching and unsound thinking to arrive there.

9. View-Grasping: Insisting One's Own Mistaken View Is Supreme

Chinese Source

見取者，謂由親近不善丈夫、聞非正法、不如理作意故，以薩迦耶見、邊執見、邪見，及所依、所緣、所因俱有相應等法，比方他見，等隨觀執為最、為上、勝妙、第一，唯用分別染汙慧為體。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for view-grasping: it arises through keeping bad company, hearing false teachings, and unsound attention, and consists in comparing self-view, extreme views, and wrong view — together with their supporting faculties, objects, and seeds, and all that co-arises and operates alongside them — against the views of others, and then observing and grasping one's own, wholly or partially, as the finest, the highest, the most sublime, the very first — a defiled discernment that is always deliberately reasoned, as its own nature.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads view-grasping as a second-order affliction — not the mistaken view itself, but the further, comparative conceit of insisting that mistaken view is superior to everyone else's. He glosses the four superlatives (finest, highest, sublime, first) as tracking four distinct grounds on which a holder claims this supremacy: calling their view "sublime" or "first" because they believe indulging desire freely is itself a kind of purity; calling it "the highest" because they believe becoming free of a lower realm's desire through meditation is itself liberation; and calling it "the finest" because they believe being reborn into a still, peaceful realm is itself genuine release from saṃsāra — the very claims doctrine 15's "purity in this present life" theorists made back in fascicle 7, now identified by their underlying psychological mechanism.

He flags this as the treatise's warning about a specific danger for meditators without adequate grounding in correct view: someone who attains real meditative concentration, but without first understanding the Buddhist teaching correctly, risks mistaking that concentrated state itself for ultimate truth or liberation, then insisting — often with genuine sincerity and conviction — that their own attainment surpasses all others. Genuine meditative attainment without a foundation in sound view, he stresses, is not a safeguard against this trap but can become its very occasion.

10. Precept-and-Vow Grasping: Mistaking a Ritual Observance for the Path Itself

Chinese Source

戒禁取者，謂由親近不善丈夫、聞非正法、不如理作意故，即於彼見、彼見隨行若戒若禁，及所依、所緣、所因俱有相應等法，等隨觀執為清淨、為解脫、為出離，唯用分別染汙慧為體。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for precept-and-vow grasping: it arises through keeping bad company, hearing false teachings, and unsound attention, and consists in taking the bodily or verbal precepts and vows that follow from those very mistaken views — together with their supporting faculties, objects, seeds, and all that co-arises with them — and observing and grasping them, wholly or partially, as themselves constituting purity, liberation, or genuine release, a defiled discernment that is always deliberately reasoned, as its own nature.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes "precepts" here as the bodily and verbal restraints such a view leads someone to adopt, and "vows" as the specific outward forms, dress, diet, and deportment that follow from it — precisely the dog-vow, cow-vow, ash-vow, and self-mortification practices already catalogued in exhaustive detail as doctrine 15's false purity back in fascicle 7. Where view-grasping is the conviction that one's underlying belief is supreme, precept-and-vow grasping is the further, distinct error of mistaking the resulting ritual practice itself — this specific observance, this specific diet, this specific bodily discipline — for the actual mechanism of purification, liberation, or escape from saṃsāra, rather than recognizing it as, at best, an arbitrary or irrelevant behavior with no real causal power over affliction at all.

11. Craving and Hatred, and the Elephant Who Learned Cruelty From Its Neighbors

Chinese Source

貪者，謂由親近不善丈夫、聞非正法、不如理作意故，及由任運失念故，於外及內可愛境界，若分別、不分別染著為體。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for craving: it arises through keeping bad company, hearing false teachings, unsound attention, or a spontaneous lapse of mindfulness, and consists, whether deliberately reasoned or spontaneous, in a defiled attachment toward agreeable objects, whether external or internal to oneself.

恚者，謂由親近不善丈夫、聞非正法、不如理作意故，及由任運失念故，於外及內非愛境界，若分別、不分別憎恚為體。

As for hatred: it arises through keeping bad company, hearing false teachings, unsound attention, or a spontaneous lapse of mindfulness, and consists, whether deliberately reasoned or spontaneous, in a resentful aversion toward disagreeable objects, whether external or internal to oneself.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes "internal" objects as one's own five aggregates of clinging and "external" objects as everything else, sentient or not — craving simply being attachment toward whatever produces pleasant feeling, hatred aversion toward whatever produces painful feeling, in either domain. He illustrates hatred's malleability — how readily it grows or shrinks depending on one's surroundings — with a classical story: a king's trained execution-elephant was stationed, for a time, near a monastery, where monks recited the Dharma daily, teaching that killing leads to the lower destinies while restraint and virtue lead to a fortunate rebirth. Hearing this repeatedly, the elephant grew gentle, merely sniffing condemned prisoners brought before it and then simply walking away, refusing to harm them. Puzzled at his elephant's sudden refusal to carry out its duty, the king learned from a perceptive minister that proximity to the monastery's teaching had softened the animal's heart, and had it relocated instead near a slaughterhouse. After a period living amid the daily brutality of butchers at work, the same elephant, once returned to its former task, had become vicious, trampling and battering condemned prisoners without hesitation.

Cháng Bǎi draws the lesson directly: if sustained proximity to either wholesome or unwholesome influence can reshape even an animal's basic disposition this dramatically, the same holds all the more for craving, hatred, and every other affliction in a human being — who one keeps company with, and what one repeatedly hears and absorbs, genuinely shapes which afflictions take root and strengthen, precisely the "keeping bad company, hearing false teachings, unsound attention" causal chain the treatise has now named as the origin of every one of the ten fundamental afflictions in turn.

12. Conceit, Ignorance, and Doubt Complete the Ten Fundamental Afflictions

Chinese Source

慢者，謂由親近不善丈夫、聞非正法、不如理作意故，及由任運失念故，於外及內高下勝劣，若分別、不分別高舉為體。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for conceit: it arises through keeping bad company, hearing false teachings, unsound attention, or a spontaneous lapse of mindfulness, and consists, whether deliberately reasoned or spontaneous, in an inflated self-regard concerning relative superiority or inferiority, in age or in virtue, whether toward oneself or toward others.

無明者，謂由親近不善丈夫、聞非正法、不如理作意故，及由任運失念故，於所知事，若分別、不分別染汙無知為體。疑者，謂由親近不善丈夫、聞非正法、不如理作意故，即於所知事，唯用分別異覺為體。

As for ignorance: it arises through keeping bad company, hearing false teachings, unsound attention, or a spontaneous lapse of mindfulness, and consists, whether deliberately reasoned or spontaneous, in a defiled unknowing regarding what should be known. As for doubt: it arises through keeping bad company, hearing false teachings, and unsound attention, and consists, always through deliberate reasoning, in a wavering, contrary understanding regarding what should be known.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses conceit's comparison as running along two axes — age (a genuinely elder person versus a younger one) and virtue (genuinely greater or lesser moral accomplishment) — with the inflation lying not in noticing a real difference but in the puffed-up self-regard that notices it. "What should be known," recurring here for both ignorance and doubt, he identifies broadly as everything conditioned and unconditioned worth understanding — the four noble truths, dependent origination, the six perfections, and the whole architecture of worldly and transcendent cause and effect already covered across the treatise so far — with ignorance a simple defiled failure to grasp any of it (distinct from an ordinary, non-defiled not-knowing that carries no moral weight and obstructs nothing), and doubt a half-formed, wavering grasp that arrives at a view genuinely at odds with the truth, neither full ignorance nor full understanding.

With these three, the treatise completes its detailed account of all ten fundamental afflictions' individual nature and generative conditions — self-view, extreme views, wrong view, view-grasping, precept-grasping, craving, hatred, conceit, ignorance, and doubt — each traced back to the same underlying pattern of causes already sketched: bad company, false teaching, unsound attention, and, for the more deeply rooted among them, sheer spontaneous habit needing no external trigger at all.

13. Six Causes Behind Every Affliction — a Practical Map for Where They Actually Come From

Having detailed each affliction's own individual causes, the treatise steps back to name six general causes that apply across all of them alike — arguably the single most practically useful passage in this entire analysis, since it names exactly where a practitioner should look to actually prevent affliction from arising.

Chinese Source

煩惱因者，謂六種因。一、由所依故；二、由所緣故；三、由親近故；四、由邪教故；五、由數習故；六、由作意故。由此六因，起諸煩惱。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for the causes of affliction: there are six kinds of cause. First, through its basis; second, through its object; third, through association; fourth, through false teaching; fifth, through habituation; sixth, through attention. It is through these six causes that every affliction arises.

所依故者，謂由隨眠起諸煩惱。所緣故者，謂順煩惱境界現前。親近故者，謂由隨學不善丈夫。邪教故者，謂由聞非正法。數習故者，謂由先植數習力勢。作意故者，謂由發起不如理作意故，諸煩惱生。

Through its basis: affliction arises from its own latent seed. Through its object: an object capable of provoking affliction actually presents itself. Through association: one follows and learns from an unwholesome person. Through false teaching: one hears a teaching that is not the true Dharma. Through habituation: the accumulated force of past repetition has already been planted. Through attention: unsound reasoning attention is what actually gives rise to every affliction.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this sixfold list as the treatise's diagnostic checklist for why affliction arises at all, and reads it as building in a deliberate order from the least to the most within a practitioner's own control. The first two — the dormant seed itself, and an actual triggering object presenting itself — are largely given: a noble one with no remaining seed of craving can walk "through a field of flowers untouched by a single petal," as the classical saying goes, precisely because no seed remains for any object to trigger, however tempting; an ordinary being still carrying the seed will find almost any suitably matched object (something genuinely desirable provoking craving, something genuinely aversive provoking hatred, something neutral provoking simple confusion) sufficient to bring it to life.

The next two — association and false teaching — are squarely a matter of choice: keeping company with someone who already carries unsound views and unwholesome habits, and absorbing whatever mistaken teaching they pass along, is precisely the "near vermilion, one turns red; near ink, one turns black" principle the elephant story just illustrated, and remains within a practitioner's power to actively avoid. The fifth, habituation, names the simple compounding effect of repetition: whichever tendency, wholesome or not, has already been rehearsed most often carries the greatest force the next time a trigger appears, meaning today's practice directly determines tomorrow's vulnerability. And the sixth, unsound attention, is where Cháng Bǎi places the treatise's real point of leverage: the very same object, presented to two different minds, produces affliction in one and not the other purely depending on whether attention meets it soundly or unsoundly — reflecting on impermanence, dependent origination, or the absence of a fixed self at the moment of contact, rather than reflexively grasping or resisting, is the one link in this whole six-part chain a practitioner can intervene on directly, in the very moment an affliction would otherwise arise.

14. Seven Stages of Affliction, From Dormant Seed to Full Intensity

The treatise's fourth item, stage, sorts affliction not by kind but by how far along it has actually progressed — from a merely latent possibility all the way to its most forceful, fully manifest form.

Chinese Source

煩惱位者，略有七種。一、隨眠位，二、纏位，三、分別起位，四、俱生位，五、軟位，六、中位，七、上位。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for the stages of affliction: there are, briefly, seven. First, the stage of the latent seed; second, the stage of active manifestation; third, the stage of deliberately-reasoned arising; fourth, the stage of innate arising; fifth, the mild stage; sixth, the middling stage; seventh, the intense stage.

由二緣故，煩惱隨眠之所隨眠。一、由種子隨逐故；二、由彼增上事故。

It is through two conditions that a being remains dogged by affliction's own latent seed: first, because that seed, unless permanently severed, continues to trail along uninterrupted; second, because whenever affliction actively manifests, it further reinforces and strengthens that same seed.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the first four stages as tracking affliction's basic life-cycle — dormant as a mere seed in the ālayavijñāna, capable of manifesting but not yet doing so; actually manifest, currently disturbing body and mind; distinguished by its immediate origin as either freshly reasoned into through unsound reflection, or simply innate, arising automatically without any reasoning at all — while the final three sort any given instance by sheer intensity, mild, middling, or fully forceful. He treats the two conditions sustaining the latent seed as explaining why affliction, left unaddressed, only ever compounds rather than fading on its own: an unsevered seed persists moment to moment purely by its own causal continuity, the way an ember keeps glowing simply by not going out, and every fresh manifestation of that affliction further perfumes and strengthens the very seed that produced it — meaning each time craving or anger is indulged, the next occurrence becomes not less likely but more so, a genuinely vicious cycle that only actual severing, not mere non-expression, can break.

15. Two Gateways of Affliction: How the Manifest Form Actually Harms

The treatise's fifth item names two gateways through which affliction troubles a being — its active, manifest form and its dormant, seed form — and details five specific ways the manifest form actually does harm.

Chinese Source

煩惱門者，略由二門煩惱所惱。謂由纏門及隨眠門。纏門有五種。一、由不寂靜住故；二、由障礙善故；三、由發起惡趣惡行故；四、由攝受現法鄙賤故；五、由能感生等苦故。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for affliction's gateways: briefly, a being is troubled by affliction through two gateways — the gateway of active manifestation and the gateway of the latent seed. The gateway of active manifestation has five aspects: first, it disturbs one's abiding peace; second, it obstructs what is wholesome; third, it gives rise to unwholesome conduct leading to an unfortunate destiny; fourth, it draws present contempt upon oneself; fifth, it brings about the suffering of rebirth and the rest.

云何隨眠門所惱？謂與諸纏作所依故，及能引發生等苦故。

How is a being troubled through the gateway of the latent seed? Because it serves as the basis for every active manifestation, and because it too brings about the suffering of rebirth and the rest.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the five harms of active manifestation as a single escalating chain: affliction first simply disturbs whatever peace of mind was present; that disturbance in turn blocks wholesome states from arising at all, since a mind agitated by craving or hatred cannot simultaneously cultivate its opposite; blocked from wholesome conduct, a being is instead driven toward the ten unwholesome courses of action that lead to an unfortunate rebirth; those same actions, even within this present life, draw open contempt, confinement, or loss of one's own good standing; and the whole chain ultimately delivers the being into the wider suffering of birth, aging, sickness, and death that keeps saṃsāra turning, ever further from nirvāṇa's peace. The latent seed, though not currently active, does its own quieter harm simply by remaining present at all: it is the ready foundation from which every future manifestation can spring, and, entirely apart from ever manifesting, its mere presence is what keeps a being bound to future rebirth's suffering — the seed itself, not only its eventual sprouting, is already part of what must be uprooted.

16. Seven Further Gateways That Specifically Block the Path of Seeing and Cultivation

Chinese Source

又由七門，一切煩惱於見及修能為障礙應知。謂邪解了故，不解了故，解了不解了故，邪解了迷執故，彼因依處故，彼怖所生故，任運現行故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, it should be understood that every affliction obstructs the path of seeing and the path of cultivation through seven gateways: through mistaken understanding; through simple non-understanding; through a half-formed understanding that both understands and fails to; through mistaken understanding compounded by clinging; through serving as the mutual basis of suffering and its origin; through the fear arising from cessation and the path; and through spontaneous, innate manifestation.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps all seven gateways directly onto the ten fundamental afflictions already detailed: mistaken understanding is self-view, extreme views, and wrong view, each actively misconstruing the four noble truths; simple non-understanding is ignorance, the flat failure to grasp them at all; the half-formed understanding is doubt, wavering between grasping and missing the truth; mistaken understanding compounded by clinging is view-grasping and precept-grasping, together with craving, hatred, and conceit riding on those views — actively insisting the mistaken position is correct rather than merely holding it. The fifth and sixth gateways describe confusion about the truths in pairs rather than singly: confusion about suffering and its origin specifically as each other's mutual cause and basis (past origin producing present suffering, which in turn breeds fresh origin, round and round), and confusion about cessation and the path specifically as a source of dread — the fear, already seen in doctrine 10's deathless equivocators back in fascicle 7, that genuine cessation might mean nothing more than annihilation, making a being flinch from the very path meant to liberate them. The seventh and final gateway, spontaneous manifestation, is simply the innate afflictions arising automatically without any reasoning at all — the same distinction, seen from yet another angle, between what the path of seeing severs in a single stroke and what only the path of cultivation's sustained effort can finally remove.

17. Six Marks of the Most Intense Grade of Affliction

The treatise's sixth item names two basic marks of affliction at its most severe — fierce and especially weighty — then details six specific ways this superior grade actually manifests.

Chinese Source

云何煩惱上品相？謂猛利相及尤重相。此相略有六種。一、由犯故；謂由此煩惱纏故，毀犯一切所有學處。二、由生故；謂由此故，生於欲界苦惡趣中。三、由相續故；謂貪等行、諸根成熟少年盛壯、無涅槃法者。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the mark of affliction at its most intense grade? It is the mark of being fierce and the mark of being especially weighty. This mark, briefly, has six aspects. First, through transgression: by reason of this affliction manifesting, one violates every precept one holds. Second, through the rebirth it produces: by reason of this affliction, one is reborn into the desire realm's suffering-filled unfortunate destinies. Third, through its continuity: it is found in those whose conduct is dominated by craving and the like, whose faculties have reached the full vigor of youth or prime adulthood, and who lack any seed of awakening at all.

四、由事故者，謂緣尊重田，若緣功德田，若緣不應行田而起。五、由起惡業故者，謂由此煩惱纏故，以增上適悅心起身語業。六、由究竟故者，謂此自性上品所攝，最初軟對治道之所斷故。

Fourth, through its object: it arises directed at a field deserving reverence, a field of genuine merit, or a field that should never be treated this way at all. Fifth, through generating unwholesome action: by reason of this affliction manifesting, one performs bodily and verbal deeds with a heightened sense of pleasure and satisfaction. Sixth, through its ultimate removal: this affliction, belonging by its very nature to the superior grade, is nonetheless the very first to be severed even by the mildest degree of the antidote path.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through all six marks as jointly painting a portrait of affliction at its worst: it breaks precepts outright rather than merely straining against them; it delivers a being straight into the desire realm's lower destinies rather than a milder consequence; it takes hold most readily in someone whose craving, hatred, and delusion already dominate their conduct, whose body is at the full vigor of youth or prime adulthood, and who carries no seed at all of śrāvaka, pratyekabuddha, or unsurpassed awakening to counterbalance it; it directs itself specifically at those most deserving of reverence (parents and elders), those most deserving of respect for their merit (the Three Jewels), or the vulnerable who deserve protection rather than harm (the poor, the elderly, the orphaned) — striking precisely where it should least be aimed; it manifests not with reluctance but with active, heightened pleasure in the wrongdoing itself; and, in a genuinely counterintuitive final twist, this same grossest, most obvious grade of affliction is actually the easiest to remove, severed by even the mildest application of the antidote path precisely because it is so coarse and visible — unlike the subtler, quieter afflictions that require the antidote's fullest strength simply to be noticed at all, let alone uprooted.

18. Seven Distortions: Mistaking Permanence, Pleasure, Purity, and Self Where None Exist

The treatise's seventh item turns to one of Buddhism's most classical teachings: the seven distortions, tracing every affliction back to a basic misperception, misjudgment, and mistaken mental grasp of what is actually impermanent, suffering, impure, and without self.

Chinese Source

煩惱顛倒攝者，謂七顛倒。一、想倒，二、見倒，三、心倒，四、於無常常倒，五、於苦樂倒，六、於不淨淨倒，七、於無我我倒。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for what affliction is subsumed under distortion: it refers to seven distortions. First, distortion of perception; second, distortion of view; third, distortion of mind; fourth, distortion regarding the impermanent as permanent; fifth, distortion regarding suffering as pleasure; sixth, distortion regarding the impure as pure; seventh, distortion regarding what is without self as having a self.

想倒者，謂於無常、苦、不淨、無我中，起常、樂、淨、我妄想分別。見倒者，謂即於彼妄想所分別中，忍可欲樂，建立執著。心倒者，謂即於彼所執著中貪等煩惱。

Distortion of perception is the mistaken conceptual grasp, applied to what is actually impermanent, suffering, impure, and without self, of permanence, pleasure, purity, and self. Distortion of view is the further step of endorsing and delighting in that very mistaken conceptual grasp, and establishing and clinging to it as a position. Distortion of mind is the craving and other affliction that arises within what has thereby been clung to.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the first three distortions as a three-stage cascade, and the remaining four as simply the specific content that cascade always latches onto. Distortion of perception comes first and is the most basic: a bare conceptual misreading, taking impermanent things as permanent, painful things as pleasurable, impure things as pure, and the empty five aggregates as a genuine self — an error of raw cognition, before any doctrine or conviction has entered in. Distortion of view is perception's error hardening into an actual endorsed position — no longer just misperceiving, but actively approving of the misperception, deriving genuine satisfaction from it, and publicly asserting and defending it as correct; Cháng Bǎi notes this stage is characteristically found among non-Buddhist renunciants and the intellectually inclined, who don't merely feel the distortion but build entire doctrines to justify it. Distortion of mind is what happens once that endorsed position has taken hold in an ordinary person's heart: craving, hatred, and the rest of the afflictions simply operate within and through the now-settled distorted view, characteristically found among ordinary laypeople who never articulate the position philosophically but live it out emotionally all the same.

The remaining four distortions name the specific content misjudged at every one of these three stages alike: mistaking the impermanent five aggregates, arising and ceasing moment to moment, for something permanent and lasting; mistaking what is actually suffering — since even pleasant feeling eventually changes and vanishes — for genuine pleasure; mistaking a body of blood, bone, and waste for something pure and beautiful; and mistaking the empty, dependently-arisen aggregates for a genuine, independent self. Cháng Bǎi treats this fourfold content as ancient Buddhism's most famous single diagnosis of why affliction exists at all: strip away the four distortions, and craving, hatred, conceit, and every other affliction lose the very ground they stand on.

19. Root, Substance, and Outflow: How the Ten Fundamental Afflictions Map Onto Distortion

Chinese Source

當知煩惱略有三種。或有煩惱是倒根本，或有煩惱是顛倒體，或有煩惱是倒等流。倒根本者，謂無明。顛倒體者，謂薩迦耶見、邊執見一分、見取、戒禁取，及貪。倒等流者，謂邪見、邊執見一分、恚、慢，及疑。

Ru-Yi Translation

It should be understood that affliction, briefly, falls into three kinds: some affliction is the root of distortion, some is the very substance of distortion, and some is distortion's own outflow. The root of distortion is ignorance. The substance of distortion is self-view, one portion of extreme views, view-grasping, precept-grasping, and craving. Distortion's outflow is wrong view, the remaining portion of extreme views, hatred, conceit, and doubt.

此中薩迦耶見是無我我倒，邊執見一分是無常常倒，見取是不淨淨倒，戒禁取是於苦樂倒。貪通二種，謂不淨淨倒及於苦樂倒。

Here, self-view is the distortion of self in the non-self; one portion of extreme views is the distortion of permanence in the impermanent; view-grasping is the distortion of purity in the impure; precept-grasping is the distortion of pleasure in suffering. Craving runs through both the distortion of purity in the impure and the distortion of pleasure in suffering.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this final regrouping as showing how all ten fundamental afflictions and all four content-distortions actually fit together into one coherent structure, rather than being two separate lists. Ignorance stands alone as the root, since without it none of the four distortions could ever take hold in the first place — it is the basic not-knowing that makes every subsequent misjudgment possible. Five afflictions constitute distortion's very substance, each directly embodying one specific misjudgment: self-view is precisely the act of seeing a self where none exists; the eternalist portion of extreme views (not its annihilationist portion, which belongs elsewhere) is precisely mistaking the impermanent for permanent; view-grasping — insisting one's own view is supreme, pure, and final — is precisely mistaking something impure for pure; precept-grasping — clinging to a vow like the dog-vow or cow-vow as itself liberating — is precisely mistaking suffering (the ascetic discomfort of the vow) for pleasure (imagined liberation); and craving cuts across both of these last two categories at once, since craving is what makes both "this vow is pure" and "this indulgence is pleasurable" feel compelling.

The remaining afflictions — wrong view, the annihilationist portion of extreme views, hatred, conceit, and doubt — count only as distortion's "outflow," a secondary consequence rather than the distortion's own direct substance: they arise once the core distortions are already in place, riding on their momentum rather than constituting a fresh mistake of their own kind. With this threefold structure — one root, five substances, five outflows — the treatise completes its account of how affliction and distortion interlock, the seventh item of its ninefold outline, and turns next to affliction's further differences and its concrete faults.

20. Twenty-Six Names for Affliction, Part One: Fetter, Flood, Yoke, Thorn-Stump, and More

The treatise's eighth item, differences, catalogues twenty-six traditional names for affliction, each capturing a different facet of how it actually operates — a vivid, image-rich vocabulary drawn from across the Buddhist canon rather than a single technical term.

Chinese Source

煩惱差別者，多種差別應知。謂結、縛、隨眠、隨煩惱、纏、暴流、枙、取、繫、蓋、株杌、垢、常害、箭，如是等類煩惱差別。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for the differences among afflictions: their many distinctions should be understood. They are called fetter, bond, latent tendency, secondary affliction, wrapping, flood, yoke, grasping, tie, hindrance, thorn-stump, stain, constant harm, and arrow — differences of this kind among the afflictions.

當知此中，能和合苦，故名為結。令於善行不隨所欲，故名為縛。一切世間增上種子之所隨逐，故名隨眠。倒染心故，名隨煩惱。數起現行，故名為纏。深難渡故，順流漂故，名暴流。邪行方便，故名為枙。能取自身相續不絕，故名為取。難可解脫，故名為繫。覆真實義，故名為蓋。壞善稼田，故名株杌。自性染汙，故名為垢。常能為害故，名為常害。不靜相故，遠所隨故，名為箭。

It is called a fetter because it binds one together with suffering. It is called a bond because it prevents wholesome conduct from proceeding as one wishes. It is called a latent tendency because it is the dominant seed that trails after every being throughout the world. It is called a secondary affliction because it distorts and defiles the mind. It is called a wrapping because it manifests again and again. It is called a flood because it is deep, hard to cross, and carries one helplessly along with its current. It is called a yoke because it is the very mechanism of wrongdoing. It is called grasping because it seizes hold of one's own continuity without interruption. It is called a tie because it is so difficult to be freed from. It is called a hindrance because it conceals what is genuinely real. It is called a thorn-stump because it ruins a good field for planting. It is called a stain because its very nature is impure. It is called constant harm because it never ceases to injure. It is called an arrow because of its restless character and how relentlessly it trails after one.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this list as evidence that the canon never settled on one single word for affliction because no single image captures everything it does — each name isolates one specific harmful mechanism. A "fetter" binds one to future suffering; a "bond" specifically blocks wholesome deeds like giving or precept-keeping from unfolding freely; a "latent tendency" is affliction dormant as a seed, present in every being who has not yet abandoned craving for desire, existence, or a mistaken sense of holy conduct; a "flood" is deep and swift enough to sweep a struggling being helplessly along — he cites the Buddha's own answer to a non-Buddhist critic who once demanded he simply remove all doubt and affliction by fiat: the Buddha replied that he had no such power over another's mind, only the power to show the way — actual crossing of the flood requires the practitioner's own insight and effort.

A "yoke" harnesses a being to wrongful action the way a wooden collar harnesses an ox to a cart; "grasping" is specifically the four kinds of clinging (to desire, to view, to precept-and-vow, to a doctrine of self) that keep pulling a being back into further existence; a "thorn-stump" ruins fertile ground the way stumps left in a salt-flat field prevent any crop from growing — Cháng Bǎi cites a well-known verse comparing an untended mind to a field overgrown with the weeds of ignorance, unable to bloom into wisdom, as a folk illustration of the same image; and an "arrow," once lodged, keeps a being in restless agony the way an unremoved poisoned arrow-wound throbs and festers long after the initial strike.

21. Twenty-Six Names, Part Two: Root, Fire, Poverty, and Thicket — the List Closes

Chinese Source

所有、根、惡行、漏、匱、燒、惱、有諍、火、熾然、稠林、拘礙，如是等類煩惱差別。能攝依事，故名所有。不善所依，故名為根。邪行自性，故名惡行。流動其心，故名為漏。

Ru-Yi Translation

Possession, root, misconduct, outflow, poverty, burning, distress, contention, fire, blazing, thicket, and obstruction — further differences of this kind among the afflictions. It is called possession because it lays hold of the very basis one depends on. It is called root because it is the source of every unwholesome deed. It is called misconduct because wrongful action is simply its own nature made visible. It is called outflow because it sets the mind adrift, flowing restlessly toward one object after another.

能令受用無有厭足，故名為匱。能令所欲常有匱乏，故名為燒。能引衰損，故名為惱。能為鬥訟諍競之因，故名有諍。燒所積集諸善根薪，故名為火。如大熱病，故名熾然。種種自身大樹聚集，故名稠林。能令眾生樂著種種妙欲塵故，能障證得出世法故，名為拘礙。

It is called poverty because it leaves one forever unsatisfied no matter how much is consumed. It is called burning because it makes what one desires seem perpetually lacking. It is called distress because it brings on sorrow and loss. It is called contention because it is the very cause of quarrels, lawsuits, and rivalry. It is called fire because it burns up the accumulated firewood of one's own wholesome roots. It is called blazing because it is like a raging fever consuming body and mind alike. It is called thicket because it produces one body after another, life after life, dense as a forest of trees. And it is called obstruction because it makes beings delight in exquisite sensory objects while blocking them from ever realizing the transcendent path.

諸如是等煩惱差別，佛薄伽梵隨所增彊，於彼種種煩惱門中建立差別。

All these differences among the afflictions were established by the Blessed Buddha according to whichever aspect happened to be most pronounced in each particular gateway through which affliction operates.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this second half as, if anything, even more psychologically pointed than the first: "possession" names how thoroughly affliction takes over a being's own five aggregates, as though claiming ownership of the very body and mind it inhabits; "poverty" and "burning" together capture the peculiar logic of craving, where consumption never produces satisfaction but only sharpens the sense of lack — he cites the classical teaching that a person who is never satisfied remains genuinely poor no matter their wealth, while a person genuinely content is wealthy regardless of material means; "fire" gives one of the list's most vivid images, affliction consuming a lifetime's carefully accumulated merit the way a single spark can consume a whole woodpile, which he ties to the old warning that a single flash of anger can burn down a forest of merit built up over ages.

"Thicket" describes how affliction, left unchecked, doesn't merely repeat itself but compounds into an entire dense growth of successive rebirths, each life feeding the next; and "obstruction" closes the list by naming affliction's most basic double action, simultaneously pulling a being toward what is pleasant and blocking the very path that would lead beyond needing it at all. Cháng Bǎi reads the closing line — that the Buddha named each synonym according to whichever quality was most pronounced in a given context — as explaining why the canon uses so many different words for what is, at bottom, a single underlying reality: no single name could ever capture all twenty-six facets of how affliction actually operates on a mind, so the Buddha simply reached for whichever image best illuminated the specific danger he was warning against in a given teaching.

22. Each Name's Actual Headcount: Nine Fetters, Eight Wrappings, Five Hindrances, and More

Having named all twenty-six terms for affliction, the treatise now shows precisely how many specific afflictions each name actually covers — grounding the poetic vocabulary in concrete, countable lists, several already familiar and one, the five hindrances, among Buddhist meditation's most foundational categories.

Chinese Source

結者，九結。縛者，三縛。謂貪瞋癡。隨眠者，七種隨眠。隨煩惱者，三隨煩惱。謂貪瞋癡。纏者，八纏。謂無慚、無愧、惛沈、睡眠、掉舉、惡作、嫉妒、慳吝。暴流者，四暴流。謂欲暴流、有暴流、見暴流、無明暴流。如暴流，枙亦爾。

Ru-Yi Translation

Fetter comprises nine fetters. Bond comprises three: craving, hatred, and delusion. Latent tendency comprises the seven latent tendencies. Secondary affliction comprises three: craving, hatred, and delusion. Wrapping comprises eight: lack of shame, lack of conscience, torpor, drowsiness, agitation, remorse, envy, and stinginess. Flood comprises four: the flood of desire, the flood of existence, the flood of view, and the flood of ignorance. As with flood, so too with yoke.

取者，四取。謂欲取、見取、戒禁取、我語取。蓋者，五蓋。謂貪欲蓋、瞋恚蓋、惛沈睡眠蓋、掉舉惡作蓋、疑蓋。株杌者，三株杌。謂貪瞋癡。如株杌，如是垢、常害、箭、所有、惡行亦爾。根者，三不善根。漏者，三漏。謂欲漏、有漏、無明漏。

Grasping comprises four: grasping desire, grasping view, grasping precept-and-vow, and grasping the doctrine of self. Hindrance comprises five: the hindrance of sensory desire, the hindrance of malice, the hindrance of torpor and sleep, the hindrance of agitation and remorse, and the hindrance of doubt. Thorn-stump comprises three: craving, hatred, and delusion — and as with thorn-stump, so too with stain, constant harm, arrow, possession, and misconduct. Root comprises the three unwholesome roots. Outflow comprises three: the outflow of desire, the outflow of existence, and the outflow of ignorance.

匱者，三匱。謂貪瞋癡。如匱，如是燒、惱、有諍、火、熾然、稠林亦爾。拘礙者，有五拘礙。一、顧戀其身，二、顧戀諸欲，三、樂相雜住，四、闕隨順教，五、得微少善便生喜足。

Poverty comprises three: craving, hatred, and delusion — and as with poverty, so too with burning, distress, contention, fire, blazing, and thicket. Obstruction comprises five: attachment to one's own body; attachment to sensory desires; delighting in mixed company; lacking proper guidance; and feeling satisfied and ceasing effort after only a slight attainment.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi highlights how many of the twenty-six names, once actually counted out, reduce to the same simple triad — craving, hatred, and delusion, the three root poisons appearing again and again under names like thorn-stump, stain, constant harm, arrow, possession, misconduct, poverty, burning, distress, contention, fire, blazing, and thicket — showing that much of this elaborate vocabulary is really just repeated emphasis on the same three fundamentals viewed through different metaphors, rather than genuinely new content each time.

He flags the "hindrances" (蓋) as this passage's most practically important entry, since the five hindrances — sensory desire, ill will, torpor and drowsiness, restlessness and remorse, and doubt — are the single most commonly cited obstacle-list in meditation instruction across the whole of Buddhist literature: sensory desire specifically blocks someone from even entering renunciant life in the first place; ill will surfaces once already practicing, resisting correction from a teacher; torpor and drowsiness directly block the development of calm-abiding; restlessness and remorse directly block the development of insight; and doubt blocks confident engagement with the teaching itself, leaving a practitioner wavering rather than committed. He likewise draws attention to the "eight wrappings" as covering exactly the mental factors most disruptive to ethical and meditative life at once — shamelessness and lack of conscience undermining precept-keeping, torpor and drowsiness undermining calm, agitation and regret undermining insight, and envy and stinginess undermining the generosity a genuine practitioner needs when it comes to either resources or the resource of teaching material.

Finally, he reads the "five obstructions" as together diagnosing exactly what keeps an otherwise sincere practitioner stuck at a plateau: clinging to comfort of body and to sensory pleasure, preferring casual mixed company over serious practice, lacking a genuine teacher's guidance, and — perhaps most subtly dangerous of all — settling contentedly for a minor attainment rather than continuing to push further, mistaking a small taste of calm for the entire path already walked.

23. Countless Faults, and Afflictive Defilement Is Fully Complete

The treatise's ninth and final item traces affliction's harm through a single cascading chain, from the first stirring of disturbance all the way to a being's ultimate failure to reach nirvāṇa's own supreme good — closing out the entire ninefold analysis of affliction that has run since this fascicle's opening.

Chinese Source

煩惱過患者，當知諸煩惱有無量過患。謂煩惱起時，先惱亂其心，次於所緣發起顛倒，令諸隨眠皆得堅固，令等流行相續而轉。能引自害、能引他害、能引俱害；生現法罪、生後法罪、生俱法罪；令受彼生身心憂苦；能引生等種種大苦；能令相續遠涅槃樂；能令退失諸勝善法；能令資財衰損散失。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for the faults of affliction: it should be understood that affliction carries countless faults. When affliction first arises, it disturbs the mind; next, it gives rise to a distorted grasp of its object; it makes the latent seeds ever more firmly entrenched; and it sets an ongoing current of similarly-flavored activity in motion. It brings about harm to oneself, harm to others, and harm to both alike; it generates guilt in this present life, guilt in a future life, and guilt spanning both; it subjects one to bodily and mental suffering in the rebirth that follows; it brings about birth and every other kind of great suffering; it keeps one's continuum ever distant from nirvāṇa's own bliss; it causes the loss of superior wholesome states already attained; and it causes one's own wealth and resources to decline and be scattered.

能令入眾不得無畏，悚懼無威；能令鄙惡名稱流布十方，常為智者之所訶毀；令臨終時生大憂悔；令身壞已墮諸惡趣，生那落迦中；令不證得自勝義利。如是等過無量無邊。

It causes one to enter an assembly without confidence, timid and lacking any presence; it causes a base, disgraceful reputation to spread in every direction, drawing the constant censure of the wise; it brings on deep regret and sorrow at the moment of death; it causes one, once the body has perished, to fall into an unfortunate destiny, reborn in the hells; and it prevents one from ever realizing one's own highest, ultimate good. Faults of this kind are without measure and without limit.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this list as a single continuous chain rather than a scattered inventory: disturbance leads to distorted perception, distorted perception reinforces the very seeds that produced it, and those strengthened seeds set off a self-perpetuating current of further similar activity — the same self-reinforcing cycle already described under affliction's stages. From there the harm radiates outward in three directions at once (self, other, or both) and across three timeframes at once (present guilt, future guilt, or both), delivering the bodily and mental suffering of an unwanted rebirth, and, more broadly still, the entire round of birth, aging, sickness, and death that keeps a being perpetually distant from nirvāṇa's peace. He notes the list's final turn toward this-worldly consequence deliberately widens the lens beyond the purely spiritual: affliction visibly damages a person's finances, their standing among peers, their reputation, and even their composure at the very moment of death — practical, observable harms meant to persuade even a listener not yet moved by talk of future lives or ultimate liberation.

He singles out the closing phrase, "prevents realizing one's own highest, ultimate good," as the summary verdict beneath every other item on the list: nirvāṇa itself, free of craving, hatred, and delusion, genuinely deserving to be loved and entirely free of fault, is what affliction, by its very nature, keeps permanently out of reach. Citing the classical teaching that self-grasping is the deepest root beneath every other affliction on this entire ninefold survey — present as an innate companion of the mind long before any doctrine or reasoning ever enters in — he notes that no amount of outward accomplishment can dislodge it; only the sustained cultivation of insight into non-self, the very practice the treatise's own earlier account of self-view pointed toward, can finally reach that root. With this ninth and final item, the treatise's entire ninefold analysis of affliction — its nature, classification, causes, stages, gateways, superior grade, distortion, differences, and now these faults — comes to its complete close.

24. Karmic Defilement Opens: What Action Itself Actually Is

With affliction fully analyzed, the treatise turns to the second of the three defilements: karma itself, given the identical ninefold treatment just completed for affliction, beginning with the most basic question of all — what karma's own nature actually consists of.

Chinese Source

云何業雜染？嗢柁南曰：自性若分別，因位及與門，增上品顛倒，差別諸過患。當知業雜染，由自性故，分別故，因故，位故，門故，上品故，顛倒故，差別故，過患故，解釋應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is karmic defilement? A summary verse states: nature, and classification, cause and stage, and gateway, superior grade, distortion, differences, and faults. It should be understood that karmic defilement is to be explained through nature, through classification, through cause, through stage, through gateway, through superior grade, through distortion, through differences, and through faults.

業自性云何？謂若法生時，造作相起；及由彼生故，身行、語行於彼後時造作而轉；是名業自性。

What is karma's own nature? It is that, when this phenomenon arises, the mark of active volition arises; and, on the basis of that arising, bodily activity and verbal activity subsequently proceed to act it out. This is called karma's own nature.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies "this phenomenon" as specifically volition (cetanā), the mental factor at the very root of karma, unfolding in three successive moments: a deliberative moment weighing the object under consideration, a decisive moment settling on a course of action, and a motivating moment that actually sets bodily or verbal activity into motion. He glosses the "mark of active volition" through its five characteristic modes: simply attending to an object as it presents itself; moving toward union with what is found agreeable; moving toward separation from what is found disagreeable; actively generating defiled karma once craving or aversion has taken hold; and, in a genuine practitioner whose mind is no longer bound by affliction, operating instead with complete freedom, entering meditative states at will rather than being dragged about by compulsion.

He treats deliberative and decisive volition together as "intentional karma" proper — the mental act itself, before anything visible occurs — and the subsequent bodily and verbal activity it triggers as "karma already acted upon," physical breath and bodily movement on one side, the inner deliberation that precedes speech on the other. He cites the classical teaching that a single stirring thought is already karma in the making, whether or not it is ever spoken or acted out — precisely why this analysis begins with volition itself, the seed from which every visible deed eventually grows, rather than with the visible deed alone.

25. Karma's Classification Opens: The Ten Wholesome and Ten Unwholesome Courses of Action

The treatise's second item classifies karma two ways — by the person who performs it and by the deed's own nature — then names Buddhism's most foundational ethical framework: the ten unwholesome courses of action and their ten wholesome opposites.

Chinese Source

業分別云何？謂由二種相應知。一、由補特伽羅相差別故；二、由法相差別故。此復二種，即善不善十種業道。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the classification of karma? It should be understood through two aspects: first, through the differing character of the person who performs it; second, through the differing character of the deed's own nature. This latter, in turn, comprises two sets: the ten wholesome and ten unwholesome courses of action.

所謂殺生，離殺生；不與取，離不與取；欲邪行，離欲邪行；妄語，離妄語；離間語，離離間語；麤惡語，離麤惡語；綺語，離綺語；貪欲，離貪欲；瞋恚，離瞋恚；邪見，離邪見。

Namely: killing, and abstaining from killing; taking what is not given, and abstaining from it; sexual misconduct, and abstaining from it; false speech, and abstaining from it; divisive speech, and abstaining from it; harsh speech, and abstaining from it; frivolous speech, and abstaining from it; covetousness, and abstaining from it; ill will, and abstaining from it; wrong view, and abstaining from it.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats "karma-path" as a deliberately chosen term: like a road, a deed of this kind actually leads somewhere — the ten unwholesome paths toward the three lower destinies, the ten wholesome paths toward a human or heavenly rebirth. He organizes the ten by which of the three doors of action produces them: three belong to the body (killing, taking what is not given, and sexual misconduct); four belong to speech (false, divisive, harsh, and frivolous speech); and three belong to the mind alone (covetousness, ill will, and wrong view) — each paired with its own wholesome opposite, giving twenty possibilities in total from a single tenfold framework. He flags the mental three as distinctive: unlike bodily and verbal karma, they require no outward action at all to already count as karma, since covetousness is simply craving co-arising with volition, ill will simply hatred co-arising with volition, and wrong view specifically the kind actively taught and spread to influence others' thinking — the treatise having already devoted an entire fascicle's survey (doctrines 6–7) to exactly this last category in its most developed non-Buddhist forms.

26. The Killer's Psychological Portrait, and Why No Life Falls Outside Compassion's Reach

The treatise now works through the first course of action, killing, in unusually vivid psychological detail — a scriptural portrait of what actually goes on in a killer's mind — before refuting two non-Buddhist attempts to draw an arbitrary line around which lives deserve protection.

Chinese Source

補特伽羅相差別建立者，謂如經言：諸殺生者，乃至廣說。最極暴惡者，謂殺害心正現前故。血塗其手者，謂為成殺，身相變故。害極害執者，謂斷彼命故，解支節故，計活命故。無有羞恥者，謂自罪生故。無有哀愍者，謂引彼非愛故。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for establishing differences by the character of the person: as the sūtra says of killers, stated in full. "Utterly savage" means the intention to kill stands directly present before the mind. "Hands smeared with blood" means the body's very appearance is transformed in the act of accomplishing the killing. "Harming, extreme harming, and clinging to it" means taking a life, dismembering the body limb from limb, and adopting killing itself as one's livelihood. "Without shame" means the guilt is self-inflicted and unacknowledged. "Without compassion" means bringing about what is thoroughly unwanted for another.

有出家外道，名曰無繫，彼作是說：百踰繕那內所有眾生，於彼律儀，若不律儀。為治彼故，說如是言：一切有情所。即彼外道復作是說：樹等外物亦有生命。為治彼故，說如是言：真實眾生所。

There is a non-Buddhist renunciant order called the Unbound, who hold: within a hundred yojanas of where one dwells, restraint from killing constitutes moral discipline, and killing beyond it does not violate any discipline at all. To counter this, the scripture says: toward all sentient beings. That same order further holds: trees and other external things likewise possess life. To counter this, the scripture says: toward genuinely sentient beings.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the scriptural portrait as tracing the killer's psychology in a deliberate sequence: intention first, fully present in the mind before any act occurs; the physical transformation of actually committing the act, blood visibly on the hands; the act itself compounding into extremity — not merely taking life but dismembering it, and, worse still, adopting killing as a settled occupation rather than an isolated lapse; and finally the two moral failures that let all of this continue unchecked — no shame at what has been done, and no compassion for what the victim actually suffers. He notes the sequence moves from a single act to an entrenched way of life, precisely the pattern doctrine 8's ritual-killing apologists back in fascicle 7 exemplified at scale, dressing an entrenched occupation of slaughter in theological justification.

He identifies the "Unbound" as a specific historical non-Buddhist ascetic order and reads their two claims as opposite errors on the same basic mistake — drawing an arbitrary boundary around which lives actually matter. The first claim, that only beings within roughly a thousand miles of one's dwelling place fall under any restraint against killing, the treatise counters by simply insisting on "all sentient beings" without qualification, since compassion that stops at a fixed distance was never really compassion at all, only a convenient local custom mistaken for an ethical principle. The second claim, that plants and trees possess life equal to animals and deserve the same protection, the treatise counters from the opposite direction — not by narrowing compassion further, but by clarifying its actual object: genuine ethical concern applies to what can genuinely suffer, to beings with sentience and feeling, not to insentient matter merely because it grows and dies; treating a cut blade of grass as morally equivalent to a slaughtered animal, however well-intentioned, actually dilutes real compassion rather than extending it, since it fails to distinguish where suffering can and cannot actually occur.

27. Indiscriminate Killing, the Door Still Open to Change, and a Story of Fleas Left Unharmed

The treatise completes its portrait of the killer with the most extreme case — killing that spares nothing, down to the smallest insect — then, notably, leaves the door open: a killer remains only a killer until the moment good conditions actually turn them away from it.

Chinese Source

乃至極下捃多蟻等諸眾生所者，此句顯示無擇殺害。於殺生事若未遠離者，此顯遇緣容可出離。謂乃至未遠離來，名殺生者。

Ru-Yi Translation

"Down to the very lowest, even ants and the like" — this phrase shows killing with no discrimination at all. "As long as one has not yet abandoned the act of killing" — this shows that, given the right conditions, one may still turn away from it. That is: only up until one has actually abandoned it does one continue to be called a killer.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads "indiscriminate killing" as the portrait's final, darkest note — a mind so given over to killing that it draws no line even at an ant, the smallest and least threatening of creatures, showing the habit has become entirely unmoored from any actual justification (self-defense, food, provocation) and become simply reflexive. Yet he stresses the very next clause deliberately refuses to make this a permanent identity: "killer" describes someone only for as long as the act continues unaddressed — the moment a person hears the true Dharma, genuinely understands its teaching on the harm of killing, and takes up the precept against it with real resolve, the label itself no longer applies, however extensive their past conduct.

He illustrates this redemptive turn with a story of a man, not identified as any particular religious follower, who from childhood could never bring himself to kill even the fleas that infested his sleeping mat in an age before modern bedding — rather than crushing them, he would carefully relocate each one when he changed clothes. In time, his own dwelling became mysteriously free of fleas for days at a stretch, as though the insects themselves recognized they had nothing to fear from him; friends staying at flea-ridden inns took to borrowing his presence for a few nights to clear the pests out. Cháng Bǎi draws the same lesson repeatedly offered by practitioners who release captured animals back into the wild: every being, however small, values its own life and can sense, in its own way, whether it is safe in a given presence — genuine non-harming is recognized even by creatures with no capacity for language or doctrine.

28. Theft's Fourteen-Part Anatomy: What Actually Counts as Taking What Is Not Given

The treatise turns to the second unwholesome course of action, theft, working through fourteen distinct elements that together define when taking something actually constitutes the offense.

Chinese Source

不與取者者，此是總句。於他所有者，謂他所攝財穀等事。若在聚落者，謂即彼事於聚落中，若積集、若移轉。若閑靜處者，謂即彼事於閑靜處，若生、若集，或復移轉。即此名為可盜物數者，謂所不與、不捨、不棄物。

Ru-Yi Translation

"One who takes what is not given" is the summary phrase. "Belonging to another" means wealth, grain, or similar goods that belong to someone else. "Within a settlement" means that very item, within a settlement, being either gathered together or moved elsewhere. "Within a secluded place" means that very item, within a secluded place, whether growing, gathered, or moved elsewhere. "What counts as stealable" refers to what its owner has not given, not relinquished, and not discarded.

若自執受者，謂執為己有。不與而取者，謂彼或時資具闕少，執為己有。不與而樂者，謂樂受行偷盜事業。於所不與、不捨、不棄而生希望者，謂劫盜他欲為己有。

"Claiming it for oneself" means taking hold of it as one's own possession. "Taking without it being given" means that, lacking some necessity, one appropriates it as one's own. "Delighting in taking without it being given" means taking pleasure in the very activity of stealing. "Harboring a hope toward what has not been given, relinquished, or discarded" means intending to seize what belongs to another and make it one's own.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this opening set of elements as establishing theft's basic scope before turning to the mental states that actually constitute the offense. "Belonging to another" covers both inanimate goods (treasure, grain, cloth, tools, incense, garlands, and the whole range of edible and drinkable produce) and, notably, what the source material calls "sentient possessions" — another person's spouse, children, servants, or livestock — extending the concept of theft well beyond simple property to include any relationship of belonging a person or family holds. "Within a settlement" and "within a secluded place" together ensure the offense applies regardless of setting, whether crowded or remote — he notes even taking a stone or plant from a public park or seashore counts, since ownership does not require witnesses, a point he says many people overlook.

"What counts as stealable" gives the offense's actual legal-ethical boundary: an item only becomes theft's object if its owner has neither given it away, formally relinquished it, nor abandoned it outright — genuinely ownerless or freely offered goods fall outside the category entirely. The remaining elements then trace the mental side of the act: physically claiming something as one's own; taking it specifically because one lacks it oneself; taking it purely for the pleasure of the act, independent of any actual need; and, subtlest of all, simply harboring the settled intention to take what is not one's own, which the treatise counts as already constituting a form of the offense even before any physical act occurs — the same principle already established for karma's own nature, that volition itself is where karma genuinely begins.

29. Theft's Remaining Elements, and How the Portraits of Wrongdoing Actually Work

The treatise completes theft's fourteen-part anatomy — extortion, greed, disputed and tainted acquisition, and the resulting karmic penalty — then clarifies an important point about the whole method used across every course of action: these vivid portraits describe the wrongdoer, not the abstract nature of the deed itself.

Chinese Source

若彼物主非先所與，如酬債法，是名不與。若彼物主於彼取者而不捨與，是名不捨。若彼物主於諸眾生不隨所欲受用而棄，是名不棄。自為而取者，謂不與而取故，及不與而樂故。饕餮而取者，謂所不與、不捨、不棄而希望故。

Ru-Yi Translation

"Not given" means the owner never originally intended to give it, yet is compelled to hand it over as though repaying a debt. "Not relinquished" means the owner, even toward the one taking it, has not actually released it. "Not discarded" means the owner has not abandoned it for anyone at all who might wish to use it. "Taking on one's own initiative" means taking it without it being given, and delighting in doing so. "Taking with sheer greed" means harboring the desire for what has been neither given, relinquished, nor discarded.

不清而取者，謂於所競物為他所勝，不清雪故。不淨而取者，謂雖勝他，而為過失垢所染故。有罪而取者，謂能攝受現法後法非愛果故。於不與取若未遠離者，如前殺生相說應知。

"Taking without vindication" means, in a dispute over a contested item, losing to another party and failing to clear one's own claim. "Taking with impurity" means winning such a dispute, yet through means tainted by wrongdoing. "Taking that is criminal" means an act that draws an undesired result, both in this present life and in lives to come. "Not yet having abandoned taking what is not given" should be understood exactly as with the killer described above.

又此中亦顯不與取者相，非不與取法相。當知餘亦爾。

What has been shown here, too, is the character of the person who takes what is not given — not the abstract nature of the deed of taking itself. The same should be understood to hold for every remaining course of action as well.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the final six elements as covering theft's less obvious forms, beyond simple grab-and-run: outright extortion, where a person is coerced into handing something over as though it were a debt they never actually owed; sheer greedy craving for what has plainly not been offered; and — his own particular emphasis — the two "dispute" cases, where even a rightful owner acquires blame if they lose a legitimate claim and fail to formally clear it, or win a claim through tainted, improper means. He treats this pair as a genuinely subtle warning: fairness in acquiring even one's own property matters, not only outright theft of another's; taking a shortcut to secure what is rightfully yours, if the method itself is improper, still counts as a moral failing, even though the resulting possession is not, strictly, theft in the ordinary sense.

He treats the closing clarification — that all of this describes the person, not the deed's own abstract nature — as a methodological key applying retroactively to the killer's portrait as well as forward to every remaining course of action still to come: the treatise's detailed psychological catalogues (utterly savage, blood-smeared hands, taking through extortion, delighting in greed) are meant to help a listener actually recognize wrongdoing as it manifests in a real person's conduct and character, a diagnostic tool for self-examination and for judging others' actions fairly, while the deed's own technical nature — what specifically constitutes killing or theft as an abstract category, independent of who commits it — will be addressed separately later. The two lenses, person and deed, together give a complete account neither alone could provide.

30. Sexual Misconduct Opens: Who Falls Under Another's Protection

The treatise turns to the third bodily course of action, sexual misconduct, opening with a careful survey of exactly whose protection an unmarried woman may fall under — the first of three broad categories this course of action will be built from.

Chinese Source

於諸父母等所守護者，猶如父母於己處女，為適事他故，勤加守護，時時觀察，不令與餘共為鄙穢。若彼沒已，復為至親兄弟姊妹之所守護。此若無者，復為餘親之所守護。此若無者，恐損家族，便自守護。或彼舅姑，為自兒故，勤加守護。

Ru-Yi Translation

"Under the protection of parents and the like": just as parents diligently protect their own unmarried daughter, watching over her constantly so that she may properly be given in marriage, and do not allow her to be defiled with another. Should the parents have passed away, she is then protected by her closest siblings. Lacking these, she is protected by more distant relatives. Lacking these too, fearing harm to her family's honor, she protects herself. Or an uncle or aunt, treating her as their own child, diligently protects her.

有治罰者，謂諸國王，若執理者，以治罰法而守護故。有障礙者，謂守門者所守護故。此中略顯未適他者三種守護。一、尊重至親眷屬自己之所守護，二、王執理家之所守護，三、諸守門者之所守護。

"Under legal protection": kings and magistrates protect her by means of the law's penalties. "Under physical safeguard": gatekeepers protect her by their watch. This briefly shows three kinds of protection for an unmarried woman: first, protection by revered close relatives or by herself; second, protection by the king or ruling authority; third, protection by those who guard the gate.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this opening survey as establishing, before anything else, precisely who counts as under someone else's protection — a chain of responsibility cascading from parents, to siblings, to more distant relatives, to self-protection, to an aunt or uncle standing in as substitute parents, ensuring no unmarried woman ever falls entirely outside anyone's care. Legal protection and gatekeeping protection then extend this same principle beyond the family into the wider civic order — the state's law and a household's own physical security both serving the identical underlying purpose. He notes the passage's underlying logic is not merely procedural but relational: violating any of these three kinds of protection is not simply an offense against the woman herself but a betrayal of the trust every one of these protective relationships represents.

31. Sexual Misconduct's Remaining Seven Elements: Coercion, Secrecy, and Wrong Time, Place, or Manner

Chinese Source

他妻妾者，謂已適他。他所攝者，謂即未適他，為三守護之所守護。若由凶詐者，謂矯亂已而行邪行。若由彊力者，謂對父母等公然彊逼。若由隱伏者，謂不對彼，竊相欣欲。

Ru-Yi Translation

"Another's wife or concubine" means one already given in marriage. "Belonging to another" means one not yet married, but under the three kinds of protection just described. "Through deception" means beguiling and confusing her before committing the act. "Through force" means openly coercing her parents and the like. "Through concealment" means, without informing them, secretly indulging mutual desire.

而行欲行者，謂兩兩交會。即於此事非理欲心而行邪行者，謂於非道、非處、非時，自妻妾所而為罪失。

"Committing the sexual act" means the actual union of two persons. "Acting on an improper desire even within this same matter" means committing an offense even with one's own wife or concubine, if done through an improper bodily manner, in an improper place, or at an improper time.

此中略義者，謂略顯示若彼所行、若行差別、若欲邪行應知。

The brief meaning here is this: it shows, in summary, the object toward which the act is directed, the manner in which it is carried out, and what actually constitutes the offense of sexual misconduct itself.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi organizes these ten elements into the three groups the treatise's own summary names: the first five (parental protection, legal protection, gatekeeping protection, another's wife or concubine, and a protected unmarried woman) name the forbidden object; the next three (deception, force, and concealment) name the forbidden manner of approach; and the final two (the act itself, and acting improperly even within one's own marriage) name the offense's core substance. He draws particular attention to the treatise's specification of "improper manner, place, or time" as extending even to a legitimate spouse — improper bodily manner meaning anything beyond natural union, improper place meaning a site of reverence, a shrine, in view of others, or ground too hard or uneven to be safe and dignified, and improper time meaning during menstruation, late pregnancy, while nursing, during a period of religious observance, or during illness. Even within a sanctioned marriage, he stresses, the treatise still holds conduct to a standard of genuine respect and appropriateness — sexual misconduct, properly understood, is not solely a matter of who, but also of how, where, and when.

32. False Speech Opens: The Many Settings Where a Lie Can Be Told

The treatise turns to the first of the four verbal courses of action, false speech, opening with a careful survey of the varied settings — from royal courts to public gatherings — where a lie might actually be spoken.

Chinese Source

復次，諸妄語者者，此是總句。若王者，謂王家。若彼使者，謂執理家。若別者，謂長者居士。若眾者，謂彼聚集。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, "those who speak falsely" is the summary phrase. "Before the king" means in a royal court. "Before his officials" means before those who administer the law. "Before an individual" means before an elder or householder. "Before an assembly" means before a gathered crowd.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this opening list of settings as establishing that false speech's severity, and the treatise's interest in it, spans every level of social and civic life at once — a lie told before a king or his officials carries the weight of a formal, legally consequential falsehood, while a lie told before a single respected elder or before an ordinary gathered crowd still counts fully as the same offense, differing only in scale of consequence rather than in its basic moral character. He notes this catalogue-of-settings approach mirrors exactly the structure already used for killing, theft, and sexual misconduct — establishing first where and to whom the offense occurs, before turning to the psychological and circumstantial detail of how it is actually carried out.

33. False Speech Completed: Fear, Greed, and the Deliberate Concealment of One's Own Mind

Chinese Source

若大集中者，謂四方人眾聚集處。若已知者，謂隨前三所經語言。若已見者，謂隨曾見所經語言。若由自因者，謂或因怖畏，或因味著。如由自因，他因亦爾。因怖畏者，謂由怖畏殺、縛、治罰、黜責等故。因味著者，謂為財穀、珍寶等故。

Ru-Yi Translation

"Before a great assembly" means a gathering of people from every direction. "Regarding what is already known" means speaking contrary to what was learned through hearing, sensing, or cognizing in any of the settings above. "Regarding what is already seen" means speaking contrary to what was directly witnessed. "Through one's own cause" means either out of fear or out of attachment to gain — and the same holds when the cause comes from another. "Out of fear" means dreading being killed, bound, punished, or dismissed and disgraced. "Out of attachment" means craving wealth, grain, or treasure.

知而說妄語者，謂覆想欲見而說語言。此中略義者，謂依處故、異說故、因緣故、壞想故，而說妄語應知。

"Knowingly speaking falsely" means concealing one's actual perception, intention, or view, and speaking otherwise. The brief meaning here, then, is that false speech should be understood through its setting, its contrariness to what was actually known, its underlying cause, and its deliberate corruption of one's own perception.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the two causes — fear and attachment — as covering essentially every practical motive for lying: fear of violence, imprisonment, official punishment, or social disgrace pushes a person to deny what they know is true, while craving for wealth, goods, or advantage pushes them to assert what they know is false, and both motives operate identically whether the pressure originates in the speaker's own mind or is imposed by someone else's coercion or inducement. He treats the final element, "knowingly concealing perception, intention, or view," as false speech's actual psychological core: perception is simply what the mind takes an experience to be, intention is what the mind actually wants, and view is what the mind has settled into believing — a lie, properly understood, is always this same basic act of burying one of these three genuine inner states and presenting a different, false one in its place, regardless of the setting or the specific motive that prompted it.

34. Divisive Speech: The Deliberate Art of Breaking Others Apart

The treatise turns to the second verbal course of action, divisive speech — the deliberate act of driving a wedge between others — working through seven distinct elements that trace its intent, method, and the corrupted pleasure it takes in its own success.

Chinese Source

復次，離間語者者，此是總句。若為破壞者，謂由破壞意樂故。聞彼語已向此宣說，聞此語已向彼宣說者，謂隨所聞順乖離語。破壞和合者，謂能生起喜別離故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, "one who speaks divisively" is the summary phrase. "Intending to break apart" means acting with the deliberate wish to cause a rift. "Hearing this and reporting it there, hearing that and reporting it here" means selectively passing along, in either direction, whatever was heard that tends toward estrangement. "Breaking apart harmony" means bringing about a positive delight in separation between two parties.

隨印別離者，謂能乖違喜更生故。喜壞和合者，謂於已生喜別離中心染汙故。樂印別離者，謂於乖違喜更生中心染汙故。說能離間語者，謂或不聞，或他方便故。

"Reinforcing an existing rift" means causing a delight in continued estrangement to arise anew. "Delighting in breaking apart harmony" means taking a defiled pleasure in a delight in separation one has already caused to arise. "Delighting in reinforcing a rift" means taking a defiled pleasure in a renewed delight in continued estrangement. "Speaking what functions as divisive speech" means either pretending not to hear something conciliatory said by one party, or accomplishing the same divisive end through some other indirect means.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi traces a deliberate escalation across all seven elements: intent comes first, the settled wish to cause a rift before any word is spoken; selective reporting is the basic mechanism, relaying only what will inflame and withholding anything that would reconcile; "breaking apart harmony" and "reinforcing an existing rift" name the two possible targets — driving apart those still on good terms, or deepening a division that has already begun to form; and "delighting in" each of these names the corrupted emotional payoff the divisive speaker actually derives from the harm done, a genuine pleasure taken in others' broken relationships that marks this affliction as more than merely instrumental.

He reads the seventh and final element as broadening the whole category beyond speech that is spoken outright: deliberately staying silent when a reconciling word was said, letting an opportunity for harmony pass unremarked, or accomplishing the same divisive result indirectly — through a mutual friend, a gift, an attendant, or any other intermediary channel — counts fully as divisive speech even without the divider's own words ever directly reaching either party. The offense, he stresses, lies in the intent to divide and the effect actually achieved, not merely in the literal act of speaking a hostile sentence aloud.

35. Harsh Speech, Told in Reverse: Twelve Marks of a Bodhisattva's Genuine Speech

Divisive speech closes with its own fivefold summary. The treatise then reaches harsh speech — and takes a method found nowhere else in this whole survey: rather than describing the vice directly, it describes its positive opposite in loving detail, since a Mahāyāna treatise takes the perspective of a bodhisattva who, by nature, never actually speaks this way at all.

Chinese Source

此中略義者，謂略顯示離間意樂、離間未壞方便、離間已壞方便、離間染汙心，及他方便應知。復次，麤惡語者者，此是總句。此中尸羅支所攝故，名語無擾動。文句美滑，故名悅耳。增上欲解所發起故，非假偽故，非諂媚故，名為稱心。

Ru-Yi Translation

The brief meaning here shows: the intention to divide, the means used before any rift exists, the means used to deepen a rift already present, the defiled delight taken in either, and indirect means — these should be understood together. Further, "one who speaks harshly" is the summary phrase. Because it belongs to genuine moral discipline, such speech is called undisturbing. Because its words are gracious and smooth, it is called pleasant to hear. Because it arises from a firm, wholehearted conviction, is never false, and never flatters, it is called heart-satisfying.

不增益故，應順時機，引義利故，名為可愛。趣涅槃宮，故名先首。文句可味，故名美妙。

Because it neither exaggerates nor overstates, and because it is timely and brings genuine benefit, it is called lovable. Because it leads toward the palace of nirvāṇa, it is called foremost. Because its words are genuinely satisfying to take in, it is called beautiful.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains this striking method directly: because this treatise is a Mahāyāna text speaking from a bodhisattva's own standpoint, and a bodhisattva who has taken up the work of saving beings in this impure world remains untouched by the very defilements described everywhere else in this ninefold survey, the text simply has no harsh speaker's portrait to draw — instead, in this one case only, it describes the reverse: the actual qualities of a bodhisattva's genuine speech, letting the reader infer harsh speech as speech's failure to embody any of them.

He walks through the first six marks as building outward from inner discipline to outer effect: speech rooted in genuine moral discipline is naturally calm rather than agitated; naturally graceful and easy on the ear rather than coarse; genuinely heartfelt and sincere rather than flattering or hollow; measured and well-timed rather than exaggerated, so that it actually serves the listener's real benefit; oriented, at the deepest level, toward guiding the listener across saṃsāra all the way to nirvāṇa itself, never merely toward some lesser worldly end; and, in its very sound, genuinely pleasing to take in — he cites the classical comparison to the kalaviṅka bird's famously beautiful song as the traditional image for how a bodhisattva's voice is said to actually sound.

36. The Remaining Six Marks, and Speech Regrouped Into Three Kinds

Chinese Source

善釋文句，故名分明。顯然有趣，故名易可解了。攝受正法，故名可施功勞。離愛味心之所發起，故名無所依止。不過度量，故名非可厭逆。相續廣大，故名無邊無盡。

Ru-Yi Translation

Because it explains its own words and phrases well, it is called clear. Because it is plainly evident and genuinely engaging, it is called easy to understand. Because it draws the listener into taking up the true Dharma, it is called worth the effort. Because it arises free of any craving for personal gain, it is called without attachment. Because it never exceeds its proper measure, it is called never tiresome. Because it flows on, sustained and expansive, it is called boundless and inexhaustible.

又從無擾動語乃至無邊無盡語，應知略攝為三種語。一、尸羅律儀所攝語，謂一種。

Further, from "undisturbing speech" through "boundless and inexhaustible speech," it should be understood that these are, in brief, subsumed under three kinds of speech. The first kind, speech subsumed under moral discipline, comprises the first alone.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the second six marks as tracing genuine teaching's actual craft and integrity: clarity in explaining what is said, so nothing remains murky; being engaging enough that a listener stays attentive rather than drifting off, itself already both a mark of skillful teaching and a quiet corrective aimed at any teacher whose material puts an audience to sleep; being genuinely worth the listener's effort because it actually leads to practice and realization, the investment of attention never wasted; arising entirely free of any hidden wish to be believed, admired, or personally validated by the listener's response; staying within a proper, undramatic measure rather than exhausting the audience with excess; and being able to unfold at length, rich and inexhaustible, precisely because it draws on genuine, deep understanding rather than a shallow well quickly emptied.

Having laid out all twelve marks individually, the treatise now begins regrouping them into three broader categories — speech subsumed under moral discipline (the first mark, undisturbing speech, standing alone as its own category), with the remaining two categories still to come. Cháng Bǎi treats this whole twelve-mark portrait, taken as a single teaching, as one of the treatise's most directly useful passages for anyone who actually teaches the Dharma to others — a complete practical standard against which a teacher's own speech can be measured, mark by mark.

37. Three Kinds Become Three Again: Language That Ripens Toward Nirvāṇa Itself

The treatise completes its threefold regrouping of the twelve marks, then regroups them a second time from a different angle — language cherished across all three times, language that induces meditative absorption, and language that leads step by step toward concentration through the fruits of a clear conscience.

Chinese Source

二、等歡喜語，謂三種。三、說法語，謂其所餘。即此最後又有三種應知。一、所趣圓滿語，謂初一。二、文詞圓滿語，謂次二。三、方便圓滿語，謂其所餘。

Ru-Yi Translation

The second kind, speech that brings equal delight, comprises three of the marks. The third kind, speech that teaches the Dharma, comprises the remaining eight. This last kind should further be understood to divide into three: first, speech perfect in its ultimate destination, comprising the first of the eight; second, speech perfect in its wording, comprising the next two; third, speech perfect in its skillful means, comprising the rest.

又於未來世可愛樂故，名可愛語。於過去世可愛樂故，名可樂語。於現在世事及領受可愛樂故，名可欣語及可意語。應知即等歡喜語，名無量眾生可愛、可樂、可欣、可意語。即說法語，名三摩呬多語。即尸羅支所攝語，名由無悔等漸次能引三摩地語。

Further: cherished by beings of the future, it is called lovable speech. Cherished by beings of the past, it is called delightful speech. Cherished by beings in the present, both as spoken and as received, it is called joyous and gratifying speech. It should be understood that this is precisely the same as speech bringing equal delight — cherished, delightful, joyous, and gratifying to countless beings across all three times. Speech that teaches the Dharma is precisely what is called samādhi-inducing speech. And speech subsumed under moral discipline is precisely what, through freedom from regret and what naturally follows from it, gradually leads to concentration itself.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the first regrouping — destination-perfect, wording-perfect, and means-perfect speech — as tracing genuine Dharma-teaching from its ultimate aim (always oriented toward nirvāṇa itself, never a lesser end) through its actual craft (beautiful, clear language a listener can readily take in) to its practical delivery (skillful enough to actually carry a listener the rest of the way to realization). The second regrouping folds the same twelve marks into a threefold scheme spanning all three times and pointing toward meditation directly: speech cherished across past, present, and future together is simply the same "equally joyful" language already named; the eight Dharma-teaching marks are together called "samādhi-inducing" speech, since their entire function is to settle a listener's mind enough to actually see reality as it is; and speech rooted in moral discipline is traced through the classical sequence the treatise's own Śrāvaka Stage lays out in full — genuine precept-keeping produces freedom from regret, regret's absence produces joy, joy produces gladness, gladness produces bodily ease, ease produces genuine pleasant feeling, and that settled pleasure is what finally allows the mind to enter concentration — precepts, Cháng Bǎi stresses, are never an end in themselves but the first link in this entire causal chain toward realization.

38. Harsh Speech Named Directly at Last: The Sting and the Blow

Having spent an entire extended passage describing wholesome speech in loving detail, the treatise finally turns to name harsh speech itself directly — remarkably briefly, since everything needed to recognize it has, in effect, already been given by contrast.

Chinese Source

此中毒螫語者，謂毀辱他言，縱瞋毒故。麤獷語者，謂惱亂他言，發苦觸故。所餘麤惡語，翻前白品應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

Here, "venomous speech" means words that insult and demean another, indulging the poison of one's own hatred. "Coarse, brutal speech" means words that disturb and provoke another, inflicting a genuinely painful impact. As for the remaining kinds of harsh speech, they should be understood simply as the reverse of every one of the twelve wholesome marks already described.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads "venomous speech" and "coarse, brutal speech" as the two named archetypes — insult driven by unrestrained hatred, likened to a sting whose pain flares up instantly, and blunt provocation that lands on the listener like an actual physical blow — but treats the closing line as the passage's real punchline: rather than laboriously naming twelve further vices to match the twelve virtues one by one, the treatise simply says every one of the twelve wholesome marks, inverted, is itself a form of harsh speech. Speech that disturbs rather than settles the mind, that grates on the ear rather than pleases it, that rings false or flattering rather than sincere, that exaggerates or mistimes itself, that leads nowhere near liberation, that sounds ugly, that obscures rather than clarifies, that confuses rather than illuminates, that wastes the listener's effort, that is spoken from self-interest, that wears the listener down, or that runs dry rather than flowing — any one of these failures, on its own, already counts as a form of coarse speech, regrouped in turn into three simple kinds: speech that violates moral restraint, speech no one actually wants to hear, and speech that has nothing to do with the Dharma at all.

39. Frivolous Speech Opens: Five Ways an Accusation Can Itself Go Wrong

The treatise turns to the fourth and final verbal course of action, frivolous speech, opening with a case that might seem surprising for this category: even a legitimate accusation of wrongdoing, brought against a fellow practitioner, can itself become a form of frivolous speech if handled improperly.

Chinese Source

復次，諸綺語者者，此是總句。於邪舉罪時，有五種邪舉罪者。言不應時故，名非時語者。言不實故，名非實語者。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, "those who speak frivolously" is the summary phrase. When bringing an improper accusation, there are five kinds of improper accuser. Because the words are spoken at the wrong time, this is called speaking untimely. Because the words are untrue, this is called speaking falsely.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this opening as continuing a theme already established for monastic community life: correcting a fellow practitioner's genuine misconduct is legitimate and sometimes necessary, but the treatise insists the correction itself must meet a real standard, or it degrades into mere frivolous, purposeless speech regardless of whether the underlying accusation happens to be true. He glosses "untimely" through both an inner and an outer sense — inwardly wrong timing being when the person raising the accusation is themselves currently overcome by craving, hatred, delusion, severe pain, or serious illness, in no state to speak with a clear and compassionate mind; outwardly wrong timing being when the person being accused is in the same compromised state, or when the accusation is raised in front of someone not yet fully ordained who has no standing to hear it. "Speaking falsely" is the more straightforward case: leveling an accusation that simply is not true, regardless of the timing or manner in which it is delivered.

40. Wrongful Accusation Completed, and Wrongful Teaching's Six Ways to Fail

The treatise finishes the five kinds of improper accusation, then turns to a second setting for frivolous speech — teaching the Dharma itself done badly, whether through carelessness, showmanship, disorder, or simply lacking any real doctrinal grounding.

Chinese Source

言引無義故，名非義語者。言麤獷故，名非法語者。言挾瞋恚故，名非靜語者。又於邪說法時，不正思審而宣說故，名不思量語。為勝聽者而宣說故，名不靜語。

Ru-Yi Translation

Because the words serve no genuine purpose, this is called pointless speech — as when an accusation is repeated after the offense has already been confessed and purified. Because the words are harsh, this is called improper speech — as when an accusation is delivered with cruel, demeaning language meant to wound. Because the words carry hatred, this is called agitated speech — as when the accusation is driven by ill will rather than genuine concern. Further, when teaching the Dharma improperly: because the teaching is delivered without careful, sound reflection beforehand, this is called unconsidered speech. Because it is delivered chiefly to impress an especially eminent listener, this is called restless speech.

非時而說，前後義趣不相屬故，名雜亂語。不中理因而宣說故，名非有教語。引不相應為譬況故，名非有喻語。顯穢染故，名非有法語。

Because it is delivered at the wrong time, its content disconnected front to back, this is called disordered speech. Because the reasoning offered does not actually hold up, this is called speech without genuine doctrinal grounding. Because the analogy drawn does not actually fit, this is called speech without a genuine example. Because it displays defilement rather than dispelling it, this is called speech without any real Dharma in it at all.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the accusation's final three failures as compounding the first two already given: raising a charge that serves no purpose because the wrongdoer has already confessed and made amends is simply cruelty dressed as discipline; delivering it with contempt — calling the accused a hopeless, shameless, incorrigible renunciant rather than addressing the actual conduct — turns correction into abuse; and bringing hatred or a wish to harm into the accusation corrupts its motive entirely, regardless of whether the underlying charge happens to be accurate. He treats this fivefold standard (timely, true, purposeful, gentle, and free of hatred) as the treatise's complete ethical test for anyone in a position to correct another, monastic or otherwise.

He reads the six failures of teaching as together diagnosing why a teacher can get every word doctrinally correct and still fail the listener: speaking without having actually thought the material through beforehand; performing for a prestigious audience rather than genuinely serving whoever is present; losing the thread so badly the sequence of ideas no longer connects; offering a supporting reason that, examined closely, doesn't actually establish the point (he ties this directly to the treatise's earlier account of the three-branch syllogism — thesis, reason, and example — back in fascicle 7, since a flawed reason here fails the identical logical test); offering an illustrative analogy that doesn't genuinely resemble the point it's meant to clarify; and, worst of all, a teaching that itself displays greed, hatred, or delusion rather than dispelling them, becoming defilement wearing the Dharma's own clothing.

41. Idle Chatter During Entertainment, Frivolous Speech Closes, and Covetousness Opens

Chinese Source

又於歌笑嬉戲等時，及觀舞樂、戲笑、俳說等時，有引無義語。此中略義者，謂顯如前說三時綺語。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, during singing, laughter, and play, and while watching dance, music, comic performance, and theater, there arises speech that serves no genuine purpose. The brief meaning here shows that frivolous speech, as already explained, occurs across these three settings.

復次，諸貪欲者者，此是總句。由猛利貪者，謂於他所有，由貪增上，欲為己有，起決定執故。於財者，謂世俗財類。具者，謂所受用資具。即此二種總名為物。凡彼所有定當屬我者，此顯貪欲生起行相。

Further, "those who covet" is the summary phrase. "Through intense craving" means holding a settled, determined conviction, driven by overwhelming desire, to make what belongs to another one's own. "Wealth" refers to ordinary worldly riches. "Goods" refers to the requisites one actually makes use of — these two together are simply called "possessions." "Whatever they have should rightly belong to me" shows the very mode in which covetousness actually arises in the mind.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the third setting for frivolous speech — casual talk during entertainment — as covering the ordinary chatter surrounding singing, joking, watching a performance, or theater, none of it malicious in the way harsh or divisive speech is, yet still ultimately purposeless, drawing the mind outward toward distraction rather than toward anything of genuine value. With this third setting named, frivolous speech closes as the last of the four verbal courses of action, having covered improper accusation, improper teaching, and idle entertainment-talk alike — three quite different contexts unified by the same underlying feature, speech that accomplishes nothing genuinely worthwhile.

The treatise then turns to the first of the three purely mental courses of action, covetousness, giving it a notably compact three-part structure: its own basic nature (an intense, settled craving that has crystallized into genuine resolve, not merely a passing wish); its object (wealth and the practical goods a person actually depends on, given as a single combined category); and its characteristic mental posture — the conviction that whatever belongs to someone else ought, by rights, to belong to oneself instead. Cháng Bǎi notes this final phrase captures covetousness's real psychological signature more precisely than "wanting" alone would: it is not simply desiring something similar for oneself, but a possessive claim laid specifically on what is already, rightfully, someone else's.

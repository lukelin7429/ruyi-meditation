Fascicle 7 · 瑜伽師地論卷第七 · Continuing Directly from Fascicle 6

Why Good People Suffer and Bad People Prosper

Fascicle 6 ended mid-topic, having just closed the doctrine positing permanence — not a natural stopping point, simply where the physical scroll divides. This page picks up exactly there: the doctrine of prior deeds as sole cause, born from watching the honest suffer and the crooked thrive, followed by the doctrine of a sovereign creator — continuing the systematic survey of sixteen non-Buddhist doctrines within the Three Grounds of Applied and Sustained Thought.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 7 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. The Sixth Doctrine: All Suffering Traces to Past Deeds Alone

Continuing directly from fascicle 6's close, the treatise takes up the sixth of sixteen non-Buddhist doctrines — the ascetic conviction that every present suffering has one and only one cause: karma committed in a past life, extinguishable only through deliberate self-mortification.

Chinese Source

宿作因論者，謂如有一，若沙門、若婆羅門，起如是見，立如是論，廣說如經。凡諸世間所有士夫補特伽羅所受者，謂現所受苦。皆由宿作為因者，謂由宿惡為因。由勤精進吐舊業故者，謂由現法極自苦行。

Ru-Yi Translation

The doctrine of prior deeds as cause: there are certain renunciants or brahmins who hold such a view and establish such a doctrine, stated in full as in the sūtra. Whatever suffering any person in the world experiences is invariably caused by prior deeds — that is, by past unwholesome karma. Through diligent, strenuous effort, one purges this old karma — that is, through extreme self-mortification practiced in this very life.

現在新業由不作因之所害故者，謂諸不善業。如是於後不復有漏者，謂一向是善性故，說後無漏。由無漏故業盡者，謂諸惡業。由業盡故苦盡者，謂宿因所作及現法方便所招苦惱。由苦盡故得證苦邊者，謂證餘生相續苦盡。謂無繫外道作如是計。

New karma in the present is prevented by the cause of non-action — that is, by refraining from every unwholesome deed. Thereafter, no further contamination arises — since one's nature has become entirely wholesome, no further contamination follows. Because there is no further contamination, karma is exhausted — namely, every unwholesome karma. Because karma is exhausted, suffering is exhausted — namely, the suffering produced both by prior karma and by present ascetic effort. And because suffering is exhausted, one realizes the very limit of suffering — that is, one realizes the complete cessation of suffering across every future rebirth. It is the Nirgrantha ascetics — the unclothed renunciants — who hold this position.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the holders of this doctrine as the Nirgranthas ("unbound ones"), the ascetic tradition better known to history as Jainism — renunciants who went unclothed, believing modesty itself a needless attachment, and who practiced extreme physical austerity: standing on one leg for hours, lying exposed on scorching ground under direct sun, or resting on beds of thorns and sharp grass. Their theology, as the treatise summarizes it, runs in a precise causal chain: every scrap of present suffering was earned entirely by past unwholesome deeds; deliberately embracing severe physical suffering now "burns off" that old karmic debt directly; refraining from all new action prevents any further karma from accumulating; once nothing new accumulates and the old debt is fully paid, the practitioner becomes permanently free of affliction; freedom from affliction exhausts every unwholesome karma still outstanding; exhausted karma means exhausted suffering; and exhausted suffering means final release from every future rebirth's suffering altogether. Cháng Bǎi flags one crucial point up front: their "freedom from contamination" is not the same uncontaminated realm Buddhism means by that term — it is simply becoming entirely, non-karmically "good" in a naive sense, not the wisdom-based liberation the treatise's own path leads to.

2. Why They Believed This: Good People Suffer, Bad People Prosper

Chinese Source

由見現法士夫作用不決定故。所以者何？彼見世間雖具正方便，而招於苦；雖具邪方便，而致於樂。彼如是思：若由現法士夫作用為彼因者，彼應顛倒。由彼所見非顛倒故，是故彼皆以宿作為因。

Ru-Yi Translation

This arises from observing that a person's present conduct does not reliably determine their outcome. Why is this? Because they observe that some, though acting through entirely proper means, nonetheless meet with suffering, while others, though acting through improper means, nonetheless arrive at happiness. They reason thus: were present conduct truly the cause of these outcomes, this observation would have to be a distortion — yet what they observe is plainly not a distortion. Therefore, they conclude, prior deeds alone must be the true cause in every case.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this as the doctrine's genuinely sympathetic starting point — the same puzzle that troubles honest people in every era: an upright, hardworking merchant goes bankrupt through no fault of his own, while a swindler grows rich; a careful, ethical person falls gravely ill, while a careless, cruel one enjoys robust health. Since this mismatch between present conduct and present outcome is plainly, repeatedly real and not some trick of perception, the Nirgranthas reasoned that present conduct could not be the operative cause at all — the entire explanation must instead lie in karma already committed long before this life began, with present virtue or vice simply irrelevant to what is now being repaid.

3. The Refutation: If Only Past Karma Matters, Why Does Austerity Do Anything?

Chinese Source

今應問彼：汝何所欲？現法方便所招之苦，為用宿作為因？為用現法方便為因？若用宿作為因者，汝先所說，由勤精進吐舊業故，現在新業由不作因之所害故，如是於後不復有漏，乃至廣說，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

One should press them thus: what, precisely, do you mean? Is the suffering brought about by present ascetic effort itself caused by prior deeds, or is it caused by that present effort? If it is caused by prior deeds, then your own earlier claim — that diligent effort purges old karma, that refraining from new action prevents its accumulation, and that freedom from contamination thereafter follows, and so on in full — makes no sense.

若用現法方便為因者，汝先所說，凡諸世間所有士夫補特伽羅所受，皆由宿作為因，不應道理。如是現法方便苦宿作為因故，現法士夫用為因故，皆不應道理，是故此論非如理說。

If it is caused by present effort, then your own earlier claim — that every experience of every person in the world is invariably caused by prior deeds alone — makes no sense. Thus, whether the suffering of present ascetic effort is said to be caused by prior deeds, or the present person's own effort is said to be its cause, neither makes sense — and so this doctrine is not a soundly reasoned teaching.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi presents this as an elegant two-horned trap built directly from the doctrine's own claims. The Nirgranthas need the pain of austerity itself to actually accomplish something — burning off old karma — which only makes sense if that pain has a real causal effect right now. But if the pain of standing on one leg or lying on thorns is itself just one more instance of "suffering caused entirely by prior deeds," then the ascetic's present exertion is doing no actual work at all — it is merely one more scene in a script already written by the past, no more capable of purging anything than sitting idle would be. Yet if the ascetic's present effort does actually cause something — actually burns off karma, as claimed — then present action clearly can produce real effects after all, directly contradicting the doctrine's founding claim that only past deeds ever determine what a person experiences. The doctrine, Cháng Bǎi notes, needs present action to be causally impotent (to explain why good people can suffer despite virtuous present conduct) and causally potent (to explain why ascetic practice actually works) at the very same time — a straightforward contradiction that dissolves the entire theory.

4. The Real Answer: Suffering Has Three Different Kinds of Cause

Having refuted the doctrine's rigid single-cause claim, the treatise states its own considerably more nuanced account: suffering arises sometimes from past karma alone, sometimes from a genuine mixture of past and present, and sometimes from present effort alone — the very complexity the Nirgranthas' theory could not accommodate.

Chinese Source

我今當說如實因相。或有諸苦唯用宿作為因。猶如有一，自業增上力故，生諸惡趣或貧窮家。或復有苦雜因所生。謂如有一，因邪事王，不獲樂果而反致苦。

Ru-Yi Translation

I shall now state the true nature of cause. There is suffering caused by prior deeds alone — as when someone, through the sheer dominant force of their own past karma, is born into an unfortunate destiny or into poverty. There is also suffering produced by a mixture of causes — as when someone, serving a king through improper means, fails to obtain happy results and instead meets with suffering.

如事於王如是，由諸言說商賈等業、由事農業、由劫盜業、或於他有情作損害事，若有福者，獲得富樂，若無福者，雖設功用而無果遂。或復有法，純由現在功用因得。如新所造引餘有業；或聽聞正法，於法覺察；或復發起威儀業路；或復修學工巧業處；如是等類，唯因現在士夫功用。

As with serving a king, so too with a writer's craft, with trade, with farming, with theft, or with harming other beings: one who has merit obtains wealth and happiness even through improper means, while one without merit finds no result despite great effort — such results are a mixture of past merit and present method. And there is, further, that which is obtained purely through present effort: newly performed karma that draws a further rebirth; hearing the true Dharma and awakening insight into it; the arising of one's own bodily conduct and bearing; or learning a craft or trade — all such things depend solely on the present person's own effort.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this threefold scheme as directly explaining the very puzzle that misled the Nirgranthas in the first place, without needing to deny present action any causal power at all. Some suffering genuinely does trace to past karma alone — being born into poverty or an unfortunate destiny through the sheer weight of past unwholesome deeds, with nothing in the present life needed to explain it. But most of ordinary life's mismatches between effort and outcome are actually mixed: a corrupt official who serves the king dishonestly and still prospers has a reserve of past merit cushioning present wrongdoing, while one with no such reserve, doing the same wrong, meets only misfortune — the very same present method producing different results depending on what past merit is or isn't already in the bank, which explains the "unfair" cases the Nirgranthas found so troubling without abandoning present causation altogether. And a considerable range of ordinary experience — the new karma a person is actively creating right now, the insight that dawns from actually listening to the Dharma, the plain physical actions of standing, sitting, and moving, and any skill a person deliberately sets out to learn — depends purely on present effort, with no need to invoke past karma to explain it at all. Cháng Bǎi notes this threefold analysis is simply more honest to how cause and effect actually interact than either extreme — pure fatalism or pure present-only causation — closing this sixth doctrine before the treatise turns to its seventh: the claim that a sovereign lord alone creates everything in the world.

5. The Seventh Doctrine: A Sovereign Creator Behind Everything

The treatise turns to the doctrine that a single sovereign power — Śiva, Brahmā, or any of several other candidates — created and directs the entire world, tracing its appeal to the same frustration everyone eventually meets: wanting one outcome and getting another.

Chinese Source

自在等作者論者，謂如有一，或沙門、或婆羅門，起如是見，立如是論：凡諸世間所有士夫補特伽羅所受，彼一切或以自在變化為因，或餘丈夫變化為因，諸如是等。謂說自在等不平等因論者作如是計。

Ru-Yi Translation

The doctrine of a sovereign lord as maker: there are certain renunciants or brahmins who hold such a view and establish such a doctrine — that whatever any person in the world experiences is caused, in every case, either by the transformative power of a sovereign lord, or by the transformative power of some other great being, and so on. It is those who propound this doctrine of an unequal cause who hold this position.

彼由現見於因果中，世間有情不隨欲轉，故作此計。所以者何？現見世間有情，於彼因時欲修淨業，不遂本欲，反更為惡。於彼果時，願生善趣樂世界中，不遂本欲，墮惡趣等。意謂受樂，不遂所欲，反受諸苦。

They arrive at this by observing that beings in the world, with respect to cause and effect, cannot make things turn out as they wish. Why is this? Because they observe beings who, at the time of acting, wish to perform wholesome deeds yet fail to realize that wish and instead do wrong; and who, at the time of receiving results, wish to be reborn into a fortunate, happy world yet fail to realize that wish and instead fall into an unfortunate destiny. They wish for happiness, and, failing to obtain what they wish, instead experience suffering.

由見此故，彼作是思：世間諸物必應別有作者、生者，及變化者為彼物父，謂自在天，或復其餘。

Seeing this, they reason: everything in the world must therefore have some further maker, producer, and transformer standing behind it as its father — namely, a sovereign lord, or some other such being.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this doctrine's motivating puzzle as genuinely universal: everyone, at some point, sincerely intends to act well and finds themselves instead speaking or acting badly in the moment; everyone who hopes for a happy rebirth has, at some point, watched that hope fail to materialize. Rather than trace this gap between intention and outcome to the ordinary, impersonal working of karma across countless prior causes — which the treatise has spent this whole fascicle patiently explaining — these thinkers concluded that some further personal agent must be pulling the strings behind the scenes, unequally and unilaterally deciding outcomes no one asked for. He notes the doctrine's "sovereign lord" is not limited to one candidate: besides Śiva and Brahmā, later scholastic sources list time, space, an original first-cause, nature itself, empty space, and a permanent self as rival answers various non-Buddhist schools proposed for the same underlying craving — a single, ultimate, personal or quasi-personal explanation for why the world does not simply give people what they want.

6. Four Dilemmas Aimed at the Sovereign Creator

The treatise answers with a tightly structured fourfold refutation, previewed in its own summary verse, testing the creator's power, its relationship to the world, its motive, and its status as sole cause.

Chinese Source

今當問彼。嗢柁南曰：功能無體性，攝不攝相違，有用及無用，為因成過失。汝何所欲？自在天等變化功能，為用業方便為因？為無因耶？若用業方便為因者，唯此功能用業方便為因，非餘世間，不應道理。若無因者，唯此功能無因而有，非世間物，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

One should now question them. A summary verse states: function has no substance; inclusion and exclusion both contradict; usefulness and uselessness; and as cause, every option fails. What do you actually intend: does the sovereign lord's transformative power have, as its cause, deliberate effort and method, or does it have no cause at all? If it has deliberate effort and method as its cause, then insisting only this power operates this way, while nothing else in the world does, makes no sense. If it has no cause at all, then insisting only this power exists uncaused, while nothing else in the world does, makes no sense either.

又汝何所欲？此大自在為墮世間攝？為不攝耶？若言攝者，此大自在則同世法，而能遍生世間，不應道理。若不攝者，則是解脫，而言能生世間，不應道理。

Further, what do you actually intend: is this sovereign lord included within the world, or excluded from it? If included, then this lord is no different from any other worldly phenomenon — yet claiming it alone can universally give rise to the entire world makes no sense. If excluded, then this lord must already be liberated — yet claiming a liberated being still produces the defiled, conditioned world makes no sense either.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the first dilemma as testing the very mechanism by which this lord supposedly creates: if its power to transform requires the same deliberate effort and method any ordinary being's action requires, there is no principled reason to withhold that same causal power from ordinary beings creating their own circumstances — the lord loses its special status; if instead its power arises with no cause whatsoever, there is equally no reason to deny the same uncaused status to the rest of the world, which would then need no creator either.

The second dilemma catches the lord in an inescapable double bind regarding its own status: if this lord is itself part of the conditioned world — subject to the same causal laws as everything else — it cannot simultaneously stand outside that world as its universal, unconditioned source; but if it is genuinely apart from and unconditioned by the world, it has effectively already attained the liberated state Buddhism itself describes, and a liberated being, by definition, generates no further defiled rebirth or suffering at all — so it could not be the source of an ordinary, suffering-filled cosmos either. Neither being fully "of" the world nor fully "beyond" it leaves any coherent place for the lord to actually stand while creating that world.

7. Two More Dilemmas Close the Case Against a Creator

Chinese Source

又汝何所欲？為有用故變生世間？為無用耶？若有用者，則於彼用無有自在，而於世間有自在者，不應道理。若無用者，無有所須，而生世間，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, what do you actually intend: does the lord transform and produce the world because it has some use for doing so, or without any use at all? If it has a use, then it lacks mastery even over that very use — yet claiming it has mastery over the world makes no sense. If it has no use at all, then producing the world with no need or purpose whatsoever likewise makes no sense.

又汝何所欲？此所出生，為唯大自在為因？為亦取餘為因耶？若唯大自在為因者，是則若時有大自在，是時則有出生；若時有出生，是時則有大自在；而言出生用大自在為因者，不應道理。

Further, what do you actually intend: is the world's production caused solely by the sovereign lord, or are other factors also its cause? If solely by the lord, then wherever the lord exists, the world's production must exist, and wherever production exists, the lord must exist — the two would have to be permanently co-present, making it senseless to still speak of the lord as the world's cause, as though one produced the other.

若言亦取餘為因者，此唯取樂欲為因？為除樂欲更取餘為因？若唯取樂欲為因者，此樂欲為唯取大自在為因？為亦取餘為因耶？若唯取大自在為因者，若時有大自在，是時則有樂欲；若時有樂欲，是時則有大自在；便應無始常有出生，此亦不應道理。又於彼欲無有自在，而言於世間物有自在者，不應道理。

If other factors are also its cause, is desire alone that further factor, or something else besides desire? If desire alone, then is this desire itself caused solely by the sovereign lord, or by something else as well? If solely by the lord, then wherever the lord exists desire must exist, and wherever desire exists the lord must exist — meaning production would have to be beginninglessly, constantly ongoing, which plainly contradicts experience. And, further, if the lord itself lacks mastery over its own desire, then claiming it has mastery over the things of the world makes no sense at all.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the third dilemma as exposing a bind built into the very idea of divine motive: a creator who acts from need or purpose is thereby shown to lack full self-mastery — a truly sovereign, complete being should want for nothing — yet a creator with absolutely no purpose at all creating an entire elaborate cosmos looks less like sovereign power than arbitrary caprice, "the fault of a madman," as the parallel passage in the Xianyang Shengjiao Lun puts it. The fourth dilemma then chases the "sole cause" claim into an infinite regress: if the lord alone causes the world's production, lord and production should be permanently co-present, with neither ever preceding the other — yet a "cause" that never actually precedes its effect is not functioning as a cause at all; and if desire is added as a further, necessary factor, the same co-presence problem simply resurfaces one level up, forcing the theory to explain what causes the desire, and so on without end, while also revealing that a lord still subject to desire is not actually the fully self-mastered, sovereign being the doctrine needs it to be. With all four approaches — function, inclusion, usefulness, and sole causation — shown incoherent, the treatise concludes: "this doctrine is not a soundly reasoned teaching," closing the seventh of sixteen doctrines and turning to the eighth: the claim that ritually killing living beings is itself the true Dharma.

8. The Eighth Doctrine: Ritual Killing as the True Dharma — A Doctrine Born of Appetite, Not Reasoning

The treatise turns to a doctrine unlike the previous seven in one crucial respect: rather than a genuine, if mistaken, attempt to understand the world, it identifies this one as pure self-serving pretext — a cover story invented to justify eating meat.

Chinese Source

害為正法論者，謂如有一，若沙門、若婆羅門，起如是見，立如是論：若於彼祠中，呪術為先，害諸生命，若能祀者、若所害者、若諸助伴，如是一切皆得生天。

Ru-Yi Translation

The doctrine that harming is the true Dharma: there are certain renunciants or brahmins who hold such a view and establish such a doctrine — that in a ritual sacrifice, once mantras are first recited, harming living beings causes everyone involved — the one who performs the sacrifice, the one who is killed, and every assistant alike — to be reborn in heaven.

此違理論，諂誑所起，不由觀察道理建立。然於諍競惡劫起時，諸婆羅門違越古昔婆羅門法，為欲食肉，妄起此計。

This doctrine, contrary to reason, arises from flattery and deceit — it is not established by any genuine examination of how things actually are. Rather, when the degenerate age of contention and strife arose, certain brahmins, departing from the ancient brahmanical teaching, fabricated this claim purely out of a desire to eat meat.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi flags this as the treatise's most pointed accusation of bad faith anywhere in its sixteen-doctrine survey: where the first seven doctrines, however mistaken, reflect real if flawed reasoning or real meditative experience misread, this one is explicitly named as flattery and deceit — a crooked, self-serving fabrication with no honest inquiry behind it at all. He explains the historical claim embedded in the text: the earliest brahmanical teaching, on this account, did not sanction meat-eating or ritual slaughter; later, in a degenerate age of growing social conflict, certain brahmins wanted to eat meat anyway, and rather than simply admit desire, invented an elaborate religious justification — mantras plus ritual killing sending everyone involved, killer and killed alike, straight to heaven — dressing appetite up as theology. This is why, he notes, the treatise's refutation that follows reads less like patient philosophical correction and more like sustained, escalating cross-examination of an excuse.

9. Five Dilemmas Expose the Sacrificial Mantra as Empty

The treatise presses the doctrine's central mechanism — the mantra that supposedly transforms killing into a path to heaven — through five escalating dilemmas, each exposing the claim as unable to do the work it is asked to do.

Chinese Source

汝何所欲？此呪術方為是法自體？為是非法自體？若是法自體者，離彼殺生，不能感得自所愛果，而能轉彼非法以為正法，不應道理。若是非法自體者，自是不愛果法，而能轉捨餘不愛果法者，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

What do you actually intend: is the mantra itself, in its own nature, wholesome, or unwholesome? If wholesome, then it should produce its own desirable result without needing killing at all — yet claiming it can transform the unwholesome act of killing into the true Dharma makes no sense. If unwholesome, then it is itself something that produces an undesirable result — so claiming it can transform other undesirable results into desirable ones makes no sense either.

如世間毒，呪術所攝，不能為害。當知此呪術方亦復如是。今應問彼：如呪術方能息外毒，亦能息內貪瞋癡毒？為不爾耶？若能息者，無處無時，無有一人貪瞋癡等靜息可得，故不中理。若不能息者，汝先所說，如呪術方能息外毒，亦能息除非法業者，不應道理。

Their reply: just as worldly poison, when countered by a mantra, loses its power to harm, so too does this sacrificial mantra. One should press them: does a mantra that can neutralize external poison also neutralize the internal poisons of craving, hatred, and delusion, or does it not? If it does, then nowhere, at no time, has even a single person's craving, hatred, and delusion ever actually been neutralized this way — this plainly contradicts experience. If it does not, then your own claim — that a mantra able to neutralize external poison can likewise neutralize an unwholesome deed — makes no sense.

又汝何所欲？此呪術方為遍行耶？不遍行耶？若遍行者，自所愛親不先用祠，不應道理。若不遍者，此呪功能便非決定，不應道理。

Further, what do you actually intend: does this mantra's effect apply universally, or not? If universally, then your failure to sacrifice your own beloved family members first makes no sense. If not universally, then this mantra's power is simply unreliable — which makes no sense for a technique claimed to guarantee heaven.

又汝何所欲？此呪功能，為唯能轉因？亦轉果耶？若唯轉因者，於果無能，不應道理。若亦轉果者，應如轉變，即令羊等成可愛妙色，然捨羊等身已，方取天身，不應道理。

Further, what do you actually intend: can this mantra's power transform the cause alone, or does it also transform the result? If the cause alone, then it has no power over the actual result — which makes no sense for something claimed to guarantee that result. If it also transforms the result, then the sacrificial animal should visibly transform into a beautiful, heavenly form on the spot — yet it must first be killed and its body discarded before any heavenly body is supposedly obtained, which makes no sense.

又汝何所欲？造呪術者，為有力能及悲愍不？若言有者，離殺彼命，不能將彼往生天上，不應道理。若言無者，彼所造呪能有所辦，不應道理。

Further, what do you actually intend: does the one who composed this mantra possess genuine power and compassion, or not? If so, then his being unable to send beings to heaven without first killing them makes no sense — genuine power and compassion should accomplish this without any killing at all. If not, then claiming his mantra can nonetheless accomplish sending the slain to heaven makes no sense either.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats all five dilemmas as different angles on a single exposure: the mantra is asked to do impossible, contradictory work. If wholesome, it needs no killing to produce its own good result — undercutting the whole ritual; if unwholesome, it cannot transform anything else into something good. The poison-charm analogy, offered to rescue the theory, backfires immediately: real spiritual power should extend to the deepest poisons of all — greed, hatred, and delusion — yet no mantra anywhere has ever actually eliminated these in a single person, a plain, checkable fact the whole system of Buddhist practice exists precisely to address through sustained discipline and insight, not incantation. The mantra's claimed effect is also suspiciously selective: if it truly worked on everyone, sincere believers should be sacrificing their own beloved family first to send them to heaven, which they conspicuously never do — showing they don't actually trust the claim themselves. And the cause-versus-result test catches the theory's sleight of hand directly: a technique that can transform a sheep's karmic destiny should, if genuine, show some visible sign of that transformation — yet the sheep must be killed and its ordinary body discarded before any heavenly body supposedly appears, meaning nothing observable ever actually changes except that the sheep is now dead. Finally, the mantra's own author is caught in the same trap as the sovereign creator of the previous doctrine: genuinely compassionate power should not require killing to bestow a blessing, and if the author lacks such power, his mantra accomplishes nothing at all. With every angle exhausted, the treatise concludes: "this doctrine is not a soundly reasoned teaching."

10. What Actually Makes an Act "Non-Dharma," and Doctrine Eight Closes

Chinese Source

我今當說非法之相。若業，損他而不治現過，是名非法。又若業，諸修道者共知此業感不愛果。又若業，一切智者決定說為不善。又若業，自所不欲。又若業，染心所起。又若業，待邪呪術方備功驗。又若業，自性無記。諸如是等皆是非法。

Ru-Yi Translation

I shall now state the mark of what is non-Dharma. Any deed that harms another without correcting an actual present fault is non-Dharma. Further, any deed that practitioners of the path uniformly recognize as bringing an undesirable result; any deed that every possessor of complete wisdom firmly declares unwholesome; any deed one would not wish done to oneself; any deed arising from a defiled mind; any deed that depends on a false mantra merely to seem effective; and any deed that is, by its own nature, karmically neutral — all such deeds are non-Dharma.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this closing definition as a practical checklist any student can actually apply, not just an abstract principle: does this act harm someone without actually correcting any real fault of theirs (ritual slaughter certainly does — the animal has no "fault" being corrected at all); would every serious practitioner recognize it as producing an unwanted result; would every truly wise person call it unwholesome without hesitation; would the actor themselves object to having it done to them; does it spring from a mind clouded by craving, hatred, or delusion (exactly the appetite for meat that generated this whole doctrine); does it need an external, dubious technique to seem to work, rather than standing on its own merit; and is it, at bottom, simply neutral rather than genuinely good. Ritual sacrifice fails every single one of these tests, which is precisely why, Cháng Bǎi notes, the treatise bothered to state this checklist explicitly here rather than simply repeating "this is wrong" — it wants the student equipped to recognize the next self-serving justification for harm dressed up as religion, whatever guise it takes. With this, the eighth of sixteen doctrines closes, and the treatise turns to its ninth: whether the world has spatial limits or is boundless.

11. The Ninth Doctrine: Is the World Bounded or Boundless? A Cosmic Cycle Misread

The treatise takes up a doctrine already previewed as a tangent within fascicle 6's discussion of permanence, now given its own full treatment: four views on whether the world has spatial limits, all traced to meditators genuinely seeing part of the cosmic cycle but misreading what they saw.

Chinese Source

邊無邊論者，謂如有一，若沙門、若婆羅門，依止世間諸靜慮故，於彼世間住有邊想、無邊想、俱想、不俱想，廣說如經。由此起如是見，立如是論：世間有邊、世間無邊、世間亦有邊亦無邊、世間非有邊非無邊。

Ru-Yi Translation

The doctrine of a bounded or boundless world: there are certain renunciants or brahmins who, relying on worldly meditative concentration, come to hold, regarding that world, a notion of it having limits, of it being boundless, of it being both, or of it being neither — stated in full as in the sūtra. On this basis they arrive at this view and establish this doctrine: that the world has limits, that the world is boundless, that the world is both bounded and boundless, or that the world is neither bounded nor boundless.

若依斷邊際求世邊時，若憶念壞劫，於世間起有邊想。若憶念成劫，則於世間起無邊想。若依方域周廣求世邊時，若下過無間更無所得，上過第四靜慮亦無所得，傍一切處不得邊際；爾時則於上下起有邊想，於傍處所起無邊想。若為治此執，但依異文，義無差別，則於世間起非有邊非無邊想。

When seeking the world's limit through the boundary of its cessation: recalling a destruction-eon gives rise to the notion the world has limits. Recalling a formation-eon gives rise to the notion it is boundless. When instead seeking the world's limit through the extent of its regions: finding nothing below beyond the Avīci hell, nor anything above beyond the fourth dhyāna, yet finding no limit at all extending sideways, one arrives at a notion of limits above and below but boundlessness to the sides. And, merely to answer this last position with different wording rather than any different meaning, one arrives at the notion that the world is neither bounded nor boundless.

今應問彼：汝何所欲？從前壞劫以來，為更有世間生起？為無起耶？若言有者，世間有邊，不應道理。若言無者，非世間住，念世間邊，不應道理。如是彼來有故，彼來無故，皆不應理，是故此論非如理說。

One should press them: what do you actually intend — since the last destruction-eon, has the world arisen again, or not? If it has arisen again, then calling the world "bounded" makes no sense, since it plainly continues on. If it has not arisen again, then there is no world at all to speak of — so debating whether it is bounded or boundless makes no sense either. Thus, whether the world has arisen again or not, neither position holds up, and so this doctrine is not a soundly reasoned teaching.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this doctrine as a case of genuine meditative vision — the four dhyānas' practitioners really can, through refined concentration, perceive fragments of the cosmos's own vast destruction-and-formation cycle already detailed back in fascicle 4 — undermined entirely by the lack of insight into how that cycle actually works as a whole. Recalling only a destruction-eon, with everything apparently ending, suggests limits; recalling only a formation-eon, with everything freshly beginning, suggests boundlessness; surveying the full vertical extent from the lowest hell to the fourth dhyāna while finding no lateral edge produces "bounded above and below, boundless to the sides"; and a fourth position merely rephrases the third without saying anything new. The refutation, he notes, is refreshingly economical: the cosmic cycle simply keeps turning — a destroyed world always eventually reforms — so at no actual point does a genuinely final, measurable "boundary" ever present itself for these four positions to even be about; the very continuation the meditators themselves witnessed elsewhere in their own practice already disproves the fixed cosmic boundary each variant assumes.

12. The Tenth Doctrine: "Deathless" Evaders Who Never Give a Straight Answer

The treatise turns to a doctrine less a positive claim than an evasive posture — teachers who call themselves "unerringly deathless" yet answer every serious question with deliberate equivocation, distinguished into four types by exactly what fear or confusion drives their evasion.

Chinese Source

不死矯亂論者，謂四種不死矯亂外道，如經廣說應知。彼諸外道，若有人來，依最勝生道問善不善，依決定勝道問苦集滅道，便自稱言：我是不死亂者。隨於處所，依不死淨天不亂詰問，即於彼所問，以言矯亂，或託餘事方便避之，或但隨問者言辭而轉。

Ru-Yi Translation

The doctrine of evasive equivocation about the deathless: this refers to four kinds of non-Buddhist teachers who equivocate about the deathless, as stated in full in the sūtra. Should anyone come to question these teachers about what is wholesome or unwholesome via the path to a favorable rebirth, or about suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path via the path of decisive liberation, they simply declare: "I am one who is deathless and unerring." Yet whenever actually pressed with the same unerring questioning proper to the truly pure, deathless state, they answer with deliberate equivocation, or divert to some unrelated topic to avoid the question, or simply echo back whatever words the questioner themselves used.

是中第一不死亂者，覺未開悟；第二，於所證法起增上慢；第三，覺已開悟而未決定；第四，羸劣愚鈍。

Among these: the first kind of "deathless equivocator" has simply never awakened to any real understanding at all; the second harbors an inflated conceit about what they have actually attained; the third has genuinely awakened to some understanding but remains undecided and uncertain; and the fourth is simply feeble-minded and dull.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies "the deathless" here as a claimed heavenly destiny — certain long-lived, exalted heavens non-Buddhist ascetics aspired to and called by this name, distinct from the genuine "pure, deathless heavens" of Buddhism's own five pure-abode realms, home to non-returners who have actually realized the four noble truths and whose minds are genuinely, unmistakably liberated. The parallel between these two very different "deathless" claims sets up the whole doctrine's central irony: non-Buddhist ascetics borrow the prestige of a term that, properly understood, names a genuine attainment they have not made, then must scramble to avoid exposure whenever someone who might actually know the difference questions them closely. He distinguishes the four psychological profiles behind the evasion precisely: the first type genuinely knows nothing at all, of either the worldly path to a good rebirth or the noble path to liberation, and is afraid outright exposure of this total ignorance will disgrace them; the second has attained nothing beyond an ordinary, temporary worldly concentration, yet has mistaken it for genuine liberation, and clings to that false confidence when questioned; the third has some genuine, partial understanding of both paths but has not brought it to full certainty or realization, and so cannot answer with real conviction; and the fourth simply lacks the basic intellectual capacity to follow either path at all, not even understanding the words used to describe them.

13. Three Kinds of Fear Behind the Evasion

The treatise details exactly what each of the first three equivocators is actually afraid of when pressed — a psychology of evasion built entirely from the fear of being caught in a lie or exposed as a fraud.

Chinese Source

又復第一，怖畏妄語及怖畏他人知其無智，故不分明答言我無所知。第二，於自所證未得無畏，懼他詰問，怖畏妄語，怖畏邪見，故不分明說我有所證。第三，怖畏邪見，怖畏妄語，懼他詰問，故不分明說我不決定。如是三種，假託餘事以言矯亂。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further: the first, fearing to speak falsely and fearing others will discover his ignorance, will not plainly say "I know nothing." The second, having no real confidence in what he has attained, dreads being questioned — fearing to speak falsely, and fearing to be exposed as holding a wrong view — and so will not plainly say "I have attained something." The third, fearing to be exposed as holding a wrong view, and fearing to speak falsely, dreads being questioned, and so will not plainly say "I remain undecided." All three, in this way, divert to unrelated topics and answer with deliberate equivocation.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads all three profiles as trapped by the identical bind, differing only in exactly what they have to hide: the first, knowing absolutely nothing of either path, cannot admit total ignorance without instantly forfeiting the prestige of being called "deathless and unerring," so he mumbles evasively rather than simply saying "I don't know." The second has mistaken an ordinary, temporary worldly concentration for genuine liberation, and dreads that a real question from a real noble one — someone who could actually recognize the difference — would expose that mistake as either an outright lie or a genuine delusion, so he too refuses a plain answer rather than risk being shown wrong. The third has real but incomplete, uncertain understanding, and fears that admitting "I'm not sure" would sound exactly like ignorance or falsehood to a listener who cannot tell partial insight from no insight at all. In every case, Cháng Bǎi notes, the actual fear is not of speaking falsely in the abstract, but very concretely of being caught: caught knowing nothing, caught believing something false, or caught being unsure — and rather than risk that exposure, all three retreat into the same tactic, changing the subject or answering a question no one asked.

14. The Fourth Equivocator, and Doctrine Ten Closes as a Fearful, Deceitful View

Chinese Source

第四，唯懼他詰，於最勝生道及決定勝道皆不了達，於世文字亦不善知，而不分明說言我是愚鈍，都無所了。但反問彼，隨彼言辭而轉，以矯亂彼。由彼外道多怖畏故，依此見住。

Ru-Yi Translation

The fourth simply dreads being questioned at all: understanding neither the path to a good rebirth nor the path of decisive liberation, and not even skilled in ordinary worldly language, he will not plainly admit "I am dull and understand nothing at all." Instead, he merely questions the questioner back, and simply echoes whatever words come back to him, thereby equivocating. It is because these non-Buddhist teachers harbor so much fear that they cling to this view at all.

若有人來有所詰問，即以諂曲而行矯亂。當知此見是惡見攝，是故此論非如理說。

Whenever anyone comes with a genuine question, they respond only with flattering, crooked, evasive words. This view should be understood as belonging among the wrong views, and so this doctrine is not a soundly reasoned teaching.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the fourth type as simply too dull to muster even a coherent evasion of his own — lacking any grasp of either path, and lacking even ordinary verbal fluency, he survives scrutiny purely by turning every question back on the questioner and mirroring whatever answer comes back, a kind of evasion by pure imitation rather than by any actual strategy. Cháng Bǎi treats the treatise's final verdict — that all four types are driven by fear, and that fear-driven flattery and crookedness is itself the very definition of a wrong view — as the doctrine's real lesson: none of these four is a genuine philosophical position at all, unlike the previous nine doctrines, each of which at least represented a mistaken but sincere attempt at understanding. This one is pure self-protective performance, closing the tenth of sixteen doctrines and turning the treatise toward a genuine philosophical claim once more: that the self and the world arise from no cause whatsoever.

15. The Eleventh Doctrine: Everything Arises Without Any Cause At All

The treatise turns to a doctrine arising two distinct ways — from a specific gap in meditative memory, and from ordinary but hasty observation of nature — both converging on the same striking conclusion: nothing has a cause at all.

Chinese Source

無因見論者，謂依止靜慮及依止尋思。應知二種如經廣說。答：略而言之，見不相續以為先故，諸內外事無量差別種種生起。或復有時，見諸因緣空無果報。

Ru-Yi Translation

The doctrine positing no cause: this arises both from reliance on meditative concentration and from reliance on ordinary reasoning, as stated in full in the sūtra. Stated briefly: this arises from first observing a lack of continuity, whereby countless different internal and external phenomena appear to arise, and, at times, from observing what seem to be causes and conditions producing no actual result at all.

謂見世間，無有因緣，或時欻爾大風卒起，於一時間寂然止息；或時忽爾瀑河彌漫，於一時間頓則空竭；或時鬱爾果木敷榮，於一時間颯然衰顇。由如是故，起無因見，立無因論。

That is: they observe, apparently without any cause at all, a great wind suddenly rising and then, at some point, falling utterly still; a raging river suddenly swelling and then, at some point, running abruptly dry; or trees and fruit suddenly flourishing in lush abundance and then, at some point, withering all at once. It is on this basis that they arrive at the view of no cause, and establish the doctrine of no cause.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes the two paths into this doctrine carefully. The meditation-based version, per the Mahāvibhāṣā, traces to someone reborn as a human after dying from the formless "non-perception" heaven, who through meditative memory can recall everything since the moment perception resumed in that prior life but nothing at all from before it — since no perception was occurring to record — and concludes from this genuine gap in memory that their own existence simply began from nothing, with no cause behind it, then generalizes this to everything else in the cosmos. The reasoning-based version instead notices that each new body seems unable to recall the body immediately prior to it (so, by the same logic, there was no continuous cause connecting them), that offspring simply resemble their parents without the parents visibly "causing" that resemblance the way a cause produces a specific effect, and that natural wonders — a peacock's plumage, oddly shaped rocks, a thorn's sharp point, a flower's particular form — appear with no visible maker or cause behind their specific design, unlike a house or pot someone deliberately built.

He treats the second passage on wind, rivers, and withering trees as this same reasoning applied to weather and seasons: since the causes of sudden wind, flood, or drought are not immediately visible to an untrained eye the way a carpenter's tools are visible behind a chair, hasty observers conclude no cause is there at all, rather than recognizing that dependent origination operates through causes too fine, distant, or numerous to be casually noticed — a fundamentally different error from doctrine 1's Vārṣagaṇyas, who at least affirmed causation but misdescribed its mechanics; these thinkers deny causation exists at all.

16. Two Dilemmas Close the Case Against No Cause At All

Chinese Source

今應問彼：汝宿住念，為念無體？為念自我？若念無體，無體之法未曾串習，未曾經識，而能隨念，不應道理。若念自我，計我先無，後欻然生，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

One should press those relying on meditative memory: does your memory of past abodes take something with no substance as its object, or does it take a self as its object? If it takes something with no substance, then recollecting something never previously experienced or become familiar with makes no sense. If it takes a self as its object, then holding that this self first did not exist and then suddenly arose from nothing makes no sense either.

又汝何所欲？一切世間內外諸物種種生起，或欻然生起，為無因耶？為有因耶？若無因者，種種生起欻然而起，有時不生，不應道理。若有因者，我及世間無因而生，不應道理。

Further, to those relying on ordinary reasoning: what do you actually intend — does the world's varied array of internal and external phenomena, and their apparently sudden arising, occur without any cause, or with a cause? If without any cause, then their arising sometimes and not arising at other times makes no sense — an uncaused event should occur constantly and indiscriminately, not selectively. If with a cause, then insisting the self and the world arise without any cause at all makes no sense.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the first dilemma as exposing the meditation-based error directly: a genuine memory must be of something once actually experienced, so a "memory" of pure non-existence, of nothing at all, is not really a memory in any coherent sense; and if what is recalled is instead a self, then that self's sudden arising from nothing still needs some explanation — the mere gap in memory proves only that perception was absent for a time, not that the underlying continuum of causes and conditions producing this life ever actually stopped.

The second dilemma catches the reasoning-based error just as cleanly: if events genuinely had no cause at all, they should occur constantly, indiscriminately, and everywhere, since nothing would be regulating when or where they happen — yet wind rises and falls, rivers swell and recede, and trees flourish and wither in patterns, not randomly at every instant; that very selectivity is itself evidence of causes and conditions at work, however subtle or unnoticed. And once any cause at all is admitted anywhere, the doctrine's blanket claim that the self and world arise from absolutely no cause collapses entirely. With this, the eleventh of sixteen doctrines closes, and the treatise turns to its twelfth: the view that a being is simply annihilated at death.

17. The Twelfth Doctrine: Total Annihilation at Death — Seven Cases, Already Previewed

The treatise takes up a doctrine glimpsed already as a brief aside back in fascicle 6's discussion of permanence, now given its own full treatment: the claim that a being is simply, totally extinguished at death, with nothing whatsoever continuing afterward.

Chinese Source

斷見論者，謂如有一，若沙門、若婆羅門，起如是見，立如是論：乃至我有麤色四大所造之身，任持未壞，爾時有病、有癰、有箭。若我死後，斷壞無有，爾時我善斷滅。如是欲界諸天，色界諸天，若無色界空無邊處所攝，乃至非想非非想處所攝，廣說如經。

Ru-Yi Translation

The doctrine of annihilation: there are certain renunciants or brahmins who hold such a view and establish such a doctrine — that as long as I possess this coarse body made of the four great elements, and it remains intact, unbroken, I am subject to sickness, to festering wounds, to the sting of resentment. But once I die, this body is utterly destroyed and ceases to exist, and at that time I am perfectly, completely extinguished. So too — stated in full as in the sūtra — for the desire realm's gods, the form realm's gods, and those belonging to the formless realm's sphere of infinite space, up through the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception.

彼如是思：若我死後復有身者，應不作業而得果異熟。若我體性一切永無，是則應無受業果異熟。觀此二種，理俱不可。是故起如是見，立如是論：我身死已，斷壞無有，猶如瓦石，若一破已，不可還合；彼亦如是，道理應知。

They reason thus: were I to have a further body after death, then I would obtain a ripened karmic result without having performed the corresponding deed — this seems wrong. Yet were my own nature to be utterly, permanently nonexistent even now, then there would be no one at all to receive the ripened result of karma already performed — this too seems wrong. Weighing these two positions, they find neither acceptable, and so arrive at this view and establish this doctrine: that once my body dies, it is utterly destroyed and ceases to exist — like a shattered tile or stone that can never be put back together — and it should be understood that I am exactly the same.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps the seven classical "cases" of this doctrine directly onto the realms surveyed all the way back in fascicles 4 and 5: ordinary desire-realm humans and the desire realm's six heavens (counted together as two cases), the form realm's four dhyāna heavens (counted as one case, differentiated by which specific level a being is annihilated from), and each of the four formless spheres up through the peak of existence (four further cases) — seven total, each holding that whatever level a being currently occupies, death from that level means total, final extinction, a curious kind of "liberation" achieved simply by ceasing to exist rather than by ending craving.

He reads the doctrine's own stated reasoning as a genuine dilemma its holders felt unable to escape: accepting real rebirth after death seems to imply receiving a result with no corresponding cause (since, on their reading, this life's specific deeds seem to end with this life's specific body); yet denying any continuing self even in this present life seems to imply no one is even here now to experience this life's own karmic results, which plainly contradicts felt experience. Unable to resolve this tension through the framework of impermanent, causally connected aggregates the treatise itself will offer, they resolved it instead by simply declaring total annihilation at the moment of death — the tile-and-stone image capturing their sense of finality: once broken, gone for good, with no possibility of any further connection at all.

18. Aggregates Never Actually Stop, and "Self" Was Never There to Be Annihilated

Chinese Source

今應問彼：汝何所欲？為蘊斷滅？為我斷滅耶？若言蘊斷滅者，蘊體無常，因果展轉生起不絕，而言斷滅，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

One should press them: what do you actually intend — is it the aggregates that are annihilated, or is it a self? If you say the aggregates are annihilated, then note that the aggregates, being impermanent, continue on through an unbroken chain of cause and effect — so calling this "annihilation" makes no sense.

若言我斷，汝先所說麤色四大所造之身有病、有癰、有箭，欲界諸天，色界諸天，若無色界空無邊處所攝，乃至非想非非想處所攝，不應道理。如是若蘊斷滅故，若我斷滅故，皆不應理。是故此論非如理說。

If you say a self is annihilated, then everything you described earlier — a coarse body made of the four elements subject to sickness and resentment, or the desire realm's gods, the form realm's gods, or those belonging to any of the formless spheres — was, in every case, only ever a description of the aggregates, never of any actual self at all; so claiming this proves a self is annihilated makes no sense. Thus, whether the aggregates are said to be annihilated, or a self is said to be annihilated, neither position holds up — and so this doctrine is not a soundly reasoned teaching.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as an elegant pincer built directly from the treatise's earlier, exhaustive refutation of self back in fascicle 6: if what supposedly ends at death is merely the aggregates, then note that the aggregates never actually stop at all in the sense the doctrine needs — one moment's aggregates give way to the next moment's, one life's aggregates give way to a new life's, in an unbroken causal chain, so nothing genuinely final ever occurs; but if what supposedly ends is a separate self, then everything the doctrine's own holders described in such detail — a body of four elements, rebirth in specific heavens — was, from the very first line, only ever a description of the aggregates themselves, never of any findable self standing apart from them. Since fascicle 6 already showed no such self exists to be found anywhere among, within, apart from, or unrelated to the aggregates, there is simply nothing left over for "annihilation" to happen to. With this, the twelfth of sixteen doctrines closes, and the treatise turns to its thirteenth: the view that denies cause and effect, and even the very existence of things, altogether.

19. The Thirteenth Doctrine: Two Kinds of "Emptiness" Gone Wrong

The treatise turns to a doctrine of denial more radical than annihilationism — one that denies moral cause and effect outright — and pointedly identifies a second, internal version of the same error: a badly misread Mahāyāna teaching on emptiness itself.

Chinese Source

空見論者，謂如有一，若沙門、若婆羅門，起如是見，立如是論：無有施與，無有愛養，無有祠祀，廣說乃至世間無有真阿羅漢。

Ru-Yi Translation

The doctrine of the empty view: there are certain renunciants or brahmins who hold such a view and establish such a doctrine — that there is no such thing as giving, no such thing as caring for one's elders, no such thing as making offerings, and so on in full detail, up to and including the claim that there exists no genuine arhat anywhere in the world.

復起如是見，立如是論：無有一切諸法體相。

There are also some who, further, hold this view and establish this doctrine: that no phenomenon whatsoever has any real characteristic or nature at all.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes two entirely different sources for the same word "emptiness" appearing here. The first is straightforward non-Buddhist moral nihilism: a flat denial that generosity produces any merit, that caring for parents or teachers means anything, that ritual offerings accomplish anything, or that any genuinely liberated being — an arhat — has ever actually existed; this is simply denying cause and effect wholesale, motivated, like several earlier doctrines, by wanting no moral constraint on behavior at all.

The second, far more consequential for Cháng Bǎi's own tradition, is what later Yogācāra scholasticism calls "wrongly-grasped emptiness" (惡取空) — a serious misreading of the Mahāyāna's own teaching that phenomena are empty of fixed, independent self-nature. Properly understood, he stresses, this teaching means precisely that things exist dependently — arising through causes and conditions, and empty of any fixed nature precisely because they are dependently arisen, the two facts implying each other rather than contradicting each other. Practitioners who grasp only the word "empty" and not this deeper structure end up sliding into denying that phenomena have ANY characteristic whatsoever — collapsing the careful middle path between "everything is a solid, independent thing" and "nothing at all really exists in any sense," landing in the second extreme just as surely as if they had never encountered emptiness teaching at all. Cháng Bǎi treats this second case as the treatise's own quiet warning to its Mahāyāna readers: emptiness teaching, badly misapplied, produces the exact same moral and ontological nihilism as the crudest non-Buddhist denial of cause and effect — the destination is identical even when the vocabulary used to arrive there sounds sophisticated and properly Buddhist.

20. Six Cases of Nihilism, Each Born From Watching Too Little For Too Short a Time

The treatise catalogues six specific observations — each genuinely seen through meditative clairvoyance, each generalized into a sweeping denial of karma, rebirth, or genuine attainment — showing a single recurring error: mistaking a limited glimpse for the whole causal picture.

Chinese Source

又依世間諸靜慮故，見世施主一期壽命恆行布施無有斷絕，從此命終，生下賤家，貧窮匱乏。彼作是思：定無施與、愛養、祠祀。復見有人，一期壽中恆行妙行，或行惡行；見彼命終，墮於惡趣，生諸那落迦，或往善趣，生於天上樂世界中。彼作是思：定無妙行及與惡行，亦無妙行、惡行二業果異熟。

Ru-Yi Translation

Relying on worldly meditative concentration, they see a generous donor who practiced giving without interruption their whole life, only to die and be reborn into a lowly, impoverished family — and conclude: there is definitely no such thing as giving, caring for elders, or making offerings. They likewise see one person who practiced wholesome conduct their whole life die and fall into an unfortunate destiny, one of the hells, while another who practiced unwholesome conduct dies and goes to a fortunate destiny, a heavenly world of pleasure — and conclude: there is definitely no such thing as wholesome or unwholesome conduct, nor any ripened result from either.

復見有一剎帝利種命終之後，生婆羅門、吠舍、戍陀羅諸種姓中；或婆羅門命終之後，生剎帝利、吠舍、戍陀羅諸種姓中。彼作是思：定無此世剎帝利等，從彼世間剎帝利等種姓中來；亦無彼世剎帝利等，從此世間剎帝利等種姓中去。又復觀見諸離欲者生於下地。

They further see a member of the warrior caste die and be reborn into the priestly, merchant, or laborer caste — or a member of the priestly caste die and be reborn into the warrior, merchant, or laborer caste — and likewise observe those who had achieved freedom from desire nonetheless reborn into a lower realm afterward, and conclude: there is definitely no continuity at all between "this world" and "that world," no fixed connection carrying any caste or any level of attainment from one life into the next.

又見母命終已，生而為女，女命終已，還作其母；父終為子，子還作父。彼見父母不決定已，作如是思：世間畢定無父無母。

They see a mother die and be reborn as her own daughter, or a daughter die and be reborn as her own mother; a father die and become his own son, a son become his own father. Having seen the roles of parent and child prove so utterly undetermined, they conclude: the world definitely has no such thing as a true father or mother at all.

或復見人身壞命終，或生無想，或生無色，或入涅槃，求彼生處不能得見。彼作是思：決定無有化生眾生，以彼處所不可知故。

They further see a person's body perish, and that person be reborn in the non-perception heaven, or in a formless realm, or enter nirvāṇa — and, seeking to observe the intermediate being that supposedly carries them there, find nothing at all visible. They conclude: there is definitely no such thing as a spontaneously arising intermediate-state being, since its whereabouts can never be found.

或於自身起阿羅漢增上慢已，臨命終時，遂見生相。彼作是念：世間必無真阿羅漢，如是廣說。

Or someone, having conceived the inflated conceit of having personally attained arhatship, sees, at the moment of their own death, the unmistakable sign of a further rebirth actually arising before them. They conclude: there must be no genuine arhat anywhere in the world at all — and so on in full detail.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads all six cases as variations on one single failure of method: real meditative clairvoyance, genuinely showing one or two lifetimes, mistaken for the entire causal picture spanning countless lives. The generous donor's poverty and the virtuous person's hellish rebirth both make sense once one recognizes that karma can ripen across many lifetimes, not necessarily the very next one — a single unlucky sequence proves nothing about karma in general, any more than one flooded field disproves that rain causes crops to grow. Caste and rebirth-level not carrying over between lives is not evidence against causality but simply evidence that caste and dhyāna-attainment are not, in themselves, what karma actually tracks. Parent-child role reversal across lives, however striking to witness, only refutes a *fixed*, essential parent-child bond — never the plain biological fact that a given birth still depended on a given set of parents in that one life. The intermediate-state being (China's classical term for what carries consciousness from death to rebirth) is genuinely invisible in exactly three cases — the formless realm (no body at all), nirvāṇa (no rebirth occurs), and the non-perception heaven (present but too subtle even for ordinary clairvoyance to detect) — and these three blind spots, not any absence of an intermediate state elsewhere, misled the observer. And the false arhat's story is Cháng Bǎi's most cautionary case of all: a diligent but under-taught monk who quickly attained the mundane four dhyānas through strong prior roots, mistook each for a genuine noble fruit up through arhatship, then, confronted at death by the plain sign of the fourth dhyāna's own rebirth still awaiting him, concluded not that his own attainment had been mistaken but that liberation itself must be a lie — a slander so severe it is said to have collapsed his rebirth prospect straight down into the Avīci hell instead. Cháng Bǎi treats this last case especially as a warning against exactly the kind of overconfident self-assessment any serious meditator, ancient or modern, can fall into.

21. The Mahāyāna's Own Blind Spot: Emptiness Badly Misread

The treatise then turns inward, explaining precisely how its own tradition's students can arrive at the identical nihilistic conclusion — not through non-Buddhist reasoning, but through genuinely misreading the Mahāyāna's own teaching on emptiness.

Chinese Source

復何因緣，或有起如是見，立如是論：無有一切諸法體相？答：以於如來所說甚深經中，相似甚深離言說法不能如實正覺了故。又於安立法相不如正理而思惟故，起於空見。彼作是念：決定無有諸法體相。

Ru-Yi Translation

By what further cause do some arrive at this view and establish this doctrine — that no phenomenon whatsoever has any real characteristic at all? Because they fail to genuinely, correctly awaken to what the Tathāgata actually meant in his profound scriptures by teachings that merely resemble a profound truth beyond all verbal expression — and because they fail to reflect soundly on how phenomena's characteristics are conventionally established, they give rise to this empty view. They come to think: phenomena definitely have no real characteristic at all.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies this as the classical Yogācāra critique of what later tradition calls "wrongly grasped emptiness" (惡取空) — students of the Mahāyāna's own profound teaching on emptiness who grasp only its most provocative-sounding surface ("all phenomena are empty of self-nature") without the deeper structure that actually makes that teaching coherent: emptiness and dependent arising imply each other, rather than contradicting each other. He maps this onto Yogācāra's own three-natures framework: the imagined nature (parikalpita) — like a dreamer's belief in the dream's people and places as genuinely real — is indeed entirely unreal, purely projected; but the dependent nature (paratantra) — the dream-mind's own actual, causally arisen activity, without which no dream imagery could appear at all — genuinely, conventionally exists, even though it lacks the fixed, independent self-nature the imagined nature falsely projects onto it; and the perfected nature (pariniṣpanna) — the dream-mind's and dream-content's shared, ultimate empty nature once fully realized — is likewise genuinely real in its own way, not itself another mere fiction.

The wrongly-grasping student, he explains, hears "all is empty" and concludes that even the dependent nature — the real, causally functioning basis beneath every provisional label — must be swept away along with the imagined nature it was never supposed to be confused with in the first place. But if the dependent, causally-arisen basis is itself denied, nothing at all remains to be empty of anything — the very teaching "everything provisional is, in truth, empty" collapses into incoherence, since there is no longer any "everything" left standing to make the claim about. Cháng Bǎi treats this as the treatise's most serious warning of the whole sixteen-doctrine survey precisely because it targets its own readers: a Mahāyāna practitioner who mishandles emptiness lands in exactly the same denial of cause, effect, and moral reality as the crudest non-Buddhist nihilist, dressed in far more sophisticated-sounding language.

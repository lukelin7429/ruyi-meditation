Fascicle 7 · 瑜伽師地論卷第七 · Continuing Directly from Fascicle 6

Why Good People Suffer and Bad People Prosper

Fascicle 6 ended mid-topic, having just closed the doctrine positing permanence — not a natural stopping point, simply where the physical scroll divides. This page picks up exactly there: the doctrine of prior deeds as sole cause, born from watching the honest suffer and the crooked thrive, followed by the doctrine of a sovereign creator — continuing the systematic survey of sixteen non-Buddhist doctrines within the Three Grounds of Applied and Sustained Thought.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 7 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. The Sixth Doctrine: All Suffering Traces to Past Deeds Alone

Continuing directly from fascicle 6's close, the treatise takes up the sixth of sixteen non-Buddhist doctrines — the ascetic conviction that every present suffering has one and only one cause: karma committed in a past life, extinguishable only through deliberate self-mortification.

Chinese Source

宿作因論者，謂如有一，若沙門、若婆羅門，起如是見，立如是論，廣說如經。凡諸世間所有士夫補特伽羅所受者，謂現所受苦。皆由宿作為因者，謂由宿惡為因。由勤精進吐舊業故者，謂由現法極自苦行。

Ru-Yi Translation

The doctrine of prior deeds as cause: there are certain renunciants or brahmins who hold such a view and establish such a doctrine, stated in full as in the sūtra. Whatever suffering any person in the world experiences is invariably caused by prior deeds — that is, by past unwholesome karma. Through diligent, strenuous effort, one purges this old karma — that is, through extreme self-mortification practiced in this very life.

現在新業由不作因之所害故者，謂諸不善業。如是於後不復有漏者，謂一向是善性故，說後無漏。由無漏故業盡者，謂諸惡業。由業盡故苦盡者，謂宿因所作及現法方便所招苦惱。由苦盡故得證苦邊者，謂證餘生相續苦盡。謂無繫外道作如是計。

New karma in the present is prevented by the cause of non-action — that is, by refraining from every unwholesome deed. Thereafter, no further contamination arises — since one's nature has become entirely wholesome, no further contamination follows. Because there is no further contamination, karma is exhausted — namely, every unwholesome karma. Because karma is exhausted, suffering is exhausted — namely, the suffering produced both by prior karma and by present ascetic effort. And because suffering is exhausted, one realizes the very limit of suffering — that is, one realizes the complete cessation of suffering across every future rebirth. It is the Nirgrantha ascetics — the unclothed renunciants — who hold this position.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the holders of this doctrine as the Nirgranthas ("unbound ones"), the ascetic tradition better known to history as Jainism — renunciants who went unclothed, believing modesty itself a needless attachment, and who practiced extreme physical austerity: standing on one leg for hours, lying exposed on scorching ground under direct sun, or resting on beds of thorns and sharp grass. Their theology, as the treatise summarizes it, runs in a precise causal chain: every scrap of present suffering was earned entirely by past unwholesome deeds; deliberately embracing severe physical suffering now "burns off" that old karmic debt directly; refraining from all new action prevents any further karma from accumulating; once nothing new accumulates and the old debt is fully paid, the practitioner becomes permanently free of affliction; freedom from affliction exhausts every unwholesome karma still outstanding; exhausted karma means exhausted suffering; and exhausted suffering means final release from every future rebirth's suffering altogether. Cháng Bǎi flags one crucial point up front: their "freedom from contamination" is not the same uncontaminated realm Buddhism means by that term — it is simply becoming entirely, non-karmically "good" in a naive sense, not the wisdom-based liberation the treatise's own path leads to.

2. Why They Believed This: Good People Suffer, Bad People Prosper

Chinese Source

由見現法士夫作用不決定故。所以者何？彼見世間雖具正方便，而招於苦；雖具邪方便，而致於樂。彼如是思：若由現法士夫作用為彼因者，彼應顛倒。由彼所見非顛倒故，是故彼皆以宿作為因。

Ru-Yi Translation

This arises from observing that a person's present conduct does not reliably determine their outcome. Why is this? Because they observe that some, though acting through entirely proper means, nonetheless meet with suffering, while others, though acting through improper means, nonetheless arrive at happiness. They reason thus: were present conduct truly the cause of these outcomes, this observation would have to be a distortion — yet what they observe is plainly not a distortion. Therefore, they conclude, prior deeds alone must be the true cause in every case.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this as the doctrine's genuinely sympathetic starting point — the same puzzle that troubles honest people in every era: an upright, hardworking merchant goes bankrupt through no fault of his own, while a swindler grows rich; a careful, ethical person falls gravely ill, while a careless, cruel one enjoys robust health. Since this mismatch between present conduct and present outcome is plainly, repeatedly real and not some trick of perception, the Nirgranthas reasoned that present conduct could not be the operative cause at all — the entire explanation must instead lie in karma already committed long before this life began, with present virtue or vice simply irrelevant to what is now being repaid.

3. The Refutation: If Only Past Karma Matters, Why Does Austerity Do Anything?

Chinese Source

今應問彼：汝何所欲？現法方便所招之苦，為用宿作為因？為用現法方便為因？若用宿作為因者，汝先所說，由勤精進吐舊業故，現在新業由不作因之所害故，如是於後不復有漏，乃至廣說，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

One should press them thus: what, precisely, do you mean? Is the suffering brought about by present ascetic effort itself caused by prior deeds, or is it caused by that present effort? If it is caused by prior deeds, then your own earlier claim — that diligent effort purges old karma, that refraining from new action prevents its accumulation, and that freedom from contamination thereafter follows, and so on in full — makes no sense.

若用現法方便為因者，汝先所說，凡諸世間所有士夫補特伽羅所受，皆由宿作為因，不應道理。如是現法方便苦宿作為因故，現法士夫用為因故，皆不應道理，是故此論非如理說。

If it is caused by present effort, then your own earlier claim — that every experience of every person in the world is invariably caused by prior deeds alone — makes no sense. Thus, whether the suffering of present ascetic effort is said to be caused by prior deeds, or the present person's own effort is said to be its cause, neither makes sense — and so this doctrine is not a soundly reasoned teaching.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi presents this as an elegant two-horned trap built directly from the doctrine's own claims. The Nirgranthas need the pain of austerity itself to actually accomplish something — burning off old karma — which only makes sense if that pain has a real causal effect right now. But if the pain of standing on one leg or lying on thorns is itself just one more instance of "suffering caused entirely by prior deeds," then the ascetic's present exertion is doing no actual work at all — it is merely one more scene in a script already written by the past, no more capable of purging anything than sitting idle would be. Yet if the ascetic's present effort does actually cause something — actually burns off karma, as claimed — then present action clearly can produce real effects after all, directly contradicting the doctrine's founding claim that only past deeds ever determine what a person experiences. The doctrine, Cháng Bǎi notes, needs present action to be causally impotent (to explain why good people can suffer despite virtuous present conduct) and causally potent (to explain why ascetic practice actually works) at the very same time — a straightforward contradiction that dissolves the entire theory.

4. The Real Answer: Suffering Has Three Different Kinds of Cause

Having refuted the doctrine's rigid single-cause claim, the treatise states its own considerably more nuanced account: suffering arises sometimes from past karma alone, sometimes from a genuine mixture of past and present, and sometimes from present effort alone — the very complexity the Nirgranthas' theory could not accommodate.

Chinese Source

我今當說如實因相。或有諸苦唯用宿作為因。猶如有一，自業增上力故，生諸惡趣或貧窮家。或復有苦雜因所生。謂如有一，因邪事王，不獲樂果而反致苦。

Ru-Yi Translation

I shall now state the true nature of cause. There is suffering caused by prior deeds alone — as when someone, through the sheer dominant force of their own past karma, is born into an unfortunate destiny or into poverty. There is also suffering produced by a mixture of causes — as when someone, serving a king through improper means, fails to obtain happy results and instead meets with suffering.

如事於王如是，由諸言說商賈等業、由事農業、由劫盜業、或於他有情作損害事，若有福者，獲得富樂，若無福者，雖設功用而無果遂。或復有法，純由現在功用因得。如新所造引餘有業；或聽聞正法，於法覺察；或復發起威儀業路；或復修學工巧業處；如是等類，唯因現在士夫功用。

As with serving a king, so too with a writer's craft, with trade, with farming, with theft, or with harming other beings: one who has merit obtains wealth and happiness even through improper means, while one without merit finds no result despite great effort — such results are a mixture of past merit and present method. And there is, further, that which is obtained purely through present effort: newly performed karma that draws a further rebirth; hearing the true Dharma and awakening insight into it; the arising of one's own bodily conduct and bearing; or learning a craft or trade — all such things depend solely on the present person's own effort.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this threefold scheme as directly explaining the very puzzle that misled the Nirgranthas in the first place, without needing to deny present action any causal power at all. Some suffering genuinely does trace to past karma alone — being born into poverty or an unfortunate destiny through the sheer weight of past unwholesome deeds, with nothing in the present life needed to explain it. But most of ordinary life's mismatches between effort and outcome are actually mixed: a corrupt official who serves the king dishonestly and still prospers has a reserve of past merit cushioning present wrongdoing, while one with no such reserve, doing the same wrong, meets only misfortune — the very same present method producing different results depending on what past merit is or isn't already in the bank, which explains the "unfair" cases the Nirgranthas found so troubling without abandoning present causation altogether. And a considerable range of ordinary experience — the new karma a person is actively creating right now, the insight that dawns from actually listening to the Dharma, the plain physical actions of standing, sitting, and moving, and any skill a person deliberately sets out to learn — depends purely on present effort, with no need to invoke past karma to explain it at all. Cháng Bǎi notes this threefold analysis is simply more honest to how cause and effect actually interact than either extreme — pure fatalism or pure present-only causation — closing this sixth doctrine before the treatise turns to its seventh: the claim that a sovereign lord alone creates everything in the world.

5. The Seventh Doctrine: A Sovereign Creator Behind Everything

The treatise turns to the doctrine that a single sovereign power — Śiva, Brahmā, or any of several other candidates — created and directs the entire world, tracing its appeal to the same frustration everyone eventually meets: wanting one outcome and getting another.

Chinese Source

自在等作者論者，謂如有一，或沙門、或婆羅門，起如是見，立如是論：凡諸世間所有士夫補特伽羅所受，彼一切或以自在變化為因，或餘丈夫變化為因，諸如是等。謂說自在等不平等因論者作如是計。

Ru-Yi Translation

The doctrine of a sovereign lord as maker: there are certain renunciants or brahmins who hold such a view and establish such a doctrine — that whatever any person in the world experiences is caused, in every case, either by the transformative power of a sovereign lord, or by the transformative power of some other great being, and so on. It is those who propound this doctrine of an unequal cause who hold this position.

彼由現見於因果中，世間有情不隨欲轉，故作此計。所以者何？現見世間有情，於彼因時欲修淨業，不遂本欲，反更為惡。於彼果時，願生善趣樂世界中，不遂本欲，墮惡趣等。意謂受樂，不遂所欲，反受諸苦。

They arrive at this by observing that beings in the world, with respect to cause and effect, cannot make things turn out as they wish. Why is this? Because they observe beings who, at the time of acting, wish to perform wholesome deeds yet fail to realize that wish and instead do wrong; and who, at the time of receiving results, wish to be reborn into a fortunate, happy world yet fail to realize that wish and instead fall into an unfortunate destiny. They wish for happiness, and, failing to obtain what they wish, instead experience suffering.

由見此故，彼作是思：世間諸物必應別有作者、生者，及變化者為彼物父，謂自在天，或復其餘。

Seeing this, they reason: everything in the world must therefore have some further maker, producer, and transformer standing behind it as its father — namely, a sovereign lord, or some other such being.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this doctrine's motivating puzzle as genuinely universal: everyone, at some point, sincerely intends to act well and finds themselves instead speaking or acting badly in the moment; everyone who hopes for a happy rebirth has, at some point, watched that hope fail to materialize. Rather than trace this gap between intention and outcome to the ordinary, impersonal working of karma across countless prior causes — which the treatise has spent this whole fascicle patiently explaining — these thinkers concluded that some further personal agent must be pulling the strings behind the scenes, unequally and unilaterally deciding outcomes no one asked for. He notes the doctrine's "sovereign lord" is not limited to one candidate: besides Śiva and Brahmā, later scholastic sources list time, space, an original first-cause, nature itself, empty space, and a permanent self as rival answers various non-Buddhist schools proposed for the same underlying craving — a single, ultimate, personal or quasi-personal explanation for why the world does not simply give people what they want.

6. Four Dilemmas Aimed at the Sovereign Creator

The treatise answers with a tightly structured fourfold refutation, previewed in its own summary verse, testing the creator's power, its relationship to the world, its motive, and its status as sole cause.

Chinese Source

今當問彼。嗢柁南曰：功能無體性，攝不攝相違，有用及無用，為因成過失。汝何所欲？自在天等變化功能，為用業方便為因？為無因耶？若用業方便為因者，唯此功能用業方便為因，非餘世間，不應道理。若無因者，唯此功能無因而有，非世間物，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

One should now question them. A summary verse states: function has no substance; inclusion and exclusion both contradict; usefulness and uselessness; and as cause, every option fails. What do you actually intend: does the sovereign lord's transformative power have, as its cause, deliberate effort and method, or does it have no cause at all? If it has deliberate effort and method as its cause, then insisting only this power operates this way, while nothing else in the world does, makes no sense. If it has no cause at all, then insisting only this power exists uncaused, while nothing else in the world does, makes no sense either.

又汝何所欲？此大自在為墮世間攝？為不攝耶？若言攝者，此大自在則同世法，而能遍生世間，不應道理。若不攝者，則是解脫，而言能生世間，不應道理。

Further, what do you actually intend: is this sovereign lord included within the world, or excluded from it? If included, then this lord is no different from any other worldly phenomenon — yet claiming it alone can universally give rise to the entire world makes no sense. If excluded, then this lord must already be liberated — yet claiming a liberated being still produces the defiled, conditioned world makes no sense either.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the first dilemma as testing the very mechanism by which this lord supposedly creates: if its power to transform requires the same deliberate effort and method any ordinary being's action requires, there is no principled reason to withhold that same causal power from ordinary beings creating their own circumstances — the lord loses its special status; if instead its power arises with no cause whatsoever, there is equally no reason to deny the same uncaused status to the rest of the world, which would then need no creator either.

The second dilemma catches the lord in an inescapable double bind regarding its own status: if this lord is itself part of the conditioned world — subject to the same causal laws as everything else — it cannot simultaneously stand outside that world as its universal, unconditioned source; but if it is genuinely apart from and unconditioned by the world, it has effectively already attained the liberated state Buddhism itself describes, and a liberated being, by definition, generates no further defiled rebirth or suffering at all — so it could not be the source of an ordinary, suffering-filled cosmos either. Neither being fully "of" the world nor fully "beyond" it leaves any coherent place for the lord to actually stand while creating that world.

7. Two More Dilemmas Close the Case Against a Creator

Chinese Source

又汝何所欲？為有用故變生世間？為無用耶？若有用者，則於彼用無有自在，而於世間有自在者，不應道理。若無用者，無有所須，而生世間，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, what do you actually intend: does the lord transform and produce the world because it has some use for doing so, or without any use at all? If it has a use, then it lacks mastery even over that very use — yet claiming it has mastery over the world makes no sense. If it has no use at all, then producing the world with no need or purpose whatsoever likewise makes no sense.

又汝何所欲？此所出生，為唯大自在為因？為亦取餘為因耶？若唯大自在為因者，是則若時有大自在，是時則有出生；若時有出生，是時則有大自在；而言出生用大自在為因者，不應道理。

Further, what do you actually intend: is the world's production caused solely by the sovereign lord, or are other factors also its cause? If solely by the lord, then wherever the lord exists, the world's production must exist, and wherever production exists, the lord must exist — the two would have to be permanently co-present, making it senseless to still speak of the lord as the world's cause, as though one produced the other.

若言亦取餘為因者，此唯取樂欲為因？為除樂欲更取餘為因？若唯取樂欲為因者，此樂欲為唯取大自在為因？為亦取餘為因耶？若唯取大自在為因者，若時有大自在，是時則有樂欲；若時有樂欲，是時則有大自在；便應無始常有出生，此亦不應道理。又於彼欲無有自在，而言於世間物有自在者，不應道理。

If other factors are also its cause, is desire alone that further factor, or something else besides desire? If desire alone, then is this desire itself caused solely by the sovereign lord, or by something else as well? If solely by the lord, then wherever the lord exists desire must exist, and wherever desire exists the lord must exist — meaning production would have to be beginninglessly, constantly ongoing, which plainly contradicts experience. And, further, if the lord itself lacks mastery over its own desire, then claiming it has mastery over the things of the world makes no sense at all.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the third dilemma as exposing a bind built into the very idea of divine motive: a creator who acts from need or purpose is thereby shown to lack full self-mastery — a truly sovereign, complete being should want for nothing — yet a creator with absolutely no purpose at all creating an entire elaborate cosmos looks less like sovereign power than arbitrary caprice, "the fault of a madman," as the parallel passage in the Xianyang Shengjiao Lun puts it. The fourth dilemma then chases the "sole cause" claim into an infinite regress: if the lord alone causes the world's production, lord and production should be permanently co-present, with neither ever preceding the other — yet a "cause" that never actually precedes its effect is not functioning as a cause at all; and if desire is added as a further, necessary factor, the same co-presence problem simply resurfaces one level up, forcing the theory to explain what causes the desire, and so on without end, while also revealing that a lord still subject to desire is not actually the fully self-mastered, sovereign being the doctrine needs it to be. With all four approaches — function, inclusion, usefulness, and sole causation — shown incoherent, the treatise concludes: "this doctrine is not a soundly reasoned teaching," closing the seventh of sixteen doctrines and turning to the eighth: the claim that ritually killing living beings is itself the true Dharma.

Fascicle 4 · 瑜伽師地論卷第四 · Three New Grounds Begin

Realms of Thought, Realms of Rebirth

This page opens Ru-Yi's translation of fascicle 4, which leaves the Mind Stage behind and begins the next three stages at once: the Ground of Applied and Sustained Thought, the Ground of Only Sustained Thought, and the Ground Without Either — defined by how much conceptual searching still accompanies a state of mind, and mapped here onto the entire structure of conditioned rebirth, from the desire realm's hells up through the formless heavens.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 4 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. Three New Grounds: Applied and Sustained Thought

Fascicle 4 leaves the Mind Stage behind and opens the next three of the treatise's seventeen stages together, distinguished by how much conceptual "thought" accompanies a given state of mind: a coarse, searching kind, and a finer, sustained kind.

Chinese Source

已說意地。云何有尋有伺地？云何無尋唯伺地？云何無尋無伺地？

Ru-Yi Translation

The Mind Stage has now been explained. What is the Ground of Applied and Sustained Thought? What is the Ground of No Applied Thought but Only Sustained Thought? What is the Ground Without Applied or Sustained Thought?

總嗢柁南曰：界相如理不如理　雜染等起最為後

The summary verse states: realm, mark, what accords with reason and what does not; defilement and its arising come last.

如是三地，略以五門施設建立。一、界施設建立，二、相施設建立，三、如理作意施設建立，四、不如理作意施設建立，五、雜染等起施設建立。

These three grounds, briefly stated, are established through five gateways: first, establishing the realms; second, establishing their marks; third, establishing what accords with reason; fourth, establishing what does not accord with reason; fifth, establishing defilement and its arising.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi frames these three grounds as covering the entire ordinary universe of conditioned rebirth: the desire realm and the lower meditative attainments, still busy with both coarse applied thought and finer sustained thought; an intermediate state with only the finer kind; and the higher meditative absorptions free of both. He treats the whole of this fascicle, and the two after it, as elaborating these three grounds through five lenses in turn: which realms fall into which ground, what each ground's defining marks are, what sound reasoning within that state looks like, what distorted reasoning looks like, and finally how defilement itself actually arises and takes hold.

2. Eight Ways to Establish the Three Realms

Chinese Source

別嗢柁南曰：數處量壽受用生　自體因緣果分別

Ru-Yi Translation

A further summary verse states: number, place, measure, lifespan, enjoyment, birth; own body, and the analysis of cause, condition, and result.

當知界建立由八相。一、數建立，二、處建立，三、有情量建立，四、有情壽建立，五、有情受用建立，六、生建立，七、自體建立，八、因緣果建立。

It should be understood that the realms are established through eight aspects: first, establishing their number; second, establishing their places; third, establishing beings' physical measure; fourth, establishing beings' lifespan; fifth, establishing beings' enjoyment; sixth, establishing their birth; seventh, establishing their own body; eighth, establishing cause, condition, and result.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this eight-part outline as governing everything through the rest of the "realm" section: how many realms there are and how they relate to liberation, where beings in each realm actually live, how large their bodies are, how long they live, what they eat and how they experience pleasure and pain, how they are born, what their bodies are actually made of, and finally the specific causes, conditions, and results that produce rebirth into each one. He reads this as a genuinely comprehensive survey — everything a being might want to know about its own existential situation, laid out methodically before the treatise even begins discussing how to get free of it.

3. The Three Realms, and What Falls Outside Them

Chinese Source

云何界施設建立？略有三界。謂欲界、色界、無色界。如是三種，名墮攝界。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the establishing of the realms? Briefly, there are three realms: the desire realm, the form realm, and the formless realm. These three are called the realms comprised within conditioned falling.

非墮攝界者，謂方便，并薩迦耶滅，及無戲論無漏界。

What is not comprised within falling is: skillful means, together with the cessation of the view of a perishable body, and the uncontaminated realm free of conceptual elaboration.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads "falling" as naming ordinary conditioned rebirth: an unawakened being's mind, however refined, still falls within one of the three realms and remains trapped in the cycle of birth and death. What escapes this falling altogether is named in three parts, corresponding to three of the four noble truths: skillful means, the path itself — the actual practices, such as the four foundations of mindfulness, that lead out of the three realms; the cessation of the view of a perishable body, meaning cessation itself, since belief in a real, solid self is what keeps a being bound to rebirth in the first place, and its removal is what actually ends the cycle; and the uncontaminated realm free of conceptual elaboration, nirvāṇa itself, realized only from within, about which every conceptual question — is it different from ordinary existence or not, does something continue after death or not — turns out to be an unanswerable proliferation of concepts rather than a genuine question with a genuine answer.

4. How the Desire Realm and the First Dhyāna Both Get Called "With Thought"

Chinese Source

此中欲界及色界初靜慮，除靜慮中間若定、若生，名有尋有伺地。

Ru-Yi Translation

Here, the desire realm, together with the first dhyāna of the form realm — excluding the intermediate dhyāna, whether as a meditative attainment or as a rebirth — is called the Ground of Applied and Sustained Thought.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes two kinds of desire behind the desire realm's name: afflictive desire, the inner craving and fantasizing that can arise even without any actual object present, as when wanting to eat while deliberately fasting; and object-based desire, the actual external things desired, from food to wealth. Both together generate the greed, hostility, and harmful intention that drive unwholesome action, which is why the desire realm counts as "with thought." The first dhyāna, though free of desire itself, still counts as "with thought" for a different reason: a meditator there must still actively apply coarse thought and then sustain finer thought to counter the desire realm's pull, session after session — a beginner needing frequent, deliberate effort, an advanced practitioner needing only the lightest touch, but neither yet resting in a state beyond thought altogether. Only at the very threshold of the second dhyāna, called the "intermediate dhyāna," does even sustained thought finally drop away.

5. Only Sustained Thought, and Neither

Chinese Source

即靜慮中間若定、若生，名無尋唯伺地。隨一有情，由修此故，得為大梵。從第二靜慮，餘有色界及無色界全，名無尋無伺地。

Ru-Yi Translation

The intermediate dhyāna itself, whether as attainment or as rebirth, is called the Ground of No Applied Thought but Only Sustained Thought. Any given being who cultivates this may, through it, become a Great Brahmā. From the second dhyāna onward — the whole of the remaining form realm and the entire formless realm — is called the Ground Without Applied or Sustained Thought.

此中由離尋伺欲道理故，說名無尋無伺地，不由不現行故。所以者何？未離欲界欲者，由教導作意差別故，於一時間亦有無尋無伺意現行。

Here, it is by reason of having become free of the craving for applied and sustained thought that it is called the Ground Without Applied or Sustained Thought — not because such thought fails to actually occur. Why is this? Because one who has not yet become free of the desire realm's craving may, through a teacher's specific instruction in attention, temporarily manifest a mind without applied or sustained thought.

已離尋伺欲者，亦有尋伺現行。如出彼定及生彼者。

One who has become free of the craving for applied and sustained thought may nonetheless still manifest applied and sustained thought — as when emerging from that very meditative absorption, or when reborn into it.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi draws out a distinction that runs against a natural assumption: the names of these grounds track whether a being still craves applied and sustained thought as a way of engaging its object, not whether such thought is literally ever occurring in that being's mind at all. Someone still fully caught in desire-realm craving can, guided by a skilled teacher's instruction — he lists six specific contemplations aimed at settling the mind on a single object, from seeing objects as free of any fixed mark to a taste of nirvāṇa's own stillness — bring about a temporary, thought-free state without having actually become free of the underlying craving; that moment still counts as belonging to the desire realm's ground. Conversely, someone who has genuinely become free of that craving, dwelling in the second dhyāna or above, may still have applied and sustained thought arise the moment they emerge from formal meditation, or upon actually being reborn there — yet this does not demote them back to the lower ground, because the craving itself, not the momentary presence or absence of thought, is what the classification actually tracks.

6. Even the Liberated Mind Can Be Classified as "With Thought"

Chinese Source

若無漏界有為定所攝初靜慮，亦名有尋有伺地。依尋伺處法，緣真如為境入此定故，不由分別現行故。餘如前說。

Ru-Yi Translation

If the uncontaminated realm is comprised within a conditioned meditative attainment belonging to the first dhyāna, this too is called the Ground of Applied and Sustained Thought. This is because one enters this absorption by relying on the very same applied-and-sustained-thought basis, taking suchness itself as the object — not because ordinary conceptual discrimination is actually occurring. The rest should be understood as already explained.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as covering a noble one who enters liberating insight by way of the first dhyāna's own structure — using applied and sustained thought as the vehicle, but directed at suchness, reality itself, rather than at any ordinary object. Such a person is still technically classified under the "with thought" ground, purely because of which structural basis was used to enter, not because any actual discrimination or conceptual proliferation is occurring in that liberated state. He notes the same logic extends, unstated in the text, to the other two grounds: a noble one entering by way of the intermediate dhyāna's structure falls under "only sustained thought," and one entering by way of the second dhyāna or above falls under "without either" — the uncontaminated realm runs through all three grounds, tracking the same structural distinctions that govern ordinary, unliberated minds.

7. Hungry Ghosts and Asuras

The treatise now takes up the second of its eight aspects, establishing where in space beings of each realm actually live, beginning with the desire realm's thirty-six places.

Chinese Source

又有餓鬼處所。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, there are the places where hungry ghosts dwell.

又有非天處所。

Further, there are the places where asuras dwell.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes classical sources describe two kinds of hungry-ghost dwelling: a "proper abode" beneath the earth, ruled by King Yama, less deep than the hells; and "peripheral abodes" scattered elsewhere, further split between ghosts of some standing, who live in palaces in mountains, sky, or by the sea with fortune surpassing humans, and those without standing, who make their homes in filth, graveyards, or ruined buildings, faring worse than ordinary humans.

Asuras — literally "non-gods" — he describes through the traditional four modes of birth: those born from an egg, elevated to the sky through merit earned protecting the Dharma, counted among the ghost realm; those born from a womb, demoted from heaven for lacking heavenly virtue and dwelling near the sun and moon, counted among humans; those born from moisture, living beneath the ocean and counted among animals; and those born by transformation, powerful enough to contest even Indra and the guardian kings, possessing heavenly fortune without heavenly virtue, counted among the gods — the same basic kind of being classified differently depending on how it came to exist.

8. Where Animals, Humans, and the Continents Fit

Chinese Source

傍生即與人天同處，故不別建立。復有四大洲，如前說。復有八中洲。

Ru-Yi Translation

Animals dwell in the very same places as humans and gods, and are therefore not separately established. Further, there are the four great continents, as already explained. Further, there are eight intermediate continents.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes that animals are found scattered everywhere humans and gods already live — cats, dogs, and insects among people, birds among the gods — so the treatise gives them no separate place of their own. Humans occupy the four great continents already named in fascicle 2 — the southern, eastern, western, and northern continents — plus eight smaller landmasses, two flanking each of the four, bringing the human world's total geography to twelve pieces of land.

9. The Six Heavens of the Desire Realm

Chinese Source

又欲界天有六處。一、四大王眾天，二、三十三天，三、時分天，四、知足天，五、樂化天，六、他化自在天。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, the desire realm's heavens comprise six places: first, the heaven of the Four Great Kings; second, the Heaven of the Thirty-Three; third, the Heaven of Time's Divisions; fourth, the Heaven of Contentment; fifth, the Heaven of Delighting in Transformation; sixth, the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations.

復有摩羅天宮，即他化自在天攝，然處所高勝。

Further, there is the palace of Māra, comprised within the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations, though its own location stands higher and more excellent.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi traces the six desire-realm heavens rising in sequence: the Four Great Kings on the slopes of Mount Sumeru, half its height; the Thirty-Three at its very summit, where Indra himself dwells; the Time-Divisions Heaven, floating free in open space above the mountain; the Contentment Heaven above it, where Maitreya Bodhisattva presently dwells awaiting his own future buddhahood; the Heaven of Delighting in Transformation, whose beings conjure their own sensory pleasures through their own supernormal power; and finally the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations, whose beings enjoy pleasures either self-conjured or drawn from the heaven just below. Māra's own palace sits within this highest heaven, at an even loftier point within it — the traditional residence of the tempter figure who challenges the Buddha and other practitioners throughout the canon.

10. Solitary and Nearby Hells, and the Desire Realm's Total: Thirty-Six

Chinese Source

復有獨一那落迦、近邊那落迦，即大那落迦及寒那落迦。以近邊故，不別立處。又於人中，亦有一分獨一那落迦可得。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, there are the solitary hells and the neighboring hells — these belong to the great hells and the cold hells already described. Because they lie nearby, no separate place is established for them. Further, even among humans, a portion of solitary hells can be found.

如尊者取菉豆子說：我見諸有情，燒然、極燒然、遍極燒然，總一燒然聚。

As the Venerable One Who Took the Mung Bean said: "I see beings burning, intensely burning, entirely and intensely burning — a single mass of flame altogether."

如是等三十六處，總名欲界。

All these places, thirty-six in number, are together called the desire realm.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains "solitary hells" as scattered, individually karma-determined places of suffering with no fixed location, unlike the eight great and eight cold hells, and "neighboring hells" as the one hundred twenty-eight subsidiary hells clustered right beside the major ones, close enough that the treatise doesn't bother giving them a separate place. He cites the classical story behind the epithet "the Venerable One Who Took the Mung Bean" — better known as Maudgalyāyana — recounting a group outing where one traveler suddenly appeared, to Maudgalyāyana's eye alone, engulfed head to foot in flame; that person had unknowingly stepped into a solitary hell invisible to everyone but a being with the corresponding karmic vision, right in the middle of ordinary human territory.

With this, the desire realm's inventory closes at exactly thirty-six places: eight great hells, eight cold hells, the hungry-ghost realm, the asura realm, four great continents, eight intermediate continents, and six heavens.

11. Eighteen Places in the Form Realm

Chinese Source

復次，色界有十八處。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, the form realm comprises eighteen places.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi divides the eighteen among the four dhyānas: the first dhyāna's three heavens — Brahmā's Retinue, Brahmā's Ministers, and Great Brahmā, ranked as commoners, ministers, and king within Brahmā's own court; the second dhyāna's three heavens of increasing light; the third dhyāna's three heavens of increasing purity; the fourth dhyāna's three heavens for ordinary meditators, plus five further "pure abode" heavens reserved for noble ones at the third fruit of the path or above; and finally a realm beyond even the pure abodes, reserved for advanced bodhisattvas. He notes the treatise elsewhere locates a tenth-ground bodhisattva's actual attainment of buddhahood there, in a reward-body — any appearance of buddhahood in the human realm being a transformation-body only.

12. The First Three Dhyānas' Nine Heavens

Chinese Source

謂梵眾天、梵前益天、大梵天，此三由軟中上品熏修初靜慮故。少光天、無量光天、極淨光天，此三由軟中上品熏修第二靜慮故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Namely: Brahmā's Retinue, Brahmā's Ministers, and Great Brahmā — these three exist because of cultivating the first dhyāna to a weak, middling, or superior degree. Lesser Light, Boundless Light, and Utmost Pure Light — these three exist because of cultivating the second dhyāna to a weak, middling, or superior degree.

少淨天、無量淨天、遍淨天，此三由軟中上品熏修第三靜慮故。

Lesser Purity, Boundless Purity, and Universal Purity — these three exist because of cultivating the third dhyāna to a weak, middling, or superior degree.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes a single, simple rule governing all nine of these heavens at once: whichever of the four dhyānas a meditator actually attains, the exact strength of that attainment — weak, middling, or superior — determines which of its own three tiers the meditator is reborn into. The same three-tier logic simply repeats itself, dhyāna after dhyāna, all the way up.

13. The Fourth Dhyāna: Ordinary Beings, and the Heaven of No Perception

Chinese Source

無雲天、福生天、廣果天，此三由軟中上品熏修第四靜慮故。無想天即廣果攝，無別處所。

Ru-Yi Translation

Cloudless, Merit-Born, and Vast Fruit — these three exist because of cultivating the fourth dhyāna to a weak, middling, or superior degree. The Heaven of No Perception is comprised within Vast Fruit, having no separate place of its own.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the same weak/middling/superior rule governing the fourth dhyāna's own three heavens for ordinary meditators. The Heaven of No Perception, where perception itself is said to cease entirely for the duration of the rebirth, leaving only the physical body and a handful of dissociated forces, gets no separate location — it sits within Vast Fruit itself. He identifies this as the very heaven certain non-Buddhist traditions mistake for ultimate liberation, actually reached through deliberately cultivating an aversion to perception and a longing for its absence, and correctly understood in this system as simply one more, still-conditioned karmic result.

14. The Five Pure-Abode Heavens for Noble Ones

Chinese Source

復有諸聖，住止不共五淨宮地。謂無煩、無熱、善現、善見，及色究竟。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, there are noble ones who dwell apart, in five pure-abode heavens not shared with ordinary beings: namely, Without Affliction, Without Heat, Skillful Appearance, Skillful Sight, and the Ultimate of Form.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses each name in turn: Without Affliction has extinguished both the desire realm's pain and the form realm's pleasure, so mind no longer engages its object at all; Without Heat has seen through the very engagement of mind with object, leaving nothing for even that subtle disturbance to attach to; Skillful Sight is fully settled in concentration, generating wisdom so clear it perceives the ten directions as a single seamless expanse; Skillful Appearance is where a noble one's own attainment becomes readily manifest; and the Ultimate of Form has traced matter down to its subtlest possible limit, the highest and finest heaven the form realm has to offer. He adds that these five, though physically overlapping the fourth dhyāna's ordinary heavens in space, remain entirely imperceptible to any unawakened being dwelling there — much as sacred sites on earth may be home to unseen arhats invisible to ordinary visitors.

15. How Refined Cultivation Produces the Pure Abodes

Chinese Source

由軟、中、上、上勝、上極品雜熏修第四靜慮故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Because of mixed cultivation of the fourth dhyāna to a weak, middling, superior, surpassingly superior, or utmost superior degree.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that, unlike the ordinary heavens reached by simple depth of attainment, the five pure abodes require a specific practice of alternating, or "mixed," cultivation: repeatedly interweaving moments of uncontaminated meditative mind with moments of ordinary contaminated mind in a precise, diminishing rhythm, until the interleaving finally settles at three consecutive moments — uncontaminated, contaminated, uncontaminated — completing one full round. Five such rounds, of increasing refinement, correspond respectively to the five pure abodes in ascending order, each demanding a subtler mastery of this alternation than the last.

16. The Bodhisattva's Realm, and the Four Formless Spheres

Chinese Source

復有超過淨宮大自在住處。有十地菩薩，由極熏修第十地故，得生其中。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, there is the abode of Great Sovereignty, surpassing even the pure-abode heavens. A bodhisattva of the tenth ground, through the utmost cultivation of that very ground, comes to be born there.

復次，無色界有四處所，或無處所。謂空無邊處、識無邊處、無所有處、非想非非想處。

Further, the formless realm comprises four places — or, in another sense, no place at all: the sphere of infinite space, the sphere of infinite consciousness, the sphere of nothingness, and the sphere of neither perception nor non-perception.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes that the form realm's highest point belongs to bodhisattvas alone, reached only through the utmost cultivation of the tenth and final bodhisattva ground. The formless realm then closes the whole inventory of conditioned rebirth: four spheres named for what their meditators take as object — boundless space once every trace of form has been abandoned, boundless consciousness once even that space is abandoned in turn, sheer absence of anything at all once consciousness itself is abandoned, and finally a state so subtle it is neither perception nor its absence. He notes a classical debate over whether "formless" truly means the complete absence of any matter whatsoever, or only the absence of matter coarse enough to be an object of ordinary perception — since some account is still needed for how a being with genuinely zero matter could ever be reborn back into a realm with form.

17. How Tall Are Humans, on Each of the Four Continents?

Having established which realms exist and where their beings live, the treatise turns to its third aspect: how large those beings' bodies actually are.

Chinese Source

有情量建立者，謂贍部洲人身量不定，或時高大，或時卑小，然隨自肘三肘半量。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for establishing beings' physical measure: the people of Jambudvīpa are not fixed in height — sometimes tall, sometimes short — yet always measuring three and a half of their own forearm-lengths.

東毗提訶身量決定，亦隨自肘三肘半量，身又高大。如東毗提訶如是，西瞿陀尼、北拘盧洲身量亦爾，轉復高大。

The people of Videha are fixed in height, likewise measuring three and a half of their own forearm-lengths, and are taller still. As with Videha, so with Godānīya and Uttarakuru: their height likewise measures three and a half of their own forearm-lengths, growing taller again at each step.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the measuring stick is always relative to the person being measured — one's own forearm, from elbow to fingertip, taken three and a half times over — rather than any fixed external unit, since human stature itself varies enormously across the eons: enormous at the very start of an age, when lifespans run to the incalculable, shrinking down to a mere fraction of that by the time human lifespans bottom out at ten years. Height rises steadily across the four continents in a fixed order — Jambudvīpa, our own world, smallest; Videha somewhat taller; Godānīya taller still; and Uttarakuru tallest of all — the same ranking the treatise applies consistently to every other measure of a continent's fortune as well.

18. How Tall Are the Gods?

Chinese Source

四大王眾天身量如拘盧舍四分之一。三十三天身量復增一足。帝釋身量半拘盧舍。時分天身量亦半拘盧舍。此上一切，如欲界天身量，當知漸漸各增一足。

Ru-Yi Translation

The Four Great Kings' height is a quarter krośa. The Thirty-Three's height adds one further quarter to that. Indra's own height is half a krośa. The Time-Divisions Heaven's height is likewise half a krośa. Above this, throughout the rest of the desire-realm heavens, it should be understood that height increases by one further quarter at each step.

梵眾天身量半踰繕那。梵前益天身量一踰繕那。大梵天身量一踰繕那半。少光天身量二踰繕那。此上一切餘天身量各漸倍增。除無雲天。應知彼天減三踰繕那。

Brahmā's Retinue is half a yojana in height. Brahmā's Ministers, one full yojana. Great Brahmā, a yojana and a half. Lesser Light, two yojanas. Above this, every remaining heaven's height roughly doubles at each step — except the Cloudless Heaven, whose height should be understood as three yojanas less than the doubling would otherwise give.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as two different but equally regular progressions: the desire-realm heavens climb by fixed quarter-krośa increments, while the form-realm heavens above them roughly double in height at each successive tier — Brahmā's Retinue at half a yojana growing all the way up to the fourth dhyāna's heavens standing many thousands of miles tall, with only the Cloudless Heaven breaking the clean doubling pattern by a small, specific deduction. He notes that ancient units of measure don't map cleanly onto modern ones, so the exact distances matter less than the underlying principle: physical stature scales directly with the refinement of the meditative state that produced the rebirth.

19. How Tall Are Hell-Beings and Asuras — and Why the Formless Have No Height at All

Chinese Source

又大那落迦身量不定。若作及增長極重惡不善業者，彼感身形其量廣大；餘則不爾。如大那落迦如是，寒那落迦、獨一那落迦、近邊那落迦、傍生、餓鬼亦爾。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, the great hells' beings are not fixed in height. Those who commit and compound extremely grave unwholesome karma acquire a correspondingly vast bodily form; others do not. As with the great hells, so too with the cold hells, the solitary hells, the neighboring hells, animals, and hungry ghosts.

諸非天身量大小，如三十三天。當知無色界無有色故，無有身量。

The asuras' height, whether large or small, is comparable to the Thirty-Three. It should be understood that, since the formless realm has no form at all, it has no bodily measure whatsoever.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads hell-beings' size as tracking the sheer weight of the karma behind their rebirth, since a larger body simply gives more surface area for suffering to be inflicted upon; the same principle carries over unchanged to every other unhappy destiny. Asuras, despite their lower standing, are given roughly the same stature as the gods of the Thirty-Three, reflecting their considerable power even without genuine heavenly virtue. And the formless realm closes the whole question outright: with no matter of any kind, the very question "how tall" simply doesn't apply there.

20. How Long Do Humans Live?

The treatise's fourth aspect establishes how long beings in each realm actually live.

Chinese Source

壽建立者，謂贍部洲人壽量不定。彼人以三十日夜為一月，十二月為一歲。或於一時壽無量歲，或於一時壽八萬歲，或於一時壽量漸減乃至十歲。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for establishing lifespan: the people of Jambudvīpa are not fixed in their lifespan. They reckon thirty days and nights as one month, and twelve months as one year. At one time their lifespan is incalculable; at another time, eighty thousand years; and at yet another, it gradually shortens all the way down to ten years.

東毗提訶人壽量決定二百五十歲。西瞿陀尼人壽量決定五百歲。北拘盧洲人壽量決定千歲。

The people of Videha have a fixed lifespan of two hundred fifty years. The people of Godānīya have a fixed lifespan of five hundred years. The people of Uttarakuru have a fixed lifespan of one thousand years.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi traces Jambudvīpa's own lifespan swinging across the entire arc of a cosmic age, from an incalculable span at the very start, down through eighty thousand years once the age of abiding truly begins, all the way down to a mere ten years at the age's lowest ebb — human fortune rising and falling together across immense stretches of time. The other three continents, by contrast, have no such volatility: each one's lifespan is simply fixed, rising in the same order already seen for height — Videha, then Godānīya, then Uttarakuru living longest of all, exactly the pattern by which the treatise consistently ranks the four continents' relative fortune.

21. How Long Do the Desire-Realm Gods Live?

Chinese Source

又人間五十歲是四大王眾天一日一夜。以此日夜，三十日夜為一月，十二月為一歲。彼諸天眾壽量五百歲。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, fifty human years equal a single day and night for the Four Great Kings. Reckoned by this day and night, thirty days and nights make one month, and twelve months make one year. That heaven's beings live five hundred such years.

人間百歲是三十三天一日一夜。以此日夜如前說，彼諸天眾壽量千歲。如是所餘，乃至他化自在天，日夜及壽量各增前一倍。

A hundred human years equal a single day and night for the Thirty-Three. Reckoned in the same way, that heaven's beings live one thousand such years. So it continues for the rest, up through the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations: each heaven's day-and-night, and its lifespan, doubles the one below it.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the desire realm's heavens run on a doubling clock at every level: a single divine day already outlasts an entire human lifetime, and each heaven's day, along with its total lifespan, is twice the length of the heaven just below it — the Four Great Kings' five hundred years already amounting to some nine million human years, the Thirty-Three's thousand years to well over thirty million, and so on doubling upward through the remaining desire-realm heavens. He illustrates the scale with a traditional story: a palace attendant of Indra's who died and was reborn as a human woman, and who, determined to return to heaven, spent her fifty human years devoted to generosity toward the Buddha and the monastic community before dying again and being reborn once more at Indra's side — Indra, on her return, having noticed nothing more than half a day's absence.

22. How Long Do Hell-Beings, Asuras, Animals, and Ghosts Live?

Chinese Source

又四大王眾天滿足壽量是等活大那落迦一日一夜，即以此三十日夜為一月，十二月為一歲。彼大那落迦壽五百歲。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, a full lifespan of the Four Great Kings equals a single day and night in the Reviving Hell; reckoned by this, thirty days and nights make one month, twelve months make one year. That hell's beings live five hundred such years.

如以四大王眾天壽量成等活大那落迦壽量，如是以三十三天壽量成黑繩大那落迦壽量，以時分天壽量成眾合大那落迦壽量，以知足天壽量成號叫大那落迦壽量，以樂化天壽量成大號叫大那落迦壽量，以他化自在天壽量成燒熱大那落迦壽量，應知亦爾。

Just as the Four Great Kings' lifespan produces the Reviving Hell's lifespan, so the Thirty-Three's lifespan produces the Black-Rope Hell's, the Time-Divisions Heaven's produces the Crushing Hell's, the Contentment Heaven's produces the Wailing Hell's, the Delighting-in-Transformation Heaven's produces the Great Wailing Hell's, and the Mastery-over-Others'-Transformations Heaven's produces the Burning Hell's — this should be understood in just the same way.

極燒熱大那落迦有情壽半中劫。無間大那落迦有情壽一中劫。

The beings of the Intensely Burning Hell live half an intermediate eon. The beings of the Hell of Uninterrupted Suffering live one full intermediate eon.

非天壽量如三十三天。傍生、餓鬼壽量不定。又寒那落迦於大那落迦次第相望，壽量近半應知。又近邊那落迦、獨一那落迦受生有情壽量不定。

The asuras' lifespan is comparable to the Thirty-Three's. Animals' and hungry ghosts' lifespans are not fixed. The cold hells, set against the great hells in the same order, have roughly half their lifespan, it should be understood. The neighboring hells' and solitary hells' beings have no fixed lifespan.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the same doubling logic that measured the desire realm's heavenly lifespans now measures its hells: each successive hell's single day-and-night equals a full lifespan of the correspondingly ranked heaven, so hell-lifespans balloon just as dramatically as heavenly ones, with the two lowest hells measured not even in years but in "intermediate eons," almost incomprehensibly long stretches of time. He reads this as the treatise's own answer to why hell-beings suffer for what seems an unbearable eternity: it is not literally forever, but it is calibrated to the same vast cosmic scale as the most exalted heavenly rebirths, simply running in the opposite direction. Cold hells run at roughly half the corresponding hot hell's span; the scattered solitary and neighboring hells, like animal and ghost lifespans generally, have no fixed length at all, varying entirely with the individual karma behind each rebirth.

23. How Long Do Beings in the Form and Formless Realms Live?

Chinese Source

梵眾天壽二十中劫一劫，梵前益天壽四十中劫一劫，大梵天壽六十中劫一劫，少光天壽八十中劫二劫。自此以上，餘色界天壽量相望，各漸倍增。唯除無雲。當知彼天壽減三劫。

Ru-Yi Translation

Brahmā's Retinue lives for one great eon of twenty intermediate eons; Brahmā's Ministers, one great eon of forty intermediate eons; Great Brahmā, one great eon of sixty intermediate eons; Lesser Light, two great eons of eighty intermediate eons each. Above this, the remaining form-realm heavens' lifespans, set against one another, each roughly double the one before — except the Cloudless Heaven, whose lifespan should be understood as three great eons less than the doubling would otherwise give.

空無邊處壽二萬劫，識無邊處壽四萬劫，無所有處壽六萬劫，非想非非想處壽八萬劫。

The sphere of infinite space lives twenty thousand eons; the sphere of infinite consciousness, forty thousand eons; the sphere of nothingness, sixty thousand eons; the sphere of neither perception nor non-perception, eighty thousand eons.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the first dhyāna's three heavens correspond exactly to how much of the world-destroying fire at the end of a great eon they actually survive, since that fire reaches only as high as the first dhyāna; above that, lifespans simply double at each successive tier, all the way up through the formless realm's eighty-thousand-eon summit — the single longest lifespan anywhere in conditioned existence. He treats this final figure as a caution rather than an aspiration, citing the classical teaching that it would be better to be reborn in hell alongside the treacherous Devadatta than at the peak of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, since even hell still permits some contact with the Dharma, however badly used, while eighty thousand eons at that summit offer no possibility of hearing the teaching at all — deep concentration with almost no wisdom, which is exactly why the tradition lists rebirth there among the recognized obstacles to genuine practice.

24. Sudden Death, and Bodies Left Behind

Chinese Source

除北拘盧洲，餘一切處悉有中夭。又人、鬼、傍生趣有餘滓身；天及那落迦與識俱沒，無餘滓身。

Ru-Yi Translation

Except for Uttarakuru, every other place has premature death. Further, in the destinies of humans, ghosts, and animals, a remaining body is left behind; for gods and hell-beings, consciousness simply vanishes together with the body, leaving no remaining body at all.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes Uttarakuru alone guarantees its full thousand-year lifespan with no possibility of dying early; everywhere else, death can come before a being's allotted span runs out. Whether a corpse is actually left behind depends on how a being was born in the first place: those born from egg, womb, or moisture leave a body to be dealt with, while those born spontaneously — gods and hell-beings alike — simply cease along with their own consciousness, with nothing physical remaining.

25. Three Kinds of Enjoyment, and Each Realm's Characteristic Suffering

The treatise's fifth aspect surveys how beings in every realm actually experience existence day to day.

Chinese Source

受用建立者，略有三種。謂受用苦樂、受用飲食、受用婬欲。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for establishing enjoyment: briefly, there are three kinds — the enjoyment of pleasure and pain, the enjoyment of food and drink, and the enjoyment of sexual activity.

受用苦樂者，謂那落迦有情多分受用極治罰苦；傍生有情多分受用相食噉苦；餓鬼有情多分受用極饑渴苦；人趣有情多分受用匱乏追求種種之苦；天趣有情多分受用衰惱墜沒之苦。

As for the enjoyment of pleasure and pain: hell-beings, for the most part, undergo the pain of extreme punishment; animals, for the most part, undergo the pain of devouring one another; hungry ghosts, for the most part, undergo the pain of extreme hunger and thirst; those in the human destiny, for the most part, undergo the pain of scarcity and endless seeking; and those in the heavenly destiny, for the most part, undergo the pain of decline and falling.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as a one-line portrait of each destiny's characteristic suffering: hell-beings tormented by deliberate punishment, animals caught in an endless food chain of eating and being eaten, hungry ghosts consumed by hunger and thirst that can never be satisfied, humans driven by scarcity and the restless pursuit of more, and even the gods — the most fortunate of all rebirths — eventually facing the distinctive suffering of decline. He describes the five classical signs that a divine life is ending — an unaccustomed restlessness, wilting flowers, an unpleasant bodily odor, and sweat where none appeared before — as the closest heaven comes to death, and by tradition the single worst suffering a god ever faces, precisely because it comes after so long an existence of nothing but pleasure.

26. Inside the Eight Hot Hells

The treatise now details, hell by hell, exactly what "the pain of extreme punishment" actually involves — root text translated for the first three; the pattern of escalating severity continues through all eight.

Chinese Source

又於等活大那落迦中，多受如是極治罰苦。謂彼有情，多共聚集，業增上生，種種苦具次第而起，更相殘害，悶絕躄地。次虛空中有大聲發，唱如是言：此諸有情可還等活！可還等活！次彼有情欻然復起，復由如前所說苦具更相殘害。由此因緣，長時受苦，乃至先世所造一切惡不善業未盡未出。故此那落迦名為等活。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, in the Reviving Hell, beings for the most part undergo just such extreme punishment as this: these beings, gathered together in great numbers through the compounding force of their own karma, find one instrument of suffering after another arising against them, and set upon one another until they collapse senseless to the ground. Then, in the open sky, a great voice cries out, saying: "Let these beings revive! Let them revive!" — and they suddenly rise up again, only to set upon one another once more with the same instruments of suffering as before. Through this cause, they undergo suffering for a long time, for as long as the unwholesome karma created in past lives remains unexhausted and unresolved. This is why this hell is called Reviving.

又於黑繩大那落迦中，多受如是治罰重苦。謂彼有情多分為彼所攝獄卒以黑繩拼之，或為四方、或為八方、或為種種圖畫文像。彼既拼已，隨其處所，若鑿、若斲、若斫、若剜。故此那落迦名為黑繩。

Further, in the Black-Rope Hell, beings for the most part undergo grave punishment such as this: guardians belonging to that hell bind them with black ropes, marking them into squares, or into octagons, or into various patterned designs, and then, following those very markings, drill, chisel, hack, and gouge them. This is why this hell is called Black-Rope.

又於眾合大那落迦中，多受如是治罰重苦。謂彼有情，或時展轉聚集和合，爾時便有彼攝獄卒驅逼令入兩鐵羺頭大山之間。彼既入已，兩山迫之；既被迫已，一切門中血便流注。如兩鐵羺頭，如是兩鐵羝頭、兩鐵馬頭、兩鐵象頭、兩鐵師子頭、兩鐵虎頭亦爾。

Further, in the Crushing Hell, beings for the most part undergo grave punishment such as this: gathered together at times, they are then driven by that hell's guardians in between two great mountains shaped like iron ram-heads. Once driven in, the two mountains close upon them, crushing them until blood streams from every opening of the body. As with the two iron ram-heads, so too with two iron goat-heads, two iron horse-heads, two iron elephant-heads, two iron lion-heads, and two iron tiger-heads.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads each hell's name as describing its own signature torment. Beings in the Reviving Hell are ground down by mutual violence until they collapse, only to be revived by a voice from the sky and made to suffer the identical violence again — a literal, physical enactment of saṃsāra's own repetitive structure, over and over, for as long as the karma behind it remains unspent. Black-Rope beings are marked out like lumber for cutting and then actually carved along those very lines — he connects this hell's specific cause, in the classical texts, to harming one's own parents or close companions and compounding it with deceit. Crushing beings are driven, herded like animals, between colossal mountains shaped like the heads of various beasts, which close on them from both sides at once.

He treats the whole eightfold sequence — Reviving, Black-Rope, Crushing, and the five increasingly severe hells that follow — as growing progressively worse in both duration and intensity, each one a scaled-up consequence matched to a scaled-up unwholesome cause: not arbitrary cruelty, on his reading, but the treatise's own account of karma's exactness.

27. Hells Four Through Eight: Wailing, Great Wailing, Burning, Intensely Burning, and Uninterrupted

The remaining five hot hells escalate, in both confinement and direct bodily torment, toward the eighth and most severe hell of all.

Chinese Source

謂彼有情尋求舍宅，便入大鐵室中。彼纔入已，即便火起；由此燒然，若極燒然，遍極燒然。既被燒已，苦痛逼切，發聲號叫。故此那落迦名為號叫。謂彼室宅其如胎藏。故此那落迦名大號叫。

Ru-Yi Translation

In the Wailing Hell, beings seeking shelter enter a great iron chamber; no sooner have they entered than fire breaks out, burning, intensely burning, entirely and intensely burning, until, overwhelmed by pain, they cry out in anguish. This is why this hell is called Wailing. In the Great Wailing Hell, that very chamber is like a womb — smaller and more confining still. This is why this hell is called Great Wailing.

謂彼所攝獄卒，以諸有情置無量踰繕那熱、極熱、遍極燒然大鐵鏊上，左右轉之，表裏燒煿。故此那落迦名為燒熱。謂以三支大熱鐵丳從下貫之，徹其兩膊及頂而出。由此因緣，眼耳鼻口及諸毛孔猛焰流出。故此那落迦名極燒熱。

In the Burning Hell, its guardians place beings upon a vast, burning, intensely burning, entirely and intensely burning iron griddle many yojanas wide, turning them from side to side, roasting them through, inside and out. This is why this hell is called Burning. In the Intensely Burning Hell, three great heated iron spikes are driven up through the body from below, emerging through both shoulders and the crown of the head, so that flame pours out from the eyes, ears, nose, mouth, and every pore. This is why this hell is called Intensely Burning.

又於無間大那落迦中，彼諸有情恆受如是極治罰苦。謂從東方多百踰繕那燒熱、極燒熱、遍極燒然大鐵地上有猛熾火騰焰而來，刺彼有情，穿皮入肉，斷筋破骨，復徹其髓，燒如脂燭，如是舉身皆成猛焰。如從東方，南西北方亦復如是。

In the Hell of Uninterrupted Suffering, its beings ceaselessly undergo such extreme punishment as this: a fierce, blazing flame comes surging from many hundreds of yojanas to the east, across ground that is burning, intensely burning, entirely and intensely burning, piercing these beings, passing through skin into flesh, severing sinew, shattering bone, and reaching even the marrow, burning them like a wick soaked in oil, until the entire body becomes nothing but raging flame. As from the east, so likewise from the south, west, and north.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads these five hells as an unbroken escalation from the third: Wailing traps beings inside a burning iron room large enough to at least move within; Great Wailing shrinks that same room down to something like the confines of a womb, intensifying the same fire within a space too small to escape it even briefly; Burning and Intensely Burning replace confinement with direct bodily torment — first roasted on a griddle like fish on a skewer, then impaled by multiple spikes at once, the torment compounding rather than merely repeating. The Hell of Uninterrupted Suffering, the lowest and most severe of all eight, earns its name through five simultaneous senses of "without interruption," he explains: no intermediate state between death and rebirth there, no break in the suffering itself, no gap in its duration, no interruption to the being's continued existence throughout, and no part of the body left untouched by it, with flame converging from all four directions at once rather than from any single source.

He notes the classical text traces each hell's specific torment to a correspondingly specific unwholesome cause — divisive speech for Wailing, theft from the Three Jewels or from the destitute for Great Wailing, deliberately setting forests or fields ablaze for Burning, slander that inverts what is lawful and unlawful for Intensely Burning — treating the entire eightfold hierarchy as a precise, escalating correspondence between the gravity of a cause and the severity of its result, not arbitrary cruelty.

28. The Neighboring Hells' Four Gardens, and the Eight Cold Hells Begin

Chinese Source

故此那落迦名為無間，多是造作無間之業來生是中。此但略說麤顯苦具，非於如是大那落迦中，所餘種種眾多苦具而不可得。

Ru-Yi Translation

This is why this hell is called Uninterrupted — those reborn there have, for the most part, committed one of the acts of uninterrupted karma. This is only a brief account of the coarser, more evident instruments of suffering; it is not that the great hells contain no further, countless other instruments beyond these.

又於近邊諸那落迦中，有情之類受用如是治罰重苦。謂彼一切諸大那落迦皆有四方、四岸、四門，鐵牆圍繞。從其四方四門出已，其一一門外有四出園。

Further, in the neighboring hells, beings undergo grave punishment such as this: every great hell is surrounded by walls of iron, with four sides, four banks, and four gates; beyond each of the four gates lie four further gardens — knee-deep hot ash-embers, a swamp of corpses and filth crawling with flesh-eating worms, a road of upturned blades and a forest of blade-shaped leaves, and finally a vast river of scalding ash-water — each dissolving the flesh at every step, only for it to regenerate as quickly as it is destroyed, so the torment never actually ends.

又於寒那落迦受生有情，多受如是極重寒苦。謂皰那落迦中受生有情，即為彼地極重廣大寒觸所觸，一切身分悉皆卷縮，猶如瘡皰。故此那落迦名皰那落迦。皰裂那落迦與此差別，猶如皰潰，膿血流出，其瘡卷皺。故此那落迦名為皰裂。

Further, beings reborn in the cold hells, for the most part, undergo such extreme cold as this: those reborn in the Blistering Hell are touched by cold so vast and severe that the entire body curls inward, like a blister. This is why this hell is called Blistering. The Bursting-Blister Hell differs from this in that the blister has burst, pus and blood flowing out, the wound curling and wrinkling further still. This is why this hell is called Bursting-Blister.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that beings arrive at the Hell of Uninterrupted Suffering specifically through the five gravest possible acts: killing one's father, killing one's mother, killing an arhat, wounding a Buddha, or causing a schism in the monastic community — the treatise's most severe warning against precisely these offenses. He treats the treatise's own disclaimer, that it has given only the "coarser, more evident" instruments of torment, as significant in itself: even this extensive an account is explicitly incomplete, meant to convey a general sense of severity rather than an exhaustive catalogue.

He reads the neighboring hells' four gardens, ringing each great hell beyond each of its four gates, as illustrating a single point: relief there is always only apparent, each garden luring beings forward with the hope of finding actual shelter, only to deliver a worse torment than the one just escaped, with guardians barring any real escape at the river's edge — saṃsāra's own logic in miniature. The eight cold hells, by contrast, involve no active infliction at all, only unbearable cold itself: the first hell's beings curl inward like a blister forms, the second's have that same blister burst open. He notes each of the eight cold hells' names, like the hot hells before them, simply describes the specific stage of frostbite, or the specific sound of suffering, that names it — a systematic progression from mild curling to outright bursting and beyond.

29. The Remaining Cold Hells, and the Solitary Hell

Chinese Source

又喝哳詀、郝郝凡、虎虎凡，此三那落迦由彼有情苦音差別以立其名。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, Chattering, Groaning, and Growling — these three hells are named for the distinct sounds of suffering their beings produce.

青蓮那落迦中，由彼地極重廣大寒觸所觸，一切身分悉皆青瘀，皮膚破裂，或五或六。故此那落迦名曰青蓮。紅蓮那落迦與此差別。過此青已，色變紅赤，皮膚分裂或十或多。故此那落迦名曰紅蓮。大紅蓮那落迦與此差別。謂彼身分極大紅赤，皮膚分裂或百或多。故此那落迦名大紅蓮。

In the Blue-Lotus Hell, beings are touched by cold so vast and severe that the entire body turns a bruised blue-black, the skin splitting into five or six parts. This is why this hell is called Blue Lotus. The Red-Lotus Hell differs from this: beyond the blue-black stage, the color turns red, the skin splitting into ten parts or more. This is why this hell is called Red Lotus. The Great Red-Lotus Hell differs from this: the body turns an intense, deep red, the skin splitting into a hundred parts or more. This is why this hell is called Great Red Lotus.

又獨一那落迦中受生有情，各於自身自業所感，多受如是種種大苦。如吉祥問采菉豆子經中廣說。故此那落迦名為獨一。

Further, beings reborn in the solitary hells each undergo, through their own individual karma, a great variety of just such sufferings as these — as explained at length in the Sūtra of the Auspicious One's Question About Taking Up the Mung Bean. This is why this hell is called Solitary.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the three middle cold hells as named purely for the sound suffering forces out of a frozen throat: a chattering that can barely form words, wordless groaning once even the lips stop moving, and a bare rasping breath once nothing above the throat can move at all. The last three take their names visually, following the same escalating pattern already seen with the hot hells' torments: skin first curling and bruising, then splitting open in ever more numerous, ever more vivid cracks resembling lotus petals, worse at each stage. The solitary hell closes the whole inventory, scattered and individually karma-determined rather than fixed in place — he points back to the same Maudgalyāyana story already cited earlier in the fascicle, and notes that with the eight hot, eight cold, neighboring, and solitary hells all now accounted for, the treatise's hell-realm survey is complete: eighteen hells in total, by one traditional way of counting.

30. Animal Suffering, and Two Kinds of Hungry Ghost

Chinese Source

又傍生趣更相殘害，如羸弱者為諸彊力之所殺害，由此因緣受種種苦。以不自在，他所驅馳，多被鞭撻，與彼人天為資生具。由此因緣具受種種極重苦惱。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, in the animal destiny, beings prey upon one another, the weak being killed by the strong, and through this cause they undergo a great variety of suffering. Lacking any freedom of their own, driven about by others, frequently beaten, they serve as a resource for the very humans and gods above them. Through this cause, they undergo every kind of extremely grave suffering.

又餓鬼趣略有三種。一者、由外障礙飲食，二者、由內障礙飲食，三者、飲食無有障礙。

Further, the hungry-ghost destiny comprises, briefly, three kinds: first, those whose food and drink are obstructed from without; second, those whose food and drink are obstructed from within; third, those whose food and drink are unobstructed.

云何由外障礙飲食？謂彼有情由習上品慳故，生鬼趣中，常與饑渴相應。皮肉血脈皆悉枯槁，猶如火炭，頭髮髼亂，其面黯黑，唇口乾焦，常以其舌舐略口面。饑渴慞惶，處處馳走。所到泉池，為餘有情手執刀杖及以羂索行列守護，令不得趣。或彊趣之，便見其泉變成膿血，自不欲飲。如是等鬼，是名由外障礙飲食。

What is being obstructed in one's food and drink from without? Those beings who have habituated an extreme degree of stinginess are born into the ghost realm and constantly beset by hunger and thirst. Their skin, flesh, and blood-vessels are all withered, like burnt charcoal; their hair is unkempt, their faces dark, their lips and mouths parched and cracked, and they constantly lick their own mouths and faces with their tongues. Frantic with hunger and thirst, they race about everywhere. Whatever spring or pool they reach is guarded on every side by other beings bearing blades and ropes, who bar them from approaching; or, forcing their way in regardless, they see the water itself turn to pus and blood, and lose all desire to drink it. Ghosts of this kind are called those obstructed in food and drink from without.

云何由內障礙飲食？謂彼有情，口或如針，口或如炬，或復頸癭，其腹寬大。由此因緣，縱得飲食，無他障礙，自然不能若噉若飲。如是等鬼，是名由內障礙飲食。

What is being obstructed in one's food and drink from within? Those beings whose mouths are like the eye of a needle, or like a blazing torch, or who have a swollen growth at the throat, or an enormously distended belly. Because of this, even when food and drink are obtained, with no external obstruction at all, they are simply, by their own nature, unable to eat or drink it. Ghosts of this kind are called those obstructed in food and drink from within.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads animal suffering as defined by two related conditions: constant predation up and down a food chain, and total lack of self-determination, since even animals not eaten outright are worked, beaten, and consumed as resources by the very humans and gods who outrank them. He divides hungry ghosts by a single underlying cause — habitual, extreme stinginess in a past life, withholding help one could easily have given — expressed in three different ways. Those obstructed from without are physically capable of eating but can never actually reach food, guarded away from every spring by hostile beings, or finding what water they do reach transformed to pus and blood the moment they try to drink. Those obstructed from within possess no such external barrier at all — the deprivation is built into their very bodies, a throat too narrow to swallow, or a mouth that turns whatever it touches to fire, so that stinginess in life becomes an inability to receive even when nothing stands in the way.

He reads the vivid physical detail throughout — the burnt-charcoal skin, the parched lips, the needle-thin mouth — not as gratuitous horror but as karma's own precise, almost clinical correspondence: whatever was withheld from others becomes, in the next life, exactly what one can no longer receive.

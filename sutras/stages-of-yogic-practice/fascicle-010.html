Fascicle 10 · 瑜伽師地論卷第十 · Continuing Directly from Fascicle 9

The Last Five Links, and Why a Chain Can Be Cut at Any Point

Fascicle 9 ended mid-sentence, partway through dependent origination's own nine-part analysis — seven of the twelve links defined and counted, five still untouched. This page picks up exactly there: craving, grasping, becoming, birth, and old-age-and-death each given their own precise anatomy, before the treatise turns to the deeper questions dependent origination itself must answer — its fixed sequence, its hardest objections, its very name, and how the sūtras themselves have taught it.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 10 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. Craving in Three Realms, and Grasping's Fourfold Object, Agent, and Purpose

Continuing directly from fascicle 9's link-by-link analysis, the treatise now defines craving — desire-realm, form-realm, and formless-realm craving, each following an identical cause-nature-result template — and grasping, whose four kinds are unpacked through what is grasped, what does the grasping, and what the grasping is actually for.

Chinese Source

欲愛云何？謂欲界諸行為緣所生，於欲界行染汙希求，由此能生欲界苦果。色愛云何？謂色界諸行為緣所生，於色界行染汙希求，由此能生色界苦果。無色愛云何？謂無色界諸行為緣所生，於無色界行染汙希求，由此能生無色界苦果。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is desire-realm craving? It arises conditioned by desire-realm formations, a defiled longing for desire-realm formations, and by this it produces the suffering-result of the desire realm. What is form-realm craving? It arises conditioned by form-realm formations, a defiled longing for form-realm formations, and by this it produces the suffering-result of the form realm. What is formless-realm craving? It arises conditioned by formless-realm formations, a defiled longing for formless-realm formations, and by this it produces the suffering-result of the formless realm.

欲取云何？謂於諸欲所有欲貪。見取云何？謂除薩迦耶見，於所餘見所有欲貪。戒禁取云何？謂於邪願所起戒禁所有欲貪。我語取云何？謂於薩迦耶見所有欲貪。初唯能生欲界苦果，餘三通生三界苦果。

What is the grasping of sensory desire? Craving directed at the objects of desire. What is the grasping of views? Craving directed at every view apart from the view of a perishable self. What is the grasping of precepts and vows? Craving directed at the precepts and vows arisen from a wrongly directed aspiration. What is the grasping of self-doctrine? Craving directed at the view of a perishable self itself. The first alone produces suffering only in the desire realm; the remaining three, in common, produce suffering throughout all three realms.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads all three cravings through one identical three-part structure — a cause (contact with a given realm's own objects), a defining nature (defiled longing, explicitly distinguished from the undefiled wish for liberation, since a genuine aspiration to renounce the three realms is never called craving), and a result (that realm's own suffering) — a template he treats as the treatise's standard method for defining any of the twelve links, seen already with formations, consciousness, and feeling in fascicle 9.

On grasping, he unpacks the commentary's own threefold analysis of each kind: what is grasped (desire itself; wrong views generally, apart from self-view; precepts and vows wrongly believed to cause liberation; and the notion of self specifically); what does the grasping (craving intensified into active clinging); and what the grasping actually aims at (pursuing and enjoying sensory pleasure; defending one's own doctrine in debate or deflecting a rival's challenge, as fascicle 6's survey of the sixteen non-Buddhist doctrines already illustrated at length; advancing toward worldly meditative attainment up through the formless spheres; and accounting for who acts, suffers, and eventually attains liberation). He notes that self-grasping and view-grasping, unlike the grasping of sensory desire, are not confined to the desire realm — a meditator absorbed in the form or formless realm can still, quietly, be grasping at a self, which is precisely how fascicle 6's extended refutation of self-view showed even advanced non-Buddhist meditators like Udraka Rāmaputra falling back into saṃsāra.

2. Becoming: Seven Angles on a Single Life, Multiplied Across Three Realms

The treatise defines becoming through a sevenfold scheme — the interval of an ordinary lifespan, the karma that projects it, the moment of death, the intermediate state between lives, and the moment of rebirth, applied across the destinies of the desire realm and, with destiny-specific omissions, the form and formless realms above it — then closes by showing all seven serve exactly three underlying functions.

Chinese Source

欲有云何？謂欲界本有、業有、死有、中有、生有，及那落迦、傍生、餓鬼、人、天有，總說名欲有。此復由先所作諸行煩惱攝受之所熏發。色有云何？謂除那落迦、傍生、餓鬼、人有，所餘是色有應知。無色有云何？謂復除中有，所餘是無色有應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is desire-realm becoming? The ordinary interval of a life, the karma that projects it, the moment of death, the intermediate state, and the moment of rebirth, together with hell, animal, ghost, human, and god existence — all this, taken together, is called desire-realm becoming, brought about entirely through the perfuming force of past formations gathered up by affliction. What is form-realm becoming? Every one of these apart from hell, animal, ghost, and human existence. What is formless-realm becoming? The same again, further apart from the intermediate state.

問：依何義故建立七有，所謂那落迦、傍生、餓鬼、人、天有，業有，中有？答：依三種所作故。一、能引有，謂一；二、趣有有，謂一；三、受用果有，謂五。

Question: On what basis are these seven kinds of becoming established — hell, animal, ghost, human, and god existence, karmic becoming, and the intermediate state? Answer: On the basis of three distinct functions. First, the projecting function, belonging to karmic becoming alone. Second, the function of heading toward a destiny, belonging to the intermediate state alone. Third, the function of actually experiencing a result, belonging to the five destinies.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through the classical fivefold "becoming" scheme in order: the ordinary interval of a life (本有) runs from the moment after conception to the moment before death; karmic becoming (業有) names the very deed, wholesome or otherwise, whose momentum will project the next existence; death-becoming (死有) is the single moment life ends; the intermediate state (中有) is the interval between one life's ending and the next one's beginning; and rebirth-becoming (生有) is the single moment a new existence actually begins — with the five destinies (hell, animal, ghost, human, god) simply naming which kind of body each of these five moments belongs to. He draws out the Abhidharmakośa's own fivefold description of the intermediate state: it is "mind-made," arising from mind alone rather than from any physical union of parents; it restlessly seeks out its next place of birth; it feeds on scent alone, having no solid food; it is properly called the "in-between" state precisely because ordinary vision cannot see it, only the purified divine eye; and it is called a "rising," since it is provisional, arising only to meet the coming birth rather than to last.

He notes the form realm's becoming simply drops the three lower destinies unreachable from meditative attainment (hell, animal, ghost — leaving only human and god, though "human" here effectively becomes vacuous since no one is reborn there from the form realm), while the formless realm drops the intermediate state entirely, since a being dying out of a formless absorption is reborn there directly, with no body-based transitional period possible where there is no body to transition through — a live doctrinal question, he notes, that different Abhidharma schools debated (whether the formless realm truly has no subtle form at all, given accounts of formless gods weeping at Śāriputra's death), which this treatise resolves by allowing a meditation-generated form when needed, without conceding it counts as ordinary karmic form. He treats the closing threefold regrouping — one function that projects, one that transitions, and five that actually receive a result — as revealing why exactly these seven, and no other number, were chosen: together they cover projection, transition, and reception, the entire mechanical anatomy of how one existence becomes the next.

3. Birth's Own Ten Angles, from the First Instant in the Womb to a Sustained Life-Faculty

The treatise anatomizes the link of birth itself into ten distinct moments and functions — from the very first instant of conception through gradual growth, the aggregates' actual arising, their causal and supporting conditions, their daily nourishment, and finally the life-faculty that holds it all together — then compresses the whole ten back into six essential categories.

Chinese Source

生云何？謂於胎、卵二生，初託生時。等生云何？謂即於彼身分圓滿，仍未出時。趣云何？謂從彼出。起云何？謂出已增長。出現云何？謂於濕、化二生，身分頓起。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is birth? For womb-birth and egg-birth, the very first instant of conception. What is equal-growth? The stage when the body is fully formed yet has not yet emerged. What is emergence? The actual coming forth from womb or egg. What is arising? Growth continuing after that emergence. What is manifestation? For moisture-birth and spontaneous birth, the body appearing whole and complete all at once.

蘊得云何？謂即於彼諸生位中五取蘊轉。界得云何？謂即彼諸蘊因緣所攝性。處得云何？謂即彼諸蘊餘緣所攝性。諸蘊生起云何？謂即彼諸蘊日日飲食之所資長。命根出現云何？謂即彼諸蘊餘壽力故，得相續住。

What is the attainment of the aggregates? The five aggregates of clinging actually occurring at every one of these stages of birth. What is the attainment of their causal element? The very same aggregates as included within their causal condition. What is the attainment of their basis? The very same aggregates as included within their remaining conditions. What is the arising of the aggregates? These same aggregates sustained and nourished, day after day, by food. What is the manifestation of the life-faculty? These same aggregates continuing on, sustained by the remaining power of their allotted lifespan.

此生支略義者，謂若生自性、若生處位、若所生、若因緣所攝、若任持所引、若俱生依持，是名略義。

The essential meaning of this link, birth, is this: its own essential nature, the stages of its process, what it actually produces, what is included within its causes and conditions, what sustains and draws it onward, and what accompanies and supports it — this is its essential meaning.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps the first five terms directly onto the embryology already given in fascicles 1, 2, and 9: birth and manifestation together cover the link's own essential nature, splitting womb- and egg-birth's gradual conception from moisture- and spontaneous-birth's instantaneous appearance; equal-growth, emergence, and arising together trace the developmental sequence from a fully formed but still-unborn body, through actual delivery, to ongoing growth afterward. The remaining five terms shift from developmental description to the aggregates' own causal analysis: the aggregates' attainment names their sheer occurrence at any of these stages; their causal-element and basis attainments name, respectively, the seed-condition that produces them and the further supporting conditions (objective, immediately-preceding, and dominant) that let them actually manifest — Cháng Bǎi cites the commentary's threefold distinction between seed-attainment, mastery-attainment, and manifestation-attainment as clarifying exactly which of these five terms tracks which kind of "attainment"; their ongoing arising names the four foods' daily sustaining work, already detailed in fascicle 5; and the life-faculty's manifestation names the karmic force of one's allotted lifespan holding the whole continuing process together until it runs out.

He treats the closing sixfold summary as folding these ten terms into a compact checklist any student could use to locate a given passage's meaning within the birth link as a whole: is it describing birth's essential nature, its process, its actual product, its causal or supporting conditions, its ongoing nourishment, or its underlying life-force — six angles on the single fact of a new existence beginning and sustaining itself.

4. Old Age in Seventeen Unsparing Details, from Trembling Limbs to a Failing Memory

The treatise anatomizes old age with startling physical precision — seventeen distinct signs, from the first tremor of weakening faculties through whitening hair, sagging skin, a stooped spine, a cane leaned on for support, and a fading memory, all the way to the body's final decline — then compresses the seventeen into eleven categories of "decay."

Chinese Source

衰云何？謂依止劣故，令彼掉動。老云何？謂髮色衰變。襵云何？謂皮膚緩皺。熟云何？謂火力衰減，無復勢力受用欲塵。氣力損壞云何？謂性多疾病故，無有勢力能作事業。黑黶間身云何？謂黯黑出現，損其容色。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is frailty? The faculties, grown weak, begin to tremble. What is aging? The hair changes and loses its color. What is wrinkling? The skin grows slack and creased. What is a failing constitution? Bodily warmth declines, leaving no strength left to enjoy the objects of desire. What is loss of vigor? Frequent illness leaves no strength left to accomplish anything. What is blotched skin? Dark spots appear, marring the complexion.

身脊傴曲、喘息奔急云何？謂行步威儀身形所顯，由此發起極重喘嗽。形貌僂前云何？謂坐威儀位，身首低曲。憑據杖策云何？謂住威儀位，依杖力而住。昏昧云何？謂臥威儀位，數重睡眠。羸劣云何？謂即於此位，無力速覺。

What is a stooped spine and labored breath? The bent form and hurried gait visible in walking, giving rise to heavy panting and coughing. What is a bowed posture? The head and body bent low while seated. What is leaning on a staff? Needing a cane's support simply to stand. What is a clouded mind? Falling repeatedly into heavy sleep while lying down. What is feebleness? In this same state, being unable to wake quickly.

損減云何？謂念慧衰退。衰退云何？謂念慧劣故，至於善法不能現行。諸根耄熟云何？謂身體尫羸。功用破壞云何？謂彼於境不復明利。諸行朽故云何？謂彼於後將欲終時。其形腐敗云何？謂壽量將盡，身形臨壞，於諸事業無復功能。

What is diminishment? Mindfulness and wisdom declining. What is decline itself? Mindfulness and wisdom so weakened that wholesome practice can no longer even be undertaken. What is the faculties' full decrepitude? The body grown utterly frail. What is broken function? The faculties no longer meeting their objects with any clarity or keenness. What is decayed formation? Being close to the very end of life. What is bodily decay? The lifespan nearly spent, the body on the verge of collapse, capable of nothing further at all.

此老略義者，謂依止變壞、鬚髮變壞、充悅變壞、火力變壞、無病變壞、色相變壞、威儀變壞、無色諸根變壞、有色諸根變壞、時分已過、壽量將盡，略義應知。

The essential meaning of old age is this: decay of one's own support, decay of hair, decay of fullness, decay of bodily warmth, decay of health, decay of complexion, decay of bearing, decay of the formless faculties, decay of the formed faculties, time having passed, and the lifespan nearing its end — this is its essential meaning.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this seventeen-item list as strikingly unsentimental by design — the treatise names each sign of aging with the same clinical precision it gave the eighteen hells or the body's own organs in fascicle 3, refusing to soften what awaits every unliberated being. He groups the seventeen exactly as the closing summary does: the first six trace visible bodily decline (weakening faculties, graying hair, slackening skin, failing constitution, mounting illness, blotching skin); the next five trace the breakdown of the four dignified postures — walking, sitting, standing, and lying down each visibly compromised in turn; the next two trace the inward decline of mindfulness and wisdom themselves, the very faculties a practitioner most needs; the next two trace the coarser physical faculties' own final decrepitude; and the last two mark simply that time has run out and the body stands at the very edge of collapse.

He reads the deliberate ordering — outward before inward, physical before mental, gradual before final — as itself a teaching device: by naming decline in such granular, undeniable detail, the treatise removes any comfortable vagueness about what aging actually is, precisely so a practitioner cannot indefinitely postpone taking the path seriously by imagining old age as a distant abstraction rather than this exact, specific, unavoidable sequence.

5. Death in Nine Precise Stages, and the Twelve Links' Differences Close Entirely

The treatise closes the twelfth link with the same unsparing precision — nine distinct stages of dying, from consciousness first leaving the body through the senses' final failure to the exact moment breath, warmth, and the last aggregate all give way — and with this, every one of the twelve links' own differences, opened at the start of this analysis back in fascicle 9, is now fully covered.

Chinese Source

終云何？謂諸有情離解支節而死。盡云何？謂諸有情由解支節而死。壞云何？謂識離身。沒云何？謂諸色根滅。捨壽云何？謂氣將盡位。捨煖云何？謂不動位。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is ending? Dying free of the pain of the joints coming apart. What is expiring? Dying through the joints actually coming apart. What is dissolution? Consciousness departing the body. What is failing? The physical faculties ceasing. What is the relinquishing of breath? The stage when the breath is about to be entirely spent. What is the relinquishing of warmth? The stage of complete stillness.

棄捨諸蘊命根謝滅云何？謂時死。死云何？謂遇橫緣，非時而死。時蘊盡云何？謂初死未久位。又死魔業名時蘊盡。此死略義者，謂若死、若死法、若死差別、若死後位，是名略義。如是名為緣起差別應知。

What is the relinquishing of every aggregate as the life-faculty itself passes away? Dying when one's time has fully come. What is death by misfortune? Dying out of season, through some untimely circumstance. What is the aggregates' expiring in time? The stage immediately after death has just occurred; it is also called the working of the lord of death. The essential meaning of death is this: death itself, the manner of dying, death's own varieties, and the stage following death — this is its essential meaning. This, then, is what is meant by the differences of dependent origination.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes "ending" from "expiring" by realm: gods and hell-beings, both spontaneously born, die without the specific agony of "joints coming apart," while every other desire-realm being dies through it — the classical "knife-wind" said to sever flesh, sinew, bone, marrow, and blood all at once, vividly described in several sūtras he cites only by name as bringing pain "no part of the body escapes." He traces the actual sequence of dying in order: consciousness first begins departing the body (from the crown downward or the feet upward, converging finally at the heart, exactly as fascicle 1's own earliest account of conception described in reverse); the physical sense-faculties fail in turn; breath is spent; the body stiffens into total stillness; and the aggregates and life-faculty finally give way together — either "on time," when a being's full allotted lifespan is genuinely exhausted, or "by misfortune," the nine specific categories of untimely death the Bhaiṣajyaguru Sūtra names (illness without a doctor, execution, loss of vital energy to a spirit, fire, drowning, wild animals, falling, poison or curse, and starvation or thirst) — a distinction, he notes, between death from genuinely spent past karma and death from present, avoidable circumstance.

He treats the final term, "the aggregates' expiring in time," as carrying a double sense worth holding together: literally, simply the brief interval immediately following death itself; and, read through the lens of the four māras named back in fascicle 5's account of genuine joy conquering them, this same expiring is also called "the working of the lord of death" (死魔業), since the sheer fact that any born being must eventually undergo this dissolution is itself death's own domain of power over the unliberated. With this, old age and death — the twelfth and final link — are fully anatomized, and the treatise's entire link-by-link analysis of dependent origination's differences (緣起差別), the fourth of the nine topics opened in fascicle 9, is now complete for all twelve links without exception.

6. Why This Order? The Twelve Links Walked Through as a Single Lived Sequence

The treatise's fifth topic tackles a hard question head-on: why does the Buddha always teach the twelve links in this exact order? The first answer simply walks the whole chain through once more as a single lived sequence, showing each link is the natural, inevitable consequence of the one before it — and a second answer shows the twelve split cleanly into two sixes, one governing the inward body, the other governing experience of the outward world.

Chinese Source

問：何因緣故，無明等諸有支作如是次第說？答：諸愚癡者要先愚於所應知事，次即於彼發起邪行。由邪行故，令心顛倒。心顛倒故，結生相續。生相續故，諸根圓滿。根圓滿故，二受用境。受用境故，若耽著，若希求。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: For what reason are ignorance and the other links taught in this particular order? Answer: One who is deluded must first be ignorant of what ought to be known, and only then, in consequence, undertakes wrongful conduct. Through wrongful conduct, the mind becomes distorted. Because the mind is distorted, rebirth is bound and continues. Because rebirth continues, the faculties become fully formed. Because the faculties are fully formed, both faculty and consciousness come to experience their objects. Because objects are experienced, one either grows attached to them or longs for them.

由希求故，於方覓時煩惱滋長。煩惱滋長故，發起後有愛非愛業。由所起業滋長力故，於五趣生死中苦果生。苦果生已，有老死等苦。謂內身變異所引老死苦，及境界變異所引憂歎苦熱惱之苦。是故世尊如是次第說十二支。

Because of this longing, affliction grows all the more in the very seeking of what is longed for. Because affliction grows, karma desirable or undesirable for a further existence is set in motion. Because of the growing force of this karma, a suffering result arises somewhere among the five destinies of birth and death. Once that suffering result has arisen, the suffering of old age and death follows — the suffering of old age and death brought on by change within the body itself, and the suffering of sorrow, lamentation, and distress brought on by change in the surrounding world. This is why the Blessed One teaches the twelve links in exactly this order.

復有次第差別，謂依二種緣建立緣起次第。一、內身緣，二、受用境界緣。內身緣，前六支所攝；受用境界緣，後六支所攝。

There is, further, a difference of sequence established by two kinds of condition: the condition of the inward body, and the condition of experiencing the outward world. The condition of the inward body is what the first six links belong to; the condition of experiencing the outward world is what the remaining six links belong to.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the first answer as tracing one continuous, psychologically inevitable slide rather than twelve arbitrary stops: not knowing the true nature of one's own existence (ignorance) leads directly to acting on that misunderstanding (formations); acting wrongly leaves the mind distorted at the moment of death (consciousness); a distorted mind binds itself to a new existence (name-and-form); that existence matures its own faculties (the six bases); mature faculties inevitably meet their objects (contact); meeting objects inevitably produces feeling; feeling inevitably tempts either attachment or longing (craving); longing inevitably intensifies, precisely in the act of pursuing its object, into grasping; grasping inevitably ripens dormant karma into becoming; becoming inevitably delivers a result somewhere among the five destinies (birth); and any birth inevitably carries old age, death, and their attendant grief. He stresses the word "inevitably" throughout: the order is not a stylistic convention but a genuine causal cascade, each link forced into existence by the one immediately prior, which is exactly why the Buddha could not teach them in any other sequence without falsifying how the chain actually operates.

On the second, complementary explanation, he notes the elegant symmetry: the first six links (ignorance through contact) together explain how an inward body — a new psychophysical existence — comes to exist at all, while the last six (feeling through old-age-and-death) explain what happens once that body begins experiencing an outward world through it. He treats this as a genuinely different, non-redundant lens on the identical twelve links: the first sequence explains why each link causes the next; this second grouping explains what larger project each half of the chain is actually accomplishing — one half building an existence, the other half explaining what that existence then does with the objects it encounters.

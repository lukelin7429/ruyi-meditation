Fascicle 10 · 瑜伽師地論卷第十 · Continuing Directly from Fascicle 9

The Last Five Links, and Why a Chain Can Be Cut at Any Point

Fascicle 9 ended mid-sentence, partway through dependent origination's own nine-part analysis — seven of the twelve links defined and counted, five still untouched. This page picks up exactly there: craving, grasping, becoming, birth, and old-age-and-death each given their own precise anatomy, before the treatise turns to the deeper questions dependent origination itself must answer — its fixed sequence, its hardest objections, its very name, and how the sūtras themselves have taught it.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 10 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. Craving in Three Realms, and Grasping's Fourfold Object, Agent, and Purpose

Continuing directly from fascicle 9's link-by-link analysis, the treatise now defines craving — desire-realm, form-realm, and formless-realm craving, each following an identical cause-nature-result template — and grasping, whose four kinds are unpacked through what is grasped, what does the grasping, and what the grasping is actually for.

Chinese Source

欲愛云何？謂欲界諸行為緣所生，於欲界行染汙希求，由此能生欲界苦果。色愛云何？謂色界諸行為緣所生，於色界行染汙希求，由此能生色界苦果。無色愛云何？謂無色界諸行為緣所生，於無色界行染汙希求，由此能生無色界苦果。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is desire-realm craving? It arises conditioned by desire-realm formations, a defiled longing for desire-realm formations, and by this it produces the suffering-result of the desire realm. What is form-realm craving? It arises conditioned by form-realm formations, a defiled longing for form-realm formations, and by this it produces the suffering-result of the form realm. What is formless-realm craving? It arises conditioned by formless-realm formations, a defiled longing for formless-realm formations, and by this it produces the suffering-result of the formless realm.

欲取云何？謂於諸欲所有欲貪。見取云何？謂除薩迦耶見，於所餘見所有欲貪。戒禁取云何？謂於邪願所起戒禁所有欲貪。我語取云何？謂於薩迦耶見所有欲貪。初唯能生欲界苦果，餘三通生三界苦果。

What is the grasping of sensory desire? Craving directed at the objects of desire. What is the grasping of views? Craving directed at every view apart from the view of a perishable self. What is the grasping of precepts and vows? Craving directed at the precepts and vows arisen from a wrongly directed aspiration. What is the grasping of self-doctrine? Craving directed at the view of a perishable self itself. The first alone produces suffering only in the desire realm; the remaining three, in common, produce suffering throughout all three realms.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads all three cravings through one identical three-part structure — a cause (contact with a given realm's own objects), a defining nature (defiled longing, explicitly distinguished from the undefiled wish for liberation, since a genuine aspiration to renounce the three realms is never called craving), and a result (that realm's own suffering) — a template he treats as the treatise's standard method for defining any of the twelve links, seen already with formations, consciousness, and feeling in fascicle 9.

On grasping, he unpacks the commentary's own threefold analysis of each kind: what is grasped (desire itself; wrong views generally, apart from self-view; precepts and vows wrongly believed to cause liberation; and the notion of self specifically); what does the grasping (craving intensified into active clinging); and what the grasping actually aims at (pursuing and enjoying sensory pleasure; defending one's own doctrine in debate or deflecting a rival's challenge, as fascicle 6's survey of the sixteen non-Buddhist doctrines already illustrated at length; advancing toward worldly meditative attainment up through the formless spheres; and accounting for who acts, suffers, and eventually attains liberation). He notes that self-grasping and view-grasping, unlike the grasping of sensory desire, are not confined to the desire realm — a meditator absorbed in the form or formless realm can still, quietly, be grasping at a self, which is precisely how fascicle 6's extended refutation of self-view showed even advanced non-Buddhist meditators like Udraka Rāmaputra falling back into saṃsāra.

2. Becoming: Seven Angles on a Single Life, Multiplied Across Three Realms

The treatise defines becoming through a sevenfold scheme — the interval of an ordinary lifespan, the karma that projects it, the moment of death, the intermediate state between lives, and the moment of rebirth, applied across the destinies of the desire realm and, with destiny-specific omissions, the form and formless realms above it — then closes by showing all seven serve exactly three underlying functions.

Chinese Source

欲有云何？謂欲界本有、業有、死有、中有、生有，及那落迦、傍生、餓鬼、人、天有，總說名欲有。此復由先所作諸行煩惱攝受之所熏發。色有云何？謂除那落迦、傍生、餓鬼、人有，所餘是色有應知。無色有云何？謂復除中有，所餘是無色有應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is desire-realm becoming? The ordinary interval of a life, the karma that projects it, the moment of death, the intermediate state, and the moment of rebirth, together with hell, animal, ghost, human, and god existence — all this, taken together, is called desire-realm becoming, brought about entirely through the perfuming force of past formations gathered up by affliction. What is form-realm becoming? Every one of these apart from hell, animal, ghost, and human existence. What is formless-realm becoming? The same again, further apart from the intermediate state.

問：依何義故建立七有，所謂那落迦、傍生、餓鬼、人、天有，業有，中有？答：依三種所作故。一、能引有，謂一；二、趣有有，謂一；三、受用果有，謂五。

Question: On what basis are these seven kinds of becoming established — hell, animal, ghost, human, and god existence, karmic becoming, and the intermediate state? Answer: On the basis of three distinct functions. First, the projecting function, belonging to karmic becoming alone. Second, the function of heading toward a destiny, belonging to the intermediate state alone. Third, the function of actually experiencing a result, belonging to the five destinies.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through the classical fivefold "becoming" scheme in order: the ordinary interval of a life (本有) runs from the moment after conception to the moment before death; karmic becoming (業有) names the very deed, wholesome or otherwise, whose momentum will project the next existence; death-becoming (死有) is the single moment life ends; the intermediate state (中有) is the interval between one life's ending and the next one's beginning; and rebirth-becoming (生有) is the single moment a new existence actually begins — with the five destinies (hell, animal, ghost, human, god) simply naming which kind of body each of these five moments belongs to. He draws out the Abhidharmakośa's own fivefold description of the intermediate state: it is "mind-made," arising from mind alone rather than from any physical union of parents; it restlessly seeks out its next place of birth; it feeds on scent alone, having no solid food; it is properly called the "in-between" state precisely because ordinary vision cannot see it, only the purified divine eye; and it is called a "rising," since it is provisional, arising only to meet the coming birth rather than to last.

He notes the form realm's becoming simply drops the three lower destinies unreachable from meditative attainment (hell, animal, ghost — leaving only human and god, though "human" here effectively becomes vacuous since no one is reborn there from the form realm), while the formless realm drops the intermediate state entirely, since a being dying out of a formless absorption is reborn there directly, with no body-based transitional period possible where there is no body to transition through — a live doctrinal question, he notes, that different Abhidharma schools debated (whether the formless realm truly has no subtle form at all, given accounts of formless gods weeping at Śāriputra's death), which this treatise resolves by allowing a meditation-generated form when needed, without conceding it counts as ordinary karmic form. He treats the closing threefold regrouping — one function that projects, one that transitions, and five that actually receive a result — as revealing why exactly these seven, and no other number, were chosen: together they cover projection, transition, and reception, the entire mechanical anatomy of how one existence becomes the next.

3. Birth's Own Ten Angles, from the First Instant in the Womb to a Sustained Life-Faculty

The treatise anatomizes the link of birth itself into ten distinct moments and functions — from the very first instant of conception through gradual growth, the aggregates' actual arising, their causal and supporting conditions, their daily nourishment, and finally the life-faculty that holds it all together — then compresses the whole ten back into six essential categories.

Chinese Source

生云何？謂於胎、卵二生，初託生時。等生云何？謂即於彼身分圓滿，仍未出時。趣云何？謂從彼出。起云何？謂出已增長。出現云何？謂於濕、化二生，身分頓起。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is birth? For womb-birth and egg-birth, the very first instant of conception. What is equal-growth? The stage when the body is fully formed yet has not yet emerged. What is emergence? The actual coming forth from womb or egg. What is arising? Growth continuing after that emergence. What is manifestation? For moisture-birth and spontaneous birth, the body appearing whole and complete all at once.

蘊得云何？謂即於彼諸生位中五取蘊轉。界得云何？謂即彼諸蘊因緣所攝性。處得云何？謂即彼諸蘊餘緣所攝性。諸蘊生起云何？謂即彼諸蘊日日飲食之所資長。命根出現云何？謂即彼諸蘊餘壽力故，得相續住。

What is the attainment of the aggregates? The five aggregates of clinging actually occurring at every one of these stages of birth. What is the attainment of their causal element? The very same aggregates as included within their causal condition. What is the attainment of their basis? The very same aggregates as included within their remaining conditions. What is the arising of the aggregates? These same aggregates sustained and nourished, day after day, by food. What is the manifestation of the life-faculty? These same aggregates continuing on, sustained by the remaining power of their allotted lifespan.

此生支略義者，謂若生自性、若生處位、若所生、若因緣所攝、若任持所引、若俱生依持，是名略義。

The essential meaning of this link, birth, is this: its own essential nature, the stages of its process, what it actually produces, what is included within its causes and conditions, what sustains and draws it onward, and what accompanies and supports it — this is its essential meaning.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps the first five terms directly onto the embryology already given in fascicles 1, 2, and 9: birth and manifestation together cover the link's own essential nature, splitting womb- and egg-birth's gradual conception from moisture- and spontaneous-birth's instantaneous appearance; equal-growth, emergence, and arising together trace the developmental sequence from a fully formed but still-unborn body, through actual delivery, to ongoing growth afterward. The remaining five terms shift from developmental description to the aggregates' own causal analysis: the aggregates' attainment names their sheer occurrence at any of these stages; their causal-element and basis attainments name, respectively, the seed-condition that produces them and the further supporting conditions (objective, immediately-preceding, and dominant) that let them actually manifest — Cháng Bǎi cites the commentary's threefold distinction between seed-attainment, mastery-attainment, and manifestation-attainment as clarifying exactly which of these five terms tracks which kind of "attainment"; their ongoing arising names the four foods' daily sustaining work, already detailed in fascicle 5; and the life-faculty's manifestation names the karmic force of one's allotted lifespan holding the whole continuing process together until it runs out.

He treats the closing sixfold summary as folding these ten terms into a compact checklist any student could use to locate a given passage's meaning within the birth link as a whole: is it describing birth's essential nature, its process, its actual product, its causal or supporting conditions, its ongoing nourishment, or its underlying life-force — six angles on the single fact of a new existence beginning and sustaining itself.

4. Old Age in Seventeen Unsparing Details, from Trembling Limbs to a Failing Memory

The treatise anatomizes old age with startling physical precision — seventeen distinct signs, from the first tremor of weakening faculties through whitening hair, sagging skin, a stooped spine, a cane leaned on for support, and a fading memory, all the way to the body's final decline — then compresses the seventeen into eleven categories of "decay."

Chinese Source

衰云何？謂依止劣故，令彼掉動。老云何？謂髮色衰變。襵云何？謂皮膚緩皺。熟云何？謂火力衰減，無復勢力受用欲塵。氣力損壞云何？謂性多疾病故，無有勢力能作事業。黑黶間身云何？謂黯黑出現，損其容色。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is frailty? The faculties, grown weak, begin to tremble. What is aging? The hair changes and loses its color. What is wrinkling? The skin grows slack and creased. What is a failing constitution? Bodily warmth declines, leaving no strength left to enjoy the objects of desire. What is loss of vigor? Frequent illness leaves no strength left to accomplish anything. What is blotched skin? Dark spots appear, marring the complexion.

身脊傴曲、喘息奔急云何？謂行步威儀身形所顯，由此發起極重喘嗽。形貌僂前云何？謂坐威儀位，身首低曲。憑據杖策云何？謂住威儀位，依杖力而住。昏昧云何？謂臥威儀位，數重睡眠。羸劣云何？謂即於此位，無力速覺。

What is a stooped spine and labored breath? The bent form and hurried gait visible in walking, giving rise to heavy panting and coughing. What is a bowed posture? The head and body bent low while seated. What is leaning on a staff? Needing a cane's support simply to stand. What is a clouded mind? Falling repeatedly into heavy sleep while lying down. What is feebleness? In this same state, being unable to wake quickly.

損減云何？謂念慧衰退。衰退云何？謂念慧劣故，至於善法不能現行。諸根耄熟云何？謂身體尫羸。功用破壞云何？謂彼於境不復明利。諸行朽故云何？謂彼於後將欲終時。其形腐敗云何？謂壽量將盡，身形臨壞，於諸事業無復功能。

What is diminishment? Mindfulness and wisdom declining. What is decline itself? Mindfulness and wisdom so weakened that wholesome practice can no longer even be undertaken. What is the faculties' full decrepitude? The body grown utterly frail. What is broken function? The faculties no longer meeting their objects with any clarity or keenness. What is decayed formation? Being close to the very end of life. What is bodily decay? The lifespan nearly spent, the body on the verge of collapse, capable of nothing further at all.

此老略義者，謂依止變壞、鬚髮變壞、充悅變壞、火力變壞、無病變壞、色相變壞、威儀變壞、無色諸根變壞、有色諸根變壞、時分已過、壽量將盡，略義應知。

The essential meaning of old age is this: decay of one's own support, decay of hair, decay of fullness, decay of bodily warmth, decay of health, decay of complexion, decay of bearing, decay of the formless faculties, decay of the formed faculties, time having passed, and the lifespan nearing its end — this is its essential meaning.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this seventeen-item list as strikingly unsentimental by design — the treatise names each sign of aging with the same clinical precision it gave the eighteen hells or the body's own organs in fascicle 3, refusing to soften what awaits every unliberated being. He groups the seventeen exactly as the closing summary does: the first six trace visible bodily decline (weakening faculties, graying hair, slackening skin, failing constitution, mounting illness, blotching skin); the next five trace the breakdown of the four dignified postures — walking, sitting, standing, and lying down each visibly compromised in turn; the next two trace the inward decline of mindfulness and wisdom themselves, the very faculties a practitioner most needs; the next two trace the coarser physical faculties' own final decrepitude; and the last two mark simply that time has run out and the body stands at the very edge of collapse.

He reads the deliberate ordering — outward before inward, physical before mental, gradual before final — as itself a teaching device: by naming decline in such granular, undeniable detail, the treatise removes any comfortable vagueness about what aging actually is, precisely so a practitioner cannot indefinitely postpone taking the path seriously by imagining old age as a distant abstraction rather than this exact, specific, unavoidable sequence.

5. Death in Nine Precise Stages, and the Twelve Links' Differences Close Entirely

The treatise closes the twelfth link with the same unsparing precision — nine distinct stages of dying, from consciousness first leaving the body through the senses' final failure to the exact moment breath, warmth, and the last aggregate all give way — and with this, every one of the twelve links' own differences, opened at the start of this analysis back in fascicle 9, is now fully covered.

Chinese Source

終云何？謂諸有情離解支節而死。盡云何？謂諸有情由解支節而死。壞云何？謂識離身。沒云何？謂諸色根滅。捨壽云何？謂氣將盡位。捨煖云何？謂不動位。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is ending? Dying free of the pain of the joints coming apart. What is expiring? Dying through the joints actually coming apart. What is dissolution? Consciousness departing the body. What is failing? The physical faculties ceasing. What is the relinquishing of breath? The stage when the breath is about to be entirely spent. What is the relinquishing of warmth? The stage of complete stillness.

棄捨諸蘊命根謝滅云何？謂時死。死云何？謂遇橫緣，非時而死。時蘊盡云何？謂初死未久位。又死魔業名時蘊盡。此死略義者，謂若死、若死法、若死差別、若死後位，是名略義。如是名為緣起差別應知。

What is the relinquishing of every aggregate as the life-faculty itself passes away? Dying when one's time has fully come. What is death by misfortune? Dying out of season, through some untimely circumstance. What is the aggregates' expiring in time? The stage immediately after death has just occurred; it is also called the working of the lord of death. The essential meaning of death is this: death itself, the manner of dying, death's own varieties, and the stage following death — this is its essential meaning. This, then, is what is meant by the differences of dependent origination.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes "ending" from "expiring" by realm: gods and hell-beings, both spontaneously born, die without the specific agony of "joints coming apart," while every other desire-realm being dies through it — the classical "knife-wind" said to sever flesh, sinew, bone, marrow, and blood all at once, vividly described in several sūtras he cites only by name as bringing pain "no part of the body escapes." He traces the actual sequence of dying in order: consciousness first begins departing the body (from the crown downward or the feet upward, converging finally at the heart, exactly as fascicle 1's own earliest account of conception described in reverse); the physical sense-faculties fail in turn; breath is spent; the body stiffens into total stillness; and the aggregates and life-faculty finally give way together — either "on time," when a being's full allotted lifespan is genuinely exhausted, or "by misfortune," the nine specific categories of untimely death the Bhaiṣajyaguru Sūtra names (illness without a doctor, execution, loss of vital energy to a spirit, fire, drowning, wild animals, falling, poison or curse, and starvation or thirst) — a distinction, he notes, between death from genuinely spent past karma and death from present, avoidable circumstance.

He treats the final term, "the aggregates' expiring in time," as carrying a double sense worth holding together: literally, simply the brief interval immediately following death itself; and, read through the lens of the four māras named back in fascicle 5's account of genuine joy conquering them, this same expiring is also called "the working of the lord of death" (死魔業), since the sheer fact that any born being must eventually undergo this dissolution is itself death's own domain of power over the unliberated. With this, old age and death — the twelfth and final link — are fully anatomized, and the treatise's entire link-by-link analysis of dependent origination's differences (緣起差別), the fourth of the nine topics opened in fascicle 9, is now complete for all twelve links without exception.

6. Why This Order? The Twelve Links Walked Through as a Single Lived Sequence

The treatise's fifth topic tackles a hard question head-on: why does the Buddha always teach the twelve links in this exact order? The first answer simply walks the whole chain through once more as a single lived sequence, showing each link is the natural, inevitable consequence of the one before it — and a second answer shows the twelve split cleanly into two sixes, one governing the inward body, the other governing experience of the outward world.

Chinese Source

問：何因緣故，無明等諸有支作如是次第說？答：諸愚癡者要先愚於所應知事，次即於彼發起邪行。由邪行故，令心顛倒。心顛倒故，結生相續。生相續故，諸根圓滿。根圓滿故，二受用境。受用境故，若耽著，若希求。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: For what reason are ignorance and the other links taught in this particular order? Answer: One who is deluded must first be ignorant of what ought to be known, and only then, in consequence, undertakes wrongful conduct. Through wrongful conduct, the mind becomes distorted. Because the mind is distorted, rebirth is bound and continues. Because rebirth continues, the faculties become fully formed. Because the faculties are fully formed, both faculty and consciousness come to experience their objects. Because objects are experienced, one either grows attached to them or longs for them.

由希求故，於方覓時煩惱滋長。煩惱滋長故，發起後有愛非愛業。由所起業滋長力故，於五趣生死中苦果生。苦果生已，有老死等苦。謂內身變異所引老死苦，及境界變異所引憂歎苦熱惱之苦。是故世尊如是次第說十二支。

Because of this longing, affliction grows all the more in the very seeking of what is longed for. Because affliction grows, karma desirable or undesirable for a further existence is set in motion. Because of the growing force of this karma, a suffering result arises somewhere among the five destinies of birth and death. Once that suffering result has arisen, the suffering of old age and death follows — the suffering of old age and death brought on by change within the body itself, and the suffering of sorrow, lamentation, and distress brought on by change in the surrounding world. This is why the Blessed One teaches the twelve links in exactly this order.

復有次第差別，謂依二種緣建立緣起次第。一、內身緣，二、受用境界緣。內身緣，前六支所攝；受用境界緣，後六支所攝。

There is, further, a difference of sequence established by two kinds of condition: the condition of the inward body, and the condition of experiencing the outward world. The condition of the inward body is what the first six links belong to; the condition of experiencing the outward world is what the remaining six links belong to.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the first answer as tracing one continuous, psychologically inevitable slide rather than twelve arbitrary stops: not knowing the true nature of one's own existence (ignorance) leads directly to acting on that misunderstanding (formations); acting wrongly leaves the mind distorted at the moment of death (consciousness); a distorted mind binds itself to a new existence (name-and-form); that existence matures its own faculties (the six bases); mature faculties inevitably meet their objects (contact); meeting objects inevitably produces feeling; feeling inevitably tempts either attachment or longing (craving); longing inevitably intensifies, precisely in the act of pursuing its object, into grasping; grasping inevitably ripens dormant karma into becoming; becoming inevitably delivers a result somewhere among the five destinies (birth); and any birth inevitably carries old age, death, and their attendant grief. He stresses the word "inevitably" throughout: the order is not a stylistic convention but a genuine causal cascade, each link forced into existence by the one immediately prior, which is exactly why the Buddha could not teach them in any other sequence without falsifying how the chain actually operates.

On the second, complementary explanation, he notes the elegant symmetry: the first six links (ignorance through contact) together explain how an inward body — a new psychophysical existence — comes to exist at all, while the last six (feeling through old-age-and-death) explain what happens once that body begins experiencing an outward world through it. He treats this as a genuinely different, non-redundant lens on the identical twelve links: the first sequence explains why each link causes the next; this second grouping explains what larger project each half of the chain is actually accomplishing — one half building an existence, the other half explaining what that existence then does with the objects it encounters.

7. A Third Explanation: Three Kinds of Beings, Each Living Out a Different Portion of the Chain

A third explanation for the twelve links' fixed sequence sorts beings themselves into three kinds — those who delight in genuine transcendent purity, those who delight only in worldly purity still bound to the three realms, and those who simply delight in clinging to sense objects — showing how each type of being actually lives out a different stretch of the very same chain.

Chinese Source

復有次第差別，謂由三種有情聚。一、樂出世清淨，二、樂世間清淨，三、樂著境界。由初聚故，滅諸緣起，增白淨品。

Ru-Yi Translation

There is, further, a difference of sequence founded on three kinds of beings: those who delight in transcendent purity, those who delight only in worldly purity, and those who simply delight in clinging to sense objects. Through the first kind of being, dependent origination is extinguished, and the pure, bright portion of the teaching increases.

由第二有情聚故，不如實知諸諦道理。若住正念，或作福業，或作有漏修所引不動業；若不住正念，便發非福業。彼又如前，於下中上生處，次第能感當來三種苦果，謂名色為先，觸為最後。

Through the second kind of being, the truths are not truly known. Abiding in mindfulness, one performs meritorious karma, or contaminated karma leading to meditative unmoving states; not abiding in mindfulness, one instead performs demeritorious karma. Just as before, in the lower, middling, or higher places of birth, this being brings about, in sequence, three future results — name-and-form first, contact last.

由第三有情聚故，依現受用境所生受，於現法中如前次第起後六支，謂受為先，老死為後。

Through the third kind of being, resting on the feeling arisen from presently experiencing sense objects, the remaining six links arise, in this very existence, in exactly the sequence already given — feeling first, old-age-and-death last.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this threefold typology as the sequence question's most practically oriented answer yet, since it maps the abstract twelve-link chain directly onto three recognizably different kinds of people. The first kind — a genuine practitioner of the path — is defined precisely by running the chain in reverse, extinguishing it link by link exactly as fascicle 9's own cessation formula described; for this being, the "sequence" question barely applies, since the whole point of practice is to dismantle the sequence rather than live it out. The second kind delights in worldly purity — pursuing merit or meditative attainment without yet grasping the Four Noble Truths — and lives out the chain's first six links (ignorance through contact) precisely as already explained, landing in one of the three realms' lower, middling, or higher regions depending on the karma performed, whether steadied by mindfulness into merit and meditation or, lapsing from it, sliding into demerit.

The third kind, simply absorbed in sense pleasure with no aspiration toward practice at all, lives out only the chain's second half (feeling through old-age-and-death) within this very life, since such a being's entire concern is with what is presently experienced rather than any future consequence. Cháng Bǎi draws the underlying point together: the same twelve links describe three very different lived realities depending on which portion of the chain currently occupies a given being's attention — proof, he stresses, that the treatise's insistence on a fixed sequence is never merely doctrinal tidiness, but a genuinely diagnostic tool for locating exactly where in the chain any particular person, including the reader, currently stands.

8. Reciting the Chain Backward, and Why an Arhat's Liberation Is Named for Name-and-Form, Not Ignorance

The treatise explains why the twelve links are sometimes taught in reverse, starting from old age and death rather than ignorance — to display the truth of suffering first, since that is what beings actually feel — then resolves an apparent puzzle: why certain sūtras call an arhat's liberation "the cessation of new name-and-form" as foremost, rather than "the cessation of ignorance," when ignorance is technically the very first link.

Chinese Source

問：何因緣故，逆次第中老死為先說諸緣起？答：依止宣說諦道理故，以生及老死能顯苦諦。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: For what reason, when the links are recited in reverse, is old-age-and-death spoken of first? Answer: This rests on the way the truths themselves are taught — birth and old-age-and-death are what make the truth of suffering visible.

如世尊言：新名色滅為上首法。問：何故不言諸無明滅為上首耶？答：依心解脫者而施設故。由彼於現法中種子苦及當來苦果不生而滅，故說名色為先，受為最後，得究竟滅。又於現法中受諸受時，愛及隨眠永拔不起說名為滅。由彼滅故，以彼為先餘支亦滅。

As the Blessed One said: the cessation of new name-and-form is the foremost teaching. Question: Why does he not instead say the cessation of ignorance is foremost? Answer: This is established from the standpoint of one who is liberated in mind. For such a one, the seed of suffering in this very existence, and the suffering result yet to come, no longer arises and thereby ceases — so it is said that name-and-form ceases first, and feeling last, reaching final cessation. And further, in this very existence, whenever any feeling is experienced, craving and its latent tendency are uprooted forever and do not arise — this is called cessation. Because craving thus ceases, every remaining link, taking it as the starting point, ceases as well.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the reverse recitation as pedagogically deliberate: since ordinary beings feel old age and death directly and vividly, while ignorance is invisible even to themselves, starting from the visible suffering and working backward through its causes — old-age-and-death, then birth, then becoming, then grasping, then craving, then feeling — lets the teaching begin exactly where a listener's own experience already is, gradually excavating causes the listener would never have located unaided.

On the "new name-and-form" puzzle, he explains the apparent inconsistency dissolves once the standpoint is made explicit: the teaching is given specifically from within an already-liberated arhat's own perspective, for whom ignorance and craving are already both fully extinguished — so naming "ignorance's cessation" would be redundant, since it has already happened and is simply presupposed. What actually marks this arhat's ongoing freedom, moment to moment, is that no new name-and-form is ever perfumed into a future seed, and that craving never arises even as ordinary feeling continues to occur — which is precisely fascicle 9's own two liberations (wisdom-liberation through ignorance's extinction, mind-liberation through craving's extinction) restated from a slightly different angle. With the reverse sequence and this scriptural puzzle both resolved, the treatise's fifth topic — the hard questions dependent origination's sequence must answer — is now complete in its entirety.

9. Why "Dependent Origination"? Five Layers of a Single Name

The treatise's sixth topic asks the most basic question of all — why is dependent origination even called by this name? — and answers it five distinct ways: by its literal characters, by the momentary rise-and-fall it actually consists of, by how a cause's influence persists even after the cause itself is gone, and, citing the Buddha's own classical formula, by the strict "this existing, that exists" logic that rules out every rival account of how anything comes to be.

Chinese Source

問：何故緣起說為緣起？答：由煩惱繫縛，往諸趣中數數生起故，名為緣起。此依字釋名。復次，依託眾緣，速謝滅已，續和合生，故名緣起。此依剎那義釋。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: Why is dependent origination called by this name? Answer: Because, bound by affliction, one is again and again brought to arise among the destinies — this is what "dependent origination" is named for. This explains the name by its literal characters. Further: resting on a gathering of conditions, a thing swiftly passes away, and, resting again on a further gathering, continues to arise once more — this is what "dependent origination" is named for. This explains the name by its momentary nature.

復次，眾緣過去而不捨離，依自相續而得生起，故名緣起。如說：此有故彼有，此生故彼生，非餘。

Further: though the gathered conditions themselves have already passed, their influence is never abandoned, and a result arises resting on one's own continuing stream — this is what "dependent origination" is named for. As it is said: this existing, that comes to exist; this arising, that arises — and nothing else is the case.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the first explanation as the plainest: "dependent" (緣) names being bound by affliction, "origination" (起) names being repeatedly brought to birth among the destinies — the compound term is simply a direct description of what actually happens to an unliberated being. The second explanation is aimed squarely at rival Buddhist schools: against those who held dependent origination itself to be an unconditioned, eternal law (untouched whether a buddha appears in the world or not) and against those who denied that conditioned things are truly momentary, the treatise insists every link is itself perishing and reappearing instant by instant — dependent origination is not one slow unfolding but an unbroken rapid sequence of arisings and cessations, so fast that ordinary perception mistakes it for a single continuous thing.

The third explanation addresses a subtler worry: if a cause has already vanished the instant its result appears, how can cause and result be meaningfully connected at all? Cháng Bǎi explains that although the originating conditions themselves are gone, their influence is never simply lost — it is perfumed as a seed into one's own continuing mental stream (the storehouse consciousness, in the treatise's own Yogācāra vocabulary), ready to ripen a corresponding result even after eons have passed, exactly as the treatise's much earlier account of karmic seeds and ripening, back in fascicle 9's causal analysis, already established. He reads the closing citation — "this existing, that comes to exist; this arising, that arises, and nothing else" — as the Buddha's own compressed rejection of every rival account surveyed across fascicles 6 and 7: not self-caused, not caused by another acting alone, not caused by both together, and not uncaused — dependent origination alone, and "nothing else," genuinely explains how anything comes to be.

10. Two Final Meanings of the Name — Endless Death-and-Rebirth, and the Buddha's Own Realization

The treatise gives the etymology's fourth and fifth explanations, closing dependent origination's sixth topic entirely: the name marks the sheer repetition of dying and arising again, and, quoting the Buddha's own words, the fact that he first realized this very teaching and then simply passed it on exactly as realized.

Chinese Source

復次，數數謝滅，復相續起，故名緣起。此依數壞數滅義釋。復次，於過去世覺緣性已，等相續起，故名緣起。如世尊言：我已覺悟，正起宣說，即由此名展轉傳說，故名緣起。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further: passing away again and again, and continuing to arise again and again — this too is what "dependent origination" is named for. This explains the name by its repeated arising and perishing. Further: having, in a past age, awakened to the very nature of conditioning, and then teaching it exactly as it was realized — this too is what "dependent origination" is named for. As the Blessed One said: "Having awakened to this, I now rightly proclaim it" — and it is by this very teaching, passed on from one to the next, that it is called dependent origination.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes the fourth explanation from the second (momentariness): the second names the instant-by-instant flicker within a single existence, while the fourth names the coarser, more visible repetition of an entire being's death and rebirth, life after life — the same underlying instability, simply observed at two different scales, from the subatomic to the biographical. He cites the Sūtra on the Noble Path of Dependent Origination for the fifth explanation's fuller context: the Buddha describes having directly seen old age and death, seen their arising, seen their cessation, and seen the path leading to that cessation, tracing the same seeing back through every link to formations — declaring this realization entirely self-attained, without a teacher, and that what makes it "dependent origination" as taught is precisely this unbroken transmission, from the Buddha's own direct realization, through his exact re-articulation of what he saw, and now through the entire lineage of teachers passing it on.

With this, all five explanations of the name — by its literal characters, by momentary rise-and-fall, by a cause's persisting influence, by repeated death-and-rebirth, and by the Buddha's own realized-then-transmitted teaching — are complete, closing dependent origination's sixth topic (緣起釋詞, its etymology) in its entirety. The treatise now turns to its seventh topic: precisely which of the four kinds of philosophical condition (causal, immediately-preceding, objective, and dominant) actually operate between each pair of adjacent links.

11. Which of the Four Conditions Actually Links Each Pair — and Why Never a Causal One

The treatise's seventh topic works out, with real technical precision, exactly which of the four classical kinds of condition operate between any two adjacent links — physical formations needing only a dominant condition from ignorance, mental formations needing three — then answers a sharp objection: if no causal condition ever operates between the links, how can the whole chain still be called a matter of cause and effect?

Chinese Source

問：無明望行為幾種緣？答：望諸色行為增上緣，望無色行為三緣，謂等無間緣、所緣緣、增上緣。如是餘支為緣多少，應如此知。謂有色支望有色支，為一增上緣；望無色支為二緣，謂所緣緣及增上緣。若無色支望有色支，唯為一緣；望無色支為三緣，謂等無間緣、所緣緣、增上緣。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: In relation to formations, how many kinds of condition does ignorance provide? Answer: In relation to physical formations, a dominant condition alone; in relation to mental formations, three conditions — the immediately-preceding condition, the objective condition, and the dominant condition. Every other pair of links should be understood the same way: a physical link, in relation to a further physical link, provides a dominant condition alone; in relation to a mental link, two conditions — the objective and the dominant. A mental link, in relation to a physical link, provides only one condition; in relation to a further mental link, three — the immediately-preceding, the objective, and the dominant.

問：何故諸支相望無因緣耶？答：因緣者，自體種子緣所顯故。問：若諸支相望無因緣者，何故說言：依因果體性建立緣起耶？

Question: Why is there never a causal condition between any two links? Answer: A causal condition is, by its own nature, revealed only through a thing's own seed. Question: If there is never a causal condition between the links, then why is it said that dependent origination is established resting on the very nature of cause and result?

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through the logic behind the precise count: bodily and verbal karma (physical formations) can only ever be pushed into motion, never directly caused, by a preceding mental state like ignorance — so ignorance provides them only a dominant condition, the weakest and most general of the four; but ignorance conditions the following moment of mental karma three ways at once — as the immediately preceding mental state that must cease for the next one to arise, as the very object that distorted thinking dwells on, and as the dominant force pushing the whole process forward. He notes this same fine-grained counting runs through every one of the twelve links, doubling or thinning out with mechanical precision depending on whether each member of the pair is physical or mental — a genuinely rigorous piece of Abhidharma engineering, he says, meant to prevent students from casually assuming a single vague notion of "causation" covers every link in the chain equally.

On the sharper objection — why is there never a genuine causal condition between adjacent links, when the whole chain is elsewhere called causal through and through — he explains the resolution rests on a careful distinction already implicit in fascicle 9's own account of karmic seeds: a causal condition, strictly speaking, is only ever a thing's own seed producing its own later manifestation (ignorance's seed producing ignorance's own arising, formations' seed producing formations' own arising), never one link's seed directly producing a different link's arising. What connects ignorance to formations is instead the weaker relations of sequence, object, and dominant support — real conditioning, but not the strict identity a causal condition requires. He flags the very question the treatise poses next — how the whole chain can still be called a matter of "cause and result" without any causal condition linking its parts — as the natural next puzzle this careful distinction creates, still to be resolved.

12. Resolving the Puzzle: Three Sub-Kinds of Dominant Condition Are What "Cause and Result" Actually Means

The treatise resolves its own puzzle: dependent origination's talk of "cause and result" rests entirely within the dominant condition, itself splitting into three finer sub-kinds — an inducing cause, a projecting cause, and a generating cause — which map with precision onto exactly which links project a future existence and which ones actually bring it to birth.

Chinese Source

答：依增上緣所攝引發因、牽引因、生起因故，說名為因。問：幾支是引因所攝？答：從無明乃至受。問：幾支是生因所攝？答：從愛乃至有。問：幾支是生、引二因果所攝耶？答：於現法後法中，識等乃至受，於生、老死位所攝諸支。

Ru-Yi Translation

Answer: It is on the basis of the inducing cause, the projecting cause, and the generating cause — all three included within the dominant condition — that dependent origination is spoken of in terms of cause. Question: Which links are included within the projecting cause? Answer: From ignorance through feeling. Question: Which links are included within the generating cause? Answer: From craving through becoming. Question: Which links are the result of both the projecting and generating causes together? Answer: In this present existence and the next, consciousness through feeling, and, at the stages of birth and old-age-and-death, the links belonging to them.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi resolves the apparent contradiction by locating all three of these sub-causes squarely within the dominant condition, the broadest and weakest of the four kinds of condition — never within the strict causal condition, which is reserved for a thing's own seed producing its own later manifestation. The inducing cause is the general name for how one link's occurrence conditions the next at all; the projecting cause, more specifically, names the first six links (ignorance through feeling) laying down the seeds — exactly as fascicle 9's own account of the "projecting cause" first explained — for a future existence not yet actualized; and the generating cause names craving, grasping, and becoming actually moistening those seeds into readiness, precisely as fascicle 9's own account of the "generating cause" described.

He treats the final question — which links belong to both causes' combined result — as simply naming the actual product each cause aims at: consciousness through feeling, in whichever existence they occur, are always the projecting cause's fruit, while birth and old-age-and-death are always the generating cause's fruit, once craving and grasping have done their moistening work. With this precise mapping, the earlier puzzle dissolves: dependent origination can rightly be called a matter of "cause and result" throughout, even though no single strict causal condition ever bridges two adjacent links, because the weaker dominant condition, working through these three named sub-causes, does the entire chain's actual causal work.

13. Why Ignorance, Not Wrong Attention, Opens the Chain — and Why Even Merit Still Depends on It

The treatise's eighth topic, distinguishing conditions link by link, opens with two sharp objections about the very first link: why does the chain begin with ignorance rather than the "unsound reasoning attention" that actually produces it, and why does even genuinely wholesome, carefully considered karma — merit and meditation alike — still get credited to ignorance as its condition?

Chinese Source

問：若說無明以不如理作意為因，何因緣故於緣起教中不先說耶？答：彼唯是不斷因故，非雜染因故。非不愚者起此作意。依雜染因說緣起教，無明自性是染汙，不如理作意自性非染汙，故彼不能染汙無明，然由無明力所染汙。又生雜染業煩惱力之所熏發，業之初因謂初緣起。是故不說不如理作意。

Ru-Yi Translation

Answer: Unsound reasoning attention is merely a cause preventing ignorance's severance, not a cause of its defilement. It is never anyone but the already-deluded who gives rise to it in the first place. The teaching of dependent origination is given specifically in terms of the cause of defilement: ignorance is itself defiled by nature, while unsound reasoning attention is not defiled by nature — so it cannot itself defile ignorance, but is rather defiled by ignorance's own power. Further, the defilement of rebirth is brought about by the power of karma and affliction together, and the very first cause of karma is this same original link of dependent origination. This is why unsound reasoning attention is not spoken of first.

問：何因緣故，福行、不動行由正揀擇功力而起，仍說用無明為緣耶？答：由不了達世俗苦因為緣，起非福行；由不了達勝義苦因為緣，生福及不動行；是故亦說彼以無明為緣。

Question: For what reason are meritorious karma and unmoving karma, which arise through the genuine effort of careful discernment, still said to have ignorance as their condition? Answer: Conditioned by failing to fathom the cause of conventional suffering, demeritorious karma arises; conditioned by failing to fathom the cause of ultimate suffering, meritorious and unmoving karma arise — and this is why these, too, are said to have ignorance as their condition.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains the first answer through a careful distinction: unsound reasoning attention only ever arises in a mind already deluded — it never itself causes ignorance to become defiled, since it isn't defiled in its own right, but is simply ignorance's own tool, itself powered by ignorance. Since dependent origination is specifically a teaching about the root cause of defilement, and ignorance is what is genuinely defiled by nature, ignorance rightly opens the chain rather than the merely instrumental attention that operates under its power — and since karma, the second of the three defilements named at fascicle 8's opening, ultimately traces back to this same root, ignorance is fittingly named the very first link of all.

On the second question, he distinguishes conventional suffering (the coarse suffering of pain, and of pleasure's own inevitable decay) from ultimate suffering (the far subtler suffering of formations, the mere fact of being conditioned and impermanent, most visible in neutral feeling as fascicle 9 already explained). Ordinary unwholesome conduct arises from simply not understanding the first, coarser kind — craving pleasure, resenting pain. But even a person who carefully cultivates merit or attains deep meditative absorption, all the way up through the formless spheres, is still operating in ignorance of the second, subtler kind — mistaking a genuinely disciplined, well-reasoned pursuit of merit or stillness for real freedom, when it has not yet touched the deeper delusion of self-view fascicle 6 dismantled at such length. This is exactly why even careful, deliberate goodness still counts as "conditioned by ignorance": discernment alone, however genuine, is not the same as having actually seen through impermanence and non-self.

14. Why This Link and Not an Equally Plausible Rival — Five More Sharp Questions Answered

The treatise continues interrogating its own formula with real rigor: why ignorance rather than the three poisons, why ignorance rather than intention itself, why formations rather than name-and-form as consciousness's condition, why consciousness alone among the six elements said to enter the womb, and why name-and-form rather than food as the six bases' condition — each answered by showing the chosen link does work no rival candidate can match.

Chinese Source

問：如經中說：諸業以貪瞋癡為緣。何故此中唯說癡為緣耶？答：此中通說福、非福、不動業緣，貪瞋癡緣唯生非福業故。問：身業、語業思所發起，是則行亦緣行；何故但說無明緣行？答：依發起一切行緣而說故，及依生善、染汙思緣而說故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: The sūtras say karma is conditioned by craving, hatred, and delusion — why does this teaching name only delusion as the condition? Answer: This teaching covers meritorious, demeritorious, and unmoving karma alike, while craving and hatred as condition produce only demeritorious karma. Question: Since bodily and verbal karma are themselves set in motion by intention, is not formations itself, in a sense, the condition for formations — why say only that ignorance conditions formations? Answer: This is stated in terms of what conditions every kind of formation, and in terms of what conditions both wholesome and defiled intention alike.

問：識亦以名色為緣，何故此中但說行為緣耶？答：行為識雜染緣，能引、能生後有果故。非如名色，但為所依、所緣，生起緣故。問：名色亦由大種所造及由觸生，何故但說識為緣耶？答：識能為彼新生因故。彼既生已，或正生時，大種及觸唯能與彼為建立因。

Question: Consciousness, too, is conditioned by name-and-form — why does this teaching name only formations as its condition? Answer: Formations condition consciousness's own defilement, capable of both projecting and generating a further existence's result — unlike name-and-form, which serves only as consciousness's basis and object, a mere condition for its arising. Question: Name-and-form, too, arises from the great elements and from contact — why name only consciousness as its condition? Answer: Consciousness alone is the cause of name-and-form's very arising; once name-and-form has arisen, or is in the process of arising, the great elements and contact serve only as its supporting, not its originating, cause.

問：如經中說：六界為緣得入母胎。何故此中唯說識界？答：若有識界，決定於母胎中精血、大種、腹穴無闕故。又識界勝故。又依一切生、一切有生時而說故。問：六處亦以飲食為緣，何故此中但說名色為緣耶？答：此中說名色是彼生因故。彼既生已，亦以飲食為任持因。

Question: The sūtras say the six elements together are the condition for entering the womb — why does this teaching name only the element of consciousness? Answer: Wherever the element of consciousness is present, the womb's blood and semen, the great elements, and the bodily cavity are all, without exception, already present as well. Further, the element of consciousness is simply the most decisive of the six. Further, this is stated in terms of what applies across every mode and every realm of birth. Question: The six sense-bases, too, are conditioned by food — why name only name-and-form as their condition? Answer: This teaching names name-and-form as their originating cause; once the bases have arisen, food then serves as the cause that merely sustains them.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats every one of these objections as sharing the same underlying shape: a rival candidate is genuinely present and genuinely relevant, but only the link the treatise actually names does the specific work dependent origination's formula requires — usually the work of originating rather than merely sustaining, or the work of covering every case rather than only some. Craving and hatred explain unwholesome karma alone, but ignorance alone explains merit and meditation as well, so only ignorance is comprehensive enough to name; intention explains bodily and verbal action, but ignorance alone also explains why that intention itself is wholesome or defiled, reaching one layer deeper.

He draws out a genuinely important technical distinction running through the middle three answers: an originating cause (what actually brings a thing newly into being) is different from a mere supporting or sustaining cause (what a thing needs once it already exists) — formations genuinely originate consciousness's defiled character by planting its seed, while name-and-form merely gives consciousness something to rest on and cognize; consciousness genuinely originates name-and-form at the very first instant of conception, while the great elements and contact merely keep it going once it exists; name-and-form genuinely originates the six bases, while food merely sustains them afterward, exactly as fascicle 9's own account of the four foods already explained. On why consciousness alone represents all six elements entering the womb, he notes the logic is almost mathematical: consciousness is simply the one element whose presence guarantees every other element is automatically present too, making it the most economical and universally applicable choice — not because the other five elements are unreal or unimportant, but because naming consciousness alone already implies them all.

15. The Remaining Six Links Defended the Same Way, Down to Old Age and Death

The treatise completes its link-by-link defense through the remaining six pairs — contact through old-age-and-death — each time showing that whatever else might plausibly serve as a rival condition, the named link alone is either the nearest cause or the one guaranteed to be present without exception.

Chinese Source

問：觸以三和為緣，何故此中但說六處為緣？答：若有六處，定有餘二無闕故。又六處勝故，由六處攝二種故。問：若自所逼迫，若他所逼迫，若時候變異，若先業所引，皆得生受；何故此中但顯觸為彼緣？答：觸是彼近因故，由觸所引故；餘緣所生受亦從觸生故，必不離觸，是故偏說。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: Contact is conditioned by the meeting of the three — faculty, object, and consciousness — why does this teaching name only the six sense-bases? Answer: Wherever the six bases are present, the other two are guaranteed present as well; and the six bases are simply the most decisive of the three, since they already include the other two. Question: Feeling can arise from self-inflicted pressure, from pressure inflicted by another, from a change of season, or from past karma — why does this teaching name only contact as its condition? Answer: Contact is feeling's nearest cause; feeling is always brought about through contact, and whatever other condition produces feeling still does so only by way of contact, which is never absent.

問：經中亦說無明為緣生愛，順愛境界亦得為緣，何故此中但說受為緣耶？答：以受力故，於相似境，或求和合，或求乖離。由愚癡力，但於諸受起盡等相不如實知，由此不能制御其心。

Question: The sūtras also say that craving is conditioned by ignorance, and that a pleasing object can itself serve as its condition — why does this teaching name only feeling? Answer: It is by the very force of feeling that one seeks union with, or seeks separation from, whatever resembles it. Through the force of delusion alone, one fails to truly know feeling's own arising and ceasing, and is thereby left unable to govern the mind at all — ignorance may lie behind this, but feeling remains the nearest cause.

問：由隨眠未斷，順彼諸法取皆得生，何故此中但說愛為取緣？答：由希望生故，於追求時，能發隨眠，及能引彼隨順法故。問：前已說無明為緣發起業有，何故今者說取緣有？答：由取力故，即令彼業於彼彼生處能引識、名色等果。

Question: Since the latent tendencies remain unsevered, grasping in all its forms could arise from them directly — why does this teaching name only craving as grasping's condition? Answer: It is craving's own longing that, in the very act of seeking its object, awakens the latent tendencies and draws grasping's other conditions into play. Question: It was already said that ignorance conditions the arising of karmic becoming — why now say that grasping conditions becoming? Answer: It is specifically grasping's own force that causes that karma to actually project consciousness, name-and-form, and the rest of its result into a given place of rebirth.

問：生亦以精血等為緣，何故此中唯說有緣生耶？答：由有有故，定有餘緣無闕。又有勝故，唯說彼為緣。問：亦由遠行、不避不平等、他所逼迫為緣，老死可得，何故此中但說生緣老死耶？答：雖由彼諸緣，必以生為根本故。縱闕彼緣，但生為緣，定有老死故。

Question: Birth, too, is conditioned by parental blood and semen — why does this teaching name only becoming as birth's condition? Answer: Wherever becoming is present, every other necessary condition is guaranteed present as well, and becoming is simply the most decisive. Question: Old age and death can also come from a hard journey, from failing to avoid danger, or from being harmed by another — why does this teaching name only birth as their condition? Answer: Whatever other conditions may be involved, birth remains their root — even lacking every other condition, birth alone is enough to guarantee that old age and death will follow.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the same two recurring arguments carry the entire remaining defense: some links (six bases for contact, becoming for birth) win by guaranteed co-presence — wherever the named link occurs, every rival condition is automatically already there too, making it the most economical choice; others (contact for feeling, craving for grasping, birth for old-age-and-death) win by being the nearest, most direct cause, the one link no rival condition can ever bypass even when it operates through other channels. He singles out the closing answer on birth and death as the chain's own memorable capstone: however death actually arrives — accident, violence, illness, old age — birth alone is the one condition that guarantees it without exception, which is why the treatise can say old age and death are rooted in birth even when every other contributing circumstance is entirely absent.

He treats the eightfold analysis of feeling embedded in the receiving section (how many kinds of feeling there are, what causes it to arise, what causes it to cease, what perpetuates it into the future, what ends that perpetuation, what makes it enticing, what its dangers are, and how to actually leave it behind) as a compact restatement of the Four Noble Truths applied specifically to feeling — arising and cessation mapping onto suffering and its origin in the present, perpetuation and its ending mapping onto the same two truths projected into the future, and the final pair (enticement and danger) mapping onto cessation and the path, since seeing feeling's real danger is what actually motivates leaving it behind.

16. The Twelve Links Sorted into Three Paths: Affliction, Karma, and Suffering

Having defended every individual link, the treatise steps back to classify all twelve at once into the classical threefold scheme — the path of affliction, the path of karma, and the path of suffering — showing that the entire chain, for all its complexity, ultimately reduces to just three moving parts endlessly driving one another.

Chinese Source

問：此十二支，幾是煩惱道？幾是業道？幾是苦道？答：三是煩惱道，二是業道，餘是苦道。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: Of these twelve links, how many belong to the path of affliction? How many to the path of karma? How many to the path of suffering? Answer: Three belong to the path of affliction; two belong to the path of karma; the remainder belong to the path of suffering.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi names the three explicitly: ignorance, craving, and grasping belong to the path of affliction, since all three are themselves defiled mental states, precisely the three defilements of the three-defilement scheme (afflictive, karmic, rebirth) that has organized the treatise's argument since fascicle 8's opening; formations and becoming belong to the path of karma, since both name actual deeds — one performed in the past, the other karma newly ripened toward the future; and the remaining seven links (consciousness, name-and-form, the six bases, contact, feeling, birth, and old-age-and-death) belong to the path of suffering, since all seven are simply the lived experience of an existing, embodied being, in whatever form that suffering takes.

He cites the classical Abhidharmakośa verse comparing affliction to a seed, a dragon, a grass-root, a tree-trunk, and rice still husked — each image stressing how affliction regenerates itself and sustains the whole cycle indefinitely unless uprooted at the root, not merely trimmed at the surface — and karma to husked rice itself, to medicinal herbs, and to a flower, each stressing that karma, once its result has fully ripened, is spent and produces nothing further on its own, unlike affliction's own self-renewing power. He treats the whole threefold scheme as the fascicle's own periodic reality-check: for all the technical granularity of nineteen kinds of ignorance, seventeen signs of old age, and dozens of individually defended conditions, the entire apparatus still reduces to exactly three things chasing each other in a circle — affliction produces karma, karma produces suffering, and suffering, met without wisdom, produces affliction all over again.

17. Cause Alone, Result Alone, or Both — and Why Nine of the Twelve Links Overlap

The treatise classifies the twelve links two more ways: by whether each is purely a cause, purely a result, or both at once; and by whether each stands alone or genuinely overlaps with its neighbors — showing that ignorance, craving, and grasping alone stay cleanly distinct, while the remaining nine links share real technical overlap with each other.

Chinese Source

問：幾唯是因？幾唯是果？幾通因果？答：初一唯因，後一唯果，餘通因果。又即於此問，更作餘答：三唯是因，二唯是果，當知所餘亦因亦果。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: How many links are purely a cause? How many purely a result? How many are both? Answer: The first alone is purely a cause; the last alone is purely a result; the rest are both. And on this same question, a further answer may be given: three are purely a cause, two are purely a result, and the remainder should be understood as both.

問：幾是獨相？幾是雜相？答：三是獨相，行等是雜相。問：何故行、有是雜相？答：由二種說故。謂能引愛非愛果故，及能生趣差別故。問：何故識與名色、六處一分有雜相？答：由三種說故。謂依雜染時故，依潤時故，依轉時故。

Question: How many links stand alone, distinct from every other? How many overlap with another? Answer: Three stand alone; formations and the rest overlap. Question: Why do formations and becoming overlap? Answer: Because both are spoken of two ways — as projecting a desirable or undesirable result, and as generating the specific destiny that result belongs to. Question: Why do consciousness, name-and-form, and part of the six bases overlap? Answer: Because consciousness is spoken of three ways — at the time it is defiled, at the time it is moistened, and at the time it actively operates.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the first classification two ways: taken most simply, only ignorance (the very first link, defiling everything downstream without ever itself being defiled by an earlier link) is purely a cause, and only old-age-and-death (the final link, produced by everything upstream but producing nothing further within the chain itself) is purely a result — with the ten links between doing double duty. Taken more precisely, only ignorance, craving, and grasping — the three genuinely afflictive links — are purely causes (the actual roots that set karma and suffering in motion), while only birth and old-age-and-death are purely results (suffering's own final, lived manifestation); the remaining seven do both, receiving from what precedes and producing what follows.

On overlap, he explains why exactly ignorance, craving, and grasping alone stand cleanly apart: as the chain's three purely afflictive links, they never technically share their defining nature with a neighboring link the way the others do. Formations and becoming overlap because they are, at bottom, the very same karmic force considered from two angles — formations as karma still merely projecting a future result, becoming as that same karma actively generating a specific rebirth; and consciousness overlaps with name-and-form and the six bases because "consciousness" itself shifts meaning across three moments — freshly defiled by karma at the moment of death, moistened into the "name" component of a new name-and-form at conception, and finally operating as the mind-base once the six bases are complete — one continuous stream wearing three different technical names as it passes through three different stages.

18. Four Meanings of "Condition" Revisited, and the Twelve Links Sorted into Suffering and Its Origin

The treatise circles back to its own etymology section with four sharper technical definitions of what "condition" actually means — ceaseless departure, the gathering of causes, ever-fresh arising, and the very fact of conditioned arising itself — then sorts all twelve links a final way: which are the truth of suffering now, which are suffering still to come, and which together make up the truth of suffering's origin.

Chinese Source

復次，於緣起中，云何數往義？謂生已不住義。云何和合義？謂諸緣聚集義。云何起義？謂諸緣和合之所引攝新新生義。云何緣起？云何緣生？謂諸行生起法性，是名緣起；即彼生已，說名緣生。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, within dependent origination, what is meant by ceaseless departure? That once arisen, nothing remains standing still. What is meant by gathering? The very gathering together of conditions. What is meant by arising? What that gathering of conditions draws in and brings to fresh, ever-new arising. What is dependent origination, and what is dependently arisen? The very nature of formations coming to be at all is called dependent origination; and that same thing, once it has actually come to be, is called what has dependently arisen.

問：幾支苦諦攝，及現法為苦？答：二，謂生及老死。問：幾支苦諦攝，當來為苦？答：識乃至受種子性。問：幾支集諦攝？答：所餘支。

Question: How many links belong to the truth of suffering, as suffering already present in this life? Answer: Two — birth and old-age-and-death. Question: How many links belong to the truth of suffering, as suffering yet to come? Answer: The seed-nature of consciousness through feeling. Question: How many links belong to the truth of suffering's origin? Answer: All the rest.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads these four re-definitions as the treatise circling back to sharpen its own earlier fivefold etymology from a slightly different angle: ceaseless departure restates the literal-character explanation (bound by affliction, endlessly arising among the destinies); gathering and arising together restate the momentary and functional explanations (a result appears only once its full complement of conditions has assembled, and what appears is always something genuinely new, never a mere repetition); and the final pair — dependent origination as the very principle by which conditioned things come to be, dependent arising as their actual concrete appearance — restates the classical "this existing, that comes to exist" formula, now formally distinguishing the abstract principle from its individual instances.

On the final sorting into the Four Noble Truths, he notes only three of the four truths actually appear here, and pointedly not cessation or the path: birth and old-age-and-death are suffering already manifest in the present life; the seed-form of consciousness through feeling is suffering not yet manifest but already guaranteed for a future life; and everything else — ignorance, formations, craving, grasping, and becoming — is the truth of suffering's origin, since these are the links that actually generate the other seven. He explains the omission is deliberate: the twelve links, as taught here, describe only the forward-flowing, defiled side of existence; cessation and the path belong to the reverse, purifying sequence fascicle 9 already gave in full, which is simply a different teaching built from the identical twelve links read backward rather than forward.

19. Three Ways Each Link Conditions the Next: Present, Immediate, and Long-Delayed

The treatise's ninth topic turns to how, exactly, each link conditions its immediate neighbor — worked out through ignorance and formations as the guiding example, showing a single link can condition the next three distinct ways at once: as a co-present obstruction clouding judgment in the moment, as the immediate trigger for the very next mental state, and as a cause whose influence persists even after the originating thought itself is long gone, ready to trigger the same result again whenever conditions permit.

Chinese Source

問：無明與行為作俱有緣？為作無間滅緣？為作久遠滅緣？答：當知具作三緣。謂由無知，於隨順諸行法中，為俱有覆障緣，為彼彼事發起諸行。又由惡見、放逸俱行無知，為無間滅生起緣，發起諸行。又由無知，為久遠滅引發緣故，建立順彼當生相續。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: Does ignorance serve as a co-present condition for formations, an immediately-preceding condition, or a long-delayed condition? Answer: It should be understood that ignorance provides all three. Through simple not-knowing, it serves as a co-present, obstructing condition amid whatever conduct is already under way, prompting formations for this or that particular purpose. Further, through not-knowing bound up with wrong views and heedlessness, it serves as the immediately-preceding condition that triggers formations' actual arising. Further, through not-knowing, it serves as a long-delayed, inducing condition, establishing the continuity that will give rise to a corresponding formation still to come.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes the three timings precisely: as a co-present condition, ignorance simply clouds judgment at the very moment a deed is performed, whatever that deed's motive — indifferent to fortune and misfortune alike, blind to the actual causes behind death and rebirth, it lets craving for existence or its opposite quietly drive the act; as an immediately-preceding condition, a specific prior moment of deluded, heedless thinking directly triggers the very next moment of intentional karma, with no gap between them; and as a long-delayed condition, ignorance perfumed into karma long ago can lie dormant for eons and still, once its proper conditions finally reassemble, trigger a corresponding formation in the present — the same seed-and-ripening mechanism fascicle 9's own causal analysis first established.

He notes the treatise then works through this identical threefold analysis systematically for every remaining adjacent pair down the chain — formations conditioning consciousness by perfuming its seed, then triggering its very next moment, then continuing to influence it across a long delay; consciousness conditioning name-and-form; and so on link by link, all the way through feeling conditioning craving, craving conditioning grasping, grasping conditioning becoming, and becoming conditioning birth and old-age-and-death — each pairing receiving its own worked example tailored to that pair's own specific mechanics (feeling's conditioning of craving, for instance, turns on how a moistened seed of craving lies dormant until a matching feeling calls it back into activity). Rather than repeat the identical three-part machinery eleven more times, Cháng Bǎi treats the ignorance-formations pair as establishing the general method once, precisely enough that a careful reader can apply it to any adjacent pair in the chain without further worked examples.

20. Why Practice Always Cuts the Chain Backward, Never Forward

The treatise draws out a genuinely practical consequence of the twelve links' fixed direction: a later link is never a condition for an earlier one, which is exactly why a practitioner seeking to end suffering always works to sever an earlier link rather than directly attacking a later one — severing the cause automatically severs everything downstream, while attacking only the downstream symptom leaves its root untouched.

Chinese Source

復次，後支非前支緣。何以故？如為斷後支故，勤作功用斷於前支，由前斷故，後亦隨斷。非為斷前故，勤作功用斷於後支。是故當知唯此為彼緣。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, a later link is never a condition for an earlier one. Why is this so? One who wishes to end a later link applies effort to end the earlier one, and because the earlier link is ended, the later link ends as well, following of its own accord. One never applies effort to end a later link for the sake of ending an earlier one. It should therefore be understood: only the earlier serves as condition for the later, never the reverse.

問：云何說言此有故彼有？答：由未斷緣，餘得生義故。問：云何此生故彼生？答：由無常緣，餘得生義故。

Question: What is meant by saying "this existing, that comes to exist"? Answer: Because, so long as a condition remains unsevered, a further thing is thereby able to arise. Question: What is meant by "this arising, that arises"? Answer: Because it is precisely through an impermanent condition that a further thing is able to arise.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi cites the Sūtra on the Noble Path of Dependent Origination for the reasoning behind this: the Buddha's own realization proceeded by asking, of old-age-and-death, "conditioned by what does this exist?" — tracing backward link by link, all the way to consciousness, where the regress stops (since consciousness at conception is where the causal trail from a prior existence terminates) — and then asking the mirror question, "absent what does this not exist?", confirming that severing each earlier link truly does sever everything built on it, down to ignorance's own cessation causing formations to cease. This backward-tracing method, he stresses, is not merely how the Buddha discovered the teaching but the direct blueprint for how any practitioner should apply it: since a downstream link can never condition an upstream one, effort spent trying to eliminate suffering (a downstream result) by direct suppression, without addressing craving or ignorance upstream, is simply misdirected — durable freedom only ever comes from moving backward up the chain to its actual root.

On the closing precision, he distinguishes "this existing, that comes to exist" from "this arising, that arises": the first describes a condition simply remaining unsevered, thereby permitting a result to occur (the seed not yet destroyed, and so capable of sprouting); the second describes that same result's own impermanent, momentary nature — what appears is never a static repetition but something genuinely new arising fresh each instant, precisely as the treatise's own earlier eightfold "meaning of dependent origination" already established (momentary, yet dependent, yet devoid of any real agent doing the causing). With this, the treatise's analysis of how links front and back relate to one another (十二支前後相望分別) is now complete.

21. A Four-Cornered Test: Is Every Case of the Condition Also a Case of the Result?

The treatise's tenth topic applies a rigorous fourfold logical test to each adjacent pair of links, checking precisely whether the two are truly coextensive — is every case of formations conditioned by ignorance, and is every case of something conditioned by ignorance actually formations? The answer, worked out through ignorance-and-formations as the guiding example, is no: uncontaminated and neutral bodily, verbal, and mental activity falls outside the twelve-link chain proper on both sides.

Chinese Source

問：若法無明為緣，彼法是行耶？設是行者，彼無明為緣耶？答：應作四句。或有行非無明為緣，謂無漏及無覆無記身語意行。或無明為緣而非是行，謂除行所攝有支所餘有支。或有亦無明為緣亦是行，謂福、非福、不動身語意行。除如是相，是第四句。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: If a thing is conditioned by ignorance, is it necessarily formations? And if it is formations, is it necessarily conditioned by ignorance? Answer: Four cases should be made. There is formations not conditioned by ignorance — namely, uncontaminated bodily, verbal, and mental activity, and morally neutral activity that does not obstruct the path. There is what is conditioned by ignorance yet is not formations — namely, every link other than formations. There is what is both conditioned by ignorance and is formations — namely, meritorious, demeritorious, and unmoving bodily, verbal, and mental activity. And apart from these three, there is a fourth case — neither conditioned by ignorance nor formations at all, belonging to none of the twelve links.

問：若行為緣，彼亦識耶？設是識者，行為緣耶？答：應作四句。或行為緣非識，謂除識所餘有支。或識非行為緣，謂無漏識及無覆無記識，除異熟生。或亦識亦行為緣，謂後有種子識及果識。除如是相，是第四句。由此道理，乃至觸緣受，隨其所應四句應知。

Question: If a thing is conditioned by formations, is it necessarily consciousness? And if it is consciousness, is it necessarily conditioned by formations? Answer: Four cases should be made. There is what is conditioned by formations yet is not consciousness — every link other than consciousness. There is consciousness not conditioned by formations — uncontaminated consciousness, and morally neutral consciousness apart from its own ripened arising. There is what is both consciousness and conditioned by formations — the seed-consciousness of a further existence, and its resulting consciousness. And, apart from these, a fourth case belonging to none of the twelve links. By this same reasoning, the four cases for every remaining pair, down through contact conditioning feeling, should be understood accordingly.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this fourfold test as a genuinely important precision instrument: it prevents a student from carelessly assuming the twelve-link chain is the exhaustive account of literally all bodily, verbal, and mental activity, when in fact an arhat's uncontaminated conduct, and ordinary morally neutral activity such as the four kinds of "neutral mind" (a ripened disposition, ordinary bearing and posture, a practiced craft, or a meditatively produced transformation), fall entirely outside it — they are activity, but not the specific defiled activity the twelve links describe. He notes the treatise itself, having established the method twice in full detail, explicitly declines to repeat the identical fourfold breakdown for every remaining pair down to contact-and-feeling as "too tedious to state in full," trusting the reader to apply the same pattern: for each pair, ask whether every instance of the earlier link's result actually falls within the twelve-link chain, and whether every instance of the later link is actually produced this specific way, always finding the same three positive cases plus a fourth residual case standing entirely outside the chain.

He draws out the payoff of this exercise: it sharpens exactly what the treatise means when it calls something "conditioned by ignorance" or "conditioned by formations" — never a claim that ignorance produces literally everything a being does, but a precise claim about the specific defiled portion of experience the twelve links are built to explain, leaving real room for uncontaminated and neutral activity that a liberated or even an ordinary mind can still perform without perpetuating the cycle.

22. Not All Longing Is Craving: The Aspiration to Liberation Itself Falls Outside the Chain

Applying the same fourfold test to feeling and craving turns up a philosophically important exception: the aspiration to liberation itself, and the wholesome longing that leads one to renounce ordinary life for the sake of the path, are technically a kind of "craving" in the everyday sense of the word — yet the treatise insists this longing is not conditioned by feeling in the defiled way the chain describes, and does not count as the craving that binds a being to saṃsāra.

Chinese Source

問：若法受為緣，彼法是愛耶？設是愛者，彼受為緣耶？答：應作四句。或有是愛非受為緣，謂希求勝解脫，及依善愛而捨餘愛。或受為緣而非是愛，謂除無明觸所生受為緣，所餘有支法生。或有受為緣亦是愛，謂無明觸所生受為緣，染汙愛生。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: If a thing is conditioned by feeling, is it necessarily craving? And if it is craving, is it necessarily conditioned by feeling? Answer: Four cases should be made. There is craving not conditioned by feeling — the aspiration to supreme liberation, and the wholesome longing by which every other, lesser longing is renounced. There is what is conditioned by feeling yet is not craving — every link other than craving, arisen from feeling not tainted by ignorance-conditioned contact. There is what is both conditioned by feeling and is craving — craving tainted by feeling arisen from ignorance-conditioned contact.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this carve-out as one of the chain's most quietly important technical points: the wish to be free of saṃsāra, and the wholesome desire (chanda) that motivates genuine renunciation and diligent practice, are structurally longings — they reach toward something not yet attained — yet they are never called "craving" (愛) in the twelve-link sense, precisely because they are not tainted by the ignorance-conditioned contact that defines every other instance of craving in the chain. This distinction matters practically: a practitioner need not, and should not, try to extinguish every form of wanting whatsoever in order to progress — the very aspiration driving the path forward is itself exempt from what the path is trying to dismantle.

He notes the treatise then shifts its logical method for the remaining pairs — craving and grasping, grasping and becoming, becoming and birth, birth and old-age-and-death — from the full fourfold test to a simpler one-directional form (順後句, "conforming to the latter clause"): rather than asking whether the two are fully coextensive in both directions, it asks only whether every instance of the later link presupposes the earlier one, while allowing that the earlier link can occur without producing the later. Applied to craving and grasping, for instance, every grasping is conditioned by craving, but genuine craving for wholesome things — diligence, mindfulness, concentration, wisdom — actively counteracts grasping rather than producing it, so craving does not always lead to grasping. The same structure, he notes, repeats down through the final links to old-age-and-death, each time confirming that the later link cannot occur without the earlier one, even though the earlier one can, in its wholesome form, occur without ever producing the later.

23. Which Links Obstruct Which Parts of the Path, and Which Are Always Defiled

The treatise's fifth topic maps the twelve links directly onto the eightfold noble path's three trainings — showing ignorance chiefly blocks wisdom, the physical links of formations and becoming chiefly block ethical conduct, and the remaining links chiefly block concentration — then asks which links are always defiled and which can go either way, singling out birth as the clearest case: born into hardship, always defiled; born into ordinary human or heavenly circumstance, capable of either.

Chinese Source

問：是諸有支，幾與道支所攝正見為勝障礙？答：無明，及彼所起意行、若有一分能為勝障。如於正見，如是於正思惟及正精進亦爾。若正語、正業、正命，以身行、語行及有一分為勝障礙。若正念、正定，以餘有支為勝障礙應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: Of these links, which most strongly obstruct right view, one factor of the noble path? Answer: Ignorance, together with the mental formation it gives rise to, and a portion of becoming, are its strongest obstruction. Right thought and right effort should be understood the same way. Right speech, right action, and right livelihood are most strongly obstructed by bodily and verbal formation, and by a portion of becoming. Right mindfulness and right concentration should be understood as most strongly obstructed by the remaining links.

問：是諸有支，幾唯雜染品？幾通雜染、清淨品？答：四唯雜染品，餘通雜染、清淨品。問：云何生支通二品耶？答：若生惡趣及有難處，唯是雜染品；若生人天諸無難處，此通染淨品。

Question: Of these links, how many belong to defilement alone? How many can belong to either defilement or purity? Answer: A few belong to defilement alone; the rest can belong to either. Question: How can the link of birth belong to either? Answer: Birth into an unfortunate destiny, or into any circumstance of genuine hardship, belongs to defilement alone; birth among ordinary humans or gods, free of such hardship, can belong to either.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps the obstruction scheme onto the three trainings the whole path reduces to: ignorance and its mental formations obstruct wisdom (right view, thought, and effort) most directly, since these path-factors are precisely what ignorance darkens; bodily and verbal formation obstruct ethical conduct (right speech, action, and livelihood), since these path-factors govern exactly the bodily and verbal karma formations names; and the remaining links obstruct concentration (right mindfulness and right concentration), since a mind still caught in consciousness, contact, feeling, craving, and the rest cannot settle into the stillness concentration requires. He treats this as the treatise's own diagnostic tool for practice: a student struggling specifically with distraction, rather than with ethical lapses or wrong view, is best served by addressing the links associated with concentration's own obstruction, not by redoubling effort on wisdom or ethics alone.

On purely-versus-mixed defilement, he cites the parallel account in the Cheng Weishi Lun that only ignorance, craving, and grasping are purely defiled by their own nature (being affliction itself), while the seven links that are simply karmic result (consciousness, name-and-form, the six bases, contact, feeling, birth, old-age-and-death) are technically neutral, becoming occasions for defilement only derivatively. He details the classical eight difficult circumstances behind "birth into hardship" — the hells, the animal realm, the hungry ghosts, the long-lived heavens (whose meditators mistake stillness for liberation and never seek the Dharma), the northern continent of effortless plenty, physical impairments barring understanding, worldly cleverness that resists real teaching, and being born in an era between buddhas — each one a rebirth so structurally unfavorable that genuine practice becomes effectively impossible, in contrast to an ordinary human or heavenly birth, where the same karmic result can still become the ground for either further defilement or a turn toward the path.

24. Three Grades of Ignorance Whose Cessation Actually Stops Formations

The treatise's sixth topic turns to precisely how each link's non-existence or cessation causes the next link's own non-existence or cessation — opening with three named grades of ignorance (the ignorance that initiates a deed, the ignorance actively manifest, and the ignorance still dormant as a seed) and showing formations only truly cease once all three are gone, then extending the same seed-versus-manifestation logic through consciousness, name-and-form, and the links beyond.

Chinese Source

問：何等無明不有故行不有，何等無明滅故行滅耶？答：有三種，發起、纏、隨眠無明。由此無明滅故，彼無明滅；由彼滅故，行亦隨滅。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: Which ignorance not existing means formations do not exist? Which ignorance ceasing means formations cease? Answer: There are three — the ignorance that initiates a deed, ignorance as active manifestation, and ignorance as dormant latent tendency. Because this dormant ignorance ceases, manifest ignorance ceases; and because that ceases, formations cease as well, following in turn.

問：何等行不有故識不有，何等行滅故識滅耶？答：諸行於自相續中已作已滅，及未起對治，又由意行有故，起身語行；由此有故彼有。彼無故，彼緣識亦無；此若全滅，當知識亦隨滅。

Question: Which formations not existing means consciousness does not exist? Which formations ceasing means consciousness ceases? Answer: Formations already performed and already passed within one's own continuing stream, not yet met by any genuine antidote, together with mental formation giving rise to bodily and verbal formation in turn — it is by these existing that consciousness, conditioned by them, exists. Where these do not exist, consciousness conditioned by them does not exist either; and where they are entirely extinguished, consciousness, it should be understood, ceases along with them.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes the three grades of ignorance by depth: the ignorance that initiates a deed is the general delusion about karmic result and ultimate truth that first sets an unwholesome or ordinary wholesome act in motion; manifest ignorance is that same delusion actually operating in a given moment of mind; and dormant ignorance is its unmanifest seed, still lodged in the mind-stream even when not currently active. He cites the treatise's own later fourfold portrait of how deeply a person is caught by delusion — some carry only the dormant seed without present disturbance; some are actively disturbed by it yet still feel shame and hold back from acting; some act on it but still feel remorse afterward; and only the last, acting without any shame or remorse at all, truly deserves to be called "a deluded person" outright — with genuine severance always working from the deepest layer outward: sever the dormant seed first, and manifest ignorance and its initiating power fall away as an automatic consequence, never the reverse.

On formations and consciousness, he explains the treatise is being precise about what "ceasing" actually requires: a deed already performed has already perfumed its seed into the mind-stream, and that seed continues producing further seeds of its own kind (seed generating seed) until an actual antidote — genuine liberating insight, not mere good intentions — interrupts it; only then does the chain from formations to consciousness genuinely stop, rather than merely pausing between one act and the next. He notes this same seed-versus-manifestation logic — a "cause" link's own seed must itself be extinguished, not just its current manifestation suppressed — is then extended without further elaboration through consciousness's conditioning of name-and-form, the six bases, contact, and feeling, since, as the treatise itself notes, the underlying causal pattern is structurally identical at every step.

25. Three Topics Close in Sequence: Cessation Finishes, the Twelve Links Located Among Eight Gateways, and Five Faults Against Five Benefits

Three shorter topics close in quick succession: the non-existence-and-cessation analysis finishes by extending its method through the remaining links by simple analogy; the twelve links are located precisely within the eight gateways of dependent origination named back in fascicle 9 — only three of the eight actually apply, and only one of those three completely; and the treatise names five specific faults that follow from never truly understanding dependent origination, mirrored by five benefits for the one who does.

Chinese Source

如前所說八緣起門，幾門是十二支緣起所顯，幾門非耶？答：三門是彼所顯，謂二一分所顯，一全分所顯；餘門非。何等為二一分所顯？謂內識生門、自業所作門。何等為一全分所顯？謂有情世間轉門。

Ru-Yi Translation

Of the eight gateways of dependent origination named earlier, how many are displayed by the twelve links, and how many are not? Answer: Three are so displayed — two only in part, one in full; the rest are not. Which two are displayed only in part? The gateway of an inward consciousness arising, and the gateway of one's own accumulated karma's dominant power. Which one is displayed in full? The gateway of a sentient being's death and birth.

問：不如實知緣起道理者，有幾種過患耶？答：有五。謂起我見，及能發起前際俱行見。如前際俱行見，如是後際俱行見、前後際俱行見亦爾。又於彼見猛利堅執，有取有怖，於現法中不般涅槃，是名第五過患。問：如實知者，有幾種勝利耶？答：翻前五過，應知勝利亦有五種。

Question: How many faults follow for one who does not truly know the nature of dependent origination? Answer: Five. Self-view arises; a view bound up with the past arises together with it; a view bound up with the future arises the same way; and a view bound up with both past and future together arises the same way; and fiercely, firmly clinging to these views — grasping them, and dreading their loss — one fails to reach nirvāṇa in this very life: this is the fifth fault. Question: How many benefits follow for one who does truly know it? Answer: The reverse of these same five faults — five benefits should be understood accordingly.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the cessation topic closes by simple analogy rather than fresh explanation — craving's cessation stopping grasping, and grasping's stopping becoming, work the same way affliction generally causes and ceases causing defilement (fascicle 8's own ninefold analysis already established this); becoming's cessation stopping birth works like any karmic cause ceasing to produce its result; and birth's cessation stopping old-age-and-death works like any supporting basis being removed. On locating the twelve links among the eight gateways, he explains the mismatch is deliberate: the gateway of consciousness arising captures only the identity link, and the gateway of karma's dominant power captures only formations — both real but partial slices of the full chain — while the gateway of a being's death and birth is simply the twelve-link chain itself, named twice under two different headings. The remaining five gateways (a crop ripening, a world-system's formation, the four foods, meditative mastery, and purification) describe real processes, but none of them is specifically about a single being's own birth-and-death cycle, so none of them is "displayed" by the twelve links at all.

On the five faults, he reads them as a tight causal cascade rather than five independent problems: failing to understand dependent origination leaves self-view standing, and self-view alone generates the sixty-two wrong views catalogued back in fascicles 6 and 7 whenever it's projected onto the past, the future, or both at once — eternalism, annihilationism, or some variant nihilism, depending on which direction the projection runs. The fifth and worst fault is not a new view but an attitude toward the first four: fierce, fear-driven attachment to whichever view one holds, which by itself is enough to permanently bar nirvāṇa, since a mind gripping tightly to any fixed position about self and time has no room left to see dependent origination's own actual, groundless flow. The five benefits, he notes, are simply this entire cascade running in reverse — genuine understanding leaves no self-view standing, so none of the downstream views or the fear-driven clinging that blocks liberation ever arise at all.

26. Fourteen More Gateways: Real or Nominal, Cause or Result, Which Feeling, Which Suffering

The treatise's ninth and final sub-topic within its systematic distinguishing of conditions runs the twelve links through fourteen further compact classifications in rapid succession — genuinely real existence versus a mere composite label, a single component versus several combined, the one link that specifically obscures ultimate truth, which links generate suffering versus which simply are suffering, cause-portion versus result-portion versus both at once, which links pair with which feeling, and which links belong to which of the three classical kinds of suffering.

Chinese Source

是十二支緣起，幾支是實有？謂九。幾支非實有？謂餘。幾一事為自性？謂五。幾非一事為自性？謂餘。幾是所知障因？謂一。幾能生苦？謂五。幾苦胎藏？謂五。幾唯是苦？謂二。

Ru-Yi Translation

Of these twelve links, how many genuinely exist in their own right? Nine. How many do not, existing only as composite labels? The rest — becoming, birth, and old-age-and-death. How many consist of a single thing? Five — ignorance, consciousness, contact, feeling, and craving. How many consist of more than one thing combined? The rest. How many are the specific cause of the obstruction to omniscience? One — ignorance. How many actually generate suffering? Five — ignorance, formations, craving, grasping, and becoming. How many are suffering still hidden in embryo, as unripened seed? Five — consciousness through feeling. How many are simply suffering itself, already manifest? Two — birth and old-age-and-death.

幾說為因分？謂前六，無明乃至觸，及愛、取、有三，說為因分。幾說為果分？謂後二說為果分。幾說為雜因果分？謂所餘支說為雜分，謂後法以觸為緣因受，謂現法與愛為緣果受。復次，幾支能生愛非愛境界果？幾支能生自體果？謂前六支能生前果，後三支能生後果，一支俱生二果。

How many count as the cause-portion? Nine — ignorance through contact, together with craving, grasping, and becoming. How many count as the result-portion? Two — birth and old-age-and-death. How many count as a mixed cause-and-result portion? The one remaining link, feeling — which is, as a seed, the cause conditioning a future life, and, as present experience, the result conditioned by craving. Further: how many links generate a desirable or undesirable environmental result? How many generate the very embodiment itself? The first six links generate the former result, the last three generate the latter, and the one remaining link generates both at once.

復次，幾支樂受俱行？謂除二，所餘支。幾支苦受俱行？謂即彼及所除中一。幾支不苦不樂受俱行？謂如樂受道理應知。幾支不與受俱行？謂所除中一。復次，幾支壞苦攝？謂樂受俱行支及非受俱行支一分。幾支苦苦攝？謂苦受俱行支及非受俱行支一分。

Further: how many links occur together with pleasant feeling? All but two — feeling itself, and old-age-and-death. How many occur together with painful feeling? Those same ten, together with old-age-and-death — eleven in all. How many occur together with neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling? The same as for pleasant feeling. How many never occur together with feeling at all? The one remaining — feeling itself. Further: how many links belong to the suffering of change? The links occurring with pleasant feeling, together with a portion of feeling itself. How many belong to the suffering of pain? The links occurring with painful feeling, together with a further portion of feeling itself.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this cluster of classifications as a final, compact review exam covering ground the fascicle has already built up in detail: becoming, birth, and old-age-and-death are "not genuinely real" because each is really just a composite label for several of the other nine links viewed at a different stage — becoming is simply the six moistened links (formations through feeling) now ready to ripen, and birth and old-age-and-death are simply those same five links (consciousness through feeling) actually manifesting; ignorance alone specifically obstructs realization of ultimate truth, distinguishing it from craving and grasping, which obstruct liberation more generally but are not themselves singled out as the obstruction to omniscience the way ignorance is.

On feeling's peculiar double status, he notes it alone counts as "mixed cause and result" because, as a dormant seed, it is itself among the causes conditioning a future life, while, as actually experienced in the present, it is itself the result of craving's own trigger — cause and result both, depending on which moment is in view; and it alone is excluded from every one of the feeling-correlation categories on the simple logical ground that a thing cannot meaningfully be said to "occur together with itself." He treats the suffering-of-change and suffering-of-pain classifications as directly recalling fascicle 9's own earlier "boil" metaphor for the three feelings — pleasant feeling itself already carries the suffering of its own inevitable ending, and painful feeling is suffering plainly as such — with the third, subtlest kind, the suffering of formations, still to come.

27. Five More Gateways: Formations' Suffering, What Meditation Removes, and Which Realm Each Link Belongs To

The remaining gateways turn from feeling to subtler questions: how the suffering of formations underlies every link; how deep meditative states like the non-perception heaven or cessation attainment leave only some links present; how a higher meditative state can temporarily suppress a lower one, though never permanently; which links are purely defiled versus capable of wholesome or neutral expression; and which links belong to which of the three realms.

Chinese Source

幾支行苦攝？謂所有壞苦、苦苦支，亦是行苦支。或有行苦所攝，非餘二苦，謂不苦不樂受俱行支及非受俱行支一分。於一切生處及三摩鉢底中，皆有一切支現行可得耶？答：不可得。謂無想天中，及滅盡定、無想定中，有色支可得，非無色支。若生無色界，無色支可得，非有色支。

Ru-Yi Translation

How many links belong to the suffering of formations? Every link belonging to the suffering of change or the suffering of pain also belongs to the suffering of formations; and there is, further, a portion belonging to the suffering of formations alone, apart from those other two kinds of suffering — the links occurring with neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling, together with a further portion of feeling itself. Are all twelve links present, manifest, in every place of birth and every meditative attainment? Answer: They are not. In the non-perception heaven, in cessation attainment, and in the attainment without perception, only the physical links are present, not the formless ones. Reborn in the formless realm, only the formless links are present, not the physical ones.

頗有依支得離支耶？答：有。謂依上地支離下地支。此但一分，非全；唯暫時，非究竟。幾支染汙？幾支不染汙？答：三染，餘通二種。若不染汙，善及無覆無記別故，分為二種應知。幾支欲界繫？答：一切支，和合等起故。

Is there a case where resting on one link, another link can be left behind? Answer: There is — resting on a higher meditative level's formations, a lower level's formations can be left behind. But this applies to only a portion, formations alone, never the whole chain; and only temporarily, while absorbed in that meditative state, never as a final, permanent removal. How many links are purely defiled, and how many are not? Answer: Three are purely defiled; the rest can be either. When not defiled, they divide further into wholesome and morally neutral. How many links belong to the desire realm? Answer: All twelve, since they arise there together as a single unified whole.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains the suffering of formations as the subtlest and most pervasive of the three sufferings, present in every link since even neutral feeling — free of both the suffering of pain and the suffering of change — still carries the bare fact of being conditioned and impermanent, exactly as fascicle 9's own "boil" metaphor first established. On which links appear in which meditative state, he notes the non-perception heaven and cessation attainment suppress mental activity so completely that only the physical, bodily links remain detectable, while the formless realm, having no body at all, retains only the mental links — confirming that the twelve-link chain's full expression requires both body and active mind together, and either alone leaves an incomplete picture.

On suppressing a lower link from a higher meditative level, he stresses the treatise's careful qualification: this works only for formations, and only for as long as the absorption lasts — a meditator generating the sense of a "higher, subtler" state to override a "lower, coarser" one is genuinely suppressing that lower state's active manifestation, but its seed remains fully intact, ready to reassert itself the moment concentration is lost or that same being is later reborn in the lower realm again. He treats this as a standing caution against mistaking meditative suppression for genuine liberation — a point already made at length through the sixteen non-Buddhist doctrines in fascicles 6 and 7 and Udraka Rāmaputra's fall in fascicle 9. Only ignorance, craving, and grasping are purely defiled by nature; every other link can appear in a wholesome, unwholesome, or neutral form depending on the deed. And because the desire realm alone contains every possible combination of these — the full range of destinies, bodies, and mental states — all twelve links are said to belong to it without exception, unlike the form and formless realms above, where several links necessarily drop away.

28. The Final Gateways: Training-Level, and How Much Each Noble Fruit Actually Cuts — 緣起分別緣 Closes Entirely

The last gateways close out the fourteen-part review, and with it the entire massive eighth topic (distinguishing conditions link by link) opened back near the start of this fascicle: no link belongs purely to a trainee or to one beyond training — only an ordinary being carries the full chain — and the four noble fruits are measured precisely by how much of it each has actually cut: a stream-enterer or once-returner only a portion, a non-returner the desire realm entirely, and an arhat all three realms without exception.

Chinese Source

幾支是學？答：無。幾支是無學？答：亦無。幾支是非學非無學？答：一切。所有善有漏支，彼何故非學耶？答：墮流轉故。若學所有善有漏法，彼與流轉相違故，及用明為緣故，非支。

Ru-Yi Translation

How many links belong to one still in training? None. How many belong to one beyond training? None, either. How many belong to neither — an ordinary being? All twelve. Why is even an ordinary being's wholesome, contaminated conduct not counted as belonging to a trainee? Because it still falls within transmigration. A trainee's own wholesome, contaminated conduct runs contrary to transmigration, and is conditioned by genuine insight rather than ignorance — so it, too, does not belong among the twelve links at all.

預流果當言幾支已斷耶？答：一切一分，無全斷者。如預流果，如是一來果亦爾。不還果當言幾支已斷耶？答：欲界一切，色無色界不定。阿羅漢當言幾支已斷耶？答：三界一切。

Of a stream-enterer, how many links should be said to be already severed? A portion of every link, but none entirely. Just as with a stream-enterer, so too with a once-returner. Of a non-returner, how many links should be said to be already severed? All belonging to the desire realm; those of the form and formless realms remain undetermined. Of an arhat, how many links should be said to be already severed? All, throughout all three realms, without exception.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains why not even a single link belongs "purely" to a trainee: the twelve links, by definition, describe defiled transmigration, and a trainee's wholesome effort — cultivated with genuine insight, oriented against the very current the chain describes — simply does not fit the category, even though a trainee has certainly not yet eliminated every trace of affliction. Only an ordinary being, whose conduct remains caught within transmigration's own current, instantiates the full twelve links without qualification.

On the four fruits, he traces a precise progression: the stream-enterer has cut the links leading to the three unfortunate destinies (severed by the single moment of direct insight into the truths) but still carries up to seven more human-and-heavenly rebirths' worth of the remaining links, so no link is fully gone, only partially thinned; the once-returner has additionally weakened six grades of desire-realm affliction through gradual cultivation, but the picture is otherwise the same; the non-returner has exhausted every desire-realm affliction entirely, guaranteeing no further rebirth there, while the form and formless realms remain genuinely open — some non-returners progress upward through the form realm's own levels before finally attaining arhatship at its summit, others rise as far as the formless peak itself before doing so, so no single fixed answer covers every case; and only the arhat has severed the chain completely across all three realms, the destination the entire fascicle's analysis of dependent origination has been building toward from its very first section. With the four fruits measured this precisely, the treatise's ninth and final sub-topic (諸門分別, its fourteen miscellaneous gateways) is complete — and with it, the entire eighth topic of dependent origination's nine-part outline, distinguishing conditions link by link (緣起分別緣), first opened near the start of this very fascicle, is now closed in its entirety.

29. The Ninth and Final Topic: Six Ways the Sūtras Themselves Teach Dependent Origination

The treatise's ninth and final topic surveys how the sūtras actually present dependent origination in practice — six recurring patterns, from reciting the full forward chain to reciting only its reverse, from naming every link to naming just one, and from tracing how suffering accumulates to tracing how it is dismantled — illustrated by the Buddha's own account, quoted at length, of realizing the chain both backward and forward beneath the awakening tree.

Chinese Source

復次，於彼彼經中，由幾種言說道理說緣起耶？謂略說由六種言說道理。一、由順次第說，二、由逆次第說，三、由一分支說，四、由具分支說，五、由黑品說，六、由白品說。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, across the various sūtras, by how many kinds of exposition is dependent origination taught? Briefly, by six. First, taught in forward sequence. Second, taught in reverse sequence. Third, taught through a single link. Fourth, taught through every link in full. Fifth, taught as the darkened portion, tracing how suffering accumulates. Sixth, taught as the bright portion, tracing how it is dismantled.

誰有故老死有？此老死誰為緣？作是念已便起現觀，生有故老死有，此老死生為緣。……我齊此識心應轉還。復作是念：何法無故無此老、病、死？何法滅故老、病、死滅？即正思惟，起如實無間等，無生則無老、病、死，生滅故，則老、病、死滅，乃至無明滅故行滅，行滅故識滅……生滅故老、病、死、憂、悲、惱、苦滅，如是如是純大苦聚滅。

"Because what does old-age-and-death exist? What is old-age-and-death's condition?" Having reflected thus, direct realization arose: because birth exists, old-age-and-death exists; birth is old-age-and-death's condition. ... "It is at consciousness that my mind should turn back." He further reflected: "Absent what does this old age, sickness, and death not exist? What ceasing causes old age, sickness, and death to cease?" Reflecting rightly, direct, unbroken realization arose: absent birth, there is no old age, sickness, or death; birth ceasing, old age, sickness, and death cease — down to: ignorance ceasing, formations cease; formations ceasing, consciousness ceases ... birth ceasing, old age, sickness, death, sorrow, grief, distress, and suffering cease — and thus this whole great mass of suffering ceases.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps the six patterns directly onto material already covered: forward and reverse sequence are simply the standard chain and its mirror image, both already given in full; teaching through a single link points back to the eight gateways surveyed earlier, where the gateway of consciousness arising or of karma's dominant power each isolate just one link to make a specific point; teaching through every link in full is the standard twelve-link recitation as a complete unit; and the darkened and bright portions are simply the forward, accumulating chain and the reverse, dismantling chain, now given their own names.

He treats the lengthy quotation — the Buddha's own account of tracing the chain backward from old-age-and-death all the way to consciousness, where the regress stops, and then forward again from ignorance's cessation to the cessation of the whole "great mass of suffering" — as the sūtras' own record of exactly how the Buddha discovered this teaching in the first place, beneath the awakening tree: not received from another teacher, but reasoned out through this same backward-then-forward method the treatise has now spent an entire fascicle unpacking link by link. He notes this quotation functions as the whole topic's own proof text — everything the treatise has methodically distinguished (essence, gateway, meaning, differences, sequence, etymology, conditioning-nature, and the fine-grained distinguishing of conditions) is here shown, in the Buddha's own words, compressed into a single continuous act of realization.

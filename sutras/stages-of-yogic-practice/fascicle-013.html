Fascicle 13 · 瑜伽師地論卷第十三 · The Ground of Meditative Equipoise Closes

From Solitude to Realization, and Across Four New Grounds

Fascicle 13 finishes the Ground of Meditative Equipoise — drawing on the Buddha's own sayings about solitude, calm, and insight — and then crosses, in a single scroll, into four further grounds: the Ground of Non-Equipoise, the Ground With-Mind, the Ground Without-Mind, and the opening of the Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 13 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. "Delight in Solitude": Bodily Seclusion, Mental Calm, and the Path They Open

Still within the Ground of Meditative Equipoise, the treatise now clarifies its meaning through the Buddha's own sayings. The first: "delight in solitude, cultivate contemplation, abide in right calm" — read as a graded path in which two kinds of seclusion lead on to insight and true realization.

Chinese Source

復次，如世尊言：汝等苾芻，當樂空閑，勤修觀行，內心安住正奢摩他者。謂能遠離臥具貪著，或處空閑，或坐樹下，繫念現前，乃至廣說，名樂空閑。當知此言顯身遠離。若能於內九種住心，如是名為內心安住正奢摩他。當知此言顯心遠離。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the World-Honored One said: "Monks, delight in solitude, diligently cultivate contemplation, and inwardly abide in right calm." "Delight in solitude" means being able to leave behind attachment to lodging and bedding, dwelling in an open place or sitting beneath a tree, with mindfulness present before one, and so on at length — this is called delighting in solitude. Know that this phrase shows bodily seclusion. "Inwardly abiding in right calm" means being able to settle the mind within by the nine ways of settling the mind. Know that this phrase shows mental seclusion.

Chinese Source

若樂處空閑，便能引發內心安住正奢摩他。若內心安住正奢摩他，便能引發毗鉢舍那。若於毗鉢舍那善修習已，即能引發於諸法中如實覺了。

Ru-Yi Translation

If one delights in dwelling in solitude, one can then draw forth the inward abiding in right calm. If one inwardly abides in right calm, one can then draw forth insight. If one has well cultivated insight, one can then draw forth a true and real realization of things as they are among all phenomena.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes that fascicle 13 opens still inside the Ground of Meditative Equipoise, now "incidentally clarifying" its meaning by drawing on the Buddha's own words. The first saying — "delight in solitude, cultivate contemplation, abide in right calm" — he reads as a graded path built on two kinds of seclusion. "Delighting in solitude" is bodily seclusion: leaving attachment to comfortable lodging and bedding, dwelling in the open or under a tree with mindfulness set before one. "Inwardly abiding in right calm" is mental seclusion: settling the mind through the nine stages of settling met earlier, from first drawing it inward up to genuine equipoise.

He stresses that the two chain forward into a whole path. Bodily solitude draws forth inner calm (śamatha); inner calm draws forth insight (vipaśyanā); and well-cultivated insight draws forth the true realization of all phenomena as they actually are. So the Buddha's brief exhortation, on Cháng Bǎi's reading, lays out the entire arc — seclusion, calm, insight, and liberating knowledge — in a single line, and shows that the outward act of withdrawing to a quiet place is not incidental but the first link of the chain that ends in realization.

2. Cultivating Concentration: The Immeasurable, the Constant-and-Thorough, and Right Mindfulness

A second saying: "toward concentration, cultivate the immeasurable, the constant-and-thorough, abiding in right mindfulness." The treatise reads the three as the marks of genuine cultivation, flanking two kinds of completeness — worldly and supramundane — and, on a second reading, as the paths of calm and insight.

Chinese Source

復次，如世尊言：汝等苾芻，於三摩地當勤修習，無量、常委、安住正念者。謂先總標於三摩地勤修習已，後以三事別顯修相。無量者，謂四無量。常委者，謂常有所作，及委悉所作，故名常委。安住正念者，顯於四念住，安住其心。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the World-Honored One said: "Monks, toward concentration diligently cultivate the immeasurable, the constant-and-thorough, abiding in right mindfulness." Having first generally enjoined diligent cultivation of concentration, [the Buddha] then displays the marks of cultivation by three things. "The immeasurable" means the four immeasurables. "The constant-and-thorough" means constantly having something to do, and doing it thoroughly and completely — hence "constant-and-thorough." "Abiding in right mindfulness" shows settling the mind in the four foundations of mindfulness.

Chinese Source

何故說此三種修相？謂依二種圓滿故：一者、世間圓滿，二者、出世圓滿。修無量故，便能引發世間圓滿；修正念故，便能引發出世圓滿；常委修故，於此二種速得通達；由此因緣，處二中說。是故但說三種修相。又無量者，顯奢摩他道；住正念者，顯毗鉢舍那道。

Ru-Yi Translation

Why are these three marks of cultivation taught? Because of two kinds of completeness: worldly completeness and supramundane completeness. By cultivating the immeasurable, one draws forth worldly completeness; by cultivating right mindfulness, one draws forth supramundane completeness; and by the constant-and-thorough cultivation, one swiftly penetrates both — for which reason it is placed between the two. So only these three marks of cultivation are stated. Again, "the immeasurable" shows the path of calm; "abiding in right mindfulness" shows the path of insight.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the three terms as the marks of genuine cultivation. "The immeasurable" is the four immeasurables — loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity. "Constant-and-thorough" is practice that is both unbroken (constantly having something to do) and complete and careful (doing it thoroughly). "Abiding in right mindfulness" is settling the mind in the four foundations of mindfulness — body, feeling, mind, and phenomena.

He gives the treatise's rationale for exactly these three: the two outer terms flank two kinds of completeness. The immeasurables draw forth worldly completeness; right mindfulness draws forth supramundane completeness; and the constant-and-thorough cultivation, set between them, is what lets one swiftly penetrate both — which is why it is named in the middle. On a second reading, "the immeasurable" marks the path of calm (śamatha) and "abiding in right mindfulness" the path of insight (vipaśyanā) — so the Buddha's three-word instruction compresses the whole of calm-and-insight practice, worldly and world-transcending together, into a single formula.

3. Two Further Readings, and Why Calm Comes Before Insight

The treatise gives two more readings of the same three terms — first sorting them by worldly versus supramundane completeness, then by calm versus insight, then by the aim toward merit versus the aim toward nirvāṇa — and finally states the practical sequence: calm must be mastered before it can run jointly with insight.

Chinese Source

謂依二種圓滿故：一者、世間圓滿，二者、出世圓滿。修無量故，便能引發世間圓滿；修正念故，便能引發出世圓滿；常委修故，於此二種速得通達。又無量者，顯奢摩他道；住正念者，顯毗鉢舍那道；常委者，顯此二種速趣證道。又無量者，顯趣福德行；住正念者，顯趣涅槃行；常委者，顯趣二種速圓滿行。

Ru-Yi Translation

This is stated in dependence on two kinds of completeness: worldly completeness and supramundane completeness. By cultivating the immeasurable, one draws forth worldly completeness; by cultivating right mindfulness, one draws forth supramundane completeness; by constant-and-thorough cultivation, one swiftly penetrates both. Again, "the immeasurable" shows the path of calm; "abiding in right mindfulness" shows the path of insight; "constant-and-thorough" shows these two swiftly advancing toward realization. Again, "the immeasurable" shows conduct tending toward merit; "abiding in right mindfulness" shows conduct tending toward nirvāṇa; "constant-and-thorough" shows conduct swiftly perfecting both.

Chinese Source

先於奢摩他善修習已，後與毗鉢舍那方得俱行。修此二種三摩地故，如實覺了所知境界。

Ru-Yi Translation

Only after one has first thoroughly cultivated calm can it then run jointly with insight. By cultivating these two concentrations, one truly and really realizes the objects to be known.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through the treatise's three successive glosses on the same short formula. First: the four immeasurables draw forth worldly completeness (mastering the four dhyānas and four formless attainments), while right mindfulness — the four foundations, seeing body as impure, feeling as suffering, mind as impermanent, and phenomena as selfless — draws forth supramundane completeness, carrying a practitioner through the thirty-seven factors of awakening to the noble fruits; constant-and-thorough cultivation is what lets both be reached swiftly, which is why it sits between them in the Buddha's own phrasing. Second: the immeasurables are simply the path of calm (śamatha) and right mindfulness the path of insight (vipaśyanā), with constant-and-thorough cultivation naming the swiftness of both paths toward realization. Third: the immeasurables tend toward the accumulation of merit, right mindfulness toward nirvāṇa itself, and constant-and-thorough cultivation toward perfecting both quickly.

He closes on the treatise's own practical rule for sequencing calm and insight: calm must first be thoroughly cultivated on its own before it can run jointly with insight — one does not begin by trying to yoke the two together, but first stabilizes śamatha, and only then brings vipaśyanā alongside it, so that the two together yield a true, direct realization of whatever object is being examined.

4. Skill in Absorption vs. Skill in Attainment: Four Kinds of Practitioner

A third saying from the Buddha distinguishes practitioners by whether they are skilled in the "unifications" (concentrations like the three doors to liberation) or the "attainments" (like the eight masteries, ten totalities, and cessation) — or both, or neither — showing that mastering one branch of meditative practice does not automatically confer mastery of another.

Chinese Source

復次，如世尊言：修靜慮者，或有等持善巧，非等至善巧；廣說如經嗢柁南頌。云何等持善巧？謂於空等三三摩地得善巧故。云何非等至善巧？謂於勝處、遍處、滅盡等至不善巧故。云何等至善巧，非等持善巧？謂於十種遍處等至及無想等至，若入若出俱得善巧，非於三三摩地。云何俱善巧？謂於彼二俱善巧故。云何俱不善巧？謂於彼二俱不善巧故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the World-Honored One said: "Among those who cultivate dhyāna, some are skilled in the unifications but not skilled in the attainments," as the sūtra's summary verse explains at length. What is skill in the unifications? Being skilled in the three concentrations — emptiness and the rest. What is not being skilled in the attainments? Not being skilled in the masteries, the totalities, and the cessation-attainment. What is being skilled in the attainments but not in the unifications? Being skilled in entering and exiting the ten totality-attainments and the no-perception attainment, but not in the three concentrations. What is being skilled in both? Being skilled in both of these. What is being skilled in neither? Being skilled in neither of these.

Chinese Source

如是於先所說等持、等至中，隨其所應，當善建立。

Ru-Yi Translation

So too among all the unifications and attainments stated earlier, one should, as fitting, rightly establish this same fourfold analysis.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that "unification" (等持, samādhi) and "attainment" (等至, samāpatti) are both words for concentration, but with different scope: samādhi extends even to the threshold concentration and ordinary scattered-mind focus, while samāpatti refers specifically to a fully-entered root concentration. Here the treatise illustrates with the three doors to liberation (emptiness, wishlessness, signlessness) as an example of "unification," and the eight masteries, ten totalities, and cessation-attainment as examples of "attainment." A practitioner may be skilled in one and not the other: skilled in emptiness, wishlessness, and signlessness but never having mastered the totalities or cessation; or the reverse, fluent in entering and leaving the ten totalities and the no-perception attainment while unfamiliar with the three doors; or skilled in both; or in neither.

He stresses this is offered as one illustrative example, not an exhaustive list — the same fourfold pattern of "skilled/unskilled" applies across all the many unifications and attainments named earlier in fascicles 11 and 12, since mastering the terminology and entry-conditions of one concentration never automatically confers skill in another. The lesson, practically, is that a meditator's training is never monolithic: real competence must be built branch by branch across the whole range of concentrative techniques.

5. Even a Bodhisattva Can Enter Countless Concentrations Without Knowing Their Names

The treatise illustrates skill-in-attainment-but-not-in-unification with a striking case: some bodhisattvas can enter hundreds or thousands of concentrations yet not know their names, or even recognize which one they are in — a gap closed only by hearing the teaching from a buddha or a truly accomplished bodhisattva, or by reaching the ultimate stage oneself.

Chinese Source

謂如有一，善知能入隨一等至諸行狀相，亦能現入；而不善知此三摩地名句文身差別之相，亦不能知我已得入如是如是等持差別。有諸菩薩，雖能得入若百若千諸三摩地，而不了知彼三摩地名句文身，亦不能知我已得入如是如是等持差別。乃至未從諸佛所聞，及於已得第一究竟諸菩薩所而得聽聞，或自證得第一究竟。

Ru-Yi Translation

Someone may well know the practice, state, and mark by which he can enter some given attainment, and can actually enter it — yet not well know the differentiating marks of that concentration's name and terms, nor be able to know "I have now entered such-and-such a unification." There are bodhisattvas who, though able to enter a hundred or a thousand concentrations, do not understand those concentrations' names and terms, nor can they know "I have now entered such-and-such a unification" — until they have heard this from the buddhas, or from bodhisattvas who have reached the ultimate stage, or have themselves realized that ultimate stage.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as a vivid, almost humbling illustration of how meditative skill and conceptual knowledge can come apart entirely. Some bodhisattvas can genuinely enter hundreds or even thousands of distinct concentrations — real attainment, not imagination — yet have no idea what any of them are called, or which one they have just entered; they can do, but cannot name what they do. He recalls the story of Venerable Upagupta, who could not identify the many samādhis he entered and had to summon his own teacher for help — only to learn that his teacher likewise could not name the concentration his teacher had entered. The gap closes only through hearing the teaching directly from a buddha, from a bodhisattva who has reached "the first ultimate" (第一究竟) — Cháng Bǎi glosses this as the twelfth and highest of the bodhisattva stages, the fully ripened stage of the Cloud of Dharma ground, effectively adjoining buddhahood itself — or by reaching that ultimate stage oneself.

He draws the practical lesson forward: even profound meditative attainment is not self-interpreting. Knowing the names, distinctions, and terminology of the concentrations is itself a real skill requiring study and a genuine teacher, entirely separate from the skill of actually entering them — which is exactly why an accomplished but untaught practitioner may be unable to teach others, however deep his own absorption runs.

6. Abiding and Emerging: What Keeps a Concentration Stable, and the Three Reasons One Leaves It

The treatise now works through eleven technical aspects of concentration one by one, beginning with "abiding" (never losing the concentration once entered) and "emerging" — sorted into three causes: something one merely intends to do, something one must do, and something previously scheduled.

Chinese Source

云何為住？謂善取能入諸三摩地諸行狀相，善取彼故，隨其所欲能住於定，於三摩地無復退失。如是若住於定，若不退失，二俱名住。云何為出？謂如有一，於能入定諸行狀相不復思惟，於不定地分別體相所攝定地不同類法作意思惟，出三摩地；或隨所作因故，或定所作因故，或期所作因故，而出於定。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is abiding? Well grasping the practice, state, and mark by which one enters the various concentrations — by grasping these well, one is able to abide in concentration as one wishes, with no further retrogression from it. Both abiding in concentration and not retrogressing from it are alike called "abiding." What is emerging? When one no longer attends to the practice, state, and mark by which one enters concentration, and instead attends to some object belonging to the unconcentrated ground, not of the same kind as the object of concentration, one emerges from concentration — whether from a cause one merely intends to act on, a cause one must act on, or a cause previously scheduled.

Chinese Source

隨所作者，謂修治衣鉢等諸所作業。定所作者，謂飲食便利、承事師長等諸所作業。期所作者，謂如有一，先立期契，或許為他當有所作，或復為欲轉入餘定。由此因緣，出三摩地。

Ru-Yi Translation

"What one intends to do" means tasks like mending one's robe or bowl. "What one must do" means tasks like eating, relieving oneself, or attending one's teacher. "What was previously scheduled" means someone who has set a prior arrangement — having promised to do something for another, or wishing to move into a different concentration. By these causes, one emerges from concentration.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains "abiding" as the fruit of thoroughly grasping a concentration's practice, state, and mark: once grasped, one can rest in the concentration at will, with no danger of falling out of it unintentionally — both the moment of stable dwelling and the durability against future loss count as "abiding." "Emerging" happens the instant attention shifts away from the concentration's own object toward anything belonging to the ordinary, unconcentrated mind. He flags a genuine cross-school debate here: in this treatise's and the Cheng Weishi Lun's account, the five sense-consciousnesses can register a sound even while absorbed (a sudden, unelaborated "bare awareness"), but one only actually exits concentration when the sixth consciousness deliberately turns to investigate it — so a buddha, undistracted even by thunder, need never leave absorption at all. The Sarvāstivāda tradition, by contrast, held (citing Mahāmaudgalyāyana's own repeated instant in-and-out of the sphere of nothingness at a sudden clap of thunder) that hearing the sound itself already constitutes emerging.

The three causes for actually choosing to emerge are practical and everyday: what one merely intends to do — small maintenance tasks like mending a torn robe or bringing in laundry before rain, matters with no urgency, best set aside if possible so as not to break concentration needlessly; what one must do — bodily necessities like eating or relieving oneself, and duties like attending a teacher, which cannot be postponed; and what was previously scheduled — a resolution set in advance, such as intending to exit the moment it rains, having promised to teach at a set hour, or planning to move from one concentration directly into another.

7. Practice, State, and the Two Marks: The Full Vocabulary of Entering a Concentration

Three more of the eleven aspects: "practice" — the mode of contemplation, worldly or world-transcending, by which one enters a given concentration; "state" — the felt signs that arise just before entry, recognizable by oneself and one's teacher alike; and "mark" — the object one holds and the provisions that make holding it possible.

Chinese Source

何等為行？謂如所緣作種種行而入於定。謂麤行、靜行、病行、癰行、箭行、無常行等，若於彼彼三摩地中所有諸行。何等為狀？謂於諸定臨欲入時，便有此定相狀先起。由此狀故，彼自了知：我於如是如是相定，不久當入，或復正入。彼教授師由此狀故，亦了知彼不久當入如是如是相定。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "practice"? Taking up various modes of practice toward the object, and thereby entering concentration — coarse-practice, calm-practice, disease-practice, boil-practice, arrow-practice, impermanence-practice, and so on: whatever practices belong to each given concentration. What is "state"? When about to enter any concentration, the signs of that concentration arise first. By these signs, one knows for oneself: "in such-and-such a state, I shall soon enter, or am now entering." And by these same signs, one's teacher likewise knows that one will soon enter a concentration of such-and-such a state.

Chinese Source

何等為相？謂二種相：一、所緣相，二、因緣相。所緣相者，謂分別體。由緣此故，能入諸定。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "mark"? Two kinds of mark: the object-mark and the causal mark. The object-mark is the discriminated substance [projected by the mind]; by taking this as object, one is able to enter the various concentrations.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi sorts "practice" into two families already familiar from earlier fascicles: coarse-and-calm practice (the six-aspect worldly method of leaving desire, seeing a lower ground as coarse and a higher as calm) belongs to the worldly path of renunciation, while disease-practice, boil-practice, arrow-practice, and impermanence-practice — seeing the aggregates as a disease, an oozing wound, a lodged arrow, arising and perishing — belong to the world-transcending path that actually uproots affliction's seed rather than merely suppressing its manifestation. Each concentration has its own characteristic practice, and the same object can, depending on which practice is applied to it, serve either calm or insight — the two are not rigidly separate techniques but interchangeable uses of a single object.

"State" is the felt sign that precedes entry — he retells the classical anecdote of the Buddha noticing Ānanda's throat move as though about to speak, and so inviting him to answer a visitor's question first: everything about to happen casts a recognizable sign before it, and meditative attainment is no exception, whether a sudden sense of heaviness, smoothness, or dissolution announcing which absorption is imminent, legible to the meditator himself and to an attentive teacher alike. "Mark" resolves into the object-mark — the image the mind's own discrimination projects, such as a skeleton or a corpse conjured by the impurity contemplation, which becomes the actual object one enters through — and the causal mark (the concentration-provisions), to be explained next.

8. The Causal Mark: What Concentration Actually Depends On

The causal mark is fleshed out as the concentration-provisions: guidance rightly received, resolve and aversion to saṃsāra rightly cultivated, scattered-mind knowledge accurately applied, and freedom from external disturbance — human, non-human, sonic, or material.

Chinese Source

因緣相者，謂定資糧。由此因緣，能入諸定。謂隨順定教誡教授，積集諸定所行資糧，修俱行欲，厭患有心，於亂不亂審諦了知，及不為他之所逼惱。或人所作，或非人所作，或音聲所作，或功用所作。

Ru-Yi Translation

The causal mark is the provisions for concentration; by this cause, one is able to enter the various concentrations. It means: rightly receiving the instruction and admonition that accord with concentration; accumulating the provisions concentration requires; cultivating the aspiration that accompanies practice; wearying of continued existence; accurately discerning whether the mind is scattered or not; and not being disturbed by others — whether the disturbance is caused by humans, by non-humans, by sound, or by material need.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi lays out the causal mark's several strands. First, rightly receiving instruction — guarding the sense-doors and the like, together with the practice, state, and mark of entry just explained. Second, actually gathering the provisions concentration needs — even something as ordinary as cooking meals for fellow practitioners during a retreat counts, or simply rejoicing in another's ability to practice when one cannot oneself. Third, "the aspiration that accompanies practice" — a genuine, joyful wish for concentration's benefits, the will that leads action; and, inseparable from it, weariness of the afflictions, karma, and suffering of the three realms, since without real disenchantment the will to practice has no urgency behind it.

He then unpacks "accurately discerning scattered from unscattered": knowing clearly whether the ten distracting signs, the eight kinds of discursive thought, the five hindrances, or dullness and agitation are currently disturbing the mind, and equally knowing the signs of genuine one-pointedness — the six perceptions (signless, without function, non-discriminative, free of longing and agitation, quiescent, and the bliss of freedom from affliction) that settle a wandering mind. Finally, freedom from external disturbance: threats from hostile people or dangerous animals ("human or non-human" harm), the noise of a busy or restless dwelling-place ("sonic" disturbance), and the plain hardship of lacking food, clothing, or shelter ("material" disturbance) — a Dharma without sustenance is hard to sustain, he notes; the body's basic needs must be met before the mind can settle.

9. Well-Adjusted vs. Not: The Difference Between a Forced Concentration and a Free One

"Well-adjusted" concentration is defined by contrast with its opposite: a concentration still gripped by deliberate effort, like water held in a vessel, or still bound to the coarse-versus-calm framework of the worldly path, is not yet well-adjusted — only one released from both constraints truly is.

Chinese Source

云何調善？謂若三摩地猶為有行之所拘執，如水被持；或為法性之所拘執，不靜不妙，非安隱道，亦非證得心一趣性；此三摩地不名調善，不隨所樂安隱而住。與此相違，名為調善。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "well-adjusted"? If a concentration is still gripped by deliberate effort — like water held in a container — or is still gripped by [the worldly] dharma-nature, being neither quiescent nor subtle, not a secure path, and not yet the realization of the mind's single course, then this concentration is not called well-adjusted; it does not rest securely as one wishes. The opposite of this is called well-adjusted.

Chinese Source

云何猶為有行拘執？謂由誓願俱行思故，制伏外緣，持心於定。又於作意，要由功用方能運轉，不令內心於外流散。故作是說，如水被持。云何法性之所拘執？謂觀下地為麤法性，觀於上地為靜法性。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "still gripped by deliberate effort"? By the intention that accompanies one's vow, one subdues external conditions and holds the mind in concentration; and in applying attention, one must exert deliberate effort to keep it moving, so as not to let the mind scatter outward — hence the saying, "like water held [in a container]." What is "gripped by dharma-nature"? Contemplating a lower ground as coarse in nature, and a higher ground as calm in nature.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains "well-adjusted" negatively, by naming its two opposites. The first, "gripped by deliberate effort," is the concentration of a beginner who must actively vow, will, and strain to hold the mind on its object — exactly the "forced-running attention" met earlier, before threshold concentration is won; like water poured into a vessel, it holds its shape only because something is holding it, not because it flows there naturally. The second, "gripped by dharma-nature," is the worldly path's coarse-versus-calm method itself: even a highly refined worldly concentration, built by seeing a lower ground as coarse and a higher as calm, never actually escapes the structure of the three realms — climb as high as the peak of existence, and one is still bound by that very framework, since it only ever compares one conditioned ground to another, never breaking free of conditioned existence altogether.

By contrast, genuine "well-adjustedness" — Cháng Bǎi ties this back to the five noble knowledges' account of purity — means the targeted affliction is forever quiescent, there is no relish even for one's own ground, the path attained does not retrogress, and one has reached the ground beyond applied and sustained thought: a concentration that rests securely of its own accord, needing neither strained effort nor a hierarchy of grounds to sustain it, because its stability comes from uprooted affliction rather than held tension.

10. Range: Why a Lower Attainment Cannot See a Higher One

"Range" names the strict ceiling on what a given concentration can know: a practitioner in the first dhyāna cannot perceive the second, just as a person of lesser faculties, cultivation, or attainment cannot fathom the mind of one who has surpassed them — though the reverse always holds.

Chinese Source

云何所行？謂三摩地所行境界。由所得定，過此已上不能知故。如初靜慮，不能觀見第二靜慮。如是根度、數取趣度，亦不能知。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "range"? The domain within which a concentration operates. Whatever lies beyond the concentration one has attained cannot be known. Just as the first dhyāna cannot perceive the second, so too the range of faculties and the range of persons cannot be known [by those below them].

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains "range" as a strict ceiling: whatever a given concentration has not itself attained lies entirely outside its field of knowledge — a meditator resting in the first dhyāna simply cannot perceive or know the second dhyāna's inner state, any more than someone standing on a lower floor can see what happens on the floor above. He extends the same logic, following the Xianyang Shengjiao Lun, across three further "ranges" (度, degrees or boundaries) of comparison: the range of ground (a lower absorption cannot know a higher one's mind), the range of faculty (one of duller faith, effort, mindfulness, concentration, or wisdom cannot know the mind of one whose faculties are sharper), and the range of person (one who has severed fewer afflictions cannot know the mind of one who has severed more — a first-fruit noble one, for instance, cannot know a second-fruit noble one's mind).

He notes the asymmetry is total: while the higher can always know the lower — someone in the second dhyāna, sharper faculties, or a higher fruit fully perceives what lies beneath — the lower can never reach upward to know the higher, however hard it strains. This, Cháng Bǎi observes, is simply built into the structure of the path: each stage's attainment is also, unavoidably, the horizon of its own knowledge.

11. Eliciting: How Concentration Draws Forth Eloquence and Supreme Qualities

The eighth aspect, "eliciting," names concentration's power to compress vast teachings into few words and to bring forth the noble qualities — the analytic knowledges, the concentration of non-contention, and the rest — already surveyed in fascicle 11.

Chinese Source

云何引發？謂能略攝廣文句義，及能成辦諸勝功德。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "eliciting"? Being able to condense and encompass vast phrasing and meaning into brief compass, and being able to accomplish the various supreme qualities.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi cross-references the treatise's own earlier account of this same "eliciting" mark from fascicle 11: a concentration mature enough elicits the power to hear one word and grasp a thousand implications, compressing vast teachings into a single character or expanding a single principle into boundless explanation, and it draws forth the noble qualities built upon it — the knowledge that fulfills whatever it vows, the four unobstructed analytic knowledges of dharma, meaning, language, and eloquence, and the concentration of non-contention. He treats this brief restatement as confirming that eliciting belongs among concentration's eleven aspects precisely because it is concentration, not mere learning, that makes such eloquence and such qualities genuinely available to a practitioner.

12. Equal-Affection: The Wholesome Qualities That Actually Settle the Mind

"Equal-affection" names the cluster of wholesome qualities — conscience and shame, love and reverence, faith, sound thought, mindfulness, clear knowing, guarded faculties, guarded precepts, and freedom from regret — whose steady accumulation is what genuinely lets the mind settle into concentration.

Chinese Source

云何等愛？謂慚、愧、愛、敬、信、正思惟、正念、正知、根護、戒護，及無悔等樂為最後。由隨樂故，心便得定。與此相違，名不等愛。云何等愛亦不等愛？謂如有一，於慚愧等少分成就，少不成就。謂具慚愧而無愛敬，乃至廣說。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "equal-affection"? Conscience, shame, love, reverence, faith, sound thought, mindfulness, clear knowing, guarding the faculties, guarding the precepts, and, last, freedom from regret leading to ease. By following that ease, the mind settles into concentration. The opposite of this is called "not equal-affection." What is "equal-affection yet also not"? Someone who accomplishes some of the above — conscience and the rest — and not others: possessing conscience and shame, say, but lacking love and reverence, and so on at length.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi traces the chain this list actually describes: conscience and shame (restraining oneself from wrongdoing whether alone or in company) ground everything else; a genuine wish for the good qualities of concentration (love) and reverence for one's teachers (敬) follow; confidence that the guidance one is following actually works (信) supports sound thought grounded in what one has heard and reflected on; mindfulness settles the mind through the nine stages already met; clear knowing tells a right thought from a wrong one; guarding the six sense-faculties keeps them from chasing their objects, and guarding the precepts — the five, the eight, the monastic vows — protects body, speech, and mind directly. The final link is freedom from regret: because one's conduct has been kept clean, no remorse disturbs the mind, and that clear conscience naturally ripens into ease — and it is this settled ease, not effort, that actually lets concentration arise.

Its opposite, "not equal-affection," is simply the absence of this whole cluster — no conscience, no reverence, no guarded faculties, chronic regret, no ease, and so no concentration. And "equal-affection yet also not" names the ordinary, mixed case of a practitioner who has some of these qualities well established and others still lacking — conscience present, say, but reverence or guarded faculties still weak. Cháng Bǎi closes with the plain lesson this whole eleven-aspect survey has been building toward: concentration is entirely a matter of causes and conditions actually assembled, never obtained by wishing — cultivate the causes thoroughly, sincerely, and without interruption, the way a careful farmer tends his field, and the harvest follows in its own time; skip the cultivation, and there is simply nothing to harvest.

13. Increase, Decrease, and Method: The Last Three Aspects Named Briefly

The final three of the eleven aspects are given in a few words: increase is a concentration's continued growth, decrease its falling away, and method is simply calm and insight themselves — or, restated, the balancing of calming, uplifting, and equanimity already explained earlier.

Chinese Source

云何為增？謂所得定轉復增長。云何為減？謂所得定還復退失。云何方便？謂趣彼二道。又止、舉、捨，當知如前止等相中已具分別。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "increase"? The concentration attained continuing to grow further. What is "decrease"? The concentration attained falling away again. What is "method"? Advancing by those two paths [calm and insight]. And calming, uplifting, and equanimity — know these as already fully distinguished in the marks of calming and the rest explained earlier.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats these three briefly, since each has effectively already been explained. "Increase" is simply a concentration continuing to deepen and expand — the same mutual reinforcement of pliancy and one-pointedness met in fascicle 11's marks of the meditation-object. "Decrease" is its opposite, a concentration once attained slipping away again. "Method" resolves into calm and insight as the two paths by which any concentration actually advances, and, restated a second way, into the threefold toolkit of calming (when the mind is agitated), uplifting (when it sinks toward dullness), and equanimity (when neither extreme is present) — the very same three named among the thirty-two marks back in fascicle 11. With these, the eleven aspects that unpack "unification" and "attainment" — abiding, emerging, practice, state, mark, causal mark, well-adjustedness, range, eliciting, equal-affection, and increase-decrease-method — are complete.

14. Mistaking Advance for Decline: The Scripture on Distorted and Undistorted Practitioners

A new scriptural citation opens a rich practical topic: some meditators mistake genuine progress for regression, and regression for progress. The treatise first explains the general structure — four permutations of advancing and retreating, crossed with distorted and undistorted recognition — then works through the case of a practitioner moving from the first dhyāna toward the second who mistakes that very advance for a loss.

Chinese Source

復次，如分別靜慮經言：有靜慮者，即於興等謂之為衰，乃至廣說。此中四轉，當知二時顛倒。謂於三摩地若退墮時，若勝進時，趣退及退，俱名為衰；趣勝進道及與勝進，俱名為興。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the Sūtra Distinguishing Dhyāna says: "There are meditators who, at the very moment of flourishing, call it decline," and so on at length. Herein four permutations occur; know that both occasions [of flourishing and of decline] admit of distortion. When concentration either falls back or advances: heading toward decline and actual decline are both called "decline"; heading toward the path of advancement and actual advancement are both called "flourishing."

Chinese Source

云何應知於三摩地進時顛倒？彼謂我今退失離生喜樂，我今退失勝三摩地。謂靜慮者勤修習故，心趣寂靜，隨捨行故。從初靜慮入於第二靜慮近分，然於此事不善了知。於此位中，初靜慮地喜樂已過，第二靜慮地中所有喜樂猶未能得。便作是念：我今退失離生喜樂。遂還從彼退攝其心。當知如是修靜慮者其心顛倒。

Ru-Yi Translation

How should one recognize distortion at the moment of advancement in concentration? Such a one thinks: "I have now lost the joy-and-bliss born of seclusion; I have now lost my excellent concentration." For, through diligent cultivation, the meditator's mind moves toward quiescence, following the practice of equanimity: passing from the first dhyāna into the threshold of the second, yet not rightly understanding this — for at this stage, the first dhyāna's joy-and-bliss has already passed, and the second dhyāna's joy-and-bliss is not yet attained. So he thinks: "I have now lost the joy-and-bliss born of seclusion," and draws his mind back to that former state. Know that such a meditator's mind is distorted.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi introduces the four permutations plainly: whether a meditator is actually declining or actually advancing, each occasion can be recognized correctly or mistakenly, giving four cases — but the text takes them up in two batches, first the two distorted recognitions (mistaking advance for decline, and mistaking decline for advance), only later the two undistorted ones. "Heading toward decline" and "having already declined" are both grouped as "decline"; "heading toward advancement" and "having already advanced" are both grouped as "flourishing" — the trajectory counts the same as its destination.

He then walks through the classic case in detail: a practitioner who has attained the first dhyāna and diligently continues to practice finds his mind growing ever more quiescent, releasing the coarser applied-and-sustained thought and settling into equanimity — which is, in fact, the exact process of moving from the first dhyāna into the threshold state just before the second. But at that threshold, a very particular gap opens: the first dhyāna's joy-and-bliss has already faded, while the second dhyāna's joy-and-bliss has not yet arisen — a genuinely joyless, blissless interval that is nonetheless real progress. Not understanding this, the meditator concludes "I have lost my joy and bliss, I have lost my excellent concentration," and deliberately pulls his mind back to the first dhyāna's remembered state rather than pressing forward — mistaking the exact threshold of advancement for a fall, and so genuinely falling, out of a misreading of what should have been recognized as progress.

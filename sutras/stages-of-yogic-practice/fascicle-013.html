Fascicle 13 · 瑜伽師地論卷第十三 · The Ground of Meditative Equipoise Closes

From Solitude to Realization, and Across Four New Grounds

Fascicle 13 finishes the Ground of Meditative Equipoise — drawing on the Buddha's own sayings about solitude, calm, and insight — and then crosses, in a single scroll, into four further grounds: the Ground of Non-Equipoise, the Ground With-Mind, the Ground Without-Mind, and the opening of the Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 13 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. "Delight in Solitude": Bodily Seclusion, Mental Calm, and the Path They Open

Still within the Ground of Meditative Equipoise, the treatise now clarifies its meaning through the Buddha's own sayings. The first: "delight in solitude, cultivate contemplation, abide in right calm" — read as a graded path in which two kinds of seclusion lead on to insight and true realization.

Chinese Source

復次，如世尊言：汝等苾芻，當樂空閑，勤修觀行，內心安住正奢摩他者。謂能遠離臥具貪著，或處空閑，或坐樹下，繫念現前，乃至廣說，名樂空閑。當知此言顯身遠離。若能於內九種住心，如是名為內心安住正奢摩他。當知此言顯心遠離。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the World-Honored One said: "Monks, delight in solitude, diligently cultivate contemplation, and inwardly abide in right calm." "Delight in solitude" means being able to leave behind attachment to lodging and bedding, dwelling in an open place or sitting beneath a tree, with mindfulness present before one, and so on at length — this is called delighting in solitude. Know that this phrase shows bodily seclusion. "Inwardly abiding in right calm" means being able to settle the mind within by the nine ways of settling the mind. Know that this phrase shows mental seclusion.

Chinese Source

若樂處空閑，便能引發內心安住正奢摩他。若內心安住正奢摩他，便能引發毗鉢舍那。若於毗鉢舍那善修習已，即能引發於諸法中如實覺了。

Ru-Yi Translation

If one delights in dwelling in solitude, one can then draw forth the inward abiding in right calm. If one inwardly abides in right calm, one can then draw forth insight. If one has well cultivated insight, one can then draw forth a true and real realization of things as they are among all phenomena.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes that fascicle 13 opens still inside the Ground of Meditative Equipoise, now "incidentally clarifying" its meaning by drawing on the Buddha's own words. The first saying — "delight in solitude, cultivate contemplation, abide in right calm" — he reads as a graded path built on two kinds of seclusion. "Delighting in solitude" is bodily seclusion: leaving attachment to comfortable lodging and bedding, dwelling in the open or under a tree with mindfulness set before one. "Inwardly abiding in right calm" is mental seclusion: settling the mind through the nine stages of settling met earlier, from first drawing it inward up to genuine equipoise.

He stresses that the two chain forward into a whole path. Bodily solitude draws forth inner calm (śamatha); inner calm draws forth insight (vipaśyanā); and well-cultivated insight draws forth the true realization of all phenomena as they actually are. So the Buddha's brief exhortation, on Cháng Bǎi's reading, lays out the entire arc — seclusion, calm, insight, and liberating knowledge — in a single line, and shows that the outward act of withdrawing to a quiet place is not incidental but the first link of the chain that ends in realization.

2. Cultivating Concentration: The Immeasurable, the Constant-and-Thorough, and Right Mindfulness

A second saying: "toward concentration, cultivate the immeasurable, the constant-and-thorough, abiding in right mindfulness." The treatise reads the three as the marks of genuine cultivation, flanking two kinds of completeness — worldly and supramundane — and, on a second reading, as the paths of calm and insight.

Chinese Source

復次，如世尊言：汝等苾芻，於三摩地當勤修習，無量、常委、安住正念者。謂先總標於三摩地勤修習已，後以三事別顯修相。無量者，謂四無量。常委者，謂常有所作，及委悉所作，故名常委。安住正念者，顯於四念住，安住其心。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the World-Honored One said: "Monks, toward concentration diligently cultivate the immeasurable, the constant-and-thorough, abiding in right mindfulness." Having first generally enjoined diligent cultivation of concentration, [the Buddha] then displays the marks of cultivation by three things. "The immeasurable" means the four immeasurables. "The constant-and-thorough" means constantly having something to do, and doing it thoroughly and completely — hence "constant-and-thorough." "Abiding in right mindfulness" shows settling the mind in the four foundations of mindfulness.

Chinese Source

何故說此三種修相？謂依二種圓滿故：一者、世間圓滿，二者、出世圓滿。修無量故，便能引發世間圓滿；修正念故，便能引發出世圓滿；常委修故，於此二種速得通達；由此因緣，處二中說。是故但說三種修相。又無量者，顯奢摩他道；住正念者，顯毗鉢舍那道。

Ru-Yi Translation

Why are these three marks of cultivation taught? Because of two kinds of completeness: worldly completeness and supramundane completeness. By cultivating the immeasurable, one draws forth worldly completeness; by cultivating right mindfulness, one draws forth supramundane completeness; and by the constant-and-thorough cultivation, one swiftly penetrates both — for which reason it is placed between the two. So only these three marks of cultivation are stated. Again, "the immeasurable" shows the path of calm; "abiding in right mindfulness" shows the path of insight.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the three terms as the marks of genuine cultivation. "The immeasurable" is the four immeasurables — loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity. "Constant-and-thorough" is practice that is both unbroken (constantly having something to do) and complete and careful (doing it thoroughly). "Abiding in right mindfulness" is settling the mind in the four foundations of mindfulness — body, feeling, mind, and phenomena.

He gives the treatise's rationale for exactly these three: the two outer terms flank two kinds of completeness. The immeasurables draw forth worldly completeness; right mindfulness draws forth supramundane completeness; and the constant-and-thorough cultivation, set between them, is what lets one swiftly penetrate both — which is why it is named in the middle. On a second reading, "the immeasurable" marks the path of calm (śamatha) and "abiding in right mindfulness" the path of insight (vipaśyanā) — so the Buddha's three-word instruction compresses the whole of calm-and-insight practice, worldly and world-transcending together, into a single formula.

3. Two Further Readings, and Why Calm Comes Before Insight

The treatise gives two more readings of the same three terms — first sorting them by worldly versus supramundane completeness, then by calm versus insight, then by the aim toward merit versus the aim toward nirvāṇa — and finally states the practical sequence: calm must be mastered before it can run jointly with insight.

Chinese Source

謂依二種圓滿故：一者、世間圓滿，二者、出世圓滿。修無量故，便能引發世間圓滿；修正念故，便能引發出世圓滿；常委修故，於此二種速得通達。又無量者，顯奢摩他道；住正念者，顯毗鉢舍那道；常委者，顯此二種速趣證道。又無量者，顯趣福德行；住正念者，顯趣涅槃行；常委者，顯趣二種速圓滿行。

Ru-Yi Translation

This is stated in dependence on two kinds of completeness: worldly completeness and supramundane completeness. By cultivating the immeasurable, one draws forth worldly completeness; by cultivating right mindfulness, one draws forth supramundane completeness; by constant-and-thorough cultivation, one swiftly penetrates both. Again, "the immeasurable" shows the path of calm; "abiding in right mindfulness" shows the path of insight; "constant-and-thorough" shows these two swiftly advancing toward realization. Again, "the immeasurable" shows conduct tending toward merit; "abiding in right mindfulness" shows conduct tending toward nirvāṇa; "constant-and-thorough" shows conduct swiftly perfecting both.

Chinese Source

先於奢摩他善修習已，後與毗鉢舍那方得俱行。修此二種三摩地故，如實覺了所知境界。

Ru-Yi Translation

Only after one has first thoroughly cultivated calm can it then run jointly with insight. By cultivating these two concentrations, one truly and really realizes the objects to be known.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through the treatise's three successive glosses on the same short formula. First: the four immeasurables draw forth worldly completeness (mastering the four dhyānas and four formless attainments), while right mindfulness — the four foundations, seeing body as impure, feeling as suffering, mind as impermanent, and phenomena as selfless — draws forth supramundane completeness, carrying a practitioner through the thirty-seven factors of awakening to the noble fruits; constant-and-thorough cultivation is what lets both be reached swiftly, which is why it sits between them in the Buddha's own phrasing. Second: the immeasurables are simply the path of calm (śamatha) and right mindfulness the path of insight (vipaśyanā), with constant-and-thorough cultivation naming the swiftness of both paths toward realization. Third: the immeasurables tend toward the accumulation of merit, right mindfulness toward nirvāṇa itself, and constant-and-thorough cultivation toward perfecting both quickly.

He closes on the treatise's own practical rule for sequencing calm and insight: calm must first be thoroughly cultivated on its own before it can run jointly with insight — one does not begin by trying to yoke the two together, but first stabilizes śamatha, and only then brings vipaśyanā alongside it, so that the two together yield a true, direct realization of whatever object is being examined.

4. Skill in Absorption vs. Skill in Attainment: Four Kinds of Practitioner

A third saying from the Buddha distinguishes practitioners by whether they are skilled in the "unifications" (concentrations like the three doors to liberation) or the "attainments" (like the eight masteries, ten totalities, and cessation) — or both, or neither — showing that mastering one branch of meditative practice does not automatically confer mastery of another.

Chinese Source

復次，如世尊言：修靜慮者，或有等持善巧，非等至善巧；廣說如經嗢柁南頌。云何等持善巧？謂於空等三三摩地得善巧故。云何非等至善巧？謂於勝處、遍處、滅盡等至不善巧故。云何等至善巧，非等持善巧？謂於十種遍處等至及無想等至，若入若出俱得善巧，非於三三摩地。云何俱善巧？謂於彼二俱善巧故。云何俱不善巧？謂於彼二俱不善巧故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the World-Honored One said: "Among those who cultivate dhyāna, some are skilled in the unifications but not skilled in the attainments," as the sūtra's summary verse explains at length. What is skill in the unifications? Being skilled in the three concentrations — emptiness and the rest. What is not being skilled in the attainments? Not being skilled in the masteries, the totalities, and the cessation-attainment. What is being skilled in the attainments but not in the unifications? Being skilled in entering and exiting the ten totality-attainments and the no-perception attainment, but not in the three concentrations. What is being skilled in both? Being skilled in both of these. What is being skilled in neither? Being skilled in neither of these.

Chinese Source

如是於先所說等持、等至中，隨其所應，當善建立。

Ru-Yi Translation

So too among all the unifications and attainments stated earlier, one should, as fitting, rightly establish this same fourfold analysis.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that "unification" (等持, samādhi) and "attainment" (等至, samāpatti) are both words for concentration, but with different scope: samādhi extends even to the threshold concentration and ordinary scattered-mind focus, while samāpatti refers specifically to a fully-entered root concentration. Here the treatise illustrates with the three doors to liberation (emptiness, wishlessness, signlessness) as an example of "unification," and the eight masteries, ten totalities, and cessation-attainment as examples of "attainment." A practitioner may be skilled in one and not the other: skilled in emptiness, wishlessness, and signlessness but never having mastered the totalities or cessation; or the reverse, fluent in entering and leaving the ten totalities and the no-perception attainment while unfamiliar with the three doors; or skilled in both; or in neither.

He stresses this is offered as one illustrative example, not an exhaustive list — the same fourfold pattern of "skilled/unskilled" applies across all the many unifications and attainments named earlier in fascicles 11 and 12, since mastering the terminology and entry-conditions of one concentration never automatically confers skill in another. The lesson, practically, is that a meditator's training is never monolithic: real competence must be built branch by branch across the whole range of concentrative techniques.

5. Even a Bodhisattva Can Enter Countless Concentrations Without Knowing Their Names

The treatise illustrates skill-in-attainment-but-not-in-unification with a striking case: some bodhisattvas can enter hundreds or thousands of concentrations yet not know their names, or even recognize which one they are in — a gap closed only by hearing the teaching from a buddha or a truly accomplished bodhisattva, or by reaching the ultimate stage oneself.

Chinese Source

謂如有一，善知能入隨一等至諸行狀相，亦能現入；而不善知此三摩地名句文身差別之相，亦不能知我已得入如是如是等持差別。有諸菩薩，雖能得入若百若千諸三摩地，而不了知彼三摩地名句文身，亦不能知我已得入如是如是等持差別。乃至未從諸佛所聞，及於已得第一究竟諸菩薩所而得聽聞，或自證得第一究竟。

Ru-Yi Translation

Someone may well know the practice, state, and mark by which he can enter some given attainment, and can actually enter it — yet not well know the differentiating marks of that concentration's name and terms, nor be able to know "I have now entered such-and-such a unification." There are bodhisattvas who, though able to enter a hundred or a thousand concentrations, do not understand those concentrations' names and terms, nor can they know "I have now entered such-and-such a unification" — until they have heard this from the buddhas, or from bodhisattvas who have reached the ultimate stage, or have themselves realized that ultimate stage.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as a vivid, almost humbling illustration of how meditative skill and conceptual knowledge can come apart entirely. Some bodhisattvas can genuinely enter hundreds or even thousands of distinct concentrations — real attainment, not imagination — yet have no idea what any of them are called, or which one they have just entered; they can do, but cannot name what they do. He recalls the story of Venerable Upagupta, who could not identify the many samādhis he entered and had to summon his own teacher for help — only to learn that his teacher likewise could not name the concentration his teacher had entered. The gap closes only through hearing the teaching directly from a buddha, from a bodhisattva who has reached "the first ultimate" (第一究竟) — Cháng Bǎi glosses this as the twelfth and highest of the bodhisattva stages, the fully ripened stage of the Cloud of Dharma ground, effectively adjoining buddhahood itself — or by reaching that ultimate stage oneself.

He draws the practical lesson forward: even profound meditative attainment is not self-interpreting. Knowing the names, distinctions, and terminology of the concentrations is itself a real skill requiring study and a genuine teacher, entirely separate from the skill of actually entering them — which is exactly why an accomplished but untaught practitioner may be unable to teach others, however deep his own absorption runs.

6. Abiding and Emerging: What Keeps a Concentration Stable, and the Three Reasons One Leaves It

The treatise now works through eleven technical aspects of concentration one by one, beginning with "abiding" (never losing the concentration once entered) and "emerging" — sorted into three causes: something one merely intends to do, something one must do, and something previously scheduled.

Chinese Source

云何為住？謂善取能入諸三摩地諸行狀相，善取彼故，隨其所欲能住於定，於三摩地無復退失。如是若住於定，若不退失，二俱名住。云何為出？謂如有一，於能入定諸行狀相不復思惟，於不定地分別體相所攝定地不同類法作意思惟，出三摩地；或隨所作因故，或定所作因故，或期所作因故，而出於定。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is abiding? Well grasping the practice, state, and mark by which one enters the various concentrations — by grasping these well, one is able to abide in concentration as one wishes, with no further retrogression from it. Both abiding in concentration and not retrogressing from it are alike called "abiding." What is emerging? When one no longer attends to the practice, state, and mark by which one enters concentration, and instead attends to some object belonging to the unconcentrated ground, not of the same kind as the object of concentration, one emerges from concentration — whether from a cause one merely intends to act on, a cause one must act on, or a cause previously scheduled.

Chinese Source

隨所作者，謂修治衣鉢等諸所作業。定所作者，謂飲食便利、承事師長等諸所作業。期所作者，謂如有一，先立期契，或許為他當有所作，或復為欲轉入餘定。由此因緣，出三摩地。

Ru-Yi Translation

"What one intends to do" means tasks like mending one's robe or bowl. "What one must do" means tasks like eating, relieving oneself, or attending one's teacher. "What was previously scheduled" means someone who has set a prior arrangement — having promised to do something for another, or wishing to move into a different concentration. By these causes, one emerges from concentration.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains "abiding" as the fruit of thoroughly grasping a concentration's practice, state, and mark: once grasped, one can rest in the concentration at will, with no danger of falling out of it unintentionally — both the moment of stable dwelling and the durability against future loss count as "abiding." "Emerging" happens the instant attention shifts away from the concentration's own object toward anything belonging to the ordinary, unconcentrated mind. He flags a genuine cross-school debate here: in this treatise's and the Cheng Weishi Lun's account, the five sense-consciousnesses can register a sound even while absorbed (a sudden, unelaborated "bare awareness"), but one only actually exits concentration when the sixth consciousness deliberately turns to investigate it — so a buddha, undistracted even by thunder, need never leave absorption at all. The Sarvāstivāda tradition, by contrast, held (citing Mahāmaudgalyāyana's own repeated instant in-and-out of the sphere of nothingness at a sudden clap of thunder) that hearing the sound itself already constitutes emerging.

The three causes for actually choosing to emerge are practical and everyday: what one merely intends to do — small maintenance tasks like mending a torn robe or bringing in laundry before rain, matters with no urgency, best set aside if possible so as not to break concentration needlessly; what one must do — bodily necessities like eating or relieving oneself, and duties like attending a teacher, which cannot be postponed; and what was previously scheduled — a resolution set in advance, such as intending to exit the moment it rains, having promised to teach at a set hour, or planning to move from one concentration directly into another.

7. Practice, State, and the Two Marks: The Full Vocabulary of Entering a Concentration

Three more of the eleven aspects: "practice" — the mode of contemplation, worldly or world-transcending, by which one enters a given concentration; "state" — the felt signs that arise just before entry, recognizable by oneself and one's teacher alike; and "mark" — the object one holds and the provisions that make holding it possible.

Chinese Source

何等為行？謂如所緣作種種行而入於定。謂麤行、靜行、病行、癰行、箭行、無常行等，若於彼彼三摩地中所有諸行。何等為狀？謂於諸定臨欲入時，便有此定相狀先起。由此狀故，彼自了知：我於如是如是相定，不久當入，或復正入。彼教授師由此狀故，亦了知彼不久當入如是如是相定。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "practice"? Taking up various modes of practice toward the object, and thereby entering concentration — coarse-practice, calm-practice, disease-practice, boil-practice, arrow-practice, impermanence-practice, and so on: whatever practices belong to each given concentration. What is "state"? When about to enter any concentration, the signs of that concentration arise first. By these signs, one knows for oneself: "in such-and-such a state, I shall soon enter, or am now entering." And by these same signs, one's teacher likewise knows that one will soon enter a concentration of such-and-such a state.

Chinese Source

何等為相？謂二種相：一、所緣相，二、因緣相。所緣相者，謂分別體。由緣此故，能入諸定。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "mark"? Two kinds of mark: the object-mark and the causal mark. The object-mark is the discriminated substance [projected by the mind]; by taking this as object, one is able to enter the various concentrations.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi sorts "practice" into two families already familiar from earlier fascicles: coarse-and-calm practice (the six-aspect worldly method of leaving desire, seeing a lower ground as coarse and a higher as calm) belongs to the worldly path of renunciation, while disease-practice, boil-practice, arrow-practice, and impermanence-practice — seeing the aggregates as a disease, an oozing wound, a lodged arrow, arising and perishing — belong to the world-transcending path that actually uproots affliction's seed rather than merely suppressing its manifestation. Each concentration has its own characteristic practice, and the same object can, depending on which practice is applied to it, serve either calm or insight — the two are not rigidly separate techniques but interchangeable uses of a single object.

"State" is the felt sign that precedes entry — he retells the classical anecdote of the Buddha noticing Ānanda's throat move as though about to speak, and so inviting him to answer a visitor's question first: everything about to happen casts a recognizable sign before it, and meditative attainment is no exception, whether a sudden sense of heaviness, smoothness, or dissolution announcing which absorption is imminent, legible to the meditator himself and to an attentive teacher alike. "Mark" resolves into the object-mark — the image the mind's own discrimination projects, such as a skeleton or a corpse conjured by the impurity contemplation, which becomes the actual object one enters through — and the causal mark (the concentration-provisions), to be explained next.

8. The Causal Mark: What Concentration Actually Depends On

The causal mark is fleshed out as the concentration-provisions: guidance rightly received, resolve and aversion to saṃsāra rightly cultivated, scattered-mind knowledge accurately applied, and freedom from external disturbance — human, non-human, sonic, or material.

Chinese Source

因緣相者，謂定資糧。由此因緣，能入諸定。謂隨順定教誡教授，積集諸定所行資糧，修俱行欲，厭患有心，於亂不亂審諦了知，及不為他之所逼惱。或人所作，或非人所作，或音聲所作，或功用所作。

Ru-Yi Translation

The causal mark is the provisions for concentration; by this cause, one is able to enter the various concentrations. It means: rightly receiving the instruction and admonition that accord with concentration; accumulating the provisions concentration requires; cultivating the aspiration that accompanies practice; wearying of continued existence; accurately discerning whether the mind is scattered or not; and not being disturbed by others — whether the disturbance is caused by humans, by non-humans, by sound, or by material need.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi lays out the causal mark's several strands. First, rightly receiving instruction — guarding the sense-doors and the like, together with the practice, state, and mark of entry just explained. Second, actually gathering the provisions concentration needs — even something as ordinary as cooking meals for fellow practitioners during a retreat counts, or simply rejoicing in another's ability to practice when one cannot oneself. Third, "the aspiration that accompanies practice" — a genuine, joyful wish for concentration's benefits, the will that leads action; and, inseparable from it, weariness of the afflictions, karma, and suffering of the three realms, since without real disenchantment the will to practice has no urgency behind it.

He then unpacks "accurately discerning scattered from unscattered": knowing clearly whether the ten distracting signs, the eight kinds of discursive thought, the five hindrances, or dullness and agitation are currently disturbing the mind, and equally knowing the signs of genuine one-pointedness — the six perceptions (signless, without function, non-discriminative, free of longing and agitation, quiescent, and the bliss of freedom from affliction) that settle a wandering mind. Finally, freedom from external disturbance: threats from hostile people or dangerous animals ("human or non-human" harm), the noise of a busy or restless dwelling-place ("sonic" disturbance), and the plain hardship of lacking food, clothing, or shelter ("material" disturbance) — a Dharma without sustenance is hard to sustain, he notes; the body's basic needs must be met before the mind can settle.

9. Well-Adjusted vs. Not: The Difference Between a Forced Concentration and a Free One

"Well-adjusted" concentration is defined by contrast with its opposite: a concentration still gripped by deliberate effort, like water held in a vessel, or still bound to the coarse-versus-calm framework of the worldly path, is not yet well-adjusted — only one released from both constraints truly is.

Chinese Source

云何調善？謂若三摩地猶為有行之所拘執，如水被持；或為法性之所拘執，不靜不妙，非安隱道，亦非證得心一趣性；此三摩地不名調善，不隨所樂安隱而住。與此相違，名為調善。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "well-adjusted"? If a concentration is still gripped by deliberate effort — like water held in a container — or is still gripped by [the worldly] dharma-nature, being neither quiescent nor subtle, not a secure path, and not yet the realization of the mind's single course, then this concentration is not called well-adjusted; it does not rest securely as one wishes. The opposite of this is called well-adjusted.

Chinese Source

云何猶為有行拘執？謂由誓願俱行思故，制伏外緣，持心於定。又於作意，要由功用方能運轉，不令內心於外流散。故作是說，如水被持。云何法性之所拘執？謂觀下地為麤法性，觀於上地為靜法性。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "still gripped by deliberate effort"? By the intention that accompanies one's vow, one subdues external conditions and holds the mind in concentration; and in applying attention, one must exert deliberate effort to keep it moving, so as not to let the mind scatter outward — hence the saying, "like water held [in a container]." What is "gripped by dharma-nature"? Contemplating a lower ground as coarse in nature, and a higher ground as calm in nature.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains "well-adjusted" negatively, by naming its two opposites. The first, "gripped by deliberate effort," is the concentration of a beginner who must actively vow, will, and strain to hold the mind on its object — exactly the "forced-running attention" met earlier, before threshold concentration is won; like water poured into a vessel, it holds its shape only because something is holding it, not because it flows there naturally. The second, "gripped by dharma-nature," is the worldly path's coarse-versus-calm method itself: even a highly refined worldly concentration, built by seeing a lower ground as coarse and a higher as calm, never actually escapes the structure of the three realms — climb as high as the peak of existence, and one is still bound by that very framework, since it only ever compares one conditioned ground to another, never breaking free of conditioned existence altogether.

By contrast, genuine "well-adjustedness" — Cháng Bǎi ties this back to the five noble knowledges' account of purity — means the targeted affliction is forever quiescent, there is no relish even for one's own ground, the path attained does not retrogress, and one has reached the ground beyond applied and sustained thought: a concentration that rests securely of its own accord, needing neither strained effort nor a hierarchy of grounds to sustain it, because its stability comes from uprooted affliction rather than held tension.

10. Range: Why a Lower Attainment Cannot See a Higher One

"Range" names the strict ceiling on what a given concentration can know: a practitioner in the first dhyāna cannot perceive the second, just as a person of lesser faculties, cultivation, or attainment cannot fathom the mind of one who has surpassed them — though the reverse always holds.

Chinese Source

云何所行？謂三摩地所行境界。由所得定，過此已上不能知故。如初靜慮，不能觀見第二靜慮。如是根度、數取趣度，亦不能知。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "range"? The domain within which a concentration operates. Whatever lies beyond the concentration one has attained cannot be known. Just as the first dhyāna cannot perceive the second, so too the range of faculties and the range of persons cannot be known [by those below them].

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains "range" as a strict ceiling: whatever a given concentration has not itself attained lies entirely outside its field of knowledge — a meditator resting in the first dhyāna simply cannot perceive or know the second dhyāna's inner state, any more than someone standing on a lower floor can see what happens on the floor above. He extends the same logic, following the Xianyang Shengjiao Lun, across three further "ranges" (度, degrees or boundaries) of comparison: the range of ground (a lower absorption cannot know a higher one's mind), the range of faculty (one of duller faith, effort, mindfulness, concentration, or wisdom cannot know the mind of one whose faculties are sharper), and the range of person (one who has severed fewer afflictions cannot know the mind of one who has severed more — a first-fruit noble one, for instance, cannot know a second-fruit noble one's mind).

He notes the asymmetry is total: while the higher can always know the lower — someone in the second dhyāna, sharper faculties, or a higher fruit fully perceives what lies beneath — the lower can never reach upward to know the higher, however hard it strains. This, Cháng Bǎi observes, is simply built into the structure of the path: each stage's attainment is also, unavoidably, the horizon of its own knowledge.

11. Eliciting: How Concentration Draws Forth Eloquence and Supreme Qualities

The eighth aspect, "eliciting," names concentration's power to compress vast teachings into few words and to bring forth the noble qualities — the analytic knowledges, the concentration of non-contention, and the rest — already surveyed in fascicle 11.

Chinese Source

云何引發？謂能略攝廣文句義，及能成辦諸勝功德。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "eliciting"? Being able to condense and encompass vast phrasing and meaning into brief compass, and being able to accomplish the various supreme qualities.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi cross-references the treatise's own earlier account of this same "eliciting" mark from fascicle 11: a concentration mature enough elicits the power to hear one word and grasp a thousand implications, compressing vast teachings into a single character or expanding a single principle into boundless explanation, and it draws forth the noble qualities built upon it — the knowledge that fulfills whatever it vows, the four unobstructed analytic knowledges of dharma, meaning, language, and eloquence, and the concentration of non-contention. He treats this brief restatement as confirming that eliciting belongs among concentration's eleven aspects precisely because it is concentration, not mere learning, that makes such eloquence and such qualities genuinely available to a practitioner.

12. Equal-Affection: The Wholesome Qualities That Actually Settle the Mind

"Equal-affection" names the cluster of wholesome qualities — conscience and shame, love and reverence, faith, sound thought, mindfulness, clear knowing, guarded faculties, guarded precepts, and freedom from regret — whose steady accumulation is what genuinely lets the mind settle into concentration.

Chinese Source

云何等愛？謂慚、愧、愛、敬、信、正思惟、正念、正知、根護、戒護，及無悔等樂為最後。由隨樂故，心便得定。與此相違，名不等愛。云何等愛亦不等愛？謂如有一，於慚愧等少分成就，少不成就。謂具慚愧而無愛敬，乃至廣說。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "equal-affection"? Conscience, shame, love, reverence, faith, sound thought, mindfulness, clear knowing, guarding the faculties, guarding the precepts, and, last, freedom from regret leading to ease. By following that ease, the mind settles into concentration. The opposite of this is called "not equal-affection." What is "equal-affection yet also not"? Someone who accomplishes some of the above — conscience and the rest — and not others: possessing conscience and shame, say, but lacking love and reverence, and so on at length.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi traces the chain this list actually describes: conscience and shame (restraining oneself from wrongdoing whether alone or in company) ground everything else; a genuine wish for the good qualities of concentration (love) and reverence for one's teachers (敬) follow; confidence that the guidance one is following actually works (信) supports sound thought grounded in what one has heard and reflected on; mindfulness settles the mind through the nine stages already met; clear knowing tells a right thought from a wrong one; guarding the six sense-faculties keeps them from chasing their objects, and guarding the precepts — the five, the eight, the monastic vows — protects body, speech, and mind directly. The final link is freedom from regret: because one's conduct has been kept clean, no remorse disturbs the mind, and that clear conscience naturally ripens into ease — and it is this settled ease, not effort, that actually lets concentration arise.

Its opposite, "not equal-affection," is simply the absence of this whole cluster — no conscience, no reverence, no guarded faculties, chronic regret, no ease, and so no concentration. And "equal-affection yet also not" names the ordinary, mixed case of a practitioner who has some of these qualities well established and others still lacking — conscience present, say, but reverence or guarded faculties still weak. Cháng Bǎi closes with the plain lesson this whole eleven-aspect survey has been building toward: concentration is entirely a matter of causes and conditions actually assembled, never obtained by wishing — cultivate the causes thoroughly, sincerely, and without interruption, the way a careful farmer tends his field, and the harvest follows in its own time; skip the cultivation, and there is simply nothing to harvest.

13. Increase, Decrease, and Method: The Last Three Aspects Named Briefly

The final three of the eleven aspects are given in a few words: increase is a concentration's continued growth, decrease its falling away, and method is simply calm and insight themselves — or, restated, the balancing of calming, uplifting, and equanimity already explained earlier.

Chinese Source

云何為增？謂所得定轉復增長。云何為減？謂所得定還復退失。云何方便？謂趣彼二道。又止、舉、捨，當知如前止等相中已具分別。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "increase"? The concentration attained continuing to grow further. What is "decrease"? The concentration attained falling away again. What is "method"? Advancing by those two paths [calm and insight]. And calming, uplifting, and equanimity — know these as already fully distinguished in the marks of calming and the rest explained earlier.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats these three briefly, since each has effectively already been explained. "Increase" is simply a concentration continuing to deepen and expand — the same mutual reinforcement of pliancy and one-pointedness met in fascicle 11's marks of the meditation-object. "Decrease" is its opposite, a concentration once attained slipping away again. "Method" resolves into calm and insight as the two paths by which any concentration actually advances, and, restated a second way, into the threefold toolkit of calming (when the mind is agitated), uplifting (when it sinks toward dullness), and equanimity (when neither extreme is present) — the very same three named among the thirty-two marks back in fascicle 11. With these, the eleven aspects that unpack "unification" and "attainment" — abiding, emerging, practice, state, mark, causal mark, well-adjustedness, range, eliciting, equal-affection, and increase-decrease-method — are complete.

14. Mistaking Advance for Decline: The Scripture on Distorted and Undistorted Practitioners

A new scriptural citation opens a rich practical topic: some meditators mistake genuine progress for regression, and regression for progress. The treatise first explains the general structure — four permutations of advancing and retreating, crossed with distorted and undistorted recognition — then works through the case of a practitioner moving from the first dhyāna toward the second who mistakes that very advance for a loss.

Chinese Source

復次，如分別靜慮經言：有靜慮者，即於興等謂之為衰，乃至廣說。此中四轉，當知二時顛倒。謂於三摩地若退墮時，若勝進時，趣退及退，俱名為衰；趣勝進道及與勝進，俱名為興。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the Sūtra Distinguishing Dhyāna says: "There are meditators who, at the very moment of flourishing, call it decline," and so on at length. Herein four permutations occur; know that both occasions [of flourishing and of decline] admit of distortion. When concentration either falls back or advances: heading toward decline and actual decline are both called "decline"; heading toward the path of advancement and actual advancement are both called "flourishing."

Chinese Source

云何應知於三摩地進時顛倒？彼謂我今退失離生喜樂，我今退失勝三摩地。謂靜慮者勤修習故，心趣寂靜，隨捨行故。從初靜慮入於第二靜慮近分，然於此事不善了知。於此位中，初靜慮地喜樂已過，第二靜慮地中所有喜樂猶未能得。便作是念：我今退失離生喜樂。遂還從彼退攝其心。當知如是修靜慮者其心顛倒。

Ru-Yi Translation

How should one recognize distortion at the moment of advancement in concentration? Such a one thinks: "I have now lost the joy-and-bliss born of seclusion; I have now lost my excellent concentration." For, through diligent cultivation, the meditator's mind moves toward quiescence, following the practice of equanimity: passing from the first dhyāna into the threshold of the second, yet not rightly understanding this — for at this stage, the first dhyāna's joy-and-bliss has already passed, and the second dhyāna's joy-and-bliss is not yet attained. So he thinks: "I have now lost the joy-and-bliss born of seclusion," and draws his mind back to that former state. Know that such a meditator's mind is distorted.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi introduces the four permutations plainly: whether a meditator is actually declining or actually advancing, each occasion can be recognized correctly or mistakenly, giving four cases — but the text takes them up in two batches, first the two distorted recognitions (mistaking advance for decline, and mistaking decline for advance), only later the two undistorted ones. "Heading toward decline" and "having already declined" are both grouped as "decline"; "heading toward advancement" and "having already advanced" are both grouped as "flourishing" — the trajectory counts the same as its destination.

He then walks through the classic case in detail: a practitioner who has attained the first dhyāna and diligently continues to practice finds his mind growing ever more quiescent, releasing the coarser applied-and-sustained thought and settling into equanimity — which is, in fact, the exact process of moving from the first dhyāna into the threshold state just before the second. But at that threshold, a very particular gap opens: the first dhyāna's joy-and-bliss has already faded, while the second dhyāna's joy-and-bliss has not yet arisen — a genuinely joyless, blissless interval that is nonetheless real progress. Not understanding this, the meditator concludes "I have lost my joy and bliss, I have lost my excellent concentration," and deliberately pulls his mind back to the first dhyāna's remembered state rather than pressing forward — mistaking the exact threshold of advancement for a fall, and so genuinely falling, out of a misreading of what should have been recognized as progress.

15. Two Kinds of Undistorted Recognition: Knowing a Genuine Decline for What It Is

Against the distorted case just given, the treatise now describes two ways a practitioner rightly recognizes an actual decline — one from relishing a concentration too much to seek anything higher, one from letting pride or the wish for patronage corrode it — closing with a careful note on how the two differ in timing.

Chinese Source

謂如有一，得初靜慮，便生喜足，不求上進，唯起愛味。由起如是欲俱行想作意故，遂便退失近欲界定。彼於此衰能了是衰，由此因緣，當知無倒。又由所得靜慮定故，自譽毀他。謂我所得此靜慮定，非餘能得。由起如此欲俱行想作意故，所有蓋纏轉增轉厚，便從定退。彼於此衰能了是衰，由此因緣，當知無倒。第二無倒，翻初無倒，應知其相。此二無倒，亦於二時應知其相。由依如是倒無倒處，安立四轉。

Ru-Yi Translation

Someone who, having attained the first dhyāna, at once feels satisfied and seeks no further advancement, giving rise only to relish — through this craving-accompanied attention arising again and again, he falls back to a state near the desire-realm's own concentration. Recognizing this decline as decline, he is, for this reason, undistorted. Again, on account of the dhyāna attained, one praises oneself and disparages others, thinking "this dhyāna I have attained, others cannot attain" — through this craving-accompanied attention, the hindrances and their entanglements thicken and grow, and one falls from the concentration. Recognizing this decline as decline, one is likewise undistorted. The second undistorted case is the reverse of the first, and its character should be understood accordingly. Both undistorted cases, too, should be understood as occurring at their own proper occasions. By these instances of distortion and non-distortion, the four permutations are established.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi presents the two undistorted cases as a pair. The first belongs to a practitioner content with the first dhyāna alone, whose relish for it — never seeking anything higher — eventually pulls him back down near the desire realm's own concentration; but he knows perfectly well that this is a decline, so his recognition, unlike the earlier case, is accurate. The second belongs to a practitioner who has attained some dhyāna — not necessarily only the first — and then, boasting "I alone have attained this, others cannot," lets the hindrances thicken through pride and, in a related case the treatise adds, through hoping that displaying his attainment to kings and ministers will win their patronage; either way the concentration is lost, and again the practitioner knows he has lost it.

He notes the two are subtly different in timing, a point on which the commentarial tradition itself divides: the first case describes someone still only heading toward decline (he has not yet fully lost the concentration, merely stagnated in relish), while the second describes someone who has actually already fallen from a concentration he once held — pride and the wish for patronage having corroded what was genuinely there. Cháng Bǎi notes Miào Jìng read the passage differently from the Piánxúnjì commentary here, and flags this as a live point of scholarly disagreement rather than settling it — the substantive lesson standing regardless: a decline correctly recognized as a decline, whatever its precise cause, is undistorted; only mistaking advance for decline (or the reverse) counts as distortion.

16. The Four-Fold Check: Inferior, Superior, Excellent, and Supreme Concentration

A new scriptural citation gives a practical diagnostic every meditator can apply to his own practice: checking whether the concentration one holds is prone to falling away, merely stable, actively advancing, or opening directly onto liberating insight — the "inferior," "superior," "excellent," and "supreme" grades.

Chinese Source

復次，如分別四檢行定經中，由四種相，檢行一切三摩地等。謂此等持是順退分，乃至此是順決擇分。云何檢行？謂此是劣分、此是勝分、此殊勝分、此最勝分，如其次第。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the Sūtra Distinguishing the Four Ways of Checking Concentration says: by four marks, one checks all unifications and the rest — whether this concentration is retrogression-prone, and so on up to whether it is penetration-prone [toward the uncontaminated]. What is "checking"? That this is the inferior grade, this the superior grade, this the excellent grade, this the supreme grade, in that order.

Chinese Source

謂修定者，從初靜慮還退出已，於諸靜慮不復樂入，亦不思惟此行狀相。然欲俱行諸想作意數數現前，如先所說，從彼起已隨念愛味。當於爾時，修靜慮者應自檢行：我三摩地今成退劣。又修定者，從初靜慮還退出已，得聞隨順此定教法。謂初靜慮諸行狀相，殷勤懇到善取其相，令所得定堅住不忘。如是隨念順定法故，成順住分。當於爾時，應自檢行：我三摩地已成其勝，我三摩地已得安住，非退、非進、非趣決擇。

Ru-Yi Translation

A meditator who, having fallen back from the first dhyāna, no longer delights in entering any of the dhyānas, nor attends to their practice, state, and mark — while craving-accompanied thoughts arise again and again, and, as said before, relish follows upon emerging — such a one, at that time, should check himself: "my concentration has now become inferior and retrogression-prone." Again, a meditator who, having fallen back from the first dhyāna, hears the teaching that accords with that concentration — the first dhyāna's practice, state, and mark — and earnestly, thoroughly grasps it well, so that the concentration attained stands firm and is not forgotten, thereby recollecting the teaching that accords with concentration, becomes stability-prone. At that time, he should check himself: "my concentration has now become superior; my concentration now abides — neither retrogressing, nor advancing, nor heading toward penetration."

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi introduces this fourfold checking as directly mapping onto the four "shares" of concentration met back in fascicle 12 — retrogression-prone, stability-prone, advancement-prone, and penetration-prone — now recast as inferior, superior, excellent, and supreme grades a meditator can diagnose in his own practice at any given moment. The inferior grade describes someone who has fallen from the first dhyāna and, no longer even interested in re-entering any dhyāna, lets craving-tinged thoughts recur unchecked, relishing whatever remnant of concentration remains rather than working to restore it — such a person should honestly recognize: "my concentration is now inferior, prone to further decline."

The superior grade describes someone equally fallen from the first dhyāna, but who, rather than drifting, seeks out the actual teaching on how that dhyāna is entered — its practice, state, and object — and grasps it firmly enough that the regained concentration now holds steady, neither slipping further nor yet advancing beyond where it stands, nor heading toward the uncontaminated. Cháng Bǎi stresses the checking language itself: "should check himself" (應自檢行) names a discipline of honest, real-time self-assessment — a meditator who does not know which of the four grades his own practice currently occupies cannot correct it, so the four-part diagnostic is offered as a tool to be applied, not merely a taxonomy to be memorized.

17. Excellent and Supreme Concentration: Advancing to the Second Dhyāna, and Opening onto Uncontaminated Insight

The four-fold check completes with its top two grades: "excellent," genuine advancement into the second dhyāna, and "supreme," where hearing the teaching of the four noble truths turns the very same concentration toward the uncontaminated path of penetrating insight.

Chinese Source

又靜慮者，從初靜慮還退出已，得聞隨順第二靜慮教授之法；既得聞已，第二靜慮道俱行諸想作意數數現前。當於爾時，應自檢行：我三摩地已成殊勝，非退、非住，唯是勝進，非趣決擇。

Ru-Yi Translation

Again, a meditator who, having fallen back from the first dhyāna, hears the teaching that accords with the second dhyāna, and, having heard it, gives rise again and again to the thoughts and attention that accompany the path of the second dhyāna, should at that time check himself: "my concentration has now become excellent — neither retrogressing nor merely abiding, but genuinely advancing, though not yet heading toward penetration."

Chinese Source

又修定者，從初靜慮還退出已，聞苦諦等相相應教法；既得聞巳，苦諦等俱行諸想作意順決擇分數數現前。彼於爾時，當自檢行：我三摩地已成最勝，非退、非住，亦非勝進，然趣決擇。

Ru-Yi Translation

Again, a meditator who, having fallen back from the first dhyāna, hears the teaching corresponding to the mark of the truth of suffering and the rest, and, having heard it, gives rise again and again to the thoughts and attention that accompany the truth of suffering and the rest, conducing to penetration, should at that time check himself: "my concentration has now become supreme — neither retrogressing, nor merely abiding, nor simply advancing, but heading toward penetration."

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi completes the fourfold set. The excellent grade belongs to a meditator who, having fallen from the first dhyāna, hears the teaching specific to the second dhyāna's own practice and takes it up so that thoughts belonging to that higher path recur — genuine advancement past the point he originally lost, though still short of anything uncontaminated. The supreme grade is qualitatively different: hearing the teaching on the truth of suffering and its companions (origin, cessation, path) and taking it up so that insight into those very truths recurs, the meditator's concentration now heads toward penetration itself — the uncontaminated breakthrough that no mere advancement in worldly dhyāna, however high, can reach on its own.

He draws out the shape of the whole diagnostic: inferior and superior both concern a concentration that has fallen and either continues to decay or merely stabilizes at its former level; excellent concerns a concentration climbing higher within the worldly ladder of dhyānas; and supreme concerns the qualitative leap from worldly concentration to the path that actually severs affliction — reached not by climbing higher within the dhyānas at all, but by turning the very same concentrative stability toward the four noble truths. A meditator who can honestly locate his own practice among these four, Cháng Bǎi stresses, knows exactly what work remains — whether to first arrest a decline, consolidate a plateau, press further up the ladder, or turn decisively toward insight.

18. Not Taking Up Any Perception: How a Practitioner in Deep Concentration Relates to the Six Sense-Fields

A new scriptural passage addresses a puzzle: a monk absorbed in the higher dhyānas still has eyes, ears, and the rest — so how does he relate to what they perceive? The treatise answers through the first dhyāna as an example: such a monk neither clings to sense-objects as truly real nor falls into blank non-perception, but holds them only as objects of insight into suffering and impermanence.

Chinese Source

復次，如經言：有眼、有色，乃至有意、有法；而諸苾芻，於此諸法若實若有都不領受。尚不受想，何況無想。謂諸苾芻於初靜慮具足安住，由此因緣，厭壞眼色乃至意法。由厭壞故威勢映奪，遂於眼中無有眼想，然有其想；乃至於法無有法想，然有其想。云何有想？謂於眼等，作意思惟是苦、是集，或是病等，彼於諸法不受自相。如是乃至無所有處。此中正說無漏作意。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the sūtra says: "there is the eye, there is form" — and so on up through "there is mind, there is a mind-object" — yet monks do not take these dharmas as real or as existent; they do not even take up a [gross] perception of them, still less a total absence of perception. Monks who abide fully in the first dhyāna, by this very cause, weary of and break down [attachment to] the eye and form, up through mind and mind-objects. Through this wearying-and-breaking-down, its force overwhelms and displaces [the ordinary perception], so that in the eye there is no [ordinary] perception of an eye — yet there is still a perception; and so on up to: in a mind-object there is no [ordinary] perception of a mind-object — yet there is still a perception. What is this perception? Attending to the eye and the rest, contemplating them as suffering, as origin, or as disease, one does not take up the self-mark of these dharmas. So it continues up through the sphere of nothingness. This refers specifically to uncontaminated attention.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi frames this as resolving a real puzzle: a monk deep in absorption still possesses all six sense-faculties and their objects — so in what sense does he "not perceive" them, and is this the same as a corpse's blank insensibility? The answer, worked through the first dhyāna as the paradigm case (extending in principle up through the sphere of nothingness), is that such a monk's wearying contemplation of the sense-fields as impermanent, suffering, and diseased is so forceful that it displaces the ordinary, self-mark-grasping perception that clings to "eye" and "form" as solid realities — yet this is not blank non-perception either, since a subtler perception remains: the perception of the sense-field precisely as suffering, as arising from craving, as disease. He notes real commentarial disagreement on exactly how far this passage describes an already-accomplished uncontaminated attainment versus a still-developing one, but the substantive point holds either way: genuine meditative freedom from the senses is not insensibility but a transformed way of perceiving, in which the object is held only as an occasion for insight, never as something to be grasped as ultimately real.

19. Four Ways to Approach the Path: Calm-First, Insight-First, and Their Pairings With Dhyāna and Insight

A new scriptural topic distinguishes four kinds of practitioner by which comes first for them — settled concentration or clear intellectual insight into the four truths — and how each then completes the missing half. The first pairs prior dhyāna-attainment with newly-won insight, illustrating "heightened mind" giving rise to "heightened wisdom."

Chinese Source

復次，如經中說四種趣道。云何宴坐，於諸法中思惟簡擇？謂有苾芻，先已證得初靜慮等，而未見諦。由聽正法及多聞故，而能宴坐，依三摩地，於苦等諦發起現觀。如是行者，依增上心，修增上慧。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the sūtra speaks of four ways of approaching the path. What is it to sit in stillness, contemplating and discerning among all phenomena? There is a monk who has already attained the first dhyāna and the rest, but has not yet seen the truths. Through hearing the true teaching and through extensive learning, he is able to sit in stillness and, resting on his concentration, gives rise to direct realization of the truth of suffering and the rest. Such a practitioner cultivates heightened wisdom by resting on heightened mind.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi introduces the four approaches to the path as sorted by which of concentration and insight a practitioner already has, and which he must still develop — matching, in reverse order, a parallel scriptural list built around calm-first versus insight-first practice, and, in its third and fourth items, calm-and-insight held jointly, or a distracted mind first brought under control. This first approach belongs to a practitioner who has already mastered the four dhyānas but has not yet directly seen the truths; through extensive hearing of the genuine teaching, he sits in stillness and, resting on the concentration he already possesses, brings forth direct realization of suffering and its companion truths — heightened mind (already accomplished) giving rise to heightened wisdom (newly won). Cháng Bǎi notes "extensive learning" here is no mere accumulation of facts but learning matured into genuine understanding and lived practice, citing the classical eightfold purification of learning (reverence for teachers, subduing conceit, diligent retention, unwavering mindfulness, tireless explanation, freedom from self-praise, sound investigation, and practice in accordance with the teaching) and the Mahāyāna's still higher standard, in which one who has truly heard even a single verse can unfold its meaning inexhaustibly.

20. The Second Way: Heightened Wisdom Giving Rise to Heightened Mind

The mirror case: a monk who already knows the four truths through and through but has not yet attained even the first dhyāna. He sits in stillness contemplating the Dharma he already understands, and it is this contemplation itself that draws forth the concentration he still lacks — wisdom leading, mind following.

Chinese Source

又有苾芻，如實知苦乃至知道，而未證得初靜慮等。彼便宴坐，思惟諸法。如是行者，依增上慧，修增上心。

Ru-Yi Translation

Again, there is a monk who truly knows suffering, up through truly knowing the path, yet has not attained even the first dhyāna. He then sits in stillness, contemplating the Dharma. Such a practitioner cultivates heightened mind by resting on heightened wisdom.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi presents this second practitioner as the mirror image of the first: where the first had already mastered the dhyānas and lacked insight, this one has already mastered insight into the four truths through sustained reflection-wisdom, but has not yet attained even the first dhyāna's calm. He simply sits and contemplates the Dharma he already understands — and Cháng Bǎi notes, citing Kuiji, that such a practitioner, being strong in wisdom but weak in concentration, typically reaches the first fruit resting only on the threshold concentration; having reached it, he must then go on to actually cultivate the dhyānas themselves in order to suppress the desire realm's afflictions and press on toward arhatship. The pairing with the first way is exact and deliberate: "heightened mind giving rise to heightened wisdom" versus "heightened wisdom giving rise to heightened mind" — two mirror routes to the same destination, distinguished only by which of concentration and insight a given temperament develops first.

21. The Third and Fourth Ways: Joint Cultivation, and Insight Fueling Concentration Through Joy

The third way is the balanced practitioner who develops calm and insight together, neither leading; the fourth belongs to one who already has the first dhyāna and then, on hearing the teaching, wins direct realization — and finds that the very joy of that realization deepens his concentration further still.

Chinese Source

第三行者，名為俱得。奢摩他、毗鉢舍那雙雜轉故。第四行者，先已證得初靜慮等，未聽正法，未習多聞。後從大師或餘尊所，聞見諦法，或復得聞斷餘結法；由此得入真諦現觀，或復證得阿羅漢果。

Ru-Yi Translation

The third practitioner is called "one who attains both together" — calm and insight turning jointly and interwoven. The fourth practitioner has already attained the first dhyāna and the rest, but has not yet heard the true teaching or undertaken extensive learning. Later, from the Teacher or some other venerable one, he hears the teaching that reveals the truths, or hears the teaching on severing the remaining fetters; by this he enters direct realization of the truths, or even attains the fruit of arhatship.

Chinese Source

彼既證得出離所引大善喜悅，由能制伏諸掉舉心，復還宴坐。如是坐已，安心住於靜慮、等至。

Ru-Yi Translation

Having attained the great, wholesome joy drawn forth by release, and being thereby able to subdue every agitated thought, he again sits in stillness; sitting thus, he settles his mind, abiding in the dhyānas and the attainments.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi describes the third practitioner as genuinely balanced: calm and insight develop together, neither one running ahead of the other, so that "joint attainment" names the entire trajectory rather than a sequence of separate stages. The fourth practitioner starts from the first dhyāna already in hand but with no prior exposure to the teaching at all; hearing it — whether the truths themselves or the specific instructions for severing the subtler cultivation-path fetters — carries him directly into realization, potentially all the way to arhatship in a single life.

He dwells on the striking second half: this practitioner's realization produces such a great, wholesome joy that the joy itself becomes fuel, subduing the restless, agitated thoughts that would otherwise disturb concentration, and drawing him back to sit again — insight deepening concentration through the sheer delight of what has been seen. Cháng Bǎi recalls the classical story of a monk on the verge of suicide from despair at his failure to reach arhatship, who, knife raised to his own throat, suddenly recalled that he had already attained the first and second fruits — a genuinely rare and precious accomplishment — and the joy of that recollection turned his mind back to sustained reflection on the aggregates as suffering, empty, impermanent, and selfless, carrying him at last to arhatship. Joy remembered rightly, he stresses, is itself a genuine engine of practice, not merely its reward.

22. Summarizing the Four Ways by What They Draw Forth

The treatise closes this topic with a compact summary: the first way draws forth the path of seeing alone, the second and third draw forth the path of cultivation, and the fourth draws forth both — a genuinely comprehensive route capable of carrying a practitioner all the way to arhatship within a single life.

Chinese Source

最初趣道，引見道故；第二、第三，引修道故；第四趣道，為俱引故。

Ru-Yi Translation

The first way of approach draws forth the path of seeing; the second and third draw forth the path of cultivation; the fourth way of approach draws forth both together.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi draws out what each of the four practitioners actually accomplishes. The first — heightened mind giving rise to heightened wisdom — draws forth the path of seeing, typically reaching the first fruit, since concentration was already mastered and the breakthrough is purely one of insight. The second and third — heightened wisdom giving rise to heightened mind, and the two developed jointly — draw forth the path of cultivation, since these practitioners must still develop or deepen concentration itself after (or alongside) their insight in order to press toward the higher fruits, suppressing the desire realm's afflictions along the way.

The fourth draws forth both paths together: already possessing the first dhyāna, and newly gifted with the teaching, such a practitioner can move straight through seeing and cultivation both, potentially completing the entire path to arhatship, together with its accompanying superknowledges and noble qualities, within this very life. Cháng Bǎi treats the four together as a genuinely exhaustive typology — every combination of concentration-first, insight-first, both-together, and both-already-in-hand accounted for — rather than four arbitrary examples, which is why the treatise can summarize them so compactly.

23. The Four Kinds of Purity-Excellence: What "Precepts" Actually Means When Fully Kept

A new scriptural topic: four kinds of purity, each analyzed into "fullness" (having actually completed it) and "being possessed of it" (having it become second nature). The first — precepts — is defined negatively first: a monk who keeps the core rules but is careless about conduct and unafraid of small faults has not yet fully purified his precepts at all.

Chinese Source

復次，如經中說有四淨勝。為求清淨，此最為勝，故名淨勝。云何為淨？云何為勝？謂所得、所證、所引戒等，若圓滿、若攝受，是名為淨。發勤精進，未滿令滿，是名為勝。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the sūtra speaks of four excellences of purity: seeking purity, these are its most excellent forms, hence "excellences of purity." What is "purity"? What is "excellence"? The precepts and the rest that one has obtained, realized, and drawn forth — whether fully completed or become one's very possession — this is called "purity." Rousing diligent effort to complete what is not yet complete is called "excellence."

Chinese Source

云何尸羅圓滿攝受？謂若有一，雖住具戒，亦能守護別解脫律儀，而於軌則及所行中未能具足，未於小罪深見怖畏。此於尸羅，未名圓滿。若於一切皆悉滿足，乃名圓滿。如是名為尸羅圓滿。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the fullness and possession of precepts? Someone may abide in complete precepts, and even guard the individual-liberation moral code, yet not be complete in proper conduct and behavior, and not see with deep dread even minor faults — such a one's precepts are not yet called full. Only when one is complete in every respect is it called "full." This is what is meant by the fullness of precepts.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains the two-part structure that will run through all four purities: "purity" itself names having fully completed and made one's own possession the given quality — precepts, concentration, view, or liberation — while "excellence" names the diligent effort still required to complete whatever remains unfinished. He works through the negative case in detail: a monk who keeps the core prātimokṣa precepts (the two-hundred-fifty rules for monks, three-hundred-forty-eight for nuns) but is careless about the finer points of conduct — proper deportment in walking, standing, sitting, and lying down; the correct handling of robe, almsbowl, and daily necessities; diligence in study, in attending elders, in caring for the sick, in teaching others the Dharma; and the discipline of where one does and does not go (avoiding taverns, brothels, and disreputable houses) — and who, above all, feels no real dread of minor transgressions easily confessed and cleared, has not yet achieved full precepts, however solid his grasp of the major rules.

Only when every one of these dimensions — the great precepts and the small, the formal rules and the countless details of bearing and conduct — is genuinely complete does "fullness of precepts" apply. Cháng Bǎi treats this distinction as pointedly practical: the danger is not usually breaking a major precept outright, but a habitual carelessness about small things that never rises to the level of real dread, and which quietly erodes what should be a seamless purity.

24. Precepts as Second Nature, and the Fullness of Concentration

Beyond mere fullness, precepts become genuinely "possessed" only when long practice makes them the very substance of one's being — an arhat's spontaneous incapacity for the five gravest wrongs. The treatise then turns to the second purity: concentration is "full" only at the fourth dhyāna, everything below it still incomplete.

Chinese Source

若於長時串修習故，便於根門善守而住，廣說乃至即於尸羅攝成自體，自性安住。如是名為尸羅攝受。

Ru-Yi Translation

If, through long habituation and practice, one comes to guard the sense-doors well and abide so — and so on at length, up to the precepts becoming one's very substance, in which one abides by one's own nature — this is called the possession of precepts.

Chinese Source

云何三摩地圓滿？謂若已得加行究竟果，或第四靜慮，乃名圓滿。於此下位，皆未圓滿。云何三摩地攝受？謂彼所得三摩地等後時清淨。又三摩地不為有行之所拘執，乃至廣說。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the fullness of concentration? Only when one has attained the fruit of the consummation of means, or the fourth dhyāna, is it called full; below this level, none of it is yet full. What is the possession of concentration? The concentration one has attained becoming, afterward, purified — and, moreover, the concentration no longer being gripped by deliberate effort, and so on at length.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that "possession of precepts" goes beyond mere completeness: through sustained, long-term practice, guarding the senses and keeping the precepts stops being an effortful task and becomes the very substance of the practitioner's character — precepts "attained as one's own nature." He cites the arhat's condition as the ultimate case: with all afflictions permanently exhausted, an arhat is simply, spontaneously incapable of five things — deliberately killing a sentient being, taking what is not given, engaging in sexual conduct, knowingly speaking falsely, or hoarding provisions against tomorrow's need — not because he restrains himself moment to moment, but because the impulse toward any of these has been uprooted entirely. He notes that even a stream-enterer can be said to have "full precepts" in this developed sense, but only a buddha achieves it with absolutely no remainder; even an arhat may retain harmless habitual traces (still humming a once-familiar tune, say) from beginningless habituation not yet fully cleared.

Turning to concentration, Cháng Bǎi is precise about the ceiling: "fullness" of concentration requires reaching the fruit-of-consummation attention or the fourth dhyāna specifically — nothing below the fourth level, however genuine, counts as full, since the fourth alone represents the completed sequence of all seven attentions carried to their final root-ground. "Possession" of concentration is this fullness made stable and unforced: purified of the relish, conceit, view, doubt, and ignorance that dhyāna can breed, and no longer gripped by the deliberate effort met earlier in the "well-adjusted" discussion — the three grades of mastery (attaining, perfecting, and gaining sovereignty over concentration) all folding into this single mark of genuine possession.

25. View and Liberation: Purity Completed Only at the Uncontaminated Threshold

Right view is genuine only once it reaches the direct realization of the uncontaminated — mere doctrinal understanding, however correct, does not yet count as "full." Liberation follows the identical pattern: only a trainee-transcending, uncontaminated knowledge-and-vision that has severed craving actually completes it.

Chinese Source

謂聞他音，及如理作意故，正見得生。由此正見，雖能知苦乃至知道，若未如實，猶不得名正見圓滿。若能於彼如實了知，爾時方名正見圓滿。云何見攝受？謂於後時諸漏永盡，乃至廣說。

Ru-Yi Translation

Right view arises through hearing the teaching from another and through sound attention. By this right view, though one is able to know suffering, up through knowing the path, if this is not yet a true, direct realization, it is not yet called the fullness of right view. Only when one truly and directly knows these is it called the fullness of right view. What is the possession of view? That afterward all the outflows are permanently exhausted, and so on at length.

Chinese Source

云何解脫圓滿？謂若由有學智見解脫貪等，未名圓滿。若由無學智見得解脫者，乃名圓滿。云何解脫攝受？謂若行、若住，常不退失現法樂住，如是名為解脫攝受。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the fullness of liberation? If release from craving and the rest comes through a trainee's knowledge and vision, it is not yet called full. Only when release comes through an adept's [uncontaminated] knowledge and vision is it called full. What is the possession of liberation? Whether walking or standing still, never losing the pleasant abiding in this very life — this is called the possession of liberation.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that right view begins in the ordinary way — hearing the teaching from a genuine source and applying sound attention to it — but this is still merely worldly right view, mixed with conceptual elaboration, and does not yet count as "full" even if its content (knowing suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path) is entirely correct. Fullness requires actually crossing into the uncontaminated: a direct realization in which the perceiving mind and the perceived object are utterly equal, neither graspable apart from the other — the non-conceptual wisdom in which faculty, object, consciousness, subject-object structure, and even the material world itself no longer appear as separate. Only this counts as genuinely full right view; "possession" of view follows once the outflows born of craving and views are permanently exhausted at the level of arhatship, pratyekabuddhahood, or buddhahood.

Liberation follows the identical logic: a trainee (from stream-entry up through the path to arhatship) may already possess considerable knowledge and insight, yet since the innate afflictions are not yet fully severed, this cannot yet be called full liberation — only an adept's (arhat's) uncontaminated knowledge and vision, which actually releases one from craving and its companions entirely, deserves the name. "Possession" of liberation is this freedom made stable through every activity, walking or sitting, never once losing the pleasant abiding that freedom from affliction brings. With this, all four excellences of purity — precepts, concentration, view, and liberation — are complete; what unites them, Cháng Bǎi stresses, is the single requirement of sustained diligence, since none of the four is reached by mere aspiration.

26. The Mind-Purifying Monk's Five Reflections: Naming the Eight Distracting Thoughts

A new and richly practical scriptural topic opens: a monk cultivating "heightened mind" faces eight kinds of distracting discursive thought, and the treatise sorts practitioners into three grades — soft, middling, and coarse — by how deeply these thoughts have taken root, each grade requiring a different remedy.

Chinese Source

復次，如經言：心清淨行苾芻，於時時間，應正作意思惟五相，乃至廣說。方便勤修增上心者，乃得名為心清淨行。諸惡不善欲等尋思，及親里等所有尋思，皆於此行能為障礙。略有三種補特伽羅，由軟中上尋思行者，有差別故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the sūtra says: a monk of purifying mind should, from time to time, rightly attend to and contemplate five marks, and so on at length. One who diligently applies himself to cultivating heightened mind is called "one of purifying mind conduct." The various evil, unwholesome discursive thoughts of desire and the rest, and the discursive thoughts of kinsfolk and so on, are all obstacles to this practice. There are, in brief, three kinds of persons, distinguished by whether their discursive thought is soft, middling, or coarse.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies "mind-purifying conduct" simply as diligent cultivation of heightened mind (concentration) resting on heightened wisdom, and names the eight discursive thoughts that obstruct it — desire, ill-will, and harm (the three gravest, capable of dragging a practitioner to the lower destinies and blocking wholesomeness outright), together with thoughts of kinsfolk, of homeland, of not-dying, of being looked down upon, and of family standing — drawing on the Compendium of Determinations' own eightfold list. These thoughts disturb the mind and obstruct the very practice meant to purify it.

He then introduces the treatise's threefold sorting of practitioners by how entrenched these thoughts are — soft (light and easily managed), middling, and coarse (deeply habituated and forceful) — each of which will receive its own tailored remedy in what follows, since a technique that suffices for a lightly-troubled mind will not touch one whose discursive thoughts run coarse and constant.

27. Five Ways to Dislodge a Distracting Thought, Matched to Three Grades of Practitioner

The treatise gives five distinct methods for dislodging unwholesome discursive thought — a graded escalation echoing the classical five-method teaching on removing distraction — matched to the soft, middling, and coarse practitioner in turn: substituting a wholesome object, contemplating the danger, simply refusing to attend, gradually starving it out, and confronting its root cause directly.

Chinese Source

初由正思惟所餘相故，令彼尋思不復現行。第二由見尋思深過患故，或復不念不思惟故，令彼尋思不復現行。云何不念及不思惟？由善於內安心等故。

Ru-Yi Translation

The first is that, by rightly attending to some other object, the discursive thought no longer manifests. The second is that, by seeing the deep dangers of the discursive thought, or else by neither dwelling on nor attending to it, the thought no longer manifests. What is "neither dwelling on nor attending to it"? Through skillfully settling the mind inward, and so on.

Chinese Source

第三補特伽羅，非初即能令彼一切皆不現行，要當方便令尋思行漸漸歇薄；麤既息已，漸當制伏。若猶未能於尋思路尋思所緣深生厭怖，當以厭患俱行之心多思惟力，於彼尋思俱行之心調練制伏。如是三種補特伽羅，分為五種。

Ru-Yi Translation

The third person is not able, from the outset, to make all such thoughts cease manifesting entirely; one must, by skillful means, let the discursive activity gradually thin and weaken — once its coarseness has subsided, one gradually subdues it further. If one still cannot give rise to deep dread toward the path and object of that discursive thought, one should, with a mind conjoined with weariness, apply the power of repeated reflection to train and subdue the mind conjoined with that discursive thought. Thus the three kinds of persons resolve into five methods.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi lays out all five methods, echoing the classical teaching on removing distracting thoughts. For the soft practitioner, one method suffices: substitution — deliberately attending to a wholesome object other than the one provoking the thought (the impurity contemplation against desire for a person, contemplating impermanence against craving for a thing, loving-kindness against ill-will toward a being, analysis of the elements against ill-will toward a thing) — like a carpenter driving out a coarse peg with a fine one. For the middling practitioner, two further methods apply: contemplating the danger — vividly seeing that such thoughts are blameworthy and lead to suffering, as viscerally as a fastidious young person would recoil from a corpse hung around the neck — and, if that alone does not suffice, simply refusing to dwell on or attend to it, deliberately looking away exactly as one shuts one's eyes to something one does not wish to see, settling the mind inward through the nine stages of settling (free of scatter, of grasped marks, of discrimination, restless movement, or expectation) until the thought has no purchase left.

For the coarse practitioner, whose discursive thoughts are deeply entrenched, two still stronger methods are needed. First, gradual starvation: not expecting the thought to vanish all at once, but patiently thinning and weakening its coarse manifestation through sustained practice — the classical image of a man walking briskly who successively slows to a walk, then stands, then sits, then finally lies down, replacing each coarser posture with a subtler one in turn (or, in a fiercer version of the same method, physically pressing tongue to palate and clenching the jaw to forcibly subdue the mind, as a strong man overpowers a weaker one). Second, and finally, confronting the root: if dread alone has not fully worked, tracing the thought back to its actual cause and condition with sustained, weariness-charged reflection until the thought, deprived of its root, simply collapses. The treatise's "three persons, five methods" thus maps a genuinely graded toolkit — substitution and dread for the lightly troubled, dread and disengagement for the moderate, and starvation-and-root-tracing for the most deeply entrenched — a practitioner's proper remedy determined by how stubborn his own distraction actually is.

28. Refining the Mind Like Gold: Three Stages from Raw Ore to Finished Ornament

A new scriptural image: just as gold is refined from raw ore through three stages — removing impurities, smelting into a workable mass, and finally polishing away every flaw — so the mind of a genuine practitioner is refined through three matching stages, from first purified faith all the way to mastery of the superknowledges.

Chinese Source

復次，盪塵經中，佛世尊言：當如陶鍊生金之法，陶鍊其心，乃至廣說。如是等義云何應知？謂陶鍊生金略有三種：一、除垢陶鍊，二、攝受陶鍊，三、調柔陶鍊。除垢陶鍊者，謂從金性中，漸漸除去麤中細垢，乃至唯有淨金沙在。攝受陶鍊者，謂即於彼鄭重銷煮。調柔陶鍊者，謂銷煮已，更細鍊治瑕隙等穢。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, in the Sūtra on Washing Away Dust, the World-Honored Buddha said: "One should refine the mind just as raw gold is refined," and so on at length. How should such matters be understood? The refining of raw gold is, in brief, of three kinds: refining that removes impurities, refining that consolidates, and refining that softens. Refining that removes impurities is gradually removing the coarse, middling, and fine impurities from the gold-bearing ore, until only pure gold-sand remains. Refining that consolidates is earnestly smelting that sand. Refining that softens is, once smelted, further finely working it to remove every flaw and blemish.

Chinese Source

如金性內所有生金，種性位中心淨行者，當知亦爾，謂堪能證般涅槃者。問：從何位名心淨行者？答：從得淨信求出家位。此於在家及出家位，有麤、中、細三種垢穢。其在家者由二為障，不令出家：一、不善業，謂常樂安處身語惡行；二、邪惡見，謂撥無世間真阿羅漢正行正至。此於已得淨信位前，能為障礙。

Ru-Yi Translation

Just as raw gold exists within gold-bearing ore, so too the mind-purifying practitioner, still at the stage of [meditative] lineage — one capable of realizing final nirvāṇa — should be understood likewise. Question: from what stage is one called a mind-purifying practitioner? Answer: from the stage of gaining pure faith and seeking to go forth. In both the householder's stage and the renunciant's stage there are coarse, middling, and fine impurities. The householder is obstructed from going forth by two things: unwholesome karma — habitually delighting in unwholesome bodily and verbal conduct; and false view — denying that genuine arhats of right conduct and right attainment truly exist in the world. These obstruct one only before the stage of gaining pure faith.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through the gold-refining process stage by stage before mapping it onto practice. Raw ore is first washed to remove the coarse stones, then the middling sand, then the fine black grit, leaving pure gold-sand — this is "removing impurities." That sand is then earnestly, repeatedly smelted in a furnace until it fuses into a single mass — "consolidating." That mass, still not yet soft, workable, lustrous, or durable, is then worked further until every flaw and blemish is removed, becoming fit to be shaped into a crown, bracelet, ring, or garland — "softening."

He then draws the parallel: just as raw gold is latent within unrefined ore, the capacity for final liberation is latent within a practitioner still at the "lineage" stage — someone genuinely capable of realizing nirvāṇa, though not yet having begun. This person is called a "mind-purifying practitioner" from the moment pure faith in the Three Jewels arises and the wish to go forth is born — not necessarily having yet taken ordination. Even before that point, an ordinary householder faces two obstacles blocking the very possibility of going forth: unwholesome karma (simply delighting in unrestrained bodily and verbal misconduct, since monastic life demands active abandonment of the ten unwholesome deeds) and false view (denying outright that genuine arhats — beings of truly right conduct and right attainment — exist at all, which drains any motivation to practice, since if no one has ever actually attained liberation, why bother). Cháng Bǎi notes these two obstruct only the pre-faith stage; once genuine faith and aspiration arise, a different, subtler set of impurities takes over — which the text addresses next.

29. The Three Refinements Mapped: From the First Dhyāna to Mastery of the Superknowledges

The gold-refining simile is now mapped precisely onto the three stages of a practitioner's own progress: removing the eight discursive thoughts to win the first dhyāna is "removing impurities"; consolidating that into the higher dhyānas is "smelting"; and reaching full mastery, free of all constraint, opens directly onto the six superknowledges — "softening."

Chinese Source

欲等尋思障出家者，令其不能心生喜樂。親等尋思障喜樂者，令其不能恆修善法。由斷彼故，恆修善法，速得圓滿純淨之心，有尋有伺，如淨金沙，是名為心除垢陶鍊。猶如生金仍未銷煮。

Ru-Yi Translation

The thoughts of desire and the rest [ill-will, harm] obstruct the renunciant, preventing joy and bliss from arising in the mind. The thoughts of kinsfolk and the rest obstruct that joy and bliss, preventing sustained cultivation of wholesome dharmas. By severing these, one sustains the cultivation of wholesome dharmas and swiftly attains a perfectly pure mind, with applied and sustained thought — like pure gold-sand. This is called the mind's refining that removes impurities — like raw gold not yet smelted.

Chinese Source

若有復能止息尋思，乃至具足安住第四靜慮，是名為心攝受陶鍊，由能攝受無尋無伺三摩地故。猶如生金已被銷煮。若三摩地不為有行之所拘執，乃至廣說，是名為心調柔陶鍊。於神通法，隨其所欲能轉變故。如彼生金，已細鍊治瑕隙等穢。

Ru-Yi Translation

If one can further still the discursive thoughts, up through abiding fully in the fourth dhyāna, this is called the mind's refining that consolidates, since one has taken up the concentration free of applied and sustained thought — like raw gold already smelted. If the concentration is no longer gripped by deliberate effort, and so on at length, this is called the mind's refining that softens: with regard to the superknowledges, one can transform at will, just as that raw gold, already finely worked, has every flaw and blemish removed.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps each refining stage precisely. Removing impurities corresponds to severing the eight discursive thoughts (desire, ill-will, and harm first, since they block the very possibility of joy and bliss arising; then kinsfolk, homeland, and the rest, since even once the first three are gone, these remaining five still prevent sustained practice) and thereby winning the first dhyāna — still "with applied and sustained thought," like pure gold-sand not yet fused, genuine but not yet capable of any refined use. Consolidating corresponds to pressing on through the second, third, and fourth dhyānas, released entirely from applied and sustained thought — like sand smelted into a single mass, no longer scattered but not yet finely finished.

Softening is the final stage: a concentration no longer gripped by deliberate effort or by the worldly coarse-versus-calm framework (the same "well-adjustedness" explained earlier), quiescent, subtle, secure, and resting in the mind's single course — at which point the practitioner gains sovereign command over the six superknowledges, transforming at will exactly as finely-worked gold can be shaped into any ornament desired. Cháng Bǎi notes candidly that most practitioners, himself presumably included, have not yet even completed the first stage — the coarse and middling impurities of ordinary discursive thought still very much present — and treats this frank admission as itself instructive: the refining process is long, and honesty about one's own current stage is the beginning of genuine progress, not a cause for discouragement.

30. Calming, Uplifting, Equanimity: Why No Single Mode of Practice Suffices Alone

A new scriptural teaching returns to a theme already met in fascicle 11 — calming, uplifting, and equanimity — now explained through the vivid image of a goldsmith who must blow on the fire, sprinkle water, and simply watch, all three in their proper turn, or the gold is ruined either by scorching, chilling, or neglect.

Chinese Source

復次，如經言：應於三相作意思惟，乃至廣說。應時時間作意思惟奢摩他等差別之相，不應一向。為欲對治沈掉等故。若於止、舉未串習者，唯一向修，是沈掉相。如此修者，當知住在方便道位。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the sūtra says: one should attend to and contemplate three marks, and so on at length. One should, from time to time, attend to and contemplate the distinguishing marks of calming and the rest — not exclusively one alone — in order to counter dullness and agitation. If one is not yet practiced in calming and uplifting and cultivates only one exclusively, this produces the very mark of dullness or agitation. One who practices this way should be known to still stand on the preparatory path.

Chinese Source

若時時間思惟捨相，如是在於成滿道位。亦由於此一向修故，於緣起法及聖諦中不思擇故，心不正定，不盡諸漏。於諸諦中，若未現觀，不能現觀；或已現觀，不得漏盡。初之二種，是三摩地能成辦道；第三一種，依三摩地盡諸漏道；是名略顯此中要義。

Ru-Yi Translation

If one attends from time to time to the mark of equanimity, this stands at the path's completion. Yet by exclusively cultivating this alone, without discerning dependent origination and the noble truths, the mind fails to reach right concentration and does not exhaust the outflows. Regarding the truths: if one has not yet directly realized them, one cannot come to realize them; or, having already realized them, one still fails to exhaust the outflows. The first two [calming and uplifting] are the path that accomplishes concentration itself; the third [equanimity] is the path that, resting on concentration, exhausts the outflows — this briefly displays the essential meaning here.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi recalls the same three marks explained back in fascicle 11 — calming, which draws the mind inward onto itself and thereby counters agitation, and uplifting, which takes up some pleasant external object (recollecting the Buddha, or a lotus, or the Three Jewels) and thereby counters dullness — and stresses that exclusive practice of either alone, without ever switching, inevitably produces exactly the fault it was meant to cure: pure calming tips into dullness, pure uplifting tips into agitation. A practitioner still alternating between the two, correcting one fault with the other as it arises, is still on the "preparatory path" — genuinely working, but not yet finished.

Equanimity — resting evenly, neither dull nor agitated — marks the "path's completion," the point where calming and uplifting have been balanced into stillness. But even this, Cháng Bǎi insists, is not the whole task: resting in bare equanimity without ever turning that stabilized mind toward actually investigating dependent origination and the four noble truths leaves the outflows entirely un-exhausted, whether or not direct realization has even begun. He closes with the treatise's own tidy division of labor: calming and uplifting together are what actually build the concentration itself, while equanimity is only the base on which the separate work of investigating the truths — vipaśyanā proper — must still be carried out to finish the job. The goldsmith's own three actions in tending the furnace (blowing to keep it hot, sprinkling water to keep it from scorching, and simply watching without over-intervening) mirror the same lesson: exclusive reliance on any one action ruins the gold, while all three in their proper alternation bring it to a soft, lustrous, unbreakable, and perfectly workable state.

31. Four Things That Uphold the Teaching: Seclusion, Practice, and Their Fruit

A new topic closing the treatise's exposition of gathered scriptural essentials: four things that let the noble teaching genuinely be upheld and transmitted — bodily seclusion, actual practice of calm and insight, and the fruit that practice yields, worked out through two mirror routes to liberation and the three "realms" of severance, release, and cessation they open onto.

Chinese Source

復次，有四正法，攝持聖教。何等為四？一者、遠離，二者、修習，三者、修果，四者、於聖教中無有乖諍。遠離者，謂山林、樹下、空閑靜室。修習者，謂住於彼勤修二法，謂奢摩他、毗鉢舍那。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, there are four true dharmas that uphold the noble teaching. What are the four? Seclusion, practice, the fruit of practice, and freedom from contention within the noble teaching. Seclusion means mountain forests, beneath trees, or a quiet, secluded dwelling. Practice means, abiding there, diligently cultivating the two dharmas — calm and insight.

Chinese Source

云何已習奢摩他，依毗鉢舍那而得解脫？謂如有一，先已得初靜慮乃至第四靜慮。彼即依此三摩地故，如實知苦乃至知道。彼即依此毗鉢舍那，於見所斷諸煩惱中，心得解脫。云何已習毗鉢舍那，依奢摩他心得解脫？謂如有一，如實知苦乃至知道。彼依如是增上慧故，發生靜慮。即由如是奢摩他故，於修所斷諸煩惱中，心得解脫。

Ru-Yi Translation

How does one who has practiced calm gain release resting on insight? Someone who has already attained the first dhyāna up through the fourth: resting on this concentration, he truly knows suffering, up through truly knowing the path; resting on this insight, his mind gains release from the afflictions cut on the path of seeing. How does one who has practiced insight gain release resting on calm in mind? Someone who truly knows suffering, up through truly knowing the path: resting on this heightened wisdom, he gives rise to dhyāna; by this very calm, his mind gains release from the afflictions cut on the path of cultivation.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies these four as what actually lets the Dharma be genuinely carried forward, generation after generation: seclusion, the bodily withdrawal to forests, tree-roots, or quiet dwellings that makes real practice possible at all; practice, the actual, diligent cultivation of calm and insight within that seclusion — the first two together letting a practitioner draw out the Dharma's own essential benefit for himself.

He then works through the treatise's own two mirror routes to fruit — the third of the four, and the one that actually produces a living embodiment of the teaching capable of transmitting it further. The first route belongs to a practitioner who has already mastered all four dhyānas and only then, resting on that concentration, comes to truly know the four noble truths — insight resting on prior calm, releasing the mind from the afflictions cut at the very moment of seeing the truths (the 112 counted across the three realms and four truths). The second route belongs to a practitioner who has already come to know the four truths through reflection alone, not yet possessing even the first dhyāna, and who then, resting on that very insight, develops the dhyānas — calm resting on prior insight, releasing the mind from the subtler afflictions only cut gradually on the path of cultivation (16 in number, headed by innate self-view). Whichever route is taken, Cháng Bǎi notes, the destination is the same three "realms": the realm of severance (freedom from the view-path afflictions), the realm of release from desire (freedom from the cultivation-path afflictions), and the realm of cessation (every clinging to the three realms of existence permanently extinguished) — together constituting the "fruit" that makes a practitioner a living vessel of the teaching, able to guide others and so genuinely uphold it.

32. No Contention Within the Teaching: Why the Buddha and Every Disciple Answer Alike

The fourth thing upholding the teaching: perfect consistency among the Teacher and all his disciples — asked the same question in the same terms, any one of them gives the same answer, unlike the shifting, mutually contradictory positions of non-Buddhist schools. The treatise gives a precise technical account of what counts as a genuinely "matching" line of questioning.

Chinese Source

於聖教中無乖諍者，所謂大師及諸弟子，若義、若句、若文，於文句義平等潤洽，互相隨順；非如異道施設見解，種種非一，差別不同。

Ru-Yi Translation

"Freedom from contention within the noble teaching" means that the Teacher and all his disciples, in meaning, in phrase, and in letter, harmonize evenly and accord with one another throughout — unlike the views set up by other paths, which are many and various, each differing from the rest.

Chinese Source

第一句者，所謂前句。若以此句問於初一，即以此句而問第二。設於初一依蘊而問，復於第二依餘問者，便不得名與第一句平等潤洽，互相隨順。

Ru-Yi Translation

The "first statement" is what is called the preceding statement. If one questions the first person with this statement, one questions the second person with this very same statement. But if one questions the first person on the basis of the aggregates, and then questions the second on some other basis, this cannot be called harmonizing evenly and according with the first statement.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi recalls the scriptural scene behind this teaching: Ānanda reports to the Buddha that he put the very same question — in meaning, phrasing, and letter — first to a senior elder, then to five hundred monks, and received identically matching answers from every one of them, exactly as the Buddha himself would answer; "Teacher and disciples," he marvels, "are entirely one in dharma, one in meaning, one in phrase, one in flavor." Cháng Bǎi contrasts this pointedly with non-Buddhist schools, whose views on ultimate reality remain forever unsettled — variant opinion, divergent opinion, shifting opinion, contradicting one another and breeding endless dispute — precisely because they never actually break through to realize the ultimate truth their reasoning merely gestures toward, whereas the Buddha and his awakened disciples, having all directly realized the identical reality, cannot help but answer alike regardless of differences in teaching-style suited to different audiences.

He then unpacks the treatise's own careful technical point about what counts as a "matching" question at all: consistency is only meaningfully tested when the very same question, in the very same terms, is put to different people. If one asks the first person "are the aggregates impermanent?" and gets one answer, then asks a second person an entirely different question — "are the eighteen elements selfless?" — any difference in the two answers proves nothing about contention, since the questions themselves never matched to begin with. He notes the treatise's classical apparatus of "forward-according" and "backward-according" statements (if all A is B, does it follow that all B is A?) as the tool for keeping such comparisons rigorous — and draws the practical lesson forward to anyone teaching the Dharma today: cite one's sources precisely, teach consistently within whichever framework (Madhyamaka, Yogācāra, or another) actually grounds a given text, and never blur one system's terms into another's, since it is exactly this kind of unacknowledged inconsistency — not genuine doctrinal diversity — that breeds needless dispute among the Buddha's own followers.

33. A Major Threshold: The Ground of Meditative Equipoise Closes, and the Ground of Non-Equipoise Opens

Here the source text marks an unmistakable structural boundary: "第六本地分中三摩呬多地" gives way to "本地分中非三摩呬多地第七" — the Ground of Meditative Equipoise, which has run continuously since fascicle 11, is declared complete, and the treatise turns at once to its seventh great ground: the Ground of Non-Equipoise (非三摩呬多地), covering every state of mind that does not rest in meditative absorption.

Chinese Source

本地分中非三摩呬多地第七。已說三摩呬多地。云何非三摩呬多地？

Ru-Yi Translation

The Ground of Non-Equipoise, the seventh in the Basic Section [of the treatise]. The Ground of Meditative Equipoise has now been explained. What is the Ground of Non-Equipoise?

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi marks this as one of the treatise's cleanest structural transitions in the entire seventeen-ground survey — no lingering topic carried over mid-sentence, as happened at so many earlier fascicle boundaries, but a genuine close ("the Ground of Meditative Equipoise has now been explained") followed immediately by a fresh opening question. The Ground of Meditative Equipoise, which opened all the way back in fascicle 11 with the four dhyānas and has run continuously through fascicles 11, 12, and now most of 13 — covering the liberations, masteries, totalities, the three doors to liberation, the immeasurables, the noble concentrations, the vajra-like and cessation attainments, and finally this closing survey of scriptural essentials — is now complete as its own major division of the treatise's seventeen grounds.

"Non-equipoise" (非三摩呬多), he explains, names the simple complement: every state of mind that has not entered meditative absorption at all — the entire ordinary, scattered, threshold, or otherwise not-yet-equipoised range of mental activity that makes up the vast majority of any being's actual experience, whether virtuous, unwholesome, or neutral. Where the prior ground asked what happens once the mind settles into genuine concentration, this new ground asks the equally necessary question: what is the mind doing the rest of the time? Cháng Bǎi treats this as no less important a topic than the meditative states themselves, since a practitioner spends far more of any given day outside formal absorption than within it, and understanding that ordinary terrain is just as essential to the path as understanding its meditative peaks.

34. The Twelve Ways a Mind Fails to Be "In Equipoise"

The entire Ground of Non-Equipoise turns out to be a single, carefully graded list: twelve distinct reasons a given state of mind does not count as genuine meditative absorption — running from the five senses' basic inability to sustain concentration at all, up through the last, subtlest case of a perfectly pure, effortless, uncontaminated concentration that has simply, for the moment, ended or been lost.

Chinese Source

當知此地相略有十二種。或有自性不定故，名非定地，謂五識身。或有闕輕安故，名非定地，謂欲界繫諸心心法；彼心心法，雖復亦有心一境性，然無輕安含潤轉故，不名為定。或有不發趣故，名非定地，謂受欲者，於諸欲中深生染著，而常受用。或有極散亂故，名非定地，謂初修定者，於妙五欲心隨流散。或有太略聚故，名非定地，謂初修定者，於內略心惛睡所蔽。

Ru-Yi Translation

Know that the marks of this ground are, in brief, twelve. Some [states] are called non-equipoised because their very nature is unstable — the five sense-consciousnesses. Some are called non-equipoised for lacking pliancy — the minds and mental factors bound to the desire realm; though these also have one-pointedness of a kind, lacking the moistening pervasion of pliancy, they are not called concentration. Some are called non-equipoised for want of any aspiration [to concentration] — those who indulge in desires, deeply defiled by attachment to them and constantly enjoying them. Some are called non-equipoised through extreme scatter — a beginning meditator whose mind still streams out after the five exquisite desires. Some are called non-equipoised through excessive contraction — a beginning meditator whose inwardly-narrowed mind is overcome by dullness and sleep.

Chinese Source

或有未證得故，名非定地，謂初修定者，雖無散亂及以略聚嬈惱其心，然猶未得諸作意故，諸心心法不名為定。或有未圓滿故，名非定地，謂雖得作意，然未證得加行究竟及彼果故，不名為定。或有雜染汙故，名非定地，謂雖證得加行究竟果作意，然為種種愛味等惑染汙其心。或有不自在故，名非定地，謂雖已得加行究竟果作意，其心亦無煩惱染汙，然於入、住、出諸定相中，未得自在，未隨所欲，梗澀艱難。或有不清淨故，名非定地，謂雖自在，隨其所欲，無澀無難，然唯修得世間定故，未能永害煩惱隨眠諸心心法，未名為定。或有起故，名非定地，謂所得定雖不退失，然出定故，不名為定。或有退故，名非定地，謂退失所得三摩地故，不名為定。

Ru-Yi Translation

Some are non-equipoised for not yet having attained [genuine absorption]: a beginning meditator who, though free of scatter or excessive contraction, has not yet attained any of the [seven] attentions, so his minds and mental factors are not yet called concentration. Some are non-equipoised for being not yet complete: though attention is attained, the consummation of means and its fruit are not yet realized. Some are non-equipoised through defilement: though the fruit of the consummation of means is attained, the mind is still stained by relish and similar afflictions. Some are non-equipoised for lacking mastery: though free of such afflictions, one has not yet gained ease in entering, abiding in, and emerging from the concentration-states, still meeting resistance and difficulty. Some are non-equipoised for lacking purity: though mastery is gained, only a worldly concentration has been cultivated, unable to permanently destroy the latent afflictions. Some are non-equipoised simply through having emerged: though the concentration attained has not been lost, one has left it, and so it is not, at this moment, called concentration. And some are non-equipoised through retrogression: the concentration once attained has been lost, and so is not called concentration.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the twelve as a single graded ladder tracing the entire distance from ordinary sensory awareness to the very summit of practice, each rung disqualified for a progressively subtler reason. The first four are basic: the five sense-consciousnesses simply cannot sustain themselves without an external object and so never count as absorption at all; ordinary desire-realm mental activity, however focused momentarily, lacks the "pliancy" (輕安) that only genuine concentration produces; someone who simply has no interest in practicing at all, absorbed instead in the ordinary enjoyment of sensory pleasure, never even begins; and a rank beginner's mind, still streaming outward after sights and sounds, is "extremely scattered." A close but opposite fault, "excessive contraction," is a beginner narrowing the mind's object down so far that dullness and sleep simply take over instead.

The next several concern genuine practice not yet ripe: not having reached even the seven attentions' first stage counts as "not yet attained"; having reached attention but not the sixth and seventh attentions (the consummation of means and its fruit — i.e., not yet actually entering a root dhyāna) is "not yet complete." Even once a root dhyāna is genuinely reached, if it is still stained by relish, view, conceit, or doubt, it counts as "defiled"; even once purified of those, if entering, abiding, and emerging are not yet effortless, it lacks "mastery"; and even with full mastery, if it remains merely a worldly concentration incapable of permanently destroying affliction's latent seed, it is "impure." Finally, even a concentration that is pure, masterful, and uncontaminated still fails to count as "equipoise" the instant one has actually emerged from it — and, more seriously, if it has been altogether lost through retrogression. Cháng Bǎi stresses the pedagogical point of the whole list: it maps, rung by rung, essentially the entire path already surveyed across fascicles 11–13, now viewed from the opposite direction — not what counts as genuine equipoise, but every one of the many ways a state of mind can fall short of it, at every stage from the rawest beginner to the nearly-perfected adept.

35. Another Major Threshold: Two Grounds at Once — With-Mind and Without-Mind

The source text closes the Ground of Non-Equipoise and opens, in a single stroke, two further grounds together: the eighth and ninth of the treatise's seventeen — the Ground With-Mind (有心地) and the Ground Without-Mind (無心地), treated jointly because they are exact mirror-opposites of one another. The treatise announces it will explain both through five shared analytical gateways.

Chinese Source

本地分中有心無心二地第八第九。已說非三摩呬多地。云何有心地？云何無心地？謂此二地，俱由五門應知其相：一、地施設建立門，二、心亂不亂建立門，三、生不生建立門，四、分位建立門，五、第一義建立門。

Ru-Yi Translation

The Grounds With-Mind and Without-Mind, the eighth and ninth in the Basic Section. The Ground of Non-Equipoise has now been explained. What is the Ground With-Mind? What is the Ground Without-Mind? These two grounds should both be known through five gateways: the gateway of establishing them by the [seventeen] grounds, the gateway of establishing them by whether the mind is disturbed or undisturbed, the gateway of establishing them by whether [mind] arises or does not arise, the gateway of establishing them by stage, and the gateway of establishing them by ultimate meaning.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes that the Ground of Non-Equipoise, once mapped through its twelve marks, closes swiftly — a genuinely short ground compared to the vast three-fascicle sweep of Meditative Equipoise before it — and the treatise moves at once to a matched pair: the Ground With-Mind, covering every state in which mind and mental factors are actually present and functioning, and the Ground Without-Mind, covering the rarer states in which they are, for a time, genuinely absent altogether (such as the no-perception attainment and the cessation attainment already met in fascicle 12, or dreamless deep sleep, or a fainting spell). Because the two are defined as strict complements of one another, the treatise treats them as a single combined topic rather than two separate expositions.

He previews the five gateways that will structure the whole discussion: first, sorting which of the treatise's seventeen grounds count as "with-mind" and which (if any) as "without-mind"; second, distinguishing the two by whether the mind is disturbed (亂, afflicted or confused) or undisturbed; third, by whether mind actually arises moment to moment or fails to arise at all; fourth, by the specific stage or occasion (分位) at which mind is absent — the handful of named states where this genuinely happens; and fifth, by the deepest, most technical sense (第一義) in which "without mind" can be said to hold at all, since even in the states popularly called "mindless," Cháng Bǎi notes, the Yogācāra tradition's own doctrine of the storehouse-consciousness complicates any claim that mind is ever totally absent — a subtlety the fifth gateway will address directly.

36. Gateway One Completes: Which Grounds Are With-Mind, Which Are Without

The first of the five gateways sorts the seventeen grounds themselves into with-mind and without-mind. Four grounds — those correlated with the five sense-consciousnesses, the Mind Ground, and the two Grounds of Applied-and-Sustained-Thought already surveyed in fascicle 4 — are entirely with-mind. A fifth, the Ground Neither Applied-nor-Sustained (spanning the second dhyāna up through the peak of existence), is with-mind except for three specific states carved out of it: the no-perception attainment, rebirth in the Heaven of No-Perception, and the cessation attainment — all three met already in fascicle 12. Those three states, wherever they occur, count as the Ground Without-Mind.

Chinese Source

地施設建立者，謂五識身相應地、意地、有尋有伺地、無尋唯伺地，此四一向是有心地。無尋無伺地中，除無想定并無想生及滅盡定，所餘一向是有心地。若無想定、若無想生及滅盡定，是無心地。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for establishing them by the [seventeen] grounds: the Ground Correlated with the Five Sense-Consciousness-Bodies, the Mind Ground, the Ground With Applied and Sustained Thought, and the Ground Without Applied but With Only Sustained Thought — these four are entirely the Ground With-Mind. Within the Ground Without Applied or Sustained Thought, apart from the no-perception attainment, together with rebirth as one without perception, and the cessation attainment, the remainder is entirely the Ground With-Mind. The no-perception attainment, rebirth as one without perception, and the cessation attainment are the Ground Without-Mind.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes this first gateway is really just a housekeeping survey: of the seventeen grounds already covered or yet to come, only three named states ever qualify as genuinely without mind, and every one of them was already introduced in fascicle 12's account of the meditative attainments. Everything else — including all ordinary desire-realm experience, every dhyāna, and even the very peak of the formless realm short of those three specific states — still counts as with-mind, however subtle or rarefied. The other grounds beyond these five (the grounds of hearing, reflection, cultivation, the śrāvaka, the pratyekabuddha, the bodhisattva, and the two grounds of nirvāṇa with and without residue) are left unmentioned here because they cut across both categories depending on the practitioner's momentary state, rather than falling wholly into one or the other.

37. Gateway Two: Disturbed Mind and Undisturbed Mind

The second gateway sorts with-mind from without-mind by a different measure entirely: not whether mental activity is present, but whether it is genuinely sound. A mind gripped by the four fundamental inversions — mistaking the impermanent for permanent, suffering for pleasant, the impure for pure, and the selfless for a self — is called "disturbed," and the treatise is willing to call a disturbed mind, in a qualified sense, no mind at all.

Chinese Source

心亂不亂建立者，謂四顛倒顛倒其心，名為亂心。若四顛倒不顛倒心，名不亂心。此中亂心，亦名無心，性失壞故。如世間見心狂亂者，便言此人是無心人，由狂亂心失本性故。於此門中，諸倒亂心，名無心地。若不亂心，名有心地。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for establishing them by whether the mind is disturbed or undisturbed: a mind inverted by the four inversions is called a disturbed mind. A mind not inverted by the four inversions is called an undisturbed mind. Here, a disturbed mind is also called no-mind, because its very nature has been ruined. It is like the world's way of speaking, on seeing someone whose mind is deranged, of calling that person "mindless" — because the deranged mind has lost its original nature. Through this gateway, every mind inverted and disturbed by the [four inversions] is called the Ground Without-Mind. An undisturbed mind is called the Ground With-Mind.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi draws out the everyday logic behind calling a disturbed mind "no mind": ordinary speech already does this when it calls a person driven mad by delusion "out of their mind," not because thought has literally stopped, but because whatever is happening no longer deserves the name of a sound, functioning mind. The four inversions run through nearly every unenlightened moment — clinging to impermanent conditioned things as though they will last, to the three kinds of suffering (change, pain, and the subtle suffering of mere formations) as though they were pleasant, to the inherently impure body as though it were clean, and to the five aggregates (or their absence) as though a real self stood behind them. He stresses that this gateway is not really a claim about mental *activity* at all, but about mental *soundness*: the same continuous stream of consciousness can be called "with-mind" or, functionally, "without-mind" depending only on whether it has been purified of these four root distortions through the sustained cultivation of precepts, concentration, and wisdom.

38. Gateway Three: The Eight Conditions Governing Whether Mind Arises

The third gateway turns from the mind's soundness to its bare arising, laying out eight conditions any one of which, if absent, prevents a moment of mind from occurring at all — and whose joint presence is precisely what allows it to occur.

Chinese Source

生不生建立者，八因緣故，其心或生，或復不生。謂根破壞故、境不現前故、闕作意故、未得故、相違故、已斷故、已滅故、已生故，心不得生。由此相違諸因緣故，心乃得生。此中若具生因緣故，心便得生，名有心地。若遇不生心因緣故，心則不生，名無心地。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for establishing them by whether [mind] arises or does not arise: owing to eight conditions, mind either arises or fails to arise. Namely: because the [sense] faculty is impaired; because the object is not present; because attention is lacking; because [the capacity] has not been attained; because [a contrary condition] is opposed [to it]; because [the afflictive condition] has already been cut off; because [its seed] has already been extinguished; because [that very moment of mind] has already arisen — for these reasons, mind fails to arise. When these conditions are reversed, mind is then able to arise. Here, when the conditions for arising are complete and mind is thereby able to arise, this is called the Ground With-Mind. When one meets the conditions for mind's non-arising, so that mind does not arise, this is called the Ground Without-Mind.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through all eight blocking conditions in turn. A damaged sense faculty (a blind eye) blocks the corresponding consciousness outright. An absent object leaves nothing for consciousness to cognize. Lacking attention means the mind simply never turns toward what is in fact present — seen but not registered, heard but not registered — and whether that attention, once it does occur, is sound or unsound is exactly what decides whether a wholesome or unwholesome mind results. "Not yet attained" covers cases like never having reached the first dhyāna, so its distinctive joy is simply unknown to that mind. "Opposed" covers ordinary cases like a pleasant object presenting itself: precisely because the object is agreeable, the causal basis for anger is blocked, so anger cannot arise there. "Already cut off" describes an attainment like the first dhyāna suppressing the manifest activity of desire, hostility, and harm even while their seeds remain. "Already extinguished" is the deeper case of an arhat, whose afflictive seeds themselves are permanently destroyed, so contact with any object whatsoever no longer produces defilement — and it also covers the no-perception and cessation attainments, where the six active consciousnesses cease outright. And "already arisen" simply names the basic Buddhist teaching that every mental moment perishes the instant after it arises, so numerically that same moment can never arise a second time. He notes the Yogācārabhūmi commentarial gloss (the 披尋記) that the first three conditions restate, from the negative side, the three requirements for cognition already laid out back in fascicle 3's Mind Ground: an intact faculty, a present object, and functioning attention.

39. Gateway Four: The Six Named Occasions of No-Mind

The fourth gateway abandons abstract conditions for a concrete list — naming exactly six specific occasions, and only these six, on which the Ground Without-Mind actually occurs.

Chinese Source

分位建立者，謂除六位，當知所餘名有心地。何等為六？謂無心睡眠位、無心悶絕位、無想定位、無想生位、滅盡定位，及無餘依涅槃界位。如是六位，名無心地。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for establishing them by stage: apart from six stages, know that the remainder is called the Ground With-Mind. What are the six? Namely: the stage of mindless sleep, the stage of mindless fainting, the stage of the no-perception attainment, the stage of rebirth as one without perception, the stage of the cessation attainment, and the stage of the nirvāṇa-realm without residue. These six stages are called the Ground Without-Mind.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this gateway as the most concrete and memorable of the five: only these six named occasions, and nothing else, ever count as the Ground Without-Mind. The first two are the most ordinary and universal — dreamless, sense-shut sleep so deep that the sixth consciousness stops discriminating entirely, and the total blackout of fainting, in which the same discrimination likewise ceases. The middle three are the meditative attainments already surveyed across fascicles 5 and 12: the no-perception attainment itself, the resulting rebirth as a being without perception in the fourth dhyāna heaven, and the far deeper cessation attainment reached only by non-returners and arhats. The sixth and final stage, nirvāṇa without residue, is the arhat's final passing — the last moment the five aggregates are ever assembled for that stream, after which no further rebirth occurs anywhere in the three realms. Everything else — every ordinary waking moment, every degree of ordinary distraction, and every meditative attainment short of these six specific states — remains, on this gateway's reckoning, squarely within the Ground With-Mind.

40. The Fifth Gateway, and a Final Milestone: With-Mind and Without-Mind Close Entirely

The fifth and final gateway pulls the rug out from under all four gateways before it. Judged by the treatise's own deepest, most ultimate reckoning, only one of the states named so far is genuinely without mind at all — because only there does the ālaya-vijñāna, the storehouse-consciousness itself, permanently cease. With this gateway's close, the treatise finishes explaining both the Ground With-Mind and the Ground Without-Mind — the eighth and ninth of the seventeen grounds — completing the arc that opened at the end of section 35.

Chinese Source

第一義建立者，謂唯無餘依涅槃界中，是無心地。何以故？於此界中，阿賴耶識亦永滅故。所餘諸位，轉識滅故，名無心地。阿賴耶識未永滅盡，於第一義非無心地。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for establishing them by ultimate meaning: only within the nirvāṇa-realm without residue is it the Ground Without-Mind. Why is this? Because within this realm, the ālaya-vijñāna, too, has permanently ceased. In the remaining stages, it is only the transforming consciousnesses that have ceased, [and on that basis] they are called the Ground Without-Mind; the ālaya-vijñāna has not yet permanently and completely ceased, so in the ultimate sense they are not the Ground Without-Mind.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this fifth gateway as the whole discussion's real point of arrival, exposing all four prior gateways as, strictly speaking, conventional rather than ultimate. Deep sleep, fainting, the no-perception attainment, and even the profound cessation attainment all silence only the six active consciousnesses — the five senses and the discriminating sixth mind. Underneath every one of them, the eighth consciousness, the ālaya or storehouse-consciousness, continues quietly holding every karmic seed and every sense faculty in readiness, which is exactly why a being can wake from sleep, revive from a faint, or emerge from even the cessation attainment with their whole prior identity, memory, and karmic continuity fully intact. Only at final nirvāṇa without residue — an arhat's last passing, when the causal round for that stream of consciousness is genuinely finished and no further rebirth will ever follow — does even the ālaya itself finally and permanently cease. That, and only that, is "without mind" in the fullest sense the treatise recognizes.

He flags one further wrinkle the commentarial tradition debates without fully resolving: since Buddhist scripture elsewhere insists the realm of sentient beings neither increases nor decreases, how can it be that an arhat's ālaya-consciousness permanently ceases at all, as though one being simply drops out of the count? Various classical explanations are offered — that from the ocean-sized totality of beings, one arhat's cessation is like one drop evaporating, negligible at scale; or, on a more Mahāyāna reading, that "the realm of sentient beings" ultimately names suchness (真如) itself, which persists whether or not any particular stream of aggregates continues, so that talk of "not increasing, not decreasing" was never a claim about individual continuity in the first place. Cháng Bǎi notes plainly that this tension — between texts insisting no being is ever finally excluded from eventual buddhahood and this fascicle's own straightforward statement that the ālaya truly ceases at final nirvāṇa — reflects different scriptures addressing different audiences with different immediate purposes, not a simple contradiction to be explained away.

41. A Major Threshold: The Ground With-Mind and Without-Mind Closes, and the Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing Opens

With section 40's fifth gateway complete, the treatise closes the Grounds With-Mind and Without-Mind entirely — the eighth and ninth of the seventeen grounds — and turns at once to the tenth: 聞所成地, the Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing. This ground opens the treatise's account of the three stages of wisdom built one on another — hearing, reflection, and cultivation — beginning with the knowledge gained simply by learning the teaching from a qualified source.

Chinese Source

本地分中聞所成地第十之一。已說有心無心地。云何聞所成地？謂若略說，於五明處名句文身無量差別，覺慧為先，聽聞、領受、讀誦、憶念。又於依止名身、句身、文身義中，無倒解了。如是名為聞所成地。

Ru-Yi Translation

The Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing, the tenth, part one, in the Basic Section. The Grounds With-Mind and Without-Mind have now been explained. What is the Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing? Briefly stated: with respect to the boundless distinctions among the name-bodies, phrase-bodies, and letter-bodies of the five sciences, [one develops], with innate discernment leading the way, the [activities of] listening, receiving, reciting, and remembering. Furthermore, with respect to the meanings that depend on those name-bodies, phrase-bodies, and letter-bodies, one comes to understand them without inversion. This is called the Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi frames this as the treatise turning, after its long survey of meditative states, to the actual curriculum a Buddhist practitioner studies — the "five sciences" (五明: inner wisdom, medicine, logic, linguistics, and the practical arts) that will occupy the rest of this ground. He unpacks the technical vocabulary carefully: a "name-body" (名身) is simply a word, called in the treatise's own technical sense a "designating expression" (增語) because ordinary experience has no fixed name of its own until language supplies one — form, sound, a house, a cart are all, strictly, names the world has agreed to attach to what is really only causally-arisen process. A "phrase-body" (句身) is a complete statement built from names that actually conveys a teaching — his example is the classic line "all conditioned things are like a dream, a phantom, a bubble, a shadow, like dew or a flash of lightning" — since the bare name "conditioned things" alone teaches nothing, but arranged into a full phrase it conveys impermanence directly. A "letter-body" (文身) is simply the written characters or syllables that make names and phrases possible in the first place.

He stresses the ground's own internal structure: hearing wisdom is not just passive listening but a four-stage process — attentive listening to the sounds and cadence of the teaching, receiving and comprehending its content, reciting and rehearsing it until it is retained, and finally holding it in memory without forgetting — and even this whole process still depends on an innate discernment (生得慧) a person already possesses before ever hearing a single word of Dharma. But hearing correctly is only the first half; the ground's definition closes by requiring that the meaning behind the words be grasped without inversion or error, which is why Cháng Bǎi calls this ground the foundation the two higher wisdoms — reflection and cultivation — will be built upon: get the hearing wrong, and everything built on it afterward inherits the same distortion.

42. The Five Sciences Named, and Inner Wisdom's Fourfold Approach

Having briefly stated what hearing-wisdom is, the treatise now turns to a detailed exposition, first naming all five sciences a Buddhist practitioner studies, then beginning with the first and most important: inner wisdom (內明), the Buddhist teaching itself, approached through four distinct analytical angles.

Chinese Source

何等名五明處？謂內明處、醫方明處、因明處、聲明處、工業明處。云何內明處？當知略說由四種相。一、由事施設建立相，二、由想差別施設建立相，三、由攝聖教義相，四、由佛教所應知處相。

Ru-Yi Translation

What are called the five sciences? Namely: the science of inner wisdom, the science of medicine, the science of logic, the science of linguistics, and the science of the practical arts. What is the science of inner wisdom? Know that, briefly stated, it has four aspects: first, the aspect of establishing it by the designation of factual matters; second, the aspect of establishing it by the differentiation of conceptions; third, the aspect of that which comprises the meaning of the sacred teaching; fourth, the aspect of what a Buddhist ought to know.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the five sciences as the complete traditional curriculum of ancient Indian higher learning, which the Buddhist tradition adopted and reoriented around its own teaching: inner wisdom (內明) is Buddhism's own doctrine, treated here first and at by far the greatest length; medicine (醫方明) is the healing arts, useful both for one's own body and for caring for others as an act of compassion; logic (因明) is the formal science of valid inference and debate, already glimpsed in fascicle 5's account of the three-branch syllogism; linguistics (聲明) covers grammar, phonetics, and the structure of language itself; and the practical arts (工業明) cover the trades and crafts of ordinary livelihood. He notes the traditional teaching that a bodhisattva pursuing the benefit of all beings needs fluency in all five, not inner wisdom alone, since genuinely helping others requires speaking their language, understanding their bodies, arguing persuasively, and often practically supporting their livelihood as well.

Of the four aspects that will structure the entire inner-wisdom exposition, he previews their logic: the first (事施設建立相) organizes Buddhist teaching by what body of texts contains it — essentially, sorting all doctrine into scripture, discipline, and commentary. The second (想差別施設建立相) organizes it by the countless specific concepts and doctrinal categories those texts actually name and define — this will prove to be by far the largest of the four, an extended catalogue of Buddhist technical terms. The third (攝聖教義相) approaches from the opposite direction, using ten overarching principles to show how the entire teaching coheres as one system — reasoning from principle to text. The fourth (佛教所應知處相) organizes doctrine numerically, from single teachings up through sets of ten — reasoning from text to principle. Cháng Bǎi calls the first and second "content-first" approaches and the third and fourth "structure-first" approaches, together giving a practitioner two complementary ways of holding the entire Buddhist teaching in mind.

43. The First Aspect: Scripture, Discipline, and Commentary — the Three Divisions Containing All of the Buddha's Teaching

The first of the four aspects sorts everything the Buddha taught into just three textual categories — corresponding to what later tradition calls the Tripiṭaka, the "three baskets" of Buddhist scripture.

Chinese Source

云何事施設建立相？謂三種事，總攝一切諸佛言教。一、素怛纜事，二、毗奈耶事，三、摩怛履迦事。如是三事，攝事分中當廣分別。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the aspect of establishing it by the designation of factual matters? It is that three kinds of matters comprehend the entirety of all the Buddhas' verbal teaching. First, the matter of the sūtras; second, the matter of the vinaya; third, the matter of the mātṛkā. These three matters will be broadly differentiated in the Compendium of Determinations.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses the three Sanskrit terms plainly: 素怛纜 (sūtra) means "discourse," the recorded teachings the Buddha gave directly; 毗奈耶 (vinaya) means "discipline" or "taming," the codes of conduct that subdue affliction through precept-keeping; and 摩怛履迦 (mātṛkā) means "matrix" or "mother" — rendered here as the "root of wisdom" — the analytical commentaries and treatises, of which this very treatise is itself an example, that unpack and systematize the sūtras' meaning. Together the three cover everything: what was taught, how to live it, and how to understand it in depth.

He draws out a subtler point from the commentarial gloss (the 披尋記): calling these three "matters" (事) is deliberate, because Buddhist teaching is never really about words in isolation — it is about the causally-arisen realities (色, 受, 眼, 耳, and so on through the five aggregates, twelve sense-fields, and eighteen elements) that the words point to. Language only arises to express some underlying content, so the content itself — the "matter" — is treated as prior, with the sūtra, vinaya, and mātṛkā categories as the vessels built to convey it. He also notes the tradition's threefold breakdown of exactly what kind of "matters" ground this teaching in the first place: fundamental matters (the basic facts of favorable and unfavorable rebirth, moral decline and advance, saṃsāra and nirvāṇa); matters of skillful means for attainment (the specific conducts — desirous, renunciate, wholesome, unwholesome, and so on — that move a practitioner toward or away from liberation); and matters of compassion for others (freeing others from desire, demonstrating the path, instructing, encouraging, and rejoicing with them) — a reminder that even this driest classificatory material ultimately serves the same compassionate aim.

44. The Second Aspect Opens: A Verse of Twelve Terms, and "Word" Illustrated Through the Śrāvakayāna's Thirteen Categories

The second and by far largest of the four aspects — the differentiation of conceptions (想差別施設建立相) — organizes the entire body of Buddhist technical vocabulary under twelve headings, given first as a single summarizing verse: word, delusion, discursive proliferation, abiding, reality, purity, excellence, tranquility, nature, principle, provisional designation, and direct realization. Each heading is then illustrated twice over — once through categories shared with the Śrāvakayāna (the path of the hearers), once through categories unique to the Mahāyāna. The first heading, "word" (句), is illustrated through thirteen classic Buddhist lists, most already familiar from earlier fascicles of this very treatise.

Chinese Source

嗢柁南曰：句迷惑戲論　住真實淨妙　寂靜性道理　假施設現觀。云何句？謂內六處無量境界、無量方所、無量時分。復有三界。謂欲界、色界、無色界。又有三界。謂小千世界、中千世界、三千大千世界。復有四眾。謂在家眾、出家眾、鄔波索迦眾、非人眾。復有三受。謂苦受、樂受、不苦不樂受。復有三世。謂過去世、未來世、現在世。復有三寶。謂佛寶、法寶、僧寶。復有三法。謂善法、不善法、無記法。復有三雜染。謂煩惱雜染、業雜染、生雜染。復有四聖諦。謂苦聖諦、集聖諦、滅聖諦、道聖諦。復有九次第等至。謂初靜慮等至，乃至滅想受等至。復有三十七菩提分法。謂四念住、四正斷、四神足、五根、五力、七覺支、八道支。復有四沙門果。謂預流果、一來果、不還果、最勝阿羅漢果。復有眾多勝妙功德。謂無量、解脫、勝處、遍處、無諍、願智、無礙解、六神通等。

Ru-Yi Translation

The summarizing verse: "Word, delusion, discursive proliferation; abiding, reality, purity, excellence; tranquility, nature, principle; provisional designation, direct realization." What is "word"? The six inner sense-bases have boundless objects, boundless locations, and boundless spans of time. There are also the three realms: desire, form, and formless. And there are [another] three realms: the small chiliocosm, the medium chiliocosm, and the great trichiliocosm. There are the four assemblies: householders, renunciants, lay devotees, and non-humans. There are the three feelings: painful, pleasant, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant. There are the three times: past, future, and present. There are the three jewels: the Buddha-jewel, the Dharma-jewel, and the Saṅgha-jewel. There are the three dharmas: wholesome, unwholesome, and indeterminate. There are the three defilements: afflictive, karmic, and rebirth defilement. There are the four noble truths: suffering, origination, cessation, and the path. There are the nine successive attainments: the first-dhyāna attainment up through the attainment of the cessation of perception and feeling. There are the thirty-seven factors of enlightenment: the four foundations of mindfulness, the four right exertions, the four bases of supernormal power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of enlightenment, and the eightfold noble path. There are the four fruits of the ascetic path: stream-entry, once-return, non-return, and the most excellent fruit of arhatship. And there are many sublime qualities: the immeasurables, the liberations, the [eight] bases of mastery, the [ten] totalities, [the concentration of] non-contention, wish-fulfilling knowledge, unobstructed understanding, and the six superknowledges, and so forth.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this whole exercise as answering "what is a word?" not with a definition but with thirteen worked demonstrations, each showing how a single technical term (三界, 四聖諦, 三十七菩提分法, and so on) compresses a vast body of teaching into a handful of syllables. He notes that this batch is entirely material already met in earlier fascicles — the desire/form/formless three realms and small-medium-great chiliocosms from fascicle 2; the three feelings and their causal analysis from fascicles 3 and 5; the four noble truths, thirty-seven factors of enlightenment, and four fruits from the classic śrāvaka path structure; the nine successive attainments, eight liberations, eight bases of mastery, ten totalities, and six superknowledges all surveyed at length across fascicles 11 and 12 — so this section functions as a compressed index of the treatise's own prior material rather than new doctrine.

He does flag two items worth a fuller gloss. The three jewels (三寶) are traditionally said to earn the name "jewel" on six counts — their rarity, their capacity to remove defilement, their power, their capacity to adorn one who holds them, their supremacy among all things, and their unchanging nature (gold remains gold whatever shape it is worked into) — with the further point that a true Saṅgha-jewel is defined by inward attainment of the path, not merely by the outward form of having taken ordination; a genuinely awakened lay practitioner is, in this sense, no less a jewel than an ordained monastic who has not yet realized the path. And the four assemblies (四眾) deliberately name only male lay devotees (鄔波索迦) rather than listing female lay devotees (鄔波斯迦) separately — the commentarial gloss explains this is not an omission but a convention, since female lay practice is understood as included within the same category by parallel structure, exactly as the treatise's earlier discussion of lay precepts likewise names only the male form while covering both.

45. "Word" Continues: The Mahāyāna's Five Bases, Two Emptinesses, and Two Selflessnesses

The treatise now illustrates "word" a second way, through categories unique to the Mahāyāna — beginning with a foundational fivefold framework for everything speech can ever address, then the two kinds of emptiness and two kinds of selflessness that distinguish Mahāyāna analysis from the Śrāvakayāna's single focus on the four truths.

Chinese Source

復有方廣大乘五事。謂相、名、分別、真如、正智。復有二空性。謂補特伽羅空性，及法空性。復有二無我性。謂補特伽羅無我性，及法無我性。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also, in the extensive Mahāyāna teaching, the five bases: characteristic-mark, name, discrimination, suchness, and correct wisdom. There are also two emptinesses: the emptiness of a person, and the emptiness of phenomena. There are also two selflessnesses: the selflessness of a person, and the selflessness of phenomena.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the five bases (五事) as the Mahāyāna's own complete inventory of everything a mind can ever be engaged with. "Characteristic-mark" (相) is the causally-arisen thing itself, prior to any name — what the five sense-consciousnesses actually take as their object before the sixth consciousness labels it. "Name" (名) is the word laid over that mark purely for communication's sake — a lamp has no inherent name until someone calls it one. "Discrimination" (分別) is simply every mental factor of every being throughout the three realms — the entire machinery of ordinary cognition. "Suchness" (真如) is what remains once both mark and name are seen through: the selfless, unchanging nature of things, known only by noble wisdom, never itself an object that ordinary speech can directly reach. And "correct wisdom" (正智) is the wisdom that knows suchness — split into the fundamental non-discriminating wisdom attained in meditative absorption, where subject, object, and even language itself drop away entirely, and the subsequently-attained wisdom that returns from that absorption still able to use words, but no longer bound or deceived by them.

The two emptinesses and two selflessnesses restate the same distinction from two angles he treats as functionally identical: "emptiness" stresses that something simply is not there at all (a person, searched for anywhere in or apart from the five aggregates, is never found), while "selflessness" stresses that whatever is there lacks any independent, controlling agency of its own (the aggregates function, but nothing stands behind them pulling the strings). Applied to a person, this yields the classic Buddhist teaching shared with the Śrāvakayāna; applied to phenomena themselves — even the very words and concepts by which anything is described — it yields the distinctly Mahāyāna teaching that not even "lamp" as a category, apart from being a name laid over process, has any reality of its own to be found.

46. Avoiding Two Extremes, and Four Grades of Reality

Next the treatise names the middle path between two opposite errors of over- and under-interpretation, followed by a ladder of four "truths" running from the merely conventional up to the deepest object a Mahāyāna path can purify.

Chinese Source

復有遠離二邊處中觀行。謂離增益邊、離損減邊。復有四種真實。謂世間所成真實、道理所成真實、煩惱障淨智所行真實、所知障淨智所行真實。

Ru-Yi Translation

There is also the middle-way contemplation that abandons two extremes: abandoning the extreme of superimposition, and abandoning the extreme of denial. There are also four kinds of reality: reality established by worldly convention, reality established by reasoning, reality that is the domain of the wisdom purified of afflictive obstruction, and reality that is the domain of the wisdom purified of the obstruction to what can be known.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains the two extremes as mirror-image errors about the same three natures that will be named explicitly two sections ahead. "Superimposition" (增益邊) is adding something that is not there — taking the merely conventional, dependently-arisen, or perfected nature and grasping it as a truly, independently existing thing, the way an ordinary person instinctively treats a lamp's name as though it named something with its own solid essence. "Denial" (損減邊) is the opposite error of subtracting something that genuinely is there — dismissing the dependently-arisen and perfected natures as utterly nonexistent, a nihilism just as mistaken as the first extreme's over-attachment. The middle way threads between both: neither inventing a solid self where there is only process, nor denying that the process, and the ultimate nature it discloses, are real in their own way.

The four grades of reality then trace a path from shallow to deep: worldly convention (everyone agrees to call earth "earth" and fire "fire," a bare social consensus); reasoned doctrine (the wise systematically lay out categories like the five aggregates or eighteen elements using direct perception, inference, and scriptural authority); the reality that purifies the Śrāvakayāna and Pratyekabuddhayāna practitioner of afflictive obstruction (the four noble truths and dependent origination, whose contemplation actually severs the root afflictions); and finally the reality that purifies even the subtler obstruction to omniscient knowledge — the emptiness of phenomena itself, the twofold emptiness of person and dharma together, which only Mahāyāna practice directly targets. Cháng Bǎi notes the clean logic: each grade doesn't replace the one before it but goes one layer deeper, ending exactly where the two emptinesses of the previous section left off.

47. The Four Investigations and Four Kinds of True Knowledge — the Working Method of Yogācāra Practice

The treatise now names the actual meditative technique a Mahāyāna practitioner uses to dismantle the two extremes just described — four progressively deeper lines of investigation, and the four corresponding grades of genuine insight they yield once fully matured. Master Cháng Bǎi calls this pairing the working heart of the entire path to realizing emptiness of phenomena.

Chinese Source

復有四尋思。謂名尋思、事尋思、自性假立尋思、差別假立尋思。復有四如實遍智。謂名尋思所引如實遍智、事尋思所引如實遍智、自性假立尋思所引如實遍智、差別假立尋思所引如實遍智。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also the four investigations: investigation into names, investigation into things, investigation into the provisional positing of intrinsic nature, and investigation into the provisional positing of differentiation. There are also the four kinds of thorough true knowledge: the thorough true knowledge induced by investigation into names, the thorough true knowledge induced by investigation into things, the thorough true knowledge induced by investigation into the provisional positing of intrinsic nature, and the thorough true knowledge induced by investigation into the provisional positing of differentiation.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through the method using the treatise's own example of a lamp. First, investigation into names: examine the word "lamp" itself, and see that it is nothing but a discrimination the sixth consciousness has laid over an object — the sound or shape of the word carries no inherent connection to what it names, so the name itself is empty of any reality of its own. Second, investigation into things: examine the "thing" the name supposedly points to, and see that apart from a confused stream of consciousness, there is no lamp-in-itself waiting behind the word to be pointed at — only bare process, prior to any naming. Third, investigation into the provisional positing of intrinsic nature: examine whether the lamp has some essential "lamp-ness" joining the name to the thing, and find that this too is a mere overlay on dependently-arisen appearance, like a reflection or a dream-image — vivid, but without any essence to be pinned down. Fourth, investigation into the provisional positing of differentiation: examine the specific qualities that seem to distinguish this lamp from that one, and find those differences, too, are only provisionally posited, neither ultimately existent nor simply nonexistent.

He stresses that this fourfold sequence deepens in one clear direction: the first investigation dissolves only the name, still leaving the "thing" seemingly intact; the second dissolves the thing as well; but the third and fourth — dissolving intrinsic nature and differentiation together — reach what Cháng Bǎi calls "the most profound domain the wise can enter," because by this point neither name nor thing survives as a fixed reference point, and yet the vivid dependently-arisen appearance is not denied either, precisely because it still functions, still deceives, still needs to be worked with skillfully. The four kinds of true knowledge are simply this same fourfold investigation matured into settled, confirmed insight, corresponding to the "heat" and "summit" stages of the classical four preparatory stages leading to the path of seeing — a technical map, but one built entirely from a single, patient question repeated at four depths: is this really here, or only provisionally so?

48. The Three Natures and Three Kinds of Non-Nature — Yogācāra's Central Teaching

The treatise now states, in its most compressed form, the doctrine underlying everything covered in this section so far: the three natures (trisvabhāva) that every phenomenon can be analyzed into, and the three corresponding senses in which each of those natures can also be said to have no nature of its own — the teaching by which Yogācāra reconciles "everything is only consciousness" with "all phenomena are empty."

Chinese Source

復有三種自性。謂遍計所執自性、依他起自性、圓成實自性。復有三無性性。謂相無性性、生無性性、勝義無性性。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also three intrinsic natures: the nature of thorough imagination and attachment, the nature of dependent arising, and the nature of perfect and complete accomplishment. There are also three kinds of the nature of having no [intrinsic] nature: the nature of having no nature with respect to characteristic-marks, the nature of having no nature with respect to arising, and the nature of having no nature in the ultimate sense.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the three natures as three ways of looking at exactly the same event, not three different kinds of things. The imagined nature (遍計所執自性) is what an ordinary mind grasps whenever it treats a name — "lamp," "self," "mine" — as though it named something with solid, independent existence; this layer is purely fabricated and, on analysis, simply is not there. The dependent nature (依他起自性) is what is actually happening underneath the fabrication: the causally-conditioned stream of consciousness itself, which the treatise (citing the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha*) reduces to eleven functional modes — the body, the one who has a body, the experiencer, what is experienced, and so on — all ultimately seeds ripening from the storehouse-consciousness, arising and perishing moment to moment, prior to any name laid over them. The perfected nature (圓成實自性) is simply what remains once the imagined layer is stripped away from the dependent layer: emptiness itself, directly realized, the object of noble wisdom that severs both the afflictive obstruction and the obstruction to omniscience.

The three non-natures then explain, without contradicting the "three natures" teaching, why the treatise elsewhere insists all phenomena are also, in a real sense, without any nature at all — countering a literalist misreading that might treat "three natures" itself as three more things to cling to. Absence of nature with respect to characteristic-marks (相無性性) applies to the imagined nature specifically: since it was only ever a name-based fabrication, it never had a characteristic-mark of its own to begin with. Absence of nature with respect to arising (生無性性) applies to the dependent nature: since everything dependently arisen exists only through other conditions and never through its own independent power, it has no nature of self-sufficient arising, however real its causal functioning is moment to moment. Absence of nature in the ultimate sense (勝義無性性) applies most subtly to the perfected nature itself, in two senses at once — first, because the dependently-arisen stream that discloses emptiness is not itself the pure object contemplated by wisdom, that stream, too, can be called "ultimately without nature"; and second, because the perfected nature — the selflessness of phenomena — simply is the ultimate truth, so calling it "ultimately without [a further, graspable] nature" points at emptiness itself, not at some fourth thing beyond it. Cháng Bǎi calls this the single most economical formula in the whole Yogācāra system: three natures account for how anything appears at all; three non-natures guard against grasping even that very account as one more solid thing to hold onto.

49. The Five Aspects of Great Enlightenment, the Five Facets of the Mahāyāna, and Five Kinds of Boundless Conception

The list continues with three more Mahāyāna-specific frameworks: what fully qualifies a buddha's awakening as "great," how the entire Mahāyāna path can be organized under five headings, and the five directions in which a bodhisattva's aspiration must remain genuinely limitless.

Chinese Source

復有五相大菩提。謂自性故、功能故、方便故、轉故、還故。復有五種大乘。一、種子，二、趣入，三、次第，四、正行，五、正行果。復有五無量想。謂有情界無量想、世界無量想、法界無量想、所調伏界無量想、調伏方便界無量想。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also five aspects of great enlightenment: by way of intrinsic nature, by way of capability, by way of skillful means, by way of activity, and by way of return. There are also five facets of the Mahāyāna: first, the seed; second, entry; third, sequence; fourth, correct practice; fifth, the fruit of correct practice. There are also five kinds of boundless conception: boundless conception with respect to the realm of sentient beings, boundless conception with respect to the world, boundless conception with respect to the realm of phenomena, boundless conception with respect to the realm to be tamed, and boundless conception with respect to the realm of skillful means for taming.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses the five aspects of great enlightenment briefly: intrinsic nature names a buddha's wisdom itself, surpassing even the awakening of the śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha; capability names the ten kinds of mastery a buddha holds over lifespan, mind, resources, karma, rebirth, resolve, aspiration, spiritual power, wisdom, and Dharma itself; skillful means names the four kinds of transformation a buddha or bodhisattva can manifest to reach beings at every level of readiness — appearing as a bodhisattva, as a buddha, or taking on forms suited to gather śrāvaka- or pratyekabuddha-natured beings; activity (轉) names the buddha's tireless, uninterrupted work for beings, both provisionally (while beings remain to be taught) and ultimately (across endless future time); and return (還) names entry into nirvāṇa — provisionally, as Śākyamuni's own passing merely appeared to be an ending while the work in truth continues, and ultimately, only once every trace of affliction and its habitual residue is genuinely exhausted.

He treats the five facets of the Mahāyāna as a simple roadmap — seed (the potential/lineage that makes bodhisattva practice possible at all), entry (the initial arousal of bodhicitta), sequence (the ordered stages of the bodhisattva path), correct practice (everything actually done along the way), and fruit (the resulting enlightenment) — each keyed to a specific chapter elsewhere in the Bodhisattva Ground for fuller treatment. And he reads the five boundless conceptions as simply naming, without further elaboration here, the scope a bodhisattva's compassionate attention must eventually cover: sentient beings without limit, worlds without limit, the entire realm of phenomena without limit, the full range of beings still to be tamed regardless of their capacity, and the correspondingly boundless range of skillful methods needed to tame them — a reminder that boundless compassion, to be genuine, must be matched by an equally boundless willingness to adapt one's method to whoever is actually in front of you.

50. The Seven Kinds of Suchness, and a Buddha's Inconceivable Qualities — "Word" Concludes

The Mahāyāna half of "word" closes with two final items: the seven specific lenses through which the single reality of suchness pervades every category of phenomena, and a summary gesture toward the full catalogue of a buddha's extraordinary qualities. With this, the treatise's demonstration of what a "word" can name — twenty-seven categories in all, thirteen shared with the Śrāvakayāna and fourteen unique to the Mahāyāna — comes to a close.

Chinese Source

復有真實義隨至。謂於一切無量法中遍隨至真如及於彼智。復有不思議威德勝解、無障礙智、三十二大士夫相、八十種隨形相、四種一切相清淨、十力、四無所畏、三念住、三不護、大悲、無忘失法、拔除習氣、一切種妙智等。如是諸句，略唯二句。謂聲聞乘中所說句，及大乘中所說句。

Ru-Yi Translation

There is also the true meaning that pervasively attends [all phenomena]: namely, suchness, which pervasively attends throughout all boundless phenomena, together with the wisdom [that knows] it. There are also a buddha's inconceivable might and resolve, unobstructed wisdom, the thirty-two marks of a great being, the eighty subsidiary marks, the four kinds of universal purity, the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, the three foundations of mindfulness, the three things requiring no guarding, great compassion, the dharma of never forgetting, the eradication of habitual residue, and the sublime wisdom of every kind, and so forth. All such words are, in brief, of only two kinds: those spoken of within the Śrāvakayāna, and those spoken of within the Mahāyāna.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi unpacks the seven kinds of suchness (七真如) as the same single unchanging reality — emptiness, or the true nature of things — examined through seven different phenomena it pervades. Suchness of flux (流轉真如): every conditioned thing arises and perishes without any fixed "first" or "last," and contemplating this frees one from imagining either a single almighty cause or an unjust, unequal cause behind saṃsāra's flow. Suchness of the true mark (實相真如): the selflessness of both persons and phenomena that is the genuine character underlying all things, whose contemplation permanently dissolves the view of a substantial self. Suchness of mere-consciousness (唯識真如): the fact — itself unshakeably real — that every phenomenon is only consciousness's own display, so a defiled mind yields a defiled world and a purified mind a purified one. Suchness of establishment (安立真如): the truth of suffering itself, that whatever is grasped as "I" and "mine" is genuinely and simply suffering. Suchness of wrong practice (邪行真如): the truth of origination, that craving is what actually drives suffering's arising and therefore must be cut. Suchness of purity (清淨真如): the truth of cessation, nirvāṇa's utterly unstained peace once both afflictive and cognitive obstructions are permanently ended. And suchness of correct practice (正行真如): the truth of the path, the noble eightfold path and its kin, all of it grounded in this same suchness. He notes that this sevenfold division simply distributes the four noble truths (and dependent origination) across the single reality of suchness, showing that "emptiness" is never an abstraction floating apart from ordinary suffering and its cure, but is discovered precisely within them.

For the final item, a buddha's inconceivable qualities, Cháng Bǎi treats the passage as a closing gesture toward an entire further body of teaching (developed at length elsewhere in the Bodhisattva Ground) rather than material to unpack term by term here: the thirty-two marks and eighty subsidiary marks are the physical splendor of a buddha's body (each mark tied to a specific quality of virtue accumulated over countless lifetimes — the webbed fingers, the wheel-marks on the soles, the crown protuberance, the between-the-brows tuft of white hair); the four purities cover the complete purification of a buddha's basis, objects, mind, and wisdom; the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, three foundations of mindfulness (never delighting, resenting, or feeling indifferent toward any of three types of disciples, however they practice), and three things requiring no guarding (a buddha's body, speech, and mind are naturally free of fault, unlike an ordinary being's, which lapses without constant vigilance) round out the classical enumeration of buddha-qualities found throughout Mahāyāna literature. He closes by noting the "and so forth" in the root text is deliberate: the Mahāyāna's fourteen categories, unlike the Śrāvakayāna's exactly-thirteen, are explicitly left open-ended, since the full scope of a buddha's qualities exceeds even this already-long list.

51. Delusion, Discursive Proliferation, the Stations of Consciousness, and Reality

The verse's remaining eleven terms move faster than "word" did, each typically answered in a single compact definition. The next four: delusion, restated as the four inversions already met in section 37; discursive proliferation, meaning affliction itself and the aggregates entangled with it; the specific stations where consciousness delights in dwelling in various cosmic realms; and reality, pointing back to suchness and the four noble truths already covered.

Chinese Source

云何迷惑？謂四顛倒。云何戲論？謂一切煩惱，及雜煩惱諸蘊。云何住？謂四識住，或七識住。云何真實？謂真如及四聖諦。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is delusion? The four inversions. What is discursive proliferation? All afflictions, and the aggregates entangled with affliction. What is abiding? The four stations of consciousness, or the seven stations of consciousness. What is reality? Suchness, and the four noble truths.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes "delusion" simply restates the same four inversions already unpacked at section 37 — mistaking the impermanent for permanent, suffering for pleasant, the impure for pure, and the selfless for a self — while "discursive proliferation" (戲論, literally "playful talk") names any mental activity that produces no genuine benefit: affliction itself is the paradigm case, and since the five aggregates both arise from affliction's operation and serve as its ongoing support and object, the aggregates too fall under the same label.

The seven stations of consciousness he treats as worth real attention, since they map an entire cosmology of where beings find their experience genuinely gratifying enough to want to stay. The first three concern realms where body and conception still vary in some combination — ordinary humans and most desire-realm gods, whose bodies and outlooks both differ from being to being; the first dhyāna's opening moment, where Brahmā and his retinue share one conceit ("I created these beings") despite differing in stature and glory; and the second dhyāna, where bodies are alike but conceptions still differ, illustrated by newly arrived gods worrying whether the great fire that ended the previous eon could reach them, while long-settled gods reassure them it cannot. The fourth is the third dhyāna, where both body and conception are finally uniform, its pervasive bliss the most complete pleasure any meditative state offers. The last three are the formless spheres of infinite space, infinite consciousness, and nothingness — each released by abandoning attachment to the one before it. Notably absent from the list is the fourth dhyāna and the two highest formless spheres, since beings there, having moved beyond ordinary delight in existence itself, are no longer said to "station" consciousness the same eager way.

52. Three Kinds of Purity, and Why the Three Jewels Are Called Supreme

The next two terms turn from cosmology back to soteriology: a threefold analysis of exactly what "purity" means across different contexts, and a brief explanation of why the three jewels in particular earn the label "sublime."

Chinese Source

云何淨？謂三清淨性。一、自體清淨性，二、境界清淨性，三、分位清淨性。云何妙？謂佛法僧寶名最微妙，墮最第一施設中故。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is purity? Three kinds of the nature of purity. First, the nature of intrinsic purity; second, the nature of the purity of [its] object; third, the nature of purity by stage. What is excellence? The jewels of the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṅgha are called the most subtle and excellent, because they fall within the very first rank of what can be designated [as excellent].

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps the three kinds of purity onto the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha*'s fuller fourfold scheme. Intrinsic purity (自體清淨性) is purity as suchness itself — emptiness, the true limit, the signless, the ultimate, the realm of phenomena — names for the same unchanging fact that all phenomena, realized or not, have always already been empty, neither arising nor ceasing, neither defiled nor pure, neither increasing nor decreasing. The purity of its object (境界清淨性) is the sublime Mahāyāna teaching itself, the sūtras and treatises that, taken as the object of insight-contemplation, actually purify the mind that studies them — a purity born from engaging the teaching, not the teaching's own intrinsic nature. Purity by stage (分位清淨性) compresses the *Saṃgraha*'s remaining two categories — the purity of having removed defilement (cessation, the fruit) and the purity of the path that removes it (the thirty-seven factors of enlightenment and the six perfections, the method) — noting that this purity comes in graded stages: a stream-enterer's purity is real but incomplete, a once-returner's more so, and so on up through full arhatship, each stage clearing away a further layer of the same original stain.

For "excellence," Cháng Bǎi keeps it brief: the three jewels earn the term for six classical reasons already touched on in this fascicle's earlier discussion (§44) — their rarity, their freedom from all stain, their inconceivable power, their capacity to adorn whoever holds them, their supremacy among all things, and their unshakeable constancy under any of the eight worldly winds. Because no other designation in the entire Buddhist teaching ranks higher, they alone are called "the most excellent within the very first rank of what can be so designated."

53. Tranquility, and the Five Aspects Under Which Anything Has a "Nature"

The ninth term of the twelve, "tranquility," names the entire path and its fruit in a single stroke; the tenth, "nature," opens a compact analytical toolkit — five distinct senses in which any phenomenon can be said to have a determinate character at all.

Chinese Source

云何寂靜？謂從善法欲，乃至一切菩提分法，及所得果，皆名寂靜。云何性？謂諸法體相。若自相、若共相、若假立相、若因相、若果相等。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is tranquility? From the wholesome aspiration [to practice] up through all the factors of enlightenment, together with the fruits thereby attained — all of this is called tranquility. What is nature? The intrinsic character of phenomena: whether their own particular characteristic, their characteristic held in common [with other phenomena], their provisionally posited characteristic, their characteristic as cause, or their characteristic as fruit, and so forth.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads "tranquility" as spanning the entire path from its very first stirring — the simple wish to renounce and practice — through every stage of cultivation (precepts, concentration, wisdom, the thirty-seven factors of enlightenment) and on to every fruit those practices yield, from stream-entry up through unsurpassed enlightenment itself. What unifies all of it under one name is a single function: each stage, in its own measure, stills the mind's turbulence and affliction a little further, so the entire path, start to finish, is nothing but progressive tranquility.

He treats "nature" as the treatise's own working vocabulary for talking about how things are structured, noting first that "nature" (性) and "characteristic" (相) are used near-interchangeably in Yogācāra texts — the imagined nature just is the imagined characteristic, fire's nature just is its characteristic of heat — except where a real distinction holds: a formless-realm being genuinely has aggregates (nature) with no visible form (no characteristic) at all, or an ox hidden behind a thicket is genuinely there (nature) though nothing visibly shows it (no characteristic) to an onlooker. The five listed senses then break "nature" down concretely: a particular characteristic (自相) is what marks a phenomenon off from every other kind of phenomenon — form's obstructiveness, cognition's capacity to discern; a common characteristic (共相) is shared across many things at once, as impermanence marks every conditioned phenomenon alike; a provisionally posited characteristic (假立相) exists only as a mental image conjured by naming, like a lotus visualized in the mind with no lotus actually present; a characteristic as cause (因相) is whatever karmic act sets a later result in motion; and a characteristic as fruit (果相) is that resulting ripening itself. He notes this fivefold scheme reappears, expanded to a full twenty-two-fold analysis, in the treatise's own Ground of What Is Attained Through Reflection still to come.

54. The Four Principles of Reasoning, Provisional Designation, and the Six Kinds of Direct Realization — the First Verse Closes

The final two terms complete the first of the treatise's four summarizing verses: the four fundamental principles by which any causal relationship can be understood at all, the two ways anything can be said to be merely "provisionally designated" rather than substantially real, and the six progressive stages by which a practitioner comes to directly realize the four noble truths.

Chinese Source

云何道理？謂諸緣起及四道理。云何假施設？謂於唯法假立補特伽羅，及於唯相假立諸法。云何現觀？謂六種現觀。如有尋有伺地已說。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is principle? Dependent origination, and the four principles [of reasoning]. What is provisional designation? Positing a person upon what is merely phenomena, and positing all phenomena upon what is merely characteristic-marks. What is direct realization? The six kinds of direct realization, as already explained in the Ground With Applied and Sustained Thought.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the four principles of reasoning (四道理) as the treatise's own compact logic toolkit, applicable to any claim whatsoever: correlation (觀待道理) — every phenomenon arises or is designated only in dependence on some further condition, nothing standing entirely on its own; function (作用道理) — every phenomenon has its own characteristic activity, the eye's seeing, water's flowing, earth's solidity; proof (證成道理) — any claim can be tested against direct perception, inference, or reliable scriptural testimony; and naturalness (法爾道理) — some facts (a mountain's height, fire's heat) simply are what they are, requiring no further "why," since to ask why fire is hot is to have already misunderstood what a brute fact is. He connects the twelve links of dependent origination directly back to this fascicle's earlier no-self teaching (§48 and its trisvabhāva discussion): once one sees that each link merely gives rise to the next, with no agent doing the giving or receiving, the entire question "who is reborn, who suffers" dissolves into the same insight reached from a different direction.

He glosses "provisional designation" as the technical heart of how emptiness and everyday language coexist: a person (補特伽羅) is a name laid over nothing but the bare causal flow of the twelve links — there being no substantial self anywhere to be found, only the label "person" applied to a process for convenience — while phenomena themselves (all the self-marks, common-marks, provisional-marks, cause-marks, and fruit-marks catalogued in the previous section) are likewise names laid over what consciousness itself displays, prior to and apart from any naming at all. Neither the person-designation nor the phenomena-designation points to something with genuine independent substance; both are, in the treatise's own phrase, "merely" so — real enough to function, empty enough that clinging to either is simply a mistake. Finally, he notes the six kinds of direct realization (思現觀/信現觀/戒現觀/現觀智諦現觀/現觀邊智諦現觀/究竟現觀— reflective, faith-based, precept-based, the direct realization of the truths themselves, the subsequently-attained wisdom that follows it, and the ultimate realization of full arhatship) were already named, without elaboration, back in fascicle 10's Ground With Applied and Sustained Thought, and are simply cross-referenced here rather than repeated. With this, the treatise's first summarizing verse — all twelve of its terms — is now completely explained.

55. The Second Verse Opens: The Five Aggregates Renamed by Their Defining Function

A second summarizing verse now organizes thirteen more terms, mostly returning to the five aggregates already analyzed at length in fascicle 3 — but this time each aggregate is given a fresh name that highlights its single most defining function, rather than its familiar Sanskrit label.

Chinese Source

復次，嗢柁南曰：方所位分別　作執持增減　冥言所覺上　遠離轉藏護。云何方所？謂色蘊。云何位？謂受蘊。云何分別？謂想蘊。云何作？謂行蘊。云何執持？謂識蘊。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, the summarizing verse says: "Location, stage, discrimination; making, holding-fast, increase and decrease; obscurity, speech, what is understood; the higher, seclusion, turning, and clinging-and-guarding." What is location? The aggregate of form. What is stage? The aggregate of feeling. What is discrimination? The aggregate of perception. What is making? The aggregate of formations. What is holding-fast? The aggregate of consciousness.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats each renaming as pointing at exactly what makes that aggregate distinctive. Form is called "location" because it alone, among the five, occupies actual physical space and location — the only aggregate you could point to and say "there." Feeling is called "stage" because pleasant, painful, and neutral feeling are really just successive stages a single stream of experience passes through moment to moment, arising and vanishing in constant transition. Perception is called "discrimination" because its entire function is cognitive sorting — recognizing a thing as small or vast, as this object rather than that one — a capacity Cháng Bǎi stresses is built entirely from learned concepts: without prior study, a mind literally cannot discriminate, which is why he calls right doctrinal knowledge itself a precondition for skillful perception, before it can ever be set aside in the higher, non-discriminating wisdom of realization.

Formations is called "making" because, of the roughly seventy-three mental factors this aggregate technically comprises, volition (思) — the factor that actually decides and drives action, whether wholesome, unwholesome, or neutral — is treated as its representative function; it is volition, not any of the other accompanying factors, that genuinely "makes" karma. And consciousness is called "holding-fast" because, of the eight consciousnesses, the ālaya-vijñāna specifically is what holds every karmic seed in store and keeps the physical sense faculties from simply decaying — without its continuous grip, Cháng Bǎi notes bluntly, a living body would collapse into a corpse on the spot. He offers a simple summary distinguishing the first three ordinary-language equivalents: feeling is emotional response (delight, aversion), perception is acquired knowledge (the concepts that make judgment possible), and formations is the will itself (the decision to act) — three quite different functions that ordinary experience blends together seamlessly.

56. Increase and Decrease, the Darkness of Ignorance and Doubt, Speech, and What Is Understood

Four more terms follow: how affliction and karma each grow stronger or weaker over time, the specific "darkness" of ignorance and doubt that obstructs wisdom, the Buddha's teaching classified into twelve genres, and the simple relation between what is spoken and what is grasped by hearing it.

Chinese Source

云何增？謂有二種。一、煩惱增，二、業增。如增有二種，當知減亦爾。云何冥？謂無明及疑。云何言？謂諸如來十二分教，說名為言。云何所覺？謂彼彼言音所說之義，名為所覺。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is increase? There are two kinds: the increase of affliction, and the increase of karma. As increase has two kinds, know that decrease is likewise so. What is darkness? Ignorance and doubt. What is speech? The Tathāgata's twelve divisions of teaching are called speech. What is that which is understood? The meaning spoken through this or that utterance is called that which is understood.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads "increase" and "decrease" as a simple, universal law of habituation applied to two things at once: every fresh burst of anger perfumes a corresponding seed in the storehouse-consciousness, making the next burst of anger easier and stronger — and precisely the same mechanism strengthens a wholesome habit, such as recurring generosity or repeated study of the teaching. Decrease is nothing more than the same mechanism run in reverse, whether through the direct power of meditative concentration and the noble path suppressing an affliction outright, or simply through sustained correct attention gradually starving it before concentration is even attained; karma, too, weakens when its causes and conditions are no longer nourished by careless repetition.

"Darkness" names the two afflictions Cháng Bǎi treats as uniquely blinding: ignorance (無明), which he divides into the ignorance grasping a self (whether within or apart from the five aggregates) and the ignorance grasping phenomena as substantially real, the latter specifically obstructing omniscient wisdom; and doubt, a chronic hesitation over the four noble truths that blocks wholesome states from ever fully arising. He offers the frank observation that only those of the very highest or very lowest capacity are immune to doubt's paralysis — the wise see clearly enough not to waver, and the simple simply follow instruction without questioning — while most practitioners, holding just enough insight to sense a problem but not enough to resolve it, oscillate in genuine uncertainty; doubt of this kind, he adds, is not itself blameworthy so much as a sign that more diligent inquiry, not less, is called for.

"Speech" and "what is understood" he treats as a simple pair: the Buddha's entire body of teaching, formally divided into twelve literary genres (discourse, verse-response, prophecy, restated verse, unprompted teaching, causal narrative, parable, past-life account of a disciple, past-life account of the Buddha himself, extensive teaching, marvelous account, and formal treatise — themselves reducible to sūtra, vinaya, and treatise, as already covered at §43), constitutes speech in the fullest sense; and whatever meaning that speech actually conveys to a listener's understanding is, correspondingly, what is understood — the content side of the same single transaction language always performs.

57. The Higher Fruits, Five Kinds of Seclusion, Transmigration, and Clinging to the Three Times — the Second Verse Closes

The verse's final four terms close with genuinely practical content: the four fruits that place a practitioner above ordinary beings, the five concrete forms a renunciant's seclusion takes, the endless wandering through the three realms and five destinies, and the specific way craving attaches itself to past, future, and present alike.

Chinese Source

云何上？謂四沙門果。云何遠離？謂五種遠離。一、惡行遠離，二、欲遠離，三、資具遠離，四、憒鬧遠離，五、煩惱遠離。云何轉？謂三界、五趣。云何藏護？謂追戀過去，希慕未來，耽著現在。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the higher? The four fruits of the ascetic path. What is seclusion? Five kinds of seclusion: first, seclusion from wrongdoing; second, seclusion from desire; third, seclusion from [attachment to] requisites; fourth, seclusion from clamor; fifth, seclusion from affliction. What is turning? The three realms and the five destinies. What is clinging-and-guarding? Longing after the past, yearning for the future, and being engrossed in the present.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses "the higher" briefly as the four fruits (stream-entry through arhatship) already covered at §44, earning the name simply because a genuine noble one now stands above ordinary, unawakened beings. The five kinds of seclusion he unpacks as a graded, mutually supporting sequence: seclusion from wrongdoing (keeping precepts, whichever level one has undertaken, and thereby leaving the ten unwholesome paths of action behind); seclusion from desire (cultivating concentration to loosen sexual craving specifically); seclusion from requisites (the austere ascetic practices — eating only what is begged, only once a day, wearing only cast-off cloth, dwelling under a tree or in a charnel ground, and similar disciplines — that free one from attachment even to necessities like food, clothing, and shelter, a discipline he notes is genuinely difficult and requires concentration and wisdom already in place to sustain); seclusion from clamor (dwelling in a genuinely quiet place, since even two people sharing a room can generate constant noise if their aims diverge, while two people equally devoted to the path can share space without disturbance); and finally seclusion from affliction itself (using calm to support insight until the afflictions of the three realms are actually severed) — each stage, he stresses, quietly depending on the one before it.

"Turning" he treats as simply naming the raw fact of transmigration through the three realms and five destinies, illustrated with a story about a grieving widower who begged an arhat to show him where his beloved wife had been reborn — only to be led to a dung-heap beetle who, when made to speak by the arhat's power, showed no recognition of him at all, saying only that she had no time to talk, since she and her new husband beetle had dung still to roll. The abrupt shock of the encounter, Cháng Bǎi notes, finally let the man release a grief he had carried for years — a blunt illustration of how little the bonds people cling to across one life actually carry into the next. He closes with an equally pointed cautionary tale about a man who conceived an impure thought toward the elegant, handsome Elder Kātyāyana and found his own body suddenly transformed into a woman's, living out an entire subsequent life, marriage, and family before eventually confessing to the elder and being restored — a story Cháng Bǎi reads not as fanciful but as making vivid exactly what "clinging-and-guarding" (藏護) means: attachment to one's own continuity (自體愛) and to what belongs to it (境界愛), whether nostalgically toward the past, hopefully toward the future, or complacently toward the present, is itself the very engine of the "turning" just described — and only complete release from that three-fold clinging brings the wandering to rest.

58. The Third Verse Opens: Six Logical Forms for Clarifying Doctrine Through Question and Answer

A third summarizing verse opens with its first and, as it turns out, most technically elaborate term: "investigation" (思擇), the discipline of thinking through what has been heard rather than simply accepting it. The treatise's own commentarial tradition (論) works largely through structured question-and-answer, and this section names the actual logical patterns that debate takes — a genuine toolkit for clarifying any doctrinal claim with precision.

Chinese Source

復次，嗢柁南曰：思擇與現行　睡眠及相屬　諸相攝相應　說任持次第。云何思擇？謂一行、順前句、順後句、四句、無事句。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, the summarizing verse says: "Investigation and manifestation; sleep, and mutual relation; comprehension, correspondence; speaking, sustaining, and sequence." What is investigation? A single series, the forward-conforming statement, the backward-conforming statement, the four-cornered statement, and the groundless statement.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through each logical form as a distinct tool for a distinct situation. A "single series" (一行) simply cross-questions one doctrine against every other in turn — is impermanence suffering? Is impermanence empty? Is impermanence selfless? — methodically working through the whole doctrinal field one pairing at a time. The "forward-conforming statement" (順前句) handles two terms of unequal scope by answering according to the narrower, first-mentioned term: since all craving is a form of attachment but not all attachment is craving, the answer to "is all craving attachment, and is all attachment craving?" conforms to the first, narrower term — yes to the first half, only partly to the second. The "backward-conforming statement" (順後句) is its mirror: since all suffering is impermanent but not everything impermanent is suffering (the path itself is impermanent yet not suffering), the answer conforms instead to the broader, second-mentioned term.

The "four-cornered statement" (四句) is reserved for two terms whose scope overlaps only partially — his worked example being desire-realm feeling and form-realm feeling, where a being can have neither, either, or both depending on whether they are simply born in the desire realm, born in the form realm, have attained form-realm concentration while still embodied in the desire realm, or have gone entirely beyond both through arhatship, the formless attainments, or realization itself — requiring all four logically possible combinations to be stated explicitly rather than assumed. And the "groundless statement" (無事句), which he also calls the "blocking statement," handles a question that was malformed from the start: when non-Buddhist debaters ask whether the self is identical to the body, different from the body, both, or neither, the fourth option is not a real fourth possibility at all but merely different words repeating the same underlying (and mistaken) assumption that a substantial self exists to begin with — the answer doesn't add new content, it simply blocks the question's false premise. Cháng Bǎi adds that ordinary life offers the same discipline: asking "is everyone physically strong also strong-willed, and vice versa?" and working out all four honest combinations is exactly this same four-cornered method, applied outside any monastery.

59. Investigation Continues: Seventy-One Paired Categories Spanning the Entire Field of Phenomena

Having covered the logical forms investigation can take, the treatise turns to what investigation actually investigates — an extended catalogue of seventy-one technical categories, given almost entirely as paired opposites, covering essentially every angle from which any phenomenon, conditioned or unconditioned, can be classified. Most of this vocabulary has already appeared piecemeal across earlier fascicles; here it is gathered into one systematic sweep.

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also: phenomena with form, and without form; visible, and invisible; resistant, and non-resistant; with outflow, and without outflow; conditioned, and unconditioned; contentious, and non-contentious; with attachment-savor, and without attachment-savor; dependent on indulgence, and dependent on renunciation; worldly, and world-transcending; bound [to the three realms], and unbound; internal, and external; coarse, and subtle; inferior, and superior; distant, and near; with an object, and without an object; conjoined, and unconjoined; with activity, and without activity; with support, and without support; causal, and non-causal; resultant, and non-resultant; ripened, and unripened; with a cause, and without a cause; with a fruit, and without a fruit; with ripening, and without ripening; appropriated, and unappropriated; made of the great elements, and not made of the great elements; of shared category, and of that same category [without the corresponding consciousness]; with something higher, and unsurpassed; and further: past, future, and present; wholesome, unwholesome, and indeterminate; bound to the desire realm, the form realm, and the formless realm; belonging to the trainee, the adept beyond training, and neither; to be severed by the path of seeing, to be severed by the path of cultivation, and not to be severed at all.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this seventy-one-item list as a comprehensive index rather than seventy-one new doctrines, since nearly every pairing recycles categories the treatise has already built up across its first thirteen fascicles: form and mind, the visible and invisible aspects of matter, the desire/form/formless bindings, past/future/present, and wholesome/unwholesome/indeterminate have all appeared repeatedly since fascicle 3. He singles out only a handful worth fresh comment. "Ripened" (異熟法), stripped of everything else it might mean, points specifically at the ālaya-vijñāna itself — identified by four marks: it is the very last moment of consciousness at death, the very first moment of consciousness knitting a new rebirth together, the continuous background awareness that persists from conception to death beneath the six active consciousnesses' comings and goings, and the one element of a person's makeup that never changes realm even after that person attains a higher meditative state while still embodied in a lower realm. "Appropriated" (有執受法) picks out exactly which parts of the body the ālaya-vijñāna actively animates — illustrated by the simple observation that cutting already-dead hair or overgrown nail feels nothing, because those portions have fallen outside the ālaya's living grip, while the attached, sensate body has not.

He also flags "the groundless statement's" logical cousin here: "with a fruit" and "without a fruit" (有果法/非有果法) apply not only to ripening karma in general but pointedly to two failure modes worth remembering — a Buddhist practitioner who has entered the gate but never applies sustained, diligent effort will not reach the fruit despite proximity to the teaching, exactly as a non-Buddhist ascetic's pointless austerities (his image: trying to press oil from sand) can never yield any fruit at all, however sincere the effort, because the method itself contains no causal link to the result sought. Real practice, he stresses, requires both the correct method and its sustained application — either alone is insufficient. Given the sheer density of this catalogue, he directs anyone wanting fuller technical detail on any single pairing to the treatise's own later Compendium of Determinations, where each is unpacked at far greater length than the root verse itself allows.

60. The Four Conditions, and the Four Reliances — "Investigation" Concludes

"Investigation" closes with two more compact frameworks before a final list of practice-categories and a summarizing statement. The four conditions name what any arising phenomenon depends on; the four reliances name the classical guideposts by which a practitioner should orient their study of the teaching itself — arguably the single most quoted formula in this whole section.

Chinese Source

又有四緣。謂因緣、等無間緣、所緣緣、增上緣。又有四依。一、法是依，非補特伽羅；二、義是依，非文；三、了義經是依，非不了義；四、智是依，非識。如是等法無量無邊，應當思擇。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also four conditions: the causal condition, the condition of equal and uninterrupted succession, the condition of the object, and the dominant condition. There are also four reliances: first, one relies on the Dharma, not on the person; second, one relies on the meaning, not on the letter; third, one relies on the definitive teaching, not on the provisional teaching; fourth, one relies on wisdom, not on [ordinary] consciousness. Phenomena such as these are boundless and beyond measure, and should be thoroughly investigated.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi illustrates the four conditions with eye-consciousness: its own karmic seed in the storehouse-consciousness is its causal condition; the immediately preceding moment of eye-consciousness, which must cease to make room, is its condition of equal and uninterrupted succession; the visible form it cognizes is its condition as object; and the eye faculty itself, without whose functioning support no seeing could occur at all, is its dominant condition — together accounting completely for why any single moment of cognition occurs at all.

He treats the four reliances as guidance every student of the Dharma needs precisely because devotion so easily misfires. Relying on the Dharma, not the person, means the teaching's truth does not depend on the moral perfection or charisma of whoever happens to be teaching it — a corrupt or mistaken teacher does not corrupt genuine Dharma, and conversely an admired teacher's personal authority is never itself sufficient grounds for accepting what they say. Relying on the meaning, not the letter, means study cannot stop at merely having heard or memorized the words; the point is always the doctrine those words point toward, which requires continued reflection to actually grasp. Relying on the definitive teaching, not the provisional, means recognizing that the Buddha, addressing beings of differing readiness, sometimes taught partial or preparatory truths suited to a given audience's capacity (guiding some merely toward a favorable rebirth) and sometimes taught the complete, final truth leading all the way to liberation — and a serious practitioner must learn to tell which is which rather than treating every teaching as equally final. And relying on wisdom, not on ordinary discriminating consciousness, means not trusting one's own untrained, habitually self-serving judgment as the final word — Cháng Bǎi cites the Buddha's own blunt warning to never trust one's own mind until it has become an arhat's, since an unawakened mind reliably talks itself out of exactly the practices that would purify it. He closes by noting the closing list of practice-categories that follows (the four immeasurables, four foundations of mindfulness, and the remainder of the thirty-seven factors of enlightenment, together with the eight liberations, eight bases of mastery, and ten totalities already covered in fascicles 11–12) is offered without fresh elaboration precisely because it has already been explained at length — it stands here only to round out "investigation" into a complete map of everything a serious practitioner is expected to examine, closing this first, largest term of the third verse with the simple instruction that all of it, without limit, calls for the same careful, patient thinking-through.

61. Manifest Affliction, Dormant Affliction, and Three Kinds of Mutual Relation

The verse's remaining terms move at the brisker pace typical of the second verse. First, a pairing already implicit in this fascicle's account of the ālaya-vijñāna: affliction actively binding the mind versus the same affliction lying dormant as an unmanifest seed. Then, three distinct senses in which one thing can be said to be "related" to another.

Chinese Source

云何現行？謂諸煩惱纏。云何睡眠？謂諸煩惱隨眠。云何相屬？謂內六處於一身中，當知展轉互相繫屬。又若此法能引彼法，當知此彼互相繫屬。又諸根境，當知能取、所取互相繫屬。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is manifestation? The afflictions that bind [the mind]. What is dormancy? The afflictions lying latent [as seeds]. What is mutual relation? The six inner sense-bases within a single body should be known to be mutually related to one another, turn by turn. Furthermore, if one phenomenon is able to draw forth another, know that the two are mutually related. Furthermore, the faculties and their objects should be known to be mutually related as grasper and grasped.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats "manifestation" and "dormancy" as simply the active and latent phases of one and the same affliction: bound up and operating, an affliction is said to "manifest" or "entangle" the mind like a rope wound tight; quiescent and un-triggered, that identical affliction is called "dormant," precisely as a sleeper who has not yet woken — an ordinary being whose afflictions are momentarily inactive is, in this sense, no different from one merely asleep, still entirely capable of waking, while only a noble one who has severed the seed itself is free of the dormancy as well as the manifestation.

He unpacks the three kinds of mutual relation as covering the entire causal web binding one part of experience to another. The six sense-faculties are related to each other within one body because they habitually cooperate — seeing a cake stirs an interest in its smell, which stirs an interest in its taste, so the eye, nose, and tongue draw each other into activity through the coordinating work of the sixth consciousness. Two things are related as "drawing forth" when one simply causes the other, exactly as each link of dependent origination gives rise to the next — ignorance conditioning formations, formations conditioning consciousness, and onward to old age and death. And faculty and object are related as grasper and grasped — the eye reaching for form, the ear for sound — the most basic relation of all, without which no cognition could occur.

62. Sixteen Ways to Comprehend the Aggregates, Elements, and Sense-Fields

The next term, "comprehension," gathers sixteen distinct analytical lenses through which the treatise's basic building blocks — the aggregates, elements, and sense-fields — can each be classified and understood, effectively a toolkit for approaching the same material from sixteen different angles.

Chinese Source

云何攝？謂十六種攝。一、界攝，二、相攝，三、種類攝，四、分位攝，五、不相離攝，六、時攝，七、方攝，八、一分攝，九、具分攝，十、勝義攝，十一、蘊攝，十二、界攝，十三、處攝，十四、緣起攝，十五、處非處攝，十六、根攝。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is comprehension? Sixteen kinds of comprehension. First, comprehension by [seed-]element; second, comprehension by characteristic; third, comprehension by category; fourth, comprehension by stage; fifth, comprehension by inseparability; sixth, comprehension by time; seventh, comprehension by place; eighth, comprehension by a single part; ninth, comprehension in full; tenth, comprehension by ultimate meaning; eleventh, comprehension by the aggregates; twelfth, comprehension by the elements; thirteenth, comprehension by the sense-fields; fourteenth, comprehension by dependent origination; fifteenth, comprehension by what is and is not the case; sixteenth, comprehension by the faculties.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi first explains why the treatise even offers three overlapping vocabularies (five aggregates, twelve sense-fields, eighteen elements) for the same body-mind complex: different audiences need different granularity — those confused about the elements specifically are taught all eighteen; those confused about the sense-fields, the twelve; those confused merely about the aggregates, the simpler five — the same teaching adjusted to whichever confusion is actually operative, whether keyed to sharpness of faculty, degree of prior training, or which specific conceit (caste pride, wealth pride, or vitality pride) needs puncturing.

Of the sixteen lenses this term names, he groups the first ten as general-purpose tools applicable to any of the three vocabularies alike: by seed-element (everything ultimately traces to its karmic seed); by characteristic (each aggregate's own particular nature — form's obstructiveness, feeling's registering — alongside the common characteristics like impermanence shared by all); by category (grouping like with like, earth/water/fire/wind together as form, the various feelings together as feeling); by stage (how a given aggregate's condition shifts as it correlates with pleasant, painful, or neutral feeling); by inseparability (no single aggregate ever exists alone — form always brings feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness along as its inseparable companions, each aggregate in some sense containing all the others); by time (past grounding present, present grounding future); by place (where a given cognition actually arises); by a single part (as when only one portion of the sense-field of mental objects — form perceptible only to mind — counts under a narrower heading); by fullness (when an entire category, with nothing left out, is meant); and by ultimate meaning (all of it, seen through emptiness, reduces to suchness). The last six he treats as simply naming the six classificatory schemes themselves — the aggregates, elements, sense-fields, dependent origination, the distinction between what is and is not logically the case, and the twenty-two faculties (the six sense-faculties, the five wholesome roots, the five feeling-faculties, the life-faculty, the male and female faculties, and the three faculties tracking progress toward and through liberation) — each one, in its own right, a complete way of comprehending the whole of experience.

63. Five Conditions for Genuine Correspondence, and Four Origins of Speech

Next, the treatise specifies exactly what must be true before mind and its accompanying mental factors can be said to genuinely "correspond" with one another — a more demanding standard than mere co-occurrence — followed by a simple fourfold account of where human speech actually comes from.

Chinese Source

云何相應？當知此相略有五種。一、與他性相應，非自性；二、於他性中，與不相違相應，非相違；三、於不相違中，軟中上品與軟中上品自相應，非餘品；四、於軟中上品中，同時相應，非異時；五、於同時中，同地相應，非異地。云何說？謂四種言說。一、見言說，二、聞言說，三、覺言說，四、知言說。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is correspondence? Know that, briefly, this characteristic has five aspects. First, it corresponds with another nature, not with its own nature. Second, among other natures, it corresponds with what does not conflict, not with what conflicts. Third, among what does not conflict, an inferior, middling, or superior grade corresponds with its own matching grade, not with any other grade. Fourth, among grades, it corresponds within the same time, not a different time. Fifth, within the same time, it corresponds within the same realm, not a different realm. What is speaking? Four kinds of verbal expression. First, speech from seeing; second, speech from hearing; third, speech from sensing; fourth, speech from knowing.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through the five conditions as a genuinely precise technical account of what "mind and mental factors correspond" actually requires, beyond simply occurring together. First, correspondence only ever holds between the mind and something other than itself — faith, shame, greed, or anger accompanying consciousness, never consciousness accompanying its own bare self. Second, the accompanying factors must not conflict with one another — greed and hatred cannot arise in the very same moment, though greed and stinginess, being compatible, readily can, just as two people of incompatible temperament or background rarely feel at ease together while two of similar disposition do. Third, mind and its factors must match in intensity — a mildly wholesome thought pairs only with mildly wholesome accompanying factors, not with an intensely powerful one. Fourth, they must occur in strictly the same moment, never one in the past cueing one in the present. And fifth, they must belong to the same cosmic realm — a desire-realm mind corresponds only with desire-realm feeling, a first-dhyāna joy only with first-dhyāna concentration, never mixing across realms. Taken together, these five conditions define precisely what "one coherent moment of mental experience" actually consists of.

For the four origins of speech, he offers everyday illustrations: speech from seeing is simply reporting what the eye witnessed — "I saw so-and-so this morning"; speech from hearing reports what was heard, as when repeating a teaching just received; speech from sensing covers judgments reached through reflection rather than direct sense-report, as when deciding how a flower arrangement ought to look and saying so; and speech from knowing, read most broadly, covers what is grasped through smell, taste, and touch, through the five accompanying-consciousness's judgments, or — on an alternate classical reading — the direct report of pleasure and pain themselves, of worldly or supramundane attainment, of a meditative breakthrough newly touched, or of a fruit of the path newly gained. Between them, the four origins account for the entire basis on which anyone ever has something worth saying at all.

64. The Four Foods That Sustain Life, Six Kinds of Sequence, and a Third Verse Complete

The third verse closes with its final two terms: the four kinds of nourishment already surveyed at length back in fascicle 5, now revisited briefly under the heading "sustaining"; and six distinct senses in which anything at all — from saṃsāra itself down to a simple flower arrangement — can be said to unfold in a genuine, necessary sequence. With this, all nine terms of the third summarizing verse are complete.

Chinese Source

云何任持？謂四食。一、段食，二、觸食，三、意思食，四、識食。云何次第？謂六種次第。一、流轉次第，二、成所作次第，三、宣說次第，四、生起次第，五、現觀次第，六、等至次第。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is sustaining? The four kinds of food. First, edible food; second, contact as food; third, mental volition as food; fourth, consciousness as food. What is sequence? Six kinds of sequence. First, the sequence of flux; second, the sequence of accomplishing a task; third, the sequence of exposition; fourth, the sequence of arising; fifth, the sequence of direct realization; sixth, the sequence of meditative attainment.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the four foods as already fully explained in fascicle 5's account of 受用飲食 and cross-references it directly rather than repeating: edible food sustains the body in coarse, portioned meals; contact as food nourishes through the sheer pleasure, joy, or equanimity of sensory engagement itself; mental volition as food nourishes through hopeful anticipation of a desired future; and consciousness as food — the ālaya-vijñāna — is strengthened by, and in turn strengthens, all three of the others, the deepest and most continuous nourishment of all. He simply notes here that all four together are exactly what a genuinely liberated arhat has severed craving for.

The six kinds of sequence he treats as a satisfying close to the entire verse, moving from the cosmic to the utterly ordinary. The sequence of flux is dependent origination's own strict causal order, each link the condition for the next. The sequence of accomplishing a task is the sequence any skilled work follows — his example is flower arranging, which needs expertise, sustained effort, and the right materials gathered together before a single arrangement can be completed. The sequence of exposition is the treatise's own three-part method for explaining any teaching clearly — stating the thesis, then unpacking what that thesis actually means, then explaining why it holds — illustrated by the Buddha's own account of his youth: first stating simply that his early life was supremely magnificent, then explaining what that magnificence consisted in (royal birth, a radiant body, robust health, sharp intelligence, every sensory pleasure available), and finally explaining why, precisely amid such magnificence, he chose to renounce it all — because he had seen aging, sickness, and death, and sought the path that ends them. The sequence of arising traces how a single moment of experience actually comes to be, from its karmic seed through its supporting faculties, companion mental factors, and object, to its final arising as a specific mind bound to one realm or utterly unbound. The sequence of direct realization is the six-stage progression toward full liberation already named at §54. And the sequence of meditative attainment is the nine successive absorptions already surveyed across fascicles 11 and 12, each building on the one before it rather than being reached all at once. With this sixth and final sequence named, the third summarizing verse — all nine of its terms — is now completely explained.

65. The Fourth and Final Verse Opens: Eight Accomplishments and Four Objects of Meditation

The treatise's fourth and last summarizing verse turns from abstract classification to the direct, practical vocabulary of meditation itself — beginning with eight concrete accomplishments a practitioner works through and attains, and four distinct kinds of object a practitioner actually takes up in calm and insight practice.

Chinese Source

復次，嗢柁南曰：所作及所緣　亦瑜伽止觀　作意與教授　德菩提聖教。云何所作？謂八種所作。一、滅依止，二、轉依止，三、遍知所緣，四、喜樂所緣，五、得果，六、離欲，七、轉根，八、引發神通。云何所緣？謂四種所緣。一、遍滿所緣，二、淨行所緣，三、善巧所緣，四、淨煩惱所緣。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, the summarizing verse says: "Accomplishment and its object; likewise yoga, calm, and insight; attention and instruction; virtue, enlightenment, and the sacred teaching." What is accomplishment? Eight kinds. First, extinguishing the [afflicted] basis; second, transforming the basis; third, thoroughly knowing the object; fourth, delighting in the object; fifth, attaining the fruit; sixth, becoming free of desire; seventh, transforming the faculty; eighth, eliciting superknowledge. What is the object [of practice]? Four kinds of object. First, the all-pervading object; second, the object that purifies conduct; third, the skillful object; fourth, the object that purifies affliction.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi divides the eight accomplishments into a cause-and-fruit pair of four. The first four are what sustained practice itself accomplishes: extinguishing the afflicted basis (worldly concentration gradually thins the body-mind's coarse burden; the noble path uproots the very seed); transforming the basis (that same body-mind gradually gains pliancy and settled joy, culminating — in Mahāyāna practice specifically — in the "transformation of consciousness into wisdom" that is enlightenment itself); thoroughly knowing the object (the arising wisdom that comprehends an object's particular and common characteristics, its full extent and true nature); and delighting in the object (the simple, genuine gladness of engaging in calm and insight practice, rather than enduring it as a chore). The remaining four are what that practice yields as fruit: attaining the fruits of the path from stream-entry to arhatship; becoming free of desire, whether worldly (the four dhyānas releasing desire-realm craving, the four formless attainments releasing form-realm craving) or supramundane (the noble path releasing craving for all three realms at once, complete only in the third and fourth fruits); transforming one's own faculty — sharpening a dull disposition into a keen one through repeated practice, or, on a further reading, even a change of physical sex, both counted as the same underlying phenomenon of a faculty being genuinely remade; and eliciting the superknowledges through the eight liberations, eight bases of mastery, and ten totalities already surveyed in fascicles 11 and 12.

The four objects he reads as a practical toolkit matched to different needs. The all-pervading object covers the full range any meditator's attention can take — an image still colored by conceptual discrimination (insight's domain), an image free of such discrimination (calm's domain), the exhaustive investigation of a topic's full extent and true nature, and the accomplished fruit that results. The object that purifies conduct is the Tiantai tradition's well-known "five methods for stilling the mind" matched to whichever affliction dominates a given practitioner — contemplating the body's impurity for strong desire, loving-kindness for strong hostility, breath-counting for a scattered mind, dependent origination for entrenched confusion, and the analysis into elements for stubborn pride — each a targeted antidote rather than a one-size-fits-all method. The skillful object is simply the six kinds of technical mastery (over the aggregates, elements, sense-fields, dependent origination, what is and is not the case, and the faculties) already introduced at §62. And the object that purifies affliction covers both the worldly method (contrasting a lower realm's coarseness against a higher realm's refinement to motivate ascent) and the supramundane method (the four noble truths or twelve links of dependent origination, which alone actually severs affliction's root rather than merely outgrowing it).

66. Yoga Itself Defined: Four Qualities That Drive Practice, and Nine Stages the Path Passes Through

The verse's third term finally defines "yoga" (瑜伽) itself — the very word that gives this treatise its name — as the union of practice with reality that the three vehicles' calm-and-insight training aims at. The treatise offers two complementary breakdowns: four inner qualities that make genuine practice possible at all, and nine stages that any path of practice necessarily passes through.

Chinese Source

云何瑜伽？謂或四種，或九種。四種瑜伽者，一、信，二、欲，三、精進，四、方便。九種瑜伽者，一、世間道，二、出世道，三、方便道，四、無間道，五、解脫道，六、勝進道，七、軟品道，八、中品道，九、上品道。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is yoga? Either four kinds, or nine kinds. The four kinds of yoga: first, faith; second, aspiration; third, diligence; fourth, skillful means. The nine kinds of yoga: first, the worldly path; second, the supramundane path; third, the preparatory path; fourth, the uninterrupted path; fifth, the path of liberation; sixth, the path of further advancement; seventh, the inferior-grade path; eighth, the middling-grade path; ninth, the superior-grade path.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the four qualities as what must be genuinely present, in this order, before practice can even begin: faith (conviction in the teaching's own logic, and confidence in a teacher's or fellow practitioner's spiritual capacity); aspiration (a real wish — to attain concentration or the path itself, to ask how it is properly done, to gather the supporting conditions such as precepts and provisions, and to feel actual eagerness for the practice once undertaken, not mere obligation); diligence (four kinds again: to hear teachings not yet heard and perfect what has already been heard, to reflect privately on their meaning until it is genuinely understood, to actually sit down and practice calm and insight at set times, and to purify the mind of obstruction through sustained daytime and nighttime effort); and skillful means (the concrete supports that make sustained practice possible — pure precepts freeing the mind from guilt and worry, correct mindfulness sustained moment to moment, freedom from carelessness, and the actual application of calm and insight). Faith opens the door, he notes, but without aspiration, diligence, and skillful means to follow, faith alone accomplishes nothing.

The nine-stage breakdown he reads as tracking the path's own internal architecture. The worldly path is ordinary meditative training that may or may not sever affliction and may lead to a favorable or unfavorable rebirth depending on how it is used; the supramundane path is the four truths, dependent origination, or the emptiness of self and phenomena, which alone genuinely uproots affliction. Beneath those two headings sit a finer-grained sequence already implicit in the seven attentions surveyed back in fascicles 11–12: the preparatory path (still gathering the conditions for practice, corresponding to the earlier "grasping the character" and "resolute understanding" attentions); the uninterrupted path (actively engaged in severing a given affliction right now, corresponding to the "attention of withdrawal," "attention that gathers joy," and the beginning of "the attention that consummates effort"); the path of liberation (the affliction just targeted has actually been severed and the mind tastes freedom from it, corresponding to the completed "attention that consummates effort and its fruit"); and the path of further advancement (having gained that freedom, pressing on to the next attention or the next dhyāna rather than resting where one is). Finally, the inferior, middling, and superior grades name the graduated strength of the antidote applied at each stage — a mild method that can only dislodge the coarsest afflictions, a moderate method for the middling ones, and the fullest method, reserved for the subtlest residue, that alone can finish the work. Cháng Bǎi treats this ninefold map as simply the four- and seven-attention frameworks already covered, now given their most granular and technical vocabulary.

67. Calm Revisited, and Insight's Two Preliminary Schemes

The verse's fourth and fifth terms name calm and insight themselves — the twin engines of the entire meditative path this treatise has surveyed since fascicle 11. Calm is answered with a single cross-reference; insight, by contrast, is broken into three distinct analytical schemes of increasing detail, the first two of which the treatise now explains.

Chinese Source

云何止？謂九種住心。云何觀？謂或三事觀，或四行觀，或六事差別所緣觀。三事觀者，一、有相觀，二、尋求觀，三、伺察觀。四行觀者，謂於諸法中，簡擇行觀、極簡擇行觀、遍尋思行觀、遍伺察行觀。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is calm? The nine stages of settling the mind. What is insight? Either the threefold contemplation, the fourfold practice of contemplation, or the contemplation differentiated by six matters. The threefold contemplation: first, contemplation through the mere mark; second, contemplation through seeking; third, contemplation through investigation. The fourfold practice of contemplation: within all phenomena, the practice of discernment, the practice of utmost discernment, the practice of pervasive seeking, and the practice of pervasive investigation.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes calm is answered here purely by cross-reference to the nine stages of settling the mind (內住/等住/安住/近住/調順/寂靜/最極寂靜/專注一趣/等持) already assumed throughout this fascicle's account of meditative equipoise, since nothing new needs adding to what fascicles 11–13 have already laid out at length.

The threefold contemplation, he explains, tracks insight's own natural deepening over time: contemplation through the mere mark is a first, still-shallow engagement with an object — noticed, but not yet measured, weighed, or examined; contemplation through seeking pushes further, actively investigating an object not yet well understood in order to grasp it clearly for the first time; and contemplation through investigation goes further still, returning to re-examine an object already basically understood in order to secure it with full, settled certainty. The fourfold scheme, drawn from a slightly different technical vocabulary in the Śrāvaka Ground, covers essentially the same three-stage deepening but adds one further distinction of scope: discernment investigates an object's full extent (its 盡所有性, everything that can be said about it); utmost discernment investigates that same object's true nature (its 如所有性, what it actually is, down to its common characteristics like emptiness and suchness); pervasive seeking is the coarser, exploratory sweep across the full range of that object's features; and pervasive investigation is the finer, more penetrating re-examination that follows. Cháng Bǎi treats both schemes as different lenses on the identical underlying process — a mind moving from first noticing, through active search, to settled, confirmed understanding.

68. Six Angles of Insight, Worked Through a Single Example: Contemplating the Body's Impurity

Insight's third and final scheme names six distinct angles from which any single meditation object can be examined — and the treatise's own commentarial tradition illustrates all six using one concrete, classical practice: the contemplation of the body's impurity, a method already prescribed back in this fascicle's account of the five methods for a scattered or desire-ridden mind.

Chinese Source

六事差別所緣觀者，一、義所緣觀，二、事所緣觀，三、相所緣觀，四、品所緣觀，五、時所緣觀，六、道理所緣觀。

Ru-Yi Translation

The contemplation differentiated by six matters: first, contemplation of the object as meaning; second, contemplation of the object as a concrete matter; third, contemplation of the object as characteristic-mark; fourth, contemplation of the object as moral category; fifth, contemplation of the object across time; sixth, contemplation of the object as principle.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through all six using the impurity contemplation as a single running example. Contemplation as meaning asks simply what the taught words actually mean — hearing that the body is "impure, foul-smelling, decaying," and coming to genuinely understand what that description is claiming, rather than merely repeating the phrase; he treats this first angle as effectively the summary of all six that follow. Contemplation as a concrete matter turns from the abstract meaning to the actual thing in front of one: the living body's own impure contents (hair, nails, teeth, down to waste), and, in its more advanced form, a corpse's visible decay — bruised, swollen, decomposing — observed stage by stage. Contemplation as characteristic-mark distinguishes the object's own particular nature from what it shares with other things: the body's contents sorted into the specific characteristics of earth (solid matter, from hair to feces) and water (fluid, from tears to urine) is its particular mark; recognizing that one's own presently-intact, apparently clean body shares the very same underlying nature as an already-decaying corpse — separated only by time, not by kind — is its common mark.

Contemplation as moral category sorts the same material into what harms and what benefits: clinging to the body's apparent purity, whether one's own or another's, is the "dark" side — productive of affliction, suffering, and the entire chain leading to future birth, aging, sickness, and grief; seeing through that apparent purity accurately is the "bright" side — free of affliction, harm, and everything that follows from it — and the meditator resolves to reject the first and actively cultivate the second. Contemplation across time traces the same body through past, present, and future: what looks clean now was never actually clean in the past and will not remain so in the future, so the body's entire timeline, start to finish, never once escapes this same impure nature, however convincing any single moment's appearance may be. And contemplation as principle applies the four principles of reasoning already introduced at §54 directly to this practice — by correlation and by ultimate truth, no substantial "person" is ever found in or apart from the body to begin with, "clean" and "unclean" being nothing but labels laid over mere form and shape; by causal reasoning, the very body now sitting here was produced by past karma and affliction, conceived from parental fluids, and sustained since birth only by food — origins that guarantee its impurity was baked in from the very start, however temporarily presentable the result now appears.

69. A Major Milestone: Attention, Instruction, Virtue, Enlightenment, and the Sacred Teaching Itself — the Fourth Verse Closes, and with It the Entire Second Aspect of Inner Wisdom

The fourth and final verse closes with its remaining five terms: the seven attentions already surveyed at length across fascicles 11–13, five ways a teacher instructs students, the broad category of "virtue" already covered piecemeal throughout this fascicle, the three grades of awakening a being can attain, and — in the longest, richest definition of this entire section — the sacred teaching itself, described through twelve distinct facets of what the Buddha's teaching actually does for those who receive it. With this term's completion, all ten terms of the fourth verse are explained, and with them, the entire second aspect of inner wisdom — the differentiation of conceptions (想差別施設建立相), spanning four verses and roughly two thousand three hundred lines of source text — comes to a close.

Chinese Source

云何作意？謂七種作意。了相等，如前說。云何教授？謂五種教授。一、教教授，二、證教授，三、次第教授，四、無倒教授，五、神變教授。云何德？謂無量、解脫等。如句中已說。云何菩提？謂三種菩提。一、聲聞菩提，二、獨覺菩提，三、阿耨多羅三藐三菩提。云何聖教？謂授以歸依，制立學處，施設說聽，建立師徒，施論、戒論、生天之論，訶欲愛味，示欲過失，顯說雜染及清淨法，教導出離及與遠離，稱讚功德，乃至廣說無量無邊清淨品法。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is attention? The seven kinds of attention: grasping the character, and the rest, as already explained. What is instruction? Five kinds of instruction: first, instruction through teaching; second, instruction through personal realization; third, instruction in proper sequence; fourth, instruction free of error; fifth, instruction through miraculous transformation. What is virtue? The immeasurables, the liberations, and so forth, as already explained above. What is enlightenment? Three kinds of enlightenment: the enlightenment of the śrāvaka, the enlightenment of the solitary buddha, and unsurpassed, perfect, complete enlightenment. What is the sacred teaching? Conferring the [three] refuges; establishing the precepts; laying down how [the Dharma] is spoken and heard; establishing teacher and disciple; discourse on giving, on precepts, and on rebirth in heaven; censuring the savor of desire; showing desire's faults; disclosing both the defiled and the pure dharmas; teaching renunciation and seclusion; praising virtue; and, at length, boundlessly expounding the pure dharmas without measure or limit.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi passes quickly over "attention" (cross-referenced to the seven attentions — grasping the character, resolute understanding, withdrawal, gathering joy, observation, consummating effort, and the fruit of consummated effort) and "virtue" (cross-referenced to the immeasurables, liberations, bases of mastery, and totalities already catalogued at §44), since both simply point back to material already fully explained. He gives real attention instead to the five kinds of instruction: teaching drawn directly from the Buddha's own scriptural word; instruction born of a teacher's personal, realized experience rather than secondhand knowledge; instruction paced correctly to a student's actual readiness and season, neither rushed nor delayed; instruction free of any distortion in how it is matched to the specific student in front of the teacher; and, reserved for one who has already attained the path, instruction conveyed directly through miraculous demonstration — the power to manifest at will, to read another's mind and speak to their exact hidden difficulty, and to guide someone directly through teaching that flows from having already exhausted one's own afflictions. The three enlightenments he treats as a simple hierarchy of depth: the arhat's realization, the solitary buddha's realization (traditionally deeper, attained without a teacher), and a fully awakened buddha's utterly unsurpassed realization — three depths of the very same fundamental insight into no-self and dependent origination.

For "the sacred teaching" itself, Cháng Bǎi reads the twelve-part list as tracing the Buddha's own pedagogical order, start to finish: first securing a student's initial refuge in the three jewels, so their subsequent practice rests on a stable foundation rather than shifting on uncertain ground; then establishing the actual precepts a student is to keep; then teaching the etiquette of how Dharma is properly taught and properly received, so that transmission itself does not go astray; then formally establishing the teacher-disciple relationship, exemplified by the Buddha's own senior disciples such as Śāriputra; then working through the classic graduated sequence of a new practitioner's first teachings — the value of giving, of precepts, and of the heavens a virtuous life leads toward — followed immediately by frank exposure of desire's dangers (his image: craving lodged in the heart like a poisoned arrow, or a small crack slowly sinking a boat), so that the earlier, gentler teaching on merit is not mistaken for the final word; then plainly laying out both what defiles (the three kinds of defilement already catalogued at §44) and what purifies (the four truths, the three trainings, the factors of enlightenment); then teaching the actual means of renunciation and physical and mental seclusion already surveyed at §57; then praising the genuine qualities such practice yields (the eight liberations, the ten totalities, a buddha's unhindered eloquence); and finally, gesturing toward everything else — the boundless further teaching on the supramundane path itself — that could not be exhaustively listed here. Cháng Bǎi calls this twelve-step sequence, taken as a whole, nothing less than a complete map of how a person is actually led, stage by careful stage, from their very first contact with the Dharma all the way to its final, unbounded fruit — a fitting close to the entire "differentiation of conceptions," the treatise's own working glossary of everything a serious student of inner wisdom is expected to know by name.

70. The Third Aspect: Ten Principles That Comprehend the Entire Meaning of the Sacred Teaching

With the second aspect's exhaustive concept-catalogue now complete, the treatise turns to its third aspect (攝聖教義相) — a far more economical approach that reasons from principle to text rather than from text to principle. Ten compact categories, the treatise claims, are sufficient to comprehend the complete meaning of everything the Buddha ever taught, mapped directly onto the structure of the four noble truths.

Chinese Source

云何攝聖教義相？此中有能修習法。謂於諸善法專志所作、相續所作方便勤修。有所修習法。謂所有諸善法。有有過患法。謂應遍知法。有有染汙法。謂應不著、制伏，初應斷法。有障礙法。謂違逆現觀究竟法。有隨順法。謂隨順現觀究竟法。有真如所攝法。謂應覺悟法。有勝德所攝法。謂所應引發法。有隨順世間法。謂應習、應斷，及斷已現行法。有得究竟法。謂究竟自義所應證法。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the aspect comprehending the meaning of the sacred teaching? Herein there is the phenomenon of being able to cultivate — namely, the diligent cultivation of wholesome phenomena through devoted application and unbroken application. There is the phenomenon to be cultivated — namely, all wholesome phenomena. There is the phenomenon possessing faults — namely, that which is to be thoroughly known. There is the phenomenon possessing defilement — namely, that which is not to be clung to, is to be subdued, and is, at the outset, to be severed. There is the obstructing phenomenon — namely, that which runs contrary to the consummation of direct realization. There is the conforming phenomenon — namely, that which conforms to the consummation of direct realization. There is the phenomenon comprised by suchness — namely, that which is to be awakened to. There is the phenomenon comprised by excellent virtue — namely, that which is to be elicited. There is the phenomenon conforming to the world — namely, that which is to be cultivated, that which is to be severed, and that which continues to manifest once already severed. There is the phenomenon of attaining the ultimate — namely, that which is to be realized as one's own ultimate aim.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi shows how cleanly these ten map onto doctrine already familiar from this fascicle and earlier ones. The first two set the practical frame before the four truths even appear: what one is capable of cultivating (devoted, uninterrupted application to any wholesome practice — precepts, concentration, the six perfections) and what, specifically, there is to cultivate (every wholesome phenomenon, worldly and supramundane alike). The next four map directly onto the four truths themselves: the phenomenon possessing faults is the truth of suffering (birth, aging, sickness, death, and the rest, simply to be recognized for what it is); the phenomenon possessing defilement is the truth of origination (craving and its kin, to be neither indulged nor immediately eliminated by force, but first patiently disenchanted with through reflection, then actually subdued through sustained uninterrupted practice); the obstructing phenomenon is specifically the view of a self and everything that clusters around it, already surveyed at length in fascicle 8's account of self-view, since this above all else blocks the consummation of direct realization; and the conforming phenomenon is the truth of the path — the thirty-seven factors of enlightenment, precepts, concentration, and wisdom — everything that actually moves a practitioner toward that same realization rather than away from it.

The remaining four round out the picture beyond the four truths' bare structure. The phenomenon comprised by suchness is the truth of cessation itself — nirvāṇa, the true nature of things, what is finally to be awakened to. The phenomenon comprised by excellent virtue names the further qualities that arise once the path bears fruit — the six superknowledges, the four kinds of unhindered eloquence, the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses already catalogued at §50. The phenomenon conforming to the world distinguishes what an ordinary being should still cultivate (worldly wholesome conduct), what should be severed (worldly defilement), and a subtler third case — an arhat who has already severed every affliction but whose stream of aggregates, not yet passed into final nirvāṇa, continues to manifest ordinary wholesome activity in the meantime. And the phenomenon of attaining the ultimate is simply full liberation itself — the trainee's completed eightfold path joined with genuine liberation and final wisdom, one's own ultimate aim finally and fully realized. Cháng Bǎi calls this tenfold scheme the treatise's most compact possible summary of everything covered so far, a single page's worth of categories underneath which the entire preceding catalogue — however exhaustive — ultimately sits.

71. The Fourth and Final Aspect Opens: Three Ways Beings Continue Living, and the Roots of Delusion, Defilement, and Suffering

The treatise's fourth and final aspect of inner wisdom (佛教所應知處相) takes yet a different organizing principle: numeric sets, beginning with categories of one and climbing, in principle, all the way to ten — altogether some six hundred and seventy-four named items by the treatise's own count. The category of "one" yields a threefold account of what actually keeps any living being alive; the much larger category of "two" then opens with three pairs naming the deepest roots of delusion, defilement, and suffering itself.

Chinese Source

當知此中，一切有情住有三種。謂日別住、盡壽住、善法可愛生展轉住。初由食增上力，第二由命行增上力，第三由於諸善法不放逸增上力。復次，依有情世間及器世間，有二種法，能攝一切諸戲論事。謂能取法，及彼所依、所取之法。又諸世間略有二種雜染根本，能引無義無利雜染。謂於真實無正解行，及彼為先希求無義。又諸有情略有二種眾苦根本。謂於有漏法喜愛俱行所有期願，及非理所引厭離俱行所有期願。

Ru-Yi Translation

Know that herein, all sentient beings continue in three ways: continuing day by day, continuing for the span of a lifetime, and continuing on through the mutual perpetuation of wholesome conduct and agreeable rebirth. The first is by the power of food; the second, by the power of the life-force; the third, by the power of diligence in wholesome phenomena. Furthermore, in dependence on the world of sentient beings and the world of their environment, there are two phenomena that comprehend the entirety of discursive proliferation: the grasping phenomena, and the phenomena they depend on and grasp at. Furthermore, in the world at large there are, briefly, two root defilements that give rise to meaningless, unbeneficial defilement: the absence of correct understanding regarding what is real, and the meaningless craving that this absence gives rise to. Furthermore, sentient beings have, briefly, two root sufferings: the yearning that accompanies delight in phenomena subject to outflow, and the yearning that accompanies an unreasoned aversion [to existence itself].

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the treatise's own count of 674 named items across ten numeric categories, of which only "one" and part of "two" survive within this fascicle's remaining source — a genuinely vast index, condensed here to its essentials. The three continuations trace increasingly durable supports: the four foods sustain a being moment to moment; the karmic life-force (identified, on the Mahāyāna reading, with the ālaya-vijñāna itself) sustains a being for one full lifespan; and sustained wholesome conduct sustains a being across lifetimes, since good karma yields an agreeable rebirth, and an agreeable rebirth in turn provides the leisure and capacity to continue practicing further wholesome conduct — a virtuous circle running as long as it is maintained.

The three root pairs that follow all repeat one underlying diagnostic pattern already familiar from this fascicle: name a foundational duality, then show that duality is really just ignorance and craving in different guises. Discursive proliferation's two comprehending phenomena — the six consciousnesses that grasp, and the six faculties and six objects they depend on and grasp at — cover the entire apparatus by which ordinary experience generates its endless, ultimately pointless mental chatter. The two roots of worldly defilement are named directly: ignorance (a fundamental failure to understand reality as it is) and the craving ignorance gives rise to — the same pairing, Cháng Bǎi notes, that also grounds non-Buddhist ascetics' eternalist and annihilationist views of self, born from that identical root ignorance applied to the question of what a person ultimately is. And the two roots of suffering are craving for continued existence (delighting in the very phenomena that keep saṃsāra turning) and craving for non-existence (an unreasoned wish simply to be annihilated) — extremes he treats as equally mistaken, since neither excessive attachment to life nor a wish to escape it through death actually resolves anything; only the path itself, addressing the ignorance underneath both, brings genuine resolution.

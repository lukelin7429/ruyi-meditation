Fascicle 13 · 瑜伽師地論卷第十三 · The Ground of Meditative Equipoise Closes

From Solitude to Realization, and Across Four New Grounds

Fascicle 13 finishes the Ground of Meditative Equipoise — drawing on the Buddha's own sayings about solitude, calm, and insight — and then crosses, in a single scroll, into four further grounds: the Ground of Non-Equipoise, the Ground With-Mind, the Ground Without-Mind, and the opening of the Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 13 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. "Delight in Solitude": Bodily Seclusion, Mental Calm, and the Path They Open

Still within the Ground of Meditative Equipoise, the treatise now clarifies its meaning through the Buddha's own sayings. The first: "delight in solitude, cultivate contemplation, abide in right calm" — read as a graded path in which two kinds of seclusion lead on to insight and true realization.

Chinese Source

復次，如世尊言：汝等苾芻，當樂空閑，勤修觀行，內心安住正奢摩他者。謂能遠離臥具貪著，或處空閑，或坐樹下，繫念現前，乃至廣說，名樂空閑。當知此言顯身遠離。若能於內九種住心，如是名為內心安住正奢摩他。當知此言顯心遠離。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the World-Honored One said: "Monks, delight in solitude, diligently cultivate contemplation, and inwardly abide in right calm." "Delight in solitude" means being able to leave behind attachment to lodging and bedding, dwelling in an open place or sitting beneath a tree, with mindfulness present before one, and so on at length — this is called delighting in solitude. Know that this phrase shows bodily seclusion. "Inwardly abiding in right calm" means being able to settle the mind within by the nine ways of settling the mind. Know that this phrase shows mental seclusion.

Chinese Source

若樂處空閑，便能引發內心安住正奢摩他。若內心安住正奢摩他，便能引發毗鉢舍那。若於毗鉢舍那善修習已，即能引發於諸法中如實覺了。

Ru-Yi Translation

If one delights in dwelling in solitude, one can then draw forth the inward abiding in right calm. If one inwardly abides in right calm, one can then draw forth insight. If one has well cultivated insight, one can then draw forth a true and real realization of things as they are among all phenomena.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes that fascicle 13 opens still inside the Ground of Meditative Equipoise, now "incidentally clarifying" its meaning by drawing on the Buddha's own words. The first saying — "delight in solitude, cultivate contemplation, abide in right calm" — he reads as a graded path built on two kinds of seclusion. "Delighting in solitude" is bodily seclusion: leaving attachment to comfortable lodging and bedding, dwelling in the open or under a tree with mindfulness set before one. "Inwardly abiding in right calm" is mental seclusion: settling the mind through the nine stages of settling met earlier, from first drawing it inward up to genuine equipoise.

He stresses that the two chain forward into a whole path. Bodily solitude draws forth inner calm (śamatha); inner calm draws forth insight (vipaśyanā); and well-cultivated insight draws forth the true realization of all phenomena as they actually are. So the Buddha's brief exhortation, on Cháng Bǎi's reading, lays out the entire arc — seclusion, calm, insight, and liberating knowledge — in a single line, and shows that the outward act of withdrawing to a quiet place is not incidental but the first link of the chain that ends in realization.

2. Cultivating Concentration: The Immeasurable, the Constant-and-Thorough, and Right Mindfulness

A second saying: "toward concentration, cultivate the immeasurable, the constant-and-thorough, abiding in right mindfulness." The treatise reads the three as the marks of genuine cultivation, flanking two kinds of completeness — worldly and supramundane — and, on a second reading, as the paths of calm and insight.

Chinese Source

復次，如世尊言：汝等苾芻，於三摩地當勤修習，無量、常委、安住正念者。謂先總標於三摩地勤修習已，後以三事別顯修相。無量者，謂四無量。常委者，謂常有所作，及委悉所作，故名常委。安住正念者，顯於四念住，安住其心。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, as the World-Honored One said: "Monks, toward concentration diligently cultivate the immeasurable, the constant-and-thorough, abiding in right mindfulness." Having first generally enjoined diligent cultivation of concentration, [the Buddha] then displays the marks of cultivation by three things. "The immeasurable" means the four immeasurables. "The constant-and-thorough" means constantly having something to do, and doing it thoroughly and completely — hence "constant-and-thorough." "Abiding in right mindfulness" shows settling the mind in the four foundations of mindfulness.

Chinese Source

何故說此三種修相？謂依二種圓滿故：一者、世間圓滿，二者、出世圓滿。修無量故，便能引發世間圓滿；修正念故，便能引發出世圓滿；常委修故，於此二種速得通達；由此因緣，處二中說。是故但說三種修相。又無量者，顯奢摩他道；住正念者，顯毗鉢舍那道。

Ru-Yi Translation

Why are these three marks of cultivation taught? Because of two kinds of completeness: worldly completeness and supramundane completeness. By cultivating the immeasurable, one draws forth worldly completeness; by cultivating right mindfulness, one draws forth supramundane completeness; and by the constant-and-thorough cultivation, one swiftly penetrates both — for which reason it is placed between the two. So only these three marks of cultivation are stated. Again, "the immeasurable" shows the path of calm; "abiding in right mindfulness" shows the path of insight.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the three terms as the marks of genuine cultivation. "The immeasurable" is the four immeasurables — loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity. "Constant-and-thorough" is practice that is both unbroken (constantly having something to do) and complete and careful (doing it thoroughly). "Abiding in right mindfulness" is settling the mind in the four foundations of mindfulness — body, feeling, mind, and phenomena.

He gives the treatise's rationale for exactly these three: the two outer terms flank two kinds of completeness. The immeasurables draw forth worldly completeness; right mindfulness draws forth supramundane completeness; and the constant-and-thorough cultivation, set between them, is what lets one swiftly penetrate both — which is why it is named in the middle. On a second reading, "the immeasurable" marks the path of calm (śamatha) and "abiding in right mindfulness" the path of insight (vipaśyanā) — so the Buddha's three-word instruction compresses the whole of calm-and-insight practice, worldly and world-transcending together, into a single formula.

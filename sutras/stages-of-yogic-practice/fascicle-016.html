Fascicle 16 · 瑜伽師地論卷第十六 · The Ground of Reflection Opens

Learning to Think Correctly About the Dharma

Fascicle 16 opens entirely fresh terrain: the Ground of What Is Attained Through Reflection (思所成地), the eleventh of the treatise's seventeen grounds, following directly on the Ground of Hearing's close in fascicle 15. Where hearing supplies the raw material of the teaching, reflection is where a practitioner actually thinks it through — and this fascicle's own frontmatter promises it will carry through to this ground's close and the opening of the twelfth, the Ground of What Is Attained Through Cultivation.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 16 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. A New Ground Opens: Why Reflection Follows Hearing

Fascicle 16 opens the eleventh of the treatise's seventeen grounds: the Ground of What Is Attained Through Reflection (思所成地). The Ground of Hearing, just closed in fascicle 15, supplied the raw material — names, sentences, the five worldly sciences, everything a practitioner needs to have actually heard. Reflection is the next stage: taking what was heard and thinking it through until it becomes one's own settled understanding. The treatise opens by explaining why this stage matters and naming the three-part framework that will structure the whole ground.

Chinese Source

本地分中思所成地第十一之一。已說聞所成地。云何思所成地？當知略說由三種相。一、由自性清淨故；二、由思擇所知故；三、由思擇諸法故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Within the Basic Section, the Ground of What Is Attained Through Reflection, the eleventh, part one. The Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing has now been explained. What is the Ground of What Is Attained Through Reflection? Know that, briefly stated, it has three aspects: first, by way of purity of self-nature; second, by way of reflecting on what is cognized; and third, by way of reflecting on the teachings.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi frames the whole transition simply: hearing accumulates the Dharma's vocabulary — names, sentences, and the meanings behind them — but merely having heard something does not yet touch the mind's actual functioning. Reflection is where a practitioner connects what was heard to their own body, speech, and mind, testing and turning it over until genuine conviction forms. Only reflection well done can subdue affliction and let wholesome conduct actually show up in how a person lives — and, for a Mahāyāna practitioner specifically, lets compassion become a lived orientation toward others rather than a doctrine merely assented to.

He previews the three-part structure this entire ground follows: purity of self-nature names nine marks of a mind that reflects correctly, so that reflection itself becomes a fit basis for what follows; reflecting on what is cognized examines all things a mind can know, sorted by whether they genuinely exist or do not; and reflecting on the teachings works through the treatise's own textual material — first by category of scriptural meaning, then through two sets of verses, one establishing the ultimate truth of no-self, the other laying out the essential aims of the entire teaching. This fascicle's own frontmatter, he notes, promises that this ground itself will close and the twelfth ground — the Ground of What Is Attained Through Cultivation — will open somewhere within the material still ahead.

2. Purity of Self-Nature: Nine Marks of a Mind That Reflects Correctly

The first of the three aspects names nine marks that together make reflection itself genuinely pure — not a checklist to complete once, but nine qualities a practitioner's thinking must actually have if reflection is to do its job: settling the Dharma so firmly that it can subdue affliction and open the way to the next ground, cultivation.

Chinese Source

云何自性清淨？謂九種相應知。一者、謂如有一，獨處空閑，審諦思惟如其所聞、如所究達諸法道理。二者、遠離一切不思議處，審諦思惟所應思處。三者、能善了知默說、大說。四者、凡所思惟，唯依於義，不依於文。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is purity of self-nature? Know that it has nine marks. First, one dwells alone in solitude and quiet, carefully and truly reflecting on the principles of things exactly as heard and exactly as investigated to their conclusion. Second, one keeps entirely clear of what should not be contemplated, carefully and truly reflecting only on what is fit to be contemplated. Third, one skillfully distinguishes provisional teaching from teaching in its fullest sense. Fourth, whatever one reflects on, one relies solely on the meaning, not on the mere letter.

Chinese Source

五者、於法少分，唯生信解；於法少分，以慧觀察。六者、堅固思惟。七者、安住思惟。八者、相續思惟。九者、於所思惟能善究竟，終無中路厭怖退屈。由此九相，名為清淨善淨思惟。

Ru-Yi Translation

Fifth, regarding some portion of the Dharma, one simply generates faith and understanding; regarding another portion, one examines it with wisdom. Sixth, one's reflection is firm. Seventh, one's reflection is settled. Eighth, one's reflection is sustained without interruption. Ninth, whatever one reflects on, one carries skillfully through to its conclusion, never growing weary, fearful, or discouraged partway through. By these nine marks, reflection is called pure and rightly purified.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the nine as building on one another rather than standing as nine independent rules. The first establishes the basic condition: real reflection needs solitude and quiet, body and mind both withdrawn from distraction, working through what was actually heard toward its deepest implications. The second sets a boundary the Compendium of Determinations spells out in detail — nine specific topics the Buddha declared unfit for speculation (questions about the self, about the world's origin, about a buddha's or meditator's inner attainment, and the fourteen classic unanswerable questions among them), each traced to one of five underlying causes (view, mere acceptance, idle speculation, self-interest, or plain distraction) and each carrying three concrete costs when pursued anyway: a mind thrown into disorder, demerit generated, and no wholesome result gained. Reflecting instead on what is actually fit to contemplate, he notes, is what keeps thinking from becoming its own obstacle.

The third and fourth marks he treats as a matched pair: knowing provisional from full teaching, and meaning over mere wording, are both ways of not getting trapped by the letter of a passage. He illustrates with the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*'s famous line that "all things are without self-nature, unborn, unceasing, originally still, self-nature is nirvāṇa" — read only at the literal surface, this could collapse into nihilism, but understood through its own threefold intended sense (no self-nature of characteristic, no self-nature of arising, no self-nature in the ultimate) it instead points precisely at emptiness rightly understood. The fifth mark he reads as intellectual honesty about one's own present limits: some of the Buddha's own realization is simply beyond an ordinary practitioner's current capacity and calls for reverent trust rather than forced comprehension, while the portion one actually can examine deserves rigorous, active investigation — mistaking the first for something to doubt, or the second for something to merely accept on faith, is itself a kind of error.

The remaining four marks — firm, settled, sustained, and carried through to genuine conclusion — he reads as describing reflection's endurance rather than its content: firmness keeps a half-formed understanding from being shaken loose by the next passing doubt; settledness lets what is newly understood actually take root rather than staying provisional; sustained, unbroken reflection keeps what was already understood from being forgotten (he cites the classic simile of one who, having repeatedly contemplated impermanence, is no more shaken by sudden loss than someone who, having already seen lightning, is not startled by the thunder that follows); and carrying reflection through to real conclusion — never abandoning a hard question halfway from fatigue or discouragement — is what actually produces new insight never reached before, the exact readiness a practitioner needs to cross from this ground into the next: cultivation. With these nine marks satisfied, reflection itself becomes pure, and the treatise turns to its second aspect: reflecting on what is cognized.

3. Reflecting on What Is Cognized Opens: Existing Things and Non-Existing Things

The second of the three aspects, by far the largest — spanning roughly 1,500 lines, nearly the whole remainder of what this fascicle covers — turns from how to reflect to what reflection actually examines: everything a mind can know, sorted at the most basic level into what genuinely exists and what does not. This section covers the opening framework and the first mark of really-existing things: ultimate-meaning existence.

Chinese Source

云何思擇所知？謂善思擇所觀察義。何等名為所觀察義？謂於有法了知有相，於非有法了知無相。如是名為所觀察義。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is reflecting on what is cognized? It means skillfully reflecting on the object under examination. What is called the object under examination? It means, regarding what genuinely exists, knowing it to have a real mark; and regarding what does not genuinely exist, knowing it to have no real mark. This is called the object under examination.

Chinese Source

何等名為所觀有法？當知此法略有五種。一、自相有法，二、共相有法，三、假相有法，四、因相有法，五、果相有法。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is called an existing thing under examination? Know that, briefly, it has five kinds: first, a thing existing by its own particular mark; second, a thing existing by a mark shared in common; third, a thing existing by a merely designated mark; fourth, a thing existing by the mark of a cause; and fifth, a thing existing by the mark of a result.

Chinese Source

何等名為自相有法？當知此法略有三種。一、勝義相有，二、相狀相有，三、現在相有。勝義相有者，謂諸法中離言說義，出世間智所行境界，非安立相。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is called a thing existing by its own particular mark? Know that, briefly, it has three kinds: first, existing in the mark of ultimate meaning; second, existing in the mark of a describable feature; and third, existing in the mark of the present moment. Existing in the mark of ultimate meaning: it refers, among all things, to the meaning beyond verbal description — the object range of transcendent wisdom, a mark not established through conventional designation.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the opening two-way split — existing things carry a real mark, non-existing things carry none — as the single most basic move in all of Yogācāra epistemology: before anything else can be examined, a practitioner must first learn to correctly sort what is actually there from what the mind merely projects onto it. Everything the rest of this aspect covers, spanning the bulk of the fascicle, is really just this one distinction worked out across five kinds of genuine existence and, later, a matching account of what does not exist at all.

Of the five kinds of existing thing, he identifies the first — existing by its own particular mark, meaning simply what has genuine, verifiable reality regardless of how it is labeled — as itself further divided into three grades. Ultimate-meaning existence is the deepest: the "suchness" (真如) beyond all conceptual overlay, the object only a noble one's transcendent wisdom directly touches, never captured in ordinary language. He cross-references the treatise's own sevenfold account of suchness from elsewhere in this text — the suchness of arising and passing (how conditioned things actually flow), the suchness of true nature (revealed once both kinds of selflessness are seen), the suchness of consciousness-only (how defiled and pure states alike are nothing but consciousness), and four more mapped directly onto the four noble truths (suffering, origination, cessation, and the path each having their own suchness) — noting that all seven are ultimately the one same reality described from seven angles, differentiated only by which facet of realization is being emphasized. The remaining two kinds of self-mark existence — describable features and present-moment existence — will follow next.

4. Two More Kinds of Real Existence: Describable Features and the Present Moment

Ultimate-meaning existence names reality beyond words entirely — but ordinary practice mostly deals with things that do have describable features and do sit in a particular moment of time. This section covers the remaining two of the three kinds of self-mark existence, closing out what genuinely, verifiably exists in its own right.

Chinese Source

相狀相有者，謂由四種所觀相狀。一、於是處名可得。二、於是處事可得。三、此名於此事非不決定。謂或迷亂不決定故，或無常不決定故。四、此名於此事無礙隨轉。非或於是處隨轉，或於是處退還。

Ru-Yi Translation

Existing in the mark of a describable feature: it means having four observable characteristics. First, a name can be found for it. Second, an actual fact can be found corresponding to it. Third, the name is not indeterminate with respect to the fact — that is, not indeterminate through confusion, nor indeterminate through impermanence. Fourth, the name applies to the fact without obstruction, consistently — never applying in one instance and failing in another otherwise just like it.

Chinese Source

現在相有者，謂若已生及因果性。如是一切，總說為一自相有法。

Ru-Yi Translation

Existing in the mark of the present moment: it means whatever has already arisen, and stands in the relation of cause and effect. All of this together is called, as one, a thing existing by its own particular mark.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the four marks of "describable-feature existence" as a filter separating genuinely dependent things from mere verbal fictions layered on top of them. Having both a findable name and a findable underlying fact rules out both suchness itself (which has no name at all) and the purely imagined (which has a name but no fact behind it) — leaving exactly the dependently-arisen middle ground the Yogācāra system calls real in a conventional sense. The third mark rules out two further slippages: names applied only through confusion (calling a tree "yellow" because jaundiced eyes see it wrong) and names that shift the moment their underlying condition changes (a shattered pot is no longer called a pot, but shards) — genuine feature-existence needs a name that tracks something stable enough to actually mean something. The fourth mark rules out names too narrow to generalize: "house" names any house anywhere, but cannot double as "village," even though villages always contain houses — a name confined to only some contexts, he notes, fails to capture a real, portable feature of things.

Present-moment existence, the third and final kind of self-mark existence, he reads as the plainest of the three: whatever has actually arisen right now, understood as both the effect of its own prior seed and the cause of whatever it will produce next — present-moment existence excludes anything merely continuous across a stretch of time (which the treatise elsewhere treats as its own kind of provisional designation) and insists on the strict Abhidharma principle that only the present instant genuinely exists, the past having already ceased and the future not yet arisen. With ultimate-meaning existence, describable-feature existence, and present-moment existence all named, the first of the five existing-thing categories — self-mark existence — is complete, and the treatise turns to the second: shared-mark existence.

5. Shared-Mark Existence: Five Ways Things Genuinely Have Something in Common

Where self-mark existence covers what makes each thing distinctly itself, the second category, shared-mark existence, covers the genuine common ground between things — real patterns that hold across many particulars at once, from the broadest classificatory categories down to the single mark shared by literally everything that exists.

Chinese Source

何等名為共相有法？當知此相復有五種。一、種類共相，二、成所作共相，三、一切行共相，四、一切有漏共相，五、一切法共相。種類共相者，謂色、受、想、行、識等各別種類，總名為一種類共相。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is called a thing existing by a shared mark? Know that this mark again has five kinds: first, shared mark of kind; second, shared mark of what is accomplished by action; third, shared mark of all conditioned things; fourth, shared mark of all that is defiled; and fifth, shared mark of all things whatsoever. Shared mark of kind: form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness, each its own distinct kind — together named, as one, the shared mark of kind.

Chinese Source

成所作共相者，謂善有漏法於感愛果，由能成辦所作共相，說名共相。如善有漏法於感愛果，如是不善法於感非愛果，念住、正勝、神足、根、力、覺支、道支菩提分法於得菩提，由能成辦所作共相，說名共相，當知亦爾。

Ru-Yi Translation

Shared mark of what is accomplished by action: wholesome but still-defiled action, in bringing about a desirable result, does so by a shared capacity to accomplish that result — this shared capacity is called the shared mark. Just as wholesome but still-defiled action does this in bringing about a desirable result, so too unwholesome action does this in bringing about an undesirable result, and the factors of enlightenment — the foundations of mindfulness, the right efforts, the bases of supernormal power, the faculties, the powers, the limbs of enlightenment, and the limbs of the path — do this in bringing about awakening: know that this too is a shared mark of what is accomplished by action.

Chinese Source

一切行共相者，謂一切行無常性相。一切有漏共相者，謂有漏行皆苦性相。一切法共相者，謂一切法空、無我性相。如是一切，總說為一共相有法。

Ru-Yi Translation

Shared mark of all conditioned things: the nature of impermanence shared by every conditioned thing. Shared mark of all that is defiled: the nature of suffering shared by everything still defiled. Shared mark of all things whatsoever: the nature of emptiness and non-self shared by absolutely everything, conditioned and unconditioned alike. All of this together is called, as one, a thing existing by a shared mark.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the five shared marks as nested rings of increasing breadth. Shared mark of kind is the narrowest: whatever genuinely functions as form, as feeling, as perception, and so on — the treatise's basic five-aggregate taxonomy, or equally the six sense-faculties and their objects — grouped by a real functional commonality rather than by superficial resemblance. Shared mark of what is accomplished by action is broader still, cutting across the entire moral order at once: wholesome but still-defiled deeds share a real capacity to ripen into pleasant results, unwholesome deeds share a real capacity to ripen into painful ones, and the thirty-seven factors of enlightenment (already met across several earlier fascicles) share a real capacity to ripen into awakening itself — different in kind from each other, yet each genuinely unified within its own class by what it actually produces.

The final three marks widen further still, each holding across some progressively larger domain: impermanence holds across every single conditioned thing without exception; suffering holds across everything still touched by defilement (he notes even pleasant feeling counts, since it too depends on conditions that will inevitably change); and emptiness together with non-self holds across literally everything that can be spoken of at all, conditioned and unconditioned alike — the widest possible shared mark, and not coincidentally the content of the deepest realization the entire path aims at. He stresses these five are not increasingly vague generalizations but increasingly universal genuine facts — each level is exactly as real as self-mark existence, just true of more things at once. With all five shared marks named, this second category of existing things is complete, and the treatise turns to its third: merely-designated existence.

6. Merely-Designated Existence: Six Kinds of Language That Point Beyond Themselves

The third category of existing things, merely-designated existence, catalogs six patterns of ordinary speech that name something real without that name itself picking out any single, independently existing thing — the linguistic fingerprint of everything conventionally real but not, in the treatise's strict sense, ultimately findable as its own separate entity.

Chinese Source

何等名為假相有法？謂若於是處，略有六種言論生起，當知此處名假相有。謂屬主相應言論、遠離此彼言論、眾共施設言論、眾法聚集言論、不遍一切言論、非常言論。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is called a thing existing by a merely designated mark? Wherever, briefly, six kinds of expression arise, know that this is called merely-designated existence: expression dependent on a possessor; expression neither directly naming this nor that; expression by common convention; expression by aggregation of many things; expression not applying universally; and expression naming what is impermanent.

Chinese Source

屬主相應言論者，謂諸言論，配屬於主方解其相，非不屬主。如說生時，此誰之生，待所屬主起此言論。謂色之生，受、想、行、識之生。是名屬主相應言論。

Ru-Yi Translation

Expression dependent on a possessor: whatever expression can only be understood once it is attached to some governing thing, and not otherwise. As when one says "arising" — arising of what? — the expression only makes sense once attached to its possessor: the arising of form, or of feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness. This is called expression dependent on a possessor.

Chinese Source

遠離此彼言論者，謂諸言論，非以此顯此，亦非以彼顯彼，是說名為遠離此彼言論。如言宅之門、舍之壁，瓶之口、甕之腹、軍之車、林之樹、百之十、十之三，如是等。

Ru-Yi Translation

Expression neither directly naming this nor that: expression that neither fully names one single thing by itself, nor names two genuinely separate things joined together. As when one says the door of a house, the wall of a dwelling, the mouth of a jar, the belly of a jug, the chariot of an army, a tree of a forest, ten within a hundred, three within ten, and so on.

Chinese Source

眾共施設言論者，謂於六種所觀相狀假立言論。眾法聚集言論者，謂於眾多和合建立自性假立言論，如說「宅」、「舍」、「瓶」、「衣」、「車」、「軍」、「林」、「樹」等。

Ru-Yi Translation

Expression by common convention: expression provisionally established on the basis of six observable features shared by mental cognition. Expression by aggregation of many things: expression provisionally established by taking a self-nature from the coming-together of many things — as with "house," "dwelling," "jar," "robe," "chariot," "army," "grove," or "tree."

Chinese Source

不遍一切言論者，謂此名於此事無礙隨轉，然於餘處退還不轉。非常言論者，謂由破壞故、由不破壞故、由加行故、由轉變故而起言論，如瓶破已捨瓶之名，飲食變已捨飲食名。

Ru-Yi Translation

Expression not applying universally: expression that applies without obstruction to one thing, but fails and withdraws when applied elsewhere. Expression naming what is impermanent: expression arising through destruction, through non-destruction yet rearrangement, through deliberate working, or through transformation — as when a shattered pot no longer bears the name "pot," or transformed food no longer bears the name "food."

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads all six patterns as variations on a single insight: language constantly names things that have no single, independently findable referent, yet still communicates something real. The first, expression dependent on a possessor, covers the treatise's own twenty-four "factors dissociated from mind" (get, non-perception attainment, and the like, first catalogued in earlier fascicles) — words like "arising," "abiding," and "impermanence" name nothing on their own until attached to a genuine subject like form or consciousness. The second, neither-this-nor-that expression, covers whole-and-part language — a door is not simply identical to a house nor simply a wholly separate thing from it, so naming it slips between the treatise's other two patterns of direct designation (met just before this section, "this-names-this" and "that-names-that," each themselves subdividing into a genuinely real and a merely conventional case, illustrated with earth's hardness, water's wetness, fire's heat, and wind's motion as the real cases, and a stone's roundness or a flame's flicker as the conventional ones).

He treats the third, common convention, as covering the deepest layer — words like "self" or "being" built directly from the six basic features any cognition takes in (the object grasped by the eight consciousnesses, what draws attention, what feels pleasant or painful, and so on) — while the fourth, aggregation, covers the far more familiar case of composite objects like houses, jars, or armies, each just a convenient label for many really-existing parts working together, with no single "house-substance" findable anywhere among them. The fifth, non-universal expression, catches words too narrow to name a genuine universal feature (a "house" cannot double as "village," even though villages contain houses) — the treatise's own check against mistaking a locally useful label for a real shared mark. The sixth, impermanent expression, covers labels that shift the instant their underlying condition changes: a shattered pot becomes shards, kneaded gold becomes a bracelet, digested food becomes waste — proof, Cháng Bǎi notes, that the name was never naming anything more solid than a temporary configuration in the first place. With all six kinds of merely-designated existence named, this third category is complete, and the treatise turns to its fourth and fifth: existence by the mark of a cause, and existence by the mark of a result.

7. Cause and Result Close the Existing-Things Framework Entirely

The fourth and fifth categories of existing things — existence by the mark of a cause, and existence by the mark of a result — trace things back to what produces them and forward to what they produce. With these two named, the entire fivefold framework of existing things (self-mark, shared-mark, merely-designated, causal, and resultant existence) reaches its close.

Chinese Source

何等名為因相有法？當知此因略有五種。一、可愛因，二、不可愛因，三、長養因，四、流轉因，五、還滅因。可愛因者，謂善有漏法。不可愛因者，謂不善法。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is called a thing existing by the mark of a cause? Know that, briefly, this cause has five kinds: first, the cause of what is desirable; second, the cause of what is undesirable; third, the cause of nurturing growth; fourth, the cause of continued flow; and fifth, the cause of reversal and cessation. The cause of what is desirable: wholesome but still-defiled action. The cause of what is undesirable: unwholesome action.

Chinese Source

長養因者，謂前前所生善、不善、無記法，修習、善修習、多修習故，能令後後所生善、不善、無記法展轉增勝，名長養因。流轉因者，謂由此種子，由此熏習，由此助伴，彼法流轉。此於彼法，名流轉因。

Ru-Yi Translation

The cause of nurturing growth: wholesome, unwholesome, or neutral action already produced, which, through practice, skillful practice, and repeated practice, causes wholesome, unwholesome, or neutral action produced afterward to grow progressively stronger — this is called the cause of nurturing growth. The cause of continued flow: through this seed, through this habituation, through these accompanying conditions, that phenomenon continues to flow onward. In relation to that phenomenon, this is called the cause of continued flow.

Chinese Source

還滅因者，謂諸行還滅、雜染還滅，所有一切能寂靜道、能般涅槃、能趣菩提，及彼資糧，并其方便，能生能辦，名還滅因。如是總名因相有法。

Ru-Yi Translation

The cause of reversal and cessation: whatever pacifies conditioned formations, reverses defilement, and — including the path itself, the requisites for it, and the skillful means leading to it — is capable of bringing about nirvāṇa, of leading toward awakening, and of actually producing and accomplishing this: this is called the cause of reversal and cessation. All of this together is called existence by the mark of a cause.

Chinese Source

何等名為果相有法？謂從彼五因，若生、若得、若成、若辦、若轉，當知是名果相有法。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is called a thing existing by the mark of a result? Whatever arises, is attained, is accomplished, is brought about, or functions, in dependence on those five causes — know that this is called existence by the mark of a result.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps the five causes onto the full arc of ordinary and spiritual life: wholesome but still-defiled action produces a favorable rebirth, unwholesome action produces an unfavorable one, and repeated cultivation of any of the three moral qualities (wholesome, unwholesome, or neutral) makes its own future recurrence progressively stronger — the same principle already met in earlier fascicles' habituation teachings, here formalized as its own causal category. The fourth cause, continued flow, is the engine of ordinary saṃsāra itself: a seed (the karmic potential laid down by past action), reinforced by habituation, and activated by supporting conditions — ignorance, craving, and grasping specifically — together keep the wheel of birth and death turning. The fifth, reversal and cessation, is its exact opposite: the path itself, together with everything that supports walking it (the two requisites of merit and wisdom, the preparatory stages, and the stages of seeing, cultivating, and completing the path already outlined in earlier Abhidharma material this treatise draws on), functioning together to actually produce liberation. He notes the treatise cross-references its own earlier tenfold, five-result causal analysis (from the Stage With and Without Application of Thought) for readers wanting the fully technical mapping — the five causes named here condense that fuller scheme into a version organized specifically around the arc from ordinary bondage to liberation.

Result-existence, the fifth and final category, he reads as simply the flip side of all five causes at once — whatever a cause actually produces, whether that is a new arising, an attainment, an accomplishment, a completed task, or an activated function — deliberately left broad enough to cover every kind of karmic and spiritual fruit without needing its own separate fivefold breakdown. With cause and result both named, the entire fivefold account of existing things — self-mark, shared-mark, merely-designated, causal, and resultant existence — is now complete. The treatise turns to the mirror question: what does it mean for something not to exist at all?

8. Non-Existing Things: Five Ways Something Genuinely Has No Real Mark

Having mapped everything that genuinely exists, the treatise turns to its mirror image: five distinct ways something can be said not to exist at all. Where the existing-things framework guards against under-recognizing what is real, this framework guards against the opposite error — mistaking what merely appears real, or is simply absent, for something with genuine substance.

Chinese Source

何等名為所觀無法？當知此相亦有五種。一、未生無，二、已滅無，三、互相無，四、勝義無，五、畢竟無。未生無者，謂未來諸行。已滅無者，謂過去諸行。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is called a non-existing thing under examination? Know that this mark, too, has five kinds: first, not-yet-arisen non-existence; second, already-ceased non-existence; third, mutual non-existence; fourth, ultimate non-existence; and fifth, absolute non-existence. Not-yet-arisen non-existence: future conditioned things. Already-ceased non-existence: past conditioned things.

Chinese Source

互相無者，謂諸餘法，由所餘相若遠離性、若非有性；或所餘法，與諸餘法不和合性。勝義無者，謂由世俗言說自性，假設言論所安立性。

Ru-Yi Translation

Mutual non-existence: among various things, one thing's mark being either entirely absent from another, or of an altogether different nature from it; or one thing standing in a relation of simple incompatibility with another. Ultimate non-existence: the conventional, verbally-designated nature of things, which simply does not appear within an ultimate cognition.

Chinese Source

畢竟無者，謂石女兒等，畢竟無類。

Ru-Yi Translation

Absolute non-existence: as with "the child of a barren woman" — something belonging to a category that could never exist in the first place.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the first two kinds as straightforward temporal absence, consistent with the strict Abhidharma view already met earlier this fascicle that only the present instant genuinely exists: the future has simply not yet arrived, and the past has already ceased, so neither is presently findable at all — one's self ten years from now, or ten years ago, is equally unavailable to direct examination right now. Mutual non-existence, the third kind, is more interesting: it names cases where one thing's absence is defined precisely by another thing's presence — space is simply the absence of the obstruction that defines physical matter, wisdom's clear knowing is defined by the very absence of the ignorance it replaces, and light and darkness cannot coexist in the same place at the same time. He notes this kind of non-existence is not mere emptiness but a genuine, informative relation between two real things.

Ultimate non-existence, the fourth kind, he treats as the deliberate mirror of ultimate-meaning existence introduced at this fascicle's opening (section 3): a noble one's transcendent cognition, resting directly in suchness, simply has no room for the conventional, word-built categories ordinary cognition constantly constructs — those categories are not annihilated so much as never arising within that mode of seeing at all. Absolute non-existence, the fifth and final kind, is the plainest: things that could never exist under any circumstances whatsoever, illustrated by the classical Buddhist logic example (already met in fascicle 15's account of inference) of "the child of a barren woman" — not merely absent, but logically incoherent as a category from the start. With all five kinds of non-existence named, the treatise pairs this analysis with a parallel fivefold scheme — five kinds of "having nature" and five kinds of "lacking nature," mapped directly onto the three-natures teaching already familiar from earlier fascicles — which the next section takes up.

9. Reflecting on What Is Cognized Concludes: The Same Ground, Seen Through Being and Non-Being

The treatise closes its account of existing and non-existing things with one more pass over the same territory — five kinds of "having a nature" and five matching kinds of "lacking a nature," mapped directly onto the three-natures teaching (the imagined, the dependent, and the perfectly real) already familiar from earlier fascicles. With this, the entire second aspect of the ground — reflecting on what is cognized — closes.

Chinese Source

復有五種有性、五種無性。何等名為五種有性？一、圓成實相有性，二、依他起相有性，三、遍計所執相有性，四、差別相有性，五、不可說相有性。此中，初是勝義相。第二是緣生相相。第三是假施設相。第四是不二相。第五由四種不可說故，名不可說相。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are, further, five kinds of having a nature and five kinds of lacking a nature. What are the five kinds of having a nature? First, the nature of the perfectly real mark; second, the nature of the dependent mark; third, the nature of the imagined mark; fourth, the nature of the differentiated mark; and fifth, the nature of the inexpressible mark. Of these, the first is the mark of ultimate meaning. The second is the mark of dependently-arisen appearance. The third is the mark of merely provisional designation. The fourth is the mark of neither-one-nor-different. The fifth is called inexpressible for four reasons.

Chinese Source

一、無故不可說……二、甚深故不可說。謂離言法性不可思議，如來法身不可思議……三、能引無義故不可說……四、法相法爾之所安立，故不可說。所謂真如於諸行等，不可宣說異不異性。

Ru-Yi Translation

First, inexpressible because it does not exist [as with a self apart from the aggregates]. Second, inexpressible because it is too profound — the nature beyond words is inconceivable, the Tathāgata's own body of truth is inconceivable. Third, inexpressible because expressing it would serve no genuine purpose. Fourth, inexpressible because this is simply how the nature of things is: suchness cannot be declared either the same as, or different from, conditioned phenomena.

Chinese Source

何等名為五種無性？一、勝義相無性，二、自依相無性，三、畢竟自相無性，四、無差別相無性，五、可說相無性。

Ru-Yi Translation

What are the five kinds of lacking a nature? First, absence of any nature in ultimate meaning; second, absence of a self-dependent nature; third, absolute absence of the imagined nature's own mark; fourth, absence of a real nature in any differentiated mark; and fifth, absence of any genuinely expressible mark.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this closing fivefold pass as a deliberate reprise, not new material: the same ground already covered — self-mark, shared-mark, merely-designated, causal, and resultant existence, and their mirror non-existences — now reorganized around the three-natures teaching so a practitioner can see how it all fits into one picture. The perfectly real nature (suchness itself) both has a nature in the deepest sense and, described positively, has none — the very same reality, spoken of as "having nature" when pointing at its genuine reality and "lacking nature" when denying it can be captured as an ordinary object among other objects. The dependent nature (everything arising through causes and conditions) has a nature in that it genuinely, functionally exists, yet lacks any nature of arising independently, from itself alone — nothing in the dependent world produces itself unconditioned. The imagined nature (whatever the deluded mind projects onto things — a fixed self, an inherent "thisness" language seems to promise) has only the flimsiest nature as a mental event, and, examined for what it claims to be, has no nature whatsoever — this is the one of the three natures the treatise treats as genuinely empty through and through.

He reads the fourth mark, the "neither-one-nor-different" mark, as the treatise's own resolution to a puzzle already implicit in the assorted differentiated features named across earlier sections (arising, aging, abiding, impermanence, and the rest): each such feature is not simply identical to the thing it describes, nor simply a separate thing added onto it — form is not literally identical with its impermanence, yet impermanence is not some second, independent object either. And the fifth mark, the "inexpressible" mark, closes with the treatise's own fourfold catalogue of principled silence — genuinely non-existent categories, matters too profound for ordinary language (the Tathāgata's own realized nature foremost among them), matters whose disclosure would serve no liberating purpose, and relations (like suchness to conditioned things) that are simply not the kind of thing sameness-or-difference language can capture at all — a close echo of the unanswerable-questions material already met in this ground's opening self-nature-purity teaching (section 2), and, before that, in fascicle 15's own logic material.

With existing things, non-existing things, and this closing having-nature/lacking-nature recap all now complete, the second of the ground's three aspects — reflecting on what is cognized — is finished in its entirety. The treatise turns to its third and final aspect, by far the largest remaining: reflecting on the teachings themselves.

10. Reflecting on the Teachings Opens: Sutra and Verse, and No-Self's Opening Line

The third and by far the largest of the ground's three aspects, reflecting on the teachings, opens with a simple two-way split: reflecting on the meaning of sutras (handled briefly here, by cross-reference to fuller treatments elsewhere in the treatise), and reflecting on the meaning of verses — three sets of verses on ultimate truth, the essential aims of practice, and the treatise's core doctrines, together running the rest of this fascicle. This section covers the opening framework and the first line of the first and longest set: forty-four verses establishing the truth of no-self.

Chinese Source

云何思擇諸法？此復二種應知。一、思擇素怛纜義，二、思擇伽他義。思擇素怛纜義，如攝事分及菩薩藏教授中當廣說。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is reflecting on the teachings? Know that this again has two kinds: first, reflecting on the meaning of sutras; and second, reflecting on the meaning of verses. Reflecting on the meaning of sutras will be broadly explained in the Compendium of Determinations and in the bodhisattva canon's own instruction.

Chinese Source

思擇伽他義，復有三種。一者、建立勝義伽他，二者、建立意趣義伽他，三者、建立體義伽他。

Ru-Yi Translation

Reflecting on the meaning of verses again has three kinds: first, establishing the ultimate-meaning verses; second, establishing the verses on essential intent; and third, establishing the verses on the essential body of the teaching.

Chinese Source

建立勝義伽他者，如經言：都無有宰主，及作者受者，諸法亦無用，而用轉非無。

Ru-Yi Translation

Establishing the ultimate-meaning verses: as the scripture says — there is no sovereign master, no doer and no experiencer; the constituents of a person have no such function of their own, yet the very functioning that occurs is not simply nothing.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes reflecting on sutra meaning is handled with unusual brevity here — the treatise simply points forward to two other locations where this work is done at length: the Compendium of Determinations' extensive commentary on the āgamas (chiefly the Saṃyukta-āgama, spanning many fascicles later in this treatise) and the Bodhisattva Ground's own teaching on the six perfections and the bodhisattva path. Reflecting on verse meaning, by contrast, receives the treatise's full direct attention here, in three parts: the ultimate-meaning verses (forty-four verses methodically establishing the truth of no-self, by far the largest of the three), the essential-intent verses (covering the three trainings — precepts, concentration, and wisdom — the path's practical arc from initial discipline to liberating insight), and the essential-body verses (fourteen topics distilling the teaching's core content). He notes Kuījī's commentary divides the forty-four no-self verses into twelve thematic groups, the first nine verses of which methodically clear away four things ordinary experience takes for granted: a sovereign self ruling over experience, a real "doer" behind action, a real "experiencer" behind feeling, and any genuine causal power belonging to a self apart from the aggregates themselves.

He glosses the opening verse itself as the entire teaching's thesis statement in miniature: what ordinary people call "I" is only a convenient label placed over the five aggregates — form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness — with no actual ruler standing above them, no independent doer separate from bodily and mental action, and no independent experiencer separate from feeling itself; the six sense faculties do not, strictly speaking, "act" as though driven by some separate agent. Yet the verse's closing line is just as important as its opening claim: denying a fixed, sovereign self is not the same as denying that anything happens at all — the functioning, the seeing and hearing and feeling and acting, genuinely occurs, exactly as dependent origination requires. This is the razor's edge the entire forty-four-verse sequence will walk: refuting self without collapsing into nihilism, the same balance the treatise has insisted on since section 2's account of interpreting "no self-nature" correctly.

11. No-Self, Verses 2–9: No Ruler Anywhere, No Function Standing on Its Own

The opening verse stated the thesis; the next eight verses unpack its two halves in careful order. Three verses show that painstaking analysis of a person into the twelve links of dependent origination, the aggregates, sense-fields, and elements never turns up a findable self. Five more verses show that even the functions ordinarily attributed to a self — seeing, hearing, producing, ceasing — belong to no single thing at all, and yet still genuinely occur.

Chinese Source

唯十二有支，蘊處界流轉，審思此一切，眾生不可得。於內及於外，是一切皆空，其能修空者，亦常無所有。我我定非有，由顛倒妄計，有情我皆無，唯有因法有。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are only the twelve links of dependent origination, flowing on as aggregates, sense-fields, and elements; examine all of this carefully, and no sentient being is to be found. Within and without, all of it is empty; even one who cultivates this very emptiness is, in the end, nowhere to be found. Self, and what is mine, definitely do not exist — they arise only through inverted, false conception; no being and no self exist at all — there exists only what arises through causes.

Chinese Source

諸行皆剎那，住尚無況用，即說彼生起，為用為作者。眼不能見色，耳不能聞聲，鼻不能齅香，舌不能嘗味，身不能覺觸，意不能知法，於此亦無能，任持驅役者。法不能生他，亦不能自生，眾緣有故生，非故新新有。法不能滅他，亦不能自滅，眾緣有故生，生已自然滅。

Ru-Yi Translation

All conditioned things are momentary — they scarcely even abide, let alone truly function; what is called their arising is merely spoken of as function, as an agent. The eye cannot itself see form; the ear cannot itself hear sound; the nose cannot itself smell fragrance; the tongue cannot itself taste flavor; the body cannot itself feel contact; the mind cannot itself know a mental object — and within all this there is no controller, no one directing or driving it. No phenomenon can produce another, nor can it produce itself; because the many conditions are present, it arises — not as something old continuing, but ever newly, freshly present. No phenomenon can extinguish another, nor can it extinguish itself; because the many conditions were present, it arose, and having arisen, it naturally ceases.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the first three verses as a methodical search-and-fail: examine the twelve links of dependent origination (ignorance through aging-and-death), examine the five aggregates, examine the sense-fields and elements — search everywhere a self might hide, inside the body or in the objects it perceives, and nothing satisfying "self" turns up, not even in the very awareness doing the searching. "Self" and "what is mine" exist only as the mind's own inverted projection layered onto what is, in reality, simply cause producing effect: afflictions producing action, action producing further conditioned states, endlessly.

The following five verses turn from searching for an owner to searching for real function itself, and find the same absence. Because every conditioned thing lasts only a single instant, it barely has time to exist, let alone to actually do anything — what looks like "seeing" or "producing" is simply how a rapid causal succession appears from the outside, not evidence of a genuine agent behind it. He walks through the seven specific functions the verses deny in turn: no sense faculty produces its own cognition unassisted (the eye alone does not see; seeing requires the full convergence of faculty, object, and consciousness together); nothing sustains or drives the process from behind the scenes; nothing produces another thing, and nothing produces itself (ruling out both other-causation and self-causation as ultimate explanations, leaving only the dependent arising of many-conditions-together); and, by the same logic, nothing genuinely destroys another or destroys itself — a thing that arose through conditions simply ceases on its own once those conditions no longer hold, no external "destroyer" required. The refrain running through both halves — "yet this is not simply nothing" — keeps the entire nine verses from tipping into nihilism: dependent origination is not empty of process, only empty of any fixed thing standing behind that process.

12. No-Self, Verses 10–16: Why Defilement Still Has Real Causes

Having shown that no fixed self exists anywhere, the treatise now guards against the opposite mistake: concluding that nothing real is happening at all. This block of seven verses shows that affliction — craving in householders, ignorance especially in renunciants of other paths — has a genuine, traceable causal structure, even though no self stands behind it, and closes by showing that this causal structure is itself neither self-caused, other-caused, nor uncaused, but simply dependent.

Chinese Source

由二品為依，是生便可得，恆於境放逸，又復邪昇進。愚癡之所漂，彼遂邪昇進，諸貪愛所引，於境常放逸。

Ru-Yi Translation

Resting on these two kinds — the householder and the renunciant — rebirth is indeed obtained: constantly heedless toward objects, and again advancing along a wrong path. Carried along by delusion, one advances along a wrong path; drawn along by craving, one is constantly heedless toward objects.

Chinese Source

由有因諸法，眾苦亦復然，根本二惑故，十二支分二。自無能作用，亦不由他作，非餘能有作，而作用非無。

Ru-Yi Translation

Because phenomena have their causes, the many sufferings likewise do; because of these two root afflictions, the twelve links divide into two groups. Nothing produces its own action by itself, nor is it produced by another; nor does anything else have the power to produce it — and yet the function is not simply absent.

Chinese Source

非內亦非外，非二種中間，由行未生故，有時而可得。設諸行已生，由此故無得，未來無有相，過去可分別。分別曾所更，非曾亦分別，行雖無有始，然有始可得。

Ru-Yi Translation

Not within, not without, not in between the two: because these formations have not yet arisen, only at the right time can they be found. Suppose these formations had already arisen — even so, they could not be found this way, since the future has no mark; only the past can be discerned. One discerns what one has already experienced, yet what has not been experienced can also be discerned; though conditioned things have no beginning, still a beginning can be found.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the opening pair of verses as diagnosing affliction by lifestyle: the householder, surrounded by sense pleasures, is heedlessly carried along mainly by craving, while the renunciant of a non-Buddhist path — having left home but without the Dharma's guidance — is carried along mainly by delusion, mistaking spiritual progress for what is actually just another kind of wandering. Both, lacking genuine wisdom, count as "advancing along a wrong path" even while believing themselves to be improving. The next two verses show this affliction's causal shape precisely: ignorance and craving, the two root afflictions, split the twelve links of dependent origination into two matched groups — ignorance and formations belong to affliction and action respectively, while consciousness through feeling belong to the resulting conditions of birth; craving, grasping, and existence repeat that same three-part pattern (affliction, action, birth) a second time, culminating in birth and aging-death. Crucially, no single link produces the next by itself, by another's power, or by anything external — and yet the whole sequence genuinely functions, because each link depends on the one before it, the exact middle way between "something causes it" and "nothing causes it."

The final three verses in this block, he notes, answer a specific objector's challenge: if a self cannot be found inside the body, outside it, or in any third location, does that mean future defilement — which has not yet arisen — cannot really be said to exist at all? The treatise's answer distinguishes what can and cannot be discerned by moment: the future, having no present mark, genuinely cannot be examined yet; the past, having already occurred, can be recalled and analyzed; and though the beginningless chain of dependent origination has, strictly speaking, no ultimate first cause, each present moment of experience does have its own findable starting point — the arising of ignorance or craving right now, in this life, is exactly where a practitioner's actual work of purification begins, regardless of how far back the chain as a whole extends. With defilement's causal structure now fully accounted for, the treatise turns next to its exact counterpart: ten verses tracing the equally real causal structure of purification.

13. No-Self, Verses 17–26: Ten Verses on Purification — Same Emptiness, Now Applied to Freedom

Where the previous block traced defilement's causal shape, this block of ten verses applies the identical two-truths method to purification itself: the aggregates seen through vivid similes, ignorance and karmic action shown equally empty of any fixed agent, and the continuity that carries a being from one life to the next explained without ever requiring a self to do the carrying.

Chinese Source

諸色如聚沫，諸受類浮泡，諸想同陽焰，諸行喻芭蕉，諸識猶幻事，日親之所說，諸行一時生，亦一時住滅。癡不能癡癡，亦不能癡彼，非餘能有癡，而愚癡非無。不正思惟故，諸愚癡得生，此不正思惟，非不愚者起。

Ru-Yi Translation

Form is like a heap of foam; feeling is like a floating bubble; perception is like a mirage; formations are like a plantain trunk; consciousness is like a conjured illusion — so the Kinsman of the Sun himself declared; conditioned things arise together, and abide and cease together. Delusion cannot deceive itself, nor can it deceive another; nothing else has the power to produce it — and yet delusion is not simply absent. Because of improper attention, delusion comes to arise; and this improper attention arises only in one who is already deluded, never in one who is free of delusion.

Chinese Source

福非福不動，行又三應知，復有三種業，一切不和合。現在速滅壞，過去住無方，未生依眾緣，而復心隨轉。畢竟共相應，不相應亦爾，非一切一切，而說心隨轉。

Ru-Yi Translation

Meritorious, demeritorious, and unmoving formations — these three should be known; and there are, further, three kinds of action — none of them ultimately combine with anything at all. What is present quickly perishes; what is past abides nowhere; what has not yet arisen depends on many conditions — and yet the mind is spoken of as flowing on accordingly. Whether ultimately conjoined or ultimately not conjoined, it is never simply everything conjoined with everything — and yet the mind is spoken of as flowing on accordingly.

Chinese Source

於此流無斷，相似不相似，由隨順我見，世俗用非無。若壞於色身，名身亦隨滅，而言今後世，自作自受果。前後差別故，自因果攝故，作者與受者，一異不可說。

Ru-Yi Translation

Within this uninterrupted stream — sometimes similar, sometimes dissimilar — because it accords with the view of a self, its conventional function is not simply absent. If the physical body is destroyed, the mental factors likewise cease with it; and yet it is said that in this life and the next, one reaps what one has sown. Because of difference between what came before and what comes after, yet because both are gathered within one and the same causal stream, whether the doer and the one who experiences the result are the same or different simply cannot be said.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the five classical similes for the aggregates as this fascicle's most memorable teaching image: form dissolves as quickly as foam on water, feeling rises and bursts like a bubble, perception is as insubstantial as a heat-shimmer mistaken for water, mental formations are as hollow at the core as a plantain trunk (peel away every layer and no solid center remains), and consciousness is as convincing yet unreal as a street magician's conjured elephant. "The Kinsman of the Sun" is simply an epithet for the Buddha, born of the solar-descended Śākya clan. He reads the next four lines as applying the prior section's exact logic to ignorance itself: ignorance cannot deceive itself, cannot be produced by anything external, and yet genuinely arises — from nothing more mysterious than improper attention, which itself can only arise in a mind already touched by delusion, a closed causal loop with no need for, and no room for, a controlling self anywhere inside it.

On the following four verses, he explains "ultimately not conjoined" as the treatise's characteristically blunt way of saying that karmic formations, examined moment by moment, never actually coexist with the consciousness supposedly carrying them: the present instant vanishes as soon as it arises, the past no longer exists anywhere to unite with anything, and the future has not yet arrived — so strictly speaking, action and consciousness are never simultaneously "there" to combine. And yet — the refrain insists — conventionally, functionally, karmic continuity still operates exactly as expected, this is what makes the teaching genuinely a middle way rather than either eternalism or annihilationism. The closing block then applies this same non-substantial continuity across death itself: even though the physical body and its mental factors both cease completely, moment-to-moment causal continuity (later fascicles will identify this as the ālaya-consciousness's own unbroken stream) still carries karmic ripening from one life into the next, driven along by the self-view innate in an unawakened mind — which is why the treatise can say, without contradiction, that one reaps in a future life what one sowed in this one, while still denying that any fixed self travels between the two. Whether the "one who acts" in a past life and the "one who experiences the result" in a later life are the same person or a different one, Cháng Bǎi notes, is consequently a question the treatise refuses to answer with a simple yes or no — exactly the kind of principled inexpressibility already met in this ground's earlier sections (2 and 9). With defilement and purification both accounted for through the same two-truths method, the treatise turns next to explaining, across five verses, precisely how cause and effect can operate without a self to anchor them — the deepest question this entire sequence has been building toward.

14. No-Self, Verses 27–31: How Self-View Itself Arises Without Any Self Behind It

Having shown that neither defilement nor purification requires a self, the treatise turns to the deepest question the whole sequence has been building toward: if there truly is no self, why does the sense of "I" feel so immediate and undeniable to begin with? Five verses trace self-view to its actual root — a deep, wordless misreading of the storehouse consciousness itself.

Chinese Source

因道不斷故，和合作用轉，從自因所生，及攝受所作。樂戲論為因，若淨不淨業，諸種子異熟，及愛非愛果。

Ru-Yi Translation

Because the causal stream is never broken, its combined function continues to operate: arising from its own proper cause, and shaped by what has been taken up and retained. Delight in conceptual proliferation is the cause; and whether the action is pure or impure, its seeds ripen into result — into what is agreeable and what is disagreeable.

Chinese Source

依諸種異熟，我見而生起，自內所證知，無色不可見。無了別凡夫，計斯為內我，我見為依故，起眾多妄見。總執自種故，宿習助伴故，聽聞隨順故，發生於我見。

Ru-Yi Translation

Resting on the ripening of these seeds, the view of a self arises; it is known only through one's own inner experience — formless, and so not visible to others. The unperceiving ordinary being takes this to be an inner self; and resting on this self-view, many further false views arise. Because of the wholesale grasping of one's own seeds, because of habituation carried from before, and because of hearing and following along with what one hears, the view of a self comes to be generated.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this block as the sequence's psychological turning point, moving from proving no-self in the abstract to explaining why belief in a self feels so natural anyway. The unbroken causal stream — later fascicles will name it explicitly as the storehouse consciousness (ālayavijñāna), already glimpsed under other descriptions in earlier fascicles' treatment of seeds and habituation — continues moment to moment because delight in conceptual proliferation (the mind's own compulsive habit of elaborating experience into fixed categories) keeps planting fresh seeds, which ripen in due course into pleasant or unpleasant results depending on whether the originating action was wholesome or not. None of this requires a self; it requires only seeds, conditions, and their ripening.

The crucial move comes next: the seventh consciousness (manas, not yet named explicitly here but clearly intended) takes the storehouse consciousness's own subtle, ever-flowing continuity — something genuinely experienced from within, but never actually inspected or verified — and simply misreads it as "myself, abiding within." Cháng Bǎi stresses this innate self-view is not learned from anyone; it is "known only through one's own inner experience," arising spontaneously in every unawakened being prior to any teaching or philosophy. From that first innate misreading spring the many further, learned varieties of wrong view — the layered account the treatise gives for why self-view has three distinct roots: the wholesale, spontaneous grasping of one's own mental continuum (innate and universal), habituation carried over from views held in past lives, and views freshly absorbed by hearing and uncritically following others' opinions in this one. Only the first of the three is inborn; the latter two depend on specific causes and can, in principle, be undone through right teaching — which is precisely why hearing, reflecting on, and eventually meditating on exactly this kind of analysis (the very activity this fascicle is walking a practitioner through) is capable of loosening self-view's grip. With cause and effect now fully explained without recourse to any self, the treatise turns to two remaining topics: suffering and its origin, and cessation and the path.

15. No-Self, Verses 32–44: Suffering's Grip, and the Forty-Four Verses Close

The closing thirteen verses complete the sequence: five verses on how self-clinging toward the storehouse consciousness actually produces suffering, and eight verses on cessation and the path — the storehouse consciousness compared to a vast pool no ordinary force can dry up, followed by a full account of what liberation actually consists in. With these, all forty-four verses establishing no-self are complete.

Chinese Source

貪愛及與緣，而生於內我，攝受希望故，染習外為所。世間真可怖，愚癡故攝受，先起愛藏已，由茲趣戲論。彼所愛藏者，賢聖達為苦，此苦逼愚夫，剎那無暫息。不平等纏心，積集彼眾苦，積集是愚夫，計我苦樂緣。諸愚夫固著，如大象溺泥，由癡故增上，遍行遍所作。

Ru-Yi Translation

Craving, together with its conditions, gives rise to the sense of an inner self; through taking hold of what is already had, and longing for what is not, one becomes habituated to treating the outer world as one's own. The world is truly fearsome, yet the deluded still cling to it; having first taken up this cherished clinging, one then proceeds into endless conceptual elaboration. What is thus cherished and clung to, the noble ones recognize as suffering itself; this suffering presses upon the deluded moment after moment, without the slightest respite. An unbalanced mind entangled in affliction accumulates these many sufferings; it is the deluded who accumulate in this way, taking self to be the very condition of pain and pleasure alike. The deluded cling fast, like a great elephant mired in mud; through delusion's own increase, it pervades and drives everything done.

Chinese Source

此池派眾流，於世流為暴，非火風日竭，唯除正法行。於苦計我受，苦樂了知苦，分別此起見，從彼生生彼。染汙意恆時，諸惑俱生滅，若解脫諸惑，非先亦非後。

Ru-Yi Translation

This pool sends forth many streams, more violent than any worldly flood; neither fire, nor wind, nor sun can dry it up — only the practice of the true Dharma can. Toward suffering, one conceives a self that experiences it, taking what is pleasant and unpleasant alike to be suffering; the discriminations arising from this give rise to views — arising from that same source, and in turn giving rise to it again. The defiled mind, at every moment, arises and ceases together with its afflictions; when freed from these afflictions, it is neither freed before them nor after them.

Chinese Source

非彼法生已，後淨異而生，彼先無染汙，說解脫眾惑。其有染汙者，畢竟性清淨，既非有所淨，何得有能淨。諸種子滅故，諸煩惱盡故，即於此無染，顯示二差別。

Ru-Yi Translation

It is not that a defiled state first arises, and then, afterward, a separate purity arises to replace it; that mind was, from the start, never actually defiled — this is what is meant by liberation from the afflictions. What appears defiled is, in its ultimate nature, already pure; since there is nothing there to be purified, how could there be anything that purifies it? Because the seeds are extinguished, and because the afflictions themselves are exhausted, this very freedom from defilement reveals two distinct kinds of liberation.

Chinese Source

自內所證故，唯眾苦盡故，永絕戲論故，一切無戲論。眾生名相續，及法想相中，無生死流轉，亦無涅槃者。

Ru-Yi Translation

Because it is realized only within, because it is simply the exhaustion of all suffering, because conceptual elaboration is utterly cut off — in this, there is no conceptual elaboration whatsoever. Within what is called "a sentient being's continuity" and within the notion of "phenomena," there is, in truth, neither one who flows on through birth and death, nor one who attains nirvāṇa.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi traces suffering's actual mechanism through these five verses: craving toward the storehouse consciousness first produces the inner sense of self, which then extends outward as grasping at external things already possessed and longing for what is not yet had — and because ordinary beings, in their delusion, still cherish this very process despite its being genuinely fearsome, they plunge into ever more elaborate conceptual proliferation built on top of it. What the noble ones recognize as suffering — the storehouse consciousness's own subtle, moment-to-moment pressure (the "suffering of formations" met in earlier fascicles) — presses on the deluded without any pause, precisely because they remain, like an elephant sunk in mud, unable to extract themselves from the very clinging that constitutes the affliction in the first place.

He reads the storehouse consciousness's comparison to a bottomless pool as this sequence's most vivid closing image: an ordinary body of water can be dried by drought, wind, or sun, but the storehouse consciousness's endless outflow of seeds ripening into new existence can be stopped by nothing worldly — only by actually practicing the path. The next block then answers the deepest remaining question: if the mind is never truly defiled in its ultimate nature (a teaching he connects to the earlier "purity of self-nature" section's own careful handling of provisional versus fully-intended teaching), what does liberation actually consist in? His answer follows the verses precisely: not one state literally replacing another, but the extinguishing of both karmic seeds and their present afflictions together — yielding what the tradition names as two distinct liberations, freedom while the aggregates still remain (afflictions gone, the body still living) and freedom without any remainder at all (full nirvāṇa at death, realized entirely within, beyond every possible conceptual elaboration). At this point the very question of whether "the one who suffered" and "the one now liberated" are the same person or different persons simply no longer applies — a being's continuity and the very notion of discrete "things" were themselves only ever conventional designations, so once no-self is genuinely realized, neither samsaric wandering nor nirvāṇa remain as objects for anyone to attain.

With this verse, the forty-four-verse sequence establishing the ultimate truth of no-self — one verse of summary statement and forty-three verses of detailed unpacking, spanning defilement, purification, self-view's own arising, suffering, and liberation — is complete in full. The treatise now turns to a long prose passage explaining these same verses' deeper purpose and working through them clause by clause.

16. The Prose Commentary Opens: Two Extremes to Avoid, and What "Agent" Actually Means

Having quoted all forty-four verses in full, the treatise now works back through them in prose — clause by clause, in far greater technical depth than the verses alone convey. This long commentary (running to roughly 1,700 lines, nearly the length of the verse sequence itself) opens by stating its purpose directly, then begins its detailed pass with the opening verse's key terms: what "ruler," "agent," and "experiencer" actually refer to once wrongly-imagined selfhood is removed.

Chinese Source

此中依止補特伽羅無我勝義，宣說如是勝義伽他。為欲對治增益、損減二邊執故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Resting on the ultimate truth of the selflessness of persons, these ultimate-meaning verses are taught, in order to counteract two extreme attachments: the extreme of over-imputation, and the extreme of under-imputation.

Chinese Source

於所攝受說為宰主，於諸業用說為作者，於諸果報說為受者。如是半頌，遮遣別義所分別我。諸法亦無用者，遮遣即法所分別我。由此遠離增益邊執。而用轉非無者，顯法有性。由此遠離損減邊執。

Ru-Yi Translation

Regarding what is taken up as the aggregates, one speaks provisionally of a "ruler"; regarding their various functions, one speaks provisionally of an "agent"; regarding their results, one speaks provisionally of an "experiencer." This much refutes the imagined self conceived as separate from the aggregates. "The constituents have no such function of their own" refutes the imagined self conceived as identical with the aggregates. By this, the extreme of over-imputation is abandoned. "Yet the functioning that occurs is not simply nothing" reveals that phenomena genuinely have a nature. By this, the extreme of under-imputation is abandoned.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the opening statement of purpose as the single most important sentence in this entire commentary, because it names the two failure modes the whole forty-four-verse sequence has been navigating between from the start: over-imputation is inventing a self that is not there — whether imagined as something separate from the five aggregates (a soul riding inside the body, unaffected by its changes) or imagined as simply identical with the aggregates themselves (mistaking the ever-changing body-mind for a fixed "I"); under-imputation is the opposite error, denying that dependent origination itself genuinely functions — the nihilistic misreading of emptiness already warned against in this ground's second section. Both errors, he stresses, miss the actual middle way: no fixed self anywhere, yet real causal functioning throughout.

He reads the commentary's return to the three terms "ruler," "agent," and "experiencer" as filling in exactly what those words are allowed to mean once a real self is removed: "ruler" is simply a manner of speaking about the five aggregates' own condition, not a separate controller standing over them; "agent" is simply a manner of speaking about the actual functioning of the six sense faculties as they engage their objects and generate karma, not a hidden self pulling levers behind the scenes; and "experiencer" is simply a manner of speaking about karmic results as they ripen, not a persisting person who collects them. Cháng Bǎi notes this three-way breakdown is exactly why the treatise can use ordinary language — "I did this," "I feel that" — without contradiction: such language works as genuine convention describing real function, the moment it stops being mistaken for a report about some fixed entity underneath. This detailed clause-by-clause commentary continues through all forty-four verses in the same spirit — filling out the constructive, functional account beneath each denial — and the sections that follow will track its most significant additions rather than repeat ground the verses themselves have already covered.

17. The Fourfold Refutation: Not Self-Made, Not Other-Made, Not Both, Not Uncaused

Deep within the prose commentary's re-explanation of the seven verses on defilement, the treatise pauses to make explicit a formal argument only implicit in the verses themselves: karmic action and its results cannot be produced by a self acting alone, by another acting alone, by both together, or by nothing at all — leaving dependent origination, many-conditions-together, as the only coherent account left standing.

Chinese Source

自無能作用者，待善惡友，他所引故。亦不由他作者，待自功用所成辦故。非餘能有作者，要待前生因差別故，方有所作。

Ru-Yi Translation

"Nothing produces its own action by itself": because it depends on being drawn out by good or bad companions, by others. "Nor is it produced by another": because it depends on being brought to completion by one's own effort. "Nor does anything else have the power to produce it": because it depends on the specific prior cause from an earlier life — only then does anything come to be produced.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies this as the treatise's own version of an argument-form found across Buddhist philosophical literature (he cross-references its appearance already in the Āgama collection): action and its karmic result cannot ultimately be traced to any of four possible sources. Not to oneself alone — a seed, however present, cannot ripen without supporting conditions like the influence of good or bad companions. Not to another alone — even the strongest outside influence produces nothing without one's own will and effort actually taking it up. Not to both together, understood as some third independent thing — because there is, in the end, nothing beyond one's own seed meeting its supporting conditions; "both together" is not a further ingredient, just the same dependent relationship named twice. And explicitly not to no cause at all — the entire twelve-link chain runs strictly on the principle "because this exists, that exists; because this arises, that arises," which categorically rules out uncaused, random arising.

What survives every branch of this fourfold elimination, he notes, is exactly dependent origination itself: not self-made, not other-made, not jointly made by some third combining agent, and not uncaused, but simply arising because supporting conditions converge, exactly as the treatise has insisted throughout this entire no-self sequence. He treats this passage as the commentary's clearest statement yet of why the two-truths method used throughout this sequence is not merely a rhetorical trick but a genuinely exhaustive analysis: every alternative account of causation collapses under scrutiny except the one dependent origination actually describes. The commentary continues in this same rigorous vein through the remaining thirty-some verses — walking clause by clause through the seventeen "has-cause" verses, the eighteen verses proper on no-self, and the verses on suffering and the path already translated in full — filling in, at every step, exactly this kind of formal precision behind what the verses themselves state more compactly. Rather than retrace each step, the next section turns to this fascicle's third and final major topic once the commentary concludes: the verses on the three trainings.

18. The Three Trainings Open: Brahma's Question, and the Buddha's Threefold Answer

With the no-self verses and their commentary complete, the treatise turns to its second set of verses: fifty-one in all, framed as a dialogue in which the great Brahmā King asks the Buddha directly what a practitioner should actually train in. The Buddha's answer names the three trainings — precepts, concentration, and wisdom — that structure the rest of this section.

Chinese Source

已釋勝義聖教伽他。次當建立意趣義伽他。如經言：一時索訶世界主大梵天王往世尊所，頂禮佛足，退坐一面，以妙伽他而讚請曰：於學到究竟，善斷諸疑網，今請學所學，修學為我說。

Ru-Yi Translation

The ultimate-meaning verses have now been explained. Next, the verses on essential intent should be established. As the scripture says: at one time, the great Brahmā King, lord of the Sahā world, went to where the Blessed One was, bowed at his feet, withdrew to sit to one side, and with a sublime verse praised him and made this request: "Having reached the very culmination of training, skillfully cutting through every net of doubt — I now ask what should be trained in, and how one should train; please, teach it to me."

Chinese Source

大仙應善聽，學略有三種，增上戒心慧，於彼當修學。

Ru-Yi Translation

Great sage, listen well: training, briefly, has three kinds — the higher training in precepts, in mind, and in wisdom. It is in these that one should train.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the great Brahmā King as the ruling deity of the first meditative heaven, already accomplished in the four immeasurables and genuinely compassionate — his praise verse honors the Buddha's own training as fully complete (self-benefit) and his skill at resolving others' doubts (benefit to others), before formally requesting instruction. Cháng Bǎi is careful to note, citing an exchange recorded in the Mahāvibhāṣā, that the Buddha's own reply elsewhere makes clear he cannot simply dissolve a listener's doubt by fiat — "I have no power in myself to free you from doubt; you must see the sublime Dharma yourself before you can cross the flood" — so what follows is not a shortcut around practice but an actual curriculum.

He glosses "higher" (增上) in its two senses used throughout the treatise: what a training leads toward, and what it most powerfully enables — precepts lead toward, and most powerfully enable, concentration; concentration leads toward, and most powerfully enables, liberating wisdom; wisdom leads toward, and most powerfully enables, liberation itself. He adds a well-known anecdote to make the same point vivid: a would-be disciple, put off on hearing the two hundred fifty monastic precepts, told the Buddha he would not ordain — until the Buddha said the entire code came down to just one rule, watching one's own mind; the disciple ordained on the spot and later attained arhatship. The remaining forty-nine verses that follow, Cháng Bǎi notes, are entirely devoted to unpacking this single opening verse's three terms in full — precisely the material the next several sections will work through.

19. Six Supports for Precepts, and How the Three Trainings Actually Unfold

The verses now turn practical: what precisely supports genuine precept-keeping, in what order the three trainings actually arise, how each purifies in stages, what result success or failure in each actually brings, and how the three trainings can be practiced both separately and together depending on a practitioner's stage.

Chinese Source

應圓滿六支，四樂住成就，於四各四行，智慧常清淨。初善住根本，次樂心寂靜，後聖見惡見，相應不相應。

Ru-Yi Translation

One should perfect the six supports, and accomplish the four blissful abidings; with the four aspects applied fourfold to each truth, wisdom becomes ever pure. At first, one dwells well in the root; next, one delights in the mind's own stillness; afterward, one accords with the noble view and no longer with wrong views.

Chinese Source

先淨樂靜慮，及於諦善巧，即於諸諦中，應生遠增長。於諸學處中，有四趣三所，遠離於二趣，於二趣證得。

Ru-Yi Translation

First, purity; then delight in meditative absorption; and skill regarding the truths — within these truths, the path should arise, the origins of suffering should be left behind, and cessation should be increased. Within these trainings there are four destinations tied to the three trainings: two destinations to be abandoned, and two destinations to be attained.

Chinese Source

二安住二種，一能趣涅槃，漸次為因緣，純雜而修習。

Ru-Yi Translation

Two trainings each support a further two in turn; the highest alone leads directly to nirvāṇa; each stage becomes the condition for what follows, whether practiced singly or in combination.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses the "six supports" that perfect precepts as a checklist already familiar in outline from the monastic discipline covered in earlier fascicles: abiding continuously in pure precepts as the foundation; guarding each individual rule of personal liberation; perfecting one's conduct and daily activities according to proper form; keeping only to fit locations (he lists the five classically unsuitable haunts — a place issuing false decrees, a brothel, a tavern, a royal court, and an outcaste's dwelling); feeling real dread even at minor faults, prompting swift confession; and actually undertaking to learn the precepts' content, not merely their letter. The "four blissful abidings" are the four meditative absorptions, three with genuine pleasant feeling and the fourth with the subtler bliss of equanimity; and the "four aspects applied fourfold" is the classical sixteen-aspect analysis of the four noble truths (impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self for suffering; cause, origin, arising, and condition for its origination; and matching fourfold analyses for cessation and the path) — standard Abhidharma material the treatise assumes rather than re-derives here.

He reads the sequencing verses as tracing one continuous arc: precepts come first as the foundation everything else rests on; concentration's inner stillness follows, delighted in for its own calm; and only then does wisdom mature into actual alignment with the noble view, decisively parting ways with wrong view. Purification runs the same course in three stages — precepts purify conduct, concentration purifies the mind's agitation, and wisdom purifies understanding itself, at which point the path should be actively cultivated while suffering's origin is abandoned and cessation is progressively deepened, tracking the standard śrāvaka path from stream-entry's initial 112 severed afflictions through the arhat's full 128. On results, he maps each training onto its own pair of destinations: precepts determine simply whether one is reborn favorably or unfavorably within the desire realm — both destinations the treatise says should ultimately be abandoned, since even a favorable desire-realm rebirth remains dangerously distracting; concentration opens rebirth in the form and formless realms; and wisdom alone opens the path to nirvāṇa itself — the two destinations genuinely worth attaining. The closing verse then explains how the three trainings interlock: in the earliest stage a practitioner cultivates each one at a time in strict sequence (precepts supporting concentration, concentration supporting wisdom), but at the most advanced stage all three operate simultaneously within a single act of liberating insight — separate practice at the start, combined practice at the finish, with the deepest wisdom alone carrying a practitioner all the way to nirvāṇa.

20. Wisdom's Supremacy, and Which Trainings Share Their Results

Continuing the general treatment of the three trainings together, the verses now name wisdom as the trainings' capstone — the only one that actually ends conceptual proliferation — before working out precisely which trainings share their fruits with one another and which stand apart, and distinguishing genuine practitioners of all three from those who merely imitate the form.

Chinese Source

從此心解脫，永滅諸戲論，諸學是為尊，於此學聰叡。若行趣不淨，亦趣於善趣，是行說為初，當知此非共。若行趣清淨，非諸趣究竟，是行說為中，當知亦非共。若行趣清淨，於諸趣究竟，是行說為尊，當知此必共。

Ru-Yi Translation

From this, the mind is liberated, and all conceptual proliferation is forever extinguished; this training is the most eminent — in this one becomes truly skilled. Where practice leads to impurity, or else to a favorable rebirth, this is called the first practice; know that it shares nothing with the others. Where practice leads to purity, yet not to the ultimate end of any rebirth, this is called the middle practice; know that this too shares nothing with the others. Where practice leads to purity and to the ultimate end of all rebirth, this is called the eminent practice; know that this one necessarily shares in the others.

Chinese Source

若有學無學，當知并聰叡，若有學無學，當知并愚夫。

Ru-Yi Translation

Where there is genuine training and freedom from wrong training, know this together as the truly skilled; where there is wrong training and no genuine training, know this together as the foolish.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads wisdom's culminating verse as the direct echo of the no-self sequence's own closing teaching (section 15): the final liberation from all conceptual proliferation, whether the aggregates and the one liberated are the same or different, no longer even arises as a question — which is exactly why wisdom, not precepts or concentration, is named "most eminent" among the three. The next three verses then sort the trainings by a precise logic of shared and unshared cause-and-effect: precepts alone determine only a favorable or unfavorable rebirth within the sense-desire realm, and share nothing with concentration's or wisdom's results; concentration alone opens the form and formless realms but likewise shares nothing with the others, since worldly meditative absorption — attainable even by non-Buddhist practitioners — merely suppresses affliction temporarily without uprooting it; but wisdom's own fruit, final nirvāṇa, is different — reaching it requires precepts and concentration already in place as its foundation, so wisdom alone "necessarily shares" in what came before it, even though precepts and concentration considered on their own terms do not reach nirvāṇa at all. This is the answer to why wisdom stands in a category by itself among the three.

The closing verse then draws the sharpest possible line between genuine and counterfeit practice: what matters is not merely going through the motions of precepts, meditation, and study, but whether that practice is free of wrong view underneath — someone who keeps every outward form yet holds views incompatible with the Dharma is, despite appearances, still counted among the foolish, while someone whose training in all three is genuinely aligned with right view, however outwardly modest, is counted among the truly skilled. With the trainings' shared and unshared fruits and the wise/foolish distinction both established, the treatise turns to the last three topics rounding out the general overview: how the trainings mature in stages, what specific causes produce each, and how they combine as a practitioner advances.

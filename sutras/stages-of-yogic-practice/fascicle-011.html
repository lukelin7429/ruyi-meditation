Fascicle 11 · 瑜伽師地論卷第十一 · A New Ground Begins

Seven Names for Stillness, and the Four Doors Into It

Fascicle 10 closed the entire three-grounds section that had run since fascicle 4 — the treatise's long survey of the three realms, their beings, and the mechanics of karma and rebirth. Fascicle 11 opens something genuinely new: the Ground of Meditative Equipoise (三摩呬多地), the treatise's own dedicated, systematic account of meditative absorption itself — what actually happens, structurally, when a mind settles into concentration, from the first stirrings of the four dhyānas onward.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 11 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. A New Ground Opens: Seven Names for the Same Stillness

The treatise turns from the three grounds just completed to its sixth of seventeen main grounds, the Ground of Meditative Equipoise (三摩呬多地, samāhita-bhūmi) — and before analyzing it, surveys seven near-synonyms Sanskrit uses for meditative attainment, each capturing a slightly different facet of the same underlying stillness: equipoise itself, one-pointed unification, actual attainment, quieted contemplation, one-pointedness of mind, calm-abiding, and dwelling in present happiness.

Chinese Source

已說有尋有伺等三。云何三摩呬多地？

Ru-Yi Translation

The Ground with Applied and Sustained Thought, and the two grounds following it, have now been fully explained. What is the Ground of Meditative Equipoise?

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi situates this opening within the treatise's full seventeen-ground architecture: the first two grounds (the five sense-consciousnesses, and the mind ground) established the basic apparatus of cognition; the three grounds just completed, spanning fascicles 4 through 10, surveyed the three realms, their beings, and the entire causal mechanics of karma and rebirth in exhaustive detail. Grounds six through nine — Meditative Equipoise, Non-Equipoise, the Ground With Mind, and the Ground Without Mind — now turn to mapping the actual states a mind can occupy, before the treatise moves on to the shared path of the three vehicles (grounds ten through twelve: hearing, reflection, cultivation) and then each vehicle's own distinct path (the śrāvaka, pratyekabuddha, and bodhisattva grounds), closing with the grounds of the two nirvāṇas already explained in fascicle 9. Ground six, he notes, is where the treatise finally turns from describing the raw material of saṃsāra to describing the tool that actually escapes it.

He unpacks the seven Sanskrit near-synonyms the source material surveys before analysis begins, each stressing a different nuance: samāhita (equipoise) stresses a mind drawn evenly toward stillness, free of both dullness and agitation, and covers the broadest territory — with mind or without, contaminated or uncontaminated; samādhi (one-pointed unification, historically rendered "samādhi" or "concentration") stresses the mind held level before and after, and covers ordinary scattered-mind states as well as full absorption; samāpatti (actual attainment) stresses a depth of settling that reaches all the way to attainment, whether or not conceptual activity remains; dhyāna (quieted contemplation, the oldest term, "chán" in Chinese) stresses a mind that is both stilled and actively investigating at once, wisdom and stillness in balance — which is exactly why the four dhyānas, uniquely among meditative states, are considered ideal ground for realizing the noble path, while the four formless attainments, strong in stillness but weak in active wisdom, are not; citta-ikāgratā (one-pointedness of mind) stresses sustained, repeated recollection of a single object until it becomes continuous; śamatha (calm-abiding) stresses the mind's coming to rest on one object; and dṛṣṭadharma-sukhavihāra (dwelling in present happiness) names specifically the four dhyānas themselves, each carrying its own measure of joy, bliss, or equanimity, distinguishing full absorption from its own preliminary access states, which do not yet count as "dwelling in happiness" in this technical sense.

2. Four Doors Named: Solitude, Stillness, Renouncing Joy, and Purified Equanimity

The treatise names the four topics that will organize this entire ground — the four dhyānas, the eight liberations, eleven kinds of unification, and six kinds of attainment — then defines the four dhyānas themselves, each named for the specific renunciation that opens its door: the first dhyāna arising from solitude while thought is still active, the second arising from settled stillness once thought falls away, the third arising from renouncing joy itself, and the fourth from purifying even equanimity and mindfulness.

Chinese Source

謂此地中略有四種。一者、靜慮，二者、解脫，三者、等持，四者、等至。靜慮者，謂四靜慮。一、從離生有尋有伺靜慮，二、從定生無尋無伺靜慮，三、離喜靜慮，四、捨念清淨靜慮。

Ru-Yi Translation

Briefly, this ground has four parts: first, the meditative absorptions; second, the liberations; third, unifications; fourth, attainments. The meditative absorptions are the four dhyānas. First, the dhyāna with applied and sustained thought, arising from solitude. Second, the dhyāna free of applied and sustained thought, arising from settled concentration. Third, the dhyāna of renouncing joy. Fourth, the dhyāna of purified equanimity and mindfulness.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi previews how these four topics will unfold across the fascicle: the four dhyānas (靜慮) are the foundational states themselves; the eight liberations (解脫) name eight specific ways affliction is actually suppressed through meditative mastery; the eleven unifications (等持, samādhi in its narrower technical sense) survey concentration's many varieties; and the six attainments (等至, samāpatti) round out the picture with states reaching even deeper than ordinary absorption. He notes each dhyāna is named precisely for what it has just renounced to arrive: the first dhyāna, reached through the classical method of applied and sustained thought, has left behind the desire realm's craving, hatred, and harmfulness, and carries five distinguishing qualities — applied thought, sustained thought, joy, bliss, and one-pointedness of mind; the second, reached by observing applied and sustained thought's own coarse disturbance and letting it settle, has left even those behind, carrying inner clarity, joy, bliss, and one-pointedness; the third, reached by observing joy's own agitating quality, has renounced joy itself while retaining the second dhyāna's bliss, now settled into mindfulness, clear comprehension, equanimity, bliss, and one-pointedness; and the fourth, reached by observing that even bliss is itself a subtle disturbance, has renounced bliss as well, arriving at purified equanimity, purified mindfulness, neutral feeling, and one-pointedness — each dhyāna, in turn, is what the one before it looks like once its own subtlest remaining agitation has also been seen through and released.

3. Eight Liberations: Using Ugliness, Then Beauty, to Break the Same Attachment Two Ways

The treatise names the eight liberations — a progression of meditative mastery running from contemplating repulsive objects while still perceiving form, through the same practice once inner form-perception has dropped away, to a striking reversal where the meditator deliberately contemplates a pure and beautiful object to prove affliction no longer arises even there, and finally through all four formless attainments to the cessation of perception and feeling itself.

Chinese Source

解脫者，謂八解脫。一、有色觀諸色解脫，二、內無色想觀外諸色解脫，三、淨解脫身作證具足住解脫，四、空無邊處解脫，五、識無邊處解脫，六、無所有處解脫，七、非想非非想處解脫，八、想受滅身作證具足住解脫。

Ru-Yi Translation

The liberations are the eight liberations. First, liberation through contemplating forms while still perceiving form. Second, liberation through contemplating outward forms while no longer perceiving form inwardly. Third, liberation through the pure absorption directly realized and fully dwelt in. Fourth, liberation of the sphere of limitless space. Fifth, liberation of the sphere of limitless consciousness. Sixth, liberation of the sphere of nothing whatsoever. Seventh, liberation of the sphere of neither perception nor non-perception. Eighth, liberation through the cessation of perception and feeling, directly realized and fully dwelt in.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi traces the classical logic behind each step: the first liberation has a practitioner still holding some inward perception of form contemplate outward forms as repulsive (resting on the first dhyāna), breaking the desire realm's attachment to beauty through deliberate exposure to ugliness; the second repeats the same repulsive contemplation once inward form-perception has itself been let go (resting on the second dhyāna), to confirm the release is genuinely stable rather than merely suppressed by the first dhyāna's own coarser state; the third liberation is the classical tradition's most striking reversal — resting on the fourth dhyāna, the practitioner now deliberately contemplates something beautiful and pure, proving mastery complete precisely by no longer being disturbed even by what used to provoke the strongest craving, "directly realized and fully dwelt in" marking this as no longer a technique but an accomplished, stable liberation.

The fourth through seventh liberations are the four formless attainments themselves, each releasing one further layer of dependency exactly as fascicle 5's own earlier survey described (space releasing the body, consciousness releasing the space-object, nothingness releasing consciousness-as-object, and neither-perception-nor-non-perception releasing even that subtle remainder); and the eighth, cessation of perception and feeling, available only to a non-returner or arhat, is the deepest attainment the path recognizes, a temporary cessation of mental activity itself used as a genuine rest from even the subtlest formless states. He notes the whole sequence is traditionally called the "eight forms of turning away" (八背捨) precisely because each one is defined by what it deliberately turns from — ugliness first to break craving, then beauty to prove craving is genuinely gone, then each successive formless dependency in turn.

4. Eleven Kinds of Unification, Six Kinds of Attainment — the Opening Outline Closes

The treatise finishes naming this ground's four topics: eleven ways concentration itself can be classified — by its object (emptiness, aimlessness, signlessness), by whether thought remains, by scale, by completeness of cultivation, by accompanying feeling, by its goal, and by the practitioner's own standing on the path — and six kinds of attainment, from the five directly-observed absorptions through the eight forms of mastery, the ten totality-spheres, the four formless spheres, the attainment without perception, and cessation itself. Full elaboration of every item is deferred to later in the fascicle; this opening section only names them.

Chinese Source

等持者，謂三三摩地。一、空，二、無願，三、無相。復有三種。謂有尋有伺、無尋唯伺、無尋無伺。復有三種。謂小、大、無量。復有二種。謂一分修、具分修。復有三種。謂喜俱行、樂俱行、捨俱行。復有四種。謂四修定。復有五種。謂五聖智三摩地。復有五種。謂聖五支三摩地。復有有因有具聖正三摩地。復有金剛喻三摩地。復有有學、無學、非學非無學等三摩地。

Ru-Yi Translation

Unification is the three concentrations: emptiness, aimlessness, and signlessness. There are, further, three more: with applied and sustained thought, without applied thought but with sustained thought alone, and without either. There are, further, three more: small, great, and immeasurable. There are, further, two more: cultivated only in part, and cultivated in full. There are, further, three more: accompanied by joy, by bliss, and by equanimity. There are, further, four more: the four cultivations of concentration. There are, further, five more: the five concentrations of noble wisdom. There are, further, five more: the five noble limbs of concentration. There is, further, the noble right concentration that has both a cause and its full equipment. There is, further, the vajra-like concentration. There are, further, three more: belonging to one still in training, to one beyond training, and to neither.

等至者，謂五現見三摩鉢底、八勝處三摩鉢底、十遍處三摩鉢底、四無色三摩鉢底、無想三摩鉢底、滅盡定等三摩鉢底。

Attainment is the directly-observed attainments, the attainments of the eight forms of mastery, the attainments of the ten totality-spheres, the four formless attainments, the attainment without perception, and the attainment of cessation, among others.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi flags this reuse of the "with applied and sustained thought / with sustained thought alone / with neither" threefold scheme as a genuine terminological trap: it is the exact same three names that organized the entire three-grounds arc spanning fascicles 4 through 10 (有尋有伺地・無尋唯伺地・無尋無伺地), but there it classified entire realms of existence, while here it classifies a single meditative state's own internal structure — the same three labels doing two structurally different jobs, one cosmological and one psychological, and worth keeping cleanly separate. He previews that most of these eleven categories, and all six attainments, receive no elaboration here — this section is purely a naming pass, with full explanation deferred until roughly a third of the way into the fascicle, once the groundwork of what actually distinguishes meditative equipoise from ordinary concentration has been laid.

He notes the emptiness/aimlessness/signlessness triad and the noble-limb triad both reach ahead to teachings usually associated with much later sections of the treatise (the three gateways to liberation, and the eightfold noble path's meditation-relevant limbs), while the vajra-like concentration — named for its capacity to shatter every remaining affliction the way a diamond shatters any other substance — marks the single decisive moment a trainee becomes an arhat, the last concentration of the path of cultivation immediately preceding final, complete direct realization.

5. What Actually Makes a State of Mind "Equipoise": Not Desire-Realm Focus, But No-Regret's Whole Causal Chain

Before defining the four dhyānas in detail, the treatise establishes what "meditative equipoise" (等引) actually means, and sharply distinguishes it from desire-realm one-pointedness: equipoise is drawn forth specifically by an unbroken causal chain running from purified ethical conduct, through freedom from regret, to genuine joy, bliss, and finally settled concentration — a chain the desire realm's own one-pointed attention, however genuinely focused or even carefully observant, never actually completes.

Chinese Source

謂唯此等名等引地，非於欲界心一境性。由此定等無悔、歡喜、安樂所引；欲界不爾。非欲界中於法全無審正觀察。

Ru-Yi Translation

Only these — the meditative absorptions, liberations, unifications, and attainments just named — are called the Ground of Meditative Equipoise; the desire realm's own one-pointedness of mind is not. This is because these states of concentration are drawn forth by freedom from regret, by joy, and by ease of body and mind; the desire realm's one-pointedness is not drawn forth this way — though it is not the case that the desire realm is entirely without careful, correct examination of things.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi traces the causal chain this passage presupposes, already taught in the Śrāvaka-bhūmi: purified ethical conduct leaves no grounds for regret; freedom from regret allows genuine joy to arise; joy settles into physical and mental ease; and that ease, in turn, is what actually allows the mind to enter real concentration — precisely why moral discipline is called concentration's own foundation rather than a separate, optional prerequisite. Desire-realm attention can be genuinely focused, even genuinely careful and correct in what it observes (the treatise is explicit that ordinary examination of phenomena is not impossible there), but it is missing this specific causal ancestry — it was never drawn forth by settled ethical confidence ripening into joy and ease — so however sharp it becomes, it is never called "equipoise" in the treatise's own technical sense.

He draws out the practical stakes of this distinction from the Yogācāra tradition's own debate about entry points to the noble path: some practitioners who cultivated genuine meditative stability in a past life can reach direct realization of the truths already at the level of "approach concentration" (未到地定, the threshold state just short of the first dhyāna proper), while those without that prior cultivation must actually reach the first dhyāna or beyond before the same breakthrough becomes possible — in both cases, however, genuine progress requires having actually crossed into equipoise's own causal chain, not merely sharpened desire-realm attention indefinitely. This distinction, he notes, is the entire reason this ground exists as its own separate topic: it is describing a qualitatively different kind of mental event, not simply "more concentrated" ordinary attention.

6. Five Things the First Dhyāna Abandons, Including a King Who Lets His Servants Do Wrong

The treatise names precisely what the first dhyāna leaves behind — joy and sorrow tied to sensory desire, joy and sorrow tied to actually committing wrongdoing, and a subtler fifth category, unwholesome equanimity, itself explained four distinct ways, including the vivid case of an authority figure who never personally does wrong but quietly tolerates wrongdoing in those under his charge.

Chinese Source

復次，初靜慮中說離生喜，由證住此，斷除五法。謂欲所引喜、欲所引憂、不善所引喜、不善所引憂、不善所引捨。欲所引喜者，於妙五欲若初得時，若已證得正受用時，或見、或聞、或曾領受；由此諸緣，憶念歡喜。欲所引憂者，於妙五欲若求不遂，若已受用更不復得，或得已便失；由此諸緣，多生憂惱。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, in the first dhyāna, called "joy arisen from solitude," one who has realized and settled into it abandons five things: joy induced by desire, sorrow induced by desire, joy induced by wrongdoing, sorrow induced by wrongdoing, and equanimity induced by wrongdoing. Joy induced by desire is this: when first obtaining an attractive sensory pleasure, or actually experiencing it, or having seen it, heard of it, or once tasted it — recalling any of these, joy arises. Sorrow induced by desire is this: seeking an attractive sensory pleasure and failing to obtain it, or having once enjoyed it and no longer being able to, or obtaining it and then losing it — from any of these, sorrow repeatedly arises.

不善所引喜者，謂如有一，與喜樂俱而行殺業，乃至邪見。不善所引憂者，謂如有一，與憂苦俱而行殺業，乃至邪見。不善所引捨者，謂如有一，或王、王等、或餘宰官、或尊、尊等，自不樂為殺等惡業，然其僕使作惡業時，忍而不制，亦不安處毗奈耶中，由縱捨故，遂造惡業。

Joy induced by wrongdoing is this: someone who commits killing or any of the other unwholesome deeds, up through wrong view, with joy and pleasure. Sorrow induced by wrongdoing is this: someone who commits killing or the rest with sorrow and distress, having failed to accomplish what was wanted. Equanimity induced by wrongdoing is this: a king, or one of equal standing, or some other official or dignitary, who does not personally delight in killing or other wrongdoing, yet who witnesses a servant committing wrongdoing and tolerates it without restraining them, neither placing them under proper discipline — through this very indulgence, wrongdoing is thereby committed, and this person is well aware of it, not unaware.

又住於捨，尋求伺察為惡方便。又於諸惡耽著不斷，引發於捨。又於不善現前轉時，發起中庸非苦樂受。

Further, resting in this indulgent equanimity, one applies coarse and subtle thought toward devising ways to do wrong. Further, indulging in wrongdoing without ever severing it, one's habitual disposition itself gives rise to this equanimity. Further, at the very moment wrongdoing occurs, a neutral feeling arises, neither painful nor pleasant.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the first dhyāna is defined as much by what it removes as by what it adds: joy and sorrow chasing sensory pleasure, and joy and sorrow bound up with actually doing wrong, are both left behind once the mind settles into genuine seclusion from desire — the desire realm's whole emotional economy simply stops applying. He treats the fifth category, unwholesome equanimity, as the most subtle and interesting of the five, precisely because it looks like virtue (no active joy or sorrow) while actually being a form of complicity: the ruler who personally refrains from wrongdoing but quietly lets subordinates commit it unchecked is, Cháng Bǎi stresses, not innocent — the classical gloss on this passage draws the parallel to an old saying, that a father who fails to instruct his child, or a teacher who fails to discipline a student, shares real responsibility for what follows.

He unpacks the four progressively deeper readings of this same "unwholesome equanimity": first, the bare toleration of witnessed wrongdoing without intervention; second, that same indifference actively turning toward planning further wrongdoing, coarse and subtle thought both devoted to scheming; third, wrongdoing indulged in so habitually and for so long that indifference itself hardens into settled character, no longer even requiring active choice; and fourth, the specific neutral, affectless feeling — neither pleasure nor pain — that accompanies wrongdoing once it has become this thoroughly normalized. He offers this whole passage as a genuinely practical mirror for meditation practice itself: a stray unwholesome thought that arises and is neither acted on nor actually confronted, simply left to pass unexamined, is exactly this same "indulgent equanimity" in miniature — and left unaddressed, it is precisely what prevents concentration from ever actually settling into genuine equipoise.

7. Five Things Cultivated: Gladness, Joy, Tranquility, Bliss, and One-Pointedness

Against the five things abandoned, the treatise names the five qualities the first dhyāna brings to full completion — a settled gladness rooted in blameless conduct, a deeper joy in the very practice of meditation itself, a bodily and mental tranquility once coarse unfitness has been shed, the resulting genuine bliss, and finally one-pointedness of mind, concentration proper, resting on all four of the others as its foundation.

Chinese Source

又於五法修習圓滿。謂歡、喜、安、樂及三摩地。歡者，謂從本來清淨行者，觀資糧地所修淨行，無悔為先，慰意適悅，心欣踊性。喜者，謂正修習方便為先，深慶適悅，心欣踊性。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, five qualities are brought to full completion: gladness, joy, tranquility, bliss, and concentration. Gladness is this: a practitioner of consistently pure conduct, reflecting on the pure practices cultivated at the stage of gathering the provisions for practice, finds no grounds for regret, and from this arises a settled comfort and contentment, the mind's own genuine delight. Joy is this: grounded first in genuinely undertaking the actual practice of meditation itself, a profound gladness and contentment arises, the mind's own delight rising still further.

安者，謂離麤重，身心調適性。樂者，謂由如是心調適故，便得身心無損害樂及解脫樂；以離彼品麤重性故，於諸煩惱而得解脫。三摩地者，謂於所緣審正觀察，心一境性。

Tranquility is this: once coarse unfitness is left behind, body and mind become genuinely well-adapted and at ease. Bliss is this: because the mind has become so well-adapted, one obtains both an unharmed bodily and mental bliss and the bliss of genuine release — and, by leaving behind that same coarse unfitness, one is freed from the afflictions it sustained. Concentration is this: careful, correct observation resting on one single object, the mind's own one-pointedness.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi traces the five as a genuine causal sequence, each resting on the one before: gladness comes first, simply from honestly reviewing one's own conduct — the ethical restraint, sense-faculty guarding, moderation in eating, and reduced sleep cultivated at the preparatory stage — and finding, quite simply, nothing to regret; joy follows once actual meditative effort is genuinely underway, a delight specifically in the practice itself, not merely in having behaved well; tranquility is what remains once the body's stiffness and the mind's restlessness — sitting cross-legged no longer feels foreign, and the mind no longer resists settling — have both genuinely eased; bliss is tranquility's own natural fruit, felt in two registers at once, an unharmed bodily ease (which he locates, in the tradition's own account, in the storehouse consciousness sustaining the body even while the five sense-consciousnesses have quieted) and a mental ease that is the direct experience of release from desire's own grip; and concentration, one-pointedness itself, is what all four of the others together finally make possible.

He offers this passage as genuinely earned from experience rather than abstract description: a beginner sitting in meditation, he notes, is often drenched in sweat, starving the moment the sitting ends, fighting a mind as unruly as a small child running in every direction at once — precisely because governing one's own mind is far harder than governing anything external, given how deeply habituated its patterns already are from beginningless saṃsāra. He treats the passage's real message as encouragement rather than intimidation: genuine progress is gradual and cumulative, exactly like learning to climb a mountain that looks impossible from the base — an outburst of anger that once lasted three years can, with sustained practice, shrink to a week, then to three hours, and eventually dissolve as soon as it arises, once a practitioner has genuinely internalized that the words provoking it are themselves empty of any real weight to carry.

8. Why the Buddha Teaches Concentration Before Liberation

The treatise explains a specific ordering choice in how the Buddha teaches the path to freedom from affliction: concentration is always taught first, liberation second — not as a matter of convenience, but because the specific insight-practice that actually severs affliction's root can only be undertaken once concentration itself is already well established.

Chinese Source

世尊於無漏方便中，先說三摩地，後說解脫。由三摩地善成滿力，於諸煩惱心永解脫故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Within the uncontaminated method, the Blessed One teaches concentration first and liberation second — because it is precisely through the accomplished power of concentration, brought to full completion, that the mind becomes forever liberated from the afflictions.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains the "uncontaminated method" this passage refers to: resting within an already-settled meditative absorption, the practitioner turns insight-practice onto the five aggregates themselves — contemplating them as a sickness, an abscess, an arrow lodged in the flesh, and, more fully, as suffering, empty, and without self, until genuine dread and revulsion toward the aggregates naturally arises, and the mind settles instead into the deathless realm of nirvāṇa; or, taking a complementary approach, contemplating suchness, the nature of things, and the ultimate reality directly, and settling the mind there. Both approaches, he stresses, are only possible resting on an already-accomplished concentration — the reason the Buddha always teaches concentration's own establishment before turning to the liberating insight built on top of it.

He notes the tradition names seven distinct facets of "suchness" itself, each corresponding to a different angle of realization: suchness as it appears in the sheer beginningless flow of conditioned existence; suchness as the selflessness of both persons and phenomena; suchness as the truth that all experience is nothing but consciousness's own display; suchness as it underlies the very fact of suffering (the truth of suffering); suchness as it underlies suffering's mistaken cause, craving (the truth of origin); suchness as the utterly stainless purity of cessation itself (the truth of cessation); and suchness as it underlies the genuine path leading there (the truth of the path) — the four noble truths and three further facets together forming a sevenfold map of the same single, unchanging reality, approached from seven different directions. Cháng Bǎi treats this teaching ordering itself as a practical corrective: a student eager to skip straight to liberating wisdom without first doing the patient work of settling the mind in concentration is, in effect, trying to run before the ground beneath their own feet has stabilized.

9. Seven Stages of Attention That Carry a Mind from Ordinary Distraction into Absorption

For ordinary, still-contaminated practice, the teaching order reverses: the Buddha explains freedom from a lower state's coarse disturbance before concentration itself, since concentration is only actually reached by first working through seven distinct stages of attention — each one progressively weakening affliction until the final stage yields the accomplished result the previous six were building toward.

Chinese Source

於有漏方便中，先說解脫，後說三摩地。由證方便究竟果作意煩惱斷已，方得根本三摩地故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Within the contaminated method, liberation is taught first and concentration second — because it is only once the culminating attention that perfects this method has severed affliction that the fundamental concentration itself is actually attained.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi names the seven stages in their working order, cross-referencing their fuller treatment in the Śrāvaka-bhūmi: attention that discerns the mark (了相作意) first correctly recognizes exactly what coarse quality is to be abandoned in the lower state and what peaceful quality is to be gained in the higher, giving rise to genuine aspiration; attention of resolve (勝解作意) actually undertakes the effort, the real turning point where wisdom born of cultivation, not merely of hearing or reflection, begins doing the work; attention of withdrawal (遠離作意) sheds the coarsest, most visible grade of the lower state's affliction; attention that gathers joy (攝樂作意) sheds its middling grade, now tasting real delight in the practice; attention of examination (觀察作意) checks whether the lower affliction has genuinely been left behind, guarding against the false confidence of mistaking partial progress for complete success; attention that perfects the effort (加行究竟作意) sheds even the subtlest remaining grade; and attention that is the very fruit of that perfected effort (加行究竟果作意) is simply the accomplished result itself, actually experienced — the fundamental concentration finally attained.

He treats "liberation" in this specific contaminated-method sense as provisional rather than final — closer to a stone weighing down grass than to actually uprooting it, a temporary suppression of a lower state's affliction from currently manifesting, not the permanent destruction of its underlying seed, which only the uncontaminated method described in the previous section can accomplish. He also notes the treatise's own further clarification that concentration and this provisional liberation can, in a stricter sense, be described as arising together — at the exact moment the seventh, culminating attention is reached, the immediately following moment of actually severing that grade of affliction and the resulting concentration are simultaneous, which is why some scriptural passages speak of the two as one and the same event rather than in sequence.

10. The Five Hindrances Begin: Desire, and the Prince Who Reasoned His Way Past It

The treatise names the five hindrances that specifically obstruct meditative absorption, then opens with desire — the mind's own compulsive chasing after sensory objects, whether currently present or merely recollected — illustrated by a classical story of a prince who achieved solitary awakening simply by recognizing that desire is nothing more than a product of his own thinking, and therefore fully within his power to stop.

Chinese Source

復次，於諸靜慮、等至障中，略有五蓋，將證彼時能為障礙。何等為五？一、貪欲蓋，二、瞋恚蓋，三、惛沈睡眠蓋，四、掉舉惡作蓋，五、疑蓋。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, among the obstructions to the meditative absorptions and attainments, there are, briefly, five hindrances, capable of obstructing their realization. What are the five? First, the hindrance of desire. Second, the hindrance of ill will. Third, the hindrance of dullness and drowsiness. Fourth, the hindrance of restlessness and remorse. Fifth, the hindrance of doubt.

貪欲者，謂於妙五欲隨逐淨相，欲見、欲聞，乃至欲觸；或隨憶念先所領受，尋伺追戀。

Desire is this: chasing after the appealing appearance of attractive sensory objects, wishing to see, to hear, and so on through to wishing to touch them; or, recalling what has been enjoyed before, dwelling on it with coarse and subtle thought, longing after it still.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi stresses that desire's actual object is never the sensory thing itself but the mind's own distorted overlay on it — the Mahāvibhāṣā's own line, that "the world's fine objects are not truly desire; true desire is a person's own discriminating craving," makes exactly this point: the five senses' raw data is intercepted by conceptual thought, which then obsessively pursues either a present object or a merely remembered one, mistaking its own fabrication for something genuinely worth chasing.

He retells the Ekottarika Āgama's story of Prince Subhūti, a former life of the Buddha himself: a childless king struck a bargain with the god Indra to receive a son on the condition the boy would later be allowed to renounce the world — the son being a dying celestial being, Subhūti, who had only agreed to be reborn as a human on that promise. Once grown, the king built the prince palace after palace and surrounded him with entertainers to make him forget his vow, until Indra himself returned to remind Subhūti of who he really was. Startled awake, the prince reasoned through his own attachment to the palace's dancing girls, recognized both their bodies and his own as impure and without any real self to be found, and composed a verse capturing the entire hindrance in one line: "Desire, I know your root — you arise from thought alone; if I do not think you, then you simply do not exist." Having seen this clearly, he left the palace, sat beneath a tree, and entered final nirvāṇa as a pratyekabuddha on the spot. Cháng Bǎi draws the moral directly for meditation practice: desire is sustained by rehearsal — dwell on an object repeatedly and craving for it only intensifies; simply decline to dwell on it, and, as the prince demonstrated, it has nothing left to stand on.

11. Ill Will, and the Seven Ordinary Causes of Dullness and Drowsiness

The second hindrance, ill will, is traced to three familiar triggers — being corrected by a fellow practitioner, dwelling on a past grievance, or rehearsing a future one — while the third, dullness and drowsiness, is given a strikingly practical sevenfold diagnosis, from broken precepts and unguarded senses to poor eating habits and simple lack of resolve, explaining why these two very different-feeling states are treated as a single combined hindrance.

Chinese Source

瞋恚者，謂或因同梵行等舉其所犯，或因憶念昔所曾經不饒益事瞋恚之相，心生恚怒；或欲當作不饒益事，於當所為瞋恚之相多隨尋伺，心生恚怒。

Ru-Yi Translation

Ill will is this: whether from a fellow practitioner pointing out one's own fault, or from recalling some past harm and its accompanying resentment, anger arises; or, intending some future harm to another, dwelling repeatedly on that resentment with coarse and subtle thought, anger arises.

惛沈者，謂或因毀壞淨尸羅等隨一善行、不守根門、食不知量、不勤精進減省睡眠、不正知住而有所作、於所修斷不勤加行，隨順生起一切煩惱，身心惛昧，無堪任性。睡眠者，謂心極昧略。是故此二合說一蓋。

Dullness is this: from breaking pure moral discipline or any other wholesome practice, from failing to guard the sense-doors, from not knowing moderation in eating, from lacking the diligence to reduce sleep, from acting without clear awareness of what one is doing, or from failing to apply effort to what should be cultivated and abandoned — through any of these, every affliction is given room to arise, and body and mind grow dull and clouded, unfit for the task. Drowsiness is this: the mind sunk into utter dimness. This is why the two are spoken of together as a single hindrance.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes ill will's first trigger carries a specific sting worth naming honestly: being corrected by a fellow practitioner for a genuine fault, however deserved, often provokes resentment rather than gratitude — precisely because it feels like a wound to "me" rather than a simple correction of mistaken conduct; recognizing that it is only the deed at fault, not some injured self, is itself already most of the way toward letting the resentment go rather than carrying it onto the meditation cushion.

On dullness and drowsiness, he treats the sevenfold diagnosis as a genuinely useful checklist for any meditator troubled by persistent sleepiness: an unresolved ethical lapse quietly draining resolve; senses left habitually unguarded, wearing the mind out before it even sits down; eating too much, too little, or the wrong kind of food (he notes sweet foods in particular are traditionally flagged as drowsiness-inducing); insufficient reduction of ordinary sleep; sitting down without the habit of clear, moment-to-moment awareness of one's own state; simple lack of energetic effort in actually applying the antidotes to affliction; and, more generally, indulging any affliction at all, which clouds body and mind together. He explains why dullness and drowsiness, despite feeling different, are combined into one hindrance: dullness is the loss of capacity itself, letting every other affliction back in and undermining the very effort meant to counter it, while drowsiness is what dullness becomes once awareness of the meditation object is lost entirely — the two are so close in mechanism that drowsiness never arises without dullness already present, even though other kinds of affliction may or may not accompany a given bout of dullness. Cháng Bǎi adds that genuine concentration always rests on three qualities working together — mindfulness holding the object, clear comprehension noticing when the mind has wandered, and equanimity that simply rests once mindfulness is already correctly in place, needing neither further exertion nor slack correction — the same three qualities dullness and drowsiness are what most directly undermine.

12. Remorse Over Leaving Home, and a Bodhisattva's Poem to a Homesick Boy

The treatise pairs restlessness with remorse — regret over having left family and comfort behind to renounce the world, or over duties neglected and improprieties committed — and closes the five hindrances with doubt, which can undermine confidence in the Three Jewels and the very possibility of liberation, or dissolve into unresolved uncertainty about one's own existence across time and the four truths themselves.

Chinese Source

惡作者，謂因尋思親屬等故，心生追悔，謂我何緣離別親屬？何緣不往如是國土？……我本何緣少小出家，何不且待至年衰老？或因追念昔所曾經戲笑等事，便生悔恨，謂我何緣於應受用戲樂、嚴具、朋遊等時，違背宗親朋友等意，令其悲戀涕淚盈目而彊出家。

Ru-Yi Translation

Remorse is this: dwelling on thoughts of family and the rest, regret arises — why did I leave my family behind? Why did I not go to such-and-such a place instead? ... Why did I renounce the world so young, rather than waiting until old age? Or, recalling past amusements once enjoyed, regret arises — why, at a time I should have been enjoying entertainment, fine adornment, and the company of friends, did I instead go against the wishes of my family and companions, leaving them grieving and in tears, and insist on renouncing the world regardless?

疑者，謂於師、於法、於學、於誨，及於證中生惑生疑。由心如是懷疑惑故，不能趣入勇猛方便，證斷寂靜。又於去來今及苦等諦生惑生疑，心懷二分，迷之不了，猶豫猜度。

Doubt is this: uncertainty and confusion arising toward one's teacher, the teaching, the trainings, the instructions given, or toward realization itself — and because the mind is caught in such doubt, it cannot enter courageously into diligent effort, or realize the peace of genuine cessation. Further, uncertainty and confusion arise toward the past, future, and present, and toward the truth of suffering and the rest — the mind torn between two possibilities, unable to resolve the confusion, wavering and merely guessing.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads remorse as restlessness's own emotional shadow — restlessness dwells excitedly on family, homeland, mortality's distance, and past pleasures, while remorse dwells on the exact same memories with regret instead of longing, which is why the two are combined into a single hindrance despite feeling different: both are the mind agitated by the same class of memory, only inflected oppositely. He retells the story of the bodhisattva Dizang, traditionally identified with the Korean prince Kim Kiao-gak who practiced on Mount Jiuhua, and his young attendant who grew homesick after years on the mountain. Rather than scold the boy, Dizang sent him home with a poem: "In this quiet gate you long for home" — acknowledging plainly that monastic life is simple, spare, short on the ordinary warmth of family — "bid the clouds farewell, descend Jiuhua." "You'd rather ride a bamboo horse with the children than gather grains of gold on this earth" — gently naming the boy's own preference without shaming it. "Don't mistake the moon reflected at the bottom of a well for something worth scooping up" — a warning that sensory pleasure below is as illusory as a reflection, alluring but ultimately empty. "And don't linger over tea and flowers in a cup" — fleeting comforts, gone as soon as savored. "Go well, and don't weep too much — I have the clouds and mist of the mountain for company, and I am never lonely." Cháng Bǎi treats the poem's real teaching as neither guilt nor forced endurance, but honest acknowledgment paired with genuine equanimity — a model, he suggests, for how a practitioner might meet their own homesickness during long practice, without either suppressing it or being ruled by it.

On doubt, he distinguishes two registers: doubt toward the Three Jewels and the path directly saps the courage needed to actually practice, since no one applies real effort toward a goal they secretly suspect is unreachable; doubt about one's own existence across the three times, and about the Four Noble Truths themselves, is a subtler, more philosophical uncertainty — the mind genuinely unable to settle whether a given proposition is true, caught oscillating between two possibilities with no resolution. Both, he notes, are equally disqualifying for concentration, since a mind still holding a live question about whether the whole enterprise is even worth pursuing has not yet actually committed itself to the object of meditation at all.

13. What Feeds Desire, and What Starves It: Eight Alluring Marks Against the Body's Own Impurity

The treatise now asks, for each hindrance in turn, what specifically "feeds" it and what "starves" it — and for desire, names eight classical alluring marks (song, dance, smile, a coquettish glance, beauty, graceful bearing, soft touch, and physical union) as its food, set against the deliberate contemplation of the body's own impurity — both what lies inside a living body and what a corpse becomes — as the one practice strong enough to prevent desire from arising at all, or to sever it once it has.

Chinese Source

此貪欲蓋以何為食？答：有淨妙相，及於彼相不正思惟多所修習，以之為食。此復云何？謂女人身上八處所攝可愛淨相；由此八處，女縛於男。所謂歌、舞、笑、睇、美容、進止、妙觸、就禮。由此因緣，所有貪欲未生令生，生已增長，故名為食。

Ru-Yi Translation

What feeds the hindrance of desire? Answer: An appealing appearance, together with sustained unsound reflection on it, is its food. What is this, specifically? It is the eight captivating features belonging to a woman's body, by which a woman binds a man — song, dance, smiling, a lingering glance, beauty of face, graceful bearing, soft touch, and physical union. Through these causes, desire not yet arisen is caused to arise, and once arisen, is caused to grow — this is why they are called its food.

此貪欲蓋誰為非食？答：有不淨相，及於彼相如理作意多所修習，以為非食。此復云何？謂青瘀等。若觀此身種種不淨雜穢充滿，名觀內身不淨之相；復觀於外青瘀等相種種不淨，名觀外身不淨之相。由觀此二不淨相故，未生貪欲令其不生，生已能斷，故名非食。

What starves the hindrance of desire? Answer: An impure appearance, together with sustained sound reflection on it, is what starves it. What is this, specifically? It is contemplating discoloration and the rest. Observing this very body as filled with every kind of impurity is called contemplating the inward body's impurity; observing outwardly the discoloration and other stages a corpse passes through is called contemplating the outward body's impurity. Through contemplating both these impurities, desire not yet arisen is prevented from arising, and once arisen, is severed — this is why they are called what starves it.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the strategic logic here as deliberately targeted: rather than attempting to suppress every minor sensory pleasure at once, the teaching isolates the single strongest driver of desire — attraction between the sexes — on the reasoning that mastering the strongest case makes every weaker case of sensory craving correspondingly easier to release. He notes the "food" is never the physical features themselves but the mind's own unsound dwelling on them as inherently attractive, exactly the same point desire's own definition made earlier in the fascicle.

On the antidote, he distinguishes the inward and outward contemplations: inwardly, a living body is thirty-some named unclean substances and structures — hair, nails, bone, marrow, organs, and the fluids that constantly seep from its nine openings — the same anatomical unsparing detail the treatise gave of the body back in fascicle 3; outwardly, a corpse's own progression through discoloration, bloating, decay, and finally bare bone makes visible, over time, exactly what the living body already conceals beneath an appealing surface. He references — without transcribing at length — the classical verses on renouncing the five desires from the Śamatha-Vipaśyanā treatise, whose central image is a body compared to a leaking sack of impurities and a corpse one nourishes for years only to bury it in the end; their conclusion, he notes, is not self-loathing but liberation — seeing the body's actual nature clearly is precisely what frees a practitioner from being deceived and controlled by desire for it, opening the way to concentration's own much deeper happiness. He treats sound and unsound reflection here as two directions of the identical habit-forming mechanism already established for desire's "food": dwelling repeatedly on a false appealing appearance strengthens craving, while dwelling repeatedly on the same body's actual impure nature weakens and eventually starves it — the antidote works by exactly the same mechanism as the poison, aimed in the opposite direction.

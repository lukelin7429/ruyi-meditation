Fascicle 11 · 瑜伽師地論卷第十一 · A New Ground Begins

Seven Names for Stillness, and the Four Doors Into It

Fascicle 10 closed the entire three-grounds section that had run since fascicle 4 — the treatise's long survey of the three realms, their beings, and the mechanics of karma and rebirth. Fascicle 11 opens something genuinely new: the Ground of Meditative Equipoise (三摩呬多地), the treatise's own dedicated, systematic account of meditative absorption itself — what actually happens, structurally, when a mind settles into concentration, from the first stirrings of the four dhyānas onward.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 11 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. A New Ground Opens: Seven Names for the Same Stillness

The treatise turns from the three grounds just completed to its sixth of seventeen main grounds, the Ground of Meditative Equipoise (三摩呬多地, samāhita-bhūmi) — and before analyzing it, surveys seven near-synonyms Sanskrit uses for meditative attainment, each capturing a slightly different facet of the same underlying stillness: equipoise itself, one-pointed unification, actual attainment, quieted contemplation, one-pointedness of mind, calm-abiding, and dwelling in present happiness.

Chinese Source

已說有尋有伺等三。云何三摩呬多地？

Ru-Yi Translation

The Ground with Applied and Sustained Thought, and the two grounds following it, have now been fully explained. What is the Ground of Meditative Equipoise?

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi situates this opening within the treatise's full seventeen-ground architecture: the first two grounds (the five sense-consciousnesses, and the mind ground) established the basic apparatus of cognition; the three grounds just completed, spanning fascicles 4 through 10, surveyed the three realms, their beings, and the entire causal mechanics of karma and rebirth in exhaustive detail. Grounds six through nine — Meditative Equipoise, Non-Equipoise, the Ground With Mind, and the Ground Without Mind — now turn to mapping the actual states a mind can occupy, before the treatise moves on to the shared path of the three vehicles (grounds ten through twelve: hearing, reflection, cultivation) and then each vehicle's own distinct path (the śrāvaka, pratyekabuddha, and bodhisattva grounds), closing with the grounds of the two nirvāṇas already explained in fascicle 9. Ground six, he notes, is where the treatise finally turns from describing the raw material of saṃsāra to describing the tool that actually escapes it.

He unpacks the seven Sanskrit near-synonyms the source material surveys before analysis begins, each stressing a different nuance: samāhita (equipoise) stresses a mind drawn evenly toward stillness, free of both dullness and agitation, and covers the broadest territory — with mind or without, contaminated or uncontaminated; samādhi (one-pointed unification, historically rendered "samādhi" or "concentration") stresses the mind held level before and after, and covers ordinary scattered-mind states as well as full absorption; samāpatti (actual attainment) stresses a depth of settling that reaches all the way to attainment, whether or not conceptual activity remains; dhyāna (quieted contemplation, the oldest term, "chán" in Chinese) stresses a mind that is both stilled and actively investigating at once, wisdom and stillness in balance — which is exactly why the four dhyānas, uniquely among meditative states, are considered ideal ground for realizing the noble path, while the four formless attainments, strong in stillness but weak in active wisdom, are not; citta-ikāgratā (one-pointedness of mind) stresses sustained, repeated recollection of a single object until it becomes continuous; śamatha (calm-abiding) stresses the mind's coming to rest on one object; and dṛṣṭadharma-sukhavihāra (dwelling in present happiness) names specifically the four dhyānas themselves, each carrying its own measure of joy, bliss, or equanimity, distinguishing full absorption from its own preliminary access states, which do not yet count as "dwelling in happiness" in this technical sense.

2. Four Doors Named: Solitude, Stillness, Renouncing Joy, and Purified Equanimity

The treatise names the four topics that will organize this entire ground — the four dhyānas, the eight liberations, eleven kinds of unification, and six kinds of attainment — then defines the four dhyānas themselves, each named for the specific renunciation that opens its door: the first dhyāna arising from solitude while thought is still active, the second arising from settled stillness once thought falls away, the third arising from renouncing joy itself, and the fourth from purifying even equanimity and mindfulness.

Chinese Source

謂此地中略有四種。一者、靜慮，二者、解脫，三者、等持，四者、等至。靜慮者，謂四靜慮。一、從離生有尋有伺靜慮，二、從定生無尋無伺靜慮，三、離喜靜慮，四、捨念清淨靜慮。

Ru-Yi Translation

Briefly, this ground has four parts: first, the meditative absorptions; second, the liberations; third, unifications; fourth, attainments. The meditative absorptions are the four dhyānas. First, the dhyāna with applied and sustained thought, arising from solitude. Second, the dhyāna free of applied and sustained thought, arising from settled concentration. Third, the dhyāna of renouncing joy. Fourth, the dhyāna of purified equanimity and mindfulness.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi previews how these four topics will unfold across the fascicle: the four dhyānas (靜慮) are the foundational states themselves; the eight liberations (解脫) name eight specific ways affliction is actually suppressed through meditative mastery; the eleven unifications (等持, samādhi in its narrower technical sense) survey concentration's many varieties; and the six attainments (等至, samāpatti) round out the picture with states reaching even deeper than ordinary absorption. He notes each dhyāna is named precisely for what it has just renounced to arrive: the first dhyāna, reached through the classical method of applied and sustained thought, has left behind the desire realm's craving, hatred, and harmfulness, and carries five distinguishing qualities — applied thought, sustained thought, joy, bliss, and one-pointedness of mind; the second, reached by observing applied and sustained thought's own coarse disturbance and letting it settle, has left even those behind, carrying inner clarity, joy, bliss, and one-pointedness; the third, reached by observing joy's own agitating quality, has renounced joy itself while retaining the second dhyāna's bliss, now settled into mindfulness, clear comprehension, equanimity, bliss, and one-pointedness; and the fourth, reached by observing that even bliss is itself a subtle disturbance, has renounced bliss as well, arriving at purified equanimity, purified mindfulness, neutral feeling, and one-pointedness — each dhyāna, in turn, is what the one before it looks like once its own subtlest remaining agitation has also been seen through and released.

3. Eight Liberations: Using Ugliness, Then Beauty, to Break the Same Attachment Two Ways

The treatise names the eight liberations — a progression of meditative mastery running from contemplating repulsive objects while still perceiving form, through the same practice once inner form-perception has dropped away, to a striking reversal where the meditator deliberately contemplates a pure and beautiful object to prove affliction no longer arises even there, and finally through all four formless attainments to the cessation of perception and feeling itself.

Chinese Source

解脫者，謂八解脫。一、有色觀諸色解脫，二、內無色想觀外諸色解脫，三、淨解脫身作證具足住解脫，四、空無邊處解脫，五、識無邊處解脫，六、無所有處解脫，七、非想非非想處解脫，八、想受滅身作證具足住解脫。

Ru-Yi Translation

The liberations are the eight liberations. First, liberation through contemplating forms while still perceiving form. Second, liberation through contemplating outward forms while no longer perceiving form inwardly. Third, liberation through the pure absorption directly realized and fully dwelt in. Fourth, liberation of the sphere of limitless space. Fifth, liberation of the sphere of limitless consciousness. Sixth, liberation of the sphere of nothing whatsoever. Seventh, liberation of the sphere of neither perception nor non-perception. Eighth, liberation through the cessation of perception and feeling, directly realized and fully dwelt in.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi traces the classical logic behind each step: the first liberation has a practitioner still holding some inward perception of form contemplate outward forms as repulsive (resting on the first dhyāna), breaking the desire realm's attachment to beauty through deliberate exposure to ugliness; the second repeats the same repulsive contemplation once inward form-perception has itself been let go (resting on the second dhyāna), to confirm the release is genuinely stable rather than merely suppressed by the first dhyāna's own coarser state; the third liberation is the classical tradition's most striking reversal — resting on the fourth dhyāna, the practitioner now deliberately contemplates something beautiful and pure, proving mastery complete precisely by no longer being disturbed even by what used to provoke the strongest craving, "directly realized and fully dwelt in" marking this as no longer a technique but an accomplished, stable liberation.

The fourth through seventh liberations are the four formless attainments themselves, each releasing one further layer of dependency exactly as fascicle 5's own earlier survey described (space releasing the body, consciousness releasing the space-object, nothingness releasing consciousness-as-object, and neither-perception-nor-non-perception releasing even that subtle remainder); and the eighth, cessation of perception and feeling, available only to a non-returner or arhat, is the deepest attainment the path recognizes, a temporary cessation of mental activity itself used as a genuine rest from even the subtlest formless states. He notes the whole sequence is traditionally called the "eight forms of turning away" (八背捨) precisely because each one is defined by what it deliberately turns from — ugliness first to break craving, then beauty to prove craving is genuinely gone, then each successive formless dependency in turn.

4. Eleven Kinds of Unification, Six Kinds of Attainment — the Opening Outline Closes

The treatise finishes naming this ground's four topics: eleven ways concentration itself can be classified — by its object (emptiness, aimlessness, signlessness), by whether thought remains, by scale, by completeness of cultivation, by accompanying feeling, by its goal, and by the practitioner's own standing on the path — and six kinds of attainment, from the five directly-observed absorptions through the eight forms of mastery, the ten totality-spheres, the four formless spheres, the attainment without perception, and cessation itself. Full elaboration of every item is deferred to later in the fascicle; this opening section only names them.

Chinese Source

等持者，謂三三摩地。一、空，二、無願，三、無相。復有三種。謂有尋有伺、無尋唯伺、無尋無伺。復有三種。謂小、大、無量。復有二種。謂一分修、具分修。復有三種。謂喜俱行、樂俱行、捨俱行。復有四種。謂四修定。復有五種。謂五聖智三摩地。復有五種。謂聖五支三摩地。復有有因有具聖正三摩地。復有金剛喻三摩地。復有有學、無學、非學非無學等三摩地。

Ru-Yi Translation

Unification is the three concentrations: emptiness, aimlessness, and signlessness. There are, further, three more: with applied and sustained thought, without applied thought but with sustained thought alone, and without either. There are, further, three more: small, great, and immeasurable. There are, further, two more: cultivated only in part, and cultivated in full. There are, further, three more: accompanied by joy, by bliss, and by equanimity. There are, further, four more: the four cultivations of concentration. There are, further, five more: the five concentrations of noble wisdom. There are, further, five more: the five noble limbs of concentration. There is, further, the noble right concentration that has both a cause and its full equipment. There is, further, the vajra-like concentration. There are, further, three more: belonging to one still in training, to one beyond training, and to neither.

等至者，謂五現見三摩鉢底、八勝處三摩鉢底、十遍處三摩鉢底、四無色三摩鉢底、無想三摩鉢底、滅盡定等三摩鉢底。

Attainment is the directly-observed attainments, the attainments of the eight forms of mastery, the attainments of the ten totality-spheres, the four formless attainments, the attainment without perception, and the attainment of cessation, among others.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi flags this reuse of the "with applied and sustained thought / with sustained thought alone / with neither" threefold scheme as a genuine terminological trap: it is the exact same three names that organized the entire three-grounds arc spanning fascicles 4 through 10 (有尋有伺地・無尋唯伺地・無尋無伺地), but there it classified entire realms of existence, while here it classifies a single meditative state's own internal structure — the same three labels doing two structurally different jobs, one cosmological and one psychological, and worth keeping cleanly separate. He previews that most of these eleven categories, and all six attainments, receive no elaboration here — this section is purely a naming pass, with full explanation deferred until roughly a third of the way into the fascicle, once the groundwork of what actually distinguishes meditative equipoise from ordinary concentration has been laid.

He notes the emptiness/aimlessness/signlessness triad and the noble-limb triad both reach ahead to teachings usually associated with much later sections of the treatise (the three gateways to liberation, and the eightfold noble path's meditation-relevant limbs), while the vajra-like concentration — named for its capacity to shatter every remaining affliction the way a diamond shatters any other substance — marks the single decisive moment a trainee becomes an arhat, the last concentration of the path of cultivation immediately preceding final, complete direct realization.

5. What Actually Makes a State of Mind "Equipoise": Not Desire-Realm Focus, But No-Regret's Whole Causal Chain

Before defining the four dhyānas in detail, the treatise establishes what "meditative equipoise" (等引) actually means, and sharply distinguishes it from desire-realm one-pointedness: equipoise is drawn forth specifically by an unbroken causal chain running from purified ethical conduct, through freedom from regret, to genuine joy, bliss, and finally settled concentration — a chain the desire realm's own one-pointed attention, however genuinely focused or even carefully observant, never actually completes.

Chinese Source

謂唯此等名等引地，非於欲界心一境性。由此定等無悔、歡喜、安樂所引；欲界不爾。非欲界中於法全無審正觀察。

Ru-Yi Translation

Only these — the meditative absorptions, liberations, unifications, and attainments just named — are called the Ground of Meditative Equipoise; the desire realm's own one-pointedness of mind is not. This is because these states of concentration are drawn forth by freedom from regret, by joy, and by ease of body and mind; the desire realm's one-pointedness is not drawn forth this way — though it is not the case that the desire realm is entirely without careful, correct examination of things.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi traces the causal chain this passage presupposes, already taught in the Śrāvaka-bhūmi: purified ethical conduct leaves no grounds for regret; freedom from regret allows genuine joy to arise; joy settles into physical and mental ease; and that ease, in turn, is what actually allows the mind to enter real concentration — precisely why moral discipline is called concentration's own foundation rather than a separate, optional prerequisite. Desire-realm attention can be genuinely focused, even genuinely careful and correct in what it observes (the treatise is explicit that ordinary examination of phenomena is not impossible there), but it is missing this specific causal ancestry — it was never drawn forth by settled ethical confidence ripening into joy and ease — so however sharp it becomes, it is never called "equipoise" in the treatise's own technical sense.

He draws out the practical stakes of this distinction from the Yogācāra tradition's own debate about entry points to the noble path: some practitioners who cultivated genuine meditative stability in a past life can reach direct realization of the truths already at the level of "approach concentration" (未到地定, the threshold state just short of the first dhyāna proper), while those without that prior cultivation must actually reach the first dhyāna or beyond before the same breakthrough becomes possible — in both cases, however, genuine progress requires having actually crossed into equipoise's own causal chain, not merely sharpened desire-realm attention indefinitely. This distinction, he notes, is the entire reason this ground exists as its own separate topic: it is describing a qualitatively different kind of mental event, not simply "more concentrated" ordinary attention.

6. Five Things the First Dhyāna Abandons, Including a King Who Lets His Servants Do Wrong

The treatise names precisely what the first dhyāna leaves behind — joy and sorrow tied to sensory desire, joy and sorrow tied to actually committing wrongdoing, and a subtler fifth category, unwholesome equanimity, itself explained four distinct ways, including the vivid case of an authority figure who never personally does wrong but quietly tolerates wrongdoing in those under his charge.

Chinese Source

復次，初靜慮中說離生喜，由證住此，斷除五法。謂欲所引喜、欲所引憂、不善所引喜、不善所引憂、不善所引捨。欲所引喜者，於妙五欲若初得時，若已證得正受用時，或見、或聞、或曾領受；由此諸緣，憶念歡喜。欲所引憂者，於妙五欲若求不遂，若已受用更不復得，或得已便失；由此諸緣，多生憂惱。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, in the first dhyāna, called "joy arisen from solitude," one who has realized and settled into it abandons five things: joy induced by desire, sorrow induced by desire, joy induced by wrongdoing, sorrow induced by wrongdoing, and equanimity induced by wrongdoing. Joy induced by desire is this: when first obtaining an attractive sensory pleasure, or actually experiencing it, or having seen it, heard of it, or once tasted it — recalling any of these, joy arises. Sorrow induced by desire is this: seeking an attractive sensory pleasure and failing to obtain it, or having once enjoyed it and no longer being able to, or obtaining it and then losing it — from any of these, sorrow repeatedly arises.

不善所引喜者，謂如有一，與喜樂俱而行殺業，乃至邪見。不善所引憂者，謂如有一，與憂苦俱而行殺業，乃至邪見。不善所引捨者，謂如有一，或王、王等、或餘宰官、或尊、尊等，自不樂為殺等惡業，然其僕使作惡業時，忍而不制，亦不安處毗奈耶中，由縱捨故，遂造惡業。

Joy induced by wrongdoing is this: someone who commits killing or any of the other unwholesome deeds, up through wrong view, with joy and pleasure. Sorrow induced by wrongdoing is this: someone who commits killing or the rest with sorrow and distress, having failed to accomplish what was wanted. Equanimity induced by wrongdoing is this: a king, or one of equal standing, or some other official or dignitary, who does not personally delight in killing or other wrongdoing, yet who witnesses a servant committing wrongdoing and tolerates it without restraining them, neither placing them under proper discipline — through this very indulgence, wrongdoing is thereby committed, and this person is well aware of it, not unaware.

又住於捨，尋求伺察為惡方便。又於諸惡耽著不斷，引發於捨。又於不善現前轉時，發起中庸非苦樂受。

Further, resting in this indulgent equanimity, one applies coarse and subtle thought toward devising ways to do wrong. Further, indulging in wrongdoing without ever severing it, one's habitual disposition itself gives rise to this equanimity. Further, at the very moment wrongdoing occurs, a neutral feeling arises, neither painful nor pleasant.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the first dhyāna is defined as much by what it removes as by what it adds: joy and sorrow chasing sensory pleasure, and joy and sorrow bound up with actually doing wrong, are both left behind once the mind settles into genuine seclusion from desire — the desire realm's whole emotional economy simply stops applying. He treats the fifth category, unwholesome equanimity, as the most subtle and interesting of the five, precisely because it looks like virtue (no active joy or sorrow) while actually being a form of complicity: the ruler who personally refrains from wrongdoing but quietly lets subordinates commit it unchecked is, Cháng Bǎi stresses, not innocent — the classical gloss on this passage draws the parallel to an old saying, that a father who fails to instruct his child, or a teacher who fails to discipline a student, shares real responsibility for what follows.

He unpacks the four progressively deeper readings of this same "unwholesome equanimity": first, the bare toleration of witnessed wrongdoing without intervention; second, that same indifference actively turning toward planning further wrongdoing, coarse and subtle thought both devoted to scheming; third, wrongdoing indulged in so habitually and for so long that indifference itself hardens into settled character, no longer even requiring active choice; and fourth, the specific neutral, affectless feeling — neither pleasure nor pain — that accompanies wrongdoing once it has become this thoroughly normalized. He offers this whole passage as a genuinely practical mirror for meditation practice itself: a stray unwholesome thought that arises and is neither acted on nor actually confronted, simply left to pass unexamined, is exactly this same "indulgent equanimity" in miniature — and left unaddressed, it is precisely what prevents concentration from ever actually settling into genuine equipoise.

7. Five Things Cultivated: Gladness, Joy, Tranquility, Bliss, and One-Pointedness

Against the five things abandoned, the treatise names the five qualities the first dhyāna brings to full completion — a settled gladness rooted in blameless conduct, a deeper joy in the very practice of meditation itself, a bodily and mental tranquility once coarse unfitness has been shed, the resulting genuine bliss, and finally one-pointedness of mind, concentration proper, resting on all four of the others as its foundation.

Chinese Source

又於五法修習圓滿。謂歡、喜、安、樂及三摩地。歡者，謂從本來清淨行者，觀資糧地所修淨行，無悔為先，慰意適悅，心欣踊性。喜者，謂正修習方便為先，深慶適悅，心欣踊性。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, five qualities are brought to full completion: gladness, joy, tranquility, bliss, and concentration. Gladness is this: a practitioner of consistently pure conduct, reflecting on the pure practices cultivated at the stage of gathering the provisions for practice, finds no grounds for regret, and from this arises a settled comfort and contentment, the mind's own genuine delight. Joy is this: grounded first in genuinely undertaking the actual practice of meditation itself, a profound gladness and contentment arises, the mind's own delight rising still further.

安者，謂離麤重，身心調適性。樂者，謂由如是心調適故，便得身心無損害樂及解脫樂；以離彼品麤重性故，於諸煩惱而得解脫。三摩地者，謂於所緣審正觀察，心一境性。

Tranquility is this: once coarse unfitness is left behind, body and mind become genuinely well-adapted and at ease. Bliss is this: because the mind has become so well-adapted, one obtains both an unharmed bodily and mental bliss and the bliss of genuine release — and, by leaving behind that same coarse unfitness, one is freed from the afflictions it sustained. Concentration is this: careful, correct observation resting on one single object, the mind's own one-pointedness.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi traces the five as a genuine causal sequence, each resting on the one before: gladness comes first, simply from honestly reviewing one's own conduct — the ethical restraint, sense-faculty guarding, moderation in eating, and reduced sleep cultivated at the preparatory stage — and finding, quite simply, nothing to regret; joy follows once actual meditative effort is genuinely underway, a delight specifically in the practice itself, not merely in having behaved well; tranquility is what remains once the body's stiffness and the mind's restlessness — sitting cross-legged no longer feels foreign, and the mind no longer resists settling — have both genuinely eased; bliss is tranquility's own natural fruit, felt in two registers at once, an unharmed bodily ease (which he locates, in the tradition's own account, in the storehouse consciousness sustaining the body even while the five sense-consciousnesses have quieted) and a mental ease that is the direct experience of release from desire's own grip; and concentration, one-pointedness itself, is what all four of the others together finally make possible.

He offers this passage as genuinely earned from experience rather than abstract description: a beginner sitting in meditation, he notes, is often drenched in sweat, starving the moment the sitting ends, fighting a mind as unruly as a small child running in every direction at once — precisely because governing one's own mind is far harder than governing anything external, given how deeply habituated its patterns already are from beginningless saṃsāra. He treats the passage's real message as encouragement rather than intimidation: genuine progress is gradual and cumulative, exactly like learning to climb a mountain that looks impossible from the base — an outburst of anger that once lasted three years can, with sustained practice, shrink to a week, then to three hours, and eventually dissolve as soon as it arises, once a practitioner has genuinely internalized that the words provoking it are themselves empty of any real weight to carry.

8. Why the Buddha Teaches Concentration Before Liberation

The treatise explains a specific ordering choice in how the Buddha teaches the path to freedom from affliction: concentration is always taught first, liberation second — not as a matter of convenience, but because the specific insight-practice that actually severs affliction's root can only be undertaken once concentration itself is already well established.

Chinese Source

世尊於無漏方便中，先說三摩地，後說解脫。由三摩地善成滿力，於諸煩惱心永解脫故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Within the uncontaminated method, the Blessed One teaches concentration first and liberation second — because it is precisely through the accomplished power of concentration, brought to full completion, that the mind becomes forever liberated from the afflictions.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains the "uncontaminated method" this passage refers to: resting within an already-settled meditative absorption, the practitioner turns insight-practice onto the five aggregates themselves — contemplating them as a sickness, an abscess, an arrow lodged in the flesh, and, more fully, as suffering, empty, and without self, until genuine dread and revulsion toward the aggregates naturally arises, and the mind settles instead into the deathless realm of nirvāṇa; or, taking a complementary approach, contemplating suchness, the nature of things, and the ultimate reality directly, and settling the mind there. Both approaches, he stresses, are only possible resting on an already-accomplished concentration — the reason the Buddha always teaches concentration's own establishment before turning to the liberating insight built on top of it.

He notes the tradition names seven distinct facets of "suchness" itself, each corresponding to a different angle of realization: suchness as it appears in the sheer beginningless flow of conditioned existence; suchness as the selflessness of both persons and phenomena; suchness as the truth that all experience is nothing but consciousness's own display; suchness as it underlies the very fact of suffering (the truth of suffering); suchness as it underlies suffering's mistaken cause, craving (the truth of origin); suchness as the utterly stainless purity of cessation itself (the truth of cessation); and suchness as it underlies the genuine path leading there (the truth of the path) — the four noble truths and three further facets together forming a sevenfold map of the same single, unchanging reality, approached from seven different directions. Cháng Bǎi treats this teaching ordering itself as a practical corrective: a student eager to skip straight to liberating wisdom without first doing the patient work of settling the mind in concentration is, in effect, trying to run before the ground beneath their own feet has stabilized.

9. Seven Stages of Attention That Carry a Mind from Ordinary Distraction into Absorption

For ordinary, still-contaminated practice, the teaching order reverses: the Buddha explains freedom from a lower state's coarse disturbance before concentration itself, since concentration is only actually reached by first working through seven distinct stages of attention — each one progressively weakening affliction until the final stage yields the accomplished result the previous six were building toward.

Chinese Source

於有漏方便中，先說解脫，後說三摩地。由證方便究竟果作意煩惱斷已，方得根本三摩地故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Within the contaminated method, liberation is taught first and concentration second — because it is only once the culminating attention that perfects this method has severed affliction that the fundamental concentration itself is actually attained.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi names the seven stages in their working order, cross-referencing their fuller treatment in the Śrāvaka-bhūmi: attention that discerns the mark (了相作意) first correctly recognizes exactly what coarse quality is to be abandoned in the lower state and what peaceful quality is to be gained in the higher, giving rise to genuine aspiration; attention of resolve (勝解作意) actually undertakes the effort, the real turning point where wisdom born of cultivation, not merely of hearing or reflection, begins doing the work; attention of withdrawal (遠離作意) sheds the coarsest, most visible grade of the lower state's affliction; attention that gathers joy (攝樂作意) sheds its middling grade, now tasting real delight in the practice; attention of examination (觀察作意) checks whether the lower affliction has genuinely been left behind, guarding against the false confidence of mistaking partial progress for complete success; attention that perfects the effort (加行究竟作意) sheds even the subtlest remaining grade; and attention that is the very fruit of that perfected effort (加行究竟果作意) is simply the accomplished result itself, actually experienced — the fundamental concentration finally attained.

He treats "liberation" in this specific contaminated-method sense as provisional rather than final — closer to a stone weighing down grass than to actually uprooting it, a temporary suppression of a lower state's affliction from currently manifesting, not the permanent destruction of its underlying seed, which only the uncontaminated method described in the previous section can accomplish. He also notes the treatise's own further clarification that concentration and this provisional liberation can, in a stricter sense, be described as arising together — at the exact moment the seventh, culminating attention is reached, the immediately following moment of actually severing that grade of affliction and the resulting concentration are simultaneous, which is why some scriptural passages speak of the two as one and the same event rather than in sequence.

10. The Five Hindrances Begin: Desire, and the Prince Who Reasoned His Way Past It

The treatise names the five hindrances that specifically obstruct meditative absorption, then opens with desire — the mind's own compulsive chasing after sensory objects, whether currently present or merely recollected — illustrated by a classical story of a prince who achieved solitary awakening simply by recognizing that desire is nothing more than a product of his own thinking, and therefore fully within his power to stop.

Chinese Source

復次，於諸靜慮、等至障中，略有五蓋，將證彼時能為障礙。何等為五？一、貪欲蓋，二、瞋恚蓋，三、惛沈睡眠蓋，四、掉舉惡作蓋，五、疑蓋。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, among the obstructions to the meditative absorptions and attainments, there are, briefly, five hindrances, capable of obstructing their realization. What are the five? First, the hindrance of desire. Second, the hindrance of ill will. Third, the hindrance of dullness and drowsiness. Fourth, the hindrance of restlessness and remorse. Fifth, the hindrance of doubt.

貪欲者，謂於妙五欲隨逐淨相，欲見、欲聞，乃至欲觸；或隨憶念先所領受，尋伺追戀。

Desire is this: chasing after the appealing appearance of attractive sensory objects, wishing to see, to hear, and so on through to wishing to touch them; or, recalling what has been enjoyed before, dwelling on it with coarse and subtle thought, longing after it still.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi stresses that desire's actual object is never the sensory thing itself but the mind's own distorted overlay on it — the Mahāvibhāṣā's own line, that "the world's fine objects are not truly desire; true desire is a person's own discriminating craving," makes exactly this point: the five senses' raw data is intercepted by conceptual thought, which then obsessively pursues either a present object or a merely remembered one, mistaking its own fabrication for something genuinely worth chasing.

He retells the Ekottarika Āgama's story of Prince Subhūti, a former life of the Buddha himself: a childless king struck a bargain with the god Indra to receive a son on the condition the boy would later be allowed to renounce the world — the son being a dying celestial being, Subhūti, who had only agreed to be reborn as a human on that promise. Once grown, the king built the prince palace after palace and surrounded him with entertainers to make him forget his vow, until Indra himself returned to remind Subhūti of who he really was. Startled awake, the prince reasoned through his own attachment to the palace's dancing girls, recognized both their bodies and his own as impure and without any real self to be found, and composed a verse capturing the entire hindrance in one line: "Desire, I know your root — you arise from thought alone; if I do not think you, then you simply do not exist." Having seen this clearly, he left the palace, sat beneath a tree, and entered final nirvāṇa as a pratyekabuddha on the spot. Cháng Bǎi draws the moral directly for meditation practice: desire is sustained by rehearsal — dwell on an object repeatedly and craving for it only intensifies; simply decline to dwell on it, and, as the prince demonstrated, it has nothing left to stand on.

11. Ill Will, and the Seven Ordinary Causes of Dullness and Drowsiness

The second hindrance, ill will, is traced to three familiar triggers — being corrected by a fellow practitioner, dwelling on a past grievance, or rehearsing a future one — while the third, dullness and drowsiness, is given a strikingly practical sevenfold diagnosis, from broken precepts and unguarded senses to poor eating habits and simple lack of resolve, explaining why these two very different-feeling states are treated as a single combined hindrance.

Chinese Source

瞋恚者，謂或因同梵行等舉其所犯，或因憶念昔所曾經不饒益事瞋恚之相，心生恚怒；或欲當作不饒益事，於當所為瞋恚之相多隨尋伺，心生恚怒。

Ru-Yi Translation

Ill will is this: whether from a fellow practitioner pointing out one's own fault, or from recalling some past harm and its accompanying resentment, anger arises; or, intending some future harm to another, dwelling repeatedly on that resentment with coarse and subtle thought, anger arises.

惛沈者，謂或因毀壞淨尸羅等隨一善行、不守根門、食不知量、不勤精進減省睡眠、不正知住而有所作、於所修斷不勤加行，隨順生起一切煩惱，身心惛昧，無堪任性。睡眠者，謂心極昧略。是故此二合說一蓋。

Dullness is this: from breaking pure moral discipline or any other wholesome practice, from failing to guard the sense-doors, from not knowing moderation in eating, from lacking the diligence to reduce sleep, from acting without clear awareness of what one is doing, or from failing to apply effort to what should be cultivated and abandoned — through any of these, every affliction is given room to arise, and body and mind grow dull and clouded, unfit for the task. Drowsiness is this: the mind sunk into utter dimness. This is why the two are spoken of together as a single hindrance.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes ill will's first trigger carries a specific sting worth naming honestly: being corrected by a fellow practitioner for a genuine fault, however deserved, often provokes resentment rather than gratitude — precisely because it feels like a wound to "me" rather than a simple correction of mistaken conduct; recognizing that it is only the deed at fault, not some injured self, is itself already most of the way toward letting the resentment go rather than carrying it onto the meditation cushion.

On dullness and drowsiness, he treats the sevenfold diagnosis as a genuinely useful checklist for any meditator troubled by persistent sleepiness: an unresolved ethical lapse quietly draining resolve; senses left habitually unguarded, wearing the mind out before it even sits down; eating too much, too little, or the wrong kind of food (he notes sweet foods in particular are traditionally flagged as drowsiness-inducing); insufficient reduction of ordinary sleep; sitting down without the habit of clear, moment-to-moment awareness of one's own state; simple lack of energetic effort in actually applying the antidotes to affliction; and, more generally, indulging any affliction at all, which clouds body and mind together. He explains why dullness and drowsiness, despite feeling different, are combined into one hindrance: dullness is the loss of capacity itself, letting every other affliction back in and undermining the very effort meant to counter it, while drowsiness is what dullness becomes once awareness of the meditation object is lost entirely — the two are so close in mechanism that drowsiness never arises without dullness already present, even though other kinds of affliction may or may not accompany a given bout of dullness. Cháng Bǎi adds that genuine concentration always rests on three qualities working together — mindfulness holding the object, clear comprehension noticing when the mind has wandered, and equanimity that simply rests once mindfulness is already correctly in place, needing neither further exertion nor slack correction — the same three qualities dullness and drowsiness are what most directly undermine.

12. Remorse Over Leaving Home, and a Bodhisattva's Poem to a Homesick Boy

The treatise pairs restlessness with remorse — regret over having left family and comfort behind to renounce the world, or over duties neglected and improprieties committed — and closes the five hindrances with doubt, which can undermine confidence in the Three Jewels and the very possibility of liberation, or dissolve into unresolved uncertainty about one's own existence across time and the four truths themselves.

Chinese Source

惡作者，謂因尋思親屬等故，心生追悔，謂我何緣離別親屬？何緣不往如是國土？……我本何緣少小出家，何不且待至年衰老？或因追念昔所曾經戲笑等事，便生悔恨，謂我何緣於應受用戲樂、嚴具、朋遊等時，違背宗親朋友等意，令其悲戀涕淚盈目而彊出家。

Ru-Yi Translation

Remorse is this: dwelling on thoughts of family and the rest, regret arises — why did I leave my family behind? Why did I not go to such-and-such a place instead? ... Why did I renounce the world so young, rather than waiting until old age? Or, recalling past amusements once enjoyed, regret arises — why, at a time I should have been enjoying entertainment, fine adornment, and the company of friends, did I instead go against the wishes of my family and companions, leaving them grieving and in tears, and insist on renouncing the world regardless?

疑者，謂於師、於法、於學、於誨，及於證中生惑生疑。由心如是懷疑惑故，不能趣入勇猛方便，證斷寂靜。又於去來今及苦等諦生惑生疑，心懷二分，迷之不了，猶豫猜度。

Doubt is this: uncertainty and confusion arising toward one's teacher, the teaching, the trainings, the instructions given, or toward realization itself — and because the mind is caught in such doubt, it cannot enter courageously into diligent effort, or realize the peace of genuine cessation. Further, uncertainty and confusion arise toward the past, future, and present, and toward the truth of suffering and the rest — the mind torn between two possibilities, unable to resolve the confusion, wavering and merely guessing.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads remorse as restlessness's own emotional shadow — restlessness dwells excitedly on family, homeland, mortality's distance, and past pleasures, while remorse dwells on the exact same memories with regret instead of longing, which is why the two are combined into a single hindrance despite feeling different: both are the mind agitated by the same class of memory, only inflected oppositely. He retells the story of the bodhisattva Dizang, traditionally identified with the Korean prince Kim Kiao-gak who practiced on Mount Jiuhua, and his young attendant who grew homesick after years on the mountain. Rather than scold the boy, Dizang sent him home with a poem: "In this quiet gate you long for home" — acknowledging plainly that monastic life is simple, spare, short on the ordinary warmth of family — "bid the clouds farewell, descend Jiuhua." "You'd rather ride a bamboo horse with the children than gather grains of gold on this earth" — gently naming the boy's own preference without shaming it. "Don't mistake the moon reflected at the bottom of a well for something worth scooping up" — a warning that sensory pleasure below is as illusory as a reflection, alluring but ultimately empty. "And don't linger over tea and flowers in a cup" — fleeting comforts, gone as soon as savored. "Go well, and don't weep too much — I have the clouds and mist of the mountain for company, and I am never lonely." Cháng Bǎi treats the poem's real teaching as neither guilt nor forced endurance, but honest acknowledgment paired with genuine equanimity — a model, he suggests, for how a practitioner might meet their own homesickness during long practice, without either suppressing it or being ruled by it.

On doubt, he distinguishes two registers: doubt toward the Three Jewels and the path directly saps the courage needed to actually practice, since no one applies real effort toward a goal they secretly suspect is unreachable; doubt about one's own existence across the three times, and about the Four Noble Truths themselves, is a subtler, more philosophical uncertainty — the mind genuinely unable to settle whether a given proposition is true, caught oscillating between two possibilities with no resolution. Both, he notes, are equally disqualifying for concentration, since a mind still holding a live question about whether the whole enterprise is even worth pursuing has not yet actually committed itself to the object of meditation at all.

13. What Feeds Desire, and What Starves It: Eight Alluring Marks Against the Body's Own Impurity

The treatise now asks, for each hindrance in turn, what specifically "feeds" it and what "starves" it — and for desire, names eight classical alluring marks (song, dance, smile, a coquettish glance, beauty, graceful bearing, soft touch, and physical union) as its food, set against the deliberate contemplation of the body's own impurity — both what lies inside a living body and what a corpse becomes — as the one practice strong enough to prevent desire from arising at all, or to sever it once it has.

Chinese Source

此貪欲蓋以何為食？答：有淨妙相，及於彼相不正思惟多所修習，以之為食。此復云何？謂女人身上八處所攝可愛淨相；由此八處，女縛於男。所謂歌、舞、笑、睇、美容、進止、妙觸、就禮。由此因緣，所有貪欲未生令生，生已增長，故名為食。

Ru-Yi Translation

What feeds the hindrance of desire? Answer: An appealing appearance, together with sustained unsound reflection on it, is its food. What is this, specifically? It is the eight captivating features belonging to a woman's body, by which a woman binds a man — song, dance, smiling, a lingering glance, beauty of face, graceful bearing, soft touch, and physical union. Through these causes, desire not yet arisen is caused to arise, and once arisen, is caused to grow — this is why they are called its food.

此貪欲蓋誰為非食？答：有不淨相，及於彼相如理作意多所修習，以為非食。此復云何？謂青瘀等。若觀此身種種不淨雜穢充滿，名觀內身不淨之相；復觀於外青瘀等相種種不淨，名觀外身不淨之相。由觀此二不淨相故，未生貪欲令其不生，生已能斷，故名非食。

What starves the hindrance of desire? Answer: An impure appearance, together with sustained sound reflection on it, is what starves it. What is this, specifically? It is contemplating discoloration and the rest. Observing this very body as filled with every kind of impurity is called contemplating the inward body's impurity; observing outwardly the discoloration and other stages a corpse passes through is called contemplating the outward body's impurity. Through contemplating both these impurities, desire not yet arisen is prevented from arising, and once arisen, is severed — this is why they are called what starves it.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the strategic logic here as deliberately targeted: rather than attempting to suppress every minor sensory pleasure at once, the teaching isolates the single strongest driver of desire — attraction between the sexes — on the reasoning that mastering the strongest case makes every weaker case of sensory craving correspondingly easier to release. He notes the "food" is never the physical features themselves but the mind's own unsound dwelling on them as inherently attractive, exactly the same point desire's own definition made earlier in the fascicle.

On the antidote, he distinguishes the inward and outward contemplations: inwardly, a living body is thirty-some named unclean substances and structures — hair, nails, bone, marrow, organs, and the fluids that constantly seep from its nine openings — the same anatomical unsparing detail the treatise gave of the body back in fascicle 3; outwardly, a corpse's own progression through discoloration, bloating, decay, and finally bare bone makes visible, over time, exactly what the living body already conceals beneath an appealing surface. He references — without transcribing at length — the classical verses on renouncing the five desires from the Śamatha-Vipaśyanā treatise, whose central image is a body compared to a leaking sack of impurities and a corpse one nourishes for years only to bury it in the end; their conclusion, he notes, is not self-loathing but liberation — seeing the body's actual nature clearly is precisely what frees a practitioner from being deceived and controlled by desire for it, opening the way to concentration's own much deeper happiness. He treats sound and unsound reflection here as two directions of the identical habit-forming mechanism already established for desire's "food": dwelling repeatedly on a false appealing appearance strengthens craving, while dwelling repeatedly on the same body's actual impure nature weakens and eventually starves it — the antidote works by exactly the same mechanism as the poison, aimed in the opposite direction.

14. Nine Occasions for Anger, and Loving-Kindness as Its One Real Antidote

The treatise names ill will's food as its own presence plus unsound reflection on nine specific occasions for resentment — toward oneself, toward those one loves, and toward those one dislikes, each of the latter two multiplied across past, present, and future — and names its antidote as sustained loving-kindness meditation, wishing genuine wellbeing on exactly the same nine categories of person the anger would otherwise target.

Chinese Source

此瞋恚蓋以何為食？答：有瞋恚性、有瞋恚相，及於彼相不正思惟多所修習，以之為食。依於種種不饒益事，心生惱害，名瞋恚性。不饒益事，名瞋恚相。於九惱事不正作意，名不正思惟。

Ru-Yi Translation

What feeds the hindrance of ill will? Answer: An angry disposition, an angry appearance, and sustained unsound reflection on it are its food. Resting on various harmful matters, the mind's own arising urge to injure is called the angry disposition. The harmful matter itself is called the angry appearance. Unsound attention toward the nine occasions for resentment is called unsound reflection.

此瞋恚蓋誰為非食？答：有仁慈賢善，及於彼相如理作意多所修習，以為非食。又此慈善，恆欲與他安樂為相，修力所攝。由思擇力所攝作意，調伏九惱。以能斷除瞋恚蓋故，經中唯說此為非食。

What starves the hindrance of ill will? Answer: Genuine kindness and goodwill, together with sustained sound reflection on it, is what starves it. This kindness and goodwill is characterized by constantly wishing others well-being, sustained through the power of cultivation; and through the power of careful discernment, it subdues all nine occasions for resentment. Because it is capable of severing the hindrance of ill will, the sūtras name this alone as what starves it.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi lists the nine occasions in full: resentment toward oneself; toward a loved one, in the past, present, or future; and toward a disliked person, likewise in the past, present, or future — six base categories, with the latter two tripled across time to make nine. He stresses the treatise's own reminder embedded here: none of "self," "loved one," or "disliked one" names anything ultimately real — each is simply a conventional label placed on the aggregates' own ceaseless flow, so resentment always targets a fiction, however real the pain it generates feels in the moment.

On the antidote, he traces loving-kindness meditation's own mechanism: sustained wish for others' wellbeing, developed through repeated cultivation until it becomes a settled disposition rather than an occasional effort, directly opposes ill will's own habitual disposition; and careful discernment — deliberately considering one's own situation, others' situations, both loved and disliked people, and the specific and shared marks of genuine loving-kindness — systematically works through the same nine occasions that would otherwise generate resentment, replacing each with a wish for that same person's happiness instead. He offers the classical verse from the Śamatha-Vipaśyanā treatise as a memorable summary rather than transcribing it in full: anger is named there as a poison whose root, once cut, brings both safety and freedom from worry, since a mind churning with resentment injures its own bearer well before it ever reaches its intended target — practical advice, he notes, worth memorizing and returning to whenever anger arises during practice.

15. Three Kinds of Light Against Dullness: Starlight, the Dharma's Own Radiance, and a Deva's Body

The treatise names darkness itself as what feeds dullness and drowsiness, and light as what starves it — then distinguishes three genuinely different kinds of light a meditator can call on: literal light countering literal darkness (starlight and moonlight by night, sunlight by day, firelight or a gem's glow indoors), the Dharma's own inward radiance (genuine understanding, and recollection of the Three Jewels' qualities), and the radiant light said to shine naturally from a deity's own body, useful as a specific meditation object in its own right.

Chinese Source

惛沈睡眠蓋以何為食？答：有黑闇相，及於彼相不正思惟多所修習，以之為食。此蓋誰為非食？答：有光明相，及於彼相如理作意多所修習，以為非食。光明有三種。一、治闇光明，二、法光明，三、依身光明。

Ru-Yi Translation

What feeds the hindrance of dullness and drowsiness? Answer: A darkened appearance, together with sustained unsound reflection on it, is its food. What starves this hindrance? Answer: A luminous appearance, together with sustained sound reflection on it, is what starves it. Light is of three kinds: light that counters literal darkness, the light of the Dharma, and light radiating from a being's own body.

治闇光明復有三種。一、在夜分，謂星月等；二、在晝分，謂日光明；三、在俱分，謂火珠等。法光明者，謂如有一，隨其所受、所思、所觸，觀察諸法；或復修習隨念佛等。依身光明者，謂諸有情自然身光。

Light countering literal darkness is of three further kinds: by night, starlight and moonlight; by day, sunlight; and at either time, firelight or a luminous gem. The light of the Dharma is this: observing all phenomena resting on what one has learned, reflected on, and directly experienced of the teaching; or, further, cultivating recollection of the Buddha and the rest. Light radiating from the body is the natural radiance certain beings' own bodies give off.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the three lights as addressing three different depths of darkness: physical light simply counters the literal darkness of night, an overcast sky, or an enclosed room, useful mainly as a concrete visualization aid when drowsiness sets in during sitting practice; the light of the Dharma counters a subtler darkness — the specific ignorance and doubt named among the nineteen kinds of not-knowing surveyed at length back in fascicle 10's own analysis of dependent origination's first link, dispelled here by genuinely internalized understanding of the teaching (what has been learned, reflected on, and directly experienced, the three wisdoms in familiar sequence) and by recollecting the Three Jewels' own qualities to rouse a flagging, sinking mind back to alertness. He cites the Platform Sūtra's own line — "one lamp dispels a thousand years of darkness; one moment of wisdom destroys ten thousand years of delusion" — as capturing exactly why the Dharma itself is called a light: it doesn't merely occupy a dark mind, it structurally cannot coexist with the ignorance that dullness feeds on.

On the body's own radiance, he notes this refers to certain heavenly beings' naturally luminous bodies, already surveyed in the treatise's own earlier cosmological material, and explains its specific meditative use: a practitioner cultivating what the tradition calls "the great mind" concentration can visualize this same radiance as a way of purifying and stabilizing the first dhyāna itself, a technique the fascicle will return to in more detail later. He treats the whole threefold scheme as moving from outermost to innermost: physical light for a physically dark room, the Dharma's light for a mind darkened by ignorance and doubt, and a visualized deity's radiance as a specific, advanced meditative support — three tools for the same underlying problem of a mind that has gone dim rather than clear.

16. What Feeds Restlessness: Family, Homeland, the Illusion of Endless Time, and Remembered Pleasure

The treatise names restlessness and remorse's shared food as dwelling on family, homeland, and one's own apparent freedom from imminent death, together with recollected laughter, games, shared pleasure, and physical intimacy — four distinct pulls, each capable of carrying a meditating mind entirely out of the present moment and into an imagined past or future.

Chinese Source

掉舉惡作蓋以何為食？答：於親屬等所有尋思，於曾所經戲笑等念，及於彼相不正思惟多所修習，以之為食。親屬尋思者，謂因親屬或盛或衰、或離或合，發欣慼行，心生籌慮等。國土尋思者，謂因國土盛衰等相。不死尋思者，謂因少年及衰老位諸有所作或利他事，發欣慼行，心生籌慮等。

Ru-Yi Translation

What feeds the hindrance of restlessness and remorse? Answer: Thoughts of family and the rest, memories of past laughter and amusement, together with sustained unsound reflection on them, are its food. Thoughts of family are these: prompted by a family member's prosperity or decline, separation or reunion, gladness or grief arises, and the mind churns over it at length. Thoughts of homeland are these: prompted by a homeland's own prosperity or decline. Thoughts of freedom from imminent death are these: prompted by what one could still accomplish, for oneself or others, in youth or in old age, gladness or grief arises, and the mind churns over it at length.

笑者，謂隨有一，或因開論、或因合論，現齒而笑，歡聚啞啞。戲者，謂雙陸、摴蒲、弄珠等戲。或有所餘種類歡樂。謂互相受用，受用境界，受諸快樂。所行事者，謂相執持手臂髮等，或相摩觸隨一身分，或抱或嗚，或相顧眄，或作餘事。

Laughter is this: through elaborate storytelling or clever, condensed wit, teeth bared in laughter, a joyful gathering's noise. Games are this: dice, gambling, and similar amusements. There are, further, other kinds of pleasure: mutual enjoyment between people, and enjoyment of sensory objects, each bringing its own pleasure. Physical intimacy is this: holding hands or hair, touching any part of the body, embracing, kissing, exchanging lingering glances, or other such acts.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes all three "thoughts" share the same structure: a genuinely neutral fact (a relative's circumstances, a homeland's condition, one's own current youth or vigor) is elaborated by the mind into an entire emotional narrative — gladness if things seem favorable, grief if not — that has nothing to do with the meditation object at hand. He treats "freedom from imminent death" as the subtlest and most dangerous of the three, since it rests on a simple illusion: a young or currently capable person unconsciously assumes there will always be more time, quietly deferring real practice to some later, more convenient season that death may never actually grant.

He summarizes the antidote reasoning the text develops for each: for family, the classical image of birds roosting together on a single branch at nightfall only to scatter in every direction come morning — kinship itself is simply karma's own temporary convergence, no more permanent than sand held loosely in a closed fist, which stays together only as long as the hand stays closed; a renunciant who has already formally left family behind gains nothing by mentally returning to it night after night. For homeland, every place has its own genuine drawbacks — too cold, too hot, too dangerous, too disease-ridden — and travel rarely lives up to its own reputation; the real point, put memorably, is that "when the mind is pure, the land is pure" — wherever one can actually practice well already is the best possible place, with no need to go seeking another. For mortality, death's timing is entirely unpredictable, and the Dharmapada's own counsel — do only what should be done, and do it always — is offered as the practical antidote to a mind quietly betting on having more time than it may actually have.

17. The Antidote to Restlessness: Nine Stages of Settling, and Neither Rejoicing Nor Grieving

The treatise names calm-abiding itself — the nine progressive stages by which a wandering mind is actually settled — as what starves restlessness and remorse, paired with a specific reflection: both decline and prosperity, in oneself or others, are equally impermanent and equally not worth the emotional investment restlessness pours into them, since prosperity is exactly as fragile as decline is painful.

Chinese Source

此蓋誰為非食？答：有奢摩他，及於彼相如理作意多所修習，以為非食。奢摩他者，謂九種住心，及奢摩他品所攝諸法。謂於自他若衰若盛可厭患法心生厭離，驚恐惡賤，安住寂靜。

Ru-Yi Translation

What starves this hindrance? Answer: Calm-abiding, together with sustained sound reflection on it, is what starves it. Calm-abiding is the nine stages of settling the mind, together with the various practices belonging to calm-abiding. This is toward whatever, in oneself or another, counts as decline or as prosperity, both being causes for genuine dismay: the mind grows dispassionate toward them, wary of the one, disinclined toward the other, and settles instead into genuine stillness.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi names the nine stages of settling in their working sequence: inward abiding, first withdrawing attention from external objects; sustained abiding, evening out the mind's tendency to wander outward moment to moment; renewed abiding, catching and re-settling the mind whenever it does wander; close abiding, bringing attention back to the object again and again until it stays near; tamed abiding, seeing through the appeal of sensory objects and affliction clearly enough that the mind no longer chases them; pacified abiding, quieting the mind's own unwholesome deliberation and secondary afflictions directly; fully pacified abiding, actively rejecting even a residual unwholesome thought the instant it arises, rather than merely tolerating it; single-pointed abiding, sustained, unbroken concentration achieved through deliberate effort; and finally genuine equipoise, where the same unbroken concentration continues effortlessly, no longer requiring active effort to maintain — the full arc from a scattered beginner's mind to settled absorption.

On the specific antidote reflection, he draws out its deliberately symmetrical logic: decline (illness, shortened life, loss of family, loss of wealth, broken precepts, corrupted view) is obviously painful and worth turning from, but the text insists prosperity deserves exactly the same wary treatment, since anyone who has ever enjoyed good fortune will eventually lose it, while the reverse — recovering from genuine decline — is far harder to count on; prosperity is, in his phrase, "easily lost and hard to regain," making it no less worth remaining equanimous about than decline itself. He treats the practical instruction as aimed at emotional evenness above all: a mind riding high on good news or crushed by bad news is equally unfit for settling into concentration, so the real skill is meeting both with the same measured wariness — genuinely grateful for neither, disturbed by neither — which alone leaves the mind free to actually rest on its meditation object rather than being pulled by whatever just happened.

18. What Feeds Doubt: Speculating About Self, Other Beings, and the Nature of the World

The treatise names doubt's food as unsound speculation about one's own existence across the three times, extended to two further forbidden territories — speculation about other beings and about the nature of the world itself (whether it is permanent, impermanent, both, or neither) — and explains, through a careful two-horned analysis, exactly why entertaining either side of these questions is a trap regardless of which answer one picks.

Chinese Source

疑蓋以何為食？答：有去來今，及於彼相不正思惟多所修習，以之為食。謂我於過去為有？為無？廣說如上。不正思惟者，謂不可思處所攝思惟。不可思處者，謂我思惟、有情思惟、世間思惟。

Ru-Yi Translation

What feeds the hindrance of doubt? Answer: The past, future, and present, together with sustained unsound reflection on them, are its food — was I, in the past, existent or not, and so on as explained at length elsewhere. Unsound reflection is reflection belonging to what should not be reflected on at all. What should not be reflected on is speculation about the self, about other beings, and about the world.

若於自處，依世差別思惟我相，名我思惟。若於他處，名有情思惟。若於有情世間及器世間處，名世間思惟。謂世間常、或謂無常、亦常亦無常、非常非無常等。

Speculating about one's own self across the differences of time is called speculation about the self. Doing the same regarding another is called speculation about other beings. Speculating about the world of sentient beings and the physical world itself — whether the world is permanent, impermanent, both, or neither, and so on — is called speculation about the world.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi cites the Xianyang Shengjiao Lun's own precise reasoning for why even asking "does a self exist?" is already a trap: answering "yes" commits the error of adding something real (a self) onto what is actually just the five aggregates, which contain no such thing; answering "no" commits the opposite error of subtracting something real — the conventional, functioning person who does experience cause and effect — reducing to a nihilism that denies karma altogether. The same double bind applies to other beings (claiming they are identical to oneself, or claiming they are entirely separate, both generate absurd consequences) and to the world's own nature (whether it is permanent, impermanent, or any combination), since the world's actual arising is, in the text's own words, "commonly known and already evident" — a functioning, observable process that speculative theorizing about its ultimate metaphysical status can only distort, never clarify.

He treats this whole prohibition as targeted specifically at what these fourteen or so classical unanswerable questions have in common: none of them, however they are answered, actually reduces affliction or advances a practitioner one step toward liberation — dwelling on them only breeds more uncertainty, since a meditator who spends the sitting period wondering whether the self is permanent has, by definition, stopped attending to the meditation object altogether. The instruction is not that these questions are forbidden to think about in principle, but that they are simply the wrong tool for the task at hand — settling the mind requires resting it on a single, workable object, not chasing metaphysical puzzles that the treatise elsewhere shows to be badly formed from the start.

19. What Starves Doubt: Understanding Dependent Origination — and the Five Hindrances Close

The treatise names genuine understanding of dependent origination itself as what starves doubt, since seeing clearly that a being is nothing more than the twelve links' own causal unfolding leaves no room for the unanswerable speculations doubt otherwise feeds on — closing out the five hindrances entirely with a final, general principle: each hindrance has its own specific right attention as antidote, tailored precisely to what that hindrance itself misunderstands.

Chinese Source

此蓋誰為非食？答：有緣緣起，及於彼相如理作意多所修習，以為非食。由彼觀見唯有於法及唯法因，唯有於苦及唯苦因故，所有一切不正思惟為緣無明，於三世境未生者不生，已生者能斷。

Ru-Yi Translation

What starves this hindrance? Answer: Conditioned dependent origination, together with sustained sound reflection on it, is what starves it. Because such a practitioner sees clearly that there is only this conditioned process and its cause, only this suffering and its cause, the ignorance conditioning every kind of unsound reflection is, regarding the three times, prevented from arising where not yet arisen, and severed where already arisen.

謂於闇中作光明想。由此方便如理作意，非不如理。於餘處所，亦有所餘如理作意。

This is like forming the perception of light within darkness. By this method, the reflection is sound, not unsound. And in every other case, there is likewise its own appropriate sound reflection.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that doubt's speculative questions (does a self exist across time, is the world permanent) only ever arise because a person has not yet clearly seen what actually is there: not a self, but the twelve links — ignorance and formations projecting consciousness through feeling, craving and grasping moistening that projection into becoming, birth and old-age-death as its lived result. Once this causal structure is genuinely understood, the question "does the self exist in the past" simply dissolves, since there was never a self there to begin with, only a causal process — dependent origination itself becomes the light dispelling the specific darkness doubt feeds on, exactly paralleling how the earlier "light" antidote countered dullness's own darkness.

He treats the closing line — every hindrance has its own tailored antidote — as the section's real summary: impurity contemplation for desire, loving-kindness for ill will, light for dullness, calm-abiding's settling stages for restlessness, and understanding dependent origination for doubt are not five arbitrary techniques but five precisely matched responses to five distinct kinds of misunderstanding, each countering exactly what generates its corresponding hindrance rather than being a generic, one-size-fits-all remedy. With this, the treatise's account of the five hindrances — their nature, and what feeds and starves each one — is complete in its entirety, and the fascicle now turns to its next major topic: the specific distinguishing qualities of each of the four dhyānas themselves.

20. The First Dhyāna's Five Qualities: Method, Benefit, and Essence Working Together

The treatise names the first dhyāna's five distinguishing qualities — applied thought, sustained thought, joy, bliss, and one-pointedness of mind — and shows they are not five separate features but three functionally distinct roles: applied and sustained thought are the method that actually produces the absorption, joy and bliss are its resulting benefit, and one-pointedness is the very essence all four of the others serve.

Chinese Source

復次，於初靜慮具足五支。一、尋，二、伺，三、喜，四、樂，五、心一境性。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, the first dhyāna is fully endowed with five qualities: first, applied thought; second, sustained thought; third, joy; fourth, bliss; fifth, one-pointedness of mind.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi structures the five according to the Xianyang Shengjiao Lun's own threefold analysis: applied and sustained thought are the method-quality, the actual coarse and fine investigation — contemplating the body's impurity to release desire, or cultivating loving-kindness and compassion to release hostility and harm — that does the work of overcoming the desire realm's two defilements (affliction and karma) in the first place; joy and bliss are the benefit-quality, the resulting reward once that method succeeds — joy specifically a mental gladness, vigor, and ease felt through the transformed sixth consciousness once the transition into absorption has occurred, and bliss specifically a bodily ease he locates, per this treatise's own Yogācāra account, in the storehouse consciousness sustaining a now-untroubled body even while the five sense-consciousnesses have quieted; and one-pointedness of mind is the essence-quality, the actual substance of the absorption itself, resting free of dullness and agitation, effortlessly unified on its object — the foundation all the other four qualities exist to establish and support.

He treats this three-part structure (method, benefit, essence) as the template the treatise will now apply to each of the remaining three dhyānas in turn, showing exactly how each dhyāna's own qualities shift as coarser methods are no longer needed, benefits deepen, and the essence itself becomes progressively more refined and less effortful.

21. The Remaining Three Dhyānas: Inner Clarity, the Swimmer Fully Submerged, and Purified Stillness

The treatise gives the remaining three dhyānas' own distinguishing qualities: the second replaces applied and sustained thought with inner clarity, settling the dust those very thoughts had stirred up; the third replaces joy's own excitement with equanimity, mindfulness, and clear comprehension, retaining only the deeper bliss beneath it; and the fourth purifies even equanimity and mindfulness themselves, arriving at neutral feeling utterly undisturbed by any of the three cosmic calamities or eight kinds of agitation.

Chinese Source

第二靜慮有四支。一、內等淨，二、喜，三、樂，四、心一境性。第三靜慮有五支。一、捨，二、念，三、正知，四、樂，五、心一境性。第四靜慮有四支。一、捨清淨，二、念清淨，三、不苦不樂受，四、心一境性。

Ru-Yi Translation

The second dhyāna has four qualities: first, inner clarity; second, joy; third, bliss; fourth, one-pointedness of mind. The third dhyāna has five qualities: first, equanimity; second, mindfulness; third, clear comprehension; fourth, bliss; fifth, one-pointedness of mind. The fourth dhyāna has four qualities: first, purified equanimity; second, purified mindfulness; third, neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling; fourth, one-pointedness of mind.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi carries the method/benefit/essence template forward: in the second dhyāna, inner clarity is now the method-quality — no longer the coarse work of applied and sustained thought, but a settled, undivided, undistorted mind that has simply let that same applied and sustained thought go, recognizing it as agitating dust now that a calmer standard has been reached; joy and bliss remain, deepened, as the benefit-quality; one-pointedness remains the essence. In the third dhyāna, equanimity, mindfulness, and clear comprehension together form the method-quality, actively working to release joy's own excitement — mindfulness holding steady the recognition that joy is itself agitation, clear comprehension catching it the instant it tries to reassert itself, equanimity resting undisturbed once it is gone; bliss alone remains as benefit, now, in his words, the most sublime bliss in the entire three realms, illustrated by the classical image of a swimmer whose head still breaks the surface in joy but who, in bliss, is fully submerged, the whole body immersed at once rather than merely touched at the surface.

In the fourth dhyāna, purified equanimity and purified mindfulness form the method-quality, now releasing bliss itself as still too subtly agitating; the benefit-quality is neutral feeling, neither pleasant nor painful, utterly still — the treatise's own description places it beyond the reach of the three cosmic calamities (fire, water, wind) and the eight kinds of movement (applied and sustained thought, joy, bliss, sorrow, pain, and the in- and out-breath itself, which ceases entirely at this level). He notes the Yogācāra tradition insists mindfulness, clear comprehension, and equanimity together are indispensable at every one of the four levels without exception — mindfulness to know which state one currently occupies, clear comprehension to notice immediately if a coarser state has crept back in, and equanimity to release it — the same three-part operating method simply growing more refined as the levels themselves grow more refined.

22. Why These Particular Qualities: Object, Support, and Enjoyment

The treatise explains why, among everything genuinely present in each dhyāna, these specific qualities alone were singled out as its defining "support" — each quality falls into one of three roles: the object actually contemplated to reach the level, the concentration itself that all four levels rest on, and the specific benefit actually enjoyed once the level is attained — with the object and its resulting benefit shifting at each level while concentration itself remains the constant foundation throughout.

Chinese Source

初靜慮中，尋伺為取所緣，三摩地為彼所依，喜為受境界，樂為除麤重。第二靜慮中，內等淨為取所緣，三摩地為彼所依，餘如前說。第三靜慮中，捨、念、正知為取所緣，三摩地為彼所依，餘如前說。第四靜慮中，捨淨、念淨為取所緣，三摩地為彼所依，餘如前說。

Ru-Yi Translation

In the first dhyāna, applied and sustained thought are what take hold of the object; concentration is what all of this rests on; joy is the experienced benefit; bliss is what removes coarse unfitness. In the second dhyāna, inner clarity is what takes hold of the object; concentration is what this rests on; the rest is as already explained. In the third dhyāna, equanimity, mindfulness, and clear comprehension are what take hold of the object; concentration is what this rests on; the rest is as already explained. In the fourth dhyāna, purified equanimity and purified mindfulness are what take hold of the object; concentration is what this rests on; the rest is as already explained.

諸靜慮中雖有餘法，然此勝故，於修定者為恩重故，偏立為支。

Although other factors are genuinely present within each of these meditative absorptions, it is specifically these that are most excellent, and of the greatest benefit to the practitioner cultivating concentration — this is why these alone are singled out and established as each absorption's own defining qualities.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the underlying three-part logic named explicitly in the Yogācāra tradition's own cross-reference: each dhyāna's qualities sort by reflecting on the object (what the practitioner actually contemplates to reach that level — impurity or loving-kindness for the first, releasing coarser thought for the second, releasing joy for the third, releasing bliss for the fourth), resting on that object without distraction (concentration itself, one-pointedness, the single constant across all four levels), and enjoying the object once attained (the specific feeling-benefit each level actually delivers — joy and bliss for the first two, bliss alone for the third, neutral feeling for the fourth). He stresses that a great many other qualities are genuinely present at each level too — the treatise itself acknowledges this — but only these particular ones are singled out because they are what actually matter most to a practitioner: the specific tool that gets a level reached, and the specific reward that level actually delivers, rather than every incidental feature that happens to accompany it.

He treats this whole comparative table as evidence of a single continuous logic running through all four levels: at each stage, a specific method releases whatever the previous stage found agitating, delivers a specific resulting benefit, and rests throughout on the identical foundation of one-pointed concentration — the entire path from the first dhyāna to the fourth is thus not four unrelated states but one continuous refinement, each level simply seeing more clearly than the last what still counts as disturbance.

23. Each Level Named for the Fault It Sees in the One Before

The treatise names a single pattern running through all four dhyānas: each higher level is reached specifically by seeing a fault in the level just below it — the second sees applied and sustained thought's own disturbance and is called "stilled of thought," the third sees joy's own agitation and is called "stilled of joy," the fourth sees bliss's own subtle disturbance and is called "stilled of bliss" — with equanimity, mindfulness, and clear comprehension present at every level throughout, simply named differently as each level's own specific target of release shifts.

Chinese Source

第二靜慮能觀彼過，是故說為尋伺寂靜。如第二靜慮見彼過故，名尋伺寂靜；如是第三靜慮見喜過故，名喜寂靜；第四靜慮見樂過故，名樂寂靜。捨念清淨，差別應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

The second dhyāna is able to observe that fault, and is therefore called stilled of applied and sustained thought. Just as the second dhyāna, seeing that fault, is called stilled of thought, so the third dhyāna, seeing joy's own fault, is called stilled of joy; and the fourth dhyāna, seeing bliss's own fault, is called stilled of bliss. The difference in how purified equanimity and mindfulness are named at each level should be understood accordingly.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi draws out why equanimity, mindfulness, and clear comprehension are present at every single level yet only explicitly named at some: in the first dhyāna they are simply folded into applied and sustained thought's own working, present but not separately called out; in the second, they surface under the name "inner clarity" because joy's own excitement still lingers as something requiring active management; in the third, joy's excitement has genuinely been released, so the three qualities appear under their own proper names — equanimity, mindfulness, clear comprehension — no longer folded into a broader label; and in the fourth, the same three qualities reach their most refined expression yet, now called "purified" specifically because even bliss, the last remaining subtle disturbance, has finally been released as well. He treats this naming pattern itself as instructive: it is never that a new tool is introduced at each level, but that the identical tool — steady awareness, immediate recognition of disturbance, and the release of whatever is recognized — simply operates on a progressively finer target, from the coarse thought of the first level down to the barely perceptible remainder still present in the third.

24. Two More Names for the Dhyānas: "Elevated Mind," and Why Only These Four Count as "Dwelling in Happiness"

The treatise gives the four dhyānas two further names — "elevated mind," since a genuinely settled, undistracted mind carries the power to investigate the truths clearly, the way still water reveals what troubled water hides; and "dwelling in happiness," since only these four, uniquely among every meditative attainment, deliver a happiness felt in both body and mind together, which is exactly why the four formless attainments, however deep, are never given this same name.

Chinese Source

復次，是諸靜慮名差別者，或名增上心。謂由心清淨增上力正審慮故。或名樂住。謂於此中受極樂故。依諸靜慮領受喜樂、安樂、捨樂身心樂故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, these dhyānas have further names as well. They are called "elevated mind," because it is through the elevating power of a purified mind that correct, careful examination becomes possible. They are called "dwelling in happiness," because within them one experiences the most complete happiness — resting on these absorptions, one experiences the happiness of joy, of ease, and of equanimity, a happiness of both body and mind together.

又得定者於諸靜慮數數入出，領受現法安樂住故。於無色定無如是受，是故不說彼為樂住。

Further, one who has attained concentration repeatedly enters and exits these absorptions, thereby experiencing this happiness, present and immediate, again and again. The formless attainments carry no such experience, which is why they are never called "dwelling in happiness."

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses "elevated mind" through the treatise's own comparison: a mind not yet settled is like a candle in the wind, guttering the instant any of the eight worldly winds (praise, blame, gain, loss, and the rest) blows through it — genuine learning and eloquence count for nothing in the moment they're actually needed, because attention was never trained to hold steady on one object long enough to build real strength. A settled mind, by contrast, is like still water: nothing troubles its surface, so whatever lies beneath becomes visible, and sound reasoning attention can actually be applied wherever it's needed rather than being swept away by habitual reactive patterns.

He explains the careful distinction behind "dwelling in happiness": the first and second dhyānas deliver joy, the third delivers bliss, and the fourth delivers a settled equanimity that itself counts as a kind of happiness — precisely because reaching total freedom from craving after happiness is itself experienced as deeply satisfying — and all four, uniquely, deliver this experience in both body and mind at once, since even the fourth dhyāna, though free of ordinary pleasant feeling, still belongs to the form realm and thus still has a body to be at ease in. The four formless attainments, by contrast, have no body at all, and therefore no bodily happiness to speak of — mental stillness alone, however profound, does not qualify for this particular name. He adds a small further point some traditions raise: even the fourth dhyāna's equanimity can rightly be called a happiness in its own right, since attaining exactly what was sought — freedom from the pull of bliss itself — is its own kind of satisfaction, distinct from but not lesser than the more obviously pleasurable happiness of the lower three levels.

25. Why a Realized Practitioner Must Say So When Asked, and Two Names Marking Absorption's Real Limits

The treatise explains a specific social obligation on an accomplished meditator: when directly asked whether one has attained the formless absorptions, silence is not humility but a disservice to the teaching itself, since it leaves a genuine questioner free to conclude the whole path is unattainable — and names two further terms for the dhyānas that mark exactly how far meditative absorption alone actually reaches: "partial nirvāṇa," since only some affliction is suppressed, and "provisional nirvāṇa," since it is not the real thing at all, being equally available to non-Buddhist meditators.

Chinese Source

然彼起已，應正宣說。何以故？若有阿練若苾芻來就彼問，彼若不答，便生譏論：此阿練若苾芻云何名為阿練若者，我今問彼超色無色寂靜解脫，而不能記。是故為說應入彼定，非為樂住。

Ru-Yi Translation

Yet, having arisen from that attainment, one should rightly declare it. Why? Because if a forest-dwelling monastic comes and asks, and one does not answer, criticism follows: "How can this forest-dweller be called such, when I asked about the peaceful liberation transcending form and formlessness, and no answer could be given?" This is why one should explain: I enter that absorption for this purpose, not for the sake of dwelling in happiness.

或復名為彼分涅槃，亦得說名差別涅槃。由諸煩惱一分斷故，非決定故，名彼分涅槃。非究竟涅槃故，名差別涅槃。

They are also called "partial nirvāṇa," and may likewise be called "provisional nirvāṇa." Because only a portion of affliction is thereby severed, and not with any final certainty, they are called partial nirvāṇa. Because this is not ultimate nirvāṇa, they are called provisional nirvāṇa.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains the obligation to speak: a genuinely accomplished meditator staying silent when directly questioned risks having the questioner conclude that no one actually attains these states, casting doubt on the entire teaching rather than simply on one modest individual — false humility, in this specific case, does real harm. He notes the deeper point buried in the required disclosure: the answer must clarify that one enters formless absorption to investigate reality (contemplating emptiness, boundless consciousness, nothingness, or neither-perception-nor-non-perception) rather than merely to enjoy a pleasant abiding — since the formless attainments, having no body at all, carry no bodily happiness and are therefore never rightly called "dwelling in happiness" the way the four dhyānas are, a point requiring exactly this kind of correction to avoid being misunderstood.

On the two further names, he stresses both mark absorption's real ceiling: "partial nirvāṇa" because each level only suppresses its own specific target of affliction (the first suppresses desire and harm, the second applied-and-sustained thought, and so on) without touching that affliction's underlying seed — cut the grass but not the root, and it grows back the moment conditions permit, which is why the text calls this "uncertain" rather than final. "Provisional nirvāṇa" makes the same point from a different angle: non-Buddhist meditators reach these identical absorptions through identical technique, proving decisively that dhyāna itself, however genuinely peaceful, is not yet the liberation the path is actually aimed at — exactly the caution fascicles 6 and 7's survey of the sixteen non-Buddhist doctrines, and fascicle 9's account of Udraka Rāmaputra's fall, already sounded at length.

26. Five Levels, Five Feelings Released: Sorrow, Pain, Joy, Pleasure, and Equanimity Itself

The treatise names a further way to describe the four dhyānas, extended one level beyond them: each level is defined by which specific feeling it releases — the first releases sorrow, the second releases pain, the third releases joy, the fourth releases pleasure, and, beyond all four, the signless concentration of the noble path itself releases even equanimity, the last and subtlest feeling of all.

Chinese Source

復次，此四靜慮亦得名為出諸受事。謂初靜慮出離憂根，第二靜慮出離苦根，第三靜慮出離喜根，第四靜慮出離樂根，於無相中出離捨根。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, these four dhyānas may also be called the release of feeling. The first dhyāna releases the faculty of sorrow; the second dhyāna releases the faculty of pain; the third dhyāna releases the faculty of joy; the fourth dhyāna releases the faculty of pleasure; and within the signless concentration, the faculty of equanimity itself is released.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps this fivefold release onto the classical five feeling-faculties (sorrow, pain, joy, pleasure, and equanimity) rather than the simpler threefold pleasant-painful-neutral scheme, giving a more granular account of exactly when each drops away: sorrow, the mental distress bound up with desire realm craving and its frustration, is released already at the threshold of the first dhyāna, since seclusion from desire is precisely what that level accomplishes; bodily pain is released at the second dhyāna, once the coarse discomfort tied to ordinary bodily existence has settled; joy's own excitement is released at the third, exactly as already explained; and pleasure itself, the fourth dhyāna's own last remaining positive feeling, is finally released at the fourth level, arriving at pure equanimity.

He treats the closing reference to the signless concentration as pointing one step beyond ordinary meditative attainment altogether: even the settled equanimity of the fourth dhyāna, however refined, is still a feeling with content, still something to rest in — the noble path's own signless concentration goes further still, releasing equanimity itself as the very last thing standing between a practitioner and genuine liberation. He treats the whole progression as confirming, from yet another angle, the point the previous section already made explicit: absorption by itself, however deep, only ever suppresses feeling level by level; only the path's own liberating insight, layered on top of these same absorptions, actually finishes the job.

27. Diagnosing Sorrow While It's Happening: Cause, Condition, Origin, Mark, and Activity

The treatise turns from simply naming what each dhyāna releases to teaching how a practitioner still working toward the first dhyāna should actually recognize sorrow the moment it arises — quoting a sūtra directly, then breaking the recognition itself into five distinct questions to ask: what is its seed-cause, what conditions support it, what specifically triggered it this time, what does it actually feel like, and what mental activity is it driving.

Chinese Source

如薄伽梵無倒經中說如是言：苾芻！憂根生已，應當如實了知生者。此於何位？謂即於此斷方便位，若為憂根間心相續，爾時應知。又應并此因、緣及序，若相，若行，皆如實知者。

Ru-Yi Translation

As the Blessed One said in the Sūtra Free of Distortion: "Monastics, once the faculty of sorrow has arisen, it should be truly known as arisen." At what stage does this apply? It applies precisely at the stage of striving to sever sorrow — whenever sorrow interrupts the mind's own continuity, one should recognize it at that very moment. Further, its cause, its condition, its origin, its mark, and its activity should all likewise be truly known.

云何知因？謂了知此種子相續。云何知緣？謂了知此種所不攝所依、助伴。云何知序？謂知憂根託此事生，即是能發憂根之相及無知種子。云何知相？謂了知此是慼行相。云何知行？謂了知此能發之行，即不如理作意相應思也。

What is knowing its cause? Knowing that this sorrow's own seed continues on. What is knowing its condition? Knowing the basis and accompanying factors not already contained within that seed. What is knowing its origin? Knowing what specific occasion sorrow rests on to arise — namely, the mark that actually triggers it, together with the seed of ignorance behind it. What is knowing its mark? Knowing that this is grief's own characteristic feel. What is knowing its activity? Knowing the activity it drives — namely, intention associated with unsound reflection.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this fivefold diagnostic as a genuinely practical meditation instruction disguised as Abhidharma classification: a practitioner still working to release sorrow entirely (someone who has renounced ordinary sensory pleasure but has not yet reached the first dhyāna) will find sorrow interrupting practice from time to time, and the instruction is not simply to suppress it but to actually examine it — where does this sorrow's dormant seed come from (its cause); what present conditions, beyond the bare seed, are letting it surface right now, such as an unguarded sense-door or unsound attention (its condition); what two specific triggers set it off this particular time — either a lingering attachment to sensory pleasure now denied, or, just as often, worry over one's own lack of visible progress toward the very absorption being sought (its origin, itself traced back to ignorance's own seed); what it actually feels like, the characteristic knitted-brow heaviness of grief (its mark); and what it is currently driving the mind to do, namely further unsound, distorted thinking (its activity).

He notes the same five feeling-faculties (pain, pleasure, sorrow, joy, and equanimity) are elsewhere given six distinct justifications for why they were singled out at all — as what actually experiences the fruit of karma, as what most powerfully triggers defilement in contact with objects both outward and inward, as what most powerfully affects the body's own wellbeing, as what most clearly reveals a being's flourishing or decline, as what tracks directly onto the four kinds of food sustaining a body, and as what clear comprehension must track moment to moment in order to keep unwholesome reactivity from ever gaining a foothold — reasons which together explain why this fivefold analysis, rather than some other scheme, was chosen as meditation's own diagnostic vocabulary. He treats the whole method as exportable well beyond sorrow specifically: exactly the same five questions — cause, condition, origin, mark, activity — can, and should, be asked of any afflictive state that surfaces during practice, turning what might otherwise be experienced as a vague, undifferentiated disturbance into something precise enough to actually work with.

28. Restraining the Mind, Then Uprooting the Seed — Why Worldly Concentration Alone Never Finishes the Job

Having diagnosed sorrow, the treatise gives the remaining two steps: restraining the mind from continuing to wander toward whatever provoked it, redirecting attention firmly back to the meditation object; and, ultimately, eliminating sorrow completely — both its active manifestation and its underlying seed. This closing distinction is decisive: ordinary worldly concentration can only ever suppress the manifestation, leaving the seed fully intact, while only uncontaminated concentration removes both, which is why worldly absorption can always be lost again and uncontaminated absorption cannot.

Chinese Source

如是知已，於出離中極制持心者，云何制持？謂於染汙行制攝其心，於思惟修任持堅住。又於是中，無餘盡滅乃至究竟者。謂滅隨眠故，滅諸纏故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Having thus recognized it, how does one firmly restrain and hold the mind in the course of releasing it? By restraining and gathering the mind away from defiled activity, and by sustaining it, firmly held, in reflective cultivation. And what is meant by its being eliminated entirely, down to the very end? It means the latent tendency itself is eliminated, and its active manifestation is eliminated.

世間靜慮但能漸捨彼品麤重，不拔種子。若異此者，種永拔故，後不應生。無漏靜慮二種俱捨。如是於餘隨應當知。

Worldly meditative absorption can only gradually shed that category's own coarse unfitness; it cannot uproot the underlying seed. Were it otherwise — were the seed truly and permanently uprooted — it should never arise again afterward. Uncontaminated meditative absorption discards both. The same should be understood, as applicable, for every other feeling-faculty as well.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi summarizes the complete four-step method this whole passage has now laid out, applicable to any afflictive state a meditator encounters, not sorrow alone: first, simply recognize that the state has arisen at all, since most people remain entirely unaware they are even caught in affliction; second, diagnose it through the five questions just given — its seed, its supporting conditions, its specific trigger, its felt quality, and the further activity it is driving; third, restrain the mind from continuing to feed it, redirecting attention firmly back to genuinely wholesome reflection rather than dwelling on what provoked it; and fourth, aim at its complete elimination, both the currently manifest disturbance and the dormant seed still capable of producing it again later.

He treats the closing distinction between worldly and uncontaminated concentration as the entire passage's real payoff: worldly absorption — concentration alone, without the specific liberating insight the treatise describes elsewhere as resting on it — can only ever suppress a given affliction's coarse, currently active form, exactly as a stone weighs down grass without killing its root; this is precisely why even a genuinely accomplished meditator can still, under the right renewed conditions, find an old affliction reasserting itself. Only uncontaminated absorption, joined with the direct insight that actually severs a seed at its root, removes both layers at once — proof, he stresses, that concentration by itself, however deep or however many levels it climbs through, was never going to be sufficient on its own; it is always concentration paired with wisdom, never concentration alone, that finishes what affliction's diagnosis and restraint can only begin.

29. Two Faces of Sorrow: Craving Denied, and the Anxious Longing for Liberation Not Yet Reached

The treatise names sorrow's own two distinct marks — one rooted in ordinary craving for sensory pleasure now lost, denied, or changed, the other a subtler and genuinely well-intentioned grief felt specifically by a sincere practitioner who has already seen through the desire realm's own impermanence but has not yet actually reached the liberation being sought — before turning briefly to pain, whose four ordinary causes even a first-dhyāna practitioner has not yet fully escaped.

Chinese Source

以何等相了知憂根？答：或染汙相、或出離欲俱行善相。

Ru-Yi Translation

By what mark is the faculty of sorrow known? Answer: Either by a defiled mark, or by a wholesome mark accompanying the wish to be free of desire.

苦根者，或由自等增上力故，或由身勞增上力故，或火燒等增上力故，或他逼等增上力故，諸離欲者猶尚生起。喜根者，謂第二靜慮中，即第二靜慮地攝。樂根者，謂第三靜慮中，即第三靜慮地攝。

The faculty of pain arises through the dominant force of harm to oneself and the like, through the dominant force of bodily fatigue, through the dominant force of burning by fire and the like, or through the dominant force of harm inflicted by another — even one who has become free of desire still experiences this. The faculty of joy belongs to the second dhyāna, included within that level. The faculty of pleasure belongs to the third dhyāna, included within that level.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes sorrow's two marks with real precision: the first, "sorrow rooted in craving," arises whenever something pleasing to the eye or any other sense is wanted but not obtained, or was once enjoyed but is now past, diminished, lost, or simply changed — the familiar grief of ordinary attachment. The second, "sorrow accompanying the wish for freedom," is subtler and, he stresses, not itself a fault: a sincere practitioner who has genuinely seen the impermanence, suffering, and selflessness of form and every other phenomenon, and who now sincerely wishes to actually dwell in the noble ones' own liberation, can still feel a real anxiety and apprehension wondering when — or whether — that attainment will actually come. He treats this second kind as worth naming precisely because it can otherwise masquerade as spiritual failure rather than what it actually is: a normal, even honorable, byproduct of genuine aspiration not yet fulfilled, distinct in kind from ordinary craving-based grief even though both are technically "sorrow."

On pain, he notes its four causes are entirely physical and circumstantial — self-inflicted or other-inflicted harm, the discomfort of holding one posture too long, injury from the elements, and other external affliction — and stresses the treatise's own honest admission that even a practitioner who has achieved genuine freedom from desire still experiences these; the first dhyāna releases sorrow specifically, not physical pain, which is exactly why it needs to be named as a still-remaining category rather than assumed already gone. Joy and pleasure, by contrast, are simply located precisely: joy belongs specifically to the second dhyāna, and pleasure specifically to the third — each one is what that particular level, and no other, actually delivers.

30. Why Pain's Seed Survives the First Dhyāna, Yet Never Manifests for a God Born There

The treatise resolves an apparent tension: pain is not yet severed even by the first dhyāna, since the coarse unfitness underlying it — dread, fatigue, elemental imbalance, and karmic result — remains untouched; yet a being actually born as a first-dhyāna god never experiences pain at all. The resolution turns on a careful distinction between a dormant, unmanifested seed and its actual expression, which requires specific supporting conditions a heavenly body simply lacks.

Chinese Source

何故苦根初靜慮中說未斷耶？答：彼品麤重猶未斷故。何緣生在初靜慮者苦根未斷而不現行？答：由其助伴相對憂根所攝諸苦彼已斷故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Why is it said that pain is not yet severed even in the first dhyāna? Answer: Because that category's own coarse unfitness has not yet been severed. Then for what reason does one actually born in the first dhyāna not have pain manifest, even though it is not severed? Answer: Because the suffering associated with sorrow's own accompanying factors has, for such a being, already been severed.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi separates two distinct situations the text is careful to distinguish: a desire-realm practitioner who has attained the first dhyāna through meditative effort still has an ordinary desire-realm body, still subject to holding a posture too long, still subject to the elements, still capable of self-harm or harm from others — so pain's seed remains fully capable of manifesting the moment absorption is left. A being actually reborn as a first-dhyāna god, by contrast, has an entirely different kind of body altogether, one that requires no solid food, needs no fixed posture maintained past comfort, and exists in a realm without hostility between its inhabitants — the specific supporting conditions pain's manifestation depends on (bodily fatigue, elemental imbalance, external harm) are simply structurally absent there, even though the underlying seed, in the strict technical sense, has still not been uprooted.

He draws out the four categories of "coarse unfitness" behind pain's four causes, each mapped precisely to a corresponding condition: dread-based unfitness underlies self- or other-inflicted harm; fatigue-based unfitness underlies prolonged posture; elemental-conflict unfitness underlies injury from fire, water, or wind; and karmic-result unfitness underlies whatever other circumstantial suffering the body's own past karma still carries. He treats this whole resolution as reinforcing the same point the previous section on worldly-versus-uncontaminated concentration already made: a seed's absence of manifestation is never the same as its actual removal — favorable circumstances alone, whether meditative absorption or a fortunate rebirth, can suppress a great deal without ever touching the root, which is precisely why the treatise insists on tracking seed and manifestation as two genuinely separate questions throughout.

31. Proving the First Dhyāna Has Not Severed Pain — and How Each Higher Level Releases One Feeling in Turn

Having said pain's seed is not yet severed even in the first dhyāna, the treatise now defends that claim against the objection that pain surely is gone once absorption is reached. It offers three linked arguments — a "no difference of feeling" fault, a contradiction of scripture, and a "no difference of severance" fault — and then generalizes the whole pattern: each ascending level releases exactly one feeling-faculty, in a fixed order.

Chinese Source

若初靜慮已斷苦根，是則行者入初靜慮及第二時，受所作住差別應無，由二俱有喜及樂故。而經中說：由出諸受，靜慮差別。又此應無尋伺寂靜麤重斷滅所作差別。如是餘根彼品麤重漸次斷故，上諸靜慮斷有差別。

Ru-Yi Translation

If the first dhyāna had already severed pain, then when a practitioner enters the first dhyāna and again the second, there would be no difference in the feelings they produce and abide in — for both alike possess joy and pleasure. Yet the scriptures teach that the dhyānas are distinguished precisely by which feelings they release. Furthermore, on that supposition there would also be no difference made by the stilling of applied-and-sustained thought and the severing of its coarse unfitness. In this same way, because each category's coarse unfitness is severed by stages, the higher dhyānas differ in what they sever.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi lays out the argument as a clean reductio. Both the first and second dhyānas carry joy and pleasure; if joy and pleasure were what set them apart, the two levels would be indistinguishable. What actually separates them is the feeling each has managed to leave behind — the first dhyāna releases sorrow, the second additionally releases pain, so a headache or an aching foot can no longer even arise there. To claim the first dhyāna already sheds pain is therefore to erase the very boundary between it and the second, which both scripture and lived experience insist is real.

He adds the second structural marker the treatise leans on: the first dhyāna still needs applied-and-sustained thought precisely because its coarse unfitness — its residual pull toward desire, ill-will, and harm — has not yet fully quieted, so it must keep actively investigating to hold desire at bay; that is why "applied-and-sustained thought" is named as one of its distinguishing qualities. The second dhyāna has stilled that thought and severed its coarseness, so it is instead marked by "inner clarity." Both markers, he stresses, are set up on the basis of what coarse unfitness has or has not been severed — which is only coherent if severance genuinely differs level by level. From this the whole ladder falls into place: the first dhyāna releases sorrow, the second pain, the third joy, the fourth pleasure, and the signless concentration equanimity itself — each higher attainment shedding one more layer of feeling's coarse residue than the one below it.

32. Equanimity's Release: The Signless Concentration Cuts the Seed, Not the Feeling Itself

The scheme now reaches its last and subtlest feeling, equanimity (neutral feeling), released not by any of the four dhyānas but by the signless mind-concentration (無相心定, animitta-samādhi). A crucial precision follows: this attainment permanently ends the latent affliction that rides on neutral feeling, but it does not — cannot — abolish the neutral feeling itself, because it is still a "with-mind" concentration in which feeling continues to occur.

Chinese Source

又無相者，經中說為無相心定。於此定中捨根永滅，但害隨眠，彼品麤重無餘斷故。非滅現纏，住無相定必有受故。於此定中容有三受，謂喜、樂、捨。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for the signless, the scriptures call it the signless mind-concentration. Within this concentration the equanimity-faculty is forever extinguished — yet only in the sense that its latent tendency is destroyed, because that category's coarse unfitness is severed without remainder. It is not that the present feeling is abolished, for one abiding in the signless concentration must still have feeling. Within this concentration three feelings may occur: joy, pleasure, and equanimity.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi is careful to keep the signless mind-concentration distinct from the cessation-of-perception-and-feeling attainment. The latter genuinely stops the first six consciousnesses' feeling and perception together with the self-grasping seventh consciousness; the signless concentration, by contrast, is an uncontaminated conscious absorption — the mind is still present and functioning, so feeling still arises within it, and specifically the neutral, neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling. What the concentration accomplishes is the total removal of the affliction-seeds that would otherwise cling to that neutral feeling and to the "this awareness is my self" grasping built on it. So "the equanimity-faculty is forever extinguished" means the latent affliction is gone, not that neutral experience has ceased.

He underlines the recurring principle: feeling itself is never the fault. Any karmic result at all is bound to carry feeling — the awakened and the unawakened alike register sensation. The difference is entirely in what follows: for the ordinary being, feeling touches off craving or aversion and so becomes fuel for further rebirth; for the noble one, the same feeling arises but sets nothing in motion, because the seed that would have reacted to it is gone. This, he notes, is exactly what the classical phrase "the sage does not receive any dharma" means — not that a sage has no sensation, but that sensation no longer binds.

33. Why This Counts as "Severance," and How Far Equanimity Reaches

The treatise closes the equanimity discussion by clarifying its own terminology — in what sense a feeling that still occurs can be called "severed" — and then fixes the range of the equanimity-faculty across the higher realms, up to the very summit of existence.

Chinese Source

非彼諸受得有隨眠，煩惱斷故，說以為斷，彼品麤重說名隨眠。又此捨根乃至何處？當知始從第四靜慮乃至有頂。

Ru-Yi Translation

It is not that these feelings themselves possess a latent tendency; rather, because the affliction is severed, it is called "severed" — the coarse unfitness of that category is what is termed the latent tendency. And how far does this equanimity-faculty extend? One should know that it runs from the fourth dhyāna all the way up to the peak of existence.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains the strict logic behind calling equanimity "released." A feeling is not itself a latent affliction; what qualifies as the latent tendency is the incapacitating coarse unfitness of the affliction that habitually accompanies that feeling. Once that accompanying affliction — chiefly the seed of ignorance and the "this thought is my self" grasping — is permanently severed so that it can never again couple with the feeling, we are entitled to say the equanimity-faculty has been released, even though neutral feeling itself persists. The name tracks the disappearance of the affliction, not the disappearance of the sensation.

On the range: neutral feeling begins to dominate at the fourth dhyāna and continues through all four formless attainments — the sphere of infinite space, of infinite consciousness, of nothingness, and of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, the last being the "peak of existence," the highest point of the three planes of becoming. He notes that reaching the release of even this most rarefied equanimity — dissolving the self-view still latent in the topmost formless attainment — is the accomplishment of an arhat alone; no lesser attainment reaches that far.

34. The Five Gateways of Favorable Release, and the Defilement Each One Answers

With all five feeling-faculties' release now accounted for, the treatise opens a new sub-topic: the five gateways of favorable release (五順出離界). These are the causes — the contemplative methods — by which the five feelings are released, and they interlock with the four dhyānas and the signless concentration. The passage then maps each release back onto the specific defilement it dissolves.

Chinese Source

復次，此五根出離，無相為後，與彼五種順出離界展轉相攝。此中由欲、恚、害出離，即說乃至樂根出離；由色出離，即說第四靜慮捨根出離；由薩迦耶滅，即說無色界一切捨根出離。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, this release of the five faculties — with the signless as the last — interlocks reciprocally with those five gateways of favorable release. Herein, from the release of desire, ill-will, and harm is said to follow the release running all the way up to the pleasure-faculty; from the release of form is said to follow the fourth dhyāna's release of the equanimity-faculty; and from the cessation of the view of a real self is said to follow the release of all equanimity in the formless realm.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads "gateway of favorable release" (順出離界) with 界 in its sense of cause: each of the five is a cause that conforms to and conduces toward release. He lines them up with the feeling-releases already established. Releasing the desire realm's three root defilements — desire, ill-will, and harm — is what carries a practitioner up through the dhyānas, releasing sorrow (first dhyāna), pain (second), joy (third), and finally pleasure (fourth); so these three defilements' antidotes together constitute the gateway for the first four feeling-releases. The method is the impurity contemplation against desire, loving-kindness against ill-will, and compassion against harm — and, as the next sections will insist, succeeding at even one of the three suffices to release all three.

The last two gateways handle equanimity, split by realm. Contemplating form as selfless, empty, and signless releases the fourth dhyāna's equanimity; contemplating the cessation of the view of a real self — seeing feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness alike as without self or anything belonging to a self — releases the equanimity that runs through the entire formless realm. He notes the summit of this whole structure is the fully accomplished arhat, who releases the pain, pleasure, sorrow, joy, and equanimity of all three realms at once, "receiving no dharma at all."

35. What "Favorable Release" Actually Means — and Why Three Separate Antidotes for One Simultaneous Severance

Two clarifying questions close this stretch. First, what does the phrase "favorable release" precisely mean, and for whom is it taught? Second — since attaining the first dhyāna severs desire, ill-will, and harm all at once — why does the treatise bother to establish three separate release-gateways rather than one?

Chinese Source

順出離言有何等義？由住此者能出離故，名順出離。不說由此出離於彼，為離欲者說此界故。

Ru-Yi Translation

What does the term "favorable release" mean? Because one who abides in these is able to be released, it is called favorable release. It is not being said that by means of these one is released from those; rather, this gateway is taught for the sake of one who seeks to be free of desire.

Chinese Source

問：諸欲、恚、害定同時斷，何緣建立別出離耶？答：彼諸出離雖復同時，約修對治有差別故，宣說三種出離差別。對治差別者，謂不淨、慈、悲，如其次第。或有唯修不淨出離一切，或慈、或悲。是故別說三種出離。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: Desire, ill-will, and harm are certainly severed at the same moment; on what grounds, then, is a separate release established for each? Answer: Although those releases are indeed simultaneous, because there is a difference in the antidote cultivated, three distinct releases are proclaimed. The difference in antidote is this: impurity, loving-kindness, and compassion, respectively. Some may cultivate the impurity contemplation alone and be released from all of them; or loving-kindness alone; or compassion alone. It is for this reason that three releases are stated separately.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi settles the phrase carefully. "Favorable release" does not mean that an ordinary or still-training practitioner uses these gateways to become released from the five feelings on the spot; rather, it names the causes — abiding and practicing within which one becomes capable of release. The teaching is aimed at anyone who wants to be free of craving, and specifically, he glosses, free of the craving rooted in the view of a real self — that is, to dissolve both the deliberately-reasoned and the innate grasping at "I." These gateways can be taken up while one is still an ordinary being; carried to completion, they yield arhatship. Maitreya is here showing the Buddha's disciples exactly how to "receive no dharma at all," how to release each of the five feeling-faculties.

On why three antidotes for one simultaneous severance: the three defilements do fall together the instant the first dhyāna is genuinely reached, so a single successful contemplation dissolves all three at once. The reason the treatise still names three is that practitioners differ in disposition — one person finds the impurity contemplation most natural and effective, another loving-kindness, another compassion. Because the methods of countering are genuinely different even though their result is the same, three separate gateways are set out, so that each temperament can find the entry that suits it. He stresses the practical payoff: you do not have to master all three; whichever one you can actually practice will, on its own, release all of desire, ill-will, and harm.

36. One Antidote for the Higher Equanimity, and How to Test Whether Desire Is Truly Gone

The release of equanimity across the form and formless realms has only a single antidote — the signless concentration dissolving the view of a real self — so, unlike the desire-realm defilements with their three distinct contemplations, these later releases do not subdivide. The treatise then opens its detailed account of releasing desire (廣欲出離): a deliberate diagnostic in which a "sharp-seeing" practitioner calls the objects of desire vividly to mind precisely in order to inspect whether any craving still stirs.

Chinese Source

此上唯有一類對治，故後出離無有差別。……云何猛利見者等隨念欲？謂由觀察作意於勝事作意故，猛利功用作意故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Above this, there is only one kind of antidote, so the later releases make no distinction. … What is it for a sharp-seeing one to call desires to mind, whether all together or one by one? It is by the attention of investigation directed at the excellent objects, and by an attention of fierce, forceful application.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi first notes why the higher releases do not split into varieties the way the desire-realm ones did: both the fourth dhyāna's equanimity and the whole formless realm's equanimity are answered by the one and the same signless concentration, which cuts the view of a real self and thereby releases them together — so there is nothing further to distinguish. In an aside he records that this treatise differs from some later meditation manuals on where the four immeasurables can be based: it holds they may be cultivated resting on any of the four dhyānas and even the first three formless attainments — loving-kindness most powerfully from the third dhyāna (whose bliss is the finest in all three realms, so wishing beings that bliss fits best), compassion from the sphere of infinite space (seeing all suffering as empty), sympathetic joy from the sphere of infinite consciousness, and equanimity from the sphere of nothingness.

He then frames the "sharp-seeing one." The term "desire" here covers both the inward affliction of craving and the outward five sense-objects it reaches for. A sage who has directly realized the truths and severed the view-path afflictions now, in order to press on and remove the cultivation-path afflictions too, deliberately raises the observation-attention — the fifth of the seven attentions — and conjures the most alluring desire-object he can, either the whole field of them or some single favorite, then watches his own mind to see whether affliction still arises. The purpose, Cháng Bǎi stresses, is guarding against spiritual overestimation: only by testing can one confirm whether desire is genuinely gone. He recalls the cautionary tale of a teacher who had proclaimed himself an arhat for sixty years and trained many disciples, until one doubting student, checking in meditation, saw his teacher had not in fact attained it; the student conjured an attacking elephant, the teacher fled in fear — and since an arhat feels no fear, the teacher understood, bowed to his own student as teacher, and only then practiced through to real arhatship. The lesson: keep testing yourself; the faintest trace of desire means the work is not done.

37. The Twelve Marks of a Mind That Has Released Desire (Part 1)

The treatise now enumerates, in the practitioner's own first-person diagnostic voice, twelve marks by which a mind genuinely free of desire recognizes itself when a tempting object is deliberately raised. The first six describe a mind that simply will not take the bait.

Chinese Source

云何於諸欲中心不趣入？謂於彼處不見勝功德故。云何不美？謂於彼處喜悅不生故。云何不住？謂於彼處不樂受用為欣悅故。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is it for the mind not to enter into desires? It is that one sees in them no superior merit. What is "finding no charm"? It is that no delight arises there. What is "not settling in"? It is that one takes no pleasure in their enjoyment, finding in it nothing to be glad about.

Chinese Source

云何無有勝解？謂於彼處不樂取著不如理相故。云何萎顇？謂雖縱任而不舒泰故。云何壞散？謂取境已，尋復棄捨故。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "having no fascination"? It is that one takes no pleasure in grasping their unwarranted marks. What is "withering"? It is that even when the mind is given free rein, it finds no ease. What is "letting fall"? It is that, having taken up the object, one at once discards it again.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads these six as a graded portrait of a mind that has lost all traction on desire. The mind "does not enter" because it no longer perceives any real excellence in sense-pleasure to be drawn toward. It "finds no charm" because the old spark of delight simply fails to catch even when the loveliest form or sweetest sound is presented. It "does not settle in" because it no longer imagines that indulging the object will leave it happy afterward. It has "no fascination" because it neither dwells on the object's supposedly pure, exquisite, pleasure-giving marks nor lets another person's enthusiasm pull it into grasping them.

The last two are the most experientially telling. "Withering" (萎顇) — Cháng Bǎi glosses it as a kind of dryness — is when the practitioner deliberately gives the mind free rein, even coaxes it to dwell on how beautiful the object is, and still no fondness or ease arises; he likens it to walking through a field of a hundred flowers without a single petal clinging to one's robe. "Letting fall" is the mind taking up the object as its focus and then, precisely because no fondness sustains it, dropping it again almost instantly — the exact opposite of the child who, wanting a toy, cannot stop thinking how lovely it is until his parents buy it. Here the object is raised and released in a moment, with no continued dwelling.

38. The Twelve Marks of a Mind That Has Released Desire (Part 2): Equanimity and Revulsion

The remaining six marks move from mere non-engagement to positive stability and active turning-away: even forcibly held on the object the mind takes no pleasure; it rests in a genuine equanimity; and it feels a settled revulsion grounded in seeing the object's three faults — impermanence, suffering, and liability to decay — traced through their initial, middle, and final aspects.

Chinese Source

云何而不舒泰？謂於所緣雖彊令住，而不愛樂故。云何等住於捨？謂行平等位，於平等位中心遊觀故。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "still finding no ease"? It is that, although one forcibly makes the mind dwell upon the object, it takes no fond pleasure in it. What is "resting evenly in equanimity"? It is that one proceeds in a state of evenness, the mind roving and observing within that even state.

Chinese Source

何等為厭？謂由於彼深見過患，棄背為性。此復三種：謂無常故、苦故、變壞法故。何等為惡？謂由於彼初見過患，棄背為性。何等為違？謂由於彼中見過患，棄背為性。何等為背？謂由於彼後見過患，棄背為性。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is revulsion? By seeing deeply the faults in them, its very nature is to abandon and turn away. This is again threefold: because they are impermanent, because they are suffering, because they are subject to decay. What is "aversion"? By seeing the fault in them at the outset, its nature is to abandon and turn away. What is "opposition"? By seeing the fault in the midst of them, its nature is to abandon and turn away. What is "turning the back"? By seeing the fault in them at the last, its nature is to abandon and turn away.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

On mark seven, Cháng Bǎi notes the sage will sometimes deliberately hold the mind on the desire-object a good while longer than necessary, precisely to make the test rigorous — and still not a flicker of craving or fondness follows. On mark eight, "resting evenly in equanimity," he draws out the two kinds of equanimity the mind may occupy: wholesome equanimity (善捨), where the strength of insight actively transforms any incipient defilement so the mind stays bright and aligned with the good — a fruit of strong contemplative power; and neutral equanimity (無記捨), where the strength of calm-abiding simply leaves the object no pull at all, so the mind never even stirs toward it — a fruit of strong stillness. Either way the mind proceeds "on level ground," neither craving nor recoiling, roving over the object and observing it in peace. He is careful to distinguish this cultivated evenness from the ordinary person's incidental neither-pleasant-nor-unpleasant blankness: the sage's version is guarded by right mindfulness and free of all affliction, and if the least craving did arise it would be countered on the spot.

On revulsion (厭) and its three sub-terms, Cháng Bǎi explains that "revulsion" is the general name for abandoning and turning away, while "aversion, opposition, turning-the-back" (惡・違・背) mark the same movement seen at three moments of contemplating the object — its beginning, middle, and end — corresponding to seeing its three faults in turn: that it is impermanent (never pausing even a moment, unstable, fraught with dread), that it is suffering (a great root of pain, a great abscess, a great barbed thorn, a great bondage), and that it is a thing bound to decay (fragile, easily broken, always diminishing). Because these faults hold of desire in the past, hold now, and will hold in the future alike, seeing them deeply is what finally lets one leave desire for good. He closes the twelve-mark set by noting its whole method: the sage, through observation-attention, deliberately raises his most cherished desire in the mind to check whether any trace of it can still arise.

39. The Mirror Image: Four Marks of a Mind Entering the Release from Desire

Having inspected the mind against desire, the practitioner now turns the observation around and inspects it toward release. Where the desire-object drew no engagement, no charm, no dwelling, and no fascination, the release from desire draws all four — but now wholesomely: the mind enters it seeing genuine merit, finds it charming, settles on it, and holds it with firm conviction.

Chinese Source

與此相違，即於離欲作意趣入者，謂於是處見勝功德故。美者，謂於是處生清淨信而證順故。住者，謂於所緣不流散故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Contrary to all the above, "the attention that enters into the release from desire" means that here one does see superior merit. "Charm" means that here a pure faith arises and one accords with its realization. "Settling" means that the mind does not scatter away from its object.

Chinese Source

勝解者，由於是處不染汙轉，於諸煩惱得離繫故。以於厭等棄背行中正流轉時，心無罣礙。又復於捨無有功用。

Ru-Yi Translation

"Firm conviction" means that here the mind proceeds undefiled and so becomes unbound from the afflictions — because when it flows on within the practice of revulsion and turning-away, the mind meets no obstruction; and moreover, it lets the object fall with no effort at all.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the four as the exact positive counterpart of the twelve marks just given. The mind "enters" release because it now perceives real excellence in leaving desire behind, so it is genuinely drawn toward it. Its "charm" is a pure confidence: knowing desire to be unclean and unworthy, the practitioner develops clean faith in release itself, practices toward it, and actually accords with and attains it. Its "settling" is the mind holding steady on the release-object — for instance the impurity-contemplation image — without drifting. Its "firm conviction" is that, dwelling here undefiled, it becomes unbound from craving: even while the observation-attention keeps raising desire-objects for inspection, right mindfulness of their faults keeps the mind unstained, so it meets no snag, and it drops each object effortlessly and spontaneously, without having to force itself.

He notes that "release from desire" itself has two dimensions, matching desire's own two sides: release from the inward affliction of craving is release-by-association (相應離), the mind-consciousness coming to be joined with wholesome factors free of craving; release from the outward sense-objects is release-by-domain (境界離), no longer being caught by the forms, sounds, and the rest that the storehouse-consciousness projects. A non-returner has fully released the desire realm's craving; only an arhat completes release across all three realms.

40. "The Mind Well Gone": The Vajra-Like Concentration and the Attentions That Lead to It

The treatise now marks the difference between the moment affliction is actually severed and the long preparation leading up to it. The severing moment is "the mind well gone" — abiding in the attention of the consummation of means (方便究竟作意), the vajra-like concentration of an aspirant's final instant before arhatship; the preparation is "well cultivating" the earlier attentions.

Chinese Source

云何其心善逝？謂住方便究竟作意故。云何善修？謂善修習餘作意故。當知此說斷位及斷方便道位。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "the mind well gone"? It is abiding in the attention of the consummation of means. What is "well cultivated"? It is thoroughly cultivating the remaining attentions. Know that this speaks of the position of actual severance and the position of the preparatory path to severance.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that "well gone" (善逝, Sugata) is properly one of the Buddha's ten epithets — "risen to the utmost, never to fall back" — here borrowed to praise the arhat's total release from desire. In the instant before arhatship the meditator abides in the "attention of the consummation of means," whose supporting concentration is likened to vajra: so hard and sharp that it cuts through even the most subtle afflictions without obstruction, which is why it is called the vajra-like concentration. Resting in it, one severs, without interval and forever, the coarse-unfitness seeds of every category of affliction, wins the knowledge of exhaustion (that the afflictions are finished) and the knowledge of non-arising (that, their cause spent, no future suffering-result can ever arise again), and so is truly "well gone" — the Buddha at his own awakening likewise entered this vajra concentration to sever all ignorance.

He then situates the earlier attentions as the runway to that moment. The seven attentions — knowing-the-marks, resolute-conviction, seclusion, delight-in-gathering, investigation, consummation-of-means, and fruit-of-consummation — are cultivated in order, each severing a coarser grade of affliction than the last (seclusion removes the lower realm's strong afflictions, delight-in-gathering the middling, and the consummation-of-means the last and finest). The middle five must be "well cultivated, well practiced, well and repeatedly practiced" before the consummation-of-means can arise at all. Hence the treatise's precise labeling: the consummation-of-means marks the actual "severance position," while knowing-the-marks through investigation mark the "preparatory-path position" — real work, but work that only clears the ground.

41. Three Words for Freedom: Loosed from Entanglements, Released from the Object, Unbound from the Seed

The treatise distinguishes three near-synonyms for liberation, each naming freedom from a different layer of affliction: from its active manifestation, from the object it fastens on, and from its dormant seed.

Chinese Source

解者，謂解脫諸纏故。脫者，謂解脫所緣相故。離繫者，謂解脫隨眠故。

Ru-Yi Translation

"Loosed" means freed from the active entanglements. "Released" means freed from the object-mark. "Unbound" means freed from the latent tendency.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi keeps the three precisely apart. "Entanglement" (纏) is a standard name for affliction in its active form — the manifest defilement that binds a being, drives evil deeds, and drags one into the lower destinies; to be "loosed" is to have that active manifestation no longer arise. "Released" is stated from the side of the object: even when the observation-attention raises an alluring desire, the mind-consciousness is no longer taken in by its supposedly pure and exquisite marks, and once out of absorption the five senses are no longer caught by the actual sense-objects either. "Unbound" is stated from the side of the seed: since all things arise from seeds, the sage who has cut off every cause and condition for affliction to arise will never again generate its manifestation — this is the deepest and final freedom, freedom from the latent tendency itself. He notes the three form a natural depth-ordering: manifestation, its object-hook, and its underlying seed.

42. Four Faults of Desire, and Being Freed, Transcending, and Unbound from Them

To explain what release from desire actually frees one from, the treatise names four faults that flow from desire — the further afflictions it breeds, the ruin it leads to, the burning it inflicts, and the grief it leaves — then maps release from all four onto the three-word scheme just given.

Chinese Source

從諸欲緣所生諸漏者，謂除欲貪，於欲界中所餘煩惱。損匱者，謂因此生執器仗等惡行差別，於此若作、若增長故，生諸惡趣。燒者，謂由此因，欲愛噉食，燒身心故。惱者，謂由此因，若事變壞，便生愁歎憂苦惱故。

Ru-Yi Translation

"The outflows born with desire as their condition" means, apart from craving for desire itself, all the remaining afflictions of the desire realm. "Ruinous loss" means that from this arise the various evil deeds of taking up weapons and the like; by committing and repeating them, one is born into the evil destinies. "Burning" means that through this cause, the craving of desire devours one, scorching body and mind. "Distress" means that through this cause, when the desired thing decays, there arise sorrow, lament, anxiety, pain, and affliction.

Chinese Source

於彼解脫、超出、離繫者，謂如前次第，解脫諸纏、所緣、隨眠故。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for being freed from, transcending, and unbound from them: this is, in the prior sequence, being loosed from the entanglements, released from the object, and unbound from the latent tendency.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi stresses that desire is never a single tidy fault — one craving spawns a whole family of afflictions. Setting aside craving proper, desire in the desire realm still breeds stinginess, arrogance, restlessness, and heedlessness, and through the mutual reinforcement of one factor kindling another, eventually the whole range of hatred, delusion, conceit, doubt, and the wrong views as well. Beyond these inward afflictions come the three outward faults: "ruinous loss," where desire drives one to take up weapons and do violence until the accumulated evil karma delivers a lower-realm rebirth stripped of anything worth having; "burning," where craving consumes its own host, body and mind alike ablaze — he recalls the Lotus Sūtra's "the three realms are not at peace, like a burning house," and the classical exhortation that when one's own head is on fire one does not dawdle; and "distress," where the moment a cherished desire-object changes or decays, sorrow and lament come flooding in. He notes fear, envy, and the endless wish to be met with everyone's warm smile are all just further faces of this same desire.

Against all four, he lines up the sage's threefold freedom exactly: "freed" from the active entanglements those faults consist of, "transcending" the alluring object-marks that hook them, and "unbound" from the seeds that would regenerate them — desire in every layer, active, object-facing, and latent, no longer able to move him.

43. Why the Sage Never "Receives" the Feelings Desire Conditions — the Red Lotus and the Water-Drop

The five gateways of favorable release close with their subtlest point: not only are desire's afflictions gone, but the sage — even one still living in the desire realm and still registering sensation — never takes up the defiled feelings that desire-objects would condition. The treatise seals this with one of the tradition's loveliest images.

Chinese Source

云何終不領納緣彼諸受？謂依將得、正得、隨念諸欲境界，染汙諸受不復現行；其所依身不為眾惑染汙而住，如紅蓮華，水滴不著。

Ru-Yi Translation

Why does the sage in the end never take up the feelings conditioned by those desires? Because, whether in relation to desire-objects about to be gained, presently being gained, or merely called to mind, the defiled feelings no longer manifest; the body that supports him abides unstained by the host of afflictions — like a red lotus, to which a water-drop does not cling.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi unpacks "the feelings conditioned by desire" into four kinds a person ordinarily takes up: sustaining feeling (the outer world of mountains, rivers, and earth that supports the body), equipment feeling (the daily necessities one uses), enjoyment feeling (the sensations of actually consuming food and pleasures), and lingering-fondness feeling (the way one keeps looking back on and pining for those sensations afterward, wanting them again and again). An arhat in the nirvāṇa-with-remainder still has a body and mind carried over as the result of past contaminated karma, but his mind is joined with nirvāṇa; all clinging to these four feelings is utterly gone, so he "never takes them up." Whether a desire is about to be reached (met with the craving that wants union, wants no separation, wants increase) or presently being enjoyed (met with the craving that wants it never to leave, wants it to last), those defiled feelings simply no longer arise in him.

He draws the crucial distinction that a sage is not a stone: sages have feeling too, and one living in the desire realm still uses its things. But the ordinary being's feeling is born of contact conjoined with ignorance — met without knowing the object is dreamlike and unreal, so craving fires; the sage's feeling is born of contact conjoined with clear knowing — met with sound attention that sees it as illusory, empty, and fraught with fault, so the feeling arises but stays quiet, binding nothing. This, Cháng Bǎi says, is the exquisite state the image captures: the red lotus on which a water-drop cannot rest but rolls straight off. With this the whole account of the five gateways of favorable release is complete — the method throughout being to catch affliction by its cause, sequence, mark, and activity, to firmly restrain the mind from letting it arise, and by sound attention and right mindfulness to extinguish it without remainder.

44. The Six Gateways of Favorable Release — and the Practitioner Still Pierced by the Poison-Arrow of Doubt

The treatise now opens a companion set drawn from scripture: six gateways of favorable release — loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, equanimity, the signless, and the abandonment of self-conceit. It opens, strikingly, with an objection: someone who claims to have cultivated all six yet is still "bewildered by the poison-arrow of doubt," and who therefore concludes that loving-kindness and the rest are not the true antidotes to ill-will, harm, and the like.

Chinese Source

復有六種順出離界，如經廣說。……謂我已修慈，乃至我已離諸我慢，然我猶為疑惑毒箭悶亂其心，是故慈等於恚、害等非正對治。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are furthermore six gateways of favorable release, as the scriptures explain at length. … [Someone objects:] "I have cultivated loving-kindness, and so on up to: I have abandoned all conceits of self; yet the poison-arrow of doubt still bewilders my mind — therefore loving-kindness and the rest are not the true antidote for ill-will, harm, and the like."

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi names the six and pairs each with what it counters: loving-kindness answers ill-will; compassion answers the wish to harm; sympathetic joy answers envy and the inability to rejoice at others' good; equanimity answers craving; the signless concentration answers the mind's habit of grasping at marks; and abandoning self-conceit answers the lingering doubt about one's own liberation and what has been realized. Cultivated to completion, these six are certain to release ill-will, harm, and the whole family of afflictions — which is exactly why they are set up as six gateways of favorable release.

He reads the opening objection as a portrait of someone who has practiced wrongly: lacking enough wisdom of learning, reflection, and cultivation, this person imitates others in cultivating the four immeasurables, the signless concentration, and the concentration on abandoning self-conceit, yet the poison-arrow of doubt still festers in the mind, muddling it. Has such a person actually realized anything? Plainly not — he has not seen non-self and non-possession-of-self, and so he slides into slander, protesting that one can cultivate all four immeasurables and still feel anger arise, and concluding that loving-kindness and the rest therefore cannot really be antidotes at all. The treatise raises this precisely in order to answer it in what follows — the fault lies in the incomplete practice, not in the gateways.

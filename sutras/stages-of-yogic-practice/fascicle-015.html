Fascicle 15 · 瑜伽師地論卷第十五 · Inner Wisdom's Catalogue Closes, and Three More Sciences Open

From Ten Things to the Sciences of Medicine and Logic

Fascicle 15 picks up exactly where fascicle 14 left off, closes inner wisdom's vast numeric catalogue at "ten things," and crosses into the remaining sciences a Buddhist practitioner studies — beginning with medicine and the classical Indian science of logic — before, its own frontmatter promises, finally closing the Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing and opening the Ground of What Is Attained Through Reflection.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 15 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. The Eighth Numeric Category Opens: The Eightfold Path as Three Trainings, Eight Kinds of Noble Person, and Seven Flawed Ways to Give Against One Flawless Way

Fascicle 15 continues directly from fascicle 14's exact stopping point: having finished "seven things," the treatise turns to "eight things" — nine planned items. The first maps the noble eightfold path onto the three higher trainings; the second sorts practitioners into eight types by which of the four fruits' path or result they occupy; and the third takes up giving itself, contrasting seven subtly flawed motivations against the one genuinely flawless way to give.

Chinese Source

已說七種佛教所應知處。次說八種。謂有八支聖道所攝，令諸苾芻究竟斷結三種修法。謂修戒、修定、修慧。正語、正業、正命，名為修戒；正念、正定，名為修定；正見、正思惟、正精進，名為修慧。又由正方便及果增上力故，建立清淨品八種補特伽羅。謂行四向、住四果者。又有二種施，八相差別。一、有過失施，二、無過失施。前七種施，名有過失；最後一種，名無過失。謂有布施懈怠所損，故有過失。或有布施不隨所欲，故有過失。謂有染心者，怖畏貧窮、希求富樂而行布施。或有布施顧戀過去，故有過失。或有布施希望未來，故有過失。或有布施有輕慢過，故有過失。或有布施希求富樂，故有過失。或有布施求他知聞，故有過失。無過失施者，謂迴向涅槃故，為彼資糧故，無染汙心為往善趣故，為得大財故，而行布施。

Ru-Yi Translation

The seven-thing category of what a Buddhist should know has now been explained. Next, the eight-thing category. There are three trainings, comprised within the eight-limbed noble path, that let monks ultimately sever the fetters: the training of precepts, the training of concentration, and the training of wisdom. Right speech, right action, and right livelihood are called the training of precepts; right mindfulness and right concentration are called the training of concentration; right view, right intention, and right diligence are called the training of wisdom. By the power of correct method and its fruit, eight kinds of pure person are established: those practicing toward each of the four fruits, and those abiding in each of the four fruits. There are also two kinds of giving, with eight distinguishing marks: flawed giving, and flawless giving. The first seven kinds are flawed; the last alone is flawless. There is giving harmed by laziness, and so flawed. There is giving that does not follow what one truly wishes, and so flawed — namely, one with a defiled mind gives out of fear of poverty or desire for wealth and pleasure. There is giving that dwells on the past with regret, and so flawed. There is giving that hopes for future return, and so flawed. There is giving tainted by contempt, and so flawed. There is giving that seeks wealth and pleasure, and so flawed. There is giving that seeks to be known and heard of by others, and so flawed. Flawless giving is giving dedicated to nirvāṇa, giving as a provision toward it, giving with an undefiled mind for the sake of a favorable rebirth, and giving in order to obtain great wealth [for others' benefit].

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi confirms the eightfold path's now-familiar mapping onto the three higher trainings (precepts: right speech, action, livelihood; concentration: right mindfulness, right concentration; wisdom: right view, intention, diligence) — the same structure already met from a different angle at fascicle 14's item 3, restated here as the direct cause of a trainee's final severance of the five upper fetters and attainment of arhatship. The eight kinds of person he reads simply as the four fruits doubled: the path leading toward each fruit, and the fruit itself once attained — eight stages in all, from the very first steps toward stream-entry up through full arhatship.

He walks through the seven flawed forms of giving as a genuinely practical checklist of what corrupts an otherwise generous act: laziness (slow or half-hearted response to a genuine need); giving that fights one's own reluctance the whole way through, whether from fear of running short or from attachment to a prized possession; giving followed by regret, sometimes merely habitual giving inherited from family custom rather than genuine intention; giving that secretly expects future repayment; giving delivered with contempt, tossed at the recipient rather than offered with respect; giving motivated by hope of even greater wealth in return; and giving performed to be seen and praised. Flawless giving, by contrast, is giving genuinely dedicated to liberation itself, giving as a deliberate provision toward that same liberation, and — specifically for a bodhisattva — giving toward one's own future wealth and favorable rebirth not for personal enjoyment but precisely so as to have the resources to relieve others' material want before then teaching them the Dharma, since a mind consumed by basic need has no room left to actually hear a teaching. Cháng Bǎi treats the "three wheels empty" principle — no real giver, gift, or recipient ultimately found — as what genuinely purifies giving of all seven flaws at once, whether practiced by a śrāvaka seeking personal liberation or a bodhisattva seeking to benefit others.

2. Eight Moments a Lazy Mind Always Finds an Excuse, and the Same Eight Moments a Diligent Mind Finds a Reason

The fourth item names four circumstances — almsround, undertaking a task, travel, and illness — each yielding two critical moments, eight in all, where a lazy disposition reliably manufactures an excuse to rest rather than practice. The same eight moments, faced by a diligent disposition, produce the opposite conclusion every time.

Chinese Source

又依四處，於八時中，趣入懈怠，不發精進。當知如是補特伽羅是懈怠類，非精進類。謂依乞食處，依所作處，依遊行處，依界不平等處。依此四處，八時差別。多食精美，身沈重時；少食麤惡，身劣頓時；將欲所作，護惜力時；已有所作，身疲倦時；將欲遊行，護惜力時；已涉長途，身疲倦時；正為病苦所纏擾時；所病已愈，恐更發時。此懈怠類補特伽羅，乃至未遇懈怠所依，少似精進；若得遇已，速發懈怠；是故名為懈怠種類。與此相違，亦依四處，於八時中發勤精進。當知如是補特伽羅能伏懈怠，勤精進類。

Ru-Yi Translation

Depending on four circumstances, in eight moments, one turns toward laziness and fails to arouse diligence: know that such a person belongs to the class of the lazy, not the diligent. Namely, depending on almsround, on undertaking a task, on travel, and on bodily imbalance. Depending on these four, there are eight distinct moments: when the body feels heavy from eating too much rich food; when the body feels weak and sluggish from eating too little or too coarsely; when about to undertake a task, sparing one's strength; when a task is finished, the body fatigued; when about to travel, sparing one's strength; when a long journey is finished, the body fatigued; when actually afflicted by illness; and when illness has just passed, fearing its return. Such a lazy person, until meeting one of these occasions for laziness, appears somewhat diligent; but once the occasion arises, laziness swiftly follows — hence called the class of the lazy. The reverse of this — also depending on the same four circumstances, in the same eight moments, arousing diligence — know that such a person, capable of subduing laziness, belongs to the class of the diligent.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes this passage draws on the Dīrghāgama's own classic account of the eight grounds for laziness and their eight mirror-image grounds for diligence — the same eight physical circumstances (a satisfying or unsatisfying almsround, a task about to be done or just completed, travel about to begin or just finished, illness present or just recovered from) producing exactly opposite conclusions depending on the practitioner's underlying disposition. A lazy mind reasons "my body is heavy/weak/tired/fragile, so I should rest instead of continuing toward what I have not yet attained"; a diligent mind, facing the identical physical fact, reasons instead "this is exactly when my ordinary attention to the teaching is most at risk of lapsing, so I should press on now rather than later." Cháng Bǎi draws out the deeper point: physical circumstance never actually determines the outcome — the same tired body serves either as a lazy person's excuse or a diligent person's spur, depending entirely on which habit of mind meets it. He adds two further variables that shape how easily one resists the lazy reading: sheer mental resolve (his example: the ailing monk-scholar Yìn Shùn, who produced major scholarly works even from a sickbed, showing willpower can outrun physical weakness) and simple familiarity with practice (a well-rehearsed meditation routine withstands distraction far better than an unfamiliar one) — both, in turn, themselves strengthened by nothing other than sustained diligence, making steady practice its own best defense against future laziness.

3. Eight Desirable Places to Be Reborn, for Those Not Yet Seeking to Leave the Desire Realm Entirely

The fifth item addresses ordinary aspiration honestly: for beings not yet seeking full liberation but still wishing for a better rebirth within the desire realm, the treatise names eight specific destinations — two grades of human birth and the six desire-realm heavens — each reachable through the same two causes, giving and precepts, applied in greater or lesser measure.

Chinese Source

又有八種正願所攝可愛生因，能令於諸欲中樂增上生，不求永離一切欲者，當生八種可愛生處。謂願人中卑惡種類，修小施戒二福業事；如是願樂人中尊貴種類、四大王天、三十三天、夜摩天、覩史多天、化樂天、他化自在天，修小施戒二福業事。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also eight causes of a desirable rebirth comprised within a correct aspiration, capable of leading one who delights in the desire realm and does not yet seek to leave all desire entirely to a favorable rebirth there, to be born in eight desirable places: wishing for birth among the lowly castes of humanity, one cultivates giving and precepts in modest measure; and likewise wishing for birth among the noble castes of humanity, or in the Heaven of the Four Great Kings, the Heaven of the Thirty-Three, the Yāma Heaven, the Tuṣita Heaven, the Heaven of Delighting in Transformation, or the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations, one cultivates giving and precepts accordingly.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this item as an honest acknowledgment that not every practitioner is yet ready to seek full renunciation, and the treatise meets them where they are: two grades of human rebirth (a modest, even impoverished family reached through minimal giving and only partial precept-keeping, versus a wealthy, noble family reached through fuller giving and complete precepts) and the six desire-realm heavens already surveyed cosmologically in fascicle 4 (the Four Great Kings, the Heaven of the Thirty-Three, and the four "space-dwelling" heavens rising progressively higher — Yāma, Tuṣita, the Heaven of Delighting in Transformation, and the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations) — each successively higher destination simply requiring correspondingly more generous giving and more complete precept-keeping to reach. He notes the same two causes, giving and precepts, run through the entire scale from the humblest human birth to the very highest desire-realm heaven; only the degree to which they are cultivated changes, not their basic kind — a reminder that even relatively modest, incomplete practice is never wasted, only calibrated to a correspondingly modest result.

4. Eight Assemblies Among Humans and Gods, and the Eight Worldly Winds No Being Escapes

Two more items: the eight distinct assemblies the Buddha addressed — four among humans, sorted by what makes each group worthy of note, and four among gods, sorted by cosmic boundary — and the eight worldly winds (gain and loss, private censure and praise, public blame and acclaim, pain and pleasure) that touch every being's life in one of exactly three recurring situations.

Chinese Source

又四因緣故，於人趣中建立如來四眾；三因緣故，於天趣中建立四眾。最增上故，世間共許為福田故，受用資財不由他故，棄捨一切世資財故；由此四緣，於人趣中建立四眾。依地邊際故，欲界邊際故，語行邊際故；由此三緣，於天趣中建立四眾。又於世間三處轉時，恆常世間八法所觸。謂樂欲處、功用處、眾緣處。於樂欲處轉時，或觸於利，或觸非利。於功用處轉時，或稱他意，或不稱意；於背面位觸於毀、譽，於現前位觸於稱、譏。於眾緣處轉時，或由先世、或由現法苦樂眾緣，觸於苦、樂。

Ru-Yi Translation

By four causal conditions, the Tathāgata's four assemblies are established among humans; by three causal conditions, four assemblies are established among gods. Being of the highest standing; being universally acknowledged as a field of merit; enjoying one's own resources without depending on others; and having renounced all worldly resources entirely — by these four conditions, four assemblies are established among humans. By the boundary of the earth; by the boundary of the desire realm; and by the boundary of verbal activity — by these three conditions, four assemblies are established among gods. Further, in three situations that recur throughout the world, one is constantly touched by the eight worldly phenomena: the situation of desire and aspiration, the situation of effortful action, and the situation of manifold conditions. In the situation of desire, one is touched either by gain or by its absence. In the situation of effortful action, one either meets others' approval or does not; behind one's back, one is touched by censure or praise; face to face, one is touched by acclaim or blame. In the situation of manifold conditions, whether from past lives or from present causes and conditions of pain and pleasure, one is touched by pain or by pleasure.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the four human assemblies as the kṣatriya (rulers, of highest worldly standing), brāhmaṇa (priests and scholars, universally regarded as worthy recipients of offerings), householders of great wealth (self-sufficient, dependent on no one), and renunciants (having given up worldly resources entirely) — the Buddha's teaching, in his own lifetime, reaching all four social classes alike. The four divine assemblies he sorts by cosmic boundary rather than social role: the Four Great Kings and the Thirty-Three at the earth-adjacent summits of Mount Sumeru and its surrounding peaks (the "boundary of the earth"), the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations at the very top of the desire realm (its "boundary"), and the first dhyāna's Brahmā-heavens, the highest point at which beings still communicate through language before all verbal activity ceases at the second dhyāna and above (the "boundary of verbal activity"). Together, the eight assemblies span everyone the Buddha's teaching was ever recorded reaching, human and divine alike.

He reads the eight worldly winds as touching every being through just three basic situations: whenever desire seeks something, either gain or its absence follows; whenever effort is made, either approval (praised behind one's back, acclaimed to one's face) or disapproval (censured behind one's back, blamed to one's face) follows; and whenever the manifold conditions of past or present karma converge, either pleasure or pain follows. He illustrates the point with an original account of the classical exchange between the poet Su Dongpo and Chan master Foyin: Su, believing himself to have written a verse proving he sat "unmoved by the eight winds," sent it across the river for praise, only to have Foyin write back two blunt words of mockery — and when Su, furious, rushed across the river to confront him, Foyin's door already bore the reply that one dismissive word had been enough to prove the eight winds still moved him completely. A later exchange, in which Su boasted of seeing his meditation partner as a heap of dung while being told he himself resembled a buddha, drew the same lesson from his own sister: what a mind sees in others reveals what that same mind actually is within — genuine freedom from the eight winds is proven only by how a mind actually responds when tested, never by how eloquently it claims to have already transcended them.

5. The Eight Liberations Revisited as Eight Resolves, Specifically Drawing Out Superknowledge and the Cessation Attainment

The eighth item returns to the eight liberations already surveyed at length in fascicle 12, this time framed specifically as "resolves" (勝解) — firm, unwavering meditative convictions — and organized around what they specifically accomplish: the first three draw out a noble one's superknowledges (never available to an ordinary being, however accomplished their concentration), and the remaining five, applied in careful sequence, draw out the supreme abiding of the cessation attainment itself.

Chinese Source

又八勝解，能引不還、或阿羅漢諸聖神通及最勝住。謂未伏內色想，外無染汙色勝解，是名第一；已伏內色想，是名第二；淨不淨非二色第一捨勝解，是名第三。此三解脫，於一切色得自在故，便能引發諸聖神通。謂諸神通不與一切異生共有。空無邊勝解、識無邊勝解、無所有勝解、非想非非想勝解、微微任運心勝解，此五勝解次第善修治故，能引想受滅等至最勝住。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also eight resolves, capable of drawing out the noble superknowledges of a non-returner or arhat, and the supreme abiding. The resolve of not yet having subdued the perception of inner form, together with the resolve of undefiled outer form, is the first. Having subdued the perception of inner form is the second. The resolve of the foremost equanimity toward form neither pure nor impure is the third. These three liberations, by attaining mastery over all form, are thereby able to draw out the noble superknowledges — for these superknowledges are not shared with any ordinary being. The resolve of infinite space, the resolve of infinite consciousness, the resolve of nothingness, the resolve of neither perception nor non-perception, and the resolve of a subtle, spontaneously-functioning mind — these five resolves, well cultivated in this sequence, are able to draw out the supreme abiding of the attainment of the cessation of perception and feeling.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi confirms these eight resolves are simply the eight liberations already translated in full at fascicle 12 — the same progression from the impurity contemplation, through its refinement into pure light, to the four formless attainments and finally the cessation attainment itself — now regrouped by function rather than by sequence alone. The first three (still working with form, whether inner, outer, or the pure light both eventually resolve into) are singled out because mastering form in all three of these ways is precisely what unlocks the noble superknowledges — powers the treatise insists are categorically unavailable to any ordinary being, however deep their concentration, since these specific powers depend on the uncontaminated insight only a non-returner or arhat possesses, not merely on meditative skill. The remaining five (the four formless attainments plus a final, exquisitely subtle "spontaneously functioning mind" resolve marking the threshold just before all mental activity ceases entirely) must be cultivated in strict order, each resolving one further layer of dependency, before the cessation attainment — the treatise's "supreme abiding" — can actually be reached. Cháng Bǎi treats this item as adding one genuinely new distinction to what fascicle 12 already covered: not just what the eight liberations are, but which three specifically unlock supernormal power and which five specifically culminate in the deepest meditative attainment any being can reach.

6. The Eight Bases of Mastery, and What "Mastery" Actually Means — the "Eight Things" Category Closes

The ninth and final item of "eight things" returns to the eight bases of mastery already surveyed in fascicle 12, built on the same three liberations just covered — and adds a genuinely new contribution: a precise, five-part definition of what "mastery" itself actually consists of, together with the observation that this same purifying observation can be practiced even outside formal meditation, in the midst of ordinary daily life. With this item, the "eight things" category — nine items in all — comes to its close.

Chinese Source

又若觀諸色，若如所觀，於初三解脫中而修習者，謂三解脫方便道所攝三勝處也。此中觀外諸色，若小若大、若好若惡、若劣若勝者，謂觀非三摩地所行現所得色。由緣三摩地所行作意不種種現前，故名為勝。於三摩地所行中，奢摩他行名知，毗鉢舍那行名見。如於三摩地所行若知若見，如於彼色已尋思、已了別，如是於外所想非三摩地所行中觀諸色亦爾。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, whether observing forms [in limited or extensive number], or observing them [as bright colors], cultivated within the first three liberations — these are the three bases of mastery comprised within the three liberations as their preparatory path. Herein, observing outer forms — whether small or great, whether pleasant or repulsive, whether inferior or superior — refers to observing form actually obtained [in ordinary experience], not that which operates within meditative absorption. By taking as object what operates within meditative absorption, attention no longer manifests in manifold, uncontrolled ways — hence it is called mastery. Within what operates in meditative absorption, the activity of calm is called knowing, and the activity of insight is called seeing. Just as, with respect to what operates within meditative absorption, there is such knowing and seeing, such investigation and discernment of that form, so too is it likewise with observing forms in what is merely imagined outwardly, not operating within meditative absorption.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi confirms the eight bases of mastery are the same eight already translated in fascicle 12, here regrouped into three: the first two (observing form, in fewer or greater number, still within one's own or another's body) and the second two (the same observation once inner form has been let go, observed only in others) both build on the first two liberations, while the final four (observing pure blue, yellow, red, and white light) build on the third liberation's purified light. What this section adds beyond fascicle 12's account is a precise five-part definition of "mastery" itself: mastery means the mind, not the object, now sets the terms (形奪卑下, the mind's own form dominating whatever inferior appearance the object presents); the mind's power subdues whatever weaker pull the object exerts (制伏羸劣); the mind can conceal the object at will, making it appear or vanish on command (能隱蔽他); the mind remains free of both craving and aversion no matter how vivid or repulsive the object becomes (厭壞所緣); and the mind can freely convert one into many or many into one, purity into impurity or impurity into purity (自在迴轉) — five distinct senses in which the practitioner, rather than the object, is now unmistakably in control.

He draws out one further point worth real attention: this same purifying observation of "small or great, pleasant or repulsive, inferior or superior" objects is not confined to formal sitting meditation at all — it applies equally to ordinary objects encountered in daily life, before any meditative absorption is even entered. Walking past a bakery, hearing a beautiful or discordant sound, meeting an attractive or unattractive stranger — all of it can become the same practice of observing without craving or aversion, calm's steady attention paired with insight's discerning investigation, exactly as within formal sitting. Cháng Bǎi treats this as the whole point the treatise wants understood: mastery achieved in meditation is meant to carry directly into ordinary experience, not remain sealed off within it — with this closing insight, "eight things," inner wisdom's eighth numeric category, comes to its end.

7. The Ninth Numeric Category — Nine Fetters, and the Nine Abodes Encompassing Every Place Any Being Actually Lives

"Nine things" is the briefest category yet — just two items. The first simply names the nine fetters by cross-reference to an earlier fascicle. The second completes a count already building since fascicle 13: the seven stations of consciousness, expanded by two further special states, to produce nine abodes that between them account for literally every place any being in the three realms can be found.

Chinese Source

已說八種佛教所應知處。次說九種。謂有九結。如攝事分當廣建立。又有九種生處受生有情，於彼彼處同所居止。謂三界中，除諸惡趣，可厭處故。如前已說。

Ru-Yi Translation

The eight-thing category of what a Buddhist should know has now been explained. Next, the nine-thing category. There are nine fetters, to be broadly established in the Compendium of Determinations. There are also nine kinds of birthplace in which sentient beings dwell together, each in their own place: within the three realms, excluding only the evil destinies, since these are utterly repugnant places — as already explained.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the nine fetters (craving, hostility, conceit, ignorance, view, [wrong-rule] attachment, doubt, envy, and stinginess) simply cross-reference an already-familiar list rather than being newly explained here, and were already named in an earlier fascicle's account of what binds a mind to suffering.

He reads the nine abodes as completing the count already begun with the seven stations of consciousness explained in full at fascicle 14's §56: the same seven, expanded by two further special states that don't quite count as consciousness "settling in gratified" the way the seven proper do — beings without perception (whose six active consciousnesses are simply absent) and the peak of existence (whose mental activity is too subtle to classify as clearly present or absent) — bringing the total to nine. Only the evil destinies are excluded from the count entirely, since no being is ever described as gratified there, however long they may in fact reside. Between them, the nine abodes map every single location in the entire three-realm cosmos where any being's existence actually unfolds — the treatise's most complete geographical inventory of saṃsāra itself.

8. A Major Milestone: The Ten Totalities and the Ten Factors of an Arhat Close "Ten Things" — and With It, the Entire Science of Inner Wisdom

The tenth and final numeric category takes up just two items: the ten totalities, completing the meditative sequence of liberations, masteries, and totalities first surveyed in fascicles 11–12; and the ten factors comprising an arhat's being, the eightfold path completed by two further factors unique to one beyond training. With this category's close, inner wisdom (內明處) — the first of the five sciences, spanning all four of its aspects since section 43 of fascicle 13 — is declared complete in its entirety.

Chinese Source

已說九種佛教所應知處。次說十種。謂十遍處。當知即諸解脫所作成就。餘解脫、勝處、遍處，如攝事分當廣分別。又有十無學支，當知無學五蘊所攝。謂戒蘊、定蘊、慧蘊、解脫蘊、解脫知見蘊。如是已說十種佛教所應知處，及前所說佛教所應知處等，當知皆是內明處攝。

Ru-Yi Translation

The nine-thing category of what a Buddhist should know has now been explained. Next, the ten-thing category. There are the ten totalities: know that these are precisely what the liberations accomplish. The remaining details of the liberations, bases of mastery, and totalities will be broadly differentiated in the Compendium of Determinations. There are also ten factors of one beyond training, comprised, know, within the five aggregates of one beyond training: the aggregate of precepts, the aggregate of concentration, the aggregate of wisdom, the aggregate of liberation, and the aggregate of the knowledge and vision of liberation. Thus the ten-thing category of what a Buddhist should know has now been explained, and, together with everything said before it, know that all of it is comprehended within inner wisdom.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi confirms the ten totalities (earth, water, fire, wind, blue, yellow, red, white, space, and consciousness, each contemplated as pervading without limit or gap) as the final fruit of the same sequence already translated in fascicles 11–12: liberation first makes an object available to resolve, mastery then lets the mind control it at will, and totality finally extends that same mastered object without any boundary at all — three stages of the identical meditative material, deepening at each step. He notes the treatise itself defers further technical detail to the Compendium of Determinations, treating this brief mention here as sufficient closure rather than fresh exposition.

He reads the ten factors of one beyond training as the familiar eightfold path completed by two further factors unique to full arhatship: the eight path-factors sort into precepts (right speech, action, livelihood), concentration (right mindfulness, right concentration), and wisdom (right view, intention, diligence) — the same three-training structure met repeatedly throughout this numeric catalogue — while the ninth and tenth factors, right liberation and right knowledge, belong to no trainee, only to one who has finished training entirely. Right liberation is the settled confidence of having actually broken free of affliction; right knowledge is the arhat's own certainty — already met at fascicle 13's account of an arhat's two-part self-knowledge — that this freedom is both real and permanent. Cháng Bǎi notes that a buddha's own five aggregates rank highest among these, a pratyekabuddha's in the middle, and a śrāvaka's lowest, not because the aggregates themselves differ in kind but because the depth of realization behind them differs.

With the ten totalities and the ten factors of one beyond training named, the treatise's own closing line makes the milestone explicit: everything covered since section 43 of fascicle 13 — the threefold textual division, the four-verse concept catalogue running from "word" through "the sacred teaching," the ten principles comprehending the teaching's meaning, and this entire numeric catalogue climbing from one thing through ten — is comprehended within inner wisdom (內明處), the first of the five sciences named at the Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing's very outset. Inner wisdom itself, spanning roughly three full fascicles of source material, is now complete.

9. The Second Science Opens: Medicine, Mapped Directly Onto the Four Noble Truths

With inner wisdom complete, the treatise turns to the second of the five sciences named back at section 42: medicine. True to form, it is treated briefly — just four kinds of skill, mapped with striking directness onto the same four noble truths that have structured this entire fascicle's numeric catalogue, before the treatise defers all further clinical detail to scripture at large.

Chinese Source

云何醫方明處？當知此明略有四種。謂於病相善巧，於病因善巧，於已生病斷滅善巧，於已斷病後更不生方便善巧。如是善巧廣分別義，如經應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the science of medicine? Know that, briefly, this science has four aspects: skill regarding the marks of illness, skill regarding the cause of illness, skill in eliminating an illness that has already arisen, and skill in the means preventing an eliminated illness from arising again. The broad differentiation of this skill's meaning should be known as found in scripture.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps all four aspects onto the four noble truths without hesitation: recognizing an illness's symptoms is the truth of suffering; understanding what actually caused it is the truth of origination; eliminating an illness already present is the truth of cessation; and the ongoing means that keep it from recurring is the truth of the path — the same structure, he notes, citing the *Xiǎnyáng Shèngjiào Lùn*, that organizes every one of the treatise's four truths elsewhere. He reads this brief treatment as entirely deliberate: medicine here is offered as a worldly convenience, useful for sustaining the very body practice depends on, but the deeper point sits just beneath it — the body itself is inescapably subject to illness only because it is subject to birth at all, and birth traces back to ignorance; treating symptoms and even their proximate causes, however genuinely valuable, never reaches that root the way the Dharma's own four truths do. He adds a brief historical note that Indian medical learning — the physician Jīvaka renowned in the Buddha's own lifetime, later authors such as Nāgārjuna associated with medical treatises still preserved in the Tibetan canon, and the classical eightfold division of medical specialties (general ailments, ophthalmology, wound care, pediatrics, toxicology, and the rest) — traveled into China alongside Buddhism itself, leaving traces still visible in classical Chinese medical texts centuries later. With this brief treatment, the science of medicine is complete, and the treatise turns next to logic.

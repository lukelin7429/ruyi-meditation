Fascicle 15 · 瑜伽師地論卷第十五 · Inner Wisdom's Catalogue Closes, and Three More Sciences Open

From Ten Things to the Sciences of Medicine and Logic

Fascicle 15 picks up exactly where fascicle 14 left off, closes inner wisdom's vast numeric catalogue at "ten things," and crosses into the remaining sciences a Buddhist practitioner studies — beginning with medicine and the classical Indian science of logic — before, its own frontmatter promises, finally closing the Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing and opening the Ground of What Is Attained Through Reflection.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 15 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. The Eighth Numeric Category Opens: The Eightfold Path as Three Trainings, Eight Kinds of Noble Person, and Seven Flawed Ways to Give Against One Flawless Way

Fascicle 15 continues directly from fascicle 14's exact stopping point: having finished "seven things," the treatise turns to "eight things" — nine planned items. The first maps the noble eightfold path onto the three higher trainings; the second sorts practitioners into eight types by which of the four fruits' path or result they occupy; and the third takes up giving itself, contrasting seven subtly flawed motivations against the one genuinely flawless way to give.

Chinese Source

已說七種佛教所應知處。次說八種。謂有八支聖道所攝，令諸苾芻究竟斷結三種修法。謂修戒、修定、修慧。正語、正業、正命，名為修戒；正念、正定，名為修定；正見、正思惟、正精進，名為修慧。又由正方便及果增上力故，建立清淨品八種補特伽羅。謂行四向、住四果者。又有二種施，八相差別。一、有過失施，二、無過失施。前七種施，名有過失；最後一種，名無過失。謂有布施懈怠所損，故有過失。或有布施不隨所欲，故有過失。謂有染心者，怖畏貧窮、希求富樂而行布施。或有布施顧戀過去，故有過失。或有布施希望未來，故有過失。或有布施有輕慢過，故有過失。或有布施希求富樂，故有過失。或有布施求他知聞，故有過失。無過失施者，謂迴向涅槃故，為彼資糧故，無染汙心為往善趣故，為得大財故，而行布施。

Ru-Yi Translation

The seven-thing category of what a Buddhist should know has now been explained. Next, the eight-thing category. There are three trainings, comprised within the eight-limbed noble path, that let monks ultimately sever the fetters: the training of precepts, the training of concentration, and the training of wisdom. Right speech, right action, and right livelihood are called the training of precepts; right mindfulness and right concentration are called the training of concentration; right view, right intention, and right diligence are called the training of wisdom. By the power of correct method and its fruit, eight kinds of pure person are established: those practicing toward each of the four fruits, and those abiding in each of the four fruits. There are also two kinds of giving, with eight distinguishing marks: flawed giving, and flawless giving. The first seven kinds are flawed; the last alone is flawless. There is giving harmed by laziness, and so flawed. There is giving that does not follow what one truly wishes, and so flawed — namely, one with a defiled mind gives out of fear of poverty or desire for wealth and pleasure. There is giving that dwells on the past with regret, and so flawed. There is giving that hopes for future return, and so flawed. There is giving tainted by contempt, and so flawed. There is giving that seeks wealth and pleasure, and so flawed. There is giving that seeks to be known and heard of by others, and so flawed. Flawless giving is giving dedicated to nirvāṇa, giving as a provision toward it, giving with an undefiled mind for the sake of a favorable rebirth, and giving in order to obtain great wealth [for others' benefit].

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi confirms the eightfold path's now-familiar mapping onto the three higher trainings (precepts: right speech, action, livelihood; concentration: right mindfulness, right concentration; wisdom: right view, intention, diligence) — the same structure already met from a different angle at fascicle 14's item 3, restated here as the direct cause of a trainee's final severance of the five upper fetters and attainment of arhatship. The eight kinds of person he reads simply as the four fruits doubled: the path leading toward each fruit, and the fruit itself once attained — eight stages in all, from the very first steps toward stream-entry up through full arhatship.

He walks through the seven flawed forms of giving as a genuinely practical checklist of what corrupts an otherwise generous act: laziness (slow or half-hearted response to a genuine need); giving that fights one's own reluctance the whole way through, whether from fear of running short or from attachment to a prized possession; giving followed by regret, sometimes merely habitual giving inherited from family custom rather than genuine intention; giving that secretly expects future repayment; giving delivered with contempt, tossed at the recipient rather than offered with respect; giving motivated by hope of even greater wealth in return; and giving performed to be seen and praised. Flawless giving, by contrast, is giving genuinely dedicated to liberation itself, giving as a deliberate provision toward that same liberation, and — specifically for a bodhisattva — giving toward one's own future wealth and favorable rebirth not for personal enjoyment but precisely so as to have the resources to relieve others' material want before then teaching them the Dharma, since a mind consumed by basic need has no room left to actually hear a teaching. Cháng Bǎi treats the "three wheels empty" principle — no real giver, gift, or recipient ultimately found — as what genuinely purifies giving of all seven flaws at once, whether practiced by a śrāvaka seeking personal liberation or a bodhisattva seeking to benefit others.

2. Eight Moments a Lazy Mind Always Finds an Excuse, and the Same Eight Moments a Diligent Mind Finds a Reason

The fourth item names four circumstances — almsround, undertaking a task, travel, and illness — each yielding two critical moments, eight in all, where a lazy disposition reliably manufactures an excuse to rest rather than practice. The same eight moments, faced by a diligent disposition, produce the opposite conclusion every time.

Chinese Source

又依四處，於八時中，趣入懈怠，不發精進。當知如是補特伽羅是懈怠類，非精進類。謂依乞食處，依所作處，依遊行處，依界不平等處。依此四處，八時差別。多食精美，身沈重時；少食麤惡，身劣頓時；將欲所作，護惜力時；已有所作，身疲倦時；將欲遊行，護惜力時；已涉長途，身疲倦時；正為病苦所纏擾時；所病已愈，恐更發時。此懈怠類補特伽羅，乃至未遇懈怠所依，少似精進；若得遇已，速發懈怠；是故名為懈怠種類。與此相違，亦依四處，於八時中發勤精進。當知如是補特伽羅能伏懈怠，勤精進類。

Ru-Yi Translation

Depending on four circumstances, in eight moments, one turns toward laziness and fails to arouse diligence: know that such a person belongs to the class of the lazy, not the diligent. Namely, depending on almsround, on undertaking a task, on travel, and on bodily imbalance. Depending on these four, there are eight distinct moments: when the body feels heavy from eating too much rich food; when the body feels weak and sluggish from eating too little or too coarsely; when about to undertake a task, sparing one's strength; when a task is finished, the body fatigued; when about to travel, sparing one's strength; when a long journey is finished, the body fatigued; when actually afflicted by illness; and when illness has just passed, fearing its return. Such a lazy person, until meeting one of these occasions for laziness, appears somewhat diligent; but once the occasion arises, laziness swiftly follows — hence called the class of the lazy. The reverse of this — also depending on the same four circumstances, in the same eight moments, arousing diligence — know that such a person, capable of subduing laziness, belongs to the class of the diligent.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes this passage draws on the Dīrghāgama's own classic account of the eight grounds for laziness and their eight mirror-image grounds for diligence — the same eight physical circumstances (a satisfying or unsatisfying almsround, a task about to be done or just completed, travel about to begin or just finished, illness present or just recovered from) producing exactly opposite conclusions depending on the practitioner's underlying disposition. A lazy mind reasons "my body is heavy/weak/tired/fragile, so I should rest instead of continuing toward what I have not yet attained"; a diligent mind, facing the identical physical fact, reasons instead "this is exactly when my ordinary attention to the teaching is most at risk of lapsing, so I should press on now rather than later." Cháng Bǎi draws out the deeper point: physical circumstance never actually determines the outcome — the same tired body serves either as a lazy person's excuse or a diligent person's spur, depending entirely on which habit of mind meets it. He adds two further variables that shape how easily one resists the lazy reading: sheer mental resolve (his example: the ailing monk-scholar Yìn Shùn, who produced major scholarly works even from a sickbed, showing willpower can outrun physical weakness) and simple familiarity with practice (a well-rehearsed meditation routine withstands distraction far better than an unfamiliar one) — both, in turn, themselves strengthened by nothing other than sustained diligence, making steady practice its own best defense against future laziness.

3. Eight Desirable Places to Be Reborn, for Those Not Yet Seeking to Leave the Desire Realm Entirely

The fifth item addresses ordinary aspiration honestly: for beings not yet seeking full liberation but still wishing for a better rebirth within the desire realm, the treatise names eight specific destinations — two grades of human birth and the six desire-realm heavens — each reachable through the same two causes, giving and precepts, applied in greater or lesser measure.

Chinese Source

又有八種正願所攝可愛生因，能令於諸欲中樂增上生，不求永離一切欲者，當生八種可愛生處。謂願人中卑惡種類，修小施戒二福業事；如是願樂人中尊貴種類、四大王天、三十三天、夜摩天、覩史多天、化樂天、他化自在天，修小施戒二福業事。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also eight causes of a desirable rebirth comprised within a correct aspiration, capable of leading one who delights in the desire realm and does not yet seek to leave all desire entirely to a favorable rebirth there, to be born in eight desirable places: wishing for birth among the lowly castes of humanity, one cultivates giving and precepts in modest measure; and likewise wishing for birth among the noble castes of humanity, or in the Heaven of the Four Great Kings, the Heaven of the Thirty-Three, the Yāma Heaven, the Tuṣita Heaven, the Heaven of Delighting in Transformation, or the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations, one cultivates giving and precepts accordingly.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this item as an honest acknowledgment that not every practitioner is yet ready to seek full renunciation, and the treatise meets them where they are: two grades of human rebirth (a modest, even impoverished family reached through minimal giving and only partial precept-keeping, versus a wealthy, noble family reached through fuller giving and complete precepts) and the six desire-realm heavens already surveyed cosmologically in fascicle 4 (the Four Great Kings, the Heaven of the Thirty-Three, and the four "space-dwelling" heavens rising progressively higher — Yāma, Tuṣita, the Heaven of Delighting in Transformation, and the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations) — each successively higher destination simply requiring correspondingly more generous giving and more complete precept-keeping to reach. He notes the same two causes, giving and precepts, run through the entire scale from the humblest human birth to the very highest desire-realm heaven; only the degree to which they are cultivated changes, not their basic kind — a reminder that even relatively modest, incomplete practice is never wasted, only calibrated to a correspondingly modest result.

4. Eight Assemblies Among Humans and Gods, and the Eight Worldly Winds No Being Escapes

Two more items: the eight distinct assemblies the Buddha addressed — four among humans, sorted by what makes each group worthy of note, and four among gods, sorted by cosmic boundary — and the eight worldly winds (gain and loss, private censure and praise, public blame and acclaim, pain and pleasure) that touch every being's life in one of exactly three recurring situations.

Chinese Source

又四因緣故，於人趣中建立如來四眾；三因緣故，於天趣中建立四眾。最增上故，世間共許為福田故，受用資財不由他故，棄捨一切世資財故；由此四緣，於人趣中建立四眾。依地邊際故，欲界邊際故，語行邊際故；由此三緣，於天趣中建立四眾。又於世間三處轉時，恆常世間八法所觸。謂樂欲處、功用處、眾緣處。於樂欲處轉時，或觸於利，或觸非利。於功用處轉時，或稱他意，或不稱意；於背面位觸於毀、譽，於現前位觸於稱、譏。於眾緣處轉時，或由先世、或由現法苦樂眾緣，觸於苦、樂。

Ru-Yi Translation

By four causal conditions, the Tathāgata's four assemblies are established among humans; by three causal conditions, four assemblies are established among gods. Being of the highest standing; being universally acknowledged as a field of merit; enjoying one's own resources without depending on others; and having renounced all worldly resources entirely — by these four conditions, four assemblies are established among humans. By the boundary of the earth; by the boundary of the desire realm; and by the boundary of verbal activity — by these three conditions, four assemblies are established among gods. Further, in three situations that recur throughout the world, one is constantly touched by the eight worldly phenomena: the situation of desire and aspiration, the situation of effortful action, and the situation of manifold conditions. In the situation of desire, one is touched either by gain or by its absence. In the situation of effortful action, one either meets others' approval or does not; behind one's back, one is touched by censure or praise; face to face, one is touched by acclaim or blame. In the situation of manifold conditions, whether from past lives or from present causes and conditions of pain and pleasure, one is touched by pain or by pleasure.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the four human assemblies as the kṣatriya (rulers, of highest worldly standing), brāhmaṇa (priests and scholars, universally regarded as worthy recipients of offerings), householders of great wealth (self-sufficient, dependent on no one), and renunciants (having given up worldly resources entirely) — the Buddha's teaching, in his own lifetime, reaching all four social classes alike. The four divine assemblies he sorts by cosmic boundary rather than social role: the Four Great Kings and the Thirty-Three at the earth-adjacent summits of Mount Sumeru and its surrounding peaks (the "boundary of the earth"), the Heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations at the very top of the desire realm (its "boundary"), and the first dhyāna's Brahmā-heavens, the highest point at which beings still communicate through language before all verbal activity ceases at the second dhyāna and above (the "boundary of verbal activity"). Together, the eight assemblies span everyone the Buddha's teaching was ever recorded reaching, human and divine alike.

He reads the eight worldly winds as touching every being through just three basic situations: whenever desire seeks something, either gain or its absence follows; whenever effort is made, either approval (praised behind one's back, acclaimed to one's face) or disapproval (censured behind one's back, blamed to one's face) follows; and whenever the manifold conditions of past or present karma converge, either pleasure or pain follows. He illustrates the point with an original account of the classical exchange between the poet Su Dongpo and Chan master Foyin: Su, believing himself to have written a verse proving he sat "unmoved by the eight winds," sent it across the river for praise, only to have Foyin write back two blunt words of mockery — and when Su, furious, rushed across the river to confront him, Foyin's door already bore the reply that one dismissive word had been enough to prove the eight winds still moved him completely. A later exchange, in which Su boasted of seeing his meditation partner as a heap of dung while being told he himself resembled a buddha, drew the same lesson from his own sister: what a mind sees in others reveals what that same mind actually is within — genuine freedom from the eight winds is proven only by how a mind actually responds when tested, never by how eloquently it claims to have already transcended them.

5. The Eight Liberations Revisited as Eight Resolves, Specifically Drawing Out Superknowledge and the Cessation Attainment

The eighth item returns to the eight liberations already surveyed at length in fascicle 12, this time framed specifically as "resolves" (勝解) — firm, unwavering meditative convictions — and organized around what they specifically accomplish: the first three draw out a noble one's superknowledges (never available to an ordinary being, however accomplished their concentration), and the remaining five, applied in careful sequence, draw out the supreme abiding of the cessation attainment itself.

Chinese Source

又八勝解，能引不還、或阿羅漢諸聖神通及最勝住。謂未伏內色想，外無染汙色勝解，是名第一；已伏內色想，是名第二；淨不淨非二色第一捨勝解，是名第三。此三解脫，於一切色得自在故，便能引發諸聖神通。謂諸神通不與一切異生共有。空無邊勝解、識無邊勝解、無所有勝解、非想非非想勝解、微微任運心勝解，此五勝解次第善修治故，能引想受滅等至最勝住。

Ru-Yi Translation

There are also eight resolves, capable of drawing out the noble superknowledges of a non-returner or arhat, and the supreme abiding. The resolve of not yet having subdued the perception of inner form, together with the resolve of undefiled outer form, is the first. Having subdued the perception of inner form is the second. The resolve of the foremost equanimity toward form neither pure nor impure is the third. These three liberations, by attaining mastery over all form, are thereby able to draw out the noble superknowledges — for these superknowledges are not shared with any ordinary being. The resolve of infinite space, the resolve of infinite consciousness, the resolve of nothingness, the resolve of neither perception nor non-perception, and the resolve of a subtle, spontaneously-functioning mind — these five resolves, well cultivated in this sequence, are able to draw out the supreme abiding of the attainment of the cessation of perception and feeling.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi confirms these eight resolves are simply the eight liberations already translated in full at fascicle 12 — the same progression from the impurity contemplation, through its refinement into pure light, to the four formless attainments and finally the cessation attainment itself — now regrouped by function rather than by sequence alone. The first three (still working with form, whether inner, outer, or the pure light both eventually resolve into) are singled out because mastering form in all three of these ways is precisely what unlocks the noble superknowledges — powers the treatise insists are categorically unavailable to any ordinary being, however deep their concentration, since these specific powers depend on the uncontaminated insight only a non-returner or arhat possesses, not merely on meditative skill. The remaining five (the four formless attainments plus a final, exquisitely subtle "spontaneously functioning mind" resolve marking the threshold just before all mental activity ceases entirely) must be cultivated in strict order, each resolving one further layer of dependency, before the cessation attainment — the treatise's "supreme abiding" — can actually be reached. Cháng Bǎi treats this item as adding one genuinely new distinction to what fascicle 12 already covered: not just what the eight liberations are, but which three specifically unlock supernormal power and which five specifically culminate in the deepest meditative attainment any being can reach.

6. The Eight Bases of Mastery, and What "Mastery" Actually Means — the "Eight Things" Category Closes

The ninth and final item of "eight things" returns to the eight bases of mastery already surveyed in fascicle 12, built on the same three liberations just covered — and adds a genuinely new contribution: a precise, five-part definition of what "mastery" itself actually consists of, together with the observation that this same purifying observation can be practiced even outside formal meditation, in the midst of ordinary daily life. With this item, the "eight things" category — nine items in all — comes to its close.

Chinese Source

又若觀諸色，若如所觀，於初三解脫中而修習者，謂三解脫方便道所攝三勝處也。此中觀外諸色，若小若大、若好若惡、若劣若勝者，謂觀非三摩地所行現所得色。由緣三摩地所行作意不種種現前，故名為勝。於三摩地所行中，奢摩他行名知，毗鉢舍那行名見。如於三摩地所行若知若見，如於彼色已尋思、已了別，如是於外所想非三摩地所行中觀諸色亦爾。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, whether observing forms [in limited or extensive number], or observing them [as bright colors], cultivated within the first three liberations — these are the three bases of mastery comprised within the three liberations as their preparatory path. Herein, observing outer forms — whether small or great, whether pleasant or repulsive, whether inferior or superior — refers to observing form actually obtained [in ordinary experience], not that which operates within meditative absorption. By taking as object what operates within meditative absorption, attention no longer manifests in manifold, uncontrolled ways — hence it is called mastery. Within what operates in meditative absorption, the activity of calm is called knowing, and the activity of insight is called seeing. Just as, with respect to what operates within meditative absorption, there is such knowing and seeing, such investigation and discernment of that form, so too is it likewise with observing forms in what is merely imagined outwardly, not operating within meditative absorption.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi confirms the eight bases of mastery are the same eight already translated in fascicle 12, here regrouped into three: the first two (observing form, in fewer or greater number, still within one's own or another's body) and the second two (the same observation once inner form has been let go, observed only in others) both build on the first two liberations, while the final four (observing pure blue, yellow, red, and white light) build on the third liberation's purified light. What this section adds beyond fascicle 12's account is a precise five-part definition of "mastery" itself: mastery means the mind, not the object, now sets the terms (形奪卑下, the mind's own form dominating whatever inferior appearance the object presents); the mind's power subdues whatever weaker pull the object exerts (制伏羸劣); the mind can conceal the object at will, making it appear or vanish on command (能隱蔽他); the mind remains free of both craving and aversion no matter how vivid or repulsive the object becomes (厭壞所緣); and the mind can freely convert one into many or many into one, purity into impurity or impurity into purity (自在迴轉) — five distinct senses in which the practitioner, rather than the object, is now unmistakably in control.

He draws out one further point worth real attention: this same purifying observation of "small or great, pleasant or repulsive, inferior or superior" objects is not confined to formal sitting meditation at all — it applies equally to ordinary objects encountered in daily life, before any meditative absorption is even entered. Walking past a bakery, hearing a beautiful or discordant sound, meeting an attractive or unattractive stranger — all of it can become the same practice of observing without craving or aversion, calm's steady attention paired with insight's discerning investigation, exactly as within formal sitting. Cháng Bǎi treats this as the whole point the treatise wants understood: mastery achieved in meditation is meant to carry directly into ordinary experience, not remain sealed off within it — with this closing insight, "eight things," inner wisdom's eighth numeric category, comes to its end.

7. The Ninth Numeric Category — Nine Fetters, and the Nine Abodes Encompassing Every Place Any Being Actually Lives

"Nine things" is the briefest category yet — just two items. The first simply names the nine fetters by cross-reference to an earlier fascicle. The second completes a count already building since fascicle 13: the seven stations of consciousness, expanded by two further special states, to produce nine abodes that between them account for literally every place any being in the three realms can be found.

Chinese Source

已說八種佛教所應知處。次說九種。謂有九結。如攝事分當廣建立。又有九種生處受生有情，於彼彼處同所居止。謂三界中，除諸惡趣，可厭處故。如前已說。

Ru-Yi Translation

The eight-thing category of what a Buddhist should know has now been explained. Next, the nine-thing category. There are nine fetters, to be broadly established in the Compendium of Determinations. There are also nine kinds of birthplace in which sentient beings dwell together, each in their own place: within the three realms, excluding only the evil destinies, since these are utterly repugnant places — as already explained.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the nine fetters (craving, hostility, conceit, ignorance, view, [wrong-rule] attachment, doubt, envy, and stinginess) simply cross-reference an already-familiar list rather than being newly explained here, and were already named in an earlier fascicle's account of what binds a mind to suffering.

He reads the nine abodes as completing the count already begun with the seven stations of consciousness explained in full at fascicle 14's §56: the same seven, expanded by two further special states that don't quite count as consciousness "settling in gratified" the way the seven proper do — beings without perception (whose six active consciousnesses are simply absent) and the peak of existence (whose mental activity is too subtle to classify as clearly present or absent) — bringing the total to nine. Only the evil destinies are excluded from the count entirely, since no being is ever described as gratified there, however long they may in fact reside. Between them, the nine abodes map every single location in the entire three-realm cosmos where any being's existence actually unfolds — the treatise's most complete geographical inventory of saṃsāra itself.

8. A Major Milestone: The Ten Totalities and the Ten Factors of an Arhat Close "Ten Things" — and With It, the Entire Science of Inner Wisdom

The tenth and final numeric category takes up just two items: the ten totalities, completing the meditative sequence of liberations, masteries, and totalities first surveyed in fascicles 11–12; and the ten factors comprising an arhat's being, the eightfold path completed by two further factors unique to one beyond training. With this category's close, inner wisdom (內明處) — the first of the five sciences, spanning all four of its aspects since section 43 of fascicle 13 — is declared complete in its entirety.

Chinese Source

已說九種佛教所應知處。次說十種。謂十遍處。當知即諸解脫所作成就。餘解脫、勝處、遍處，如攝事分當廣分別。又有十無學支，當知無學五蘊所攝。謂戒蘊、定蘊、慧蘊、解脫蘊、解脫知見蘊。如是已說十種佛教所應知處，及前所說佛教所應知處等，當知皆是內明處攝。

Ru-Yi Translation

The nine-thing category of what a Buddhist should know has now been explained. Next, the ten-thing category. There are the ten totalities: know that these are precisely what the liberations accomplish. The remaining details of the liberations, bases of mastery, and totalities will be broadly differentiated in the Compendium of Determinations. There are also ten factors of one beyond training, comprised, know, within the five aggregates of one beyond training: the aggregate of precepts, the aggregate of concentration, the aggregate of wisdom, the aggregate of liberation, and the aggregate of the knowledge and vision of liberation. Thus the ten-thing category of what a Buddhist should know has now been explained, and, together with everything said before it, know that all of it is comprehended within inner wisdom.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi confirms the ten totalities (earth, water, fire, wind, blue, yellow, red, white, space, and consciousness, each contemplated as pervading without limit or gap) as the final fruit of the same sequence already translated in fascicles 11–12: liberation first makes an object available to resolve, mastery then lets the mind control it at will, and totality finally extends that same mastered object without any boundary at all — three stages of the identical meditative material, deepening at each step. He notes the treatise itself defers further technical detail to the Compendium of Determinations, treating this brief mention here as sufficient closure rather than fresh exposition.

He reads the ten factors of one beyond training as the familiar eightfold path completed by two further factors unique to full arhatship: the eight path-factors sort into precepts (right speech, action, livelihood), concentration (right mindfulness, right concentration), and wisdom (right view, intention, diligence) — the same three-training structure met repeatedly throughout this numeric catalogue — while the ninth and tenth factors, right liberation and right knowledge, belong to no trainee, only to one who has finished training entirely. Right liberation is the settled confidence of having actually broken free of affliction; right knowledge is the arhat's own certainty — already met at fascicle 13's account of an arhat's two-part self-knowledge — that this freedom is both real and permanent. Cháng Bǎi notes that a buddha's own five aggregates rank highest among these, a pratyekabuddha's in the middle, and a śrāvaka's lowest, not because the aggregates themselves differ in kind but because the depth of realization behind them differs.

With the ten totalities and the ten factors of one beyond training named, the treatise's own closing line makes the milestone explicit: everything covered since section 43 of fascicle 13 — the threefold textual division, the four-verse concept catalogue running from "word" through "the sacred teaching," the ten principles comprehending the teaching's meaning, and this entire numeric catalogue climbing from one thing through ten — is comprehended within inner wisdom (內明處), the first of the five sciences named at the Ground of What Is Attained Through Hearing's very outset. Inner wisdom itself, spanning roughly three full fascicles of source material, is now complete.

9. The Second Science Opens: Medicine, Mapped Directly Onto the Four Noble Truths

With inner wisdom complete, the treatise turns to the second of the five sciences named back at section 42: medicine. True to form, it is treated briefly — just four kinds of skill, mapped with striking directness onto the same four noble truths that have structured this entire fascicle's numeric catalogue, before the treatise defers all further clinical detail to scripture at large.

Chinese Source

云何醫方明處？當知此明略有四種。謂於病相善巧，於病因善巧，於已生病斷滅善巧，於已斷病後更不生方便善巧。如是善巧廣分別義，如經應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the science of medicine? Know that, briefly, this science has four aspects: skill regarding the marks of illness, skill regarding the cause of illness, skill in eliminating an illness that has already arisen, and skill in the means preventing an eliminated illness from arising again. The broad differentiation of this skill's meaning should be known as found in scripture.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps all four aspects onto the four noble truths without hesitation: recognizing an illness's symptoms is the truth of suffering; understanding what actually caused it is the truth of origination; eliminating an illness already present is the truth of cessation; and the ongoing means that keep it from recurring is the truth of the path — the same structure, he notes, citing the *Xiǎnyáng Shèngjiào Lùn*, that organizes every one of the treatise's four truths elsewhere. He reads this brief treatment as entirely deliberate: medicine here is offered as a worldly convenience, useful for sustaining the very body practice depends on, but the deeper point sits just beneath it — the body itself is inescapably subject to illness only because it is subject to birth at all, and birth traces back to ignorance; treating symptoms and even their proximate causes, however genuinely valuable, never reaches that root the way the Dharma's own four truths do. He adds a brief historical note that Indian medical learning — the physician Jīvaka renowned in the Buddha's own lifetime, later authors such as Nāgārjuna associated with medical treatises still preserved in the Tibetan canon, and the classical eightfold division of medical specialties (general ailments, ophthalmology, wound care, pediatrics, toxicology, and the rest) — traveled into China alongside Buddhism itself, leaving traces still visible in classical Chinese medical texts centuries later. With this brief treatment, the science of medicine is complete, and the treatise turns next to logic.

10. The Third Science Opens: Logic, and Its Sevenfold Framework

With medicine's brief treatment finished, the treatise turns to the third of the five sciences: logic (因明處, hetuvidyā), the discipline of sound reasoning and formal debate. This is new terrain — nothing in the fourteen preceding fascicles has addressed argumentation as its own subject — and it is vast: roughly 3,900 lines of source material, by far the largest single topic in this fascicle. The treatise opens by naming a sevenfold framework that will structure everything to follow.

Chinese Source

已說醫方明處。云何因明處？謂於觀察義中諸所有事。此復云何？嗢柁南曰：論體論處所　論據論莊嚴　論負論出離　論多所作法　當知此中略有七種。一、論體性，二、論處所，三、論所依，四、論莊嚴，五、論墮負，六、論出離，七、論多所作法。

Ru-Yi Translation

The science of medicine has now been explained. What is the science of logic? It refers to all the matters bound up in examining a meaning to be established. What, again, does this comprise? A summary verse states: the nature of discourse, the venue of discourse, the basis of discourse, the adornment of discourse, discourse's defeat, discourse's withdrawal, and the methods of greatest use in discourse. Know that, briefly, there are seven kinds here: first, the nature of discourse; second, the venue of discourse; third, the basis of discourse; fourth, the adornment of discourse; fifth, discourse's defeat; sixth, discourse's withdrawal; and seventh, the methods of greatest use in discourse.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses "examining a meaning" as the work of establishing a thesis — such as impermanence, non-self, or the peace of nirvāṇa — through the reasons and examples that support it; "all the matters" are everything that goes into that work of proof. He previews what each of the seven headings will cover: the nature of discourse sorts all debate into six basic kinds; the venue names where a debate should properly be held; the basis is the largest heading by far — the actual logical machinery of proof, built on valid reasons and examples grounded in direct perception, inference, and scriptural authority; the adornment covers the skills that let a debater grasp both his own position and his opponent's and present persuasively; defeat names the ways an argument fails; withdrawal asks whether a debate is even worth entering into; and the methods of greatest use describe the benefits — for oneself and others — that follow when the first six are done well.

He adds, as orientation rather than translation, that Indian logic developed in two broad phases: an older tradition and a "new logic" reorganized several centuries later around eight technical categories — valid and fallacious proof, valid and fallacious refutation, and the four combinations of direct perception and inference with their fallacious counterparts. This treatise reflects the older tradition; Cháng Bǎi treats the newer scheme only as background for readers who may encounter it in later commentarial literature, not as something the root text itself teaches here.

11. The Nature of Discourse (I): Verbal Discourse and Worldly-Esteemed Discourse

The first of the seven headings, the nature of discourse, sorts all debate and discussion into six basic kinds. The treatise names them together, then takes each in turn — beginning with the most basic, ordinary speech itself, and the discourse the world generally holds worth listening to.

Chinese Source

云何論體性？謂有六種。一、言論，二、尚論，三、諍論，四、毀謗論，五、順正論，六、教導論。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the nature of discourse? It has six kinds: first, verbal discourse; second, worldly-esteemed discourse; third, contentious discourse; fourth, discourse of denigration; fifth, discourse in accord with the right; and sixth, instructional discourse.

Chinese Source

言論者，謂一切言說、言音、言詞，是名言論。尚論者，謂諸世間隨所應聞所有言論。

Ru-Yi Translation

Verbal discourse: it refers to all speech, all vocal sound, and all words — this is called verbal discourse. Worldly-esteemed discourse: it refers to whatever discourse in the world is fit to be heeded.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi breaks verbal discourse, the most basic kind, into three components: speech itself, arising from seeing, hearing, sensing, and knowing; vocal sound, the raw acoustic material — pitch, length, tone — that carries it; and words, the actual terms chosen once the wish to speak arises. He reads these as substance, form, and function of the same act: a skilled speaker commands all three at once. Worldly-esteemed discourse, the second kind, he glosses as whatever a given society already holds to be right speech regardless of whether it engages Buddhist teaching directly — filial devotion, honesty, respect for elders and law, the ordinary ethical consensus any community esteems and hands down. Both kinds are neutral raw material: the sections that follow sort discourse increasingly by its moral and soteriological quality, and by the end of this heading the treatise will ask directly which of the six kinds are actually worth cultivating.

12. The Nature of Discourse (II): Contentious Discourse

The third kind, contentious discourse, is quarreling itself — and the treatise traces it to three distinct roots: disputes over desired objects, disputes over misdeeds, and disputes over views. Each root is analyzed separately, but all three converge on the same underlying mechanism and the same closing definition.

Chinese Source

諍論者，謂或依諸欲所起。若自所攝諸欲，他所侵奪；若他所攝諸欲，自行侵奪；若所愛有情所攝諸欲，更相侵奪；或欲侵奪。若無攝受諸欲，謂歌舞戲笑等所攝，若倡女僕從等所攝；或為觀看，或為受用。

Ru-Yi Translation

Contentious discourse: it may arise dependent on desired objects. Whether objects one holds are seized by another, or objects another holds are seized by oneself, or objects held by a being one cherishes are seized by each from the other — or merely the wish to seize them arises. Or it may concern objects held by no one in particular, such as those bound up with song, dance, and merriment, or with courtesans and attendants — whether the dispute is over watching them or over making use of them.

Chinese Source

或依惡行所起。若自所作身語惡行，他所譏毀；若他所作身語惡行，自行譏毀；若所愛有情所作身語惡行，互相譏毀。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or it may arise dependent on misdeeds. Whether misdeeds of body or speech committed by oneself are mocked and denigrated by another, or such misdeeds committed by another are mocked and denigrated by oneself, or such misdeeds committed by a being one cherishes are mocked and denigrated by each from the other.

Chinese Source

或依諸見所起。謂薩迦耶見、斷見、無因見、不平等因見、常見、雨眾見等種種邪見，及餘無量諸惡見類。於如是諸見中，或自所攝，他所遮斷；或他所攝，自行遮斷；或所愛有情所攝，他正遮斷；或已遮斷；或欲攝受所未攝受。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or it may arise dependent on views — the view of a real self in the aggregates, the view of annihilation, the view of no cause, the view of an unequal cause, the view of permanence, the view held by the followers of Varṣa, and other such wrong views without measure. Regarding such views: whether a view one holds is blocked by another, or a view another holds is blocked by oneself, or a view held by a being one cherishes is being blocked by another, or has already been blocked, or one wishes to draw another toward a view not yet accepted.

Chinese Source

於如是等諸欲事中，未離欲者，為欲界貪所染汙者，因堅執故、因縛著故、因耽嗜故、因貪愛故，發憤乖違；喜鬥諍者，興種種論、興怨害論，故名諍論。

Ru-Yi Translation

In all such matters, one who has not become free of desire, who is stained by craving for the desire realm — through firm attachment, through bondage, through insatiable indulgence, through craving — grows resentful and falls into conflict. One who delights in quarreling gives rise to discourse of every kind, discourse meant to wound: this is called contentious discourse.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi traces all three roots to the same four-step chain: firm attachment (refusing to let go), bondage (refusing to step outside the object's pull), insatiable indulgence (never having enough), and craving (finding real pleasure in it) — the same causal analysis of desire met repeatedly elsewhere in this treatise, here specifically producing resentment and quarrel rather than any other outcome. On the third root, disputes over views, he identifies the "followers of Varṣa" as India's Sāṃkhya school, naming its founder and its elaborate twenty-five-category metaphysics of primordial nature, intellect, ego, and the true self at some length — he treats this purely as background for readers who may meet the school again in later polemical contexts, not as anything the root text itself asks a reader to master. His main point is simpler: whichever the root — a contested object, a contested action, or a contested view — the same craving-driven mechanism produces the same result, discourse meant to injure rather than to clarify, which is why the treatise gives all three the single name "contentious discourse."

13. The Nature of Discourse (III): Denigration, Right Accord, and Instruction — And Which Are Worth Cultivating

The remaining three kinds close out the six: discourse of denigration, born of open hostility; discourse in accord with the right, which teaches the Dharma to resolve doubt; and instructional discourse, which guides a practitioner from concentration to liberation. With all six now named, the treatise asks directly which are genuinely worth cultivating.

Chinese Source

毀謗論者，謂懷憤發者，以染汙心振發威勢，更相擯毀所有言論。謂麤惡所引，或不遜所引，或綺言所引；乃至惡說法律中，為諸有情宣說彼法，研究決擇，教授教誡。如是等論，名毀謗論。

Ru-Yi Translation

Discourse of denigration: it refers to one who, harboring resentment, rouses his own force with a defiled mind and casts aside and denigrates another's discourse. It is drawn out by coarse and vicious speech, by insolent speech, or by frivolous speech — extending even to expounding, investigating, and teaching a wrongly taught doctrine to others. Discourse of this kind is called discourse of denigration.

Chinese Source

順正論者，謂於善說法律中，為諸有情宣說正法，研究決擇，教授教誡。為斷有情所疑惑故，為達甚深諸句義故，為令智見畢竟淨故；隨順正行、隨順解脫，是故此論名順正論。

Ru-Yi Translation

Discourse in accord with the right: it refers to expounding, investigating, and teaching the true Dharma to beings within a well-taught doctrine and discipline. It is done to sever beings' doubts, to reach the deep meaning of its statements, and to make knowledge and vision utterly pure — in accord with right practice, in accord with liberation. This is why this discourse is called discourse in accord with the right.

Chinese Source

教導論者，謂教修習增上心學、增上慧學補特伽羅，心未定者令心得定，心已定者令得解脫，所有言論。令彼覺悟真實智故，令彼開解真實智故，是故此論名教導論。

Ru-Yi Translation

Instructional discourse: it refers to all discourse that teaches a person to cultivate the higher training in mind and the higher training in wisdom — bringing an unconcentrated mind to concentration, and bringing an already-concentrated mind to liberation. It is done to awaken that person to true wisdom and to open their understanding of true wisdom; this is why this discourse is called instructional discourse.

Chinese Source

問：此六論中，幾論真實，能引義利，所應修習？幾不真實，能引無義，所應遠離？答：最後二論是真是實，能引義利，所應修習；中間二論不真不實，能引無義，所應遠離；初二種論，應當分別。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: among these six kinds of discourse, how many are genuine, capable of bringing real benefit, and worth cultivating? How many are not genuine, bring no real benefit, and should be avoided? Answer: the last two are genuine and real, capable of bringing real benefit, and worth cultivating. The middle two are neither genuine nor real, bring no real benefit, and should be avoided. The first two kinds must be judged case by case.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads discourse of denigration as contentious discourse's uglier sibling — not merely a dispute over some object, action, or view, but an outright attempt to shame and dismantle an opponent's standing, whether through vicious or insolent speech or by twisting a wrongly taught doctrine into a weapon. Discourse in accord with the right and instructional discourse, by contrast, he distinguishes by audience: the former meets beginners with the basic teaching, closer to the training in precepts, while the latter guides practitioners already advanced enough to work directly on concentration and wisdom — an order that also explains why the treatise names them last and ranks them, without qualification, as the two kinds genuinely worth cultivating.

On the closing judgment, he notes that contentious discourse and discourse of denigration are ruled out entirely — they only wound, never illuminate — while verbal discourse and worldly-esteemed discourse, the first two kinds, cannot be judged wholesale: ordinary speech and a society's conventional wisdom are sometimes wholesome and sometimes not, so each case must be weighed on its own, by whether it actually leads toward or away from what is skillful. With this judgment, the nature of discourse — the first of the seven headings — is complete.

14. The Venue of Discourse: Six Fitting Places to Debate

With the nature of discourse complete, the treatise turns to its second heading: where a debate ought properly to be held. Six venues are named, unified by a single principle — each one places the debate before witnesses capable of judging it fairly.

Chinese Source

云何論處所？當知亦有六種。一、於王家，二、於執理家，三、於大眾中，四、於賢哲者前，五、於善解法義沙門、婆羅門前，六、於樂法義者前。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the venue of discourse? Know that this too has six kinds: first, at the royal court; second, before a magistrate; third, amid a great assembly; fourth, before the wise and virtuous; fifth, before ascetics and brāhmaṇas well versed in the meaning of the Dharma; and sixth, before those who delight in the meaning of the Dharma.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses each venue by the character of its witnesses rather than its physical setting: a royal court works only under a ruler genuinely impartial and respectful of every teaching; a magistrate's hall requires a judge who reasons fairly rather than one already biased; a great assembly must itself be even-handed, since a crowd already committed to one side makes fair debate impossible; the wise and virtuous can judge right and wrong on their own merits; scholars of the Dharma can follow and adjudicate technical points a general audience would miss; and those who already delight in the meaning of the Dharma are the natural audience for the two kinds of discourse just named as worth cultivating — discourse in accord with the right and instructional discourse — since an audience with no interest in the Dharma will simply not listen. The unifying thread, he notes, is impartiality: whichever of the six is chosen, what makes it fit for debate is witnesses capable of judging the outcome honestly, not the setting's grandeur.

15. The Basis of Discourse Opens: What a Thesis Establishes

The third heading, the basis of discourse, is where the treatise's actual logical machinery lives — the classical apparatus for proving a thesis before an audience. It has ten parts in all: two kinds of "meaning to be established" (the thesis itself) and eight "means of establishing" it. This heading alone runs to roughly 2,100 lines, well over half of the entire science of logic, and will occupy this fascicle's remaining sections. It opens by defining what a thesis actually is.

Chinese Source

云何論所依？當知有十種。謂所成立義有二種，能成立法有八種。所成立義有二種者，一、自性，二、差別。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the basis of discourse? Know that it has ten parts: the meaning to be established has two kinds, and the means of establishing it has eight kinds. The meaning to be established has two kinds: first, self-nature; second, differentiation.

Chinese Source

所成立自性者，謂有立為有，無立為無。所成立差別者，謂有上立有上，無上立無上；常立為常，無常立無常。如是有色無色、有見無見、有對無對、有漏無漏、有為無為，如是等無量差別門，當知名所成立差別。

Ru-Yi Translation

The self-nature to be established: what has being is posited as being; what has no being is posited as having no being. The differentiation to be established: what is surpassable is posited as surpassable, what is unsurpassed is posited as unsurpassed; what is permanent is posited as permanent, what is impermanent is posited as impermanent. Likewise possessing form or formless, visible or invisible, resistant or non-resistant, defiled or undefiled, conditioned or unconditioned — such immeasurable gateways of differentiation should be known as the differentiation to be established.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads "self-nature" as the plainest possible claim a thesis can make — that something exists or does not — and grounds it explicitly in the Yogācāra three-natures teaching already met in earlier fascicles: what is dependently arisen and what is perfectly realized are both posited as having being, while what is merely imagined and clung to is posited as having none. "Differentiation," the second kind, is everything else a thesis might claim about a thing once its bare existence is settled — permanent or impermanent, conditioned or unconditioned, and so on through an open-ended list the treatise explicitly declines to close, since any meaningful property a debater might wish to establish about something falls under this second heading. Together, he notes, the two kinds cover the entire space of what a philosophical claim can assert: that something is (or is not), and what it is like.

16. The Eight Means of Establishing a Thesis, and the Three-Part Syllogism Begins

Having defined what a thesis claims, the treatise names the eight means by which it is actually proved — then works through them one by one, beginning with the thesis, the reason, and the example: the classic three-part structure (宗因喻, thesis–reason–example) at the core of Buddhist syllogistic reasoning.

Chinese Source

能成立法有八種者，一、立宗，二、辯因，三、引喻，四、同類，五、異類，六、現量，七、比量，八、正教。

Ru-Yi Translation

The means of establishing has eight kinds: first, positing the thesis; second, distinguishing the reason; third, citing the example; fourth, the similar class; fifth, the dissimilar class; sixth, direct perception; seventh, inference; and eighth, valid scriptural authority.

Chinese Source

立宗者，謂依二種所成立義，各別攝受自品所許，或攝受論宗。

Ru-Yi Translation

Positing the thesis: it means, in dependence on the two kinds of meaning to be established, each distinctly taking up what one's own position accepts — or taking up a thesis in order to win over another's position.

Chinese Source

辯因者，謂為成就所立宗義，依所引喻、同類、異類、現量、比量及與正教，建立順益道理言論。引喻者，亦為成就所立宗義，引因所依諸餘世間串習共許易了之法，比況言論。

Ru-Yi Translation

Distinguishing the reason: it means, in order to accomplish the meaning of the thesis posited, establishing discourse that favorably supports it, in dependence on the example cited, the similar class, the dissimilar class, direct perception, inference, and valid scriptural authority. Citing the example: likewise in order to accomplish the meaning of the thesis posited, drawing on some other fact already familiar and mutually agreed upon in the world, on which the reason itself depends, to present discourse by way of comparison.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes, as background rather than as anything the root text itself teaches, that this eight-part scheme belongs to the older logical tradition; other early masters counted four or three, and several centuries later Dignāga's reformed "new logic" narrowed the means of proof to just reason and example, treating the thesis itself as what gets proved rather than as part of the proof. This treatise follows the eightfold scheme throughout.

He illustrates the three-part core — thesis, reason, example — with the tradition's standard case: the thesis "sound is impermanent"; the reason "because it is something produced"; the example "like a clay pot," which everyone already agrees is impermanent because it too was produced. He stresses positing the thesis is not simply asserting an opinion — it requires first knowing precisely what one's own school actually accepts, so the claim being defended is neither vaguer nor stronger than one's own position warrants. He also draws out a second use the treatise names for positing a thesis: not just building one's own case, but framing a thesis specifically to draw an opponent's mistaken view into the open where it can be refuted — citing the young Xuanzang's own practice in India of first drawing out a rival's full position before answering it, so that any reply actually meets what the opponent believes rather than a caricature of it. On the reason and the example, he notes both draw their force from the same three sources of valid knowledge named next — direct perception, inference, and scriptural authority — since a reason or an example that no one can actually verify persuades no one.

17. The Similar and Dissimilar Class: What Makes an Example Actually Work

The fourth and fifth means of proof examine what makes a cited example genuinely useful: an example only supports a thesis if it shares some real point of resemblance with it, and the treatise names five ways two things can resemble each other — before noting that a dissimilar-class example is simply the mirror case, sharing none of the five.

Chinese Source

同類者，謂隨所有法望所餘法，其相展轉少分相似。此復五種。一、相狀相似，二、自體相似，三、業用相似，四、法門相似，五、因果相似。

Ru-Yi Translation

The similar class: it means that, considering any given thing against some other thing, their features turn out, in some small part, to resemble each other. This is again of five kinds: first, resemblance of appearance; second, resemblance of essence; third, resemblance of function; fourth, resemblance of category; and fifth, resemblance of cause and effect.

Chinese Source

相狀相似者，謂於現在或先所見相狀相屬展轉相似。自體相似者，謂彼展轉其相相似。業用相似者，謂彼展轉作用相似。法門相似者，謂彼展轉法門相似。如無常與苦法，苦與無我法，無我與生法，生法與老法，老法與死法……如是等類無量法門展轉相似。因果相似者，謂彼展轉若因若果、能成所成展轉相似。是名同類。

Ru-Yi Translation

Resemblance of appearance: features seen now or seen before turn out, joined together, to resemble each other. Resemblance of essence: their natures turn out, in turn, to resemble each other. Resemblance of function: their activities turn out, in turn, to resemble each other. Resemblance of category: their conceptual domains turn out, in turn, to resemble each other — as with impermanence and suffering, suffering and non-self, non-self and birth, birth and old age, old age and death... and immeasurable domains of this kind that turn out, in turn, to resemble one another. Resemblance of cause and effect: their status as cause and effect, as what accomplishes and what is accomplished, turns out, in turn, to resemble each other. This is called the similar class.

Chinese Source

異類者，謂所有法望所餘法，其相展轉少不相似。此亦五種。與上相違，應知其相。

Ru-Yi Translation

The dissimilar class: it means that, considering any given thing against some other thing, their features turn out not to resemble each other even slightly. This too has five kinds; their character should be known as the reverse of what was said above.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi illustrates the standard case first: to prove sound is impermanent, a clay pot works as a similar-class example because it shares both the reason (being produced) and the thesis property (impermanence) — while space fails as a similar example precisely because it shares neither, making it instead a dissimilar-class example. He works through the five kinds of resemblance with everyday cases: two ox horns share appearance; a leaf and a lotus-leaf share the underlying quality of being green (essence); an ox and a donkey both plow a field, sharing function though not form; impermanence leading to suffering, suffering to non-self, non-self to birth, birth to old age, and old age to death form a chain of resemblance-by-conceptual-proximity (category) rather than by any single shared trait; and a wholesome cause producing a pleasant result, an unwholesome cause producing a painful one, resemble each other only in the causal relation itself (cause and effect), not in the things related. He stresses throughout that "similar" never means identical — only that some real, checkable point of contact exists — which is exactly why an example needs no further proof itself: everyone already agrees on it, and that shared agreement is what lets the debate move forward rather than stall on a fresh dispute.

18. Direct Perception Opens: Three Conditions for Valid Knowledge

The sixth means of proof, direct perception, is the first of the three sources of valid knowledge (現量, 比量, 正教量 — direct perception, inference, and scriptural authority) that the reason and example were said to depend on back in section 16. It receives by far the longest treatment of any topic in this fascicle — roughly 800 lines — because getting perception right is foundational: every inference ultimately rests on something perceived without error. The treatise opens with a threefold definition, each part ruling out one way perception can go wrong.

Chinese Source

現量者，謂有三種。一、非不現見，二、非已思應思，三、非錯亂境界。

Ru-Yi Translation

Direct perception: it has three kinds. First, it is not a case of failing to see directly; second, it is not a matter already deliberated on or yet to be deliberated on; and third, it is not a deceptive object.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses the three conditions plainly: the object must actually be present and directly cognized right now, not merely inferred (this rules out inference standing in for perception); the cognition must be this instant's bare contact, not a memory of a past object or an anticipation of a future one (this rules out thought and imagination masquerading as perception); and the cognizing sense faculty must not itself be disordered or deceived, whether by illness, drugs, or illusion (this rules out hallucination and trickery counting as perception). Together the three isolate perception's genuine core: an undistorted faculty, meeting a present object, with no intervening thought.

As background, he notes Dignāga's later, more technical fourfold division of what counts as direct perception — the five sense consciousnesses' bare first instant of contact; the accompanying sixth consciousness in that same instant, before naming and conceptualization begin; the mind's own reflexive awareness of its own cognition; and meditative consciousness, whether mundane concentration or the non-conceptual wisdom of the noble path. He treats this only as a later systematization useful for orientation — what follows here is the root text's own threefold framework, worked out condition by condition.

19. Not Failing to See Directly: When a Working Sense Faculty Actually Counts

The first of the three conditions, not failing to see directly, sets out what a healthy, unobstructed sense faculty actually requires to produce valid perception. The treatise names four requirements — matched realms, functioning organs, freedom from four kinds of obstruction, and freedom from three kinds of excessive distance — each explained in turn.

Chinese Source

非不現見現量者，復有四種。謂諸根不壞，作意現前，相似生故；超越生故；無障礙故；非極遠故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Not failing to see directly, as a condition of direct perception, again has four aspects: that the faculties are unimpaired and attention is present, because of matched arising; because of transcending arising; because there is no obstruction; and because it is not excessively distant.

Chinese Source

相似生者，謂欲界諸根於欲界境，上地諸根於上地境……是名相似生。超越生者，謂上地諸根於下地境，已生等如前說，是名超越生。

Ru-Yi Translation

Matched arising: it means desire-realm faculties meeting desire-realm objects, and higher-realm faculties meeting higher-realm objects... this is called matched arising. Transcending arising: it means higher-realm faculties meeting lower-realm objects, arising as explained above; this is called transcending arising.

Chinese Source

無障礙者，復有四種。一、非覆障所礙，二、非隱障所礙，三、非映障所礙，四、非惑障所礙……若不為此四障所礙，名無障礙。

Ru-Yi Translation

Free of obstruction again has four aspects: first, not hindered by a covering obstruction; second, not hindered by a concealing obstruction; third, not hindered by an eclipsing obstruction; and fourth, not hindered by a deluding obstruction... where none of these four kinds of obstruction applies, this is called freedom from obstruction.

Chinese Source

非極遠者，謂非三種極遠所遠。一、處極遠，二、時極遠，三、損減極遠……如是一切，總名非不現見。非不現故，名為現量。

Ru-Yi Translation

Not excessively distant means being distant in none of three ways: first, excessive distance of place; second, excessive distance of time; and third, excessive distance through diminution... All of this together is called not failing to see directly. Because it does not fail to see directly, it is called direct perception.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi works through each requirement concretely. Matched arising means a desire-realm eye sees desire-realm colors, a form-realm eye (available to one who has attained form-realm concentration) sees form-realm colors — faculty and object belong to the same cosmological tier. Transcending arising is the one-way exception: a practitioner who has attained form-realm concentration gains a "heavenly eye" or "heavenly ear" capable of perceiving desire-realm objects from above, though the reverse — a desire-realm faculty perceiving form-realm objects — never occurs without that same attainment.

The four obstructions he illustrates vividly: a covering obstruction is darkness itself, graded from a faint shadow through a general dimness to total blackness; a concealing obstruction is something rendered invisible outright, as by drugs, spells, or supernormal power (he cites Nāgārjuna's legendary invisibility potion); an eclipsing obstruction is one thing swamped by a larger or brighter one — a spice lost in a stew, starlight washed out by sunlight, an antidote-contemplation like impurity-meditation deliberately eclipsing its opposite (a meditator eclipsing the perception of "attractive" with the perception of the body's actual impurity); and a deluding obstruction is any perception corrupted by illness, dizziness, drunkenness, sleep, fainting, or a stage magician's sleight of hand exploiting the eye's inability to keep pace with fast motion. The three excessive distances are more straightforward: an object too far away to make out clearly, an event too far in the past or future to be presently perceived, and an object diminished past the threshold of perceptibility (the treatise's example is the theoretical partless particle, too fine ever to be directly seen). Only when all four requirements are satisfied together — matched or rightly transcended realm, no obstruction, no excessive distance — does a sense faculty's contact with its object count as genuinely direct.

20. Not Already Deliberated Nor Yet to Be Deliberated: The Instant Before Thought

The second condition isolates direct perception in time: it is neither a memory of something already thought over nor an anticipation of something yet to be considered, but the bare instant of present contact, prior to any conceptual overlay. The treatise gives two cases — one from ordinary sense contact, one from a meditator's cultivated visualization — to show what this instant actually looks like.

Chinese Source

非已思應思現量者，復有二種。一、纔取便成取所依境，二、建立境界取所依境。

Ru-Yi Translation

Not a matter already deliberated on or yet to be deliberated on, as a condition of direct perception, again has two aspects: first, an object that, the very instant it is grasped, already serves as the basis of that grasping; and second, an object established through visualization that serves as the basis of that visualization's grasping.

Chinese Source

猶如良醫授病者藥，色、香、味、觸皆悉圓滿，有大勢力，成就威德。當知此藥色、香、味、觸纔取便成取所依止。藥之所有大勢威德，病若未愈，名為應思；其病若愈，名為已思。

Ru-Yi Translation

It is like a skilled physician giving medicine to a patient — its color, smell, taste, and texture all complete, possessed of great potency, accomplished in efficacy. Know that this medicine's color, smell, taste, and texture, the very instant they are taken, already serve as the basis of that taking. But the medicine's great potency and efficacy — if the illness is not yet cured, this is called "yet to be deliberated"; if the illness is cured, this is called "already deliberated."

Chinese Source

如瑜伽師，於地思惟水、火、風界。若住於地思惟其水，即住地想轉作水想。若住於地思惟火、風，即住地想轉作火、風想……此中地想，即是建立境界之取。地者，即是建立境界取之所依。

Ru-Yi Translation

It is like a yogic practitioner who, from the earth-element, contemplates the water, fire, and wind elements. Abiding in the earth and contemplating its water, the perception of earth turns into the perception of water; abiding in the earth and contemplating its fire or wind, the perception of earth turns into the perception of fire or wind... Here, the perception of earth is precisely the grasping that establishes the object; the earth itself is precisely the basis of that grasping.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the medicine example as separating two different things happening at once when a patient takes medicine: the bare sensory contact with the medicine's color, smell, taste, and texture is direct perception, complete and immediate the instant it happens — but whether the medicine actually works is a separate question that can only be settled by watching over time, which is why "yet to be deliberated" describes the illness not yet cured and "already deliberated" describes it once cured. The point: perceiving an object directly and knowing its effects through observation over time are two entirely different cognitive acts, and only the first counts as direct perception.

The meditation example extends the same principle to a trained visualizer's inner practice: a yogin cultivating the earth-totality or the eight bases of mastery (already met in fascicles 11–12) first stabilizes a vivid mental image of the earth-element until it appears clearly and steadily — at which point that image itself, appearing without further deliberation, counts as a genuine object of direct perception for the meditating mind, exactly as a physically present object would for the eye. Once stabilized, the practitioner can shift the same clear, immediate perception to water, fire, or wind in turn. Cháng Bǎi stresses that before the image stabilizes it is still "yet to be deliberated" — effortful, constructed, uncertain — and only becomes "already deliberated," and hence a genuine current object of direct perception, once vividness and stability are actually achieved. The shared thread across both examples: whatever is immediately present to a properly functioning cognition, without depending on memory of the past or expectation of the future, is direct perception — everything else is inference layered on top of it.

21. Direct Perception Complete: Seven Errors to Avoid, Four Kinds Confirmed

The third and final condition, freedom from illusion, closes out the definition of direct perception by naming seven specific ways cognition can be deceived — five belonging to the senses, two belonging to the mind alone. With all three conditions now established, the treatise poses a closing question and answers it with a fourfold summary that ties the whole discussion together.

Chinese Source

非錯亂境界現量者，謂或五種、或七種。五種者，謂非五種錯亂境界。何等為五？一、想錯亂，二、數錯亂，三、形錯亂，四、顯錯亂，五、業錯亂。七種者，謂即前五，及餘二種遍行錯亂，合為七種。何等為二？一、心錯亂，二、見錯亂。

Ru-Yi Translation

Not a deceptive object, as a condition of direct perception, means being free of either five or seven kinds of deception. The five: what are they? First, deception of perception; second, deception of number; third, deception of shape; fourth, deception of color; and fifth, deception of function. The seven: these are the same five, together with two further deceptions that pervade all the rest, making seven in all. What are the two? First, deception of mind; and second, deception of view.

Chinese Source

想錯亂者，謂於非彼相，起彼相想。如於陽焰、鹿渴相中，起於水想。數錯亂者，謂於少數起多數增上慢。如翳眩者，於一月處見多月像。業錯亂者，謂於無業事起有業增上慢。如結拳馳走，見樹奔流。

Ru-Yi Translation

Deception of perception: taking up the perception of one thing where that thing is not present — as when a mirage, mistaken by thirsty deer, gives rise to the perception of water. Deception of number: an inflated conviction of many where there are few — as one with clouded, dazzled eyes sees many moons where there is one. Deception of function: an inflated conviction of activity where there is none — as one running with a clenched fist sees the trees themselves rushing past.

Chinese Source

心錯亂者，謂即於五種所錯亂義，心生喜樂。見錯亂者，謂即於五種所錯亂義，忍受、顯說、生吉祥想，堅執不捨。若非如是錯亂境界，名為現量。

Ru-Yi Translation

Deception of mind: taking delight in these same five deceptions. Deception of view: accepting these same five deceptions, proclaiming them, regarding them as auspicious, and holding to them firmly without letting go. Where the object is free of deception of this kind, it is called direct perception.

Chinese Source

問：如是現量，誰所有耶？答：略說四種所有。一、色根現量，二、意受現量，三、世間現量，四、清淨現量。世間現量者，謂即二種，總說為一世間現量。清淨現量者，謂諸所有世間現量，亦得名為清淨現量。或有清淨現量，非世間現量，謂出世智，於所行境，有知為有，無知為無，有上知有上，無上知無上。如是等類，名不共世間清淨現量。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: to whom, then, does direct perception of this kind belong? Answer: briefly, it belongs to four kinds of possessors. First, direct perception of the physical faculties; second, direct perception received by the mind; third, worldly direct perception; and fourth, pure direct perception. Worldly direct perception is these first two kinds together, spoken of as one. Pure direct perception refers to all worldly direct perception, which may equally be called pure direct perception — but there is also a pure direct perception that is not worldly direct perception: namely, transcendent wisdom which, regarding its object, knows what has being as having being and what has no being as having none, knows what is surpassable as surpassable and what is unsurpassed as unsurpassed. Direct perception of this kind is called pure direct perception not shared with the world.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the five sensory deceptions as a rogues' gallery of illusion familiar from everywhere in life: mistaking a shimmering mirage for actual water; a diseased eye seeing two moons where there is one; a spinning torch tracing a false ring of fire (deception of shape); jaundice or color-blindness painting everything the wrong hue (deception of color); and the seasick illusion of trees rushing backward past a moving vehicle (deception of function) — he adds the classic image of a fast-moving torch-tip appearing as a solid ring of flame for deception of shape specifically. The two "pervading" mental deceptions then compound any of the five: deception of mind is simply taking pleasure in an illusion once it has occurred, while deception of view is worse still — actively endorsing the illusion, telling others it is real, and clinging to it stubbornly even when shown otherwise. He notes the progression is deliberate: an honest mind that notices "that was just a mirage" has avoided deception of mind and view even though the initial sensory deception briefly occurred; real epistemic harm begins only once the false perception is embraced and defended.

On the closing fourfold summary, he shows how it maps directly back onto everything just covered: perception via the five physical senses and perception received by the mind (the two sub-cases of "not deliberated" from the previous section) together make up ordinary "worldly" direct perception, which is automatically also "pure" in the limited sense of being undistorted — but a further, higher pure direct perception exists that ordinary beings never touch: the non-conceptual wisdom of a noble one directly realizing the two truths, seeing existence and non-existence, the surpassable and the unsurpassed, exactly as they are. With this fourfold summary, the sixth means of proof — direct perception, the treatise's single longest topic — is now complete in full.

22. Inference Opens: Reasoning From Marks Already Seen

The seventh means of proof, inference, is the second source of valid knowledge — everything direct perception is not: a conclusion reached through deliberation rather than bare present contact. The treatise names five kinds of inference and works through each with a wealth of concrete examples; this section covers the definition and the first kind, inference from a characteristic mark.

Chinese Source

比量者，謂與思擇俱，已思應思所有境界。此復五種。一、相比量，二、體比量，三、業比量，四、法比量，五、因果比量。

Ru-Yi Translation

Inference: it refers to whatever object, joined with discerning deliberation, has already been deliberated on or is yet to be deliberated on. It again has five kinds: first, inference from characteristic marks; second, inference from essence; third, inference from function; fourth, inference from association; and fifth, inference from cause and effect.

Chinese Source

相比量者，謂隨所有相狀相屬，或由現在，或先所見，推度境界。如見幢故，比知有車；由見煙故，比知有火……以執持自相，比知道俗；以樂觀聖者、樂聞正法、遠離慳貪，比知正信……以具如來微妙相好、智慧、寂靜、正行、神通，比知如來、應、等正覺具一切智。

Ru-Yi Translation

Inference from characteristic marks: it means, following some characteristic mark bound up with a thing, whether seen now or seen before, reasoning one's way to the object itself. As seeing a banner, one infers there is a chariot; seeing smoke, one infers there is fire... By what someone holds and carries, one infers whether they are a renunciant or a layperson; by delight in seeing the noble, delight in hearing the true Dharma, and freedom from stinginess, one infers genuine faith... By possessing the Tathāgata's sublime marks, wisdom, tranquility, right conduct, and supernormal powers, one infers a Tathāgata, worthy of offerings, a perfectly awakened one possessed of all-knowing wisdom.

Chinese Source

以於老時，見彼幼年所有相狀，比知是彼。如是等類，名相比量。

Ru-Yi Translation

By seeing, in someone's old age, the traces of the features they had in youth, one infers this is the same person. Discourse of this kind is called inference from characteristic marks.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi runs through the treatise's own long chain of examples, which moves deliberately from the mundane to the spiritually significant: smoke signaling fire is the tradition's textbook case, but the list quickly turns toward reading character and attainment from outward signs — a disorganized husband suggesting a neglectful wife, soft skin and restless energy suggesting youth, wrinkles and white hair suggesting old age, monastic robes and a shaved head suggesting a renunciant. It climbs higher still: delight in noble company and freedom from stinginess inferring genuine faith; skillful thought, speech, and action inferring wisdom; compassion, kind speech, courage, and generosity inferring a bodhisattva (he cross-references the five-fold bodhisattva-mark teaching from the Bodhisattva Ground); restlessness and frivolity inferring one still bound by desire, constant composure inferring one free of it; and, at the summit, the thirty-two marks, boundless wisdom, and six supernormal powers inferring a fully awakened Buddha. He draws out the shared logical shape beneath every example: a visible mark reliably tracks something not directly visible because the two are bound together by real connection, not mere coincidence — which is exactly why the very last example in the passage, recognizing an old man from his childhood features, works: the youthful mark and the aged face are causally continuous traces of a single life, not two unrelated things.

23. Inference Complete: From Essence, Function, Association, and Cause to Effect

The remaining four kinds of inference close out the treatise's treatment of reasoning: from a known portion of something to its unknown remainder, from an observed effect back to its cause and forward again, from a perceivable activity to what must be producing it, and from one member of a tightly linked pair to the other. With all five complete, inference itself is finished.

Chinese Source

體比量者，謂現見彼自體性故，比類彼物不現見體；或現見彼一分自體，比類餘分。如以現在比類過去，或以過去比類未來……又如飲食、衣服、嚴具、車乘等事，觀見一分得失之相，比知一切。又以一分成熟，比餘熟分。如是等類，名體比量。

Ru-Yi Translation

Inference from essence: seeing directly some thing's own nature, one infers the nature of that same thing where it is not directly seen; or seeing directly one part of its nature, one infers the remaining parts. As inferring the past from the present, or the future from the past... Or again, as with food, clothing, ornaments, and vehicles: observing in one instance a mark of gain or loss, one infers the same of all; or from one part being ripe, one infers the rest is ripe as well. Discourse of this kind is called inference from essence.

Chinese Source

業比量者，謂以作用比業所依。如見遠物無有動搖，鳥居其上，由是等事比知是杌；若有動搖等事，比知是人……見比於眼，聞比於耳，齅比於鼻，嘗比於舌，觸比於身，識比於意……若見熱灰，比知有火；叢林掉動，比知有風……如是等類以業比度，如前應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

Inference from function: it means inferring what an activity depends on from the activity itself. As seeing a distant object entirely without movement, with a bird perched upon it, one infers from this that it is a dead stump; but where there is movement, one infers it is a person... The act of seeing implies an eye; hearing implies an ear; smelling implies a nose; tasting implies a tongue; touching implies a body; cognizing implies a mind... Seeing hot ash, one infers there was fire; a grove swaying, one infers there is wind... Inference of this kind, by function, should be understood as before.

Chinese Source

法比量者，謂以相鄰相屬之法，比餘相鄰相屬之法。如屬無常，比知有苦；以屬苦故，比空、無我。以屬生故，比有老法；以屬老故，比有死法……如是等類，名法比量。

Ru-Yi Translation

Inference from association: it means inferring one thing bound up with another from a thing already closely bound up with it. As what belongs to impermanence, one infers belongs to suffering; what belongs to suffering, one infers is empty and without self. What belongs to birth, one infers has the nature of aging; what belongs to aging, one infers has the nature of death... Discourse of this kind is called inference from association.

Chinese Source

因果比量者，謂以因果展轉相比。如見有行，比至餘方；見至餘方，比先有行……見有靜慮，比知離欲；見離欲者，比有靜慮。若見修道，比知當獲沙門果證；若見有獲沙門果證，比知修道。如是等類，當知總名因果比量。是名比量。

Ru-Yi Translation

Inference from cause and effect: it means inferring, cause and effect turning upon each other, from one to the other. As seeing someone walking, one infers they will arrive elsewhere; seeing they have arrived elsewhere, one infers they were walking before... Seeing meditative concentration, one infers freedom from desire; seeing one free of desire, one infers meditative concentration. Seeing one cultivate the path, one infers they will attain the fruit of a renunciant; seeing one who has attained that fruit, one infers they cultivated the path. Discourse of this kind should be known, all together, as inference from cause and effect. This is called inference.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes inference from essence works two ways: extending a known trait across time (an elephant observed today tells us what elephants looked like in the past) or across parts (tasting one bite of noodles tells the cook whether the whole pot is done). Inference from function runs through nineteen vivid cases in the source — a motionless silhouette with a bird perched on it is a dead stump, not a person; a swaying, S-shaped track through the grass is a snake, not a straight-walking animal; a long whinny is a horse, a roar is a lion; ash still hot proves fire was there; a swaying grove proves wind — and extends the same logic inward, inferring each sense faculty from its distinctive function (sight implies an eye, hearing an ear), and outward to spiritual attainment exactly as inference from marks did in the prior section.

Inference from association he distinguishes from inference from marks by tightness of connection: two things are "associated" when one's very meaning entails the other, not merely resembles it — impermanence entails suffering, suffering entails no fixed self, birth entails aging, aging entails death, each link following necessarily rather than by loose family resemblance. Inference from cause and effect, the last and in his view the clearest, works symmetrically in both directions at once: walking implies arrival and arrival implies prior walking; serving a ruler faithfully implies future rank and visible rank implies past faithful service; meditative stability implies freedom from desire and freedom from desire implies meditative stability. With all five kinds of inference now named — from marks, from essence, from function, from association, and from cause and effect — the seventh means of proof is complete, and the treatise turns next to the third and final source of valid knowledge: scriptural authority.

24. Scriptural Authority Opens: The Third Source of Valid Knowledge

The eighth and final means of proof, scriptural authority, completes the trio of valid knowledge alongside direct perception and inference. It covers teaching that traces back to the Buddha's own complete wisdom, whether spoken by him directly or transmitted faithfully by those who realized it after him — and the treatise names three further conditions a transmitted teaching must meet to count as genuinely reliable.

Chinese Source

正教量者，謂一切智所說言教，或從彼聞，或隨彼法。

Ru-Yi Translation

Scriptural authority: it refers to the teaching spoken by the all-knowing one, or what is heard directly from him, or what accords with his teaching.

Chinese Source

此復三種。一、不違聖言，二、能治雜染，三、不違法相。不違聖言者，謂聖弟子說，或佛自說經教，展轉流布至今，不違正法、不違正義。能治雜染者，謂隨此法善修習時，能永調伏貪瞋癡等一切煩惱及隨煩惱。不違法相者，謂翻違法相，當知即是不違法相。

Ru-Yi Translation

This again has three kinds: first, not contradicting the sacred word; second, capable of curing defilement; and third, not contradicting the nature of things. Not contradicting the sacred word: whether spoken by a noble disciple or by the Buddha himself, and transmitted down to the present, it does not contradict the true Dharma or its true meaning. Capable of curing defilement: when this teaching is well practiced, it is capable of permanently subduing craving, aversion, delusion, and all other afflictions great and small. Not contradicting the nature of things: its character should be known as the reverse of contradicting the nature of things.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies three sources folded into "the teaching spoken by the all-knowing one": the Buddha's own words, transmitted across the generations to the present; a noble disciple's report of what they personally heard from him; and a later disciple's own teaching, arrived at by applying the Buddha's method — careful reflection on what was heard — even without having heard the original words directly. He notes the Compendium of Determinations distinguishes ordinary disciples, still learning through much study, from noble disciples who have already directly seen the truths; both kinds of transmission can carry scriptural authority; what matters is fidelity to the source, not the transmitter's own attainment.

Of the three further conditions, he treats the second as the real test in practice: authentic Dharma, sincerely practiced, actually weakens craving, aversion, and delusion over time — a teaching that leaves the practitioner's afflictions untouched, however scripturally dressed, fails this test regardless of its pedigree. The third condition, not contradicting the nature of things, is the largest and most technical of the three — the treatise devotes an extended passage to six specific ways a teaching can misrepresent reality even while claiming Buddhist authority, which the next section takes up directly.

25. Six Ways to Misrepresent Reality — And the Basis of Discourse Completes

The treatise closes its account of scriptural authority — and with it, the entire basis of discourse, the largest of the seven headings on logic — by naming six specific ways a teaching can distort the nature of things even while sounding orthodox. Each pairs a false move with its opposite, and together they read as a checklist for detecting corrupted doctrine.

Chinese Source

何等名為違法相耶？謂於無相，增為有相。如執有我、有情、命者、生者等類；或常、或斷，有色、無色，如是等類。或於有相，減為無相。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is called contradicting the nature of things? It means, regarding what has no real mark, augmenting it into having one — as in holding there to be a self, a sentient being, a life, a born one, and so on; or holding it permanent, or annihilated, possessing form, or formless, and so on. Or, regarding what has a real mark, diminishing it into having none.

Chinese Source

或於決定，立為不定。如一切行皆是無常，一切有漏皆性是苦，一切諸法皆空無我，而妄建立一分是常，一分無常……於佛所立不可記法，尋求記別，謂為可記，或安立記。或於不定，建立為定。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or, regarding what is fixed, positing it as unfixed — as with all conditioned things being impermanent, all that is defiled being of the nature of suffering, all things being empty and without self, one falsely holds that some part is permanent and some part impermanent... Or, seeking to pronounce upon matters the Buddha established as beyond pronouncement, holding them pronounceable, or actually pronouncing upon them. Or, regarding what is unfixed, positing it as fixed.

Chinese Source

或於有相法中，無差別相，建立差別；有差別相，立無差別。又於有相，不如正理立因果相。如立妙行感不愛果，立諸惡行感可愛果。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or, within things that have marks, positing differentiation where there is none, or positing no differentiation where there is. And, regarding things with marks, positing the relation of cause and effect contrary to correct principle — as positing that wholesome conduct brings an undesirable result, and unwholesome conduct brings a desirable one.

Chinese Source

計惡說法毗奈耶中，習諸邪行，能得清淨；於善說法毗奈耶中，修行正行，謂為雜染。於不實相，以假言說立真實相；於真實相，以假言說種種安立。如是等類，名違法相。與此相違，當知即是不違法相。是名正教。

Ru-Yi Translation

Holding that practicing wrong conduct within a wrongly taught doctrine and discipline yields purity, while practicing right conduct within a well-taught doctrine and discipline yields defilement. Establishing a merely provisional designation as the true mark of what is not truly marked; and, regarding what is truly marked, elaborating it through various provisional designations. Discourse of this kind is called contradicting the nature of things. The reverse of this should be known as not contradicting the nature of things. This is called scriptural authority.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through the six as a diagnostic sequence. First, inventing a self where there is none — every variety of "I," permanent or annihilated, embodied or formless, is a false addition to the five aggregates, which contain no such owner. Second, its mirror error: denying real distinctions that do exist, which he identifies specifically with the "nihilistic emptiness" some Mahāyāna students fall into — treating dependent origination and its causal texture as themselves illusory rather than as precisely how emptiness actually operates. He retells the well-known Chan story of the fox-spirit elder to make the point vivid: a teacher who once denied that a great practitioner still falls under cause and effect was reborn a fox for five hundred lives for that single wrong word, freed only when a later master corrected it to "not obscured by cause and effect" rather than "not subject to" it — the moral being that even a small doctrinal error about causality carries real weight.

Third, treating what the Buddha stated flatly (impermanence, suffering, non-self, as universal truths) as merely partial — and its opposite, treating what the Buddha deliberately left unanswered (whether the world is eternal, whether a Tathāgata exists after death, and the other classic fourteen unanswerable questions) as something requiring a definite answer after all; Cháng Bǎi notes the Buddha's silence on these was itself principled, not evasive, since answering would either encourage further false views or simply serve no purpose for liberation. Fourth, confusing what does and does not admit further differentiation — such as claiming conditioned and unconditioned phenomena are ultimately no different from each other — and getting causality backward, as claiming good conduct brings suffering and bad conduct brings happiness. Fifth, reversing purity and defilement by claiming wrong practice under a false teaching purifies while right practice under the true teaching defiles. Sixth, confusing the provisional and the ultimate — treating a merely conventional label as if it captured reality's true, ineffable nature, the classic error of mistaking the finger pointing at the moon for the moon itself.

With these six errors named and their reverses confirmed as the standard for "not contradicting the nature of things," scriptural authority — the eighth and final means of proof — is complete. This closes the entire basis of discourse (論所依), the third of the seven headings on logic and by far its largest, spanning both kinds of thesis and all eight means of establishing it. The treatise next turns to a closing question-and-answer clarifying how these pieces fit together, before moving to the fourth heading: the adornment of discourse.

26. Closing Questions: Why Each Piece Comes in Its Order — The Basis of Discourse Concludes

Having named all ten parts of the basis of discourse, the treatise steps back and asks why each piece belongs where it does — why posit a thesis at all, if things already are what they are regardless of language; why reason before example; why example before the remaining five means of proof. The answers tie the whole eight-part structure into a single logical sequence, and close the largest of the seven headings on logic.

Chinese Source

問：若一切法自相成就，各自安立己法性中，復何因緣建立二種所成義耶？答：為欲令他生信解故，非為生成諸法性相。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: if all things are already established in their own nature, each abiding in its own inherent character, then for what reason does one establish the two kinds of meaning to be established? Answer: it is in order to generate confidence and understanding in others — not in order to generate the nature or mark of things themselves.

Chinese Source

問：為欲成就所成立義，何故先立宗耶？答：為先顯示自所愛樂宗義故。問：何故次辯因耶？答：為欲開顯依現見事決定道理，令他攝受所立宗義故。問：何故次引喻耶？答：為欲顯示能成道理之所依止現見事故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: in order to accomplish the meaning to be established, why does one posit the thesis first? Answer: in order first to display the thesis one cherishes oneself. Question: why does one next distinguish the reason? Answer: in order to unfold, resting on directly observed fact, a settled principle, so as to bring another to accept the thesis posited. Question: why does one next cite the example? Answer: in order to display the directly observed fact on which the reasoning's principle itself depends.

Chinese Source

問：何故後說同類、異類、現量、比量、正教等耶？答：為欲開示因喻二種相違、不相違智故。又相違者，由二因緣。一、不決定故；二、同所成故。不相違者，亦二因緣。一、決定故；二、異所成故。其相違者……不能為量，故不名量。不相違者……能為正量，故名為量。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: why does one then speak of the similar class, the dissimilar class, direct perception, inference, and scriptural authority? Answer: it is in order to reveal the knowledge of whether the reason and example are, or are not, in conflict with the thesis. Being in conflict comes from two causes: first, being indeterminate; second, merely restating what was to be established in the first place. Not being in conflict likewise comes from two causes: first, being determinate; second, being genuinely distinct from what was to be established. What is in conflict... cannot serve as valid knowledge, and so is not called valid knowledge. What is not in conflict... can serve as genuinely valid knowledge, and so is called valid knowledge.

Chinese Source

是名論所依。

Ru-Yi Translation

This is called the basis of discourse.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the opening question as the whole heading's real justification: things are exactly what they are, with or without anyone naming them — sound was impermanent long before any debater said so — so why bother positing a thesis and proving it at all? His answer, following the root text closely, is that language and proof exist entirely for the listener's sake, not for reality's: an opponent or student who has not yet seen a truth needs it demonstrated in terms they can verify for themselves, which is exactly why the treatise elsewhere cross-references the Bodhisattva Ground's teaching that ultimate reality is beyond language, and yet language remains indispensable as the raft that carries a listener to the point where they can finally see for themselves and no longer need the raft.

He then reads the ordering of the remaining questions as strict logical necessity, not mere convention: the thesis must come first because a debate needs a stated position before anything else is possible; the reason comes next because a bare thesis persuades no one without grounds; the example comes third because even a stated reason means nothing until it is anchored to some fact both sides already accept; and only once thesis, reason, and example are all on the table does it make sense to ask the deeper question — whether the reason and example actually support the thesis or merely restate it in disguise, which is exactly what the similar class, dissimilar class, and the three sources of valid knowledge exist to test. A reason is "in conflict" with its thesis, he explains, either when it is genuinely indeterminate — equally compatible with the thesis being true or false — or when it turns out to be nothing more than the thesis said again in different words, proving nothing new; a reason is "not in conflict" only when it is both determinate and genuinely independent of what it is meant to prove. Only reasons and examples that pass this test count as valid means of knowledge at all — everything else is merely apparent proof, persuasive in form but empty underneath.

With this closing question-and-answer, the basis of discourse (論所依) — the third of the seven headings on logic, covering both kinds of thesis and all eight means of establishing it, and by a wide margin the largest single topic in this fascicle — is now complete in full. The treatise turns next to the fourth heading: the adornment of discourse.

27. The Adornment of Discourse Opens: Mastering Both Positions, Perfecting Speech

With the basis of discourse complete, the treatise turns to its fourth heading: the adornment of discourse — the personal qualities that make a debater genuinely persuasive rather than merely technically correct. Five qualities are named; this section covers the first two: mastering one's own position and the opponent's alike, and speech perfected in both substance and delivery.

Chinese Source

論莊嚴者，略有五種。一、善自他宗，二、言具圓滿，三、無畏，四、敦肅，五、應供。

Ru-Yi Translation

The adornment of discourse: briefly, it has five kinds. First, mastery of one's own and other positions; second, speech perfected in every respect; third, fearlessness; fourth, dignified restraint; and fifth, being worthy of respect.

Chinese Source

善自他宗者，謂如有一，若於此法毗奈耶中深生愛樂，即於此論宗旨，讀誦、受持、聽聞、思惟、純熟修行，已善、已說、已明。若於彼法毗奈耶中不愛不樂，然於彼論宗旨，讀誦、受持、聞思純熟，而不修行，然已善、已說、已明。是名善自他宗。

Ru-Yi Translation

Mastery of one's own and other positions: it means that one who deeply delights in this Dharma and discipline reads, recites, upholds, hears, reflects on, and ripens in practicing its own thesis, until it is thoroughly mastered, thoroughly articulable, and thoroughly clear. And though taking no delight in another's dharma and discipline, one still reads, recites, upholds, hears, and reflects on that thesis until thoroughly ripened — without practicing it — so that it too is thoroughly mastered, thoroughly articulable, and thoroughly clear. This is called mastery of one's own and other positions.

Chinese Source

言具圓滿者，謂如有一，凡有所說，皆以其聲，不以非聲。何等為聲？謂具五德，乃名為聲。一、不鄙陋，二、輕易，三、雄朗，四、相應，五、義善。

Ru-Yi Translation

Speech perfected in every respect: it means that whatever one says, one always speaks with genuine speech, never with what falls short of it. What is genuine speech? Only speech possessing five virtues deserves the name: first, free of coarseness; second, readily understood; third, vigorous and clear; fourth, coherent; and fifth, wholesome in meaning.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads mastery of both positions as a discipline of genuine intellectual honesty: a practitioner studies and practices their own tradition out of real conviction, but studies an opponent's position with equal rigor purely to understand it accurately — reading, reciting, and reflecting on it without ever practicing it — because only a debater who actually understands what the other side believes can identify where it goes wrong, rather than attacking a caricature. He notes this cuts against a natural temptation to dismiss an unfamiliar teaching without first learning it well enough to critique it fairly.

On speech's five virtues, he glosses each briefly: free of coarseness means avoiding crude regional slang; readily understood means using vocabulary the audience actually shares; vigorous and clear means language shaped skillfully enough to carry real persuasive force, not just correct content; coherent means the thesis, reason, and example stay logically connected from start to finish rather than drifting; and wholesome in meaning means the discourse actually leads a listener toward a better rebirth or, better still, toward liberation itself — rather than toward the distorted views of permanence, pleasure, self, and purity the treatise has repeatedly warned against. He adds that the treatise goes on to specify nine further marks of well-formed speech (freedom from disorder, from coarseness, clarity, proper measure, coherence with the meaning, correct timing, decisiveness, clarity, and continuity) which together produce these same five virtues — refined technique in service of genuinely persuasive, genuinely truthful communication, not eloquence for its own sake.

28. The Adornment of Discourse Completes: Fearlessness, Dignity, and Twenty-Seven Praised Virtues

The remaining three qualities close out the adornment of discourse: fearlessness before any audience, dignified restraint in timing one's words, and a bearing genuinely worthy of respect. The treatise then names twenty-seven specific virtues that flow from cultivating all five together, sealed with a closing simile, and the fourth heading is complete.

Chinese Source

無畏者，謂如有一，處在多眾、雜眾、大眾、執眾、諦眾、善眾等中，其心無有下劣憂懼，身無戰汗，面無怖色，音無謇吃，語無怯弱。如是說者，名為無畏。敦肅者，謂如有一，待時方說，而不儳速。是名敦肅。

Ru-Yi Translation

Fearlessness: it means that, standing before a large assembly, a mixed assembly, a royal assembly, a judging assembly, a wise assembly, or a virtuous assembly, one's mind is free of inferior anxiety, one's body does not tremble or sweat, one's face shows no fear, one's voice does not stammer, and one's speech shows no timidity. Speaking in this way is called fearlessness. Dignified restraint: it means waiting for the right moment to speak, never blurting out words hastily. This is called dignified restraint.

Chinese Source

應供者，謂如有一，為性調善，不惱於他；終不違越諸調善者調善之地；隨順他心而起言說；以時，如實，能引義利；言詞柔軟，如對善友。是名應供。

Ru-Yi Translation

Worthy of respect: it means that one is, by nature, well-tamed and gentle, never troubling others; never straying from the ground proper to those who are well-tamed; speaking in accord with another's state of mind; speaking at the right time, truthfully, and in a way that brings real benefit; and speaking with words so gentle it is as though addressing a cherished friend. This is called being worthy of respect.

Chinese Source

若有依此五論莊嚴興言論者，當知復有二十七種稱讚功德。一、眾所敬重；二、言必信受；三、處大眾中都無所畏……十七、辯才無盡……二十五、令對論者心生淨信；二十六、凡有所行，不招怨對；二十七、廣大名稱，聲流十方，世咸傳唱此大法師，處大師數。

Ru-Yi Translation

One who takes up discourse resting on these five adornments should be known to gain, in addition, twenty-seven praised virtues: first, being honored by all; second, one's word being trusted and accepted without question; third, complete fearlessness before any assembly... seventeenth, inexhaustible eloquence... twenty-fifth, inspiring genuine faith in one's opponent; twenty-sixth, that whatever one does draws no enmity; and twenty-seventh, a reputation so vast it spreads in every direction, until the world speaks of this person as a great master, counted among the ranks of the great teachers.

Chinese Source

如受欲者，以末尼、真珠、琉璃等寶，廁環釧等寶莊嚴具，以自莊嚴，威德熾盛，光明普照。如是論者，以二十七稱讚功德，廁此五種論莊嚴具，以自莊嚴，威德熾盛，光明普照，是故名此為論莊嚴。

Ru-Yi Translation

It is like one who enjoys sensual pleasures, adorning themselves with jewels of beryl, pearl, and lapis lazuli set among bracelets and rings, so that their splendor blazes forth and their radiance shines everywhere. Just so, one engaged in discourse, adorning themselves with these twenty-seven praised virtues set among these five adornments of discourse, has splendor that blazes forth and radiance that shines everywhere. This is why this is called the adornment of discourse.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps fearlessness across the same six assemblies named earlier as fitting venues for debate — the crowd, the mixed human-and-celestial assembly, the royal court, the judges, the wise, and the learned — noting that genuine fearlessness always shows in three registers together: the mind (no inward dread), the body (no visible trembling), and speech (no stammering); a debater who is calm in mind but still shakes visibly, he notes, has not yet actually mastered fear. Dignified restraint he reads as simple patience — never interrupting, never rushing to speak before the moment is actually right. Being worthy of respect he connects to the classical five qualities required of anyone empowered to point out another's fault within the monastic community — speaking only at the right time, only about what is true, only for real benefit, only gently, and only from a mind of genuine goodwill — the same standard, he notes, that separates constructive correction from mere criticism.

On the twenty-seven virtues, he treats them as a single organic consequence of cultivating the five adornments together rather than twenty-seven separate achievements to pursue one by one — natural authority, trustworthiness, tireless composure, an unshakable memory for both one's own and an opponent's position, a voice that never tires even across a full day of debate, and ultimately a reputation earning the title "great master" (阿闍梨/ācārya-level renown). The closing simile — comparing these virtues to jewelry that makes an already handsome person radiant — makes the same point the entire heading has been building toward: none of these twenty-seven virtues is decoration bolted onto discourse from outside; they are what genuinely skillful, sincere discourse simply looks like from the outside, the way real splendor is not manufactured by jewels but only revealed by them. With this, the adornment of discourse — the fourth of the seven headings on logic — is complete, and the treatise turns to its darker mirror image: the fifth heading, discourse's defeat.

29. Discourse's Defeat Opens: Thirteen Ways to Formally Concede

The fifth heading turns from what makes discourse shine to its opposite: how a debate is actually lost. Three kinds of defeat are named — formally conceding, being visibly cornered, and speech itself carrying a disqualifying fault. This section covers the first: the treatise's remarkably specific list of thirteen set phrases by which a debater formally admits defeat.

Chinese Source

論墮負者，謂有三種。一、捨言，二、言屈，三、言過。捨言者，謂立論者，以十三種詞謝對論者，捨所言論。

Ru-Yi Translation

Discourse's defeat: it has three kinds. First, formally conceding; second, being verbally cornered; and third, speech itself carrying a fault. Formally conceding: it means that the one positing the thesis addresses the opponent with thirteen kinds of words, thereby abandoning the discourse advanced.

Chinese Source

謂立論者謝對論者曰：我論不善，汝論為善；我不善觀，汝為善觀；我論無理，汝論有理；我論無能，汝論有能；我論屈伏，汝論成立；我之辯才唯極於此，過此已上更善思量，當為汝說；且置是事，我不復言。

Ru-Yi Translation

The one positing the thesis addresses the opponent, saying: my discourse is unskillful, yours is skillful; I did not observe well, you observed well; my discourse has no principle, yours has principle; my discourse has no capacity, yours has capacity; my discourse is overcome, yours stands established; my eloquence reaches only this far — beyond this, I would need to reflect further before I could speak to you; let this matter rest — I will say no more.

Chinese Source

以如是等十三種詞謝對論者，捨所言論。捨所論故，當知被破，為他所勝，墮在他後，屈伏於彼。是故捨言，名墮負處。

Ru-Yi Translation

Addressing the opponent with these thirteen kinds of words, one abandons the discourse advanced. Having abandoned it, know that one is thereby refuted, overcome by the other, fallen behind, and subdued by them. This is why formally conceding is called falling into defeat.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi groups the thirteen set phrases into six paired admissions plus a closing formula: unskillful versus skillful discourse, poor versus good observation of the logical relations at stake, lacking versus possessing principle, lacking versus possessing capacity in one's reason and example, and one's own position overcome versus the opponent's standing established — each pair naming exactly what failed, so that conceding defeat is itself an act of intellectual honesty rather than mere capitulation. The thirteenth and final formula, "let this matter rest, I will say no more," he treats as the classical, dignified way of ending a debate entirely rather than trailing off. He illustrates the spirit of it with a modern instance: the early-twentieth-century scholar Ouyang Jingwu, who debated Dharma-master Fazun repeatedly and reportedly told his own students near the end of his life that he had lost and the matter should be dropped — precisely the same graceful concession the treatise formalizes here nearly two millennia earlier. Formal concession, Cháng Bǎi stresses, is actually the best of the three ways to lose: it ends the exchange cleanly, without the flailing self-defense the next section describes.

30. Discourse's Defeat Completes: Visible Collapse and the Nine Faults of Speech

The remaining two kinds of defeat close out the fifth heading: thirteen visible signs of a debater cornered but refusing to formally concede, and nine specific faults that can disqualify speech outright regardless of how the exchange ends. With both covered, discourse's defeat — and the darker half of the treatise's account of debate — is complete.

Chinese Source

言屈者，如立論者為對論者之所屈伏，或託餘事方便而退，或引外言，或現憤發，或現瞋恚，或現憍慢，或現所覆，或現惱害，或現不忍，或現不信，或復默然，或復憂慼，或竦肩伏面，或沈思詞窮。

Ru-Yi Translation

Being verbally cornered: as when one positing a thesis, overcome by the opponent, resorts to some pretext and withdraws under that cover, or brings in irrelevant talk, or displays outburst, or displays hostility, or displays arrogance, or exposes the other's hidden faults, or displays malice, or displays refusal to accept defeat, or displays disbelief, or falls silent, or falls into visible dejection, or slumps the shoulders and lowers the face, or sinks into thought with words utterly exhausted.

Chinese Source

由如是等十三種事，當知言屈。前二，妄行矯亂；中七，發起邪行；後四，計行窮盡。是名言屈，墮在負處。

Ru-Yi Translation

By these thirteen signs, know this is being verbally cornered. The first two are a false attempt to confuse the exchange; the middle seven are the arising of misconduct; the last four are the exhaustion of one's own resources. This is called being verbally cornered, and it falls into defeat.

Chinese Source

言過者，謂立論者為九種過汙染其言，故名言過。何等為九？一、雜亂，二、麤獷，三、不辯了，四、無限量，五、非義相應，六、不以時，七、不決定，八、不顯了，九、不相續。

Ru-Yi Translation

Speech itself carrying a fault: it means the one positing the thesis has their speech tainted by nine kinds of fault, and so it is called a fault of speech. What are the nine? First, disorder; second, coarseness; third, failing to make oneself understood; fourth, lacking proper measure; fifth, incoherence with the meaning; sixth, wrong timing; seventh, indecisiveness; eighth, lack of clarity; and ninth, discontinuity.

Chinese Source

雜亂者，謂捨所論事，雜說異語。不辯了者，謂若法若義，眾及對論所不領悟。不決定者，謂立已復毀，毀而復立，速疾轉換，難可了知。凡所言論犯此九失，是名言過，墮在負處。

Ru-Yi Translation

Disorder: abandoning the matter under discussion and mixing in unrelated talk. Failing to make oneself understood: neither the statement nor its meaning is grasped by the assembly or by the opponent. Indecisiveness: positing a point, then retracting it, then positing it again, switching back and forth so rapidly it becomes impossible to follow. Whatever discourse falls into these nine faults is called a fault of speech, and it falls into defeat.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi sorts the thirteen visible signs of being cornered into the treatise's own three groups: the first two — inventing a pretext to withdraw, or steering the conversation onto food, gossip, or scandal — are attempts to dodge the loss rather than face it; the middle seven — outburst, hostility, arrogance, exposing the opponent's own past faults, malice, stubborn refusal to accept the verdict, open disbelief — are misconduct that lashes out rather than concedes; and the last four — falling silent, visible dejection, a slumped and downcast posture, and sitting in thought with genuinely nothing left to say — are simple exhaustion, no longer performing anything, just out of resources. He notes the progression is itself instructive: from active evasion, through active aggression, to passive collapse — three different ways of failing to do what the previous section's thirteen dignified phrases do cleanly.

The nine faults of speech he reads as the exact photographic negative of the nine marks of perfected speech from the adornment of discourse (section 27): where that heading asked for order, gentleness, clarity, proper measure, coherence, right timing, decisiveness, transparency, and continuity, this heading names their failures one for one — and singles out the ten sub-varieties of "incoherence with meaning" (the fifth fault) as the largest and most consequential, since a reason or example that fails to actually connect to its own thesis, however fluently delivered, has failed at the one thing debate exists to do. With formal concession, visible cornering, and disqualifying speech all now covered, discourse's defeat — the fifth of the seven headings on logic — is complete, and the treatise turns to its next heading: discourse's withdrawal, on whether a debate is even worth entering in the first place.

31. Discourse's Withdrawal Opens: Weighing Gain and Loss Before Speaking

The sixth heading turns from how to win or lose a debate to a more basic question: whether to enter one at all. Discourse's withdrawal names three self-examinations a would-be debater should complete before speaking a word — this section covers the first: an unflinching accounting of what a debate stands to gain or cost, for oneself, for the opponent, and for everyone watching.

Chinese Source

論出離者，謂立論者，先應以彼三種觀察觀察論端，方興言論，或不興論，名論出離。三種觀察者，一、觀察得失，二、觀察時眾，三、觀察善巧及不善巧。

Ru-Yi Translation

Discourse's withdrawal: it means that one positing a thesis should first apply three kinds of examination at the outset of discourse, and only then either take up discourse or refrain from it — this is called discourse's withdrawal. The three examinations: first, examining gain and loss; second, examining the assembly at hand; and third, examining one's own skill or lack of it.

Chinese Source

觀察得失者，謂立論者方興論端，先當觀察我立是論，將無自損、損他及俱損耶？不生現法後法及俱罪耶？勿起身心諸憂苦耶？莫由此故，執持刀杖、鬥罵諍訟、諂誑妄語而發起耶？將無種種惡不善法而生長耶？非不利益安樂若自若他及多眾耶？非不憐愍諸世間耶？不因此故，諸天世人無義無利不安樂耶？

Ru-Yi Translation

Examining gain and loss: it means that, before beginning discourse, one should first ask: will this discourse harm myself, or harm the other, or harm us both? Will it produce fault in this life, in future lives, or in both? Will it stir up affliction of body or mind? Will it, through this very act, lead to violence, quarreling, deceit, and false speech? Will it cause various unwholesome states to grow? Will it fail to bring benefit and ease to myself, to the other, and to the many? Will it fail to come from compassion for the world? And will it, because of this, leave gods and humans without benefit, without gain, and without ease?

Chinese Source

彼立論者如是觀時，若自了知我所立論，能為自損，乃至天人無義無利，亦無安樂；便自思勉，不應立論。若如是知我所立論，不為自損，乃至能引天人義利及與安樂；便自思勉，當立正論。是名第一或作不作論出離相。

Ru-Yi Translation

When one positing a thesis examines in this way, if one recognizes that this discourse would harm oneself and, further, bring no benefit, no gain, and no ease to gods and humans, one should exert oneself in reflection and not take up the discourse. But if one recognizes that this discourse would not harm oneself and, further, would bring benefit, gain, and ease to gods and humans, one should exert oneself in reflection and take up right discourse. This is called the first mark of discourse's withdrawal, concerning whether or not to act.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the eight questions as splitting cleanly into two groups: five checks against harm (to self, to other, to both; to present or future consequence; to one's own peace of mind; to the risk of provoking real violence or deceit; to unwholesome states taking root) and three checks for actual benefit (to oneself, the other, and the wider audience; to whether compassion, not the wish to win, is the motive; to whether it genuinely serves the welfare of gods and humans, not merely a debater's ego). The rule that follows is symmetrical and, he notes, deliberately not about winning: a discourse that fails the harm-checks or lacks the benefit-checks should not be undertaken at all regardless of whether the debater is confident of winning it, while a discourse that clears both should be undertaken as a positive duty — protecting and clarifying the true teaching — even where the outcome is uncertain. The entire first examination, in other words, asks not "can I win this argument" but "should this argument happen at all."

32. Discourse's Withdrawal Completes: Reading the Room, Then Reading Oneself

The remaining two examinations close out discourse's withdrawal: whether the assembly present is actually capable of being reached, and whether the debater themselves genuinely has the skill and resilience the exchange will demand. With both settled, the sixth heading — the treatise's account of when a debate is even worth entering — is complete.

Chinese Source

觀察時眾者，謂立論者方起論端，應善觀察現前眾會，為有僻執，為無執耶？為有賢正，為無有耶？為有善巧，為無有耶？如是觀時，若知眾會唯有僻執……便自思勉，於是眾中不應立論。若知眾會無所僻執……便自思勉，於是眾中應當立論。

Ru-Yi Translation

Examining the assembly at hand: it means that, at the outset of discourse, one should skillfully examine the assembly gathered — are they bound by biased views, or free of them? Are they worthy and upright, or not? Are they capable, or not? Examining in this way, if one finds the assembly bound entirely by biased views... one should exert oneself in reflection and not take up discourse before this assembly. If one finds the assembly free of biased views... one should exert oneself in reflection and take up discourse before this assembly.

Chinese Source

觀察善巧不善巧者，謂立論者方起論端，應自觀察善與不善。我於論體、論處、論依、論嚴、論負、論出離等，為善巧耶？不善巧耶？我為有力能立自論、摧他論耶？於論負處能解脫耶？如是觀時，若自了知我無善巧……不應立論。若自了知我有善巧……當共立論。是名第三或作不作論出離相。

Ru-Yi Translation

Examining one's own skill or lack of it: it means that, at the outset of discourse, one should examine oneself for skill or its absence — am I skilled or unskilled in the nature of discourse, its venue, its basis, its adornment, its defeat, and its withdrawal? Do I have the capacity to establish my own thesis and overturn another's? Can I recover with equanimity if I fall into defeat? Examining in this way, if one recognizes a lack of skill... one should not take up discourse. If one recognizes genuine skill... one should take up discourse together with the opponent. This is called the third mark of discourse's withdrawal, concerning whether or not to act.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the audience examination's decisive case: an assembly of committed adherents to a fixed wrong view — a crowd of non-Buddhist zealots with no interest in actually being persuaded — makes debate pointless no matter how sound the argument, since nothing can land where there is no openness to hear it; a worthy, discerning, receptive audience, by contrast, makes debate genuinely worthwhile even where the outcome is uncertain, because a receptive listener can actually be reached. He treats this as a corrective to a natural but mistaken instinct: that a good argument should always be made regardless of who is listening — the treatise insists the audience matters as much as the argument.

The final self-examination he reads as the most demanding of the three, because it requires honest self-assessment rather than merely assessing external conditions: mastery across all six of the preceding headings (the nature, venue, basis, adornment, defeat, and withdrawal of discourse already covered in this fascicle), the actual capacity to both establish one's own position and dismantle an opponent's, and — the subtlest test — whether one could actually lose gracefully, recovering equanimity rather than being destabilized by defeat. He notes this last point circles back to the thirteen dignified concession-phrases from two sections earlier: a debater unprepared to use them if needed is not yet ready to debate at all. With all three examinations — gain and loss, the assembly, and self-knowledge — now complete, discourse's withdrawal is finished, and the treatise turns to its seventh and final heading on logic: the methods of greatest use in discourse.

33. The Science of Logic Concludes: The Methods of Greatest Use, and All Seven Headings Complete

The seventh and final heading names three qualities that, together, make everything covered so far actually useful in practice — and with it, the entire science of logic (因明處), opened many sections ago as the third of the treatise's five worldly sciences, reaches its close. This single heading spans roughly 3,900 lines of source material, easily the largest topic in this fascicle, covering the sevenfold framework, both kinds of thesis, all eight means of proof, the five adornments, the three kinds of defeat, and the three self-examinations — thirty-six components in all, as Cháng Bǎi's closing tally confirms.

Chinese Source

論多所作法者，謂有三種於所立論多所作法。一、善自他宗，二、勇猛無畏，三、辯才無竭。

Ru-Yi Translation

The methods of greatest use in discourse: there are three qualities of great use to any discourse advanced. First, mastery of one's own and other positions; second, courageous fearlessness; and third, inexhaustible eloquence.

Chinese Source

問：如是三法，於所立論，何故名為多有所作？答：能善了知自他宗故，於一切法能起談論；勇猛無畏故，處一切眾能起談論；辯才無竭故，隨所問難皆善酬答。是故此三，於所立論多有所作。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: why are these three qualities called of such great use to discourse? Answer: because mastering one's own and other positions well, one can engage in discourse on any matter; because of courageous fearlessness, one can engage in discourse before any assembly; and because of inexhaustible eloquence, one can skillfully answer whatever difficulty is raised. This is why these three are of such great use to discourse.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes this final heading deliberately draws only three of the five adornments of discourse from section 27 — mastery of both positions, fearlessness, and eloquence (folded here into "inexhaustible eloquence," which he identifies with the earlier "speech perfected in every respect") — leaving out dignified restraint and worthiness of respect, since those two describe a debater's personal bearing rather than what actually makes discourse functionally useful across any topic, any audience, and any challenge. The three that remain map exactly onto the three benefits named: broad mastery lets a debater engage any subject matter, fearlessness lets them face any assembly without being unseated, and inexhaustible eloquence lets them meet any objection as it comes. Together, he reads them as the treatise's own summary of what all thirty-six preceding components were actually for — not technical virtuosity for its own sake, but the practical capacity to teach and to protect the true doctrine wherever and whenever the need arises.

With this, Cháng Bǎi offers his own closing tally of the entire science of logic: the nature of discourse contributes six components, the venue six, the basis ten, the adornment five, defeat and withdrawal and the methods of greatest use three each — thirty-six components in total, or forty-three if the seven headings themselves are counted alongside their contents. This closes 因明處 (the science of logic) in its entirety — the third of the five worldly sciences opened back at section 10, and by a wide margin the largest single topic this translation has yet covered, spanning the sevenfold framework of discourse; the six kinds of discourse and six fitting venues; the vast basis of discourse with its two kinds of thesis and eight means of proof (positing the thesis, reason, example, similar and dissimilar class, and the three sources of valid knowledge — direct perception, inference, and scriptural authority); the five adornments and twenty-seven resulting virtues; the three kinds of defeat; the three self-examinations of withdrawal; and now, finally, the three methods of greatest use. The treatise next turns to the fourth of the five sciences: linguistics (聲明處).

34. The Fourth Science Opens: Linguistics, and Its Sixfold Framework

With logic complete, the treatise turns to the fourth of the five worldly sciences: linguistics (聲明處, śabdavidyā) — the study of how words, meaning, and grammar are actually built. Six aspects are named; this section covers the framework and the first: how words themselves are constructed, and the five virtues (already met in the adornment of discourse) that make speech genuinely well-formed.

Chinese Source

已說因明處。云何聲明處？當知此處略有六相。一、法施設建立相，二、義施設建立相，三、補特伽羅施設建立相，四、時施設建立相，五、數施設建立相，六、處所根栽施設建立相。

Ru-Yi Translation

The science of logic has now been explained. What is the science of linguistics? Know that, briefly, it has six aspects: first, the establishment of words; second, the establishment of meaning; third, the establishment of grammatical person; fourth, the establishment of tense; fifth, the establishment of number; and sixth, the establishment of grammatical treatises and their roots.

Chinese Source

云何法施設建立？謂名身、句身、文身，及五德相應聲。一、不鄙陋，二、輕易，三、雄朗，四、相應，五、義善。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the establishment of words? It refers to the body of names, the body of sentences, the body of letters, and speech endowed with five virtues: first, free of coarseness; second, readily understood; third, vigorous and clear; fourth, coherent; and fifth, wholesome in meaning.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies linguistics as the treatise's account of grammar in the fullest sense: a name-body is a concept condensed into a word (like "table"), a sentence-body is a complete statement (like "all conditioned things are impermanent"), and a letter-body is the individual sounds — in Sanskrit, fourteen vowels and thirty-three consonants — that combine to build both. The five virtues of speech, he notes, are the exact same five named earlier for perfected discourse in the adornment of discourse (section 27): linguistics supplies the raw grammatical material, while the earlier heading concerned how skillfully that material gets deployed in actual debate. He treats this opening aspect as linguistics' foundation — everything the remaining five aspects cover (meaning, grammatical person, tense, number, and the treatises themselves) is built from these same three units: names, sentences, and letters.

Fascicle 3 · 瑜伽師地論卷第三 · Mind Stage, continued

The Four Elements That Build All Matter

This page continues Ru-Yi's translation of the Mind Stage from where fascicle 2 left off. The treatise now shows how the five topics it has been analyzing — self-nature, support, object, accompaniments, and activity — all reduce to just three categories, then turns to a systematic account of matter itself: how the four great elements generate, support, establish, sustain, and nourish all derived matter, why there is no such thing as a truly indivisible physical atom, and how many kinds of matter can be found packed into any given lump of the physical world.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 3 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. Everything Reduces to Three Categories

Fascicle 3 opens by drawing together the five topics used throughout the Mind Stage's analysis — self-nature, support, object, accompaniments, and activity — into a simpler, threefold division.

Chinese Source

復次，即前所說自性乃至業等五事，當知皆由三處所攝。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, one should understand that these five things previously discussed — self-nature, and so on up through activity — are all comprised within three categories.

謂由色聚故，心心所品故，及無為故。

Namely: the category of matter aggregates, the category of mind and mental factors, and the category of unconditioned dharmas.

除餘假有法。

This excludes all other conceptually existent dharmas.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as a tidying move: everything the Mind Stage has been describing through five sense-consciousnesses and the sixth, mental consciousness — their nature, what they lean on, what they take as object, what accompanies them, and what they do — ultimately falls into just three baskets. The physical world (matter aggregates) accounts for the sense-faculties and their objects; mind and its mental factors account for the eight consciousnesses and everything that arises together with them; and unconditioned dharmas account for the small remainder of reality that neither arises nor ceases, such as space or the true nature of things.

The explicit exclusion he treats as equally important: the twenty-four so-called "dharmas dissociated from mind" — things like speed, cohesion, and time — are deliberately left out, because on this system they are not separately real entities at all, only convenient labels applied to the relative configuration of matter and mind that already exists. Naming them "conceptually existent" is the treatise's way of denying them a place among what is actually, irreducibly there.

2. How the Four Elements Generate Matter

The treatise now opens an extended discussion of the great elements — earth, water, fire, and wind — and the derived matter that depends on them, beginning with an objection.

Chinese Source

問：一切法生，皆從自種而起。云何說諸大種能生所造色耶？云何造色依彼，彼所建立，彼所任持，彼所長養耶？

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: All dharmas arise from their own seed. How, then, can it be said that the great elements generate derived matter? And how is derived matter said to depend on them, to be established by them, to be sustained by them, and to be nourished by them?

答：由一切內外大種及所造色種子，皆悉依附內相續心。乃至諸大種子未生諸大以來，造色種子終不能生造色。

Answer: All the seeds of the great elements and of derived matter, whether internal or external, depend on the inwardly continuing mind. So long as the seeds of the great elements have not yet produced the great elements themselves, the seeds of derived matter can never produce derived matter.

要由彼生，造色方從自種子生，是故說彼能生造色，要由彼生為前導故。由此道理，說諸大種為彼生因。

Only once the great elements have arisen does derived matter then arise from its own seed. This is why it is said that the great elements generate derived matter: their arising must come first, as what leads the way. For this reason, the great elements are said to be the generating cause of derived matter.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the opening question as posing a real tension: Yogācāra holds that every dharma arises strictly from its own seed, so in what sense can the four elements "generate" a separate thing, derived matter, which is supposed to have its own seed too? His resolution is to distinguish the direct causal seed of derived matter — which is real and does the actual producing — from the great elements' role as an indispensable lead condition: nothing can generate derived matter's seed into action until the elements have already occupied the ground it needs. This becomes the first of five distinct roles the elements play toward derived matter, worked out one by one across the sections that follow.

3. Support, Establishment, Sustenance, and Growth

Having explained generation, the treatise answers the remaining three parts of the opening question, then adds a fifth function not originally asked about.

Chinese Source

云何造色依於彼耶？由造色生已，不離大種處而轉故。

Ru-Yi Translation

How is derived matter said to depend on the great elements? Because once derived matter has arisen, it does not occur apart from the place occupied by the great elements.

云何彼所建立？由大種損益，彼同安危故。云何彼所任持？由隨大種等量不壞故。

How are the great elements said to establish derived matter? Because whenever the great elements are diminished or enhanced, derived matter shares the very same fortune, whether harm or safety. How are the great elements said to sustain derived matter? Because derived matter follows the exact measure of the great elements, remaining undiminished as long as they do.

云何彼所長養？由因飲食、睡眠、修習梵行三摩地等，依彼造色倍復增廣，故說大種為彼養因。如是諸大種望所造色有五種作用應知。

How are the great elements said to nourish derived matter? Because through food, sleep, the cultivation of pure conduct, meditative absorption, and the like, derived matter that depends on the great elements grows and expands all the more. This is why the great elements are said to be the nourishing cause of derived matter. Thus it should be understood that the great elements have five kinds of function toward the derived matter that depends on them.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through each function with an everyday case: a body swells when the water element runs high, stiffens or grows heavy when earth predominates, runs hot with excess fire, or struggles to breathe when wind is disturbed — this is "establishment," derived matter rising and falling in lockstep with the elements it depends on. "Support" he reads spatially: derived matter never appears anywhere except exactly where its elements already occupy space, the way dough never extends past the flour and water kneaded into it. "Sustenance" keeps derived matter's own extent precisely matched to the elements underneath it, neither more nor less. And "nourishment" is the one function that actually adds growth over time, driven by ordinary food, rest, and — distinctively for a monastic audience — sustained meditative and ethical discipline, all of which visibly enlarge and strengthen the body built on the elements.

Taken together, he treats these five — generating, supporting, establishing, sustaining, nourishing — as a fixed analytic template the treatise will keep returning to whenever it needs to describe exactly how the four elements relate to the matter built upon them.

4. Are There Truly Indivisible Particles?

The treatise turns next to a different question: whether matter is ultimately composed of real, indivisible physical atoms.

Chinese Source

復次，於色聚中，曾無極微生。若從自種生時，唯聚集生，或細或中或大。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, within a matter aggregate, no particle has ever, strictly speaking, arisen on its own. When matter arises from its own seed, it arises only as an aggregate, all at once — whether fine, middling, or coarse.

又非極微集成色聚。但由覺慧分析諸色極量邊際，分別假立以為極微。

Nor is a matter aggregate built up out of particles. Rather, it is only through discerning wisdom analyzing matter down to its very outermost limit that a "particle" is conceptually distinguished and provisionally established.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as a direct denial of the idea, held in some other schools, that gross matter is literally assembled out of preexisting real, indivisible atoms the way a wall is built up brick by brick. On this treatise's account, a lump of matter arises whole and all at once from its own seed, at whatever scale — fine, middling, or coarse — that seed produces; a "particle" is not a hidden building block but simply the name given to the point where analytical reasoning, pushing the division of matter as far as thought can take it, has to stop. He offers a classical illustration for why this matters: a dewdrop on a leaf does not shrink toward some final, smallest remaining droplet before vanishing in the sun — it simply dries away all at once — which is meant to make vivid that the decrease of a mass of matter was never a process of peeling off real atoms one at a time. Because a particle was never actually produced in the first place, it is said to have neither arising nor ceasing.

5. Particles Have Location, But Not Parts

Chinese Source

又色聚亦有方分，極微亦有方分。然色聚有分非極微。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, a matter aggregate has spatial location, and so does a particle. But a matter aggregate has further parts; a particle does not.

何以故？由極微即是分，此是聚色所有，非極微復有餘極微，是故極微非有分。

Why is this? Because the particle itself already is the part — the part that belongs to a matter aggregate — and a particle does not, in turn, contain some further particle within it. This is why a particle is said to have no further parts.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi clarifies a distinction easy to blur: both a matter aggregate and a particle occupy space, so both can loosely be said to have "location." But only the aggregate — a lamp, a table — can actually be cut or divided into smaller pieces. A particle cannot, not because it is some mysterious point-sized ultimate reality, but simply because "particle" is defined, by the very act of analysis that posits it, as the stopping point beyond which no further division is being carried out. Calling it "partless" is a statement about where the reasoning stops, not a discovery about what matter is really made of.

6. Matter and Its Elements Never Separate

The treatise now explains two distinct senses in which the great elements and the derived matter built on them are never found apart from each other.

Chinese Source

一、同處不相離。謂大種極微與色香味觸等，於無根處有離根者，於有根處有有根者，是名同處不相離。

Ru-Yi Translation

First, non-separation in the same place: this means that the particles of the great elements, together with visible form, smell, taste, and the tangible, occur — in a place without sense-faculties, as particles apart from any faculty; in a place with sense-faculties, as particles belonging to a faculty. This is called non-separation in the same place.

二、和雜不相離。謂即此大種極微與餘聚集，能造、所造色處俱故，是名和雜不相離。

Second, non-separation through commingling: this means that these very particles of the great elements, together with other aggregated particles, occupy the very same place at once — the generating together with the generated matter. This is called non-separation through commingling.

又此遍滿聚色，應知如種種物，石磨為末，以水和合，互不相離；非如胡麻、綠豆、粟、稗等聚。

Further, it should be understood that this thoroughly pervading matter aggregate is like various substances ground by a millstone into powder and then mixed with water, mutually inseparable — not like a heap of sesame, mung beans, millet, and unhusked grain.

又一切所造色皆即依止大種處，不過大種處量；乃至大種所據處所，諸所造色還即據此；由此因緣，說所造色依於大種。即以此義，說諸大種名為大種。由此大種其性大故，為種生故。

Further, all derived matter depends entirely on the place occupied by the great elements and does not exceed the measure of that place; wherever the great elements occupy a place, derived matter occupies that very same place. For this reason it is said that derived matter depends on the great elements. It is by this very meaning that the great elements are called "great elements": because their nature is vast, and because they serve as the seed for what is generated.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes two different ways the elements and derived matter are said to never separate. "Same place" means an eye's elements sit exactly where the eye's derived matter sits, whether in a living faculty or in inert external matter — location, not fusion. "Commingling" goes further: earth, water, fire, and wind, along with whatever derived matter they generate, actually interpenetrate into one indistinguishable mass — like flour ground fine and kneaded with water into dough — rather than remaining separately identifiable the way sesame seeds and mung beans stay recognizable when merely poured together in the same bowl. He flags this second point as a live disagreement across schools: certain Śrāvakayāna traditions hold that co-located elements remain discrete substances only sitting side by side, while the Mahāyāna position taken here is that they genuinely fuse into a single whole.

He closes with the etymology this whole discussion has been building toward: the four are called "great" because all derived matter, everywhere, depends on them without exception, and "elements" — literally, seeds — because they must arise first, as the generative lead, before what they generate can ever arise.

7. The Fourteen Kinds of Matter

The treatise now catalogues everything that counts as "matter" in this technical sense.

Chinese Source

復次，於諸色聚中，略有十四種事。謂地、水、火、風、色、聲、香、味、觸，及眼等五根。除唯意所行色。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, within matter aggregates in general, there are, in brief, fourteen categories of thing: earth, water, fire, wind; visible form, sound, smell, taste, the tangible; and the five faculties beginning with the eye. This excludes matter cognized by the mind-consciousness alone.

一切色聚，有色諸根所攝者，有一切，如所說事界。如有色諸根所攝聚如是，有色諸根所依大種所攝聚亦爾。所餘色聚，除有色諸根，唯有餘界。

Every matter aggregate belonging to a physical sense-faculty comprises all fourteen, in the sense of the causal elements already explained. Just as this holds for the aggregate belonging to a physical sense-faculty, so too it holds for the aggregate of the great elements on which a physical sense-faculty depends. As for the remaining matter aggregates — apart from the physical sense-faculties themselves — they comprise only the remaining elements.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this list as exhaustive by design: the four elements, the five sense-objects (form, sound, smell, taste, touch), and the five sense-faculties together account for everything the treatise means by "matter" here. He notes the deliberate exclusion of a further class — subtle matter cognized by mind-consciousness alone, such as the invisible force generated by taking a moral vow, or matter manifested through advanced meditative mastery — as outside the scope of this particular fourteen-item analysis, reserved for treatment elsewhere.

He then draws out the practical payoff: a living sense-faculty like the eye, and the gross elemental base that physically supports it, both count as containing all fourteen categories in causal terms, since a functioning eye needs the elements, its own faculty-substance, and its objects all at once. Ordinary external matter with no sense-faculty of its own — a stone, a river — falls short of that full set, comprising only the remaining, non-faculty categories.

8. Three Ways to Count What's There

The treatise now distinguishes three different senses in which a given lump of matter can be said to "contain" the fourteen categories — numbers that do not always agree.

Chinese Source

又約相攝有十四事，即由相攝施設事極微。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, counted by distinguishing mark, there are fourteen categories; and it is by this same distinguishing mark that "particles" of each category are conceptually posited.

若約界攝，隨於此聚有爾所界，即說此聚爾所事攝。

Counted by causal element, however many elements are present within a given aggregate, that many categories are said to be comprised within that aggregate.

若約不相離攝，或內、或外所有諸聚，隨於此聚中，乃至有爾所法相可得，即說此聚爾所事攝應知。

Counted by non-separation — whether of an internal or an external aggregate — however many distinguishing marks can actually be found within a given aggregate, that many categories are said to be comprised within it; this should be understood accordingly.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this as a deliberate corrective to a natural but mistaken assumption — that "how many of the fourteen categories does this thing have" must have one single correct answer. Counted by mark, an eye is simply one thing, an eye. Counted by causal element, an eye's very arising depends on all four great elements plus its own faculty-substance plus the objects it takes, so in that sense it can be said to comprise as many as ten of the fourteen. Counted by actual, present co-occurrence, one counts only whatever marks are literally detectable in that exact lump right now — which for a smooth stone might be as little as earth alone, and for a living body, all fourteen at once. The right number, on his reading, always depends on which of these three legitimate questions is actually being asked.

9. How Many Elements Show Up in a Given Thing

The treatise illustrates the previous point with concrete examples, sliding from things with only one detectable element up to the four-element case of a living body.

Chinese Source

或有聚中唯一大種可得。如石、末尼、真珠、琉璃、珂貝、璧玉、珊瑚等中，或池、沼、溝、渠、江、河等中，或火焰、燈燭等中，或四方風輪有塵、無塵風等中。

Ru-Yi Translation

In some aggregates, only a single great element can be detected — as in stone, gems, pearls, lapis lazuli, coral, and the like; or in ponds, marshes, ditches, canals, and rivers; or in flame and lamplight; or in the winds of the four quarters, whether dust-bearing or dust-free.

或有聚中二大種可得。如雪、濕樹、葉、華、果等中，或熱末尼等中。或有聚中三大種可得。如即熱樹等中，或動搖中。

In some aggregates, two great elements can be detected — as in snow, a moist tree, leaf, flower, or fruit, or in a heated gem. In some aggregates, three great elements can be detected — as in a tree that has caught fire, or in something being shaken by wind.

或有聚中四大種可得。謂於內色聚中。如薄伽梵說：於各別內身，若髮毛等乃至糞穢，是內地界；若小便等，是內水界；若於身中所有煖等，是內火界；若上行等風，是內風界。

In some aggregates, all four great elements can be detected — namely, within the aggregates that make up an internal, living body. As the Blessed One said: within each individual body, hair, body-hair, and the rest, down to excrement, are the internal earth element; urine and the like are the internal water element; whatever warmth is present within the body is the internal fire element; the upward-moving and other winds are the internal wind element.

如是若於此聚彼相可得，說彼相為有；若不可得，說彼相為無。

Thus, whenever a given mark can be detected within an aggregate, that mark is said to be present; whenever it cannot be detected, it is said to be absent.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks the sliding scale from one detectable element up to all four, culminating in the Buddha's own fourfold division of the human body — solids as earth, fluids as water, warmth as fire, breath and circulatory movement as wind. He notes this same division appears elsewhere in the canon as a basic meditation for loosening attachment to the body as a single, solid self: seen this way, the body dissolves into nothing more than an assembled composite of the very same ordinary elements found everywhere in stone, water, flame, and wind — nothing uniquely "self" about any of it.

10. Sound and Wind Are Not Always There

The treatise closes this stretch by noting two categories — sound and wind — that behave differently from the others: present in potential everywhere, but not always present in fact.

Chinese Source

復次，聲於一切色聚中，界故說有，相即不定。由現在方便生故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, as for sound: in every matter aggregate, it is present in the sense of its causal element, but its actual mark is not fixed. This is because sound arises only given a present, contributing condition.

風有二種。謂恆相續及不恆相續。恆相續者，謂於彼彼聚有恆旋轉風。不恆相續者，謂旋風及空行風。

Wind is of two kinds: continuous and non-continuous. The continuous kind refers to the constantly circulating wind present within this or that aggregate. The non-continuous kind refers to whirlwinds and winds that move through open space.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads sound as carrying the same latent-versus-actual distinction found throughout this section: every material thing carries the underlying capacity to produce sound — a struck table, clapped hands, a rung bell — yet the actual sound appears only once the right present condition is met, hence "present as element, not fixed as mark." Wind, by contrast, gets a genuinely two-way split rather than a latent/actual one: the constant, unbroken wind found in every aggregate — paradigmatically, one's own breath, sustaining life moment to moment without pause — as against occasional winds such as a whirlwind or a gust moving through open space, which come and go. Two different kinds of wind exist in the world, on his reading, one always active and one only sometimes so — not a claim about hidden potential waiting to manifest, the way sound's case works.

11. Darkness and Light Are Kinds of Matter Too

The treatise now classifies darkness and light themselves as a kind of visible matter, and asks where each is found continuously and where only intermittently.

Chinese Source

又闇色、明色，說名空界及孔隙。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, darkness and light are called the sphere of open space, and the sphere of an opening or cavity.

又諸闇色恆相續者，謂世界中間；不恆相續者，謂於餘處。

As for darkness that continues without interruption: this refers to the interval between world-systems. As for darkness that does not continue without interruption: this refers to every other place.

如是明色恆相續者，謂於自然光明天中；不恆相續者，謂於餘處。

Light that continues without interruption, in the same way, refers to the naturally luminous heavens. Light that does not continue without interruption refers to every other place.

又明闇色，謂於顯色增聚應知。

Further, it should be understood that light and darkness are simply an intensification within the category of visible color.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes two competing readings of "the interval between world-systems." One takes it as the empty eon between a world's destruction and the next world's formation, when no sun exists and no being dwells there, so darkness truly never breaks. The other — which he credits to both Master Kuījī and Venerable Miào Jìng, and finds echoed in the Lotus Sūtra — takes it literally, as the dark gap of space lying between one great trichiliocosm and its neighbor, permanently uninhabited and permanently dark; he leans toward this second reading as the more natural fit. Wherever beings actually live, by contrast, day and night alternate, so darkness there is never unbroken.

The naturally luminous heavens are the desire-realm heavens and above, whose bodies shine of their own accord and produce nothing foul, unlike beings who eat solid food and must excrete. Below them, light and dark simply alternate as usual. He closes by noting that light and dark are not some third, separate category of color at all — both are just the ordinary visible colors (blue, yellow, red, white) intensified in one direction or the other.

12. Matter Aggregates Grow and Shrink

Chinese Source

又由依止色聚種子功能故，若遇相似緣時，或小聚無間大聚生，或大聚無間小聚生。由此因緣，施設諸聚有增有減。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, owing to the function inherent in the seed of a matter aggregate, when a similar condition is met, a small aggregate may immediately give rise to a large aggregate, or a large aggregate may immediately give rise to a small one. For this reason, matter aggregates are said to increase and decrease.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi's example: a body suddenly grows larger given a nourishing condition such as good food, and suddenly shrinks given a depleting condition such as illness. He then extends the same principle beyond individual bodies, citing an observation attributed to Venerable Miào Jìng: the very same mountain that took a full night to climb during a past Buddha's era, when human lifespans ran to twenty thousand years, could be climbed almost instantly in Śākyamuni's own time of hundred-year lifespans — suggesting that even the physical world itself contracts in step with the accumulated karma of the beings who depend on it, not only individual bodies. He stresses that this only happens given a matching, contributing condition, the way a planted tree needs watering and fertilizing to actually grow — the seed's potential alone is never sufficient by itself.

13. What the Sutras Mean by "Solid"

The treatise now works through a set of technical terms drawn from the sutras, using the earth element as its worked example.

Chinese Source

如經言：若堅、堅攝、近攝、非近攝、執受，乃至廣說。堅云何？謂地。堅攝云何？謂彼種子。

Ru-Yi Translation

As the sutra says: solidity, what belongs to solidity, what is held near, what is not held near, what is grasped by consciousness — and so on, at length. What is solidity? It is the earth element. What belongs to solidity? It is the seed of that same element.

又堅者，即彼界。堅攝者，謂髮毛等，或土塊等。

Further, solidity is that very causal element itself; what belongs to solidity is hair, body-hair, and the like, or a clod of earth, and the like.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the sutra's vocabulary — solid, what-belongs-to-solid, held-near, not-held-near, grasped-by-consciousness — as the springboard for a full technical gloss of the earth element, glossed here two different ways. Read as manifest versus seed, "solidity" names the currently manifest earth element as the eye actually sees it as solid, while "what belongs to solidity" names its seed, not yet manifest. Read as generating cause versus generated matter, "solidity" is instead the great element acting as cause, while "what belongs to solidity" is the derived matter it actually produces — literal hair, nails, or a clod of soil.

14. What Makes Matter "Mine"

The treatise continues the same set of technical terms, now distinguishing matter that belongs to a living body from inert matter in the outside world.

Chinese Source

近攝云何？謂有執受。執受云何？謂內所攝。非近攝云何？謂無執受。無執受云何？謂外所攝。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is held near? It is what is grasped by consciousness. What is grasped by consciousness? It is what is comprised within the internal body. What is not held near? It is what is not grasped by consciousness. What is not grasped by consciousness? It is what is comprised in the external world.

又心心所所執種子，名近攝，名執受；與此相違，名非近攝，名非執受。又隨逐自身故，名近攝、執受，如前說。

Further, a seed that is grasped by mind and mental factors is called held near, and is called grasped by consciousness; the opposite of this is called not held near, and not grasped by consciousness. Further, because it follows along with one's own body, it is called held near and grasped by consciousness, as explained above.

如是水等界，如理應知。

The water element, and the rest, should be understood in just the same way.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads "held near" and "grasped by consciousness" as marking earth-element matter belonging to a living body and animated by mind — pull a hair right at the scalp and there is pain — against inert external earth-element matter, such as a clod of soil, or that same hair once it has been cut and can no longer be felt: "not held near." He flags one technical caveat: this particular pass works the distinction out through seeds specifically, which is not quite identical to the fuller fourfold grasped/ungrasped analysis found elsewhere in the treatise's supplementary discussions — worth remembering if the terminology looks slightly different across fascicles. The whole apparatus built here for earth — solid, what-belongs-to-solid, held-near, not-held-near — then simply carries over unchanged to water, fire, and wind, which is why the treatise does not spell each of them out separately.

15. All Four Elements Are Present in Everything, Always

The treatise now demonstrates, with concrete examples, its earlier claim that every one of the four great elements is present as a causal element in every matter aggregate, even where only one or two are actually detectable.

Chinese Source

又於一切色聚中，一切時具有一切大種界。如世間現見乾薪等物，鑽即火生，擊石等亦爾。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, within every matter aggregate, at all times, every one of the four great elements is present as a causal element. As is commonly observed in the world: dry firewood, when drilled, produces fire; striking flint against stone likewise produces fire.

又銅鐵金銀等，極火所燒即銷為水。從月愛珠，水便流出。

Further, copper, iron, gold, silver, and the like, when melted by intense fire, turn to a liquid state; and from the moon-loving pearl, water flows forth.

又得神通者，由心勝解力，變大地等成金銀等。

Further, one who has attained supernormal power can, through the force of the mind's resolute conviction, transform the great earth and the like into gold, silver, and so on.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads these as concrete demonstrations backing the treatise's abstract claim from section 9 that all four elements are always present, even where only one seems detectable: friction or striking releases the latent fire element hidden in ordinary wood or stone; extreme heat reveals the latent water element hidden even in solid metal, and a certain legendary gem is said to release water simply when turned toward moonlight; and a meditator with sufficiently developed concentration can, by the sheer force of mental conviction, manifest the earth element outright as gold or silver. He adds one caution about that last case: the gold conjured by an eighth-ground bodhisattva's power is said to genuinely function as real gold, while the same feat attempted through an ordinary meditator's concentration only produces something that looks like gold but cannot actually be used as such — the depth of the underlying realization, not the technique itself, is what makes the difference.

16. Three Ways Matter Keeps Existing

The treatise now asks a different question: not what matter is made of, but why a given lump of matter keeps appearing, moment after moment, instead of simply vanishing.

Chinese Source

又色聚有三種流轉。一者、長養，二者、等流，三者、異熟生。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, a matter aggregate continues in existence through three processes: first, nourishment; second, homogeneous outflow; third, karmic-ripening arising.

長養有二種。一、處遍滿長養，二、相增盛長養。

Nourishment is of two kinds: first, nourishment that fills out its extent; second, nourishment that intensifies its characteristic.

等流有四種。一、長養等流，二、異熟等流，三、變異等流，四、自性等流。

Homogeneous outflow is of four kinds: first, the homogeneous outflow of nourishment; second, the homogeneous outflow of karmic ripening; third, the homogeneous outflow of transformation; fourth, the homogeneous outflow of its own nature.

異熟生有二種。一、異熟體生，名異熟生；二、從異熟生，名異熟生。

Karmic-ripening arising is of two kinds: first, that which arises as the very substance of ripening, called karmic-ripening arising; second, that which arises from ripening, likewise called karmic-ripening arising.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats these three as the standing answer to why a lump of matter — a body, a mountain — keeps reappearing instead of simply lapsing out of existence. Nourishment covers everything that visibly builds it up: one sub-mode fills out its sheer bulk (eating well and growing stouter or taller), the other intensifies its finer qualities without changing its size (a healthy glow, a clear complexion) — only physical matter gets the bulk-filling kind, while mind gets only the quality-intensifying kind, since mind occupies no space to fill.

Homogeneous outflow is the ordinary moment-to-moment continuity of a thing simply carrying on as itself, in four flavors: driven onward by nourishment, driven by the ripening of past karma, showing visible change over time — the aging face, the changing weight — or showing no visible change at all, like an old friend who looks exactly the same after years apart. Living sense-faculties get only the first two of these four; non-sentient outer matter gets all four. Karmic-ripening arising, finally, distinguishes the ālayavijñāna itself, as the direct fruit of past karma settling which realm one is even born into, from everything that unfolds afterward as its further consequence — the actual particulars of a given life, such as wealth, looks, or family standing, decided by what is elsewhere called "supplementary" karma.

17. Six Places Matter Depends On

Chinese Source

又諸色聚，略說依六處轉。謂建立處、覆藏處、資具處、根所依處、根處、三摩地所行處。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, matter aggregates, briefly stated, occur dependent on six places: the place of foundation, the place of shelter, the place of provisions, the place a sense-faculty depends on, the place of the sense-faculty itself, and the place where meditative absorption operates.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi divides these six between the outer world and the living body. The first three belong to the outer world: "foundation" names the great wind, water, and earth disks on which the physical universe as a whole is built at its formation; "shelter" names buildings and dwellings that protect beings from the elements; "provisions" names the ten ordinary requisites of a life — food and drink, transport, clothing, ornament, song and dance, fragrance and unguents, household goods, lighting, and whatever else sustains daily living. The next two belong to the body itself: the gross physical base beneath a sense-faculty, and the subtle sense-faculty proper. The sixth belongs to neither — meditative absorption is the ground on which the special matter perceptible to mind-consciousness alone (already set aside back in section 7) actually appears. Between them, these six places are said to account for every kind of matter a being ever encounters, whether in the surrounding world, in the body, or purely within meditation.

18. The Mind and Its Fifty-Three Companions

Having finished the analysis of matter aggregates, the treatise turns to the second of the three categories from section 1: mind and mental factors.

Chinese Source

復次，於心心所品中，有心可得，及五十三心所可得。謂作意等，乃至尋伺為後邊，如前說。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, within the category of mind and mental factors, there is mind itself, and fifty-three mental factors, to be found — namely, attention and the rest, down to investigation and analysis at the furthest end, as already explained.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes that this simply names the full roster of fifty-three mental factors already defined back in fascicle 1's account of the Mind Stage's accompaniments, without redefining any of them again here. What the treatise is about to ask is a new question about that same list — not what each factor is, but exactly how widely, and under what conditions, each one arises.

19. Five Questions About When a Mental Factor Arises

The treatise now sorts all fifty-three mental factors into five fixed groups, testing each one against four measures at once: moral quality, meditative ground, time, and whether it accompanies every single moment of mind.

Chinese Source

問：如是諸心所，幾依一切處心生，一切地、一切時、一切耶？答：五。謂作意等，思為後邊。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: Of these mental factors, how many arise together with mind in every moral quality, every ground, every time, and every instance of mind? Answer: Five — attention, and the rest, down to volition at the furthest end.

幾依一切處心生，一切地，非一切時、非一切耶？答：亦五。謂欲等，慧為後邊。

How many arise together with mind in every moral quality and every ground, but not every time and not every instance of mind? Also five — aspiration, and the rest, down to wisdom at the furthest end.

幾唯依善，非一切處心生；然一切地，非一切時、非一切耶？答：謂信等，不害為後邊。

How many arise only together with a wholesome mind — not in every moral quality — yet in every ground, though not every time and not every instance of mind? Faith, and the rest, down to non-harming at the furthest end.

幾唯依染汙，非一切處心生，非一切地、非一切時、非一切耶？答：謂貪等，不正知為後邊。

How many arise only together with a defiled mind — not in every moral quality, not in every ground, not every time, and not every instance of mind? Craving, and the rest, down to incorrect awareness at the furthest end.

幾依一切處心生，非一切地、非一切時、非一切耶？答：謂惡作等，伺為後邊。

How many arise together with mind in every moral quality, but not in every ground, not every time, and not every instance of mind? Regret, and the rest, down to sustained thought at the furthest end.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads these five questions as sorting the fifty-three mental factors by how widely each one's presence actually extends, tested along four axes at once: moral quality (wholesome, unwholesome, or neutral), meditative ground (the nine grounds of the three realms), time (past, present, future), and co-arising with any given moment of consciousness.

The five universal factors — attention, contact, feeling, ideation, and volition — pass all four tests without exception: they accompany literally every act of consciousness, in any moral quality, any realm, any time, whenever any of the eight consciousnesses is active. The five object-determined factors — aspiration, resolve, mindfulness, concentration, and wisdom — likewise range across every moral quality and every ground, but each still depends on its own specific triggering condition (aspiration only toward something desired, resolve only toward something already settled), so they fail the "every time" and "every mind" tests that the universal five pass. The eleven wholesome factors are restricted from the start by moral quality — they simply never arise together with an unwholesome or neutral mind — even while still ranging across every meditative ground. The twenty-eight defiled factors are the most restricted of all, failing every one of the four tests; Cháng Bǎi notes, for instance, that outright hatred cannot actually manifest for beings born into the form or formless realms, which is part of why the defiled factors fail the "every ground" test as well. And the four indeterminate factors — regret, sleep, and the two modes of thought, investigation and analysis — range across every moral quality, since they can serve either a wholesome or an unwholesome act, but are restricted by ground (sleep, for instance, does not occur above the form realm) and, like the object-determined five, arise only intermittently rather than with every single moment of mind.

He treats the whole five-way grid less as trivia to memorize than as training in a habit of mind: for any mental factor at all, the right question is never simply "is it present," but exactly how far its reach extends along each of these four measures.

20. Two Ways a Sense-Faculty Can Fail

The treatise now opens a new question: under exactly what conditions does a consciousness actually arise? The first condition is that its sense-faculty must not be impaired.

Chinese Source

云何根不壞？謂有二種因。一、不滅壞故；二、不羸劣故。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is meant by "the sense-faculty is not impaired"? There are two causes: first, that it is not destroyed; second, that it is not weakened.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes outright destruction — total loss, as in blindness or deafness — from mere weakening, as in poor eyesight that must squint, or a dull ear that needs a raised voice; either one blocks a sense-consciousness from arising properly, though only the first is a complete loss. He traces the destruction of a physical faculty to four possible causes: injury from another being or from an external accident (a blow, an animal bite, fire, flood); self-inflicted damage from an irregular way of living; karmic causes, as in blindness present from birth; and simple natural change in the body over time, such as aging.

The mental faculty — either the immediately preceding moment of mental consciousness that serves as this moment's basis, or the seventh, defiled consciousness that underlies the sixth — is impaired by a different set of four: the five hindrances (desire, ill will, torpor and sleepiness, restlessness and regret, and doubt) that block clarity and stall meditative progress; excessive mental agitation, as in restless overthinking to the point of losing sleep or appetite; forcing concentration before the mind is genuinely ready for it; and attempting more than one's actual capacity allows, or using the wrong method, inviting real harm.

21. Six Kinds of "Object Present"

The second condition for a consciousness to arise is that its object must actually be present. The treatise distinguishes six different senses in which an object can be said to be "present."

Chinese Source

云何境界現前？謂或由所依處故，或由自性故，或由方故，或由時故，或由顯了不顯了故，或由全分及一分故。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is meant by "the object is present"? It is by reason of the base it depends on; or by reason of its own nature; or by reason of direction; or by reason of time; or by reason of whether it is manifest or unmanifest; or by reason of whether the whole, or only a part, is taken.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi works through all six. By base, an object belongs either to a sentient being or to the inanimate outer world. By own-nature, objects differ in mark (color, shape, gesture), in function (the moral or amoral force behind an act), and in momentary quality (pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral) — he cross-references a fuller threefold-nature analysis (the imagined, the dependent, and the fully accomplished) developed elsewhere in the treatise's supplementary discussions.

By direction, the very same mountain looks entirely different depending on where one stands — he cites the Song-dynasty poet Su Shi's famous couplet on Mount Lu, seen as a ridge from one angle and a peak from another, its true face unrecognizable simply because the viewer stands within it — and notes that ordinary proximity works the same way: a neighbor close at hand comes readily to awareness, while someone far off easily slips from mind. By time, the changing seasons — spring blossoms, an autumn moon, summer's blazing color, winter frost — continually shift what stands before the senses, and he notes that attachment to any given object tends to loosen simply as time carries it further into the past. By manifest versus unmanifest, something concretely present to the senses right now — an actual flower, an actual lamp — is sharply distinct from something only recalled or imagined, which arrives far less vividly and needs deliberate effort to pin down. And by whole versus part, one may take in an entire arrangement — three Buddha images side by side — or attend to only the central figure, leaving the rest at the margin of awareness.

22. Four Obstructions, and Two Kinds of "Too Far"

Even a present object fails to trigger a consciousness if it is blocked in one of four ways, or lies at an extreme distance.

Chinese Source

若四種障所不障礙，亦非極遠。謂覆蔽障、隱沒障、映奪障、幻惑障。

Ru-Yi Translation

Provided it is not obstructed by any of four kinds of obstruction, and is not at an extreme distance. Namely: the obstruction of concealment, the obstruction of being hidden away, the obstruction of being overwhelmed, and the obstruction of illusion.

極遠有二種。謂處所極遠、損減極遠。

Extreme distance is of two kinds: distance of place, and distance through attenuation.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains the four obstructions in turn. Concealment is simple physical blocking — a wall between the eye and what lies behind it. Being hidden away is a deliberate masking by drug, herb, or supernormal power. He recounts a traditional story of the elder Upagupta, whose disciple kept dozing off during seated meditation; through his own meditative mastery, the elder made the ground around the disciple's seat appear to fall away into a sheer cliff on every side but one, so that the startled disciple — convinced that one careless nod would send him over the edge — forced himself to stay fully alert until his practice at last succeeded. Only then did Upagupta reveal that the cliff had never been real, simply concealed terrain skillfully used to break a stubborn habit of drowsiness.

Being overwhelmed is what happens when something too faint or small goes unnoticed beside something larger or stronger nearby — starlight vanishing in daylight, though the stars themselves are still there, or a pinch of medicine disappearing unnoticed once stirred into food. And illusion covers both genuine magical creation and purely internal disturbance — dizziness, drunkenness, delirium, or heedless intoxication — any of which blurs what would otherwise be clearly perceived. As for the two kinds of extreme distance: distance of place is simply being too far off in space or time to be perceived at all, while distance through attenuation is the same point already made about particles back in section 4 — matter analyzed down below the threshold that any sense, or its karmic scope, can actually register.

23. Four Forces That Trigger Attention

The final condition for a consciousness to arise is that attention itself must actually be triggered. The treatise names four forces capable of doing this.

Chinese Source

云何能生作意正起？由四因故。一、由欲力，二、由念力，三、由境界力，四、由數習力。

Ru-Yi Translation

What gives rise to the arising of attention? It is due to four causes: first, the force of desire; second, the force of mindfulness; third, the force of the object itself; fourth, the force of habituation.

云何由欲力？謂若於是處心有愛著，心則於彼多作意生。云何由念力？謂若於彼已善取其相、已極作想，心則於彼多作意生。

What is the force of desire? Wherever the mind is attached with fondness, the mind generates attention toward that place, again and again. What is the force of mindfulness? Wherever a mark has already been thoroughly grasped and thoroughly dwelt upon, the mind generates attention toward that place, again and again.

云何由境界力？謂若彼境界或極廣大、或極可意正現在前，心則於彼多作意生。云何由數習力？若於彼境界已極串習、已極諳悉，心即於彼多作意生。

What is the force of the object? Whenever an object is either extremely vast, or extremely pleasing, and is directly present, the mind generates attention toward it, again and again. What is the force of habituation? Wherever an object has already been thoroughly practiced and thoroughly mastered, the mind generates attention toward it, again and again.

若異此者，應於一所緣境，唯一作意一切時生。

Were it otherwise, attention toward a single object would arise in only a single, fixed way, at every single time.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats these four forces as jointly explaining why attention is selective rather than uniform. Fondness pulls attention toward whatever one loves — a food-lover notices flavor, a reader notices new titles, a devout practitioner notices news of a dharma gathering — and he extends this to explain even the larger shape of a life: one habitually drawn to generosity tends toward a fortunate rebirth, while one single-mindedly seeking the noble path tends to notice exactly the teachers and methods capable of actually leading there. Mindfulness pulls attention toward whatever has already been closely studied and held in mind, as in visualizing a Buddha-image already made deeply familiar through repeated practice. The object's own force works through sheer scale or appeal, the way a striking advertisement or an eye-catching public figure draws notice simply by being large or pleasing. And habituation pulls attention toward whatever has become an ingrained habit — reaching for chili pepper with every meal, or being unable to sleep without reading first.

He reads the closing line as the reason all four forces have to be posited in the first place: without them, the very same object would trigger exactly the same attention every single time, which plainly is not what happens — the same sight or sound is sometimes seized upon eagerly and sometimes passes entirely unnoticed, and only causes that vary from moment to moment can account for that difference.

24. The Five Senses Never Chain Into Each Other

The treatise now turns to how the five sense-consciousnesses and the sixth, mental consciousness, actually succeed one another in time.

Chinese Source

又非五識身有二剎那相隨俱生，亦無展轉無間更互而生。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, the five sense-consciousnesses in the body neither arise together across two consecutive moments, nor arise one after another, each triggering the next in unbroken succession.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that once a sense-faculty is intact, its object present, and attention triggered, one, several, or all five sense-consciousnesses may arise together within a single moment — but never for two consecutive moments running, since the very next moment belongs to mental consciousness investigating what was just perceived. Nor can eye-consciousness, once it arises, directly cause ear-consciousness to arise next: each of the five depends on its own distinct faculty, object, and triggering attention, and none has any power to compel another into being. Only the sixth, mental consciousness, through its own act of attention, can draw a different sense-consciousness into activity — the five senses are entirely passive in this respect, never able to hand off to one another on their own.

25. The Sixth Consciousness Investigates, Then Decides

Chinese Source

又一剎那五識身生已，從此無間，必意識生。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, once the five sense-consciousnesses have arisen for a single moment, immediately following this, mental consciousness necessarily arises.

從此無間，或時散亂，或耳識生，或五識身中隨一識生。若不散亂，必定意識中第二決定心生。

Immediately following this, either the mind becomes scattered, or ear-consciousness arises, or one of the five sense-consciousnesses arises. If it does not become scattered, mental consciousness necessarily generates a second, decisive moment of mind.

由此尋求、決定二意識故，分別境界。

It is through these two — the investigating mind and the deciding mind — that mental consciousness discriminates its object.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi lays out the classical sequence of a single act of perception: a first, bare "startled" moment where a sense simply registers its object with no discrimination yet, immediately followed by mental consciousness "investigating" what was just registered — which either scatters, attention drifting elsewhere or a different sense-consciousness intervening, or else settles into a "deciding" mind that actually pins down what was seen or heard. His everyday case: listening to a lecture, the ear registers a sound in the first instant, the mind investigates what was said in the second, and only in the third does it actually settle into real understanding — but if attention wanders to an unrelated thought partway through, that settling never happens, and the thread of what was heard is lost.

26. Defiled or Wholesome — and Where It Comes From

Chinese Source

又由二種因故，或染汙，或善法生。謂分別故，及先所引故。意識中所有，由二種因。在五識者，唯由先所引故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, it is owing to two causes that either a defiled or a wholesome state arises: discrimination, and what has already been drawn forward from a previous moment. Within mental consciousness, both causes are present. Within the five sense-consciousnesses, only the second cause is present.

所以者何？由染汙及善意識力所引故，從此無間，於眼等識中，染汙及善法生。不由分別，彼無分別故。由此道理，說眼等識隨意識轉。

Why is this? Because, driven by the force of a defiled or wholesome mental consciousness, immediately following it, a defiled or wholesome state arises within the eye-consciousness and the rest — not through discrimination, since they are without discrimination. For this reason, it is said that the eye-consciousness and the rest turn in step with mental consciousness.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as tracing whether a given moment is morally wholesome or defiled to two possible causes: mental consciousness's own act of discrimination, actively dwelling on something with attachment or aversion, or simple momentum carried forward unchanged from the moment just before. The sixth consciousness can generate its own wholesome or defiled quality either way. The five sense-consciousnesses, lacking any power of discrimination of their own, can only ever inherit it secondhand — carried over from whatever quality mental consciousness has just established — which is exactly why the eye and the other senses are said to simply turn in step with mental consciousness, rather than ever setting their own moral tone.

27. What "One Mind" Really Means

The treatise now resolves an apparent puzzle from the sutras about how a single moment of devotion could ever accumulate great merit.

Chinese Source

如經言：起一心，若眾多心。云何安立此一心耶？謂世俗言說一心剎那，非生起剎那。

Ru-Yi Translation

As the sutra says: "one moment of mind arises" — or is it, rather, many? How is this "one mind" to be established? It refers to the conventionally spoken-of "one mind-moment," not the strictly momentary arising itself.

云何世俗言說一心剎那？謂一處為依止，於一境界事有爾所了別生，總爾所時名一心剎那。

What is the conventionally spoken-of "one mind-moment"? It is when, resting on a single base, that many acts of discernment arise with respect to a single object — the whole of that span of time is called one mind-moment.

又相似相續，亦說名一，與第二念極相似故。

Further, a close, unbroken succession is also spoken of as "one," because each moment closely resembles the moment that immediately follows it.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi frames this as resolving a real puzzle: the Buddha is recorded saying that a being who, even once, offers a single reverent thought and a single utterance of praise toward the Tathāgata has already accomplished a great deal of good — which seems strange, since one strict instant of mind seems far too brief to accumulate much merit at all. His resolution: ordinary speech about "a single moment of mind" was never referring to one indivisible instant of arising, but to an entire stretch of closely linked moments treated as a single unit — either because they all rest on the same sense-base and stay fixed on the same object throughout, as in a whole session of bowing and reciting the Buddha's praises with sustained devotion, or simply because each moment so closely resembles the one right after it that ordinary language collapses the whole run into "one."

On this reading, praise for "a single reverent thought" is really praise for a sustained stretch of devoted attention, and he draws a practical lesson from it: a genuinely single instant of feeling would accomplish little on its own, but "one mind" carried through with full sincerity for even a short devotional exercise — a complete session of bows and recitation — already carries real weight.

28. When the Sixth Consciousness Tracks the Present, and When It Slips to the Past

Chinese Source

又意識任運散亂，緣不串習境時無欲等生，爾時意識名率爾墮心，唯緣過去境。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, when mental consciousness is spontaneously scattered, taking as its object something unfamiliar, so that none of the triggering forces such as desire arise, that mental consciousness is then called the startled-and-scattered mind, and it takes only a past object as its object.

五識無間所生意識，或尋求、或決定，唯應說緣現在境，若此即緣彼境生。

The mental consciousness that arises immediately following the five sense-consciousnesses, whether investigating or deciding, should be said to take only a present object as its object — arising by taking that very object as its object.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi connects this directly back to section 23's four triggering forces. When none of them — desire, mindfulness, the object's own pull, habituation — are actually active, mental consciousness drifts off on its own, disengaged from whatever the five senses are currently registering, and ends up dwelling instead on some already-past moment it had been investigating a beat before; this is why its object counts as "past" in that scattered state. By contrast, whenever mental consciousness stays properly engaged, whether still investigating or already deciding, it tracks the very same object the five senses are registering right now, in real time — which is why its object then counts as strictly present. He reads this as simply naming the everyday experience of zoning out during a lecture: the ear keeps registering sound perfectly well, but the mind has quietly slipped into thinking about something else entirely, no longer actually with the words being spoken.

29. Five Functions, Five Different Things Discerned

The treatise now specifies exactly what distinct job each of the five universal mental factors performs within a single act of perception.

Chinese Source

又識能了別事之總相。即此所未了別所了境相，能了別者，說名作意。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, consciousness itself discerns only the general mark of a thing. That which discerns the particular mark not yet discerned by consciousness is called attention.

即此可意、不可意、俱相違相，由觸了別。即此攝受、損害、俱相違相，由受了別。

Whether that mark is pleasing, displeasing, or neither, is discerned by contact. Whether it is beneficial, harmful, or neither, is discerned by feeling.

即此言說因相，由想了別。即此邪、正、俱相違行因相，由思了別。

The mark that serves as the basis for naming it in speech is discerned by ideation. Whether the course of action toward it is correct, incorrect, or neither, is discerned by volition.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi connects this back to section 19's finding that these same five — attention, contact, feeling, ideation, volition — are the only mental factors universal to literally every moment of consciousness, and now shows exactly why each one earns its place. Bare consciousness itself grasps only the crudest overall shape of an object; attention is what actually zeroes in on whatever finer aspect consciousness has not yet caught; contact sorts that aspect into pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral; feeling further sorts it by whether it actually helps or harms; ideation extracts whatever nameable feature lets it be put into words and recognized again later; and volition assesses whether acting on it would be right, wrong, or neither.

He treats this as showing that even the simplest act of perceiving something already involves five distinct, cooperating jobs happening at once, not one single undifferentiated "seeing" — which is exactly why these five, and only these five, had to be posited as present in every single moment of mind.

30. The Nature of the Five Universal Factors

Having closed its account of the five universal mental factors, the treatise now works through all ten factors discussed so far — the five universal and five object-determined — asking first what each one's essential nature is.

Chinese Source

是故說彼作意等，思為後邊，名心所有法。遍一切處、一切地、一切時、一切生。

Ru-Yi Translation

For this reason, it is said that these — attention, and the rest, down to volition — are called mental factors pervading every moral quality, every ground, every time, and every arising of mind.

作意云何？謂心迴轉。觸云何？謂三和合。受云何？謂領納。

What is the nature of attention? It is the turning of the mind toward its object. What is the nature of contact? It is the coming together of the three — faculty, object, and consciousness. What is the nature of feeling? It is the receiving of an object's flavor.

想云何？謂了像。思云何？謂心造作。

What is the nature of ideation? It is the apprehending of an image. What is the nature of volition? It is the mind's own activity of forming an action.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats attention's "turning of mind" as the pivotal factor for practice: the treatise elsewhere, in its account of meditative equipoise, works this single factor out into seven and then forty distinct kinds relevant to actual training, since where attention turns is where practice either takes hold or doesn't. He reads "the coming together of three" as naming contact's essential difference between ordinary beings and sages — an ordinary being's contact already comes tinged with ignorance, reflexively grasping self and world as solidly real and generating craving, aversion, and delusion in the same instant, while a sage's contact is free of that overlay, able to engage the world without the mind coming to rest anywhere.

Feeling's "receiving" he glosses as simply taking in whatever flavor an experience carries; ordinary beings crave pleasant feeling, resent painful feeling, and grow dull toward neutral feeling, while the noble ones see all three as inherently unsatisfying and refuse to be moved to further craving by any of them. Ideation's "apprehending an image" is what makes ordinary communication possible in the first place, but he also flags it as the exact hinge where a basic distortion creeps in — mistaking a merely conceptual image for something solidly real — which is why the treatise elsewhere compares ideation to a shimmering mirage: vivid and useful, but not what it appears to be. And volition's "forming an action" he ties to a canonical teaching that a single distorted intention is the true seed from which craving, envy, quarrel, and every kind of careless speech eventually grow — remove the distortion at the root, and the entire chain collapses behind it.

31. The Nature of the Five Object-Determined Factors

Chinese Source

欲云何？謂於可樂事，隨彼彼行，欲有所作性。勝解云何？謂於決定事，隨彼彼行，印可隨順性。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the nature of aspiration? It is the state of wishing to act, with respect to something one finds delightful, in whatever way that delight takes. What is the nature of resolve? It is the state of assenting to and going along with something already settled, in whatever way that settling takes.

念云何？謂於串習事，隨彼彼行，明了記憶性。三摩地云何？謂於所觀察事，隨彼彼行，審慮所依心一境性。

What is the nature of mindfulness? It is the state of clearly remembering something one has habitually practiced, in whatever way that habituation takes. What is the nature of concentration? It is one-pointedness of mind serving as the basis for careful examination, with respect to something under observation, in whatever way that observation takes.

慧云何？謂即於所觀察事，隨彼彼行，簡擇諸法性。或由如理所引，或由不如理所引，或由非如理非不如理所引。

What is the nature of wisdom? It is the state of discerning the nature of dharmas, with respect to something under observation. It may be led by what accords with reason, by what does not accord with reason, or by neither.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads all five of these as earning the name "object-determined" precisely because, unlike the universal five, each only shows up given its own specific triggering object: aspiration only toward something desired, resolve only toward a settled matter, mindfulness only toward something familiar through repetition, and concentration and wisdom both only toward something actually being examined — sharing the same object but performing different jobs on it, one settling the mind on it one-pointedly, the other actually discerning its nature.

He treats the three-way division at the end — wisdom led by sound reasoning, unsound reasoning, or neither — as a recurring template found elsewhere in the treatise for sorting genuine insight (seeing impermanence, suffering, and non-self clearly, without adding false permanence or denying what is actually there) from ordinary wrong view (imposing permanence, pleasure, or self onto conditioned things, or denying real cause and effect) or from simple morally neutral deliberation.

32. What the Five Universal Factors Actually Do

Having established each factor's nature, the treatise now asks the complementary question: what function does each one actually perform?

Chinese Source

又作意作何業？謂引心為業。觸作何業？謂受、想、思所依為業。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, what is the function of attention? It is to draw the mind toward its object. What is the function of contact? It is to serve as the basis for feeling, ideation, and volition.

受作何業？謂愛生所依為業。想作何業？謂於所緣，令心發起種種言說為業。

What is the function of feeling? It is to serve as the basis from which craving arises. What is the function of ideation? It is, with respect to its object, to prompt the mind to generate speech of various kinds.

思作何業？謂發起尋伺身語業等為業。

What is the function of volition? It is to give rise to investigation, to analysis, to bodily and verbal action, and the like.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads attention's function as the literal trigger: without it, no consciousness gets going toward an object at all. Contact is the shared foundation the next three factors build on — nothing to feel, conceive, or intend toward without faculty, object, and consciousness first meeting. Feeling's function he singles out as pivotal: it is specifically what breeds craving, since painful feeling breeds the craving to be rid of it, pleasant feeling breeds the craving to hold onto it, and neutral feeling breeds a craving neither to unite with it nor part from it. He treats this exact mechanism — feeling conditioning craving — as the same link named in the standard twelve-link chain of dependent origination, and as the practical leverage point where the chain leading to further rebirth can actually be interrupted.

Ideation's function is turning raw experience into speech — what lets ordinary beings communicate at all, but also, on his reading, the exact place where reifying language ("this is real, this is mine") first slips in unnoticed; a sage still uses ideation skillfully to teach, without being taken in by the labels it produces. And volition's function is to actually launch investigation, analysis, and outward bodily or verbal action — he cites a canonical teaching that removing a single distorted intention collapses the entire downstream chain of craving, resentment, and quarrel that would otherwise follow from it, tracing all the way back to the root of a great mass of ordinary suffering.

33. What the Five Object-Determined Factors Actually Do

Chinese Source

欲作何業？謂發勤為業。勝解作何業？謂於所緣，印持功德過失為業。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the function of aspiration? It is to rouse effort. What is the function of resolve? It is, with respect to its object, to fix and hold in place a judgment of its merit or its fault.

念作何業？謂於久遠所思、所作、所說，憶念為業。三摩地作何業？謂智所依為業。

What is the function of mindfulness? It is to recollect what was long ago thought, done, or said. What is the function of concentration? It is to serve as the basis for wisdom.

慧作何業？謂於戲論所行染汙清淨，隨順推求為業。

What is the function of wisdom? It is to investigate, in accordance with what has been discerned, whether what conceptual elaboration expresses is defiled or pure.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads aspiration's function as what actually pushes a person into sustained effort — wanting something accomplishes nothing on its own unless the wanting itself rouses real diligence. Resolve's function is to lock a judgment in place so it cannot easily be shaken loose by outside pressure; he notes the treatise elsewhere lists resolve toward the qualities of the Three Jewels, toward the Buddha's power, and toward the ultimate meaning of things among the very first ground a bodhisattva must firmly establish, since without it one is too easily swayed off course by whatever circumstance happens to arise. Mindfulness's function lets a person's own past thought, action, and speech actually be recalled and examined — indispensable, on his reading, to every stage of ethical and meditative training, since a mind that cannot remember what it did cannot correct it either.

Concentration's function exists entirely in service of wisdom: a settled mind is simply the platform from which insight becomes possible, not an end in itself. And wisdom's function is specifically to sort out, from everything expressed through ordinary speech and conceptual elaboration, what is genuinely defiled and what is genuinely pure — Cháng Bǎi glosses "conceptual elaboration" broadly, as any talk that adds nothing of real benefit and subtracts nothing of real harm, and treats the wise person's task as simply refusing to let such empty talk pass unexamined, whether heard from others or tempting to say oneself.

34. How the Three Times Are Established

The treatise now takes up a new topic: since time has no reality of its own in this system, how are past, present, and future actually established?

Chinese Source

云何建立三世？謂諸種子不離法故，如法建立。又由與果、未與果故。

Ru-Yi Translation

How are the three times established? Because seeds are never apart from the dharmas they belong to, they are established in accordance with those dharmas. Further, they are also established by whether a seed has already yielded its result or has not yet yielded it.

若諸果法，若已滅相，是過去；有因未生相，是未來；已生未滅相，是現在。

As for resultant dharmas: if their mark has already ceased, this is the past; if the cause is present but the mark has not yet arisen, this is the future; if the mark has already arisen and has not yet ceased, this is the present.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi stresses that a seed's "when" is first read off whatever manifest dharma it belongs to — past once that dharma is past, present while present, future while still latent. A second method reads a seed's own time directly from whether it has already yielded its fruit: having yielded and being spent counts as past, not yet having yielded and still awaiting conditions counts as future. Manifest resultant dharmas are sorted the same basic way — already arisen and already ceased is past, cause present but effect not yet arisen is future, arisen and not yet ceased is present. He treats seed and manifest dharma as mutually defining here: neither one alone accounts for time on its own; each is understood only in relation to the other.

35. The Four Marks of a Conditioned Thing

The treatise now explains the four marks that every conditioned dharma passes through: arising, aging, abiding, and impermanence.

Chinese Source

云何建立生、老、住、無常？謂於一切處識相續中，一切種子相續俱行建立。由有緣力故，先未相續生法，今最初生，是名生有為相。

Ru-Yi Translation

How are arising, aging, abiding, and impermanence established? They are established within the continuum of consciousness in every place, together with the continuum of seeds that accompanies it. Owing to the power of conditions, a dharma that has not previously arisen in continuity now arises for the first time: this is called the conditioned mark of arising.

即此變異性，名老有為相。此復二種。一、異性變異性，二、變性變異性。由有相似生故，立異性變異性。由有不相似生故，立變性變異性。

That very change is called the conditioned mark of aging. This is again of two kinds: change that remains similar in kind, and change that becomes dissimilar in kind. Because there is a similar arising, similar-kind change is established. Because there is a dissimilar arising, dissimilar-kind change is established.

即已生時，唯生剎那隨轉故，名住有為相。生剎那後，剎那不住故，名無常有為相。如是即約諸法分位差別，建立四相。

At the very moment it has arisen, it merely continues for that single moment: this is called the conditioned mark of abiding. After the moment of its arising, it does not remain into a second moment: this is called the conditioned mark of impermanence. Thus it is with respect to the differing momentary phases of dharmas that these four marks are established.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi anchors all four marks in the ālayavijñāna's own unbroken continuity across the three realms, carrying its seeds along with it; anything at all arises only when the right conditions converge. He splits "aging" into two very different scales: barely perceptible, moment-to-moment change that still looks like "the same thing" from one instant to the next — yesterday's face and today's, a mind mostly consistent thought to thought — against dramatic, visible change, such as hair turning white overnight or a mind swinging from wholesome to unwholesome in a single moment. Both are technically the same underlying process, differing only in how conspicuous the change happens to be. "Abiding" names nothing more than the bare fact that a thing lasts exactly one moment before giving way, and "impermanence" simply names that same moment's inevitable ending.

He treats impermanence as the single most powerful force in the world, citing the classical teaching that only nirvāṇa itself counts as truly permanent — everything else, without exception, wise or foolish, rich or poor, awakened or not, falls within its reach — and recalls a verse said to have been spoken by the elder Aniruddha at the Buddha's own passing, likening even the Buddha's boundless merit to a reflection on water or a banana tree's trunk: however vast, still broken by the wind of impermanence in the end.

36. The Four Conditions That Give Rise to All Dharmas

The treatise now turns to the four conditions Yogācāra recognizes behind the arising of any dharma, first by their essential nature, then by the principle that justifies naming each one.

Chinese Source

又有四緣。一、因緣，二、等無間緣，三、所緣緣，四、增上緣。因緣者，謂種子。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, there are four conditions: first, the causal condition; second, the condition of immediate succession; third, the object-condition; fourth, the dominant condition. The causal condition is the seed.

等無間緣者，謂若此識無間，諸識決定生，此是彼等無間緣。所緣緣者，謂諸心心所所緣境界。

The condition of immediate succession is this: when this consciousness ceases without interruption, a consciousness of the same kind necessarily arises — this ceasing consciousness is the condition of immediate succession for that arising one. The object-condition is the object taken by mind and its mental factors.

增上緣者，謂除種子，餘所依，如眼及助伴法。望眼識，所餘識亦爾。又善不善性能取愛非愛果。如是等類，名增上緣。

The dominant condition is, apart from the seed, every other basis it depends on — such as the eye, and its accompanying factors. What holds for eye-consciousness holds equally for the rest. Further, a wholesome or unwholesome nature is capable of securing a desirable or undesirable result. Conditions of this whole kind are called the dominant condition.

又由種子故，建立因緣。由自性故，立等無間緣。由所緣境故，立所緣緣。由所依及助伴等故，立增上緣。

Further, it is by reason of the seed that the causal condition is established. It is by reason of self-nature that the condition of immediate succession is established. It is by reason of the object taken that the object-condition is established. It is by reason of the basis depended on, and its accompanying factors, that the dominant condition is established.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the causal condition as the seed that does the actual producing — the one condition that is both cause and condition at once, while the remaining three merely contribute without themselves generating the result. The condition of immediate succession he restricts strictly to a consciousness's own kind: only a moment of eye-consciousness ceasing can serve as the immediate condition for the next moment of eye-consciousness, never for a different sense; its "immediacy" names an unbroken succession within one continuous stream, not literal single-instant adjacency. The object-condition is simply whatever mind and its mental factors actually take as their object in a given moment. And the dominant condition sweeps in everything else genuinely needed — the sense-organ, light, space, the deeper supporting layers of consciousness, the cooperating mental factors — plus, on a broader reading, the way wholesome or unwholesome conduct itself dominantly shapes whether a desirable or undesirable result eventually ripens.

He reads the second pass as simply restating the same four conditions from the angle of what specifically warrants naming each one: seed for the causal condition, a consciousness's own continuing nature for immediate succession, the object actually taken for the object-condition, and everything a consciousness depends on plus its cooperating factors for the dominant condition — the same four things, seen twice, once by what they essentially are and once by the principle that justifies calling them a "condition" at all.

37. Scripture Confirms: Only These Four

Chinese Source

如經言：諸因諸緣能生識者，彼即此四。因緣一種，亦因亦緣，餘唯是緣。

Ru-Yi Translation

As the sutra says: all the various causes and conditions capable of generating consciousness are precisely these four. The causal condition alone is both cause and condition; the remaining three are conditions only.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this as closing the four-conditions discussion with scriptural backing: whatever a sutra elsewhere calls a "cause" or "condition" for the arising of consciousness always reduces to one of these four, with nothing left over. He repeats the key technical distinction one final time — only the seed genuinely produces its result, while the faculty, the object, and the immediately preceding moment merely provide the supporting circumstances a result needs in order to actually appear.

38. How Many Kinds of "Wholesome" Are There? One Through Three

The treatise now opens an extended catalogue of the wholesome, counting it in as many as ten different, non-competing ways — each scheme cutting the same territory along a different axis, from a single defining trait up through ten distinct categories.

Chinese Source

又如經言：善、不善、無記者，彼差別云何？謂諸善法，或立一種。由無罪義故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, as the sutra says: wholesome, unwholesome, indeterminate — what is the distinction among them? As for wholesome dharmas: they may be established as a single kind, in the sense of being blameless.

或立二種。謂生得善及方便善。

Or they may be established as two kinds: innate goodness, and goodness cultivated through effort.

或立三種。謂自性善、相應善、等起善。

Or they may be established as three kinds: intrinsic goodness, associated goodness, and goodness that arises together with them.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads "one kind" as the simplest possible definition: wholesome just means blameless, free of the guilt and later suffering that unwholesome action carries with it. As two, goodness splits by origin: goodness a being is simply born with, carried over as habituation from past lives — an instinctive ease with generosity or patience, needing no effort to arise — against goodness actually built up now, through seeking out good teachers, hearing the teaching, and practicing accordingly. As three, it splits by role: the eleven wholesome mental factors themselves — faith, conscientiousness, and the rest — as intrinsically good; whatever mind or other mental factor arises in association with them as good by association; and the actual bodily and verbal action those factors jointly produce as good by co-arising.

39. Four and Five Kinds

Chinese Source

或立四種。謂順福分善、順解脫分善、順決擇分善，及無漏善。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or they may be established as four kinds: goodness conducive to merit, goodness conducive to liberation, goodness conducive to decisive discernment, and uncontaminated goodness.

或立五種。謂施性善、戒性善、修性善、愛果善、離繫果善。

Or they may be established as five kinds: goodness by nature of giving, goodness by nature of precepts, goodness by nature of cultivation, goodness as a desirable result, and goodness as the result of freedom from bondage.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi grades the fourfold scheme by how far along the path each kind sits: ordinary merit-making, such as the five precepts and the ten wholesome courses of action, aimed at a favorable rebirth; the deeper practice of a beginner actually gathering the provisions for liberation; the sharper, more decisive practice of one approaching the path of seeing; and finally the uncontaminated goodness of a noble one who has actually broken through — the first three still carry some trace of self-clinging, only the last entirely free of it. The fivefold scheme he grades instead by character and fruit: generosity, ethical conduct, and meditative cultivation as the three basic kinds of practice, against the pleasant rebirth such practice can yield, or the deeper freedom from bondage it can yield when aimed at liberation rather than mere reward.

40. Six and Seven Kinds

Chinese Source

或立六種。謂善色、受、想、行、識，及擇滅。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or they may be established as six kinds: wholesome form, feeling, ideation, formations, and consciousness, together with analytical cessation.

或立七種。謂念住所攝善、正勤所攝善、神足所攝善、根所攝善、力所攝善、覺支所攝善、道支所攝善。

Or they may be established as seven kinds: goodness comprised in the foundations of mindfulness, in right effort, in the bases of supernormal power, in the faculties, in the powers, in the branches of enlightenment, and in the branches of the path.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that in the sixfold scheme, the five aggregates themselves — form, feeling, ideation, formations, consciousness — count as wholesome whenever a wholesome mental factor is actually present within them; the sixth item, "analytical cessation," is nirvāṇa itself, the aggregates entirely free of any trace of contamination, reached through wisdom's own discerning power. The sevenfold scheme maps wholesome practice directly onto the thirty-seven factors of enlightenment in their traditional seven groupings: the foundations of mindfulness (observing body, feeling, mind, and phenomena), right effort (abandoning existing unwholesome states and preventing new ones, generating and sustaining wholesome ones), the bases of power (conditions that strengthen concentration), the faculties and the powers (the same five — faith, diligence, mindfulness, concentration, wisdom — first as budding capacities, then as forces strong enough to overcome affliction outright), the factors of enlightenment (present only once the path of seeing is reached), and the eightfold path itself — one continuous map of practice running from a beginner's first steps to full awakening.

41. Eight Kinds — Wholesome Deeds in Daily Life

Chinese Source

或立八種。謂起迎、合掌、問訊、禮敬業所攝善，讚彼妙說、稱揚實德所攝善，供承病者所攝善，敬事師長所攝善，隨喜所攝善，勸請所攝善，迴向所攝善，修無量所攝善。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or they may be established as eight kinds: the goodness comprised in rising to greet, joining palms, inquiring after another's wellbeing, and paying reverence; the goodness comprised in praising skillful teaching and extolling genuine virtue; the goodness comprised in attending upon the sick; the goodness comprised in honoring one's teachers; the goodness comprised in rejoicing at another's good; the goodness comprised in exhorting others to practice; the goodness comprised in dedicating merit; and the goodness comprised in cultivating the four immeasurables.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this eightfold list as the catalogue's most immediately practical entry — ordinary, repeatable acts open to anyone, requiring no advanced meditation. Simple courtesy toward a visitor, rising, folding the hands, a respectful greeting, already counts as genuine goodness, since it honors another being and eases the way toward further good relations. Praising a skillful teaching or someone's real virtue, rather than staying silent out of envy, is likewise wholesome, as is the plain work of caring for the sick — which he notes the Buddha ranked as the very first among the traditional "fields of merit," since sickness is among the sharpest of ordinary sufferings. Honoring one's own teachers with real service and attention is what actually lets their guidance reach the student.

Rejoicing at another's good fortune or virtue, the direct antidote to envy, he treats as unusually powerful precisely because it costs nothing but a shift of heart; encouraging others to practice, dedicating one's own merit toward awakening rather than hoarding it, and cultivating the four immeasurables — lovingkindness wishing others well, compassion wishing to relieve their suffering, sympathetic joy at their happiness, and equanimity free of favoritism — round out the list. He recalls a canonical story of a monk troubled by hostile spirits at his forest dwelling, told by the Buddha to cultivate lovingkindness toward them rather than flee; the very spirits that had been tormenting him became his protectors instead.

42. Nine Kinds — The Stages of Overcoming Affliction

Chinese Source

或立九種。謂方便、無間、解脫、勝進道所攝善，及軟、中、上、世、出世道所攝善。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or they may be established as nine kinds: goodness comprised in the preparatory path, the uninterrupted path, the path of liberation, and the path of further advancement; together with goodness comprised in the weak, middling, and superior paths, and in the mundane and supramundane paths.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the first four as sorting wholesome practice by its place in the actual moment of overcoming an affliction: the preparatory path building toward the breakthrough, the uninterrupted path where the affliction is actively being cut through, the path of liberation the very next moment after it is cut, and the path of further advancement that continues on to whatever afflictions still remain. The last five sort the same practice by sheer strength — weak, middling, or superior — and by whether it is mundane, ordinary meditative attainment still bound within the three realms, or supramundane, the noble path itself that actually leads beyond them.

43. Ten Kinds, Five Different Ways

The treatise closes its numerical catalogue of the wholesome with five separate tenfold schemes, each cutting the same territory along yet another axis.

Chinese Source

或立十種。謂有依善、無依善、聞所生善、思所生善、律儀所攝善、非律儀非不律儀所攝善、根本眷屬所攝善、聲聞乘所攝善、獨覺乘所攝善、大乘所攝善。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or they may be established as ten kinds: goodness with a dependency, goodness without a dependency, goodness born of learning, goodness born of reflection, goodness comprised within formal precepts, goodness comprised neither within formal precepts nor their absence, goodness comprised in the fundamental act and its preliminaries, goodness comprised in the Śrāvaka Vehicle, goodness comprised in the Pratyekabuddha Vehicle, and goodness comprised in the Great Vehicle.

又立十種。謂欲界繫善；初、二、三、四靜慮繫善；空無邊處、識無邊處、無所有處、非想非非想處繫善；無漏所攝善。

Further, they may be established as ten kinds: goodness bound to the desire realm; goodness bound to the first, second, third, and fourth meditative absorptions; goodness bound to the sphere of infinite space, the sphere of infinite consciousness, the sphere of nothingness, and the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception; and uncontaminated goodness.

又有十種。謂十善業道。又有十種。謂無學正見，乃至正解脫、正智。又有十種。謂能感八福生，及轉輪王善，及趣不動善。

Further, they may be established as ten kinds: namely, the ten wholesome courses of action. Further, they may be established as ten kinds: namely, the adept's right view, and so on, down through right liberation and right knowledge. Further, they may be established as ten kinds: namely, the goodness capable of bringing about the eight kinds of blessed rebirth, the goodness of a wheel-turning king, and the goodness that leads to the unshakable.

如是等類，諸善差別。

Differences of the wholesome, of this and similar kinds, are to be understood accordingly.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi works through all five tenfold schemes. The first sorts by how practice is undertaken: "with a dependency" means seeking some worldly return for one's giving — fame, favor, a heavenly reward — while "without a dependency" means giving dedicated purely to awakening, with no such expectation, which he notes the treatise elsewhere makes the very model a bodhisattva's own giving should follow. Learning-born and reflection-born goodness track the standard progression from hearing the teaching to actually thinking it through; formal-precept goodness is goodness performed under an actual vow, while "neither" covers the vast middle ground of ordinary people who have taken no formal vow and hold no active bad intention either, simply doing good as it comes; "fundamental and preliminary" distinguishes full completion of the ten wholesome courses of action from the partial effort still building toward it; and the last three simply sort goodness by which of the three vehicles' path it belongs to.

The second scheme sorts by which realm goodness is bound to: ordinary desire-realm goodness, the goodness proper to each of the four form-realm absorptions, the goodness proper to each of the four formless-realm absorptions, and finally uncontaminated goodness, which alone is bound to no realm at all. The third is simply the ten wholesome courses of action themselves — abstaining from killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying, harsh speech, divisive speech, idle chatter, covetousness, ill will, and wrong view — the most basic ethical template running through the whole tradition. The fourth is the adept's own eightfold path with two items added once liberation is complete: right liberation, a mind genuinely free of every trace of affliction, and right knowledge, the twin realization that afflictions are exhausted and that no further rebirth will occur. And the fifth grades goodness by the very rebirth it brings about — a wealthy human birth, then each of the six desire-realm heavens and the first Brahmā heaven in turn (together, the "eight blessed rebirths"), the goodness of a universal monarch, and finally goodness leading to rebirth in the unshakable form and formless realms, where meditative attainment is settled and secure.

44. Two Meanings of "Wholesome," and What Makes Something Unwholesome

Having catalogued the wholesome ten different ways, the treatise steps back to name what actually makes something count as wholesome, or its opposite, at all.

Chinese Source

略說善有二種義。謂取愛果義，善了知事及彼果義。

Ru-Yi Translation

Briefly stated, "wholesome" carries two meanings: the meaning of securing a desirable result, and the meaning of rightly understanding a matter, together with its result.

不善法者，謂與善法相違，及能為障礙。由能取不愛果故，及不正了知事故。

As for unwholesome dharmas: they are what runs contrary to wholesome dharmas, and what is capable of obstructing them — because they secure an undesirable result, and because they prevent the right understanding of a matter.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the first meaning as covering ordinary, mundane goodness — the kind that secures a favorable rebirth — and the second as covering the deeper, liberating sense: rightly understanding a matter as it actually is, together with the freedom that understanding itself yields. Unwholesome dharmas are defined by simple contrast: running against wholesome dharmas and actively obstructing them, since they secure an undesirable result instead, and prevent that same right understanding from ever taking hold.

45. Four Kinds of Neither Wholesome Nor Unwholesome

Chinese Source

無記法者，略有四種。謂異熟生，及一分威儀路、工巧處及變化。

Ru-Yi Translation

Indeterminate dharmas, briefly stated, are of four kinds: karmic-ripening arising; and a portion of comportment, of craft, and of transformation.

若諸工巧但為戲樂，不為活命，非習業想，非為簡擇，此工巧處業是染汙；餘是無記。如工巧處，威儀路亦爾。

If any craft is pursued purely for amusement, not to sustain one's livelihood, not out of habituated purpose, and not for the sake of discernment, then that act of craft is defiled; the rest is indeterminate. As with craft, so too with comportment.

變化有二種。謂善及無記。

Transformation is of two kinds: wholesome, and indeterminate.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats karmic-ripening arising as always indeterminate: one's inherited lifespan, looks, and native intelligence, simply the direct fruit of past karma, carry no moral charge of their own. Comportment, craft, and supernormal transformation, by contrast, can each go any of three ways — wholesome, unwholesome, or indeterminate — and the treatise means to include only their indeterminate portion here. A craft or ordinary posture practiced purely for idle amusement, with no honest livelihood behind it and no real discernment applied, actually counts as defiled rather than neutral; craft or comportment undertaken to sustain oneself, or generated through settled meditative calm, is genuinely wholesome instead; it is only the vast ordinary middle ground, neither indulgent nor especially virtuous, that counts as indeterminate. Supernormal transformation is simpler still: performed to benefit others, it is wholesome; performed merely for play, indeterminate — never itself defiled, since no affliction actually drives it either way.

46. The Eye, Counted One Through Six Ways

The treatise now turns from mental categories to the physical senses themselves, opening with the eye and cataloguing it, like the wholesome before it, in as many as eleven different ways.

Chinese Source

復次，眼有一種。謂能見色。或立二種。謂長養眼、異熟生眼。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, the eye is of one kind: that which is capable of seeing form. Or it may be established as two kinds: the nourished eye, and the eye arisen from karmic ripening.

或立三種。謂肉眼、天眼、慧眼。或立四種。謂有瞬眼、無瞬眼、恆相續眼、不恆相續眼。恆相續者，謂色界眼。

Or it may be established as three kinds: the fleshly eye, the divine eye, and the eye of wisdom. Or it may be established as four kinds: the blinking eye, the unblinking eye, the continuously enduring eye, and the not continuously enduring eye. The continuously enduring eye is the eye of the form realm.

或立五種。謂五趣所攝眼。或立六種。謂自相續眼、他相續眼、端嚴眼、醜陋眼、有垢眼、無垢眼。

Or it may be established as five kinds: the eye comprised within each of the five destinies. Or it may be established as six kinds: the self-continuing eye, the other-continuing eye, the handsome eye, the unsightly eye, the defiled eye, and the undefiled eye.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses the bare definition, "one kind," as simply "what sees form." As two, the treatise distinguishes an eye actually built up through this-life nourishment — food, sleep, and, for a monastic audience, chastity and meditative discipline — from the raw karmic endowment one is simply born with. As three, it distinguishes the ordinary fleshly eye of a desire-realm being, limited by the four obstructions from section 22, from the divine eye of a heavenly being, which sees through obstruction without limit, and the eye of wisdom, not physical at all but the mind's own capacity to see into the nature of things — he notes this is the root of the everyday expression "an eye for talent," recognizing someone's true character beneath the surface.

As four, it distinguishes an ordinary blinking human eye from an unblinking one — he notes cicadas and fish are traditionally said to share this trait with heavenly beings — and an eye that endures without interruption, proper to the peaceful, conflict-free form realm, from one liable to sudden loss, proper to the desire realm where beings' bodies remain vulnerable to violence and decay. As five, the eye simply differs by which of the five destinies — heavenly, human, hell, hungry ghost, or animal — a being is born into. And as six, it is sorted by whose body it belongs to, by physical beauty or plainness, and by whether it is still bound up with affliction, an ordinary being's eye, or entirely free of it, a noble one's.

47. The Eye, Counted Seven Through Eleven Ways

Chinese Source

或立七種。謂有識眼、無識眼、彊眼、弱眼、善識所依眼、不善識所依眼、無記識所依眼。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or it may be established as seven kinds: the eye accompanied by consciousness, the eye not accompanied by consciousness, the strong eye, the weak eye, the eye that serves as basis for a wholesome consciousness, the eye that serves as basis for an unwholesome consciousness, and the eye that serves as basis for an indeterminate consciousness.

或立八種。謂依處眼、變化眼、善業異熟生眼、不善業異熟生眼、食所長養眼、睡眠長養眼、梵行長養眼、定所長養眼。

Or it may be established as eight kinds: the physical-basis eye, the transformation eye, the eye arisen from wholesome karmic ripening, the eye arisen from unwholesome karmic ripening, the eye nourished by food, the eye nourished by sleep, the eye nourished by pure conduct, and the eye nourished by meditative absorption.

或立九種。謂已得眼、未得眼、曾得眼、未曾得眼、得已失眼、應斷眼、不應斷眼、已斷眼、非已斷眼。

Or it may be established as nine kinds: the already-obtained eye, the not-yet-obtained eye, the previously-obtained eye, the never-before-obtained eye, the obtained-then-lost eye, the eye that should be cut off, the eye that should not be cut off, the already-cut-off eye, and the not-yet-cut-off eye.

或立十種者，無。或立十一種。謂過去眼、未來眼、現在眼、內眼、外眼、麤眼、細眼、劣眼、妙眼、遠眼、近眼。

As for establishing it as ten kinds — there is none. Or it may be established as eleven kinds: the past eye, the future eye, the present eye, the internal eye, the external eye, the coarse eye, the fine eye, the inferior eye, the excellent eye, the distant eye, and the near eye.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that, as seven, an eye "accompanied by consciousness" is actually attending to what it sees, while an eye that merely looks without the mind registering anything — staring blankly while thinking of something else entirely — is "unaccompanied"; strength and weakness name ordinary visual acuity, and the last three sort the eye by whether the consciousness using it happens to be wholesome, unwholesome, or neutral in a given moment. As eight, the "physical-basis eye" is the ordinary fleshly organ and the "transformation eye" the divine eye or any specially transformed seeing-capacity; two are the fixed karmic inheritance of past wholesome or unwholesome action, and the remaining four are the same four nourishing factors named back in section 3 — food, sleep, pure conduct, meditative absorption — now applied specifically to the eye's own ongoing health.

As nine, the eye is sorted across time — already possessed in the past, not yet obtained but due in future, currently held and also held in some previous life, currently held for the very first time, or once possessed and then lost, as in an eye injured by accident or in battle — and by moral standing: an ordinary being's eye, given over to affliction, "should be cut off" in the sense of being something to outgrow, against the wisdom-eye of a noble one, which should not, and an eye already free of affliction against one still bound by it. Ten simply has no distinct scheme of its own — the treatise explicitly notes the gap rather than forcing an artificial category. Eleven closes the whole analysis with time, ownership of one's own eye versus another's, realm (the coarser desire-realm eye against the subtler form-realm eye), moral quality (inferior, from unwholesome karma, against excellent, from wholesome karma), and finally distance, whether in time or in space.

48. Ear, Nose, Tongue, and Body: What's Different from the Eye

The treatise now applies the same eleven-way analysis to the remaining four physical senses, flagging only where each one's third and fourth categories actually diverge from the eye's.

Chinese Source

是中差別者，謂增三、增四。三種耳者，謂肉所纏耳、天耳、審諦耳。四種耳者，謂恆相續耳、不恆相續耳、高聽耳、非高聽耳。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for the ear: the same holds throughout as for the eye, save for what is added at the third and fourth kind. The three kinds of ear are: the ear wrapped in flesh, the divine ear, and the attentive ear. The four kinds of ear are: the continuously enduring ear, the not continuously enduring ear, the ear that hears what is lofty, and the ear that does not hear what is lofty.

三種鼻舌者，謂光淨、不光淨，及被損。四種鼻舌者，謂恆相續、不恆相續、有識、無識。

The three kinds of nose and tongue are: the bright and pure, the not bright and pure, and the impaired. The four kinds of nose and tongue are: the continuously enduring, the not continuously enduring, the one accompanied by consciousness, and the one not accompanied by consciousness.

三種身者，謂滓穢處、非滓穢處，及一切遍諸根所隨逐故。四種身者，謂恆相續、不恆相續、有自然光、無自然光。

The three kinds of body are: what is a place of impurity, what is not a place of impurity, and what pervasively accompanies and is followed by all the other faculties. The four kinds of body are: the continuously enduring, the not continuously enduring, the one possessing its own natural light, and the one without natural light.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains the ear's substitutions: the fleshly ear of an ordinary being, the divine ear that hears at any distance, and the "attentive ear" that listens closely enough to actually take the teaching in — he recalls the Buddha's own refrain, urging listeners to "listen closely, listen closely, and reflect well on what you hear" — and, as fourth kind, an ear that specifically hears teaching capable of turning an ordinary being into a noble one, against an ear that hears only ordinary matters. For nose and tongue: bright and healthy against impaired, and, as fourth kind, whether consciousness is actually engaged, so that smell or taste is genuinely registered, or absent, so that eating happens without any flavor actually being tasted.

For the body: a body that is a "place of impurity," needing to eliminate waste, as in humans and animals, against a body entirely free of that need, as in desire-realm and form-realm heavenly beings; and, as third kind, the body's basic role as what every other sense-faculty depends on and follows — without a body, he asks, where would the eye even be located? He notes the canonical case of a king born blind and without a nose who nonetheless possessed the divine eye, evidence that the ordinary faculties and the body's supporting role are not strictly identical. The fourth kind for the body repeats the continuous-versus-discontinuous split from the eye, adding a body that shines with its own natural light, proper to desire- and form-realm heavenly beings, against one that does not.

49. The Mind, Counted One Through Four Ways

The treatise now turns to the sixth faculty, the mind, cataloguing it — as it will turn out — in as many as twelve different ways.

Chinese Source

或立一種意。謂由識法義故。或立二種。謂墮施設意、不墮施設意。初謂了別名言者意，後謂嬰兒意。又初謂世間意，後謂出世間意。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or it may be established as one kind of mind: that which discerns the meaning of dharmas expressed in language. Or it may be established as two kinds: the mind that falls under conceptual designation, and the mind that does not. The first is the mind that discerns names and words; the second is the infant's mind. Further, the first may also be understood as the worldly mind, and the second as the world-transcending mind.

或立三種。謂心、意、識。

Or it may be established as three kinds: mind, mental faculty, and consciousness.

或立四種。謂善、不善、有覆無記、無覆無記。

Or it may be established as four kinds: wholesome, unwholesome, obscured-yet-indeterminate, and unobscured indeterminate.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses "one kind" as simply the mind's basic capacity to grasp meaning conveyed by names, phrases, and sentences. "Two kinds" he reads two ways: developmentally, a mind that has learned to think in language against an infant's mind that has not yet fallen under the spell of naming; or spiritually, the ordinary worldly mind still caught in conceptual designation against the sage's mind, which has moved beyond it and is no longer swayed by praise, blame, or any of the eight worldly winds. "Three kinds" gives the technical distinction between mind as the ālayavijñāna, the storehouse gathering and holding every experience; mental faculty as the seventh consciousness, the pivot relaying between the storehouse and the six active senses; and consciousness as the six active senses themselves — he notes the earlier Āgama scriptures use these three terms far more loosely, often interchangeably, while this system sharpens the distinction.

"Four kinds" sorts the mind by moral quality: wholesome, unwholesome, and two grades of indeterminate — "obscured yet indeterminate," the seventh consciousness bound up with self-view, self-love, self-conceit, and self-delusion, morally neutral in itself yet still an obstacle to liberation; and "unobscured indeterminate," the eighth consciousness itself, entirely free of any such obstruction.

50. The Mind, Counted Five Through Seven Ways

Chinese Source

或立五種。謂五位差別。一、因位，二、果位，三、樂位，四、苦位，五、不苦不樂位。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or it may be established as five kinds, by five distinct phases: first, the causal phase; second, the resultant phase; third, the pleasant phase; fourth, the painful phase; fifth, the neither-painful-nor-pleasant phase.

或立六種。謂六識身。

Or it may be established as six kinds: the six classes of consciousness.

或立七種。謂依七識住。

Or it may be established as seven kinds, according to the seven abodes of consciousness.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the five phases as tracking the mind through cause and effect — a seed counts as causal relative to the manifest activity it produces, and that manifest activity in turn counts as resultant relative to its seed — and through the three basic tones of feeling it can carry: pleasant, painful, or neutral. Six kinds simply names the familiar six classes of consciousness — the five senses plus mental consciousness.

Seven kinds catalogues the traditional "abodes of consciousness," the kinds of existence where consciousness settles in comfortably enough to want to stay, distinct from realms it actively avoids. The seven run from most varied to most uniform: bodies and perceptions both varied, as among most humans and the lower heavens; bodies varied but perceptions uniform, as in Brahmā's realm, where the great Brahmā and his retinue all share one mistaken belief about who created whom, despite differing in stature and radiance; bodies uniform but perceptions varied, as in the second dhyāna heaven, where otherwise-identical beings still differ in outlook — some newly arrived and anxious about the world's periodic destruction by fire, others long settled and unworried; bodies and perceptions both uniform, as in the third dhyāna heaven, the most contentedly blissful of the meditative abodes; and finally the three formless spheres of infinite space, infinite consciousness, and nothingness, each defined by what its meditators take as their object of contemplation.

51. The Mind, Counted Eight and Nine Ways

Chinese Source

或立八種。謂增語觸相應、有對觸相應、依耽嗜、依出離、有愛味、無愛味、世間、出世間。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or it may be established as eight kinds: that associated with the contact of verbal designation, that associated with resistant contact, that grounded in indulgence, that grounded in renunciation, that carrying a savor of craving, that free of any such savor, the worldly, and the world-transcending.

或立九種。謂依九有情居。

Or it may be established as nine kinds, according to the nine abodes of beings.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that "verbal-designation contact" belongs specifically to the sixth, mental consciousness, which alone among the six can attach a name to what it perceives — he notes this is exactly why scholars and writers, whose minds run especially strongly on this capacity, tend to have so much to say about even the smallest thing, such as an ant crossing the floor. "Resistant contact" belongs instead to the five physical senses, which meet only genuinely obstructive, tangible objects. "Grounded in indulgence" is a mind still caught up in craving for the senses, most acutely sexual desire; "grounded in renunciation" is a mind that has moved beyond it, whether through ordinary meditative attainment in the higher realms or through the deeper freedom of a noble one entirely free of the three realms' defilements. "Carrying a savor" and "free of savor" repeat essentially the same contrast in terms of craving's actual flavor, and "worldly" versus "world-transcending" names the mind still caught in conceptual elaboration against the mind entirely free of it.

The ninefold scheme extends the seven abodes of consciousness from the previous section by adding two further classes of beings that consciousness does not, properly speaking, dwell in at all: beings without perception, and beings at the very summit of conditioned existence, in the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. Together with the seven already named, these nine account for every kind of existence a being can be reborn into, apart from the three lower, unwanted destinies.

52. The Twelve Minds

The treatise closes its analysis of the mind faculty with its most complete tally: twelve kinds, combining moral quality, realm, and liberated status all at once.

Chinese Source

謂欲界善心、不善心、有覆無記心、無覆無記心。色界有三心，除不善；無色界亦爾。出世間心有二種。謂學及無學。

Ru-Yi Translation

Namely: the desire realm's wholesome mind, unwholesome mind, obscured-yet-indeterminate mind, and unobscured indeterminate mind. The form realm has three of these minds, omitting the unwholesome; the formless realm likewise. The world-transcending mind is of two kinds: that of one still in training, and that of one beyond training.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi totals these directly: four desire-realm minds, since all four moral qualities are possible there; three form-realm minds and three formless-realm minds, each omitting the unwholesome, since outright hatred and its like cannot actually manifest at those refined levels; and two world-transcending minds — one still progressing through the stages toward arhatship, one who has completed the path — for twelve in all. He treats this as the treatise's most complete tally of "how many kinds of mind," combining moral quality, realm, and worldly-versus-liberated status into a single scheme.

53. Form, Counted One Through Five Ways

The treatise now turns to the six external sense-objects, beginning with form — what the eye operates upon — and cataloguing it, like the faculties before it, in as many as ten ways.

Chinese Source

或立一種色。謂由眼所行義故。或立二種。謂內色、外色。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or form may be established as one kind: that which the eye operates upon. Or it may be established as two kinds: internal form, and external form.

或立三種。謂顯色、形色、表色。或立四種。謂有依光明色、無依光明色、正不正光明色、積集住色。

Or it may be established as three kinds: color, shape, and gesture. Or it may be established as four kinds: light dependent on a source, light not dependent on any source, light that is full or not full, and form that is a mere aggregation.

或立五種。謂由五趣差別故。

Or it may be established as five kinds, according to differences among the five destinies.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses "two kinds" as the body's own faculties against everything external to it. As three, following the earlier fivefold-sense analysis, form splits into color proper, shape — length, roundness, size, however matter is spatially arranged — and gesture, matter's capacity to actually express what is on someone's mind through posture and movement: walking, turning a page, writing.

As four, light is split by whether it depends on an external source — light cast by a lamp, or reflected off a surface once removed from the lamp itself — or shines of its own accord, as it did, he notes, when the world was newly formed and its first inhabitants, having descended from the second dhyāna heaven, still glowed with their own inner light before needing sun or moon at all, exactly as form-realm heavens still do; and by whether that light is currently full, as a full moon, or incomplete, as a crescent. As five, the very same water looks entirely different depending on which of the five destinies perceives it — a human sees water, a hungry ghost sees pus and blood, a fish sees its home, and a heavenly being sees lapis lazuli — the same object appearing utterly differently according to each viewer's karma.

54. Form, Counted Six Through Ten Ways

Chinese Source

或立六種。謂建立所攝色、覆藏所攝色、境界所攝色、有情數色、非有情數色、有見有對色。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or it may be established as six kinds: form comprised in what is founded, form comprised in what shelters, form comprised as an object, form belonging to sentient beings, form not belonging to sentient beings, and form that is both visible and resistant.

或立七種。謂由七種攝受事差別故。或立八種。謂依八世雜說。一、地分雜色，二、山雜色，三、園林池沼等雜色，四、宮室雜色，五、業處雜色，六、綵畫雜色，七、鍛業雜色，八、資具雜色。

Or it may be established as seven kinds, according to seven distinct matters of attachment. Or it may be established as eight kinds, spoken of together as eight worldly varieties: the varied forms of the earth's surface, of mountains, of gardens, groves, and ponds, of palaces and dwellings, of one's occupation, of painted pictures, of the metalworker's craft, and of ordinary requisites.

或立九種。謂若過去、若未來、若現在、若麤、若細、若劣、若妙、若遠、若近。或立十種。謂十種資具。

Or it may be established as nine kinds: past, future, present; coarse, fine; inferior, excellent; distant, near. Or it may be established as ten kinds: the ten kinds of requisite.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains "six kinds" as: the wind, water, and earth disks that first found the physical universe; buildings that shelter beings within it; the six sense-objects taken as an object of consciousness; the sense-organs of a living being against inert external matter; and form that both can be seen and physically obstructs, the paradigm case of ordinary matter. "Seven kinds" sorts form by the seven areas of life a person becomes attached to, already catalogued back in fascicle 1 — parents, spouse and children, servants, friends and colleagues and kin, land and property, one's livelihood and its methods, and stored wealth. "Eight kinds" sorts form by everyday domain — the varied colors and textures of open ground, of mountains, of gardens and ponds, of buildings, of one's actual work, of painted artwork, of metalworking, and of ordinary household goods.

"Nine kinds" sorts form across time, by realm — coarse desire-realm form against the finer form-realm kind — by moral origin — inferior form from unwholesome karma against excellent form from wholesome karma — and by distance. And "ten kinds" returns to the same ten ordinary requisites of daily life already catalogued in fascicle 1 — food, drink, transport, clothing, ornament, song and dance, fragrance and unguents, household goods, lighting, and whatever a man or woman needs to actually live — now simply reclassified as forms perceived by the eye.

Fascicle 3 · 瑜伽師地論卷第三 · Mind Stage, continued

The Four Elements That Build All Matter

This page continues Ru-Yi's translation of the Mind Stage from where fascicle 2 left off. The treatise now shows how the five topics it has been analyzing — self-nature, support, object, accompaniments, and activity — all reduce to just three categories, then turns to a systematic account of matter itself: how the four great elements generate, support, establish, sustain, and nourish all derived matter, why there is no such thing as a truly indivisible physical atom, and how many kinds of matter can be found packed into any given lump of the physical world.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 3 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. Everything Reduces to Three Categories

Fascicle 3 opens by drawing together the five topics used throughout the Mind Stage's analysis — self-nature, support, object, accompaniments, and activity — into a simpler, threefold division.

Chinese Source

復次，即前所說自性乃至業等五事，當知皆由三處所攝。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, one should understand that these five things previously discussed — self-nature, and so on up through activity — are all comprised within three categories.

謂由色聚故，心心所品故，及無為故。

Namely: the category of matter aggregates, the category of mind and mental factors, and the category of unconditioned dharmas.

除餘假有法。

This excludes all other conceptually existent dharmas.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as a tidying move: everything the Mind Stage has been describing through five sense-consciousnesses and the sixth, mental consciousness — their nature, what they lean on, what they take as object, what accompanies them, and what they do — ultimately falls into just three baskets. The physical world (matter aggregates) accounts for the sense-faculties and their objects; mind and its mental factors account for the eight consciousnesses and everything that arises together with them; and unconditioned dharmas account for the small remainder of reality that neither arises nor ceases, such as space or the true nature of things.

The explicit exclusion he treats as equally important: the twenty-four so-called "dharmas dissociated from mind" — things like speed, cohesion, and time — are deliberately left out, because on this system they are not separately real entities at all, only convenient labels applied to the relative configuration of matter and mind that already exists. Naming them "conceptually existent" is the treatise's way of denying them a place among what is actually, irreducibly there.

2. How the Four Elements Generate Matter

The treatise now opens an extended discussion of the great elements — earth, water, fire, and wind — and the derived matter that depends on them, beginning with an objection.

Chinese Source

問：一切法生，皆從自種而起。云何說諸大種能生所造色耶？云何造色依彼，彼所建立，彼所任持，彼所長養耶？

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: All dharmas arise from their own seed. How, then, can it be said that the great elements generate derived matter? And how is derived matter said to depend on them, to be established by them, to be sustained by them, and to be nourished by them?

答：由一切內外大種及所造色種子，皆悉依附內相續心。乃至諸大種子未生諸大以來，造色種子終不能生造色。

Answer: All the seeds of the great elements and of derived matter, whether internal or external, depend on the inwardly continuing mind. So long as the seeds of the great elements have not yet produced the great elements themselves, the seeds of derived matter can never produce derived matter.

要由彼生，造色方從自種子生，是故說彼能生造色，要由彼生為前導故。由此道理，說諸大種為彼生因。

Only once the great elements have arisen does derived matter then arise from its own seed. This is why it is said that the great elements generate derived matter: their arising must come first, as what leads the way. For this reason, the great elements are said to be the generating cause of derived matter.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the opening question as posing a real tension: Yogācāra holds that every dharma arises strictly from its own seed, so in what sense can the four elements "generate" a separate thing, derived matter, which is supposed to have its own seed too? His resolution is to distinguish the direct causal seed of derived matter — which is real and does the actual producing — from the great elements' role as an indispensable lead condition: nothing can generate derived matter's seed into action until the elements have already occupied the ground it needs. This becomes the first of five distinct roles the elements play toward derived matter, worked out one by one across the sections that follow.

3. Support, Establishment, Sustenance, and Growth

Having explained generation, the treatise answers the remaining three parts of the opening question, then adds a fifth function not originally asked about.

Chinese Source

云何造色依於彼耶？由造色生已，不離大種處而轉故。

Ru-Yi Translation

How is derived matter said to depend on the great elements? Because once derived matter has arisen, it does not occur apart from the place occupied by the great elements.

云何彼所建立？由大種損益，彼同安危故。云何彼所任持？由隨大種等量不壞故。

How are the great elements said to establish derived matter? Because whenever the great elements are diminished or enhanced, derived matter shares the very same fortune, whether harm or safety. How are the great elements said to sustain derived matter? Because derived matter follows the exact measure of the great elements, remaining undiminished as long as they do.

云何彼所長養？由因飲食、睡眠、修習梵行三摩地等，依彼造色倍復增廣，故說大種為彼養因。如是諸大種望所造色有五種作用應知。

How are the great elements said to nourish derived matter? Because through food, sleep, the cultivation of pure conduct, meditative absorption, and the like, derived matter that depends on the great elements grows and expands all the more. This is why the great elements are said to be the nourishing cause of derived matter. Thus it should be understood that the great elements have five kinds of function toward the derived matter that depends on them.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through each function with an everyday case: a body swells when the water element runs high, stiffens or grows heavy when earth predominates, runs hot with excess fire, or struggles to breathe when wind is disturbed — this is "establishment," derived matter rising and falling in lockstep with the elements it depends on. "Support" he reads spatially: derived matter never appears anywhere except exactly where its elements already occupy space, the way dough never extends past the flour and water kneaded into it. "Sustenance" keeps derived matter's own extent precisely matched to the elements underneath it, neither more nor less. And "nourishment" is the one function that actually adds growth over time, driven by ordinary food, rest, and — distinctively for a monastic audience — sustained meditative and ethical discipline, all of which visibly enlarge and strengthen the body built on the elements.

Taken together, he treats these five — generating, supporting, establishing, sustaining, nourishing — as a fixed analytic template the treatise will keep returning to whenever it needs to describe exactly how the four elements relate to the matter built upon them.

4. Are There Truly Indivisible Particles?

The treatise turns next to a different question: whether matter is ultimately composed of real, indivisible physical atoms.

Chinese Source

復次，於色聚中，曾無極微生。若從自種生時，唯聚集生，或細或中或大。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, within a matter aggregate, no particle has ever, strictly speaking, arisen on its own. When matter arises from its own seed, it arises only as an aggregate, all at once — whether fine, middling, or coarse.

又非極微集成色聚。但由覺慧分析諸色極量邊際，分別假立以為極微。

Nor is a matter aggregate built up out of particles. Rather, it is only through discerning wisdom analyzing matter down to its very outermost limit that a "particle" is conceptually distinguished and provisionally established.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as a direct denial of the idea, held in some other schools, that gross matter is literally assembled out of preexisting real, indivisible atoms the way a wall is built up brick by brick. On this treatise's account, a lump of matter arises whole and all at once from its own seed, at whatever scale — fine, middling, or coarse — that seed produces; a "particle" is not a hidden building block but simply the name given to the point where analytical reasoning, pushing the division of matter as far as thought can take it, has to stop. He offers a classical illustration for why this matters: a dewdrop on a leaf does not shrink toward some final, smallest remaining droplet before vanishing in the sun — it simply dries away all at once — which is meant to make vivid that the decrease of a mass of matter was never a process of peeling off real atoms one at a time. Because a particle was never actually produced in the first place, it is said to have neither arising nor ceasing.

5. Particles Have Location, But Not Parts

Chinese Source

又色聚亦有方分，極微亦有方分。然色聚有分非極微。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, a matter aggregate has spatial location, and so does a particle. But a matter aggregate has further parts; a particle does not.

何以故？由極微即是分，此是聚色所有，非極微復有餘極微，是故極微非有分。

Why is this? Because the particle itself already is the part — the part that belongs to a matter aggregate — and a particle does not, in turn, contain some further particle within it. This is why a particle is said to have no further parts.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi clarifies a distinction easy to blur: both a matter aggregate and a particle occupy space, so both can loosely be said to have "location." But only the aggregate — a lamp, a table — can actually be cut or divided into smaller pieces. A particle cannot, not because it is some mysterious point-sized ultimate reality, but simply because "particle" is defined, by the very act of analysis that posits it, as the stopping point beyond which no further division is being carried out. Calling it "partless" is a statement about where the reasoning stops, not a discovery about what matter is really made of.

6. Matter and Its Elements Never Separate

The treatise now explains two distinct senses in which the great elements and the derived matter built on them are never found apart from each other.

Chinese Source

一、同處不相離。謂大種極微與色香味觸等，於無根處有離根者，於有根處有有根者，是名同處不相離。

Ru-Yi Translation

First, non-separation in the same place: this means that the particles of the great elements, together with visible form, smell, taste, and the tangible, occur — in a place without sense-faculties, as particles apart from any faculty; in a place with sense-faculties, as particles belonging to a faculty. This is called non-separation in the same place.

二、和雜不相離。謂即此大種極微與餘聚集，能造、所造色處俱故，是名和雜不相離。

Second, non-separation through commingling: this means that these very particles of the great elements, together with other aggregated particles, occupy the very same place at once — the generating together with the generated matter. This is called non-separation through commingling.

又此遍滿聚色，應知如種種物，石磨為末，以水和合，互不相離；非如胡麻、綠豆、粟、稗等聚。

Further, it should be understood that this thoroughly pervading matter aggregate is like various substances ground by a millstone into powder and then mixed with water, mutually inseparable — not like a heap of sesame, mung beans, millet, and unhusked grain.

又一切所造色皆即依止大種處，不過大種處量；乃至大種所據處所，諸所造色還即據此；由此因緣，說所造色依於大種。即以此義，說諸大種名為大種。由此大種其性大故，為種生故。

Further, all derived matter depends entirely on the place occupied by the great elements and does not exceed the measure of that place; wherever the great elements occupy a place, derived matter occupies that very same place. For this reason it is said that derived matter depends on the great elements. It is by this very meaning that the great elements are called "great elements": because their nature is vast, and because they serve as the seed for what is generated.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes two different ways the elements and derived matter are said to never separate. "Same place" means an eye's elements sit exactly where the eye's derived matter sits, whether in a living faculty or in inert external matter — location, not fusion. "Commingling" goes further: earth, water, fire, and wind, along with whatever derived matter they generate, actually interpenetrate into one indistinguishable mass — like flour ground fine and kneaded with water into dough — rather than remaining separately identifiable the way sesame seeds and mung beans stay recognizable when merely poured together in the same bowl. He flags this second point as a live disagreement across schools: certain Śrāvakayāna traditions hold that co-located elements remain discrete substances only sitting side by side, while the Mahāyāna position taken here is that they genuinely fuse into a single whole.

He closes with the etymology this whole discussion has been building toward: the four are called "great" because all derived matter, everywhere, depends on them without exception, and "elements" — literally, seeds — because they must arise first, as the generative lead, before what they generate can ever arise.

7. The Fourteen Kinds of Matter

The treatise now catalogues everything that counts as "matter" in this technical sense.

Chinese Source

復次，於諸色聚中，略有十四種事。謂地、水、火、風、色、聲、香、味、觸，及眼等五根。除唯意所行色。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, within matter aggregates in general, there are, in brief, fourteen categories of thing: earth, water, fire, wind; visible form, sound, smell, taste, the tangible; and the five faculties beginning with the eye. This excludes matter cognized by the mind-consciousness alone.

一切色聚，有色諸根所攝者，有一切，如所說事界。如有色諸根所攝聚如是，有色諸根所依大種所攝聚亦爾。所餘色聚，除有色諸根，唯有餘界。

Every matter aggregate belonging to a physical sense-faculty comprises all fourteen, in the sense of the causal elements already explained. Just as this holds for the aggregate belonging to a physical sense-faculty, so too it holds for the aggregate of the great elements on which a physical sense-faculty depends. As for the remaining matter aggregates — apart from the physical sense-faculties themselves — they comprise only the remaining elements.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this list as exhaustive by design: the four elements, the five sense-objects (form, sound, smell, taste, touch), and the five sense-faculties together account for everything the treatise means by "matter" here. He notes the deliberate exclusion of a further class — subtle matter cognized by mind-consciousness alone, such as the invisible force generated by taking a moral vow, or matter manifested through advanced meditative mastery — as outside the scope of this particular fourteen-item analysis, reserved for treatment elsewhere.

He then draws out the practical payoff: a living sense-faculty like the eye, and the gross elemental base that physically supports it, both count as containing all fourteen categories in causal terms, since a functioning eye needs the elements, its own faculty-substance, and its objects all at once. Ordinary external matter with no sense-faculty of its own — a stone, a river — falls short of that full set, comprising only the remaining, non-faculty categories.

8. Three Ways to Count What's There

The treatise now distinguishes three different senses in which a given lump of matter can be said to "contain" the fourteen categories — numbers that do not always agree.

Chinese Source

又約相攝有十四事，即由相攝施設事極微。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, counted by distinguishing mark, there are fourteen categories; and it is by this same distinguishing mark that "particles" of each category are conceptually posited.

若約界攝，隨於此聚有爾所界，即說此聚爾所事攝。

Counted by causal element, however many elements are present within a given aggregate, that many categories are said to be comprised within that aggregate.

若約不相離攝，或內、或外所有諸聚，隨於此聚中，乃至有爾所法相可得，即說此聚爾所事攝應知。

Counted by non-separation — whether of an internal or an external aggregate — however many distinguishing marks can actually be found within a given aggregate, that many categories are said to be comprised within it; this should be understood accordingly.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this as a deliberate corrective to a natural but mistaken assumption — that "how many of the fourteen categories does this thing have" must have one single correct answer. Counted by mark, an eye is simply one thing, an eye. Counted by causal element, an eye's very arising depends on all four great elements plus its own faculty-substance plus the objects it takes, so in that sense it can be said to comprise as many as ten of the fourteen. Counted by actual, present co-occurrence, one counts only whatever marks are literally detectable in that exact lump right now — which for a smooth stone might be as little as earth alone, and for a living body, all fourteen at once. The right number, on his reading, always depends on which of these three legitimate questions is actually being asked.

9. How Many Elements Show Up in a Given Thing

The treatise illustrates the previous point with concrete examples, sliding from things with only one detectable element up to the four-element case of a living body.

Chinese Source

或有聚中唯一大種可得。如石、末尼、真珠、琉璃、珂貝、璧玉、珊瑚等中，或池、沼、溝、渠、江、河等中，或火焰、燈燭等中，或四方風輪有塵、無塵風等中。

Ru-Yi Translation

In some aggregates, only a single great element can be detected — as in stone, gems, pearls, lapis lazuli, coral, and the like; or in ponds, marshes, ditches, canals, and rivers; or in flame and lamplight; or in the winds of the four quarters, whether dust-bearing or dust-free.

或有聚中二大種可得。如雪、濕樹、葉、華、果等中，或熱末尼等中。或有聚中三大種可得。如即熱樹等中，或動搖中。

In some aggregates, two great elements can be detected — as in snow, a moist tree, leaf, flower, or fruit, or in a heated gem. In some aggregates, three great elements can be detected — as in a tree that has caught fire, or in something being shaken by wind.

或有聚中四大種可得。謂於內色聚中。如薄伽梵說：於各別內身，若髮毛等乃至糞穢，是內地界；若小便等，是內水界；若於身中所有煖等，是內火界；若上行等風，是內風界。

In some aggregates, all four great elements can be detected — namely, within the aggregates that make up an internal, living body. As the Blessed One said: within each individual body, hair, body-hair, and the rest, down to excrement, are the internal earth element; urine and the like are the internal water element; whatever warmth is present within the body is the internal fire element; the upward-moving and other winds are the internal wind element.

如是若於此聚彼相可得，說彼相為有；若不可得，說彼相為無。

Thus, whenever a given mark can be detected within an aggregate, that mark is said to be present; whenever it cannot be detected, it is said to be absent.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks the sliding scale from one detectable element up to all four, culminating in the Buddha's own fourfold division of the human body — solids as earth, fluids as water, warmth as fire, breath and circulatory movement as wind. He notes this same division appears elsewhere in the canon as a basic meditation for loosening attachment to the body as a single, solid self: seen this way, the body dissolves into nothing more than an assembled composite of the very same ordinary elements found everywhere in stone, water, flame, and wind — nothing uniquely "self" about any of it.

10. Sound and Wind Are Not Always There

The treatise closes this stretch by noting two categories — sound and wind — that behave differently from the others: present in potential everywhere, but not always present in fact.

Chinese Source

復次，聲於一切色聚中，界故說有，相即不定。由現在方便生故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, as for sound: in every matter aggregate, it is present in the sense of its causal element, but its actual mark is not fixed. This is because sound arises only given a present, contributing condition.

風有二種。謂恆相續及不恆相續。恆相續者，謂於彼彼聚有恆旋轉風。不恆相續者，謂旋風及空行風。

Wind is of two kinds: continuous and non-continuous. The continuous kind refers to the constantly circulating wind present within this or that aggregate. The non-continuous kind refers to whirlwinds and winds that move through open space.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads sound as carrying the same latent-versus-actual distinction found throughout this section: every material thing carries the underlying capacity to produce sound — a struck table, clapped hands, a rung bell — yet the actual sound appears only once the right present condition is met, hence "present as element, not fixed as mark." Wind, by contrast, gets a genuinely two-way split rather than a latent/actual one: the constant, unbroken wind found in every aggregate — paradigmatically, one's own breath, sustaining life moment to moment without pause — as against occasional winds such as a whirlwind or a gust moving through open space, which come and go. Two different kinds of wind exist in the world, on his reading, one always active and one only sometimes so — not a claim about hidden potential waiting to manifest, the way sound's case works.

11. Darkness and Light Are Kinds of Matter Too

The treatise now classifies darkness and light themselves as a kind of visible matter, and asks where each is found continuously and where only intermittently.

Chinese Source

又闇色、明色，說名空界及孔隙。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, darkness and light are called the sphere of open space, and the sphere of an opening or cavity.

又諸闇色恆相續者，謂世界中間；不恆相續者，謂於餘處。

As for darkness that continues without interruption: this refers to the interval between world-systems. As for darkness that does not continue without interruption: this refers to every other place.

如是明色恆相續者，謂於自然光明天中；不恆相續者，謂於餘處。

Light that continues without interruption, in the same way, refers to the naturally luminous heavens. Light that does not continue without interruption refers to every other place.

又明闇色，謂於顯色增聚應知。

Further, it should be understood that light and darkness are simply an intensification within the category of visible color.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes two competing readings of "the interval between world-systems." One takes it as the empty eon between a world's destruction and the next world's formation, when no sun exists and no being dwells there, so darkness truly never breaks. The other — which he credits to both Master Kuījī and Venerable Miào Jìng, and finds echoed in the Lotus Sūtra — takes it literally, as the dark gap of space lying between one great trichiliocosm and its neighbor, permanently uninhabited and permanently dark; he leans toward this second reading as the more natural fit. Wherever beings actually live, by contrast, day and night alternate, so darkness there is never unbroken.

The naturally luminous heavens are the desire-realm heavens and above, whose bodies shine of their own accord and produce nothing foul, unlike beings who eat solid food and must excrete. Below them, light and dark simply alternate as usual. He closes by noting that light and dark are not some third, separate category of color at all — both are just the ordinary visible colors (blue, yellow, red, white) intensified in one direction or the other.

12. Matter Aggregates Grow and Shrink

Chinese Source

又由依止色聚種子功能故，若遇相似緣時，或小聚無間大聚生，或大聚無間小聚生。由此因緣，施設諸聚有增有減。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, owing to the function inherent in the seed of a matter aggregate, when a similar condition is met, a small aggregate may immediately give rise to a large aggregate, or a large aggregate may immediately give rise to a small one. For this reason, matter aggregates are said to increase and decrease.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi's example: a body suddenly grows larger given a nourishing condition such as good food, and suddenly shrinks given a depleting condition such as illness. He then extends the same principle beyond individual bodies, citing an observation attributed to Venerable Miào Jìng: the very same mountain that took a full night to climb during a past Buddha's era, when human lifespans ran to twenty thousand years, could be climbed almost instantly in Śākyamuni's own time of hundred-year lifespans — suggesting that even the physical world itself contracts in step with the accumulated karma of the beings who depend on it, not only individual bodies. He stresses that this only happens given a matching, contributing condition, the way a planted tree needs watering and fertilizing to actually grow — the seed's potential alone is never sufficient by itself.

13. What the Sutras Mean by "Solid"

The treatise now works through a set of technical terms drawn from the sutras, using the earth element as its worked example.

Chinese Source

如經言：若堅、堅攝、近攝、非近攝、執受，乃至廣說。堅云何？謂地。堅攝云何？謂彼種子。

Ru-Yi Translation

As the sutra says: solidity, what belongs to solidity, what is held near, what is not held near, what is grasped by consciousness — and so on, at length. What is solidity? It is the earth element. What belongs to solidity? It is the seed of that same element.

又堅者，即彼界。堅攝者，謂髮毛等，或土塊等。

Further, solidity is that very causal element itself; what belongs to solidity is hair, body-hair, and the like, or a clod of earth, and the like.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the sutra's vocabulary — solid, what-belongs-to-solid, held-near, not-held-near, grasped-by-consciousness — as the springboard for a full technical gloss of the earth element, glossed here two different ways. Read as manifest versus seed, "solidity" names the currently manifest earth element as the eye actually sees it as solid, while "what belongs to solidity" names its seed, not yet manifest. Read as generating cause versus generated matter, "solidity" is instead the great element acting as cause, while "what belongs to solidity" is the derived matter it actually produces — literal hair, nails, or a clod of soil.

14. What Makes Matter "Mine"

The treatise continues the same set of technical terms, now distinguishing matter that belongs to a living body from inert matter in the outside world.

Chinese Source

近攝云何？謂有執受。執受云何？謂內所攝。非近攝云何？謂無執受。無執受云何？謂外所攝。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is held near? It is what is grasped by consciousness. What is grasped by consciousness? It is what is comprised within the internal body. What is not held near? It is what is not grasped by consciousness. What is not grasped by consciousness? It is what is comprised in the external world.

又心心所所執種子，名近攝，名執受；與此相違，名非近攝，名非執受。又隨逐自身故，名近攝、執受，如前說。

Further, a seed that is grasped by mind and mental factors is called held near, and is called grasped by consciousness; the opposite of this is called not held near, and not grasped by consciousness. Further, because it follows along with one's own body, it is called held near and grasped by consciousness, as explained above.

如是水等界，如理應知。

The water element, and the rest, should be understood in just the same way.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads "held near" and "grasped by consciousness" as marking earth-element matter belonging to a living body and animated by mind — pull a hair right at the scalp and there is pain — against inert external earth-element matter, such as a clod of soil, or that same hair once it has been cut and can no longer be felt: "not held near." He flags one technical caveat: this particular pass works the distinction out through seeds specifically, which is not quite identical to the fuller fourfold grasped/ungrasped analysis found elsewhere in the treatise's supplementary discussions — worth remembering if the terminology looks slightly different across fascicles. The whole apparatus built here for earth — solid, what-belongs-to-solid, held-near, not-held-near — then simply carries over unchanged to water, fire, and wind, which is why the treatise does not spell each of them out separately.

15. All Four Elements Are Present in Everything, Always

The treatise now demonstrates, with concrete examples, its earlier claim that every one of the four great elements is present as a causal element in every matter aggregate, even where only one or two are actually detectable.

Chinese Source

又於一切色聚中，一切時具有一切大種界。如世間現見乾薪等物，鑽即火生，擊石等亦爾。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, within every matter aggregate, at all times, every one of the four great elements is present as a causal element. As is commonly observed in the world: dry firewood, when drilled, produces fire; striking flint against stone likewise produces fire.

又銅鐵金銀等，極火所燒即銷為水。從月愛珠，水便流出。

Further, copper, iron, gold, silver, and the like, when melted by intense fire, turn to a liquid state; and from the moon-loving pearl, water flows forth.

又得神通者，由心勝解力，變大地等成金銀等。

Further, one who has attained supernormal power can, through the force of the mind's resolute conviction, transform the great earth and the like into gold, silver, and so on.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads these as concrete demonstrations backing the treatise's abstract claim from section 9 that all four elements are always present, even where only one seems detectable: friction or striking releases the latent fire element hidden in ordinary wood or stone; extreme heat reveals the latent water element hidden even in solid metal, and a certain legendary gem is said to release water simply when turned toward moonlight; and a meditator with sufficiently developed concentration can, by the sheer force of mental conviction, manifest the earth element outright as gold or silver. He adds one caution about that last case: the gold conjured by an eighth-ground bodhisattva's power is said to genuinely function as real gold, while the same feat attempted through an ordinary meditator's concentration only produces something that looks like gold but cannot actually be used as such — the depth of the underlying realization, not the technique itself, is what makes the difference.

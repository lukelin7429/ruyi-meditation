Fascicle 2 · 瑜伽師地論卷第二 · Mind Stage, continued

How a Body Grows in the Womb

This page continues Ru-Yi's translation of the Mind Stage from where fascicle 1 left off — at the moment of conception — carrying it through the growth of the embryo, the process of birth, and, further on, the treatise's account of the destruction and formation of the world: the last two of the Mind Stage's seventeen distinctive powers.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 2 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. Seeds of All Three Realms, Wherever One Is Born

Fascicle 2 opens by completing a point about the ālayavijñāna first raised in fascicle 1: no matter which of the three realms a being is born into, the seeds of the other two realms remain latent within it.

Chinese Source

復次，此一切種子識，若般涅槃法者，一切種子皆悉具足。不般涅槃法者，便闕三種菩提種子。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, as for this all-seeds consciousness: in one who has the nature to attain final nirvāṇa, all seeds are fully present. In one who does not have this nature, the seeds of the three kinds of awakening are lacking.

隨所生處，自體之中，餘體種子皆悉隨逐。是故欲界自體中，亦有色、無色界一切種子。如是色界自體中，亦有欲、無色界一切種子。無色界自體中，亦有欲、色界一切種子。

Wherever one is born, within that very existence, the seeds belonging to the other realms of existence all continue to follow along. Hence, within an existence in the desire realm, all the seeds of the form and formless realms are also present. Likewise, within an existence in the form realm, all the seeds of the desire and formless realms are also present. And within an existence in the formless realm, all the seeds of the desire and form realms are also present.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the first passage as a stark line dividing beings by spiritual potential: only those with the seeds for full liberation carry both worldly and world-transcending seeds together in the ālayavijñāna; those without them still carry ordinary seeds, but lack the very capacity, at present, for any of the three vehicles' awakening.

The second point he treats as quietly encouraging: it explains, on the doctrinal level, why someone born in the ordinary desire realm can still cultivate the deep meditative absorptions associated with the form or formless realms — the seeds for them were never absent, only waiting on conditions. Nothing about one's present birth, on this reading, permanently forecloses what can still be cultivated.

2. The Embryo Grows Toward Complete Support

The treatise now returns to the kalala from the close of fascicle 1, describing how it grows, together with the mental aggregates, toward what is called "complete support."

Chinese Source

又羯羅藍漸增長時，名之與色平等增長，俱漸廣大。如是增長，乃至依止圓滿應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, as the kalala gradually grows, name (the mental aggregates) grows evenly together with form (the kalala's matter), both gradually expanding. This growth continues until what is called complete support is reached.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi marks the kalala's very first moment as birth itself in the technical sense used here; everything after that, up until the sense faculties and their physical supports become fully formed and distinct, is "gradual growth"; and the point where they are fully formed — able to reliably support the arising of mind and mental factors — is "complete support," after which no further growth of this kind occurs; the being simply awaits birth. He notes that classical sources describe this whole gestational process, across its traditional thirty-eight weeks, as one of considerable discomfort for the being involved — a corrective, in his reading, to any romanticizing of life in the womb as a purely peaceful state.

3. How the Four Elements Build the Body

Chinese Source

此中由地界故，依止造色漸漸增廣。由水界故，攝持不散。由火界故，成熟堅硬。由無潤故，由風界故，分別支節各安其所。

Ru-Yi Translation

Here, through the earth element, the derived matter dependent on it gradually grows and expands. Through the water element, it is held together and does not disperse. Through the fire element, it ripens into firmness. And, once moisture no longer holds them fused, through the wind element, the limbs and joints are separated out, each set in its own proper place.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads each of the four elements as contributing one distinct job to fetal growth: earth for sheer expansion, water for holding tissue together, fire for ripening it into firmness, and wind for separating what would otherwise remain a single undifferentiated mass into distinct fingers, limbs, and joints, each in its right place.

4. What Actually Produces a Life

The treatise now distinguishes two very different roles that habituated seeds play in shaping a new existence.

Chinese Source

又一切種子識，於生自體雖有淨不淨業因，然唯樂著戲論為最勝因。於生族姓、色力、壽量、資具等果，即淨不淨業為最勝因。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, in the arising of a being's own existence — the [six] sense-faculties themselves — although pure and impure karma serve as a cause, it is delight in elaboration alone that is the dominant cause. But in the fruits of one's lineage, physical beauty and strength, lifespan, and material resources, it is pure and impure karma that is the dominant cause.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this through the Cheng Weishi Lun's three kinds of habituated seed: the seeds of conceptual proliferation (delight in elaboration), which are what actually produce the six sense-faculties — a mind and body capable of having a life at all; the seeds of self-grasping; and karmic seeds, which do not produce the faculties themselves but only shape the particular quality of the life built on them — noble or humble birth, beauty or plainness, health or frailty, a long or short life, wealth or want. Karma decides what kind of life one has; the deeper habit of taking things as conceptually real is what produces a life to have in the first place.

He adds a companion point: ordinary beings take these very faculties to be "I" and "mine," giving rise to conceit, while the noble ones see the same faculties as nothing but suffering — not the obvious pain and pleasure of ordinary experience, but the subtler, ever-present unsatisfactoriness of anything conditioned, which only wisdom can actually perceive.

5. Feeling in the Womb

Chinese Source

又處胎分中，有自性受，不苦不樂，依識增長。唯此性受，異熟所攝。餘一切受，或異熟所生，或境界緣生。又苦受、樂受，或於一時從緣現起，或時不起。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, within the womb, there is an intrinsic feeling — neither painful nor pleasant — that grows in step with consciousness. Only this intrinsic feeling belongs strictly to the ripened result. All other feeling arises either from the ripened result or from present sensory conditions. Painful and pleasant feeling, moreover, sometimes arise from such conditions, and sometimes do not arise at all.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the constant, neutral "intrinsic feeling" as present throughout gestation simply because it belongs directly to the ripening consciousness itself, while occasional pleasure or pain — say, from the mother eating something too hot or too cold — are conditional add-ons layered on top, present only sometimes. His deeper point: this baseline neutral feeling is already a subtle form of suffering that ordinary beings never notice, masked by the sharper pains and pleasures that come and go on top of it — much as a burning sore feels neither painful nor pleasant in itself until something cold or hot is pressed against it, at which point its own underlying rawness becomes briefly hidden by whichever sensation is layered over it.

6. How Seeds Keep Producing Results, Life After Life

Chinese Source

又種子體，無始時來相續不絕。性雖無始有之，然由淨不淨業差別熏發，望數數取異熟果，說彼為新。若果已生，說此種子為已受果。由此道理，生死流轉相續不絕，乃至未般涅槃。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, the continuum of seeds has continued without break since beginningless time. Though its nature has existed from the beginning, because it is activated again and again, in different ways, by pure and impure karma, and repeatedly gives rise to a ripened result, it is spoken of, each time, as though newly arisen. Once a result has arisen, that seed is said to have already yielded its result. Through this principle, the cycle of birth and death continues without interruption, until final nirvāṇa is attained.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi stresses that no seed is ever really "new" — the entire continuum has existed since beginningless time; what changes, moment to moment, is only which seeds are currently ripening into effect. Once a given seed has produced its full result — a completed lifespan, for instance — its power for that particular result is spent, and some other seed among the countless others stored in the ālayavijñāna takes over to produce the next existence. It is this endless relay, one exhausted seed handing off to the next, that constitutes the beginningless cycle of birth and death — and it is broken only by reaching final nirvāṇa.

7. Fixed and Uncertain Karma

The treatise now distinguishes karma whose result is fixed to ripen from karma whose result may never ripen at all.

Chinese Source

又諸種子未與果者，或順生受，或順後受。雖經百千劫，從自種子一切自體復圓滿生。雖餘果生要由自種，若至壽量盡邊，爾時此種名已受果。所餘自體種子未與果故，不名已受果。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, of seeds that have not yet yielded their result, some yield it in the very next life, others only in some later life. Even after hundreds of thousands of eons, once conditions ripen, a complete new existence still arises from that very seed. Though every new existence depends on its own seed, it is only when the present lifespan reaches its end that this seed is said to have already yielded its result; other seeds belonging to this same existence, not yet having yielded a result, are not said to have already yielded one.

又諸種子，即於此身中應受異熟，緣差不受，順不定受攝故。然此種子亦唯住此位。

Further, there are seeds that ought to produce a ripened result within this very body, but which, for lack of the right conditions, do not do so — these belong to the class of uncertain karma. Such a seed, nonetheless, simply continues to reside within the ālayavijñāna.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi divides fixed karma into two kinds — ripening in the very next life, or only in some later one — and stresses that once karma is truly fixed, it never simply vanishes, even across a hundred thousand eons, echoing the classical verse that karma once done is never lost, and is experienced by oneself once the right conditions meet. Uncertain karma, by contrast, is typically a lighter action — his example is the same offering made with wholehearted devotion versus made carelessly, which plant seeds of very different weight. What decides whether an action becomes fixed or uncertain karma, on his reading, is not the act alone but the intention and completeness behind it.

8. Self-Love Is the Same Everywhere

Chinese Source

是故一一自體中，皆有一切自體種子。若於一處有染欲，即說一切處有染欲。若於一處得離欲，即說於一切處得離欲。

Ru-Yi Translation

Therefore, within each and every existence, the seeds of every possible existence are all present. If, in one place, defiled craving is present, it is said that defiled craving is present in every place. And if, in one place, freedom from craving is attained, it is said that freedom from craving is attained in every place.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads "one place, every place" here as specifically about self-love, which he holds to be identical in nature across every realm of rebirth, from hell to the most refined heaven. This is why, on his reading, non-Buddhist meditators who reach even the most exalted formless absorptions still eventually fall back to a lower rebirth: their practice has only suppressed craving toward the realms beneath them, without ever uprooting self-love itself, so it remains fully intact and reasserts itself once that meditative attainment's own karmic fruit runs out. Only uprooting self-love through insight into non-self achieves freedom in every place at once, rather than realm by realm.

9. "Coarse Burden" and "Latent Tendency"

The treatise now distinguishes three grades of seed by the technical names they earn.

Chinese Source

又於諸自體中所有種子，若煩惱品所攝，名為麤重，亦名隨眠。若異熟品所攝，及餘無記品所攝，唯名麤重，不名隨眠。若信等善法品所攝種子，不名麤重，亦非隨眠。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, among all the seeds present within any existence: those belonging to the class of afflictions are called both "coarse burden" and "latent tendency." Those belonging to the class of ripened results, and other indeterminate classes, are called only "coarse burden," not "latent tendency." And those belonging to the class of wholesome factors such as faith are called neither "coarse burden" nor "latent tendency."

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads "coarse burden" as naming any seed that leaves the mind without the fitness needed for wholesome practice — but only the afflictive seeds additionally earn the name "latent tendency," because they do not merely weigh the mind down; they lie dormant, ready to actively generate further affliction, much as indulging sleep only deepens it. Neutral seeds (ripened results, ordinary conduct) are a burden but generate nothing further on their own; wholesome seeds like faith carry no burden at all, since they are precisely what gives the mind its capacity for practice rather than taking it away.

10. Why Even a Neutral Existence Counts as Suffering

Chinese Source

是故一切所依自體，麤重所隨故，麤重所生故，麤重自性故，諸佛如來安立為苦，所謂由行苦故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Therefore, every underlying existence — followed by coarse burden, produced by coarse burden, and having coarse burden as its very nature — is established by the Buddhas as suffering: this is what is meant by suffering through the nature of conditioned formations.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this as the doctrinal payoff of the whole discussion of seeds: even an existence untouched, at this very moment, by any actively arising affliction still counts as suffering in the technical sense the Buddhas use it, simply because it is followed by, produced by, and made of the very same "coarse burden" as affliction itself. This, he notes, is why the classical shorthand for suffering is simply "the five aggregates of clinging" — not merely painful feeling, but any conditioned existence whatsoever.

11. Eleven Names for "Seed"

Chinese Source

又諸種子，乃有多種差別之名。所謂名界，名種姓，名自性，名因，名薩迦耶，名戲論，名阿賴耶，名取，名苦，名薩迦耶見所依止處，名我慢所依止處。如是等類，差別應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, these seeds go by many different names: realm, lineage, own-nature, cause, personal existence, elaboration, the storehouse, appropriation, suffering, the basis of the view of a personal existence, and the basis of conceit. Distinctions of this kind should be understood.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads each name as highlighting one facet of the seed: "realm" for underlying the eighteen sense-elements; "lineage" for the specific seeds that make liberation possible; "own-nature" because a thing's individual character just is its own seed; "cause" for producing effects; "personal existence" for the fragile, composite unreality it names; "elaboration" for its link to meaningless conceptual proliferation; "the storehouse" as the very substance of the ālayavijñāna; "appropriation" once craving has taken hold of it; "suffering" once it has actually ripened; and the last two for underlying the view of self and the conceit built on it.

12. Two Paths to Transforming the Basis

Chinese Source

又般涅槃時，已得轉依諸淨行者，轉捨一切染汙法種子所依。於一切善、無記法種子，轉令緣闕，轉得內緣自在。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, at the time of final nirvāṇa, those pure practitioners who have attained the transformation of the basis relinquish the basis for all seeds of defiled dharmas. As for the seeds of wholesome and indeterminate dharmas: in one case, the conditions for their future ripening are made to fail; in the other, one attains mastery over these very seeds from within.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as describing two different outcomes for the same category of seeds, depending on who attains liberation. The śrāvaka, motivated by turning away from cyclic existence altogether, simply lets these wholesome and indeterminate seeds lose the conditions they would need to ever ripen again — they become permanently inert at final nirvāṇa. The bodhisattva, motivated instead by skillful means and the wisdom that does not separate saṃsāra from nirvāṇa, gains full mastery over these same seeds instead, using them at will to manifest transformation-bodies that continue to benefit other beings.

13. Full Term in the Womb

Chinese Source

又於胎中，經三十八七日，此之胎藏，一切支分皆悉具足。從此以後，復經四日方乃出生。如薄伽梵於入胎經廣說。此說極滿足者，或經九月、或復過此。若唯經八月，此名圓滿，非極圓滿。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, within the womb, after thirty-eight weeks, the fetus has all its limbs and parts fully formed. After this, a further four days pass before birth. The Blessed One explained this at length in the Sūtra on Entering the Womb. What is here called utterly complete refers to nine months, or slightly more. If only eight months have passed, this is called complete, but not utterly complete.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes this distinguishes a technical "utterly complete" (the full nine-month term) from a lesser "complete" (eight months) — groundwork for the treatise's next step, sorting the ordinary range of birth timing and circumstance into its proper categories.

14. Eight Weekly Stages of the Fetus

The treatise now sets out the traditional eight-stage timetable of prenatal development, week by week, along with how the fetus is nourished at each stage.

Chinese Source

若經七月六月，不名圓滿，或復闕減。又此胎藏六處位中，由母所食生麤津味而得資長。於羯羅藍等微細位中，由微細津味資長應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

If only seven or six months have passed, this is not called complete, and may even be called deficient. Further, once the fetus reaches the stage of the sense-faculties, it is nourished by the coarser nutrient fluid the mother consumes; in the subtler stages, from the kalala onward, it should be understood to be nourished by a subtler nutrient fluid.

復次，此之胎藏，八位差別。何等為八？謂羯羅藍位、遏部曇位、閉尸位、鍵南位、鉢羅賒佉位、髮毛爪位、根位、形位。

Further, this fetus passes through eight distinct stages. What are the eight? The kalala stage; the arbuda stage; the peśī stage; the ghana stage; the praśākhā stage; the stage of hair, down, and nails; the stage of the sense-faculties; and the stage of bodily form.

若已結凝，箭內仍稀，名羯羅藍。若表裏如酪，未至肉位，名遏部曇。若已成肉，仍極柔軟，名閉尸。若已堅厚，稍堪摩觸，名為鍵南。即此肉摶增長，支分相現，名鉢羅賒佉。從此以後，髮毛爪現，即名此位。從此以後，眼等根生，名為根位。從此以後，彼所依處分明顯現，名為形位。

When it has just curdled, still thin within like an arrow-wound, this is called the kalala stage. When, outside and within, it is like curdled milk, but not yet flesh, this is called the arbuda stage. When it has become flesh, but is still extremely soft, this is called the peśī stage. When it has become firm and thick, able to bear some slight touch, this is called the ghana stage. When this mass of flesh grows further and the marks of the limbs appear, this is called the praśākhā stage. From this point, when hair, down, and nails appear, this very stage is so named. From this point, when the eye and the other faculties arise, this is called the stage of the sense-faculties. From this point, when the physical supports of these faculties become clearly manifest, this is called the stage of form.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads these eight weekly stages as filling in, week by week, the same process fascicle 1 already sketched at the kalala stage: curdling, thickening into soft flesh, then firm flesh, then visible limbs, then hair and nails, then the sense-faculties themselves, and finally their complete physical form. He treats the shift in nourishment — a subtler nutrient essence in the earliest weeks, the same coarse nutrient fluid the mother herself eats once the sense-faculties are established — as marking the fetus's gradual transition from an existence sustained almost entirely by the ālayavijñāna's own subtle process toward one that depends, like any other living being, on ordinary physical food.

15. Four Kinds of Fetal Variation

The treatise now explains why fetuses vary in hair, complexion, skin, and limbs — tracing each to a combination of past karma and the mother's present conduct.

Chinese Source

又於胎藏中，或由先業力，或由其母不避不平等力所生隨順風故，令此胎藏或髮、或色、或皮，及餘支分變異而生。髮變異生者，謂由先世所作能感此惡不善業，及由其母多習灰鹽等味若飲、若食，令此胎藏髮毛希尠。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, within the womb, whether through the force of past karma, or through a wind arising from the mother's failure to avoid what is out of balance, the fetus's hair, complexion, skin, or other bodily parts may develop abnormally. Abnormal hair arises through unwholesome karma from a past life capable of bringing about this result, and through the mother's habitual consumption of ash- or salt-flavored food or drink, causing the fetus's hair to become sparse.

色變異生者，謂由先業因，如前說，及由其母習近煖熱現在緣故，令彼胎藏黑黯色生。又母習近極寒室等，令彼胎藏極白色生。又由其母多噉熱食，令彼胎藏極赤色生。

Abnormal complexion arises through the same kind of past karma, and through the mother's habitual exposure to warmth and heat, causing the fetus's complexion to darken; or through her habitual exposure to extreme cold, causing it to become extremely pale; or through her eating much hot food, causing it to become extremely ruddy.

皮變異生者，謂由宿業因，如前說，及由其母多習婬欲現在緣故，令彼胎藏或癬、疥、癩等惡皮而生。支分變異生者，謂由先業因，如前說，及由其母多習馳走、跳躑威儀，及不避不平等現在緣故，令彼胎藏諸根支分闕減而生。

Abnormal skin arises through the same kind of past karma, and through the mother's habitual indulgence in sexual activity, causing the fetus to develop conditions such as ringworm, scabies, or a leprous skin. Abnormalities of the limbs arise through the same kind of past karma, and through the mother's habitual running, jumping, and other unbalanced conduct in the present, causing the fetus's faculties and limbs to be deficient or incomplete.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats all four variations as consistently two-factor outcomes: past karma sets the underlying tendency, while the mother's present diet and conduct supply the triggering condition that actually brings it about. The pattern repeats identically across hair, complexion, skin, and limbs — an inherited predisposition, activated or not depending on present circumstance.

16. Position in the Womb

Chinese Source

又彼胎藏，若當為女，於母左脅，倚脊向腹而住。若當為男，於母右脅，倚腹向脊而住。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, if the fetus is to be female, it dwells on the mother's left side, its back against her spine, facing her belly. If it is to be male, it dwells on the mother's right side, its back against her belly, facing her spine.

17. Birth

Chinese Source

又此胎藏極成滿時，其母不堪持此重胎，內風便發，生大苦惱。又此胎藏業報所發生分風起，令頭向下，足便向上，胎衣纏裹而趣產門。其正出時，胎衣遂裂，分之兩腋。出產門時，名正生位。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, when the fetus reaches its full term, the mother's body can no longer bear to carry this heavy burden, and an internal wind arises within her, bringing great distress. Further, a wind proper to the process of birth, brought about by the ripening of karma, then arises, turning the fetus's head downward and its feet upward; wrapped in the birth-membrane, it moves toward the birth canal. As it is actually born, the birth-membrane splits open along both sides. The moment it emerges from the birth canal is called the stage of actual birth.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes two different winds at work here: an ordinary bodily distress in the mother from simply carrying a full-term pregnancy, and a separate, karma-specific "wind of birth" that actually repositions and expels the fetus itself — two distinct forces cooperating at the moment of delivery.

18. The Seven Stages of Growing Up

The treatise now traces an entire ordinary life, from a newborn's first contact with the world through to one of two possible outcomes.

Chinese Source

生後漸次觸生分觸，所謂眼觸乃至意觸。次復隨墮施設事中，所謂隨學世事言說。次復耽著家室，謂長大種類故，諸根成熟故。

Ru-Yi Translation

After birth, one gradually comes into contact proper to this stage of existence — that is, contact through the eye, and so on up to contact through the mind. Next, one falls into the conventions of the world, learning to speak and act as the world does. Next, one becomes attached to household life, as one's kind matures and one's faculties ripen.

次造諸業，謂起世間工巧業處。次復受用境界，所謂色等若可愛、不可愛。受此苦樂，謂由先業因，或由現在緣。隨緣所牽，或往五趣，或向涅槃。

Next, one takes up various occupations — the skilled trades of the world. Next, one makes use of objects — forms and the rest — whether pleasant or unpleasant. One experiences pleasure and pain from these, whether through the force of past karma or through present conditions. And, drawn along by these very conditions, one proceeds either to the five realms of rebirth, or toward nirvāṇa.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads these seven stages as a single continuous arc: an infant's bare sensory contact, then language and social convention, then marriage and household life, then a livelihood, then the pleasures and pains that come with using what one has earned. The arc then forks. Without ever encountering the true teaching, this same ordinary life simply feeds back into further rebirth. But hearing the teaching and reflecting rightly on that very same experience of pleasure and pain — recognizing it as empty of any real self to whom it is happening — redirects the identical life toward nirvāṇa instead. On his reading, the outcome turns less on what happens to a person than on how it comes to be understood.

19. Four Ways Others Sustain a New Life

Chinese Source

又諸有情，隨於如是有情類中自體生時，彼有情類於此有情作四種緣。謂種子所引故，食所資養故，隨逐守護故，隨學造作身語業故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, whenever a being is born within any kind of beings, other beings of that same kind provide four conditions for this new being: being drawn forth by their generative seed; being nourished by the food they provide; being followed and protected by them; and being taught by them how to act and speak.

初謂父母精血所引。次彼生已，知其所欲，方求飲食而用資長。次常隨逐，專志守護，不令起作非時之行及不平等行。

The first is being drawn forth by the parents' generative fluid. Next, once born, those who know what the child needs seek out food to nourish and sustain it. Next, they constantly attend to it, dedicatedly protecting it, so that it does not act at an improper time or in a way that is out of balance.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as a quiet teaching on interdependence: no being, however it enters the world, ever grows up unassisted — conception itself depends on others, and so does every meal, every moment of safety, and every word and skill a child ever learns. From this, he draws a practical instruction: given how vast the past has been, it is entirely plausible that almost anyone one meets has, at some point across countless lifetimes, stood in the place of a parent — an ordinary fact about universal dependency that becomes, on reflection, a basis for goodwill rather than hostility toward others.

20. An Endless Relay — and Why It Is So Hard to See Through

The treatise closes its account of birth with the endless chain of beings raising beings, and with the single deepest reason this chain has never, on its own, come to an end.

Chinese Source

次令習學世俗言說等事。由長大種類故，諸根成熟故，此復於餘，此復於餘。如是展轉，諸有情類無始時來受苦受樂，未曾獲得出苦樂法，乃至諸佛未證菩提。若從他聞音，及內正思惟，由如是故，方得漏盡。

Ru-Yi Translation

Next, they teach the child the world's conventional speech and other such matters. As this being grows, matures, and its faculties ripen, it in turn does the very same for another, who in turn does the same for yet another. Turning on in this way, beings have, since beginningless time, experienced pleasure and pain without ever finding a way beyond pleasure and pain — until the Buddhas attain awakening. It is only by hearing the teaching from another, and by rightly reflecting within, that one finally attains the exhaustion of the defilements.

如是句義甚為難悟。謂我無有若分、若誰、若事，我亦都非若分、若誰、若事。

This very point is exceedingly difficult to realize: that the self has no temporal portion, no enduring someone, and no possessed thing; and that the self, likewise, is not itself any portion, any someone, or any thing.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the endless relay of parent raising child, who in turn raises another, as the treatise's own picture of saṃsāra's machinery: an unbroken chain handing life on to life, with no one inside it ever finding the way out — until a Buddha appears and teaches it. And the reason that way out is so hard to find, on his reading, is not a lack of information but the sheer difficulty of the insight itself: there is no fixed self persisting across past, present, and future, no single "someone" who is born and dies, and no genuine possessor behind what is loosely called "mine." "Self," on this account, is simply a manner of speaking convenient for describing an ever-changing stream of conditioned events — not a name for something that actually exists behind them. With this, the treatise's entire account of birth closes on its deepest point: everything just described — conception, gestation, birth, growing up, one generation sustaining the next — has no self running through it at any point.

21. The Fifteenth Power Begins: The World's Destruction

Having finished the inner aspect — death and birth — the treatise turns to the outer aspect: how the physical world itself is destroyed and formed.

Chinese Source

如是略說內分死生已。云何外分若壞若成？謂由諸有情所作能感成壞業故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Thus has the inner aspect — death and birth — been briefly explained. What now is the outer aspect: its destruction and its formation? It comes about through karma created by beings, capable of bringing about the world's formation and destruction.

若有能感壞業現前，爾時便有外壞緣起，由彼外分皆悉散壞，非如內分由壽量盡。何以故？由一切外分所有麤色，四大所成，恆相續住，非如內分。

When the karma capable of bringing about destruction actually manifests, the outer condition for destruction then arises, and the outer world is entirely dispersed and destroyed — unlike the inner aspect, which ends through the exhaustion of a lifespan. Why is this? Because all the coarse matter of the outer world, made of the four great elements, remains in unbroken continuity for far longer than does the inner aspect.

又感成器世間業，此業決定能引劫住，不增不減。

Further, the karma that brings the physical world into formation is fixed in its power to sustain it for a full eon — neither more nor less.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi contrasts two very different timescales: an individual body ends when its own allotted lifespan runs out, but the outer world — mountains, rivers, the physical universe — persists across an entire eon, ending only when the shared karma of all the beings within it ripens toward destruction at once, not through any "lifespan" of its own but through a collective karmic cause. He notes the traditional structure of a great eon as four periods of twenty intermediate eons each — formation, abiding, destruction, and emptiness — a fixed span that karma alone can neither lengthen nor shorten.

22. Beings' Lifespans Are Not Fixed Like the World's

Chinese Source

若有情數，時無決定。所以者何？由彼造作種種業故，或過一劫，或復減少乃至一歲。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for the lifespans of beings, however, their duration is not fixed. Why is this? Because beings create karma of many different kinds, a lifespan may exceed a full eon, or may be reduced to as little as a single year.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi contrasts the fixed, collective span of the physical world with the highly variable lifespans of the beings within it: an individual's own karma can shorten a life to a single year or extend it past a full eon, entirely apart from the world's own schedule of formation and destruction.

23. Three Calamities That Destroy the World

Chinese Source

又彼壞劫，由三種災。一者、火災，能壞世間，從無間獄乃至梵世。二者、水災，能壞一切，乃至第二靜慮。三者、風災，能壞一切，乃至第三靜慮。第四靜慮無災能壞。由彼諸天身與宮殿俱生俱沒故，更無能壞因緣法故。復有三災之頂。謂第二靜慮、第三靜慮、第四靜慮。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, that eon of destruction comes about through three kinds of calamity. The first is fire, which destroys the world from the hell of uninterrupted suffering up to the Brahmā heavens. The second is water, which destroys everything up to the second dhyāna heaven. The third is wind, which destroys everything up to the third dhyāna heaven. The fourth dhyāna heaven is destroyed by no calamity at all, because the bodies of its gods arise and cease together with their own palaces, and there is simply no cause capable of destroying it. There are, moreover, three peaks of calamity: the second dhyāna, the third dhyāna, and the fourth dhyāna.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the three calamities as escalating in reach — fire to the first dhyāna heaven, water to the second, wind to the third — each stopping exactly at the level whose corresponding meditative attainment is too settled for that particular calamity to disturb it further. The fourth dhyāna stands entirely outside this cycle: its heavens have no shared physical world to destroy in the first place — each god and its dwelling arises and vanishes together, with no common ground for any calamity to act upon.

24. How Long a Great Eon Lasts

Chinese Source

又此世間二十中劫壞，二十中劫壞已空，二十中劫成，二十中劫成已住；如是八十中劫，假立為一大劫數。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, in this world, twenty intermediate eons make up the eon of destruction; twenty more, once destroyed, make up the eon of emptiness; twenty more make up the eon of formation; and twenty more, once formed, make up the eon of abiding. These eighty intermediate eons together are provisionally called one great eon.

又梵世間壽量一劫，此最後壞，亦最初成。當知此劫異相建立。謂梵眾天，二十中劫合為一劫，即依此劫施設壽量。梵前益天，四十中劫合為一劫，即依此劫施設壽量。

Further, the lifespan in the Brahmā heavens is a full eon — this realm is the last to be destroyed, and the first to be formed. It should be understood that this "eon" is established differently at each level. For the multitude of Brahmā, twenty intermediate eons together make one eon, and their lifespan is reckoned by this eon. For the ministers of Brahmā, forty intermediate eons together make one eon, and their lifespan is reckoned by this eon.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the "great eon" as breaking cleanly into four quarters of twenty intermediate eons each — destruction, emptiness, formation, abiding — the same rhythm underlying every physical universe. He notes that beings in the Brahmā heavens, though all said to live "one eon," actually live spans of very different absolute length depending on rank: twenty intermediate eons for the ordinary multitude, forty for Brahmā's ministers, and presumably more still for Great Brahmā himself — because "one eon," for them, is a relative unit scaled to their own position, not an identical fixed duration shared by everyone who uses the term. Fittingly, the lowest of the form-realm heavens — the first stop above ordinary existence — is also both the first world to form and the last to be destroyed.

25. One Intermediate Eon: A Rise and Fall Driven by Conduct

The treatise now explains what actually happens, in human terms, within a single intermediate eon — and how it always ends in exactly the same way.

Chinese Source

若大梵天，六十中劫合為一劫，即依此劫施設壽量。云何火災能壞世間？謂有如是時，世間有情壽量無限。從此漸減，乃至壽量經八萬歲。彼復受行不善法故，壽量轉減，乃至十歲。彼復獲得厭離之心，受行善法，由此因緣，壽量漸增，乃至八萬。如是壽量一減一增，合成一中劫。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for Great Brahmā, sixty intermediate eons together make one eon, and his lifespan is reckoned by this eon. How does fire come to destroy the world? There is a time when the lifespan of beings in the world is unlimited. From this it gradually decreases, down to a lifespan of eighty thousand years. Then, because beings take up unwholesome practices, their lifespan decreases further, down to ten years. Then, having gained a mind of renunciation, they take up wholesome practices, and through this their lifespan gradually increases again, up to eighty thousand years. Such a single decrease and increase together make up one intermediate eon.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this rise-and-fall cycle as running on a moral engine: lifespans collapse from a peak of eighty thousand years down to a mere ten, driven purely by beings' own accumulating unwholesome conduct, and rise back up again only once beings grow disgusted enough with the resulting suffering to actually take up virtue. The pattern, on his reading, is never externally imposed — it tracks nothing but the aggregate moral condition of the beings living through it.

26. Two of the Three Minor Calamities: Famine and Disease

Chinese Source

又此中劫，復有三種小災出現。謂儉、病、刀。儉災者，所謂人壽三十歲時，方始建立。當爾之時，精妙飲食不可復得，唯煎煮朽骨共為宴會。若遇得一粒稻、麥、粟、稗等子，重若末尼，藏置箱篋而守護之。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, within this intermediate eon, three minor calamities occur: famine, disease, and the sword. Famine begins when the average human lifespan falls to thirty years. At that time, fine food can no longer be found at all, and people boil old bones together for their meals. If one happens upon a single grain of rice, wheat, millet, or barnyard grass, it is treasured like a precious jewel, hidden away and carefully guarded.

彼諸有情多無氣勢，蹎僵在地不復能起。由此饑儉，有情之類亡沒殆盡。此之儉災經七年七月七日七夜，方乃得過。彼諸有情復共聚集，起下厭離。由此因緣，壽不退減，儉災遂息。

Beings, mostly without strength, collapse to the ground and cannot rise again. Through this famine, beings very nearly perish altogether. This calamity of famine lasts seven years, seven months, seven days, and seven nights before it finally passes. Beings then gather together and give rise to a rudimentary sense of renunciation; through this, their lifespan ceases to decline, and the famine comes to an end.

又若人壽二十歲時，本起厭患今乃退捨。爾時多有疫氣、瘴癘、災橫、熱惱相續而生。彼諸有情遇此諸病，多悉殞沒。如是病災經七月七日七夜，方乃得過。

Further, when the average human lifespan falls to twenty years, the renunciation that had earlier arisen is abandoned once again. At that time, epidemics, pestilence, disasters, and feverish afflictions arise one after another. Beings who encounter these illnesses mostly perish. This calamity of disease lasts seven months, seven days, and seven nights before it finally passes.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats these three minor calamities — famine, disease, and (still to come) the sword — as recurring, more localized punctuations within a single intermediate eon, distinct from the three great calamities of fire, water, and wind that end an entire great eon. Each minor calamity, on his reading, ends the same way the larger cycle does: not through any external rescue, but through beings themselves finally growing weary enough of suffering to turn back toward goodness — a pattern of moral cause and moral recovery repeated at every scale, from a single eon down to a single localized disaster.

27. The Third Minor Calamity — the Sword, and the Eon of Abiding Completed

Chinese Source

又人壽十歲時，本起厭患今還退捨。爾時有情展轉相見，各起猛利殺害之心。由此因緣，隨執草木及以瓦石，皆成最極銳利刀劍，更相殘害，死喪略盡。如是刀災極經七日，方乃得過。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, when the average human lifespan falls to ten years, the renunciation that had arisen is abandoned once again. At that time, beings, encountering one another, each give rise to a fierce intent to kill; through this, whatever grass, wood, tiles, or stones they grasp all become the sharpest of blades, and they slaughter one another until nearly all have perished. This calamity of the sword lasts at most seven days before it finally passes.

爾時有情復有三種最極衰損。謂壽量衰損、依止衰損、資具衰損。壽量衰損者，所謂壽量極至十歲。依止衰損者，謂其身量極至一搩，或復一握。資具衰損者，爾時有情唯以粟稗為食中第一，以髮毼為衣中第一，以鐵為莊嚴中第一。五種上味悉皆隱沒，所謂酥蜜油鹽等味，及甘蔗變味。

At that time, beings suffer three extremes of decline: decline in lifespan, decline in bodily support, and decline in material resources. Decline in lifespan means a lifespan that reaches, at most, ten years. Decline in bodily support means bodies that reach, at most, the span of a hand's width, or even just a fist's breadth. Decline in material resources means that beings then consider coarse grain the finest of foods, rough hair the finest clothing, and iron the finest ornament; the five superior flavors — butter, honey, oil, salt, and the taste of sugarcane — have all vanished entirely.

爾時有情展轉聚集，起上厭離，不復退減。又能棄捨損減壽量惡不善法，受行增長壽量善法。由此因緣，壽量、色力、富樂、自在皆漸增長，乃至壽量經八萬歲。如是二十減、二十增，合四十增減，便出住劫。

At that time, beings gather together and give rise to the highest degree of renunciation, never again to decline. They abandon the unwholesome practices that diminish lifespan, and take up the wholesome practices that increase it. Through this, lifespan, physical vigor, wealth, and ease of life all gradually increase once more, up to a lifespan of eighty thousand years. In this way, twenty periods of decrease and twenty periods of increase, forty in all, together complete the eon of abiding.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the sword calamity as the most severe of the three, since it is driven by murderous intent itself rather than by external scarcity or illness. He treats the description of bodies shrinking to a hand's span and lifespans to a mere ten years as a vivid way of showing how thoroughly moral decline degrades not just circumstance but beings themselves. He also notes that this entire forty-fold sequence of paired decrease-and-increase cycles is what constitutes the full "eon of abiding" — the period, within the great eon's four quarters, during which the world actually exists in habitable form and this repeating moral rhythm plays out in full.

28. The World's Destruction: Beings Depart, Then Seven Suns Burn Away the Earth

The treatise now describes the eon of destruction itself: how beings first leave the lower and middle realms, and how the world is then destroyed by fire — the famous account of the seven suns.

Chinese Source

於最後增已，爾時那落迦有情唯沒不生。如是漸漸乃至沒盡，當知說名那落迦世間壞。如那落迦壞，傍生、餓鬼壞亦如是。爾時人中隨一有情，自然法爾所得第二靜慮。其餘有情展轉隨學，亦復如是。皆此沒已，生極淨光天眾同分中。當知爾時說名人世間壞。如人趣既爾，天趣亦然。

Ru-Yi Translation

After the final increase, beings in hell then only pass away and are no longer born there; gradually, until they are entirely gone, this is called the destruction of the hell-world. As with hell, so with the animal and hungry-ghost worlds. At that time, some one being among humans spontaneously attains the second dhyāna; the rest, in turn, learn to do likewise. All of them, upon passing away, are reborn among the multitude of the Radiant Light heaven. This is called the destruction of the human world; and as with humans, so too with the heavenly realms below.

當於此時，五趣世間居住之處無一有情可得，所有資具亦不可得。非唯資具不可復得，爾時天雨亦不可得。由無雨故，大地所有藥草叢林皆悉枯槁。復由無雨之所攝故，令此日輪熱勢增大。又諸有情能感壞劫業增上力故，及依六種所燒事故，復有六日輪漸次而現。彼諸日輪望舊日輪所有熱勢，踰前四倍。既成七已，熱遂增七。

At this time, not a single being remains anywhere in the dwelling-places of the five realms, nor can any of life's requisites be found. Not only can requisites no longer be found — even rain no longer falls from the sky. Because there is no rain, all the plants and forests of the earth wither away entirely. And because there is no rain, the heat of the sun grows ever greater. Further, through the dominant force of beings' karma capable of bringing about this eon of destruction, and depending on six things yet to be burned, six further suns gradually appear, one after another; each one's heat, compared to the original sun's, exceeds it fourfold. Once seven suns stand in the sky, the heat has thus intensified through seven stages.

一、小大溝坑，由第二日輪之所枯竭；二、小河大河，由第三日輪之所枯竭；三、無熱大池，由第四日輪之所枯竭；四者、大海，由第五日輪及第六一分之所枯竭；六、蘇迷盧山及以大地，體堅實故，由第六一分及第七日輪之所燒然。即此火焰為風所鼓，展轉熾盛，極至梵世。

First, all the small and large pools and pits are dried up by the second sun. Second, the small and great rivers are dried up by the third sun. Third, the great lake of no heat is dried up by the fourth sun. Fourth, the great oceans are dried up by the fifth sun and part of the sixth. Finally, Mount Sumeru and the great earth itself, being of solid substance, are burned away by the remaining part of the sixth sun together with the seventh. This flame, fanned by the wind, blazes on and on, reaching all the way up to the Brahmā world.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this famous "seven suns" account as a graduated countdown: beings first migrate out of the lower and middle realms through meditative attainment or simple mortality, the world then dries up stage by stage — puddles, rivers, the legendary Lake of No Heat, the oceans — and only once every drop of water is gone does the fire finally reach and consume even Mount Sumeru and the solid earth itself. His point: it is water's total absence, not merely the sun's presence, that finally allows fire to destroy even what seems most permanent.

29. Water and Wind: The Larger Cycle of Destruction

Having described fire, the treatise now nests water and wind calamities within a still larger cycle: water arrives only after seven fire-calamities, and wind only after seven of water.

Chinese Source

如是世界皆悉燒已，乃至灰燼及與餘影皆不可得，廣說如經。從此名為器世間已壞。滿足二十中劫。如是壞已，復二十中劫住。

Ru-Yi Translation

Thus, once the world has been entirely burned away, not even ash or any trace of it can be found — as explained at length in the sūtras. From this point, the physical world is said to have been destroyed. This fills out twenty intermediate eons. Once thus destroyed, a further twenty intermediate eons of emptiness follow.

云何水災？謂過七火災已，於第二靜慮中，有俱生水界起，壞器世間，如水消鹽。此之水界與器世間一時俱沒。如是沒已，復二十中劫住。

What is the calamity of water? After seven fire-calamities have passed, within the second dhyāna heaven, an innate water element arises, destroying the physical world there as water dissolves salt. This water element and that world vanish together at one and the same time. Once thus vanished, a further twenty intermediate eons of emptiness follow.

云何風災？謂七水災過已，復七火災；從此無間，於第三靜慮中有俱生風界起，壞器世間，如風乾支節，復能消盡。此之風界與器世間一時俱沒。所以者何？現見有一由風界發，乃令其骨皆悉消盡。從此壞已，復二十中劫住。

What is the calamity of wind? After seven water-calamities have passed, seven more fire-calamities occur; immediately following these, within the third dhyāna heaven, an innate wind element arises, destroying the physical world there, as wind dries out the body's joints and wastes them entirely away. This wind element and that world vanish together at one and the same time. Why is this so? It is observed that when the wind element becomes active in illness, it can waste even the bones away entirely. From this destruction, a further twenty intermediate eons of emptiness follow.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi lays out the full nested cycle: fire recurs every great eon, destroying up through the first dhyāna each time; after seven such fire-cycles, a water-calamity additionally reaches the second dhyāna, dissolving that world as water dissolves salt; after seven water-cycles (each interspersed with its own seven fire-cycles), one wind-calamity finally reaches the third dhyāna — on, by this count, the sixty-fourth great eon — eroding even that world's substance the way wind can waste away bone in certain illnesses. Only the fourth dhyāna, as already noted, remains untouched by any of the three.

30. The Sixteenth Power Begins: The World's Formation

Having finished the world's destruction, the treatise now turns to the last of its seventeen powers: how the world forms again, reversing the very order in which it was destroyed.

Chinese Source

如是略說世間已壞。云何世間成？謂過如是二十中劫已，一切有情業增上力故，世間復成。爾時最初於虛空中，第三靜慮器世間成。如第三靜慮，第二及初亦復如是。

Ru-Yi Translation

Thus has the world's destruction been briefly explained. What now is the world's formation? After these twenty intermediate eons of emptiness have passed, through the dominant force of the collective karma of all beings, the world is formed once again. At that time, first of all, within empty space, the physical world of the third dhyāna comes into being. As with the third dhyāna, so too, in turn, with the second and the first.

爾時第三災頂有諸有情由壽盡故，業盡故，福盡故，從彼沒已，生第三靜慮。

At that time, beings at the peak of the third calamity, whose lifespan, karma, and merit have all been exhausted, pass away from there and are reborn in the third dhyāna.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the world's re-formation as running in the exact reverse order of its destruction: whichever realm was last to fall is the first to reappear. Its first inhabitants, moreover, are not new arrivals from elsewhere, but simply beings from the level immediately above whose own lifespan, karma, and accumulated merit there have run out — the natural next stop for beings already due to move on, not a fresh population summoned from nowhere.

31. Mahābrahmā's Wish, and the Desire-Realm Heavens

The treatise now describes how the first dhyāna's Brahmā world is repopulated — including the origin of Mahābrahmā's mistaken reputation as a creator god — and how the desire realm's four heavens form afterward.

Chinese Source

復從第二災頂生第二靜慮。餘一切處，應知亦爾。復從第一災頂，有一有情由壽等盡故，從彼沒已，生初靜慮梵世界中，為最大梵。由獨一故，而懷不悅。便有希望：今當云何令餘有情亦來生此。當發心時，諸餘有情由壽等盡故，從第二靜慮沒已，生初靜慮彼同分中。

Ru-Yi Translation

Then, from the peak of the second calamity, beings are reborn in the second dhyāna, and it should be understood that this holds in the same way for every remaining level. Then, from the peak of the first calamity, one being, its lifespan and the rest having been exhausted, passes away from there and is reborn in the Brahmā world of the first dhyāna, becoming Mahābrahmā. Being alone, it feels displeased, and this wish arises: "How shall I now bring other beings to be born here as well?" At the very moment this wish arises, other beings, their lifespan and the rest having been exhausted, pass away from the second dhyāna and are reborn as this being's companions in the first dhyāna.

如是下三靜慮器及有情世間成已，於虛空中，欲界四天宮殿漸成。當知彼諸虛空宮殿皆如化出。又諸有情，從極淨光天眾同分沒，而來生此諸宮殿中。

Once the physical and living worlds of the lower three dhyānas have thus formed, the palaces of the four heavens of the desire realm gradually form within empty space; it should be understood that these palaces all arise as if by transformation. And beings come to be born in these palaces, having passed away from the multitude of the Radiant Light heaven.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads Mahābrahmā's loneliness and wish for company as the treatise's own quiet irony: this figure, later mistaken by some traditions for an all-powerful creator who willed other beings into existence, is in fact simply the first being to arrive in a newly formed world, whose private wish happens to coincide with — but does not actually cause — the arrival of others who were, quite independently, already due for rebirth there by their own karma.

32. Wind, Water, Earth — Then Mount Sumeru Itself

The treatise now builds the physical world up in layers: a wheel of wind first, then water, then solid earth, and finally Mount Sumeru and its surrounding mountains.

Chinese Source

自此以後，有大風輪，量等三千大千世界，從下而起，與彼世界作所依持，為欲安立無有宮殿諸有情類。此大風輪有二種相。謂仰周布及傍側布，由此持水令不散墜。次由彼業增上力故，於虛空界金藏雲興。從此降雨，注風輪上。次復起風，鼓水令堅，此即名為金性地輪。上堪水雨之所激注，下為風飈之所衝薄。

Ru-Yi Translation

From this point, a great wheel of wind arises from below, equal in extent to a great trichiliocosm, to serve as the support for that world, so as to provide a dwelling-place for those beings who have no palace of their own. This great wheel of wind has two aspects — one spreading upward, one spreading outward to the sides — by which it holds water so that it does not disperse and fall away. Then, through the dominant force of beings' karma, clouds bearing the treasury of the elements arise in empty space, and rain falls from them, pouring down upon the wheel of wind. Then wind arises again, churning the water until it becomes solid; this is called the golden wheel of earth. Above, it bears the pouring rain and water; below, it is buffeted by the force of the wind.

此地成已，即由彼業增上力故，空中復起諸界藏雲，又從彼雲降種種雨。然其雨水，乃依金性地輪而住。次復風起，鼓水令堅。即由此風力所引故，諸有清淨第一最勝精妙性者，成蘇迷盧山。此山成已，四寶為體。所謂金、銀、頗胝、琉璃。若中品性者，成七金山。

Once this earth is formed, through the dominant force of beings' karma, clouds bearing the treasury of the elements again arise, and various rains fall from them. This rainwater rests upon the golden wheel of earth. Then wind arises once more, churning the water until it becomes solid. Through the force of this wind, whatever is of the purest, most excellent, most refined nature becomes Mount Sumeru. Once this mountain is formed, it has four precious substances as its substance: gold, silver, crystal, and lapis lazuli. Whatever is of a middling nature becomes the seven encircling gold mountains.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as the treatise's cosmology built up in strict layers: wind first, to hold everything else in place; then water, condensed from karma-generated clouds; then wind again, compressing that water into solid earth; and finally, from the very same rain-and-wind process repeated once more, the purest matter separating out to form Mount Sumeru, with lesser grades of the same matter forming the surrounding ring of seven gold mountains. The whole physical world, on this account, precipitates out of collective karma much as rain precipitates out of a cloud.

33. Mount Sumeru and the Seven Gold Mountains, Measured

Chinese Source

謂持雙山、毗那矺迦山、馬耳山、善見山、朅達洛迦山、持軸山、尼民達羅山。如是諸山，其峰布列，各由形狀差別為名，繞蘇迷盧次第而住。

Ru-Yi Translation

[The seven gold mountains are named:] Yugandhara, Vinataka, Aśvakarṇa, Sudarśana, Khadiraka, Īṣādhara, and Nimindhara. These mountains, their peaks arrayed in sequence, are each named for their distinctive shape, and stand encircling Mount Sumeru in order.

蘇迷盧量，高八萬踰繕那，廣亦如之，下入水際，量亦復爾。又持雙山，等彼之半。從此次第，餘六金山其量漸減，各等其半。

Mount Sumeru measures eighty thousand yojanas in height, the same in breadth, and the same again in depth beneath the water's surface. Yugandhara measures half of this. From there in sequence, each of the remaining six gold mountains measures half again of the one before it.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the halving pattern — eighty thousand yojanas for Sumeru itself, then forty thousand, twenty thousand, ten thousand, five thousand, twenty-five hundred, twelve-fifty, and finally six-twenty-five for the seven rings outward — as a deliberately symmetrical structure: concentric rings, each exactly half the size of the one within it, all centered on the world's axis.

34. The Four Continents, and the Lower Realms

Chinese Source

若下品性者，於蘇迷盧四邊七金山外，成四大洲及八中洲，并輪圍山。此山輪圍四洲而住，量等尼民達羅之半。復成非天宮殿，此宮在蘇迷盧下，依水而居。復成大雪山及無熱池周圍崖岸。次成最下八大那落迦處，諸大那落迦，及獨一那落迦、寒那落迦、近邊那落迦。復成一分鬼、傍生處。

Ru-Yi Translation

Whatever is of the lowest grade, beyond the seven gold mountains surrounding Mount Sumeru, becomes the four great continents, the eight intermediate continents, and the encircling ring of mountains. This encircling mountain surrounds the four continents, measuring half of Nimindhara. There also forms the palace of the asuras, situated beneath Mount Sumeru, resting upon the water. There also form the great snow mountains and the cliffs surrounding the Lake of No Heat. Next form the eight great hells at the very bottom, together with the great hells, the solitary hells, the cold hells, and the neighboring hells. There also forms a portion of the realms of hungry ghosts and animals.

四大洲者，謂南贍部洲、東毗提訶洲、西瞿陀尼洲、北拘盧洲。其贍部洲，形如車箱；毗提訶洲，形如半月；瞿陀尼洲，其形圓滿；北拘盧洲，其形四方。贍部洲量，六千五百踰繕那；毗提訶洲量，七千踰繕那；瞿陀尼洲量，七千五百踰繕那；拘盧洲量，八千踰繕那。

The four great continents are: Jambudvīpa in the south, Pūrvavideha in the east, Aparagodānīya in the west, and Uttarakuru in the north. Jambudvīpa is shaped like a cart's box; Pūrvavideha is shaped like a half-moon; Aparagodānīya is perfectly round; Uttarakuru is square. Jambudvīpa measures 6,500 yojanas; Pūrvavideha, 7,000; Aparagodānīya, 7,500; and Uttarakuru, 8,000.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes that this traditional cosmology places the world inhabited by humans as we know them specifically in Jambudvīpa, the southern, cart-shaped continent, one of four. Each continent's shape and size, on his reading, is presented as a fixed cosmological fact rather than approximate description — part of the same layered, symmetrical structure already seen in Sumeru and its rings of mountains.

35. Seas, Dragons, and the Guardians of the World

The treatise now populates this newly formed world with its traditional guardians: dragon kings in the seas between the mountains, minor spirits on Sumeru's ascending tiers, yakṣas at its summit, and the Four Heavenly Kings on the mountain that surrounds it.

Chinese Source

又七金山，其間有水，具八支德，名為內海。復成諸龍宮。有八大龍，并經劫住。復有餘水，在內海外，故名外海。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, between the seven gold mountains lies water endowed with eight excellent qualities, called the inner seas. There also form the palaces of the dragons — eight great dragon kings, who dwell there for the full duration of the eon. Beyond the inner seas lies yet more water, called the outer sea.

又依蘇迷盧根，有四重級。從蘇迷盧初級，傍出一萬六千踰繕那量。即從此量半半漸減，如其次第，餘級應知。有堅手神住最初級，血手神住第二級，常醉神住第三級，持鬘神住第四級。蘇迷盧頂四隅之上有四大峰，各高五百踰繕那量。有諸藥叉，謂金剛手止住其中。

Further, resting on the base of Mount Sumeru there are four tiers. From Sumeru's first tier, it extends outward sixteen thousand yojanas; from there, each subsequent tier's extent is halved in turn, and so on for the remaining tiers. The spirit Sthirahasta dwells on the first tier, Raktapāṇi on the second, Sadāmatta on the third, and Mālādhara on the fourth. At the four corners of Sumeru's summit stand four great peaks, each five hundred yojanas high, where the yakṣas — headed by Vajrapāṇi — dwell.

又持雙山，於其四面有四王都。東南西北隨其次第，謂持國、增長、醜目、多聞四大天王之所居止。諸餘金山，是彼四王村邑部落。

Further, on the four sides of Yugandhara stand the capitals of the Four Heavenly Kings: in the east, south, west, and north, in order, dwell Dhṛtarāṣṭra, Virūḍhaka, Virūpākṣa, and Vaiśravaṇa, the four great heavenly kings. The remaining gold mountains serve as the towns and villages of these four kings' domains.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as the world's guardian hierarchy laid out concentrically outward and upward from Mount Sumeru: dragon kings in the inner seas (traditionally invoked especially for rain), successive ranks of minor spirits on Sumeru's ascending tiers, the yakṣas at its very summit, and the Four Heavenly Kings stationed on the mountain that surrounds it, each guarding one cardinal direction. The whole arrangement functions, on his reading, as a kind of concentric watch guarding the world's central axis.

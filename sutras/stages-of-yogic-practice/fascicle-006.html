Sixteen Doctrines the Buddha Rejected

Fascicle 5 ended mid-topic, having just finished sound reasoning attention — not a natural stopping point, simply where the physical scroll divides. This page picks up exactly there: unsound reasoning attention, unpacked through a systematic survey of sixteen non-Buddhist doctrines current in the Buddha's own India — their claims, the reasoning and scriptural authority behind each, and the treatise's own point-by-point refutation — continuing the Three Grounds of Applied and Sustained Thought's exhaustive survey of the three realms.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 6 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. Unsound Reasoning Attention Opens: Sixteen Doctrines Named

Continuing directly from fascicle 5's close, the treatise opens its fourth great lens on the three grounds: unsound reasoning attention, defined here not abstractly but through a roster of sixteen specific non-Buddhist doctrines actually current in the Buddha's own India — the treatise's most sustained engagement anywhere with rival philosophical and religious systems.

Chinese Source

復次，云何不如理作意施設建立？嗢柁南曰：執因中有果，顯了有去來，我常宿作因，自在等害法，邊無邊矯亂，計無因斷空，最勝淨吉祥，由十六異論。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, how is the establishment of unsound reasoning attention to be understood? A summary verse states: holding that effect exists within cause, manifestation, past and future truly existing, self, permanence, prior deeds as cause, sovereign lordship and the rest, harming as the true Dharma, having or lacking limits, evasive equivocation, positing no cause, annihilation, and emptiness, supremacy, purity, and auspiciousness — through sixteen divergent doctrines.

由十六種異論差別，顯不如理作意應知。何等十六？一、因中有果論，二、從緣顯了論，三、去來實有論，四、計我論，五、計常論，六、宿作因論，七、計自在等為作者論，八、害為正法論，九、有邊無邊論，十、不死矯亂論，十一、無因見論，十二、斷見論，十三、空見論，十四、妄計最勝論，十五、妄計清淨論，十六、妄計吉祥論。

It should be understood that unsound reasoning attention is made manifest through these sixteen distinct divergent doctrines. What are the sixteen? First, the doctrine that effect exists within cause; second, the doctrine of manifestation through condition; third, the doctrine that past and future truly exist; fourth, the doctrine positing a self; fifth, the doctrine positing permanence; sixth, the doctrine of prior deeds as cause; seventh, the doctrine positing sovereign lordship and the like as creator; eighth, the doctrine that harming is the true Dharma; ninth, the doctrine of having or lacking limits; tenth, the doctrine of evasive equivocation about the deathless; eleventh, the doctrine positing no cause; twelfth, the doctrine of annihilation; thirteenth, the doctrine of emptiness; fourteenth, the doctrine of false supremacy; fifteenth, the doctrine of false purity; sixteenth, the doctrine of false auspiciousness.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi previews the treatise's method for all sixteen: each will be named, its specific school or teacher identified, its scriptural or reasoned basis given fairly and in some detail, and only then refuted — a far more rigorous and historically grounded engagement with rival views than a mere list of errors. He notes that classical India's roughly ninety-five or ninety-six non-Buddhist schools, and the sixty-two speculative views catalogued in sources like the Brahmajāla Sūtra, all reduce in substance to these sixteen basic positions, making this fascicle's survey a genuine comparative-religion map of the philosophical landscape the Buddha's teaching arose within and against.

He flags the danger these doctrines pose precisely because they are not obviously foolish: many rest on real logical reasoning or real meditative experience, and without the Buddha's own insight to check them against, can seem entirely coherent on their own terms — which is exactly why unsound reasoning attention, rather than mere ignorance, is the treatise's chosen name for what is wrong with them. He previews the first, the doctrine that effect exists within cause, as belonging to the Sāṃkhya school's Vārṣagaṇya branch — the view that a sprout's rice-nature must already exist latent within its seed, since rice only ever grows from rice-seed and never from wheat, extended into an entire cosmology in which everything unfolds already-present from a single eternal, unchanging root-cause.

2. The First Doctrine in Full: Why the Vārṣagaṇya School Held That Effect Exists Within Cause

True to its promised method, the treatise first states the doctrine that effect pre-exists within cause fully and fairly, tracing it to a specific teacher and school, then gives the four reasoned arguments its holders actually offered — before beginning, in the next section, to dismantle it.

Chinese Source

因中有果論者，謂如有一，若沙門、若婆羅門，起如是見，立如是論：常常時、恆恆時，於諸因中具有果性。謂雨眾外道作如是計。

Ru-Yi Translation

The doctrine that effect exists within cause: there are certain renunciants or brahmins who hold such a view and establish such a doctrine — that always, at every time without exception, effect exists as a property already within its cause. It is the Vārṣagaṇya school of non-Buddhist teachers that holds this position.

彼作是思：若從彼性此性得生，一切世間共知、共立彼為此因，非餘。又求果者，唯取此因，非餘。又即於彼，加功營構諸所求事，非餘。又若彼果，即從彼生，不從餘生。是故彼果因中已有。

They reason thus: if this effect-nature arises from that cause-nature, the whole world alike recognizes and establishes that cause-nature, and no other, as the cause of this effect. Further, one who seeks a given effect takes up only this cause, and no other. Further, it is on that very cause that effort and labor are actually applied in pursuit of the desired outcome, and on no other. Further, that effect arises specifically from that cause, and from no other. Therefore, that effect must already exist within its cause.

若不爾者，應立一切是一切因，為求一果應取一切，應於一切加功營構，應從一切一切果生。如是由施設故，求取故，所作決定故，生故，彼見因中常有果性。

Were it otherwise, then anything at all should be the cause of anything at all; seeking a single effect should require taking up every possible cause; effort should have to be applied to everything without distinction; and any effect at all should arise from anything at all. It is thus through convention, through seeking, through the fixed determinacy of effort, and through arising itself, that they hold effect to exist perpetually within cause.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies these teachers as followers of Vārṣagaṇya, a leading disciple of the Sāṃkhya founder Kapila, and lays out their larger cosmology: an eternal, unchanging root-cause called prakṛti (self-nature) unfolds, in fixed sequence, into twenty-three further principles — intellect, ego-sense, the five subtle elements, the five gross elements, the five sense-faculties, the five action-faculties, and mind — all ultimately for the enjoyment of a twenty-fifth principle, puruṣa (the true self). Because rice sprouts only ever come from rice seed, never from wheat, they reasoned, rice-nature must already exist latent within the seed before it ever sprouts — the whole edifice of twenty-three unfolding principles is simply this same logic scaled up to the whole of reality. Liberation, on their view, comes only when this self, wearied by prakṛti's ceaseless, agitating transformations, achieves such profound revulsion toward them that prakṛti itself withdraws and stops producing the twenty-three principles at all.

He walks through their four supporting arguments as genuinely reasoned, not merely dogmatic: the whole world already agrees that rice-seed, and nothing else, is what people call the "cause" of rice; anyone actually wanting rice seeks out rice-seed specifically, not any other seed; the labor of cultivation is applied specifically to that seed, not scattered indiscriminately across every possible seed; and the effect specifically and only ever arises from that particular cause. Reversed, they argued, the absurdity is obvious — if effect were not already latent in its proper cause, anything could produce anything, seeking any single result would require gathering literally everything, effort would have to be spread everywhere at once, and every effect would spring from every cause indiscriminately, none of which matches how the world actually and reliably works. It is this reasoning, resting on real and carefully observed regularities, that the treatise takes seriously enough to refute point by point rather than simply dismiss.

3. The Refutation: A Cause Only Ever Needs a Condition to Produce Its Effect

The treatise now turns to demolish the doctrine with a classical dilemma: are cause and effect the same thing, or different things? Either answer, pressed carefully, collapses.

Chinese Source

應審問彼：汝何所欲？何者因相？何者果相？因果兩相為異不異？若無異相，便無因果二種決定。因果二種無差別故，因中有果，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

One should press them with this question: what, precisely, do you mean? What is the mark of a cause? What is the mark of an effect? Are these two marks, cause and effect, different from each other or not different? If they are not different, then there can be no fixed distinction between the two at all — cause and effect being simply identical, it makes no sense to speak of "effect existing within cause."

若有異相，汝意云何？因中果性為未生相？為已生相？若未生相，便於因中果猶未生，而說是有，不應道理。若已生相，即果體已生，復從因生，不應道理。是故因中非先有果；然要有因，待緣果生。

If they are different, then consider this: is the effect-nature within the cause already-arisen, or not-yet-arisen? If not-yet-arisen, then the effect has, within the cause, not yet come to be at all — to call it "existing" makes no sense. If already-arisen, then the effect's own substance has already come to be, and to then say it "arises again from the cause" equally makes no sense. Therefore, effect does not pre-exist within cause. Rather, there must be a cause, and, given a further condition, the effect then arises.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through the dilemma as airtight on its own terms: press the Vārṣagaṇya position on whether cause and effect actually differ, and both horns fail. If they are not different — milk simply is yogurt, with no real distinction — then the very words "cause" and "effect" lose all meaning, since nothing is actually being produced from anything. If they are different, a second question immediately follows: within the cause, does that different effect-nature already exist in fully arisen form, or not yet? If not yet arisen, then it plainly does not yet exist, so calling it "already present" is simply false; if already arisen, then it is already a complete, existing thing in its own right, making the claim that it then "arises from" the cause equally incoherent — an already-existing thing cannot meaningfully arise a second time.

He reads the treatise's own positive conclusion as the genuinely Middle Way position, avoiding both extremes at once: effect is neither strictly identical to its cause nor strictly separate from it — milk and yogurt are related, in the sense that only milk (never water) can become yogurt, yet also distinct, since yogurt clearly has qualities milk lacks — and this relation is possible only because a cause, on its own, is never sufficient; it must always still wait upon a further condition (the yogurt-culture, the fermentation, the right length of time) before any effect actually appears. The Vārṣagaṇya error, in Cháng Bǎi's reading, was mistaking a real and rigid correlation between a specific cause and its specific effect for the effect's own hidden prior existence, when what that correlation actually shows is only that a cause reliably needs the right condition, never any condition whatsoever, to produce its own proper result.

4. Five Tests for Whether One Existing Thing Truly Contains Another, and the First Doctrine Closes

The treatise closes its refutation with a five-part practical test: whenever one already-existing thing genuinely contains another, five specific marks should be findable — marks conspicuously absent from the claim that effect already exists within cause.

Chinese Source

又有相法於有相法中，由五種相方可了知。一、於處所可得，如甕中水。二、於所依可得，如眼中眼識。三、即由自相可得，如因自體，不由比度。四、即由自作業可得。五、由因變異故，果成變異；或由緣變異故，果成變異。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, whenever one existing thing genuinely exists within another existing thing, this can be known through five marks. First, it can be found occupying a location, as water is found within a jar. Second, it can be found within its proper support, as eye-consciousness is found within the eye. Third, it can be found through its own characteristic, as a cause's own substance is found — not through mere inference. Fourth, it can be found through its own distinct function. Fifth, when the cause undergoes change, the effect correspondingly changes; or when the condition undergoes change, the effect correspondingly changes.

是故彼說常常時、恆恆時，因中有果，不應道理。由此因緣，彼所立論非如理說。

It is for this reason that their claim — that effect exists within cause always, at every time without exception — does not accord with reason. For this reason, the doctrine they establish is not a soundly reasoned teaching.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats these five marks as a practical checklist for testing any claim that one thing already, actually contains another — water genuinely inside a jar can be pointed to occupying real space within it; eye-consciousness genuinely depends on the eye as its specific physical support; a jar's own clay substance can be directly perceived as itself, not merely inferred; each thing has its own distinct, locatable function (water flows, clay holds firm); and change in the container or its conditions produces a matching, traceable change in what it holds. Effect supposedly hidden within cause, he notes, satisfies none of these: no one can point to rice-nature actually occupying space inside a seed, or perceive it directly the way one perceives water in a jar — its "presence" is asserted, never actually found, which is exactly what distinguishes the Vārṣagaṇya claim from an ordinary, verifiable case of real containment.

With the doctrine now refuted on both its logical structure (the same-or-different dilemma) and this practical evidentiary test, the treatise's first of sixteen non-Buddhist doctrines closes. Cháng Bǎi notes the broader stakes: getting causation wrong here is not a merely academic error, since the Vārṣagaṇya's eternal, unchanging prakṛti functions, in their system, exactly like a hidden permanent self — the same fundamental mistake, dressed in cosmological rather than personal terms, that the treatise will meet again and again across the fifteen doctrines still to come.

5. The Second Doctrine: Effects Are Manifested by Conditions, Not Produced by Them

The treatise turns to a close cousin of the first doctrine — held, tellingly, by the very same Sāṃkhya school together with grammarian Sound-theorists — which agrees that an effect already fully exists, but insists conditions only ever reveal it rather than actually generate it.

Chinese Source

從緣顯了論者，謂如有一，若沙門、若婆羅門，起如是見，立如是論：一切諸法，性本是有，從眾緣顯，不從緣生。謂即因中有果論者及聲相論者作如是計。

Ru-Yi Translation

The doctrine of manifestation through condition: there are certain renunciants or brahmins who hold such a view and establish such a doctrine — that the nature of every phenomenon fundamentally already exists, and is merely made manifest by an assembly of conditions, rather than actually produced by them. It is the very same holders of the doctrine that effect exists within cause, together with the Sound-nature theorists, who hold this position.

彼如是思：果先是有，復從因生，不應道理。然非不用功為成於果，彼復何緣而作功用？豈非唯為顯了果耶？彼作如是妄分別已，立顯了論。

They reason thus: if an effect already exists beforehand, then to say it again "arises from" a cause makes no sense. Yet it is not the case that no effort at all is needed to bring about the effect — so for what reason, then, is effort actually exerted? Is it not solely in order to make the effect manifest? Having reasoned in this mistaken way, they establish the doctrine of manifestation.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as a patch applied to the first doctrine's most obvious weakness: if rice-nature is already fully present in the seed, why does a farmer still need to plant, water, and tend it? Rather than abandon the claim that effect pre-exists, its holders instead redefine what conditions actually do — not creating the effect, only clearing away whatever was hiding it from view, the way removing darkness from a room does not create the table that was there all along, only reveals it. He notes the Sound-nature theorists (grammarian and Mīmāṃsā theorists of eternal, uncreated speech) as natural allies here: on their view, a spoken word's sound-essence is eternal and uncaused, and the act of speaking merely manifests, rather than produces, a reality that was already there before anyone opened their mouth.

6. Three Dilemmas That Dismantle "Manifestation Without Production"

Chinese Source

應當問彼：汝何所欲？為無障緣而有障礙？為有障緣耶？若無障緣者，無障礙緣而有障礙，不應道理。若有障緣者，屬果之因何故不障？同是有故，不應道理。譬如黑闇障甕中水，亦能障甕。若言障緣亦障因者，亦應顯因，俱被障故。而言但顯因中先有果性，不顯因者，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

One should press them thus: what, precisely, do you mean — is there no obstructing condition at all, yet an obstruction is present? Or is there an obstructing condition? If there is no obstructing condition, then to say an obstruction is present with no obstructing condition present makes no sense. If there is an obstructing condition, then why does it not equally obstruct the cause to which the effect belongs, since both equally exist? This makes no sense — just as darkness obstructing the water within a jar equally obstructs the jar itself. And if you say the obstructing condition obstructs the cause as well, then the cause too should be manifested once it is removed, since both were equally obstructed — yet to say only the effect already present within the cause is manifested, and not the cause, makes no sense either.

復應問彼：為有性是障緣？為果性耶？若有性是障緣者，是即有性常不顯了，不應道理。因亦是有，何不為障？若言果性是障緣者，是則一法亦因亦果，如芽是種子果，是莖等因，是即一法亦顯不顯，不應道理。

One should further press them: is bare existence itself the obstructing condition, or is it specifically the nature of being an effect? If bare existence is the obstructing condition, then existence itself should perpetually remain unmanifested — which makes no sense, since the cause too exists, and yet is never said to be obstructed. If the nature of being an effect is the obstructing condition, then consider that one and the same phenomenon is often both an effect and a cause at once — a sprout is the effect of its seed, yet the cause of the stalk that follows it — so that one and the same thing would have to be simultaneously manifested and unmanifested, which makes no sense.

又今問汝，隨汝意答。本法與顯為異不異？若不異者，法應常顯，顯已復顯，不應道理。若言異者，彼顯為無因耶？為有因耶？若言無因，無因而顯，不應道理。若有因者，果性可顯，非是因性，以不顯因能顯於果，不應道理。

Further, I now ask you, answer as you see fit: is the original phenomenon identical to, or different from, its manifestation? If not different, then the phenomenon should always already be manifest — to then speak of it being "manifested" again makes no sense. If different, then is that manifestation itself uncaused, or caused? If uncaused, then an uncaused manifestation makes no sense. If caused, then it is only the effect's own nature that becomes manifest, never the cause's — and manifesting an effect without ever manifesting its cause makes no sense either.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through all three dilemmas as variations on a single relentless pressure test. The first asks what actually removes the "obstruction" hiding the effect: with no obstructing condition at all, an obstruction should not be there to begin with; with one present, it should logically obstruct the cause just as much as the effect — darkness hiding water in a jar obviously hides the jar too — yet the doctrine's holders only ever claim the effect, never the cause, becomes visible once conditions are supplied, an asymmetry they never actually explain.

The second dilemma asks what kind of thing is doing the obstructing: bare existence itself, or specifically the property of being an effect. If existence itself obstructs, nothing could ever become visible, since everything that exists would be equally hidden forever — including causes, which the doctrine's own holders admit are visible. If instead it is specifically "being an effect" that obstructs, the scheme collapses on ordinary examples: a sprout is the effect of its seed and, at the very same time, the cause of the stalk still to come — is it, at that single moment, hidden or visible? The theory gives no coherent answer.

The third dilemma finally asks about the relationship between the hidden "original" thing and its later manifestation: if they are simply the same thing, it should have been visible all along, making "manifestation" an empty word; if they are different, that manifestation is itself either uncaused (absurd, since nothing uncaused ever appears) or caused — and if caused, the same asymmetry recurs, since the theory only ever explains the effect becoming visible, never the cause that supposedly obstructed it in the first place. Cháng Bǎi notes all three dilemmas converge on the same diagnosis: the doctrine invented "manifestation" specifically to rescue "effect already exists" from its own contradiction, but manifestation turns out to raise exactly the same unanswerable questions as production did.

7. Why "Unseen" Never Means "Nonexistent," and the Sound-Theorists Fall the Same Way

Having dismantled the doctrine's logic, the treatise states its own positive teaching — a genuinely useful epistemological principle reaching well beyond this one debate — before applying the identical refutation to the grammarian Sound-theorists and closing out the doctrine entirely.

Chinese Source

是故汝言：若法性無，是即無相，若法性有，是即有相；性若是無，不可顯了，性若是有，方可顯了者；不應道理。我今當說：雖復是有，不可取相。謂或有遠故，雖有而不可取。又由四種障因障故，而不可取。復由極微細故，而不可取。或由心散亂故，而不可取。或由根損壞故，而不可取。或由未得彼相應智故，而不可取。

Ru-Yi Translation

Therefore, your claim — that if a phenomenon's nature is absent, it has no characteristic, and if its nature is present, it has a characteristic; that if its nature is absent, it cannot be manifested, and if its nature is present, only then can it be manifested — does not accord with reason. I shall now state the matter correctly: a thing may indeed exist, and yet its characteristic still not be apprehensible. This may be because it is too distant, and so, though existing, is not apprehensible. It may be because it is obstructed by one of four kinds of obstruction. It may be because it is exceedingly subtle. It may be because the mind is distracted. It may be because a faculty is impaired. Or it may be because one has not yet attained the wisdom that corresponds to it.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this as the treatise correcting a hidden assumption both rival doctrines share: that a thing's existence and its being currently perceptible must always match exactly, so that anything unseen must either not exist yet (the ordinary view) or must be hidden behind some single removable veil (the doctrines just refuted). The Buddhist position instead lists six genuinely different reasons a real, existing thing can fail to appear to a given observer at a given moment, none of which requires positing that the thing was somehow already "there" waiting to be revealed: sheer distance (a mountain city genuinely exists though invisible from far away); four distinct kinds of obstruction — concealment by darkness or ignorance, suppression by a herb's or a spirit's power, being eclipsed by something brighter (a star still exists once the sun has risen, merely outshone), and illusion (an elephant made to look like a rabbit through magical deception); extreme subtlety, itself of three kinds — physical matter analyzed down past the point of visibility, an intermediate-state being unseen yet real, or a siddha's subtle supernormal power; a mind too scattered to perceive a meditative state that genuinely exists for someone with actual concentration; a damaged sense-faculty, whether partially impaired (blurred, doubled, or miscolored vision) or entirely destroyed (blindness, which does not erase the mountains and rivers still there); and, subtlest of all, ultimate truth's own characteristic, genuinely existing yet inapprehensible to anyone who has not yet attained the specific wisdom that alone can grasp it.

He treats this sixfold list as valuable well beyond refuting one rival school — a standing corrective against the ordinary human habit of mistaking "I cannot currently perceive this" for "this does not exist," useful for a meditator assessing their own progress as much as for a philosopher assessing causation.

8. The Sound-Theorists Fall the Same Way, and the Second Doctrine Closes

Chinese Source

如因果顯了論不應道理，當知聲相論者亦不應理。此中差別者，外聲論師起如是見，立如是論：聲相常住，無生無滅；然由宣吐方得顯了。是故此論如顯了論，非應理說。

Ru-Yi Translation

Just as the doctrine of manifestation through condition does not accord with reason, so too the Sound-nature theorists' doctrine should be understood as unreasonable. Their distinctive claim is this: non-Buddhist Sound-theorists hold such a view and establish such a doctrine — that the nature of sound is permanent, neither arising nor ceasing, and is merely made manifest through its utterance. This doctrine, therefore, like the doctrine of manifestation, is not a soundly reasoned teaching.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the Sound-theorists' claim is simply the doctrine of manifestation applied to a single special case: an eternal, uncreated "sound-nature" behind every spoken word, with the physical act of speaking doing nothing but revealing, never creating, that eternal reality — the same theoretical move the grammarian and Mīmāṃsā schools used to secure the eternal authority of the Vedas' own words. Since it rests on the identical claim already dismantled — something wholly existing beforehand, merely awaiting a condition to become manifest — the treatise simply refers back to its own prior refutation rather than repeating it, closing this second of sixteen doctrines and turning next to the third: whether the past and future truly, substantially exist.

9. The Third Doctrine: Do Past and Future Truly, Substantially Exist?

The treatise's third doctrine marks a notable shift — for the first time, the position under scrutiny is held not only by outside teachers but by certain Buddhists themselves, who read scripture as teaching that past and future are just as real as the present.

Chinese Source

去來實有論者，謂如有一，若沙門、若婆羅門、若在此法者，由不正思惟故，起如是見，立如是論：有過去，有未來，其相成就，猶如現在，實有非假。

Ru-Yi Translation

The doctrine that past and future truly exist: there are certain renunciants, brahmins, or even those within this very teaching who, through unsound reflection, hold such a view and establish such a doctrine — that the past exists, the future exists, their characteristics fully accomplished exactly as the present's are, truly existing rather than merely nominal.

謂如經言：一切有者，即十二處。此十二處，實相是有。又薄伽梵說有過去業。又說有過去色、有未來色，廣說乃至識亦如是。

That is: as the sūtra states, "all that exists" refers to the twelve sense-bases — and so, they reason, these twelve sense-bases truly, substantially exist. Further, the Blessed One spoke of past karma existing. Further, he spoke of past form existing and future form existing, and so on in full detail through consciousness as well.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies this doctrine's Buddhist wing as the Sarvāstivāda ("all exists") school, whose very name announces the position: past, present, and future phenomena all substantially exist, karma performed long ago retaining a real, persisting substance somewhere in the past rather than simply vanishing. He traces their scriptural basis to three passages easily misread this way: the Buddha's statement that "all that exists" is the twelve sense-bases, taken to mean all twelve substantially exist, full stop; his repeated references to "past karma" as though naming a still-existing thing rather than merely a past event; and the classical contemplation formula instructing practitioners to observe form "whether past, future, or present, internal or external, coarse or fine" as equally real objects of observation — all three, on the Sarvāstivādin reading, implying the past and future must be just as substantially there as the present, or the Buddha's own words would be speaking of nothing at all.

10. Why They Thought Denying Past and Future Would Destroy Karma and Cognition

Chinese Source

彼如是思：若法自相安住，此法真實是有。此若未來無者，爾時應未受相；此若過去無者，爾時應失自相。若如是者，諸法自相應不成就。由此道理，亦非真實，故不應理。由是思惟，起如是見，立如是論：過去、未來性相實有。

Ru-Yi Translation

They reason thus: if a phenomenon's own characteristic remains established, that phenomenon truly exists. Were this phenomenon nonexistent while future, it would, at that time, never have taken on its characteristic at all; were it nonexistent once past, it would, at that time, have already lost its own characteristic. Were this so, no phenomenon's own characteristic could ever be genuinely established — and by this reasoning, nothing would be truly real, which makes no sense. Reasoning in this way, they arrive at this view, and establish this doctrine: that the characteristics of the past and future truly, substantially exist.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reconstructs the underlying worry driving this whole doctrine: if a future phenomenon is simply nonexistent until the moment it arises, then nothing at all would actually be waiting to arise — arising would come from sheer nothing, which feels deeply wrong; and if a past phenomenon simply ceases to exist the instant it passes, then how could a deed committed years ago still somehow determine a result appearing only now? Without treating both past and future as substantially real, they worried, the entire mechanism of karmic cause and effect across time would collapse into incoherence — a deed that no longer exists cannot cause anything, and a result that does not yet exist cannot be caused by anything. It is this genuine anxiety about preserving karmic continuity, Cháng Bǎi notes, not mere stubbornness, that drove otherwise careful Buddhist thinkers to conclude past and future must be every bit as real as the present — setting up the treatise's own, very different solution still to come.

11. Two Dilemmas: Is a Past or Future Thing the Same as, or Different From, a Present One?

Chinese Source

應審問彼：汝何所欲？去來二相與現在相為一為異？若言相一，立三世相，不應道理。若相異者，性相實有，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

One should press them thus: what, precisely, do you mean — are the characteristics of the past and future the same as, or different from, the characteristic of the present? If you say they are the same, then establishing three distinct times makes no sense. If they are different, then claiming past and future substantially, truly exist just as the present does makes no sense.

又汝應說自意所欲。墮三世法，為是常相？為無常相？若常相者，墮在三世，不應道理。若無常相，於三世中恆是實有，不應道理。

Further, you should state what you actually intend: is a phenomenon falling within the three times permanent in character, or impermanent? If permanent, then for it to fall within three distinct times at all makes no sense. If impermanent, then for it to be equally, perpetually, truly existent throughout all three times makes no sense.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads both dilemmas as exposing the same underlying tension from two angles. The first: if a thing's past, present, and future characteristics are simply identical, there is nothing left to distinguish "three times" at all — the very words past, present, and future would be empty labels for one undifferentiated reality; yet if they genuinely differ, only the present characteristic is ever actually apprehended (a phenomenon exists as itself only once it has arisen and before it ceases), so calling the past and future's different characteristics equally "truly existent" contradicts the very fact that only the present is ever actually found.

The second dilemma presses the same point through change itself: something permanent, by definition, cannot fall into distinct temporal phases at all — permanence and having a past, present, and future are simply incompatible; yet something impermanent is, by definition, exactly what does not remain equally existent across all three times, since impermanence just means having arisen and having yet to cease. Either way, Cháng Bǎi notes, the Sarvāstivādin claim runs directly against ordinary reasoning about what "past," "future," and "impermanent" actually mean — the correct Buddhist teaching, given a few sections later, is that only present phenomena, already arisen and not yet ceased, genuinely have a characteristic that can be found at all; the past and future are real only in the sense that a cause has already left its residue as a seed, or a result is already assured by conditions now in place — never as substances somehow persisting or pre-existing in their own right.

12. Seven Ways a Future Thing Might "Arrive" at the Present — All Refuted

Pressing further, the treatise poses seven specific mechanisms by which a future phenomenon could supposedly become a present one, and dismantles each in turn — the treatise's most granular single refutation anywhere in this fascicle so far.

Chinese Source

又今問汝，隨汝意答。為計未來法來至現在世耶？為彼死已，於此生耶？為即住未來為緣，生現在耶？為本無業，今有業耶？為本相不圓滿，今相圓滿耶？為本異相，今異相耶？為於未來有現在分耶？

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, I now ask you, answer as you see fit: do you hold that a future phenomenon arrives in the present world? Or that it dies, and only then is born here? Or that, remaining in the future, it serves as a condition that gives rise to the present? Or that it has no function while future, and gains a function now? Or that its characteristic is incomplete while future, and becomes complete now? Or that its characteristic while future differs from its characteristic now? Or that a portion of the present already exists within the future?

若即未來法來至現在者，此便有方所；復與現在應無差別；復應是常；不應道理。若言未來死已現在生者，是即未來不生於今；現在世法本無今生；又未來未生而言死沒；不應道理。

If a future phenomenon simply arrives in the present, it would have to already occupy a location — it would then be indistinguishable from a present phenomenon, and would have to be permanent; none of this makes sense. If you say it dies while future and is then born in the present, then what was future does not itself give rise to the present; the present phenomenon would then have no genuine "arising" at all; and, further, to speak of something dying that was never even born makes no sense.

若言法住未來，以彼為緣生現在者，彼應是常；又應本無今生，非未來法生；不應道理。若本無業用，今有業用，是則本無今有，便有如前所說過失，不應道理。

If you say the phenomenon, remaining in the future, serves as a condition producing the present, then it would have to be permanent, and, again, the present would have no genuine arising and would not actually be produced by the future; this makes no sense. If it has no function while future and only gains a function now, this is again a case of something nonexistent becoming existent, carrying the same faults already stated; this makes no sense.

如無業用有此過失，如是相圓滿、異相、未來分相應知亦爾。此中差別者，復有自性雜亂過失故，不應道理。如未來向現在如是，現在往過去，如其所應過失應知。

Just as the "gaining a function" claim carries this fault, so too should the "characteristic becomes complete," "characteristic differs," and "a portion already present" claims each be understood to carry it as well. What further distinguishes these is the fault of the very self-nature of the phenomenon becoming confused — one and the same thing being, at once, incomplete and complete, or both cause-aspect and result-aspect; this makes no sense. Just as these faults apply to the future becoming present, so too, correspondingly, should they be understood to apply to the present passing into the past.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through all seven mechanisms as exhausting every conceivable way "arrival" could actually work, each failing for its own precise reason. Literal arrival requires the future thing to already have a location to arrive from — but nothing about tomorrow can be located anywhere today — and if it did have one, it would already be indistinguishable from something present, and permanent besides, since it would have existed unchanged the whole time. Dying-then-being-reborn fares no better: what dies cannot be what causes the new birth, so the present would have no real source at all, and worse, calling something "dead" that was never yet born is simply incoherent. Remaining future while merely "serving as a condition" repeats the same permanence problem, since only an already-arisen thing can properly function as a condition for anything. And the remaining four — gaining a function it lacked, becoming complete where it was incomplete, differing in characteristic from its future self, or already containing a "present portion" while still future — all reduce to the same basic move: something nonexistent quietly becoming existent, dressed up in different vocabulary each time, and the last three additionally saddle one single phenomenon with two flatly contradictory natures at once (incomplete and complete, cause-aspect and result-aspect), a confusion of self-nature no coherent theory can survive. He notes the identical seven failures recur, symmetrically, when a present phenomenon is said to "pass into" the past — the same impossible mechanics run in reverse.

13. If Past and Future Aren't Real, How Can the Mind Even Think About Them?

Having pressed the Sarvāstivādin position to its breaking point across nine separate angles, the treatise now answers the objection its own conclusion seems to invite: if past and future do not truly exist, how can the mind cognize them at all without cognizing sheer nothing?

Chinese Source

如是自相故，共相故，來故，死故，為緣生故，業故，相圓滿故，相異故，未來有分故，說過去未來體實有論，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

Thus, through self-characteristic, through shared characteristic, through arrival, through dying, through serving as a condition of production, through function, through completeness of characteristic, through difference of characteristic, and through the future already containing a portion of the present — through these nine approaches, the doctrine that the substance of past and future truly exists does not accord with reason.

復有難言：若過去、未來是無，云何緣無而有覺轉？若言緣無而有覺轉者，云何不有違教過失？如說：一切有者，謂十二處。我今問汝，隨汝意答。世間取無之覺為起耶？為不起耶？若不起者，能取無我、兔角、石女兒等覺皆應是無，此不應理。

A further objection is raised: if the past and future do not exist, how can cognition operate by taking something nonexistent as its object? And if cognition can indeed operate by taking something nonexistent as its object, how does this not contradict the Buddha's own teaching that "all that exists" is the twelve sense-bases? I now ask you in turn, answer as you see fit: does the world's cognition of what is nonexistent arise at all, or does it not? If it does not arise, then cognition taking non-self, a hare's horn, or a barren woman's child as its object should likewise be nonexistent — which makes no sense.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this as a genuinely fair objection the Sarvāstivādins raise against the treatise's own position, not a strawman: everyday experience clearly shows the mind thinking about the past and imagining the future constantly, so if neither substantially exists, doesn't cognition end up grasping pure nothingness — which sounds absurd, and which also seems to contradict the Buddha's own statement that "all that exists" is the twelve sense-bases, since past and future form and consciousness were explicitly included among what the Buddha called existent?

The treatise's counter-question exposes the objection's own hidden assumption: everyday cognition plainly does take non-existent things as objects all the time — the concept of "no-self," a hare's horn, or a barren woman's child are all classic Buddhist examples of coherently thinkable things that are nonetheless not substantially, independently existing entities out there in the world. If the objector insists cognition of the nonexistent never arises at all, they would have to deny that anyone can even think about non-self or a hare's horn, which plainly happens and which no one, Buddhist or otherwise, actually wants to deny. Cháng Bǎi flags this as the treatise's opening move in a longer answer still to come — showing that the mind's capacity to cognize the past and future is fully explicable without requiring either to be a substantially, independently existing thing, dissolving the apparent contradiction with the Buddha's twelve-sense-bases teaching along the way.

14. The Hidden Intent Behind "All That Exists Is the Twelve Sense-Bases"

Having shown that cognition of the nonexistent is perfectly ordinary and unproblematic, the treatise turns to explain what the Buddha's own scriptural statements actually meant — beginning with the very passage the Sarvāstivādins built their whole doctrine on.

Chinese Source

又雖說一切有者，謂十二處；然於有法密意說有有相，於無法密意說有無相。所以者何？若有相法能持有相，若無相法能持無相；是故俱名為法，俱名為有。若異此者，諸修行者唯知於有，不知於無；應非無間觀所知法，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, although it is said that "all that exists" refers to the twelve sense-bases, this is spoken with hidden intent: of what has existent characteristics, it is said to have the characteristic of existing; of what has no such characteristics, it is said to have the characteristic of not existing. Why is this? Because whatever has an existent characteristic can sustain that existent characteristic, and whatever has no such characteristic can sustain that absence — and so both alike are called a "phenomenon," and both alike are called "existent." Were it otherwise, practitioners would know only the existent and never the nonexistent, and so could not continuously and without interruption contemplate every knowable phenomenon — which makes no sense.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi unpacks "hidden intent" as the treatise's standard tool for reconciling an apparently literal scriptural statement with its own deeper teaching: the Buddha's word "phenomenon" (法) in "all that exists is the twelve sense-bases" was never meant to certify that everything nameable is a substantially existing thing — it was meant to cover both what genuinely exists (the five aggregates, causally arisen and directly experienced) and what does not (a permanent self, a hare's horn), precisely because the sixth consciousness, unlike the five sense-consciousnesses that can only cognize their own present physical objects, is uniquely capable of taking either the existent or the nonexistent as its object. Both count as "phenomena" and both are loosely called "existent" in this technical sense — one existing as an actual referent, the other existing only as a coherently thinkable absence — precisely so that a meditator practicing insight can systematically contemplate every knowable thing without gaps, including the crucial insight that no fixed self exists at all. Had the Buddha's statement really meant only substantially real things could ever be objects of the mind, no practitioner could ever complete the contemplation of non-self that is central to the entire path — an absurd result that itself proves the "hidden intent" reading correct.

15. "Past Karma Exists" Really Means Its Habitual Imprint Persists

The treatise now unpacks the second scriptural pillar of the Sarvāstivādin position — the Buddha's own statements about "past karma" — showing they point not to the deed itself somehow still existing, but to something the treatise has already carefully explained: the seed it leaves behind.

Chinese Source

又雖說言有過去業；由此業故，諸有情受有損害受、受無損害受。此亦依彼習氣，密意假說為有。謂於諸行中，曾有淨不淨業若生若滅。由此因緣，彼行勝異相續而轉，是名習氣。由此相續所攝習氣故，愛不愛果生。是故於我無過，而汝不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, although it is said that past karma exists, and that by reason of this karma beings experience harmful or harmless feeling, this too is spoken provisionally, with hidden intent, in reference to its habitual imprint. That is: among the formations, pure or impure karma once arose and ceased. By reason of this, those formations continue on, transformed and intensified in succession — this is called a habitual imprint. It is by reason of this habitual imprint, held within the mental continuum, that desirable and undesirable results arise. There is, therefore, no fault in my position — it is your own position that does not accord with reason.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi ties this directly back to the treatise's own earlier account, in fascicle 5, of the impelling cause and the moistened seed: when the Buddha spoke of "past karma" producing present pleasant or painful feeling, he never meant the karmic act itself somehow still exists somewhere in a real past — the act arose and ceased just once, in the ordinary way. What actually persists, carried forward moment to moment in the ālayavijñāna's own continuum, is the habitual imprint that act left behind: a seed, progressively reinforced by subsequent craving and clinging, which eventually ripens into exactly the desirable or undesirable result that seed was fitted to produce. Speaking of "past karma existing" is simply an economical, provisional way of referring to this seed's continued presence in an unbroken mental stream — not a claim that the past itself, as a substantial reality, is still sitting somewhere waiting to be visited. Read this way, the doctrine of karmic continuity across time is fully preserved — nothing about moral cause and effect is lost — without ever requiring the incoherent claim that a completed, vanished event somehow still exists.

16. Cause-Aspect, Own-Aspect, Result-Aspect: Why Form Is Called Past, Present, and Future

The treatise closes its threefold "hidden intent" explanation with the third scriptural pillar — why the Buddha spoke of form, feeling, and the rest as past, present, and future at all — resolving it into three aspects that point to impermanence rather than three substantially real time-periods.

Chinese Source

復雖說言有過去色、有未來色、有現在色，如是乃至識亦爾者，此亦依三種行相密意故說。謂因相、自相、果相。依彼因相，密意說有未來；依彼自相，密意說有現在；依彼果相，密意說有過去；是故無過。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, although it is said that past form exists, future form exists, and present form exists, and so on in the same way through consciousness, this too is spoken with hidden intent, in reference to three aspects: the cause-aspect, the own-aspect, and the result-aspect. It is with reference to the cause-aspect that future existence is spoken of with hidden intent; with reference to the own-aspect that present existence is spoken of with hidden intent; and with reference to the result-aspect that past existence is spoken of with hidden intent. There is, therefore, no fault in this teaching.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses the three aspects concretely: the cause-aspect names a phenomenon not yet actually experienced, still awaiting the right conditions to arise — this is what "future" really refers to, a real causal potential rather than a substantial thing already sitting in wait; the own-aspect names a phenomenon actually being experienced right now, its own substance not yet having ceased — this is simply what "present" means; and the result-aspect names a phenomenon already experienced, its substance already having ceased — this is what "past" really refers to, the settled result a cause has already produced, not a persisting entity. He notes the entire threefold scheme is explicitly designed, in the treatise's own words, to display the impermanence of all conditioned phenomena across the three times — the very opposite conclusion from the Sarvāstivādin claim that past, present, and future are all equally, substantially real. Where the Sarvāstivādins heard "past color exists" and concluded past color must be a persisting substance, the correct reading hears the same words and concludes only that a result now stands where its cause once operated — form is called "past" the moment its own aspect gives way to being merely someone's completed result, nothing more.

With this, the treatise has fully answered why scripture speaks of past and future "existing" at all, without needing to grant them any substantial reality: the twelve sense-bases' "existence" covers the nonexistent as an object of thought no less than the existent; "past karma" names a persisting seed, not a persisting deed; and "past and future form" names a cause-aspect and result-aspect relative to the present moment, not a parallel substantial reality running alongside it. The treatise now turns to directly refute the claim that past and future have any truly existing characteristic at all, closing the third of sixteen doctrines.

17. Twelve Marks Each of Future, Present, and Past — and Doctrine Three Closes

The treatise's final word on the third doctrine is not merely negative. It offers twelve marks each for future, present, and past phenomena — thirty-six in all — turning what began as a technical refutation of substantial three-time realism into a practical framework for how a practitioner should actually regard each of the three times.

Chinese Source

應知未來有十二種相故。一、因所顯相，二、體未生相，三、待眾緣相，四、已生種類相，五、可生法相，六、不可生法相，七、未生雜染相，八、未生清淨相，九、應可求相，十、不應求相，十一、應觀察相，十二、不應觀察相。

Ru-Yi Translation

It should be understood that the future has twelve marks: first, the mark of being made manifest through a cause; second, the mark of a substance not yet arisen; third, the mark of awaiting an assembly of conditions; fourth, the mark of belonging to a kind already arisen elsewhere; fifth, the mark of a phenomenon still capable of arising; sixth, the mark of a phenomenon no longer capable of arising; seventh, the mark of a not-yet-arisen defilement; eighth, the mark of a not-yet-arisen purity; ninth, the mark of what should rightly be sought; tenth, the mark of what should not be sought; eleventh, the mark of what should be contemplated; twelfth, the mark of what should not be contemplated.

當知現在亦有十二種相。一、果所顯相，二、體已生相，三、眾緣會相，四、已生種類相，五、一剎那相，六、不復生法相，七、現雜染相，八、現清淨相，九、可喜樂相，十、不可喜樂相，十一、應觀察相，十二、不應觀察相。

It should be understood that the present likewise has twelve marks: first, the mark of being made manifest as a result; second, the mark of a substance already arisen; third, the mark of an actual assembly of conditions; fourth, the mark of belonging to a kind already arisen; fifth, the mark of lasting but a single instant; sixth, the mark of not arising again once spent; seventh, the mark of present defilement; eighth, the mark of present purity; ninth, the mark of what is genuinely delightful; tenth, the mark of what is not delightful; eleventh, the mark of what should be contemplated; twelfth, the mark of what should not be contemplated.

當知過去亦有十二種相。一、已度因相，二、已度緣相，三、已度果相，四、體已壞相，五、已滅種類相，六、不復生法相，七、靜息雜染相，八、靜息清淨相，九、應顧戀處相，十、不應顧戀處相，十一、應觀察相，十二、不應觀察相。

It should be understood that the past likewise has twelve marks: first, the mark of a cause already passed; second, the mark of a condition already passed; third, the mark of a result already passed; fourth, the mark of a substance already destroyed; fifth, the mark of belonging to a kind already ceased; sixth, the mark of never arising again; seventh, the mark of defilement now stilled; eighth, the mark of purity now stilled; ninth, the mark of what should rightly be looked back on with longing; tenth, the mark of what should not be looked back on with longing; eleventh, the mark of what should be contemplated; twelfth, the mark of what should not be contemplated.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the first six marks in each set as simply cataloguing how each time genuinely relates to causal process — the future as what a present cause points toward yet still awaits its own conditions before arising; the present as an actual result now manifest, existing for a single fleeting instant before it is inevitably spent, never to recur in that exact form; the past as what has already fully run its course, its causes, conditions, and result all spent, its substance already destroyed, gone for good. None of the three, he stresses, is granted the substantial, persisting existence the Sarvāstivādins wanted — only the present even has a "substance already arisen" at all, and even that lasts a single instant.

The remaining six marks in each set, he notes, turn the same analysis into direct practical guidance: a future defilement not yet arisen can still be prevented, a future purity not yet arisen can still be cultivated, and unsurpassed awakening is the one future thing genuinely worth seeking, while craving for desire, meditative bliss, or a mistaken sense of liberation are not; the present offers what is genuinely delightful and what is not, and calls for actively contemplating the Buddha's own teaching rather than whatever breeds fresh defilement; and the past, its defilement or purity now equally stilled, divides into what is worth fondly recalling and continuing (past wholesome cultivation) and what is not (past indulgence in desire, better simply released once already confessed and purified, since dwelling on old faults, he says, is itself an arrow lodged in the heart that blocks further practice).

With this thirty-six-mark framework, the treatise completes its refutation of both the non-Buddhist and the internal Sarvāstivādin claim that past, present, and future are all equally, substantially real — only the present genuinely exists as an arisen substance, if only for an instant — while converting the entire debate into something a practitioner can actually use: how to relate rightly to what has not yet come, what is happening now, and what has already passed. This closes the third of sixteen doctrines. The treatise turns next to the fourth: the doctrine positing a self.

18. The Fourth Doctrine: A Self That Persists Through the Five Aggregates

The treatise's fourth doctrine confronts what may be the single most natural and universal error it addresses: the ordinary, near-automatic sense that a stable "self" exists behind experience — one held not by any single school but, in some form, by non-Buddhist teachers generally.

Chinese Source

計我論者，謂如有一，若沙門、若婆羅門，起如是見，立如是論：有我、薩埵、命者、生者、有養育者、數取趣者，如是等諦實常住。謂外道等作如是計。

Ru-Yi Translation

The doctrine positing a self: there are certain renunciants or brahmins who hold such a view and establish such a doctrine — that there exists a self, a sentient being, a life-possessor, one who is born, one who is nurtured, one who repeatedly takes up a destiny — all such terms naming something truly, permanently existing. It is non-Buddhist teachers generally who hold this position.

由二種因故。一、先不思覺，率爾而得有薩埵覺故；二、先已思覺，得有作故。彼如是思：若無我者，見於五事不應起於五有我覺。

This arises from two causes. First, without any prior deliberation, the sense of a sentient being spontaneously arises. Second, having first deliberately conceived of a self, one is then able to act. They reason thus: were there no self, upon perceiving any of five things, five corresponding senses of "self" ought never to arise.

一、見色形已，唯應起於色形之覺，不應起於薩埵之覺。二、見順苦樂行已，唯應起於受覺，不應起於勝劣薩埵之覺。三、見已立名者名相應行已，唯應起於想覺，不應起於剎帝利、婆羅門、吠舍、戍陀羅、佛授、德友等薩埵之覺。

First: upon seeing a bodily form, one should only ever arrive at the sense of a bodily shape, never at the sense of a sentient being. Second: upon witnessing what accords with pain or with pleasure, one should only ever arrive at a sense of mere feeling, never at a sense of a sentient being as superior or inferior. Third: upon seeing one already given a name and the conduct associated with that name, one should only ever arrive at a sense of mere conception, never at a sense of this sentient being as a warrior, a brahmin, a merchant, a laborer, or as "Buddhadatta" or "Guṇamitra" and the like.

四、見作淨不淨相應行已，唯應起於行覺，不應起於愚者、智者薩埵之覺。五、見於境界，識隨轉已，唯應起於心覺，不應起於我能見等薩埵之覺。由如是先不思覺，於此五事唯起五種薩埵之覺，非諸行覺。是故先不思覺，見已率爾而起，有薩埵覺故，如是決定知有實我。

Fourth: upon seeing conduct that is either pure or impure, one should only ever arrive at a sense of mere formation, never at a sense of this sentient being as foolish or wise. Fifth: upon seeing consciousness follow along with its object, one should only ever arrive at a sense of mere mind, never at a sense of "I am the one who sees" and the like. Yet since, without prior deliberation, these five things each give rise to a sense of a sentient being rather than a sense of mere formations, and since this sense arises spontaneously the very instant of perceiving, they conclude with certainty that a real self must exist.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this first argument as the most psychologically compelling of the whole doctrine, precisely because it describes something every unenlightened mind actually experiences: seeing a body, the mind does not stop at "a shape" but leaps immediately to "someone"; seeing pain or pleasure, it does not stop at "a feeling" but leaps to judging that person as fortunate or unfortunate; seeing a name and its associated conduct, it does not stop at "a concept" but leaps to caste and identity — warrior, brahmin, merchant, laborer, or a specific named individual; seeing wholesome or unwholesome conduct, it does not stop at "an action" but leaps to judging the doer wise or foolish; and seeing consciousness track its object, it does not stop at "cognition occurring" but leaps to "I am the one seeing." Since this leap to "a sentient being" happens instantly and without any deliberate reasoning, across all five aggregates uniformly, non-Buddhist teachers concluded it must be tracking something real and stable — a genuine, permanent self underlying the five aggregates' constant flux — rather than being, as the treatise will go on to argue, simply a deeply ingrained habitual projection with no real referent at all.

19. The Second Reason: Self Assumed Wherever Deliberate Action Occurs

The treatise completes the doctrine's second supporting reason — that a self must exist because deliberate intention seems to precede every action — before turning, across a long and rigorous sequence of dilemmas, to dismantle both reasons entirely.

Chinese Source

又彼如是思：若無我者，不應於諸行中，先起思覺，得有所作。謂我以眼當見諸色，正見諸色，已見諸色；或復起心，我不當見。如是等用，皆由我覺行為先導。如於眼見，如是於耳鼻舌身意，應知亦爾。

Ru-Yi Translation

They further reason thus: were there no self, one could not, among the formations, first conceive an intention and thereby come to act. That is: "I shall see these forms with my eye," "I am now seeing these forms," "I have already seen these forms" — or else the thought arises, "I shall not see." All such functions are led by a prior sense of "I." As with seeing through the eye, so too should it be understood with the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind.

又於善業造作、善業止息，不善業造作、不善業止息，如是等事皆由思覺為先方得作用，應不可得。如是等用，唯於諸行，不應道理。由如是思，故說有我。

Further, the performing of wholesome karma, the ceasing of wholesome karma, the performing of unwholesome karma, and the ceasing of unwholesome karma — such actions, too, could only ever function given a prior deliberate intention; without one, they could never be found to occur at all. Such functioning, they conclude, could never belong to mere impersonal formations alone — this makes no sense. It is by reasoning in this way that they maintain a self exists.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this second reason as complementing the first: where the first reason pointed to an involuntary, instantaneous sense of "someone" arising the moment any of the five aggregates is perceived, this second reason points to the equally familiar experience of deliberate intention — "I shall look," "I am looking," "I have looked," or choosing not to — running through every one of the six sense-faculties, and through every wholesome or unwholesome deed a person chooses to perform or to refrain from performing. Since mere impersonal, momentary formations, on their own, seemed to this school incapable of accounting for that felt sense of a deciding agent standing behind each act, they concluded a genuine, persisting self must be what actually decides and acts — closing out the doctrine's full positive case before the treatise turns to methodically dismantle it.

20. Four Dilemmas That Dismantle the Sense of a Self

The treatise now presses the doctrine with a sequence of precise dilemmas, each forcing a choice between two options and showing both to fail.

Chinese Source

我今問汝，隨汝意答。為即於所見事起薩埵覺？為異於所見事起薩埵覺耶？若即於所見事起薩埵覺者，汝不應言：即於色等計有薩埵、計有我者，是顛倒覺。若異於所見事起薩埵覺者，我有形量，不應道理；或有勝劣，或剎帝利等，或愚或智，或能取彼色等境界，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

I now ask you, answer as you see fit: does the sense of a sentient being arise directly within what is seen, or apart from what is seen? If directly within what is seen, then you should not also claim that positing a sentient being, positing a self, directly within form and the rest, is itself a distorted perception — yet your own school says exactly this elsewhere. If apart from what is seen, then for the self to have any size or shape makes no sense; for it to be superior or inferior, a warrior or a brahmin, foolish or wise, or capable of apprehending form and the rest as its object, likewise makes no sense — since none of these are properties a self apart from the aggregates could ever possess.

又汝何所欲？為唯由此法自體起此覺耶？為亦由餘體起此覺耶？若唯由此法自體起此覺者，即於所見起彼我覺，不應說名為顛倒覺。若亦由餘體起此覺者，即一切境界各是一切境界覺因，故不應理。

Further, what do you actually intend: does this sense arise solely from the aggregates' own substance, or also from some other substance? If solely from the aggregates' own substance, then arriving at a sense of self directly within what is seen should not be called a distorted perception after all. If also from some other substance, then every object whatsoever would equally be the cause of every other sense of self — which makes no sense.

又汝何所欲？於無情數有情覺，於有情數無情覺，於餘有情數餘有情覺，為起為不起耶？若起者，是即無情應是有情，有情應是無情，是餘有情應是餘有情，此不應理。若不起者，則誹撥現量，不應道理。

Further, what do you actually intend: does a sense of a sentient being ever arise regarding something insentient, a sense of the insentient regarding something sentient, or a sense of one sentient being regarding a different one — or does this never happen? If it does happen, then the insentient would have to actually be sentient, the sentient insentient, and one being actually another — none of which makes sense. If it never happens, then this denies what is plainly observed every day — which likewise makes no sense.

又汝何所欲？此薩埵覺為取現量義？為取比量義耶？若取現量義者，唯色等蘊是現量義，我非現量義，故不應理。

Further, what do you actually intend: does this sense of a sentient being take its object through direct perception, or through inference? If through direct perception, then only the aggregates — form and the rest — are ever objects of direct perception; a self is never an object of direct perception at all, so this makes no sense.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through all four dilemmas as systematically closing off every place a self could possibly be hiding. The first asks whether the sense of "someone" arises right within the aggregates themselves or somewhere apart from them: if right within them, the doctrine's own claim that seeing a self within the aggregates is a "distorted perception" directly contradicts itself; if apart from them, a bodiless, sizeless, propertyless self could never explain why the sense of self always comes dressed in exactly the aggregates' own properties — a body's shape, a feeling's quality, a caste identity, a wise or foolish act, an object apprehended — since a self entirely separate from the aggregates could not intelligibly possess any of these at all.

The second asks whether that sense arises solely from the aggregates' own nature or from some further, separate substance: solely from the aggregates concedes the whole game, since then "self" is nothing but a name for the aggregates themselves, exactly what the treatise wants to show; from a separate substance leads to absurdity, since if a sense of self can arise from something entirely apart from what generates it, any object at all should be equally capable of generating a sense of self about anything else, dissolving any reliable connection between what is perceived and what is judged.

The third exposes the same fragility from another angle — everyday experience clearly shows people mistaking a distant stump for a person, a motionless guard for a tree, or one person for another; if the doctrine denies such perceptual mix-ups ever happen, it denies plain, common experience, but if it admits they happen, it has just admitted that the very same faculty generating the "sense of self" is fully capable of generating a mistaken, groundless impression — undermining any claim that the self-sense reliably tracks something real. And the fourth cuts most directly: a self is never actually, directly perceived the way the aggregates are — no one has ever seen, heard, or touched a bare self apart from color, sound, feeling, and thought — so the felt sense of "I" cannot be resting on direct perception at all, only, at best, on an unreliable inference the treatise has still further dilemmas waiting to dismantle.

21. Four More Dilemmas, Now Aimed at the Second Reason — Deliberate Action

Having closed off the "instantaneous sense of a being" reason, the treatise turns its identical dilemma-method onto the doctrine's second reason: that deliberate intention preceding action proves a self exists.

Chinese Source

若取比量義者，如愚稚等未能思度，不應率爾起於我覺。又我今問汝，隨汝意答。如世間所作，為以覺為因？為以我為因？若以覺為因者，執我所作，不應道理。若以我為因者，要先思覺，得有所作，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

If through inference, then even foolish children, incapable of reasoning, should never spontaneously arrive at a sense of self — yet they plainly do. Further, I now ask you, answer as you see fit: is worldly action caused by mere cognition, or caused by a self? If caused by mere cognition, then to insist it is caused by a self makes no sense. If caused by a self, then requiring a prior deliberate intention before any action can occur makes no sense — since the self, on your own account, is what already causes it.

又汝何所欲？所作事因常無常耶？若無常者，此所作因，體是變異；執我有作，不應道理。若是常者，即無變異；無變有作，不應道理。

Further, what do you actually intend: is the cause of action permanent, or impermanent? If impermanent, its own substance undergoes change — yet you hold the self to be permanent, so claiming this changeable cause is the self makes no sense. If permanent, it undergoes no change at all — yet something utterly unchanging producing action makes no sense either.

又汝何所欲？為有動作之我能有所作？為無動作之我有所作耶？若有動作之我能有所作者，是即常作，不應復作。若無動作之我有所作者，無動作性，而有所作，不應道理。

Further, what do you actually intend: does a self with motion produce action, or a self without motion? If a self with motion produces action, it should then act perpetually and without pause — never needing to act again after having already acted — yet this plainly is not so. If a self without motion produces action, then something with no capacity for motion at all nonetheless producing action makes no sense.

又汝何所欲？為有因故我有所作？為無因耶？若有因者，此我應由餘因策發方有所作，不應道理。若無因者，應一切時作一切事，不應道理。

Further, what do you actually intend: does the self act because of some further cause, or without any cause at all? If because of some further cause, then the self must first be roused to action by that further cause — yet you claim the self itself is the cause, so this makes no sense. If without any cause at all, then, being permanent, it should act on everything at all times without exception — yet this plainly is not so.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads all four dilemmas as converging on a single, elegant proof: whatever genuinely causes action must itself be impermanent — capable of sometimes being active and sometimes not, the way a person sometimes cooks and sometimes rests, sometimes reads and sometimes stops — while a permanent self, by the doctrine's own definition, can never change at all, and so could never explain why action starts and stops as it plainly does. The first dilemma catches the doctrine contradicting itself: if mere cognition already causes action, the self is redundant; if the self causes it, requiring a further prior "intention" makes no sense, since the doctrine had claimed the self itself does the causing.

The second and third dilemmas press the same point through permanence directly: a permanent cause cannot change, so it cannot explain why action sometimes occurs and sometimes doesn't; an unmoving self could not produce motion in the first place, while a moving self — if it is genuinely permanent — should be moving and acting at every single moment without exception, since permanence rules out the very starting and stopping every observer actually witnesses (the eye looks, then rests; the ear listens, then stops). The fourth exposes the same tension from the angle of causation itself: if the self needs some further cause to prompt it into action, it is not really the ultimate cause of anything; if it needs no cause at all, being permanent it should act ceaselessly and universally, which manifestly does not happen. Cháng Bǎi notes that every one of these eight dilemmas, across both of the doctrine's original two reasons, keeps arriving at the identical structural flaw: a permanent, unchanging self simply cannot do the one thing the doctrine needs it to do — start and stop producing particular actions at particular times — while only impermanent, momentary mental factors, exactly as the treatise's own analysis of thought and volition already described, can actually account for why action happens when and how it does.

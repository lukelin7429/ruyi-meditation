Fascicle 6 · 瑜伽師地論卷第六 · Continuing Directly from Fascicle 5

Sixteen Doctrines the Buddha Rejected

Fascicle 5 ended mid-topic, having just finished sound reasoning attention — not a natural stopping point, simply where the physical scroll divides. This page picks up exactly there: unsound reasoning attention, unpacked through a systematic survey of sixteen non-Buddhist doctrines current in the Buddha's own India — their claims, the reasoning and scriptural authority behind each, and the treatise's own point-by-point refutation — continuing the Three Grounds of Applied and Sustained Thought's exhaustive survey of the three realms.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 6 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. Unsound Reasoning Attention Opens: Sixteen Doctrines Named

Continuing directly from fascicle 5's close, the treatise opens its fourth great lens on the three grounds: unsound reasoning attention, defined here not abstractly but through a roster of sixteen specific non-Buddhist doctrines actually current in the Buddha's own India — the treatise's most sustained engagement anywhere with rival philosophical and religious systems.

Chinese Source

復次，云何不如理作意施設建立？嗢柁南曰：執因中有果，顯了有去來，我常宿作因，自在等害法，邊無邊矯亂，計無因斷空，最勝淨吉祥，由十六異論。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, how is the establishment of unsound reasoning attention to be understood? A summary verse states: holding that effect exists within cause, manifestation, past and future truly existing, self, permanence, prior deeds as cause, sovereign lordship and the rest, harming as the true Dharma, having or lacking limits, evasive equivocation, positing no cause, annihilation, and emptiness, supremacy, purity, and auspiciousness — through sixteen divergent doctrines.

由十六種異論差別，顯不如理作意應知。何等十六？一、因中有果論，二、從緣顯了論，三、去來實有論，四、計我論，五、計常論，六、宿作因論，七、計自在等為作者論，八、害為正法論，九、有邊無邊論，十、不死矯亂論，十一、無因見論，十二、斷見論，十三、空見論，十四、妄計最勝論，十五、妄計清淨論，十六、妄計吉祥論。

It should be understood that unsound reasoning attention is made manifest through these sixteen distinct divergent doctrines. What are the sixteen? First, the doctrine that effect exists within cause; second, the doctrine of manifestation through condition; third, the doctrine that past and future truly exist; fourth, the doctrine positing a self; fifth, the doctrine positing permanence; sixth, the doctrine of prior deeds as cause; seventh, the doctrine positing sovereign lordship and the like as creator; eighth, the doctrine that harming is the true Dharma; ninth, the doctrine of having or lacking limits; tenth, the doctrine of evasive equivocation about the deathless; eleventh, the doctrine positing no cause; twelfth, the doctrine of annihilation; thirteenth, the doctrine of emptiness; fourteenth, the doctrine of false supremacy; fifteenth, the doctrine of false purity; sixteenth, the doctrine of false auspiciousness.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi previews the treatise's method for all sixteen: each will be named, its specific school or teacher identified, its scriptural or reasoned basis given fairly and in some detail, and only then refuted — a far more rigorous and historically grounded engagement with rival views than a mere list of errors. He notes that classical India's roughly ninety-five or ninety-six non-Buddhist schools, and the sixty-two speculative views catalogued in sources like the Brahmajāla Sūtra, all reduce in substance to these sixteen basic positions, making this fascicle's survey a genuine comparative-religion map of the philosophical landscape the Buddha's teaching arose within and against.

He flags the danger these doctrines pose precisely because they are not obviously foolish: many rest on real logical reasoning or real meditative experience, and without the Buddha's own insight to check them against, can seem entirely coherent on their own terms — which is exactly why unsound reasoning attention, rather than mere ignorance, is the treatise's chosen name for what is wrong with them. He previews the first, the doctrine that effect exists within cause, as belonging to the Sāṃkhya school's Vārṣagaṇya branch — the view that a sprout's rice-nature must already exist latent within its seed, since rice only ever grows from rice-seed and never from wheat, extended into an entire cosmology in which everything unfolds already-present from a single eternal, unchanging root-cause.

2. The First Doctrine in Full: Why the Vārṣagaṇya School Held That Effect Exists Within Cause

True to its promised method, the treatise first states the doctrine that effect pre-exists within cause fully and fairly, tracing it to a specific teacher and school, then gives the four reasoned arguments its holders actually offered — before beginning, in the next section, to dismantle it.

Chinese Source

因中有果論者，謂如有一，若沙門、若婆羅門，起如是見，立如是論：常常時、恆恆時，於諸因中具有果性。謂雨眾外道作如是計。

Ru-Yi Translation

The doctrine that effect exists within cause: there are certain renunciants or brahmins who hold such a view and establish such a doctrine — that always, at every time without exception, effect exists as a property already within its cause. It is the Vārṣagaṇya school of non-Buddhist teachers that holds this position.

彼作是思：若從彼性此性得生，一切世間共知、共立彼為此因，非餘。又求果者，唯取此因，非餘。又即於彼，加功營構諸所求事，非餘。又若彼果，即從彼生，不從餘生。是故彼果因中已有。

They reason thus: if this effect-nature arises from that cause-nature, the whole world alike recognizes and establishes that cause-nature, and no other, as the cause of this effect. Further, one who seeks a given effect takes up only this cause, and no other. Further, it is on that very cause that effort and labor are actually applied in pursuit of the desired outcome, and on no other. Further, that effect arises specifically from that cause, and from no other. Therefore, that effect must already exist within its cause.

若不爾者，應立一切是一切因，為求一果應取一切，應於一切加功營構，應從一切一切果生。如是由施設故，求取故，所作決定故，生故，彼見因中常有果性。

Were it otherwise, then anything at all should be the cause of anything at all; seeking a single effect should require taking up every possible cause; effort should have to be applied to everything without distinction; and any effect at all should arise from anything at all. It is thus through convention, through seeking, through the fixed determinacy of effort, and through arising itself, that they hold effect to exist perpetually within cause.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies these teachers as followers of Vārṣagaṇya, a leading disciple of the Sāṃkhya founder Kapila, and lays out their larger cosmology: an eternal, unchanging root-cause called prakṛti (self-nature) unfolds, in fixed sequence, into twenty-three further principles — intellect, ego-sense, the five subtle elements, the five gross elements, the five sense-faculties, the five action-faculties, and mind — all ultimately for the enjoyment of a twenty-fifth principle, puruṣa (the true self). Because rice sprouts only ever come from rice seed, never from wheat, they reasoned, rice-nature must already exist latent within the seed before it ever sprouts — the whole edifice of twenty-three unfolding principles is simply this same logic scaled up to the whole of reality. Liberation, on their view, comes only when this self, wearied by prakṛti's ceaseless, agitating transformations, achieves such profound revulsion toward them that prakṛti itself withdraws and stops producing the twenty-three principles at all.

He walks through their four supporting arguments as genuinely reasoned, not merely dogmatic: the whole world already agrees that rice-seed, and nothing else, is what people call the "cause" of rice; anyone actually wanting rice seeks out rice-seed specifically, not any other seed; the labor of cultivation is applied specifically to that seed, not scattered indiscriminately across every possible seed; and the effect specifically and only ever arises from that particular cause. Reversed, they argued, the absurdity is obvious — if effect were not already latent in its proper cause, anything could produce anything, seeking any single result would require gathering literally everything, effort would have to be spread everywhere at once, and every effect would spring from every cause indiscriminately, none of which matches how the world actually and reliably works. It is this reasoning, resting on real and carefully observed regularities, that the treatise takes seriously enough to refute point by point rather than simply dismiss.

3. The Refutation: A Cause Only Ever Needs a Condition to Produce Its Effect

The treatise now turns to demolish the doctrine with a classical dilemma: are cause and effect the same thing, or different things? Either answer, pressed carefully, collapses.

Chinese Source

應審問彼：汝何所欲？何者因相？何者果相？因果兩相為異不異？若無異相，便無因果二種決定。因果二種無差別故，因中有果，不應道理。

Ru-Yi Translation

One should press them with this question: what, precisely, do you mean? What is the mark of a cause? What is the mark of an effect? Are these two marks, cause and effect, different from each other or not different? If they are not different, then there can be no fixed distinction between the two at all — cause and effect being simply identical, it makes no sense to speak of "effect existing within cause."

若有異相，汝意云何？因中果性為未生相？為已生相？若未生相，便於因中果猶未生，而說是有，不應道理。若已生相，即果體已生，復從因生，不應道理。是故因中非先有果；然要有因，待緣果生。

If they are different, then consider this: is the effect-nature within the cause already-arisen, or not-yet-arisen? If not-yet-arisen, then the effect has, within the cause, not yet come to be at all — to call it "existing" makes no sense. If already-arisen, then the effect's own substance has already come to be, and to then say it "arises again from the cause" equally makes no sense. Therefore, effect does not pre-exist within cause. Rather, there must be a cause, and, given a further condition, the effect then arises.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks through the dilemma as airtight on its own terms: press the Vārṣagaṇya position on whether cause and effect actually differ, and both horns fail. If they are not different — milk simply is yogurt, with no real distinction — then the very words "cause" and "effect" lose all meaning, since nothing is actually being produced from anything. If they are different, a second question immediately follows: within the cause, does that different effect-nature already exist in fully arisen form, or not yet? If not yet arisen, then it plainly does not yet exist, so calling it "already present" is simply false; if already arisen, then it is already a complete, existing thing in its own right, making the claim that it then "arises from" the cause equally incoherent — an already-existing thing cannot meaningfully arise a second time.

He reads the treatise's own positive conclusion as the genuinely Middle Way position, avoiding both extremes at once: effect is neither strictly identical to its cause nor strictly separate from it — milk and yogurt are related, in the sense that only milk (never water) can become yogurt, yet also distinct, since yogurt clearly has qualities milk lacks — and this relation is possible only because a cause, on its own, is never sufficient; it must always still wait upon a further condition (the yogurt-culture, the fermentation, the right length of time) before any effect actually appears. The Vārṣagaṇya error, in Cháng Bǎi's reading, was mistaking a real and rigid correlation between a specific cause and its specific effect for the effect's own hidden prior existence, when what that correlation actually shows is only that a cause reliably needs the right condition, never any condition whatsoever, to produce its own proper result.

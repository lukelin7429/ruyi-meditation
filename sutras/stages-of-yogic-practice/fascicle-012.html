Fascicle 12 · 瑜伽師地論卷第十二 · The Ground of Meditative Equipoise Continues

Cultivating the Marks, and the Four Powers That Open Absorption

Fascicle 11 ended mid-topic, deep inside the Ground of Meditative Equipoise (三摩呬多地). Fascicle 12 picks up exactly there: how one actually cultivates attention on the object's marks and what that accomplishes; the four powers by which a mind enters absorption at all; and the four flawed ways of attaining dhyāna — relishing it, misreading it as a permanent self, growing conceited over it, or doubting the path beyond it.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 12 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. Cultivating Attention on the Marks — and the Four Things It Accomplishes

Fascicle 12 opens by joining the two analyses fascicle 11 finished — the seven and forty attentions, and the four and thirty-two object-marks — into a single practice: cultivating attention on the object's various marks (修習所緣諸相作意). Repeatedly turning sound attention onto those marks, the treatise says, accomplishes four things at once.

Chinese Source

復次，云何修習所緣諸相作意？謂即於彼彼諸相作意思惟。以思惟故，能作四事：謂即修習如是作意；又能遠彼所治煩惱；又能練此作意及餘，令後所生轉更明盛；又即修習此作意時，厭壞所緣，捨諸煩惱，任持斷滅，令諸煩惱遠離相續。是故修習如是所緣諸相作意。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, what is cultivating attention on the object's various marks? It is attending to and contemplating each of those marks. Because of this contemplation, four things are accomplished: one cultivates such attention itself; one distances the affliction to be countered; one refines this attention and its companions, so that what arises later grows ever brighter and stronger; and, while cultivating this attention, one wearies of and breaks down the object, casts off the afflictions, holds firm to their severance, and makes the afflictions withdraw from the continuum. Therefore one should cultivate such attention on the object's various marks.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains the four accomplishments as a working sequence. First, repeatedly cultivating the attention itself lets one come to know the object as it truly is — its self-mark, common mark, suchness, and exhaustive extent. Second, sustained sound contemplation distances the very affliction it targets — the four obstacles of dullness, agitation, scatter, and attachment named in fascicle 11, and, more broadly, any affliction at all: contemplate desire as impure, suffering, impermanent, and selfless, and craving is pushed to a distance; but only if one does this again and again, for a single reflection is soon forgotten and cannot summon right mindfulness when a situation demands it. Third, the practice "refines" both the attention (a mental factor) and its accompanying mind — he likens it to smelting gold, burning off the dross so that each successive moment of mind arises brighter and stronger.

The fourth accomplishment he unfolds as the full path in miniature, exactly as with the "counteracting attention" of fascicle 11: wearying of and breaking down the object (厭壞所緣) is the preparatory path, loosening a desire-realm being's attachment to sights, sounds, and the rest through the impurity contemplation; casting off the afflictions is the uninterrupted path that, resting on the threshold concentration, severs their manifestation and even their seed; holding firm to their severance is the liberation path, where the lower ground's afflictions are gone and one attains the first dhyāna or the first fruit; and making the afflictions withdraw from the continuum is the path of distinction, pressing on to cut the subtler afflictions still remaining, layer by layer. This is why, he stresses, the whole apparatus of cultivating attention on the marks matters: it is the machinery of actual liberation, not mere technique.

2. The Four Powers That Open Absorption, and the Four Kinds of Contemplative

What actually lets a mind enter absorption — anything from the first dhyāna up to the peak of existence? The treatise names four powers: the power of a prior cause, of present method, of teaching heard, and of a teacher's instruction — and shows that these define four distinct types of practitioner.

Chinese Source

復次，由四因緣，入初靜慮乃至有頂，謂因力、方便力、說力、教授力。云何因力？謂曾鄰近入靜慮等。云何方便力？謂雖不鄰近入靜慮等，然由數習無間修力，能入諸定。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, by four conditions one enters the first dhyāna and so on up to the peak of existence: the power of the cause, the power of method, the power of teaching, and the power of instruction. What is the power of the cause? Having, in the past, drawn near to entering the dhyānas and the rest. What is the power of method? Though one has not [before] drawn near to entering the dhyānas, yet by the force of repeated, unbroken cultivation one is able to enter the concentrations.

Chinese Source

云何說力？謂於靜慮等增上緣法，多聞任持，乃至廣說。即依此法，獨處空閑，離諸放逸，勇猛精進，自策而勵，住法隨法行，由此能入靜慮等定。云何教授力？謂於親教軌範師所，或於隨一餘尊長所，獲得隨順初靜慮等無倒教授；從此審諦作意思惟，能入靜慮及諸餘定。如是顯示四觀行者：謂具因力者、方便力者、若利根者，及鈍根者。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the power of teaching? Having widely heard and retained the teachings that are the dominant condition for the dhyānas and the rest, and so on at length; then, relying on that teaching, dwelling alone in a quiet place, free of heedlessness, valiantly diligent, spurring and rousing oneself, abiding in the practice of the Dharma in accord with the Dharma — by this one is able to enter the dhyānas. What is the power of instruction? Receiving, from one's preceptor or master, or from some other elder, undistorted instruction in accord with the first dhyāna and the rest; then, closely attending to and contemplating it, one is able to enter the dhyānas and the other concentrations. Thus are displayed four contemplatives: one endowed with the power of the cause, one with the power of method, one of sharp faculties, and one of dull faculties.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the four powers as two pairs: nature and effort, then self-reliance and reliance on another. The power of the cause is the force of seeds — one who cultivated absorption in a past life (or earlier this life) finds the entry-signs of a dhyāna arising as he approaches, and knows he will soon enter; he recalls a pilgrim-monk who mastered in three years what normally takes ten, simply because his prior cultivation was so strong. The power of method is present effort: even without that stock of seeds, unbroken and repeated cultivation can open the concentrations — where the first power is innate endowment, this is acquired diligence.

The power of teaching and the power of instruction he ties to the two classical faculty-types. One who enters by the power of teaching is the follower-by-Dharma (隨法行), of sharp faculties: having widely heard and firmly retained the meditation teachings — knowing, when he sits, exactly how to practice breath-counting or the impurity contemplation and what states will arise — he then withdraws to solitude, sheds heedlessness, drives himself valiantly, and practices the Dharma in accord with the Dharma (the "Dharma" being the goal, nirvāṇa; the "in accord with" being the eightfold path). One who enters by the power of instruction is the follower-by-faith (隨信行), typically of duller faculties but strong confidence: he receives undistorted instruction from a genuine teacher — the impurity contemplation, or that all formations are impermanent, all contaminated things suffering, all dharmas selfless — and, because the guidance accords with the scriptures, he can trust it and enter. Cháng Bǎi adds the practical caution that even the follower-by-faith should read the treatises himself, so as to verify that a teacher's instruction truly matches what the scriptures say.

3. Two Flawed Attainers: The One Who Relishes Dhyāna, and the One Who Reads It as a Permanent Self

The treatise now warns that attaining absorption is not itself safety: there are four kinds of dhyāna-attainer marred by affliction — by craving, by wrong view, by conceit, and by doubt. The first two are drawn here: the practitioner who, told only of dhyāna's merits, falls to relishing it, and the one who, taught eternalism, uses dhyāna and past-life memory to lock in the view that self and world are permanent.

Chinese Source

復次，有四得靜慮者：一、愛上靜慮者，二、見上靜慮者，三、慢上靜慮者，四、疑上靜慮者。云何愛上靜慮者？謂如有一，先聞靜慮諸定功德，而不聞彼出離方便。於彼一向見勝功德，勇猛精勤；由此因緣，入初靜慮或所餘定。如是入已，後生愛味。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, there are four attainers of dhyāna: one who relishes dhyāna, one who [rests] on a view above dhyāna, one who [rests] on conceit above dhyāna, and one who [rests] on doubt above dhyāna. What is the one who relishes dhyāna? Someone who has heard of the merits of the dhyānas and concentrations but has not heard of the means of release from them; seeing in them only surpassing merit, he is valiantly diligent, and by this enters the first dhyāna or another concentration. Having entered thus, he afterward gives rise to a relish for it.

Chinese Source

云何見上靜慮者？謂如有一，從自師所，或餘師所，聞諸世間皆是常等，如是方便入初靜慮乃至有頂，能得清淨、解脫、出離。彼依此見，勇猛精勤；由是因緣，入初靜慮或所餘定。如是入已，能自憶念過去多劫，遂生是見：我及世間皆是常等。從定起已，即於此見堅執不捨。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the one who rests on a view above dhyāna? Someone who, from his own teacher or another, has heard that the world and the rest are all permanent, and [believes] that by such means, entering the first dhyāna up to the peak of existence, one can attain purity, liberation, and release. Relying on this view he is valiantly diligent, and by this enters the first dhyāna or another concentration. Having entered thus, he can recollect his own past across many eons, and so gives rise to the view: "self and world are all permanent." Having emerged from the concentration, he clings to this view and will not let it go.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi frames all four attainers as cautionary: absorption reached without the Dharma's own corrective becomes a trap. The relisher has heard only that the dhyānas bring joy, sublime bliss, stillness, and imperturbability — never that the contaminated aggregates they rest on are still "like a sickness, like a boil, like an arrow," still impermanent, suffering, and selfless, and that one must seek release from them too. So having driven himself into absorption, he emerges relishing its flavor, leaving genuine "pure concentration" for a "concentration of relish," his mellowed temper fooling him into thinking his afflictions are gone. Cháng Bǎi repeats Miào Jìng's constant warning: never practice concentration apart from the scriptures, or you will mistake the first dhyāna for the first noble fruit — the very "conceit of overestimation" the whole ground keeps guarding against.

The view-attainer is worse off, because his absorption actively serves a wrong view. Taught by a non-Buddhist teacher that self and world are permanent — the eternalism the treatise refuted at length back in fascicle 6's sixteen-doctrine survey — he uses the recollection of past lives that absorption unlocks to "confirm" it: "I existed life after life, my mind flowing on unbroken, so I and the world must be permanent." Having only the concentration and no insight into dependent origination, he emerges gripping the view all the harder, and mistakes what he calls "purity, liberation, and release" — really just the meditative suppression of desire — for genuine escape from saṃsāra. Cháng Bǎi notes the decisive contrast: one who has truly seen the truths needs no such anxious re-examination afterward, because direct realization, unlike a meditative inference, does not have to keep reassuring itself.

4. Two More Flawed Attainers: Conceit and Doubt

The remaining two: the practitioner who enters dhyāna out of conceit — "if that elder can do it, why not I?" — and then decides only he can attain it; and the dull practitioner who reaches worldly absorption but, unable to break through to the four truths, falls to doubting whether any noble realization exists at all.

Chinese Source

云何慢上靜慮者？謂如有一，聞如是名諸長老等入初靜慮乃至有頂。聞是事已，遂生憍慢：彼既能入靜慮等定，我復何緣而不當入。依止此慢，勇猛精勤；由是因緣，入初靜慮及所餘定。如是入已，後生憍慢；或入定已，作是思惟：唯我能得如是靜慮，餘不能得。彼依此慢，復於後時，於諸靜慮審思、審慮、審諦觀察。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the one who rests on conceit above dhyāna? Someone who hears that such-and-such elders and others have entered the first dhyāna up to the peak of existence, and, hearing this, gives rise to conceit: "if they can enter the dhyānas, why should I not?" Relying on this conceit he is valiantly diligent, and thereby enters the first dhyāna or another concentration. Having entered, he afterward gives rise to conceit; or, having entered, he thinks: "Only I can attain such dhyāna; others cannot." Relying on this conceit, he later, in the dhyānas, closely thinks, considers, and scrutinizes.

Chinese Source

云何疑上靜慮者？謂如有一，為性闇鈍，本嘗樂習奢摩他行；由此因緣，入諸靜慮或所餘定。如是入已，復於上定勤修方便，為得未得，於四聖諦勤修現觀；性闇鈍故，不能速證聖諦現觀。由此因緣，於餘所證便生疑惑；依此疑惑，復於勝進審思、審慮、審諦觀察。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the one who rests on doubt above dhyāna? Someone dull by nature who has long delighted in cultivating calm, and thereby enters the dhyānas or another concentration. Having entered, he diligently cultivates the means toward the higher concentrations and toward what is not yet attained; he diligently cultivates the direct realization of the four noble truths, but being dull by nature cannot quickly realize it. By this, doubt arises in him regarding what others have realized; relying on this doubt, he again, in advancing, closely thinks, considers, and scrutinizes.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes that the conceit-attainer's absorption is genuine but stained: because conceit rides along with it, the concentration is never "perfected, pure, and bright" — pure meaning free of affliction, bright meaning continuously so — which is exactly why he must keep re-examining it afterward, uneasy beneath the pride. His conceit takes two forms, ranking himself equal to those who have attained ("why not I?") and then superior to those who have not ("only I can do this").

The doubt-attainer, he explains, is a dull but karmically unobstructed type with a real past-life fondness for calm, so he reaches worldly absorption readily; but his slowness with dependent origination keeps him from breaking through to direct realization of the four truths, and, seeing others apparently attain what he cannot, he slides into doubt — doubting whether there is any noble path to be reached at all — and so keeps anxiously scrutinizing as he tries to advance. Cháng Bǎi's throughline for all four flawed attainers is the same: absorption reached without genuine insight is unstable and forever re-checking itself, whereas one who has actually seen the truths, having directly realized reality, needs no such reassurance.

5. Three Kinds of Concentration (Part 1): Relish-Associated, and Pure

The treatise now sorts absorption itself into three: concentration associated with relish, pure concentration, and uncontaminated concentration. The first belongs to the dull or heavily-afflicted who cling to the flavor of what they attain; the second, to the middling or sharp who have heard of both its merits and its faults and so do not cling.

Chinese Source

復次，云何愛味相應靜慮等定？謂有鈍根，或貪行故，或煩惱多故，彼唯得聞初靜慮等所有功德，廣說如前愛上靜慮。於上出離不了知故，便生愛味，戀著堅住。其所愛味，當言已出；其能愛味，當言正入。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, what is dhyāna-and-concentration associated with relish? [The practice of] one with dull faculties, or of a greedy disposition, or with many afflictions, who has heard only the merits of the first dhyāna and the rest, as explained above for the relisher of dhyāna. Not understanding release to the higher states, he gives rise to relish, clinging and firmly settling. What is relished should be called "already left"; the relishing should be called "just entered."

Chinese Source

云何清淨靜慮等定？謂有中根，或利根性，等煩惱行，或薄塵行。彼從他聞初靜慮等愛味過患及上出離，勇猛精進，入初靜慮或所餘定。如是入已，便能思惟諸定過患，於上出離亦能了知，不生愛味。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is pure dhyāna-and-concentration? [The practice of] one with middling or sharp faculties, of an even-affliction or a thin-defilement disposition, who has heard from another of the relish, the faults, and the higher release regarding the first dhyāna and the rest; valiantly diligent, he enters the first dhyāna or another concentration. Having entered, he can contemplate the faults of the concentrations and knows the release to the higher states, and gives rise to no relish.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi frames the three concentrations — relish-associated, pure, and uncontaminated — as the classical triad also found in the Abhidharmakośa. The first is the trap of the dull, greedy, or heavily-afflicted who have heard only that absorption is pleasant: not knowing how to press on to the higher release, they settle in and cling to the flavor. He draws out the treatise's precise phrasing — "what is relished should be called 'already left,' the relishing 'just entered'" — to make a sharp point: relish never actually occurs within pure concentration; it arises only once one has come out of the pure concentration and slipped into a "concentration of relish." So the pure absorption is the thing relished (now left behind), and the relishing is the tainted state just entered. He notes relish comes in three modes — savoring a past attainment in memory, craving a future one, and clinging in the present.

Pure concentration belongs instead to those of middling or sharp faculties whose temperament is "even-affliction" (every affliction present but all mild, from a past life of little indulgence) or "thin-defilement" (afflictions light, and their faults already clearly seen). Because such a practitioner has heard not only of absorption's merits but of its relish-fault and of the release to be won beyond it, he enters without clinging: once in, he can still contemplate the concentration's own faults and see the higher release, so no relish takes hold. Cháng Bǎi stresses that even a genuine root-absorption is not yet "perfected, pure, and bright" so long as relish, conceit, view, doubt, or ignorance can still stain it — which is why seeing the faults, not just the merits, is what keeps a concentration pure.

6. Three Kinds of Concentration (Part 2): Uncontaminated — Entering Without Deliberating the Marks

The third and highest is uncontaminated concentration, belonging to a noble follower-by-faith or follower-by-Dharma who has realized, or is realizing, the four truths. Its distinguishing sign: he no longer needs to deliberately raise the marks of practice, state, and object by which a beginner labors into absorption — he simply enters.

Chinese Source

云何無漏靜慮等定？謂如有一，是隨信行，或隨法行，薄塵行類。彼或先時於四聖諦已入現觀，或復正修現觀方便。彼先所由諸行狀相，入初靜慮或所餘定，今於此行、此狀、此相不復思惟。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is uncontaminated dhyāna-and-concentration? Someone who is a follower-by-faith or a follower-by-Dharma, of the thin-defilement type, who has either already entered the direct realization of the four noble truths, or is now cultivating the means toward it. The marks of practice, of state, and of object by which he formerly entered the first dhyāna or another concentration — these marks of practice, state, and object he now no longer needs to contemplate.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies uncontaminated concentration as the possession of the noble follower-by-faith or follower-by-Dharma of thin-defilement temperament — either one who has already realized the four truths (and is thus a noble one) or one now on the direct-realization path. Its mark is a hard-won ease: the beginner must deliberately raise three things to enter absorption, but the adept no longer needs to think them at all. He glosses the three: practice (行) is the coarse-versus-calm contemplation — seeing the lower ground as coarse and yearning toward the higher as calm, the six-aspect method that lifts one from ground to ground; state (狀) is the cluster of entry-signs that arise as the elements shift at the threshold of absorption, by which both the meditator and his teacher can tell which concentration he is about to enter; and object (相) is the two marks already given — the object-mark (the image the discriminating mind projects) and the causal mark (the concentration-provisions of precepts, sense-restraint, moderate eating, and clear comprehension).

On the "state" or entry-signs, Cháng Bǎi points to Zhiyi's account of the first dhyāna's arising — where, out of the threshold concentration's deep stillness, the body settles into a motionless "congealed" quiet and then a subtle movement rises (from the crown, the feet, or the waist), attended by a cluster of wholesome factors: one-pointed concentration, a sense of spacious emptiness, luminous purity, joy, bodily bliss, a wholesome mind, clear knowing, and an unburdened release. He keeps only the theme rather than reproducing the passage, consistent with the discipline of synthesizing secondary sources; the point for the treatise is simply that the practiced adept, having entered so often, no longer needs to summon any of these marks deliberately — the uncontaminated concentration opens of itself.

7. How an Absorption Is Made Uncontaminated: Insight Layered onto Stillness

The treatise completes the uncontaminated concentration by naming what actually turns an ordinary absorption uncontaminated: not the stillness alone, but the insight laid on top of it — seeing even the refined aggregates as disease and boil, dreading the conditioned, and fixing the mind on the deathless.

Chinese Source

然於諸色乃至識法思惟如病、如癰等行；於有為法心生厭惡，怖畏制伏；於甘露界繫念思惟；如是方能入無漏定。

Ru-Yi Translation

Yet he contemplates form up through mind-objects as a disease, as a boil, and so on; toward the conditioned his mind gives rise to revulsion, dread, and subdual; and he fixes his mindfulness upon the deathless realm — only thus is he able to enter the uncontaminated concentration.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi stresses the decisive point: an absorption becomes uncontaminated only when insight is layered onto the calm. The practitioner turns on his own five aggregates — form, feeling, perception, formations, consciousness, including the very refined aggregates of the absorption he is in — and contemplates them as a sickness that constantly aches, a boil oozing the pus of affliction, an arrow, a barb, a killer, impermanent, suffering, empty, and selfless; toward everything conditioned his mind generates revulsion, dread, and active subdual of its own clinging. He notes the treatise's "and so on" (如病如癰等) reaches through the full range of what an out-of-the-world absorption contemplates — impermanence, suffering, emptiness, non-self, the four truths, suchness, dharma-nature, and the real limit (citing the Xianyang Shengjiao Lun).

Finally, he fixes his mindfulness on the "deathless realm" (甘露界, amṛta) — nirvāṇa itself, the truth of cessation, the peace of all phenomena's stilling — which Cháng Bǎi glosses (with the Ground of the Nirvāṇa-without-Remainder) as another name for the nirvāṇa-without-remainder into which the śrāvaka enters on laying down the aggregates. His practical warning is exact: merely counting the breath without seeing the breath as impermanent, suffering, empty, and selfless, or contemplating impurity without pressing on to the same, can never accord with an uncontaminated concentration. The calm is the base; the insight into the aggregates' faults and the mind fixed on cessation is what makes it uncontaminated.

8. Four Trajectories of Concentration: Falling Back, Staying Put, Advancing, and Penetrating

The treatise sorts meditators by where their absorption tends: the retrogression-prone who cannot bear its sublimity and fall back; the stability-prone who relish it and neither rise nor fall; the advancement-prone who, knowing the release beyond it, press higher; and the penetration-prone who, seeing the faults of self-embodiment, enter the uncontaminated — the final, decisive share.

Chinese Source

復次，云何順退分定？謂有鈍根，下劣欲解，勤精進故，入初靜慮或所餘定，於喜、於樂、於勝功德不堪忍故，從靜慮退。如如暫入諸定差別，如是如是還復退失，乃至未善調練諸根。云何順住分定？謂有中根，或利根性，彼唯得聞諸定功德，廣說如前愛味相應。於所得定唯生愛味，不能上進，亦不退下。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, what is retrogression-prone concentration? [The practice of] one with dull faculties and inferior aspiration who, being diligent, enters the first dhyāna or another concentration, but — unable to bear its joy, its bliss, or its supreme merit — falls back from the dhyāna. Just as he momentarily enters the various concentrations, so does he fall from them again, so long as he has not well tempered his faculties. What is stability-prone concentration? [The practice of] one with middling or sharp faculties who has heard only the merits of the concentrations, as above for the relish-associated; toward the concentration attained he gives rise only to relish, unable to advance yet not falling back.

Chinese Source

云何順勝分定？謂有亦聞出離方便，於所得定不生喜足，是故於彼不生愛味，更求勝位；由此因緣，便得勝進。云何順決擇分定？謂於一切薩迦耶中，深見過患；由此因緣，能入無漏。又諸無漏名決擇分，極究竟故。猶如世間珠瓶等物，已善簡者名為決擇，自此已後無可擇故；此亦如是，過此更無可簡擇故，名決擇分。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is advancement-prone concentration? [The practice of] one who has also heard the means of release and gives rise to no contentment with the concentration attained; therefore he gives rise to no relish for it but seeks a higher station, and thereby gains advancement. What is penetration-prone concentration? Deeply seeing the faults in all self-embodiment, and by this able to enter the uncontaminated. Moreover, all the uncontaminated is called "penetration," being utterly final — just as, among worldly gems and vessels, what has been well sorted is called "decided," since after that there is nothing more to sort; so too here, since beyond this there is nothing more to discriminate, it is called "penetration."

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps the four "shares" (順退／住／勝／決擇分) onto four trajectories. The retrogression-prone practitioner has dull faculties and only a feeble aspiration; he is diligent enough to enter absorption, but cannot bear its sublimity — joy (the first two dhyānas), bliss (the first three), and the supreme merit of the fourth's imperturbable equanimity — and so keeps slipping back out. Miào Jìng's image: a poor man who suddenly wins a fortune, so unaccustomed to wealth that he cannot rest until he has spent it all back to normal. What such a one lacks is the patience to "temper the faculties" (調練諸根 / 練根) — the sustained work, drawing on the five motives named in the Viniścaya, that turns dull faculties into middling and sharp — so he enters and falls, enters and falls. The stability-prone practitioner has middling or sharp faculties but has heard only of absorption's merits, so he relishes what he attains and simply stays there: his diligence keeps him from falling, but ignorance of the release keeps him from rising.

The advancement-prone practitioner has the same faculties but has also heard the means of release; never content — not with the first dhyāna, not even with the fourth — he relishes nothing and keeps seeking a higher station, and so genuinely advances. The penetration-prone is the decisive one: "penetration" (決擇) is the decisive discernment of insight (vipaśyanā), and by deeply seeing the faults of all "self-embodiment" (薩迦耶 — the innate craving-for-a-self that, Cháng Bǎi notes, pervades all three realms and is the root support of every affliction: "where there is defiled desire in one place, there is defiled desire everywhere") such a one enters the uncontaminated. He draws out the treatise's own gem-sorting simile: as a jeweler who has picked out every finest stone has nothing left to sort, so non-self insight is the final discrimination — past it there is nothing more to examine, no more anxious re-checking, which is exactly why the uncontaminated, reached through it, is called "penetration."

9. Entering the Attainments Without Interval, and by Leaping

Two ways of moving through the eight attainments. One runs the whole ladder unbroken, forward and back; the other, once mastery is won, leaps a level at a time — but no more than one level, except for a Buddha or an advanced bodhisattva, who enter any concentration at will.

Chinese Source

復次，云何無間入諸等至？謂如有一，得初靜慮乃至有頂，然未圓滿清淨鮮白。先順次入乃至有頂，後逆次入至初靜慮。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, what is entering the attainments without interval? Someone who has attained the first dhyāna up to the peak of existence, but not yet perfectly, purely, and brightly. He first enters in forward sequence up to the peak of existence, and afterward in reverse sequence down to the first dhyāna.

Chinese Source

復次，云何超越入諸等至？謂即於此已得圓滿清白故，從初靜慮無間超入第三靜慮，第三無間超入空無邊處，空處無間超入無所有處。乃至廣說逆超亦爾。以極遠故，無有能超第三等至。唯除如來，及出第二阿僧企耶諸大菩薩，彼隨所欲入諸定故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, what is entering the attainments by leaping? Because he has now attained the perfect and pure [mastery], from the first dhyāna he leaps without interval into the third dhyāna, from the third leaps into the sphere of infinite space, and from the space-sphere leaps into the sphere of nothingness; and, as explained at length, reverse-leaping is likewise. Because [the further stations] are too remote, none can leap into a third attainment [two levels off] — except a Tathāgata and the great bodhisattvas who have passed the second incalculable eon, for they enter the concentrations however they wish.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi ties the two to a difference in purity. Unbroken entry belongs to one who possesses all eight attainments but is "not yet perfectly pure and bright" — his mind still stained by the relish, conceit, view, doubt, and ignorance the dhyānas breed; to strengthen his command he runs the whole ladder without a gap, forward from the first dhyāna up to the peak of existence, then in reverse all the way back down. Leaping entry belongs to one who has cleared those stains and reached sovereignty over the attainments (三摩地自在): he can skip exactly one station — first to third, third to the sphere of infinite space, that sphere to the sphere of nothingness, and the same in reverse — but never two at once, because the object-signs of stations two apart are simply too far to bridge.

He notes the single exception: a Tathāgata, and a bodhisattva who has passed the second of the three incalculable eons (the eighth ground and above), can enter any concentration whatever at will, with no restriction of sequence or distance — a single act of attention carrying them from the first dhyāna straight to the peak of existence. Cháng Bǎi reads this as a window onto the mind's boundless latent capacity: what looks impossible is only untrained; a mind cultivated across those eons moves instantly where an ordinary practitioner is stalled for a lifetime in scattered states.

10. Permeation-Cultivation: Interleaving the Contaminated and the Uncontaminated for Mastery

To make an absorption stable and fruitful, a practitioner "permeates" it — deliberately interleaving contaminated and uncontaminated dhyāna until the two can follow each other without a gap. This wins mastery over the attainments and its four fruits, culminating, for one who dies with a residue of grasping, in rebirth among the Pure Abodes.

Chinese Source

復次，云何熏修靜慮？謂如有一，已得有漏及與無漏四種靜慮，為於等至得自在故，為受等至自在果故，長時相續入諸靜慮，有漏、無漏更相間雜。乃至有漏無間無漏現前，無漏無間還入有漏，當知齊此熏修成就。若於是處、是時、是事欲入諸定，即於此處、此時、此事能入諸定，是名於諸等至獲得自在。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, what is permeation-cultivation of dhyāna? Someone who has attained both the contaminated and the uncontaminated four dhyānas, in order to gain mastery over the attainments and to receive the fruit of that mastery, enters the dhyānas continuously over a long time, the contaminated and the uncontaminated alternating with each other — up to: the contaminated immediately followed by the uncontaminated made present, the uncontaminated immediately re-entering the contaminated. Know that at just this point the permeation-cultivation is accomplished. If, at whatever place, time, and object he wishes to enter the concentrations, he can there and then enter them, this is called gaining mastery over the attainments.

Chinese Source

等至自在果者，謂於現法樂住轉更明淨。又由此故，得不退道。又淨修治解脫、勝處及遍處等勝品功德能引之道。若有餘取而命終者，由此因緣，便入淨居。

Ru-Yi Translation

The fruit of mastery over the attainments is this: the pleasant abiding in this very life grows ever brighter and purer. By this too he gains the non-retrogressing path. And he purifies the path that can elicit the supreme qualities — the [eight] liberations, the [eight] masteries, the [ten] totalities, and the rest. And if he dies with a remaining grasping, by this cause he enters the Pure Abodes.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains permeation-cultivation (熏修) as deliberately interleaving the two kinds of absorption a practitioner already commands: the contaminated dhyāna cultivated by the coarse-versus-calm ascent, and the uncontaminated dhyāna cultivated by contemplating the four truths and non-self. One does a long stretch of the contaminated, then a short stretch of the uncontaminated — perhaps thirty minutes of the one, five of the other — gradually lengthening the uncontaminated and shortening the contaminated, until the two can follow each other with no gap at all. The purpose is to steady the absorption against the relish, conceit, view, and doubt that would otherwise erode it, so that it becomes "pure and bright" and no longer retrogresses. He notes that a threshold concentration is hard-won and easily lost, and even the dhyānas above it can be lost; permeation with the uncontaminated is what finally makes them secure.

Mastery (自在) is then the ability to enter any concentration at whatever place (each dhyāna-level with its grades), time (day or night), and object one wishes, with no difficulty or snag. Its four fruits Cháng Bǎi lines up: the present pleasant abiding grows steadily brighter and purer (Miào Jìng's candle-power image — a twenty-watt and a forty-watt bulb both give light, but the stronger gives a clearer, steadier one); the path becomes non-retrogressing; and it purifies the path that elicits the supreme qualities — the eight liberations (severing the afflictions of all nine grounds), the eight masteries (mastery over the visualization objects), the ten totalities, and beyond them the concentration of non-contention, the knowledge-of-vows, and the four analytic knowledges. The fourth fruit is rebirth: one who dies still carrying a "remaining grasping" — not desire-realm grasping, but a residual form-realm craving with innate self-grasping not yet fully cut — is reborn spontaneously in the Five Pure Abodes (無煩・無熱・善現・善見・色究竟), the fourth-dhyāna heights inhabited only by non-returners.

11. How Dhyāna-Cultivation Shapes Rebirth — and Why the Bliss Is the Same in Either Realm

The grade of one's dhyāna determines where one is reborn in the form heavens; but only pure dhyāna carries one there, relished dhyāna makes one fall, and the absorption's bliss itself is identical whether the body is of the desire realm or the form realm — only the karmic support-body differs.

Chinese Source

由軟中上品修諸靜慮，有差別故，於一切處受三地果。如前有尋有伺地已廣分別。修習無尋唯伺三摩地故，得為大梵。由軟、中、上、上勝、上極品熏修力故，生五淨居。

Ru-Yi Translation

By cultivating the dhyānas in soft, middling, and superior grades — because of this difference — one receives in each case the fruit of the three levels [of that dhyāna's heaven], as already analyzed at length in the Ground With Applied-and-Sustained Thought. By cultivating the concentration of only-sustained-thought, one becomes a Great Brahmā. And by the force of permeation-cultivation in the soft, middling, superior, super-superior, and utmost grades, one is reborn in the Five Pure Abodes.

Chinese Source

當知因修清淨靜慮定故，生靜慮地，不由習近愛味相應。既生彼已，若起愛味，即便退沒；若修清淨，還生於彼。或生下定，或進上定。先於此間修得定已，後往彼生。何以故？非未離欲得生彼故，非諸異生未修得定能離欲故。又非此間及在彼處入諸等至樂有差別，唯所依身而有差別。

Ru-Yi Translation

One should know that it is by cultivating pure dhyāna-concentration that one is reborn in the dhyāna grounds, not by resorting to relish-associated [concentration]. Having been reborn there, if one gives rise to relish one falls away; if one cultivates purity, one is reborn there again — either reborn in a lower concentration or advancing to a higher. One must first, here [in the desire realm], cultivate and attain the concentration, and afterward go to be reborn there. Why? Because one who has not left desire cannot be reborn there, and no ordinary being who has not cultivated and attained the concentration can leave desire. Moreover, the bliss of entering the attainments here or there is no different; the difference is only in the supporting body.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi lays out the rebirth-grades, referring back to fascicle 4's fuller account: each dhyāna cultivated in soft, middling, or superior grade yields rebirth in that dhyāna's three heavens (soft, middling, and superior first-dhyāna practice giving the Brahmā-multitude, Brahmā-ministers, and — via the "only-sustained-thought" intermediate concentration between the first and second dhyānas — the Great Brahmā himself; and likewise up through the fourth). The five finer grades of permeation-cultivated fourth dhyāna — soft, middling, superior, super-superior, and utmost — yield the Five Pure Abodes, which, being reserved for non-returners, ordinary beings never reach (they attain only the fourth dhyāna's Cloudless, Merit-Born, and Great-Fruit heavens).

He then draws out three points from the "clarification." First, rebirth in the form heavens comes only from pure dhyāna; a relished dhyāna makes one fall away, and only renewed pure cultivation restores the rebirth. Second, one must actually attain the dhyāna in this present desire-realm life to be reborn in its heaven — for no one reaches the form realm without leaving desire, and no ordinary being leaves desire without the dhyāna. Third, and strikingly, the bliss of the absorption itself is exactly the same whether experienced in a desire-realm body or a form-realm one; what differs is only the karmic support-body. The lower dhyāna is the "lower concentration," each higher one the "higher," and from a desire-realm body a practitioner's form-realm craving arises toward whichever level he has cultivated — carrying him there at death.

12. Gathering the Essentials of the Scriptures: The Eight Liberations

The Ground of Meditative Equipoise now opens its fourth and final great division — gathering the essentials of the scriptures (攝諸經宗要), drawing together the meditation teachings scattered through the Āgamas and Mahāyāna sūtras. It begins with the eight liberations, and fixes at once why they are called "liberation."

Chinese Source

復次，已說修習作意相差別。云何攝諸經宗要？謂八解脫等，如經廣說。八解脫者，謂如前說有色觀諸色等。前七解脫，於已解脫生勝解故，名為解脫；第八解脫，棄背想受，故名解脫。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, the cultivation of attention on the marks and their distinctions having been explained, what is the gathering of the essentials of the scriptures? It is the eight liberations and so on, as the scriptures explain at length. The eight liberations are, as stated before, "having form, one contemplates forms," and the rest. The first seven liberations are called "liberation" because one generates a firm conviction toward what is already liberated; the eighth liberation is called "liberation" because it casts away perception and feeling.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi frames this closing division as the treatise turning from its own systematic analysis to the meditation teachings as the sūtras present them — beginning with the eight liberations (八解脫, also called the eight releasings, 八背捨), the eight masteries (八勝處), and the ten totalities (十遍處), which together are the foundation for the superknowledges and the concentration of non-contention. The eight liberations are, in effect, the nine successive attainments: the first two contemplating form (to release craving for form), the third the "pure liberation directly realized in the body" (deliberately contemplating a beautiful object to prove mastery is genuine), the fourth through seventh the four formless spheres, and the eighth the cessation of perception and feeling.

On the naming he is precise. The first seven are called "liberation" not because one is only now freed — attaining any given level already means one has left the lower ground's affliction — but because one generates an intensified conviction (勝解) toward that already-won release, driving the severance deeper and making the attainment more sovereign and stable, so it will not slip back. The eighth is called "liberation" in a different sense: it casts away the very apparatus of perception and feeling, a near-nirvāṇa rest available only to non-returners and arhats. Cháng Bǎi notes in passing that the traditions differ on which dhyāna each liberation rests upon and exactly what is contemplated — the Kośa and this treatise diverge, especially on the second liberation — and treats these as school differences to be noted rather than adjudicated, the deeper lesson being that all of it is accomplished by sustained, deepening contemplation: the more clearly and repeatedly one contemplates an object, the more thoroughly its affliction is subdued.

13. The First Liberation: Contemplating Form With a Perception of Light

The first liberation is spelled out. Its practitioner has already left desire-realm craving, so — unlike the beginner's impurity contemplation — he contemplates desire-realm forms with a perception of light, not to kill a craving already gone, but to win sovereign command over form itself, the basis for the transformative powers.

Chinese Source

云何有色觀諸色？謂生欲界，已離欲界欲，未離色界欲。彼於如是所解脫中已得解脫，即於欲界諸色，以有光明相作意思惟，而生勝解。由二因緣，名為有色：謂生欲界故，得色界定故；又於有光明而作勝解故。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "having form, one contemplates forms"? One born in the desire realm who has left desire-realm desire but not form-realm desire: having already gained release within what is thus to be released, he attends to and contemplates desire-realm forms with a mark of luminosity, and generates a firm conviction. For two reasons it is called "having form": because he is born in the desire realm, and because he has attained a form-realm concentration; and also because he generates conviction toward luminosity.

Chinese Source

問：觀諸色者，觀何等色？復以何行？答：欲界諸色。於諸勝處所制少色，若好、若惡、若劣、若勝；如是於多，乃至廣說。何故修習如是觀行？為淨修治能引最勝功德方便。何等名為最勝功德？謂勝處、遍處、諸聖神通、無諍、願智、無礙解等。雖先於彼欲界諸色已得離欲，然於彼色未能證得勝解自在。為證得故，數數於彼思惟勝解。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: In contemplating forms, what forms does he contemplate, and by what practice? Answer: Desire-realm forms — the lesser forms controlled by the [eight] masteries, whether good or bad, inferior or superior; and likewise the many forms, and so on at length. Why cultivate such a contemplation? To purify the path that can elicit the supreme qualities. What are the supreme qualities? The [eight] masteries, the [ten] totalities, the noble superknowledges, non-contention, the knowledge-of-vows, the analytic knowledges, and the rest. Although he has already left desire for those desire-realm forms, he has not yet attained sovereign conviction over them; in order to attain it, he repeatedly contemplates them with conviction.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi flags the distinctive point of this treatise's first liberation. A beginner contemplates form as impure to break craving; but the practitioner of the first liberation has already left desire-realm craving on attaining his form-realm concentration, so he contemplates desire-realm forms instead with a perception of luminosity (光明想). The aim is no longer to kill a craving that is gone, but to remove the obstacle to transformation — to gain sovereign command (勝解自在) over form. "Having form," Cháng Bǎi explains, holds in two senses: he still has a body, being born in the desire realm, and he uses a form-realm concentration (both realms having material form), plus he generates his conviction specifically toward luminosity. He notes the traditions differ on the supporting concentration and the object — the Kośa has the first liberation rest on the first dhyāna and contemplate impurity, whereas this treatise (following Asaṅga) allows any of the four dhyānas and a luminosity perception — a divergence to note, not resolve.

The forms contemplated are sorted as the first four of the eight masteries sort them: the "lesser forms" (少色) are beings' own bodies and everyday possessions — a desk, a robe, a bowl — small in size; the "many forms" (多色) are large things like palaces and houses; each is taken as good or bad in its visible color, inferior or superior in its sound, scent, taste, and touch. Cháng Bǎi underlines the treatise's own statement of purpose: the whole point of this contemplation is to "purify the path that can elicit the supreme qualities" — the eight masteries resting on it, the ten totalities resting on those, and beyond them the noble superknowledges, the non-contention concentration, the knowledge that fulfills vows, and the four analytic knowledges. Freedom from craving for form was step one; sovereign command over form, so that these powers can be drawn out, is what the first liberation now cultivates.

14. The Second and Third Liberations: Contemplating External Form, and the "Pure Liberation"

The second liberation climbs a step higher — the practitioner now has a formless attainment, so he drops the internal light-perception and contemplates only "external" forms. The third, the "pure liberation," rests on the fourth dhyāna and works pure and impure form together until transformation becomes effortless — one of the two liberations in which the noble ones chiefly dwell.

Chinese Source

云何內無色想觀外諸色？謂生欲界，已離色界欲，無色界定不現在前，又不思惟彼想明相，但於外色而作勝解。若於是色已得離欲，說彼為外。由二因緣，名內無色想：謂已證得無色等至，亦自了知得此定故，不思惟內光明相故。餘如前說。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is "inwardly perceiving no form, one contemplates external forms"? One born in the desire realm who has left form-realm desire, whose formless attainment is not [taken as] present, who moreover does not contemplate that [internal] mark of luminosity, but generates conviction only toward external forms. Whatever form one has already left desire for is called "external." For two reasons it is called "inwardly perceiving no form": because he has attained a formless attainment and himself knows he has attained it, and because he does not contemplate the internal mark of luminosity. The rest is as stated before.

Chinese Source

云何淨解脫身作證具足住？謂如有一，已得捨念圓滿清白，以此為依，修習清淨聖行圓滿，名淨解脫。何以故？三因緣故：謂已超過諸苦樂故，一切亂動已寂靜故，善磨瑩故。身作證者，於此住中，一切賢聖多所住故。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the pure liberation directly realized in the body and dwelt in complete? Someone who has attained the perfected, pure, and bright equanimity-and-mindfulness [of the fourth dhyāna] and, resting on this, cultivates to completion the pure noble practice — this is called the pure liberation. Why? For three reasons: because he has passed beyond all pain and pleasure, because all agitation is already stilled, and because [the object] is well polished. "Directly realized in the body" means that all the worthy and noble ones abide much in this dwelling.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the second liberation as a rung above the first: the practitioner has now left form-realm desire — that is, attained a formless attainment — so he no longer needs the internal perception of luminosity that the first liberation used. Resting on the formless attainment as his base (though not taking its formless objects — space and the rest — as what he contemplates), he generates conviction only toward "external" forms, where "external" precisely means forms he has already freed himself of desire for. He notes the schools diverge sharply here — the Kośa has this liberation rest on the second dhyāna and contemplate impurity, while this treatise grounds it in a formless attainment — and treats the divergence as a matter of lineage, the shared point being the removal of the obstacle to transformation.

The third, the pure liberation, rests on the fourth dhyāna's "perfected, pure, and bright" equanimity and mindfulness, and cultivates the three perceptions of pure-and-impure form: mutual dependence (pure and impure exist only by contrast), mutual inclusion (purity within impurity and impurity within purity — the human body's "thin skin covering the unclean" that fools the eye into seeing it as lovely), and single taste (all resolved into one pure, luminous perception radiating blue, yellow, red, and white), until transforming form to pure or impure at will becomes effortless. It is called "pure" for three reasons Cháng Bǎi aligns exactly: having passed beyond pain and pleasure yields the pure equanimity; all agitation stilled (of thought, breath, even the three cosmic calamities) yields the pure mindfulness; and being "well polished" is the mastery of the pure-impure transformation itself. "Directly realized in the body" marks this and the eighth liberation as the two the noble ones dwell in most — cultivated only by third-fruit sages and above; and the first three liberations together form the base on which the eight masteries are built.

15. The Four Formless Liberations: Making the Boundless Attainments Sovereign

The next four liberations take the four formless spheres a practitioner has already attained and, by an intensified conviction toward each sphere's object, make the attainment stable and sovereign — again for the sake of the powers, not to sever a craving already left behind.

Chinese Source

云何空無邊處解脫？謂如有一，於彼空處已得離欲，即於虛空思惟勝解。如是識無邊處解脫，於彼識處已得離欲，即於是識思惟勝解。無所有處解脫者，謂已得無所有處，於識無邊處思惟勝解。有頂解脫，更不於餘而作勝解；乃至遍於想可生處，即於是處應作勝解。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the liberation of the sphere of infinite space? Someone who has left desire for the space-sphere and generates conviction toward space itself. Likewise the liberation of the sphere of infinite consciousness: having left desire for the consciousness-sphere, he generates conviction toward that consciousness. The liberation of the sphere of nothingness: having attained the sphere of nothingness, he generates conviction toward the sphere of infinite consciousness [as being nothing]. The liberation of the peak of existence generates conviction toward nothing further; reaching everywhere that perception can arise, he generates conviction just there.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that each of the four formless liberations takes a sphere the practitioner has already attained and, by generating an intensified conviction (勝解) toward its object, drives out the remaining obstacle and makes the attainment sovereign and secure — the aim throughout being to unlock the noble superknowledges, the non-contention concentration, the knowledge-of-vows, and the analytic knowledges, not to remove a craving already gone. Space-liberation fixes conviction on space as boundless; consciousness-liberation, on consciousness as boundless; nothingness-liberation, on the recognition that even boundless consciousness is "nothing at all." The peak-of-existence liberation (neither-perception-nor-non-perception) has no further object to convince toward: it releases both the "something-is-there" perceptions of the space and consciousness spheres and the "nothing-is-there" perception of the nothingness sphere — which is exactly why it is called "neither perception nor non-perception."

He flags one careful point, following Kuiji: the treatise pointedly does not say the practitioner has "left desire" for the top two spheres, because these are the practice of third-fruit non-returners, who still carry innate self-grasping — only an arhat has fully left the desire of all three realms. So for these the language is "releasing and stabilizing the lower ground" rather than "leaving desire." Cháng Bǎi closes by noting the many school variations on which concentration each liberation rests upon, and that this treatise names all eight together "the eight noble abidings" — the first seven called liberation for the intensified conviction toward an already-won release, the eighth for casting away perception and feeling.

16. The Eight Masteries: When the Mind Grows Stronger Than the Object

Built on the first three liberations, the eight masteries (八勝處) mark the turning point where the mind becomes stronger than its object — able to conceal it and reshape it at will. The treatise gives five senses of "mastery" and shows what the masteries add beyond the liberations.

Chinese Source

五、自在迴轉，故名為勝，謂世君王，隨所欲為處分臣僕。……於此義中，意顯隱蔽及自在勝。前解脫中勝解自在，今於勝處制伏自在。

Ru-Yi Translation

Fifth, because of sovereign turning [of the object] it is called "mastery" — as a worldly king disposes of his ministers and servants at will. … In this sense, what is highlighted is the mastery of concealment and of sovereignty: in the liberations there was sovereign conviction; here in the masteries there is sovereign subdual.

Chinese Source

觀色少者，謂諸有情資具等色。觀色多者，謂諸宮殿房舍等色。言好色者，謂美妙顯色，一向淨妙故；與此相違，名為惡色。言劣色者，謂聲、香、味、觸不可意色；與此相違，當知勝色。言勝知者，謂數數隱蔽所緣勝解。有如是想者，謂有制伏想也。

Ru-Yi Translation

"Contemplating little form" means the forms of beings and their possessions; "contemplating much form" means the forms of palaces, houses, and the like. "Good form" means a beautiful visible color, wholly fine; the opposite is "bad form." "Inferior form" means displeasing sound, scent, taste, and touch; the opposite, know, is "superior form." "Sovereign knowing" means repeatedly concealing the object by conviction. "Having such a perception" means having a perception of subdual.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that before liberation the object dominates the mind, dragging one through the six destinies; once the eight liberations are mastered and contemplation ripens, the mind grows stronger than the object, so that "all objects turn according to the mind" — this reversal is what "mastery" (勝處, literally "outdoing-station") names. "Sovereign knowing" (勝知) comes by the power of calm, able to make an object vanish; "sovereign seeing" (勝見) by the power of insight, able to turn it at will. He lists the five senses of the word: outshining (a superior skill puts another's in the shade — a non-Buddhist's powers outshone by a śrāvaka's, a śrāvaka's by a bodhisattva's, a bodhisattva's by a Buddha's); overpowering (the strong subdues the weak — a stream-enterer's non-self insight crushing the surge of self-love at the moment of death); concealing (a jar hides what is behind it; herbs, mantras, or powers hide an object — sunrise concealing the stars and lamps); wearying (wearying of the object so as to cast off affliction, on both the worldly and the world-transcending paths); and sovereign turning (a king disposing of his servants at will — seeing much as little and little as much).

The eight masteries themselves pair internal form-perception (or its absence) with contemplating external forms as "little" (beings and their possessions) or "much" (palaces and houses), each sorted good/bad/inferior/superior, and the last four take the four colors — blue, yellow, red, white. What the masteries add beyond the liberations, Cháng Bǎi stresses, is exactly concealment and sovereign turning: the liberations won sovereign conviction over form, the masteries win sovereign subdual of it. And "perception of subdual" (制伏想) means commanding the object completely — turning blue to yellow, pure to impure, little to much — without a trace of self-overestimation. He recounts the story of Venerable Aniruddha, whom four dazzling celestial women, able to transform into any color at will, offered to serve: he tried the impurity contemplation on them through all four dhyānas and through every color he asked them to display, and could not raise a single impure perception — his concentration and their color-mastery were "like two wrestlers of equal strength, neither able to prevail." The point, Cháng Bǎi notes, is that a sharp-faculty great arhat like Śāriputra, whose sovereign concentration exceeds any object, would have overpowered them — mastery is a matter of degree.

17. The Ten Totalities: Pervading Everywhere Without a Boundary

The ten totalities (十遍處) take the masteries' objects and extend each without limit, so that the whole of space is, in conviction, earth — or water, fire, wind, one of the four colors, space, or consciousness. The treatise explains why only form and touch serve as objects, and closes the triad that founds the powers.

Chinese Source

復次，由諸遍處，於勝解事生遍勝解，故名遍處。言無二者，謂諸賢聖無我、我所二差別故。言無量者，遍一切故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, because of the totalities, one generates a pervasive conviction toward the object of conviction, so they are called "totalities." "Non-dual" means that the worthy and noble ones have no duality of self and mine. "Immeasurable" means pervading everywhere.

Chinese Source

何故遍處唯就色、觸二處建立？由此二種，共自他身，遍有色界，常相續故。眼等根色唯屬自身，香、味二塵不遍一切，聲聲有間，是故不說如是有色諸遍處定。色界後邊，於無色中，空遍一切，故立遍處。

Ru-Yi Translation

Why are the totalities established only on the form-base and the touch-base? Because these two, shared by one's own and others' bodies, pervade the form realm and are constantly continuous. The eye-faculty and the rest belong only to one's own body; scent and taste do not pervade everywhere; sound is intermittent — therefore such totality-concentrations are not stated for them. At the far edge of the form realm, within the formless, space pervades everything, so a totality is established [on it].

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi lists the ten: earth, water, fire, wind (the four elements, as touch), blue, yellow, red, white (the four colors, as form), plus space and consciousness. A "totality" (遍處) generates a conviction that its object fills the whole of space without any boundary — contemplate earth, and everywhere is earth; and likewise for each. "Non-dual" because the noble one contemplates these without any grasping at self or mine; "immeasurable" because the sign pervades limitlessly. He explains why only form and touch furnish the objects, and not scent, taste, or sound: only visible color and the tangible elements are shared by one's own and others' bodies, pervade the entire form realm, and are constantly continuous — whereas the sense-faculties belong to one's own body alone, scent and taste do not even exist in the form realm, and sound is intermittent, arising only on occasion. The ninth totality, space, is built at the very edge of the form realm, contemplating form dissolved into an all-pervading space; and the tenth is consciousness, contemplating consciousness pervading all.

He treats the eight liberations, eight masteries, and ten totalities as one graded ascent of command over the material and formless attainments — the liberations winning sovereign conviction, the masteries sovereign subdual, and the totalities sovereign pervasion — and as together the finest foundation for the noble superknowledges and the transformative powers the ground has been pointing toward throughout.

18. The Consciousness Totality, the Sequence of the Three Sets, and the Powers They Unlock

The tenth totality completes the set, and the treatise fixes the developmental order of the whole triad — liberation, mastery, totality — while naming the transformative powers each unlocks when purified, crowned by the image of a potter working clay.

Chinese Source

識所行境遍一切故，亦立遍處。復次，修觀行者，先於所緣思惟勝解，次能制伏；既於制伏得自在已，後即於此遍一切處，如其所欲而作勝解；是故此三如是次第。

Ru-Yi Translation

Because consciousness's field of activity pervades everything, a totality is also established [on it]. Furthermore, a practitioner of contemplation first generates conviction toward the object, next can subdue it; and once he has gained mastery over subduing it, he then generates conviction that it pervades everywhere, just as he wishes — therefore these three follow in this sequence.

Chinese Source

八色遍處善清淨故，能引賢聖勝解神通，及於諸事轉變神通。如其勝解，隨所轉變，皆能成就。又能變作金銀等物，堪有所用。由識遍處善清淨故，便能引發無諍、願智、無礙解等諸勝功德。由空遍處善清淨故，隨其所欲皆轉成空。譬如世間瓦鐵金師，初和泥等，未善調練，解脫位亦爾。如善調練，勝處位亦爾。如調練已，隨欲轉變，遍處位亦爾。

Ru-Yi Translation

Because the eight form-totalities are well purified, they can elicit the noble ones' conviction-superknowledge and the superknowledge of transforming things: however one is convinced, so it transforms, and all is accomplished; one can even transform things into gold, silver, and the like, fit for use. Because the consciousness-totality is well purified, one can elicit non-contention, the knowledge-of-vows, the analytic knowledges, and other supreme qualities. Because the space-totality is well purified, whatever one wishes all turns into space. Just as a worldly potter or smith, at first mixing the clay, has not yet skillfully worked it — so too the liberation-stage; as he skillfully works it — so too the mastery-stage; as, having worked it, he reshapes it at will — so too the totality-stage.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes that the consciousness-totality contemplates consciousness pervading everywhere, completing the ten, and draws out the strict developmental sequence the three sets form: with the liberations one first generates sovereign conviction toward the object, winning command over it; with the masteries one subdues it, able to conceal and turn it; and with the totalities, having gained that mastery, one generates conviction that it pervades all of space, however one wishes. Each set presupposes the one before.

He then lines up the powers each unlocks once purified: the eight form-totalities (the four elements and four colors) elicit the transformation superknowledges — whatever one is convinced of, so the thing transforms, even into gold or silver fit for actual use; the consciousness-totality elicits the non-contention concentration, the knowledge that fulfills vows, and the four analytic knowledges; the space-totality turns anything at will into space. The potter's image crowns the whole triad: the liberation-stage is like clay first mixed but not yet worked; the mastery-stage like the clay now skillfully kneaded; the totality-stage like the finished command that shapes the worked clay into whatever form one pleases — the graded mastery over form and the formless that founds every transformative power.

19. The Emptiness Concentration, and the Four Kinds of Emptiness

The treatise turns to the three concentrations — the three doors to liberation. The first is the emptiness concentration, fixing the mind on the absence of any self or being behind the aggregates; it is unfolded through four kinds of emptiness — investigative, its fruit, internal, and external — and the method that interweaves them.

Chinese Source

復次，三三摩地者。云何空三摩地？謂於遠離有情、命者，及養育者、數取趣等，心住一緣。當知空性略有四種：一、觀察空，謂觀察諸法空無常樂，乃至空無我、我所等。二、彼果空，謂不動心解脫，空無貪等一切煩惱。三者、內空，謂於自身空無計我、我所及我慢等一切僻執。四者、外空，謂於五欲空無欲愛。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, the three concentrations. What is the emptiness concentration? Fixing the mind single-pointedly on the absence of a being, a living soul, a nurturer, a transmigrating person, and so on. Know that emptiness is, in brief, of four kinds: first, investigative emptiness — contemplating all phenomena as empty of permanence and pleasure, down to empty of self and mine; second, the emptiness that is its fruit — the unshakable liberation of mind, empty of craving and every affliction; third, internal emptiness — one's own body empty of any reckoning of I, mine, and the conceit of self, and all crooked grasping; fourth, external emptiness — the five desires empty of craving-for-desire.

Chinese Source

如說：我已超過一切有色想故，於外空身作證具足住，乃至廣說。此中緣妙欲想，名為色想；此想所起貪欲斷故，說為外空。又修行者，由彼果空，或時作意思惟外空，或時作意思惟內空。由觀察空，或時思惟內外空性；由此力故，心俱證會。

Ru-Yi Translation

As it is said: "Because I have transcended all perceptions of form, I dwell complete, directly realizing external emptiness in the body," and so on at length. Here the perception conditioned by fine desire is called "perception of form"; because the craving arisen from this perception is severed, it is called "external emptiness." Moreover, a practitioner, by [resting in] the fruit-emptiness, sometimes attends to external emptiness and sometimes to internal emptiness; by investigative emptiness he sometimes attends to internal-and-external emptiness — and by this force, the mind realizes them together.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi introduces the emptiness concentration (空三摩地) as the first of the three doors to liberation: the mind fixed single-pointedly on the fact that behind the aggregates there is no being, no living soul, no nurturer, no transmigrating person — none of the things the various words for a self name. He distinguishes its four kinds. Investigative emptiness is the analytic insight that sees all phenomena as empty of permanence, of pleasure, and finally of self and mine. Its fruit-emptiness is what that insight wins — the "unshakable liberation of mind" of an arhat, empty of craving and every affliction. Internal emptiness is one's own body and aggregates seen as empty of any I, mine, self-conceit, or crooked grasping. External emptiness is the five sense-objects seen as empty of the craving-for-desire they provoke — glossed through the scriptural line "having transcended all perceptions of form," where "perception of form" means the perception of desirable objects, so that "external emptiness" names the severing of the craving that such perception sets off.

He notes the method interweaves the four: standing in the fruit-emptiness, the practitioner alternates his attention between external and internal emptiness; and through investigative emptiness he attends to internal-and-external emptiness together, until the force of that contemplation lets the mind realize both at once. This is the first of the three concentrations; the wishlessness and signlessness concentrations follow.

20. Breaking Through With "the Unwavering," and the Wishlessness Concentration

If contemplating emptiness directly does not yet break through, the treatise offers a second angle — the perception of impermanence or suffering, "the unwavering," which dislodges self-conceit so both emptinesses are realized at once. Then the second door: the wishlessness concentration.

Chinese Source

設復於此內外空性不證會者，便應作意思惟無動。言無動者，謂無常想，或復苦想。如是思惟，便不為彼我慢等動。由彼不為計我、我慢乃至廣說動其心故，便於二空心俱證會。

Ru-Yi Translation

Should he still not realize this internal-and-external emptiness, he should attend to and contemplate "the unwavering." "The unwavering" means the perception of impermanence, or the perception of suffering; contemplating thus, he is no longer moved by conceit-of-self and the rest. Because his mind is no longer moved by reckoning a self, by self-conceit, and so on, the mind then realizes both emptinesses together.

Chinese Source

云何無願心三摩地？謂於五取蘊，思惟無常，或思惟苦，心住一緣。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the wishlessness concentration of mind? Fixing the mind single-pointedly, contemplating the five grasping-aggregates as impermanent, or contemplating them as suffering.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes that the mind is complex, so a single angle of contemplation may not sever an affliction; when direct contemplation of internal and external emptiness fails to land, one adds "the unwavering" — the perception of impermanence or of suffering. Seeing the aggregates as impermanent and suffering, self-conceit, self-view, self-love, and self-delusion lose their purchase, and the mind, no longer moved by any reckoning of a self, realizes both the internal and external emptiness at once. He underlines the balance the treatise draws (via the Compendium of Ascertainments) between wrong and right emptiness: a genuine see-er of the truths avoids the "wrong emptiness" of nihilistic blankness and enters the "right non-emptiness" of dependent arising — the aggregates are not utterly nothing (they arise illusorily by conditions and must be "known as existent"), yet they are empty of any real self; the five sense-objects are not nothing, but the craving for them is to be severed. This is what contemplating internal-and-external emptiness truly means.

The wishlessness concentration (無願, the second of the three doors to liberation) then fixes the mind single-pointedly by contemplating the five grasping-aggregates as impermanent, or as suffering — suffering-of-pain, suffering-of-change, suffering-of-formations — so that no fondness for form, feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness arises, and no craving for further existence is generated. The mind rests wishless, its grasping stilled. "Grasping" (取), Cháng Bǎi glosses, means clinging: beings' consciousness perpetually clings to the aggregates, which is why they are called the "grasping-aggregates."

21. The Signless Concentration: Neither Low Nor Exalted

The third door fixes the mind on the quiescent cessation of the grasping-aggregates, turning from every sign. The treatise unfolds the scriptural phrase "neither low nor exalted" and the two conditions by which one enters — not attending to any sign, and rightly attending to the signless.

Chinese Source

云何無相心三摩地？謂即於彼諸取蘊滅，思惟寂靜，心住一緣。如經言：無相心三摩地，不低不昂，乃至廣說。云何名為不低不昂？違順二相不相應故。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the signless concentration of mind? Fixing the mind single-pointedly, contemplating the quiescence [that is] the cessation of those grasping-aggregates. As the sūtra says: "The signless concentration of mind is neither low nor exalted," and so on at length. What is "neither low nor exalted"? Not corresponding to either the repellent or the agreeable sign.

Chinese Source

又二因緣，入無相定：一、不思惟一切相故；二、正思惟無相界故。由不思惟一切相故，於彼諸相不厭不壞，唯不加行作意思惟，故名不低。於無相界正思惟故，於彼無相不堅執著，故名不昂。

Ru-Yi Translation

Moreover, by two conditions one enters the signless concentration: first, not attending to any sign; second, rightly attending to the signless realm. Because one does not attend to any sign, one neither wearies of nor breaks down those signs, but simply does not apply attention to them — hence "not low." Because one rightly attends to the signless realm yet does not cling firmly to the signless, it is called "not exalted."

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that the signless concentration (無相, the third door) fixes the mind on the quiescent unconditioned — the cessation of the five grasping-aggregates — withdrawing from all signs. Following Kuiji, he answers why "wishlessness" and "signlessness" carry the word "mind" (心) while "emptiness" does not: the emptiness concentration directly contemplates non-self, which accords with the empty itself, so no "mind" is added; the other two contemplate upon the conditioned aggregates, so "mind" is added. The "signs" to be removed are the classical set — the five sense-objects, greed, hatred, delusion, and male and female (ten), sometimes given with birth, aging, and death, or with suffering, pleasure, and equanimity added — in short, the whole range of conditioned marks the mind fastens on.

"Neither low nor exalted" he reads two ways. First, it corresponds to neither the repellent sign (the conditioned aggregates, whose faults make the mind sink in aversion — "low") nor the agreeable sign (the signless realm, whose merits make the mind rise in attachment — "exalted"). Second, it names the two conditions of entry: not attending to any sign means one neither wearies of the signs nor tries to destroy them, but simply withdraws effortful attention from them — "not low"; and rightly attending to the signless realm while not clinging firmly to it — seeing its merit through sound attention but not grasping at it — is "not exalted." He notes the schools diverge on the term (the Mahāvibhāṣā treats "signless" as a synonym for the emptiness concentration, whereas this treatise distinguishes them as the intellect's fruit and its own distinct door).

22. The Signless Concentration's Means and Fruit, and What "Clear Knowing" Yields

The signless concentration has a laborious means-phase, harried by the "sign-following consciousness," and a fruit-phase that becomes effortless — "utterly-good liberation." The treatise closes the three concentrations by unpacking their "fruit of clear knowing" three ways.

Chinese Source

此三摩地略有二種：一者、方便，二、方便果。言方便者，數數策勵思擇安立，於彼諸相未能解脫，由隨相識，於時時中擾亂心故。彼復數數自策自勵思擇安立，方能取果，解脫隨相。於此解脫又解脫故，不自策勵思擇而住，是故名為極善解脫。若數策勵思擇安立方得住者，雖名解脫，非善解脫。

Ru-Yi Translation

This concentration is, in brief, of two kinds: first, the means; second, the fruit of the means. The means: repeatedly spurring oneself and discerning to establish [the signless], while not yet freed from those signs — because the sign-following consciousness disturbs the mind from time to time. Repeatedly spurring and rousing oneself, discerning to establish it, one is then able to win the fruit and be freed from the sign-following. Because one is freed from this and freed again, one abides without spurring oneself to discern — therefore it is called utterly-good liberation. If one abides only by repeated spurring and discerning-to-establish, though it is called liberation, it is not utterly-good liberation.

Chinese Source

又曉了果、曉了功德者，謂煩惱斷究竟故，現法樂住究竟故。又復滅、道俱應曉了，即此二種，隨其次第，名曉了果、曉了功德。又諦現觀、阿羅漢果俱應曉了。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for the fruit of clear knowing and the merit of clear knowing: because the severance of affliction is final, and because the pleasant abiding in this very life is final. Moreover, both cessation and path should be clearly known — these two, in sequence, are the fruit of clear knowing and the merit of clear knowing. Moreover, the direct realization of the truths and the fruit of arhatship should both be clearly known.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes the signless concentration's two phases. In the means-phase the beginner must keep spurring himself to establish the signless in mind — but, never having seen the genuinely signless, he can only fix a conceptual "sign of the signless" by name, and the sign-following consciousness (隨相識 — "following" meaning "resembling," the imagined signless that merely resembles the real one) keeps disturbing him. Through repeated effort he wins the fruit — real freedom from the sign-following. When that freedom itself becomes effortless — freed, and freed again, so that he enters the signless the moment he inclines toward it without any labor — it is "utterly-good liberation" (極善解脫); a liberation still requiring strained effort each time is liberation, but not the utterly-good kind, because the conceptual signless is only similar to the real, and only realizing emptiness itself, transcending the sign-following altogether, is genuinely effortless.

He gives the "fruit of clear knowing" and "merit of clear knowing" (曉了果／曉了功德) three readings, each pairing a result with what produces it: the final severance of affliction (fruit) with the final present pleasant-abiding (merit); the cessation-truth realized (fruit) with the path-truth that realizes it (merit); and the four-truths direct realization together with the fruit of arhatship, both penetrating the signless ultimate. With this the three concentrations — the three doors to liberation — are complete.

23. Why the Three Doors Bear Their Names — and Why Emptiness Comes First

The treatise fixes why the three concentrations are called emptiness, wishlessness, and signlessness, and resolves an ordering puzzle: elsewhere the sequence runs impermanence → suffering → no-self → emptiness, but here emptiness is placed first — because no-self is what grounds the whole contemplation.

Chinese Source

若於此處無有彼物，由此道理觀之為空，故名空性。即所觀空無可希願，故名無願。觀此遠離一切行相，故名無相。

Ru-Yi Translation

Where a thing lacks something, by this principle it is contemplated as empty — hence "emptiness." Because the emptiness contemplated has nothing to be wished for, it is called "wishlessness." Because this is contemplated as removed from all activity-marks, it is called "signlessness."

Chinese Source

何故此中先說空性；餘處宣說無常故苦，苦故無我，後方說空？謂若無無我，無常苦觀終不清淨。要先安住無我之想，從此無間方得無願。是故經言：諸無常想，依無我想而得安住，乃至廣說。彼於無常觀無我已，不生希願，唯願無相，專求出離，故此無間宣說無相。

Ru-Yi Translation

Why is emptiness stated first here, whereas elsewhere it is taught "because impermanent, therefore suffering; because suffering, therefore without self," and only afterward emptiness? Because without [the perception of] no-self, the contemplation of impermanence and suffering is never purified. One must first abide in the perception of no-self, and only then, without interval, does one attain wishlessness. Therefore the sūtra says: "The perceptions of impermanence rest upon the perception of no-self," and so on at length. Having, upon impermanence, contemplated no-self, one generates no wish [for existence] but wishes only for the signless, single-mindedly seeking release — therefore, without interval, signlessness is taught next.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses the three names cleanly: a thing is "empty" where it lacks something — the aggregates empty of any self; "wishlessness" because the emptiness one has seen leaves nothing to wish for, no craving for existence toward what has no self; and "signlessness" because it is contemplated as removed from every conditioned activity-mark. On the ordering, he explains that the usual teaching-sequence — impermanent, therefore suffering, therefore no-self, therefore empty — is reversed here, emptiness (no-self) placed first, for a structural reason: the contemplation of impermanence and suffering never becomes pure unless it is grounded in no-self, since otherwise one still covertly grasps an "I" that is impermanent and that suffers. So one must first stand in the perception of no-self; from that, wishlessness follows without interval (seeing no self to preserve, one ceases to wish for existence); and from that, signlessness (wishing only for the signless peace and single-mindedly seeking release). Cháng Bǎi cites the treatise's own scriptural support — "the perceptions of impermanence rest upon the perception of no-self" — to make the point that the three doors are a strict progression built on no-self, not three interchangeable techniques.

24. Concentration With Applied Thought, With Only Sustained Thought, and With Neither

A different classification of concentration, by the presence of applied and sustained thought — a psychological sorting, not the cosmological one of the earlier fascicles. It carries rebirth up the form and formless realms, and the treatise gives the distinctive mark of the "neither" concentration.

Chinese Source

復次，云何有尋有伺三摩地？謂三摩地尋伺相應。云何無尋唯伺三摩地？謂三摩地唯伺相應。大梵修已，為大梵王。云何無尋無伺三摩地？謂三摩地尋伺二種俱不相應。修習此故，生次上地乃至有頂。唯除無漏諸三摩地。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, what is the concentration with applied and sustained thought? A concentration associated with applied and sustained thought. What is the concentration with only sustained thought? A concentration associated only with sustained thought — the Great Brahmā, having cultivated it, becomes the Great Brahmā King. What is the concentration with neither applied nor sustained thought? A concentration associated with neither; by cultivating it one is reborn in the successively higher grounds up to the peak of existence. Only the uncontaminated concentrations are excepted.

Chinese Source

云何無尋無伺三摩地相？謂於尋伺心生棄捨，唯由一味，於內所緣而作勝解，又唯一味平等顯現。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the mark of the concentration with neither applied nor sustained thought? The mind gives rise to a casting-off of applied and sustained thought; by a single flavor alone it generates conviction toward the inner object, and only that single flavor appears, level and even.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes this sorts concentration by applied-and-sustained thought (尋伺) as a psychological matter — reusing the very names (有尋有伺, 無尋唯伺, 無尋無伺) that ran cosmologically through fascicles 4–10, but here for states of mind, exactly as fascicle 11 flagged. The concentration with both is the threshold concentration and the first dhyāna, where coarse applied thought and fine sustained thought are both present. The concentration with only sustained thought is the intermediate dhyāna between the first and second, where coarse applied thought has dropped but fine sustained thought remains — and cultivating precisely this is what makes one the Great Brahmā King. The concentration with neither is the second dhyāna and above, up to the peak of existence, where both are stilled. He stresses that these three are the contaminated (worldly) concentrations, the ones that carry rebirth up the form and formless realms; the uncontaminated concentrations are excepted, because they generate no saṃsāric rebirth.

On the mark of the "neither" concentration: the mind casts off both coarse and fine thought, so that in place of thought's restless discriminations it rests in "a single flavor" — one even, level conviction toward the inner object, with none of the up-and-down of investigating. Cháng Bǎi treats this evenness of a single sustained taste as the felt signature of an absorption past all applied and sustained thought.

25. The Great-Mind Concentration: Contemplating the Light of the Gods

The small, great, and immeasurable concentrations are sorted by their scope. The treatise unfolds the great-mind concentration as a light-visualization — the gods' radiance spreading from beneath one's tree to the bounds of the earth and sea — and notes how the light itself differs by level.

Chinese Source

此中大心三摩地者，謂於一樹下，想諸天光而生勝解，乃至廣說。無量三摩地者，謂四無量。云何於一樹下想諸天光而生勝解？謂於欲界極厭壞已，得初靜慮，為令此定善清淨故，更修方便。又聞諸天身帶光明，便思惟彼身光明相，遍一樹下，乃至大地、大海邊際，發生勝解。

Ru-Yi Translation

Here the great-mind concentration is: under a single tree, contemplating the light of the gods and generating conviction, and so on at length. The immeasurable concentration is the four immeasurables. How does one, under a single tree, contemplate the gods' light and generate conviction? Having thoroughly wearied of the desire realm and attained the first dhyāna, in order to make this concentration well purified he further cultivates the means; and having heard that the gods' bodies carry light, he contemplates the mark of that bodily light spreading beneath a single tree, and on to the bounds of the great earth and the great ocean, generating conviction.

Chinese Source

問：初二靜慮諸天光明有何差別？答：如末尼珠，外有光明，內無光明；初靜慮身亦復如是，外放光明，內則不爾。譬如明燈，外發光明，內自照了；第二靜慮身亦如是，若內若外俱有光明。是故經說：彼地已上惟一種身，非於下地。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: what is the difference between the light of the first- and second-dhyāna gods? Answer: like a maṇi-jewel, which has light outside but none within — so too the first-dhyāna body radiates light outward but not within. Like a bright lamp, which sends light outward and also illumines itself within — so too the second-dhyāna body has light both within and without. Therefore the sūtra says: from that ground upward there is only one kind of body, unlike the lower grounds.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains the great-mind concentration (大心三摩地) as a light-visualization used to purify the first dhyāna: having wearied of desire and won that absorption, the practitioner, hearing that the gods' bodies glow, contemplates that radiance spreading from beneath his own tree out to the edges of the earth and ocean, generating a vivid conviction. He notes the light itself differs by level — the first-dhyāna gods glow only outward (like a maṇi-jewel, bright without but dark within), while the second-dhyāna gods and above glow both within and without (like a lamp) — which is why "from the second dhyāna up there is only one kind of body." He fills in the classical four kinds of form-beings by body and perception: body-different-and-perception-different (humans and some gods); body-different-but-perception-same (Brahmā's retinue at the start of an eon, all imagining themselves Brahmā's creation though Brahmā's own body towers over theirs); body-same-but-perception-different (the second dhyāna, one luminous kind of body but alternating pleasant and neutral perception); and body-same-and-perception-same (the third dhyāna's Universally-Pure gods, one body and one blissful perception).

He then traces the concentration's deepening through a strict "only-two" — inferior and superior — chain: the strength of one's conviction sets the attention as inferior or superior; that sets the cultivated concentration as inferior or superior; that sets the karmic formation (the imperturbable seed) as inferior or superior; and that in turn sets the reborn being as inferior or superior. This, Cháng Bǎi notes (citing the Xianyang Shengjiao Lun), is the very grading that gives each form-heaven its levels — attention, cultivation, karma, and person all moving in step from lesser to greater.

26. The Four Immeasurables: Loving-Kindness, Compassion, Sympathetic Joy, and Equanimity

The immeasurable concentration is the four immeasurables, built on three kinds of beings and four wishes. The first three answer the neutral, the suffering, and the happy; the fourth, equanimity, adds to each the wish that they let go of delusion, ill-will, and craving.

Chinese Source

復次，云何建立四無量定？謂諸有情有三品故：一者、無苦無樂，二者、有苦，三者、有樂。如其次第，欲與其樂、欲令離苦、欲令其樂永不相離。於彼作意有四種故，如其次第，建立四種。謂由與樂作意故、拔苦作意故、樂不相離隨喜作意故，建立前三。即於此三欲與樂等，為欲令彼不樂思慕不染汙作意故，瞋恚不染汙作意故，貪欲不染汙作意故，建立於捨。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, how are the four immeasurable concentrations established? Because beings are of three kinds: those without suffering or pleasure, those with suffering, and those with pleasure. In that order, one wishes to give them happiness, wishes to free them from suffering, and wishes their happiness never to part from them. Because attention toward them is fourfold, four are established in order: by the attention of giving happiness, the attention of removing suffering, and the attention of rejoicing that their happiness not part, the first three are established. And toward these same three — wishing to give happiness and so on — by the undefiled attention [freeing them] from non-delight and longing, the undefiled attention [freeing them] from ill-will, and the undefiled attention [freeing them] from craving, equanimity is established.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi lays out how the four immeasurables are built on the three kinds of beings and the wishes fitting each: loving-kindness (慈) toward the neutral being, wishing it happiness (countering ill-will); compassion (悲) toward the suffering being, wishing to pull its suffering out (countering harm); and sympathetic joy (喜) toward the happy being, wishing its happiness never to leave it (countering envy). Equanimity (捨) then takes the same three beings and adds a further, corrective aim: to free the neutral being from the delusion of longing after worldly pleasure, the suffering being from ill-will, and the happy being from craving — so that, having given happiness, removed suffering, and rejoiced, one now teaches each to release greed, hatred, and delusion, and extends one even, undefiled regard to all beings alike, past the partiality of friend and enemy.

He stresses (citing the Dàshèng Yìzhāng) that the four mutually assist and cannot stand alone: loving-kindness needs compassion actually to relieve suffering, and compassion needs loving-kindness actually to confer happiness; both need sympathetic joy to clear away the envy that would block them; all three need equanimity to become impartial rather than partial; and equanimity needs the first three, or it decays into mere indifference — the first three being "with-activity" and equanimity "space-like emptiness," each holding the other's excess in check. He adds that these are the noble ones' immeasurables, open also to ordinary beings and even non-Buddhists but differing in quality and in their supporting concentration — śrāvakas basing them on the four dhyānas, while great bodhisattvas and certain great arhats can base them even on the formless attainments.

27. Reading the Sūtra's Loving-Kindness Formula, Term by Term

The treatise glosses the stock scriptural phrase for the immeasurables — "with a mind of loving-kindness … without enmity, without hostility, without harm, vast, great, immeasurable, well-cultivated" — and its summarizing description, "resolved, pervading, complete, and abiding."

Chinese Source

現前饒益，故名慈俱。饒益相故，名慈善友。其饒益相略有二種：一、欲利益、二、欲安樂。此二種相，一切無量之所顯示。無怨者，離惡意樂故。無敵者，離現乖諍故。無惱害者，離不饒益事故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Because one benefits the being present before one, it is called "accompanied by loving-kindness." Because it is the mark of benefiting, it is called "a good friend of loving-kindness." Its mark of benefiting is, in brief, of two kinds: wishing to benefit, and wishing to give ease. These two marks are what all the immeasurables display. "Without enmity" means free of ill-willed intent. "Without hostility" means free of present quarrel. "Without harm" means free of un-benefiting deeds.

Chinese Source

廣者，所緣廣大故。大者，利益安樂思惟最勝故。無量者，果無量故，如四大河，眾流雜處。善修習者，極純熟故。設有問言：慈俱等心有何等相？故次答言：勝解遍滿，具足而住。勝解遍滿者，是增上意樂勝解周普義。具足者，圓滿清白故。住者，所修觀行日夜專注，時專注故。

Ru-Yi Translation

"Vast" means the object is vast. "Great" means the thought of benefit-and-ease is supreme. "Immeasurable" means the fruit is immeasurable — like the four great rivers, whose many streams mingle together. "Well-cultivated" means utterly ripened. Should someone ask, "what are the marks of the mind accompanied by loving-kindness and the rest?" the answer follows: "resolved, pervading, complete, and abiding." "Resolved and pervading" means a heightened resolve that reaches all-round; "complete" means perfect and pure; "abiding" means the practice is sustained day and night, moment by moment.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the formula term by term. "Accompanied by loving-kindness" is actually benefiting the being before one; "good friend of loving-kindness" is the benefiting-mark itself, of two kinds — wishing benefit (this life and the next, by transforming the being's mind and so its karma) and wishing ease (bodily and mental happiness), the two aims every immeasurable shares. The three negations map to the three doors of action: "without enmity" is freedom from ill-willed intent (mind), "without hostility" is freedom from present quarrel (speech), and "without harm" is freedom from un-benefiting deeds (body). "Vast" points to the immensity of the object — all beings of the ten directions and three times; "great," to the supremacy of the thought of their benefit and ease; and "immeasurable," to the boundlessness of the fruit, which he likens to the four great rivers whose many streams merge — all goodness gathered, so the merit has no measure (he recalls the classical fifteen benefits of loving-kindness: sleeping and waking at ease, dreaming no evil, guarded by gods and men, unharmed by fire, water, poison, or blade, reborn in Brahmā's world, and so on). "Well-cultivated" is the practice utterly ripened.

The summarizing phrase "resolved, pervading, complete, and abiding" he unpacks as the felt shape of a mature immeasurable: a heightened resolve (增上意樂勝解) — a settled, unshakable intention of benefit and ease — that reaches all-round to every being (resolved-and-pervading); perfect and pure, momentarily and continuously free of any defiled intent (complete); and sustained through day and night, moment after moment (abiding).

28. Where Each Immeasurable Reaches Its Height, and Why Only the Noble Truly Cultivate Them

Each immeasurable peaks on a base whose felt quality matches it: compassion on the sphere of infinite space, sympathetic joy on infinite consciousness, equanimity on nothingness (as loving-kindness peaks on the third dhyāna). All four, the treatise insists, are noble practice, cultivated together with the awakening-factors.

Chinese Source

憶念空處修習悲心，亦最第一。以修悲者樂欲拔苦，無色界中遠離眾苦，斷壞等苦彼都無故。是故憶念無邊空處修悲等至，作如是念：當令一切有苦有情，到無眾苦及所依處。修喜定者，亦常憶念無邊識處，慶諸有情所得安樂。作如是念：當令一切有情之類受無量樂，猶如識處，識無限量。是故憶念識無邊處修習喜定，為最第一。

Ru-Yi Translation

Recalling the space-sphere and cultivating compassion is likewise supreme. Because one who cultivates compassion wishes to remove suffering, and in the formless realm the manifold sufferings are left behind — the sufferings of breaking and injury and the rest being wholly absent there. Therefore, recalling the sphere of infinite space, one cultivates the compassion-attainment, thinking: "may all suffering beings reach the place free of every suffering and its support." One who cultivates the joy-concentration likewise constantly recalls the sphere of infinite consciousness, rejoicing in the ease beings attain, thinking: "may all beings receive measureless happiness, like consciousness itself, without limit." Therefore recalling the sphere of infinite consciousness and cultivating the joy-concentration is supreme.

Chinese Source

修捨定者，亦常憶念無所有處。作如是念：無所有處，無漏心地最為後邊，捨最第一；如阿羅漢苾芻，一切苦、樂、不苦不樂現行位中，皆無染汙。當令一切有情之類，得如是捨。是故憶念無所有處修習捨定，為最第一。如是一切皆是聖行，唯聖能修，故經宣說覺分俱行。

Ru-Yi Translation

One who cultivates the equanimity-concentration likewise constantly recalls the sphere of nothingness, thinking: "the sphere of nothingness is the very last ground of the uncontaminated mind, where equanimity is supreme — as an arhat monk, in every occurrence of pain, pleasure, or the neither, is without defilement; may all beings attain such equanimity." Therefore recalling the sphere of nothingness and cultivating the equanimity-concentration is supreme. All these are noble practice, cultivable only by the noble; therefore the sūtra proclaims [them] as cultivated together with the awakening-factors.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi shows each immeasurable reaching its height on a base whose character matches its own wish. Loving-kindness (already given) peaks at the third dhyāna, whose bliss is the finest in all three realms — so wishing beings that happiness is loving-kindness at its fullest. Compassion peaks at the sphere of infinite space, because the wish to remove suffering is answered most completely where the formless realm has already shed every bodily suffering — the "breaking and injury" a body undergoes when struck by hand, stone, blade, cold, heat, hunger, thirst, insects, or snakes — so one wishes suffering beings into that place free of all suffering and of the very body that suffers. Sympathetic joy peaks at the sphere of infinite consciousness, wishing beings a happiness as boundless as consciousness pervading all. Equanimity peaks at the sphere of nothingness — which he calls "the last ground of the uncontaminated mind," the highest place the uncontaminated path can still be cultivated (unlike the peak of existence, whose perception is too faint for insight) — the equanimity of an arhat who meets pain, pleasure, and the neither alike with no defilement; one wishes all beings that arhat's equanimity.

He stresses these are noble practices "not shared with non-Buddhists": non-Buddhists cultivate the four immeasurables too, but cannot carry them this far, lacking the insight into no-self — which is why the sūtra pairs them with the seven awakening-factors. He raises the classical puzzle (the immeasurables are contaminated, the awakening-factors uncontaminated — how "together"?) and gives its resolution: not simultaneously but interleaved — the immeasurables make the mind pliant and fit and so draw forth the awakening-factors, which in turn draw the immeasurables, alternating; so that a see-er of the truths cultivating the immeasurables has the awakening-factors accompany them, and, as the Āgama promises, "I assure you the fruit of the non-returner" — not because loving-kindness itself cuts fetters, but because the merit and wisdom it gathers make the path that cuts them possible.

29. One-Part and Complete Cultivation, and the Eleven Difficulties of the Light-Concentration

Two more concentrations, sorted by whether one attends to light alone, form alone, or both — a method for the divine eye. The treatise then names the eleven obstacles that beset the light-concentration, tracing them to the two marks that sustain any concentration.

Chinese Source

復次，云何一分修三摩地？謂於此中或唯作意思惟光明相，或唯作意思惟色相，而入於定。如是二種，隨其次第，或了光明，或覩眾色。云何具分修三摩地？謂俱思惟而入於定，亦了光明，亦見眾色。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, what is one-part cultivation of concentration? Here, either attending only to the mark of light or attending only to the mark of form, one enters the concentration; these two, in order, either discern light or behold forms. What is complete-cultivation of concentration? Attending to both, one enters the concentration, discerning light and also seeing forms.

Chinese Source

如是修習光明定者，定難差別有十一種，所謂疑等，如經廣說。問：此誰難耶？答：三摩地相。相有二種，謂所緣相及因緣相。用彼為依，住三摩地；若退彼相，便不能住。

Ru-Yi Translation

One who thus cultivates the light-concentration meets eleven kinds of concentration-difficulty — doubt and the rest, as the scripture explains at length. Question: whose difficulties are these? Answer: the marks of concentration. The marks are of two kinds: the object-mark and the causal mark. Resting on these, one abides in concentration; if one loses those marks, one cannot abide.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that one-part cultivation attends to either the light-mark alone or the form-mark alone — which Kuiji maps to the first liberation (desire-realm form contemplated with a light-perception) and the second (external form contemplated); complete-cultivation attends to both, discerning light and seeing forms together, mapped to the third, "pure" liberation before its completion — a classical way of developing the divine eye. Cultivating this light-concentration, however, meets eleven obstacles (doubt and the rest — the four that obstruct the light-perception of reflection-wisdom and the seven that obstruct the light-perception of cultivation-wisdom, fully enumerated in the Ground of the Fruit of Cultivation).

He notes the treatise pins these difficulties to the two marks that sustain any concentration at all: the object-mark (the image the mind holds — here the light or the form) and the causal mark (the concentration-provisions — moderate eating, sense-restraint, precepts, wakeful practice, clear comprehension, the whole discipline of daily conduct that lets one sit at ease). Resting on both, one abides in the concentration; lose either, and one cannot — which is exactly where the eleven difficulties strike. They are now spelled out one by one.

30. The Eleven Difficulties, One by One

The treatise names each of the eleven obstacles to the light-concentration, several sealed with a vivid image — the over-tight grip that lets the bird escape, the man who stumbles on two treasures, the sudden lunge from both sides.

Chinese Source

此中最初，於所顯現光明色相不善知故，便覺有疑。方便緩故，有不作意。如於眾色不欲見者，或閉於目，或復背面；此觀行者，於諸色中不欲作意，亦復如是。由不善守根門等故，有身麤重。多習睡眠，或多覺悟，便增惛睡，不見眾色，設有所見而不圓滿。為此二事，極作功用力勵思惟，故有太過勇猛精進。

Ru-Yi Translation

Here the first: because one does not well know the light-and-form mark that appears, doubt arises. Because the effort is slack, there is non-attention. As one who does not want to see forms shuts his eyes or turns his back, so this contemplative, not wanting to attend to forms, is likewise [obstructed]. Because one does not well guard the sense-doors and the rest, there is bodily heaviness. Sleeping too much, or waking too much, dullness-and-sleep increases, so one does not see forms, or, seeing them, sees incompletely. Straining effort against these two, there is excessive exertion.

Chinese Source

由有太過策勵過故，還極下劣，如急捉持斥鷃鳥者。彼唯思求光明之相，此與見色若俱生時，希一得二，便生踊躍，猶如有人得二伏藏。遍於諸方，欻然并見不祥之色，便生大怖，猶如有人兩邊旋轉卒起。於彼行時，或復住時，於世雜類起種種想，如是外想，與定為難。或復因其所修習定，謂己為勝，觀他為劣，便自高舉，如是亦得名種種想。

Ru-Yi Translation

Because of that excessive spurring, one sinks back into extreme sluggishness — like one who grips a quail too tightly. Seeking only the mark of light, when it arises together with the seeing of form — hoping for one and getting two — one leaps with elation, like a man who finds two buried treasures. Seeing, all at once, inauspicious forms in every direction, one is seized with great fear, like a man startled by something spinning up on both sides. Whether moving or resting, one gives rise to assorted thoughts of the world's medley — such external thoughts are a difficulty for concentration. Or, on account of the concentration cultivated, one thinks oneself superior and regards others as inferior, and grows self-exalted — this too is called an assorted thought.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi walks the eleven. Doubt: not clearly knowing the light-or-form mark, uncertainty dogs the contemplation. Non-attention: slack, lazy effort that fails to hold the object. Not wanting to attend to forms: turning away from the very object one should hold, like shutting one's eyes or turning one's back. Bodily heaviness: the fruit of failing to guard the senses, eat in measure, and keep the concentration-provisions. Dullness-and-sleep: too much or too little sleep clouds the mind, so forms cannot be seen, or only faintly. Excessive exertion: forcing effort against that dullness. Extreme sluggishness: the rebound — exhausted by over-forcing, the mind goes limp; his image is catching a quail, gripped too hard it is crushed and too loose it escapes, exactly as concentration is lost by effort too tense or too slack.

The last four turn on reactions to success. Elation: seeking only light, one gets light and form together — "hoping for one, getting two" — and leaps with joy like a man stumbling on two buried treasures, the excitement itself breaking the concentration. Great fear: suddenly seeing inauspicious forms — spectres — in every direction, terror seizes one like something lunging up on both sides. Assorted external thoughts: in activity or rest, stray worldly thoughts of sights, sounds, music, and letters — matters outside the Dharma and outside the practice — intrude and disturb. And self-exaltation: with a little pliancy won, one judges oneself superior and others inferior, the conceit a clinging to one's own attainment that is itself counted among the assorted thoughts. All eleven, Cháng Bǎi notes, are ways the object-mark or the causal mark slips, so that the concentration can no longer be sustained.

31. The Last Two Difficulties, and How They Undo a Concentration

The remaining two obstacles — wearying the body with talk or prolonged pondering, and abandoning the inner work to stare at the forms the concentration reveals — complete the eleven; the treatise then states plainly how all of them undo a concentration.

Chinese Source

或多言論，或久尋思，令身疲勞，心不得定，如是多言，與定為難。若從定生光明之相，及見色時，便捨內修相續作意，願樂於外諦視眾色，故極思察，與定為難。如是諸難，隨其所應，障三摩地所緣境相及因緣相。或有遇此，退失所緣、因緣相故，如其次第，二相俱沒。

Ru-Yi Translation

Or, by much talk or prolonged pondering, the body is wearied and the mind cannot settle — such much-talking is a difficulty for concentration. If, when light arises from the concentration and forms are seen, one abandons the continuous inner cultivation and, delighting in the outward, stares intently at the forms — this over-scrutiny is a difficulty for concentration. All such difficulties, each as fits, obstruct the object-mark and the causal mark of concentration; and some, meeting them, lose the object-mark and the causal mark, so that in sequence both marks sink away.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi completes the eleven with two more: much talk or prolonged discursive pondering, which tires the body until the mind cannot settle; and, once the concentration yields its light and reveals forms, abandoning the sustained inner cultivation to gaze outward at those forms in fascination — an over-scrutiny that pulls one out of the absorption. His concluding point is the treatise's own: every one of the eleven strikes at exactly the two marks that hold a concentration together — the object-mark (the image the mind holds) and the causal mark (the concentration-provisions of moderate eating, sense-restraint, precepts, wakeful practice, and clear comprehension). Where a difficulty takes hold, one or both marks is lost, and when they go, the concentration sinks with them.

32. Concentration by Its Feeling, and the Four Aims of Cultivating Concentration

Two more classifications: concentration sorted by the feeling that accompanies it (joy, bliss, equanimity), and the celebrated fourfold sorting by why one cultivates it — for present ease, for knowledge-and-vision, for discerning wisdom, and for exhausting the outflows.

Chinese Source

復次，云何喜俱行三摩地？謂初二靜慮諸三摩地。云何樂俱行三摩地？謂第三靜慮諸三摩地。云何捨俱行三摩地？謂第四靜慮已上諸三摩地。復次，云何修定為得現法樂住？謂於四種現法樂住方便道中所有修定，及未圓滿清淨鮮白諸根本地所有修定。云何修定為得智見？謂諸苾芻，於光明相，殷勤懇到審諦而取，如經廣說。當知此在能發天眼前方便道所有修定。此中天眼，於諸色境能照、能觀，說名為見；能知諸天如是名字、如是種類，乃至廣說，如勝天經，是名為智。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, what is concentration accompanied by joy? The concentrations of the first and second dhyānas. Accompanied by bliss? The concentrations of the third dhyāna. Accompanied by equanimity? The concentrations of the fourth dhyāna and above. Furthermore, what is cultivating concentration for a pleasant abiding in this very life? The cultivation of concentration on the preparatory path toward the four pleasant abidings of this life, and the cultivation of concentration in the root grounds not yet perfectly pure and bright. What is cultivating concentration for knowledge-and-vision? Monks who, upon the mark of light, take it up earnestly, thoroughly, and rightly, as the scripture explains at length. Know that this is the cultivation of concentration on the preparatory path that can generate the divine eye. Here the divine eye, illumining and contemplating form-objects, is called "vision"; knowing the gods' such names, such kinds, and so on at length — as in the Supreme-Gods Sūtra — is called "knowledge."

Chinese Source

云何修定生分別慧？謂諦現觀預流果向方便道中所有修定，或為修習諸無礙解。云何修定為盡諸漏？謂阿羅漢果方便道中所有修定。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is cultivating concentration for giving rise to discerning wisdom? The cultivation of concentration on the preparatory path of the direct realization of the truths, toward the fruit of stream-entry; or for cultivating the analytic knowledges. What is cultivating concentration for exhausting the outflows? The cultivation of concentration on the preparatory path toward the fruit of arhatship.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi first sorts concentration by its accompanying feeling — joy accompanies the first and second dhyānas, bliss the third, and equanimity the fourth and above. Then he lays out the famous fourfold cultivation-of-concentration, sorted by its aim (from the Āgama, and matched in the Viniścaya to four kinds of practitioner pursuing four fruits). For a present pleasant abiding: cultivating the threshold and root dhyānas to reach the "four pleasant abidings in this very life" — the distinct happiness each dhyāna delivers (joy-and-bliss from seclusion, joy-and-bliss from concentration, sublime bliss, and pure equanimous stillness) — and to purify a root dhyāna still stained by relish, conceit, view, or doubt; he notes this is stressed over the "future" pleasant abiding because present ease is what even the unwise and the non-Buddhist believe in, and because a present dhyāna, when it arises, unfailingly delivers its happiness. For knowledge-and-vision: cultivating the light-perception on the path to the divine eye — "vision" being the eye that illumines and sees form-objects, "knowledge" being what it learns of the gods (their names, kinds, food, and lifespan, the classical eight ways a being's life is described).

For discerning wisdom: cultivating concentration on the path toward stream-entry — the sound thinking that in daily life knows each act and each feeling for what it is (knowing, while dressing, that one dresses; while walking, that one walks) — or to develop the four analytic knowledges of dharma, meaning, language, and eloquence. For exhausting the outflows: cultivating the vajra-like concentration of the final moment before arhatship, so hard and sharp it severs the innate afflictions at their root. The same absorption, Cháng Bǎi observes, serves radically different ends depending on the aim one brings to it — from a pleasant rest in this life all the way to the total exhaustion of every outflow.

33. The Five Noble Knowledges: How a Noble One Verifies His Own Concentration

The five-noble-knowledge concentration is a set of five self-verifying reflections a meditator makes about his own uncontaminated absorption — knowing its nature, who resorts to it, its purity, its fruit, and his own mindful entering and exiting — each answering to a phrase of the sūtra.

Chinese Source

謂我此三摩地，是聖、無染、無執，廣說如經。此中示現五行相智，謂自體智、補特伽羅智、清淨智、果智、入出定相智。聖者，善故名聖，又無漏故名聖。無染者，顯善聖性。無執者，顯無漏聖性。非凡夫所近者，謂諸佛及聖弟子所親近故。是聰叡所讚者，謂即彼所稱讚故。是諸聰叡同梵行者常不訶毀者，謂一切時常稱讚故。非如世間初靜慮等，為背下地修方便故，先以靜相而稱讚之；為趣上地修方便故，後以麤相而復訶毀。

Ru-Yi Translation

"This concentration of mine is noble, undefiled, ungrasping," and so on as the scripture explains at length. Herein five aspect-knowledges are displayed: self-nature knowledge, person knowledge, purity knowledge, fruit knowledge, and entering-and-exiting knowledge. "Noble": because wholesome it is called noble, and because uncontaminated it is called noble. "Undefiled" shows its wholesome noble nature; "ungrasping" shows its uncontaminated noble nature. "Not resorted to by ordinary beings" — because it is resorted to by the buddhas and the noble disciples. "Praised by the wise" — because they extol it. "Never censured by the wise, one's fellow practitioners" — because at all times they praise it — unlike the worldly first dhyāna and the rest, which, to turn away from the lower ground, one first praises by its calm mark, and then, to advance to the higher ground, censures by its coarse mark.

Chinese Source

寂靜者，所治煩惱永寂靜故。微妙者，自地煩惱不愛味故。得安隱道者，所得之道無退轉故。證心一趣者，已得無尋無伺地故。現在安樂者，能得現法樂住故。後樂異熟者，引無餘依涅槃樂故。正念而入者，善取能入三摩地相，無忘失故。正念而出者，善取能出三摩地相，無忘失故。

Ru-Yi Translation

"Quiescent" — because the afflictions it counters are forever stilled. "Subtle" — because there is no relish for its own ground's afflictions. "Attaining a secure path" — because the path attained does not retrogress. "Realizing the mind's single course" — because one has reached the ground with neither applied nor sustained thought. "Present ease" — because it can win the pleasant abiding in this very life. "Future blissful ripening" — because it draws on the bliss of nirvāṇa-without-remainder. "Mindfully entering" — because one firmly grasps, without forgetting, the mark by which one enters the concentration. "Mindfully exiting" — because one firmly grasps, without forgetting, the mark by which one emerges from it.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi presents the five noble knowledges (五聖智) as the inward confirmations by which a noble one verifies, from within his own uncontaminated concentration, that it is the genuine kind. Self-nature knowledge: "this concentration is noble, undefiled, ungrasping" — noble because both wholesome and uncontaminated, undefiled naming its wholesome noble nature, ungrasping its uncontaminated one. Person knowledge: it is "not resorted to by ordinary beings," only by buddhas and noble disciples, "praised by the wise and never censured" — and here he draws a sharp contrast with worldly absorption, which the six-aspect contemplation must first praise as calm (to leave the lower ground) and then censure as coarse (to climb higher), whereas the noble concentration is praised at all times and never disparaged, because there is nothing coarse in it to leave behind.

Purity knowledge: it is "quiescent" (its target afflictions forever stilled), "subtle" (no relish even for its own ground's afflictions), "a secure path" (non-retrogressing), and "the mind's single course" (having reached the ground beyond applied and sustained thought). Fruit knowledge: it yields "present ease" (the pleasant abiding here and now) and "future blissful ripening" (drawing toward the bliss of nirvāṇa-without-remainder). Entering-and-exiting knowledge: one "mindfully enters and mindfully exits," firmly holding without forgetting the very marks by which the concentration is entered and left. Cháng Bǎi treats these five together as the noble practitioner's own reflective certainty — not another's testimony but his direct knowledge that his absorption is noble, pure, fruitful, and under his mindful command.

34. The Noble Five-Limb Concentration (First Limb): The Body Drenched in the First Dhyāna's Bliss

The noble five-limb concentration is the famous scriptural image in which the meditator's very body is saturated with the joy-and-bliss of each successive dhyāna. The first limb is the first dhyāna's joy-and-bliss born of seclusion — illustrated by a bath-attendant kneading a moist ball of bath-powder that is soaked through without a drop spilling.

Chinese Source

復次，云何聖五支三摩地？謂諸苾芻，即此身內離生喜樂，廣說如經。離生喜樂者，謂初靜慮地所攝喜樂。所滋潤者，謂喜所潤；遍滋潤者，謂樂所潤。遍充滿者，謂加行究竟作意位；遍適悅者，謂在已前諸作意位，由彼位中，亦有喜樂時時間起，然非久住，亦不圓滿。於此身中無有少分而不充滿者，謂在加行究竟果作意位。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, what is the noble five-limb concentration? Monks who, within this very body, [experience] the joy-and-bliss born of seclusion, as the scripture explains at length. "Joy-and-bliss born of seclusion" is the joy-and-bliss included in the first-dhyāna ground. "Drenched" means moistened by joy; "pervadingly drenched" means moistened by bliss. "Wholly filled" refers to the consummation-of-means attention; "pervadingly gladdened" refers to the earlier attention-stages, in which joy and bliss also arise from time to time, but do not last and are not complete. "Not the least part of the body left unfilled" refers to the fruit-of-the-consummation-of-means attention.

Chinese Source

譬如黠慧能沐浴人，或彼弟子者，當知喻於修觀行者。銅器、瓦器、或蚌蛤器者，喻為離欲生喜樂故，教授教誡。細沐浴末者，喻能順彼出離尋等。水澆灌者，當知喻於尋清淨道。沐浴摶者，以喻於身。帶津膩者，喻喜和合；膩所隨者，喻樂和合；遍內外者，喻無間隙喜樂和合。不彊者，喻無散動。

Ru-Yi Translation

The skilled bath-attendant, or his pupil, should be known to illustrate the practitioner of contemplation. The copper, clay, or shell vessel illustrates the instruction and admonition [given] for the sake of the joy-and-bliss born of leaving desire. The fine bath-powder illustrates what conforms to the applied thought of renunciation and the rest. The water poured over it should be known to illustrate the purifying path of applied thought. The kneaded ball of bath-powder illustrates the body. Its being moist and greasy illustrates the union of joy; its being suffused with grease illustrates the union of bliss; its being so throughout, within and without, illustrates the seamless union of joy and bliss. Its not falling apart illustrates the absence of scattering and disturbance.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi introduces the noble five-limb concentration (聖五支三摩地) as the celebrated Āgama description in which the meditator's very body is drenched in the happiness of each dhyāna in turn — the four dhyānas forming four limbs, with a fifth "reviewing sign" completing the set. In the first limb, the first dhyāna's joy-and-bliss born of seclusion "drenches" the body (moistened by joy) and "pervadingly drenches" it (moistened by bliss). He draws out the precise gradation the treatise marks: at the earlier attention-stages the body is only "pervadingly gladdened," joy and bliss arising intermittently, neither lasting nor complete; at the consummation-of-means attention it is "wholly filled"; and at the fruit-of-the-consummation attention "not the least part of the body is left unfilled" — the finished absorption saturating the whole frame.

The soap-ball simile he maps point for point: the skilled bath-attendant (or his pupil) is the practitioner of contemplation; the copper, clay, or shell vessel is the teacher's instruction and admonition that produce the seclusion-born joy-and-bliss; the fine bath-powder is what conforms to the applied thought of renunciation; the water poured over it is the purifying path of applied thought; the kneaded ball of powder is the body itself; its being moist and greasy through and through, within and without, without a drop spilling, is joy and bliss uniting seamlessly through the body; and its holding together and not falling apart is the mind's undistracted one-pointedness. The same figure, he notes, will now be varied for each of the higher dhyānas' limbs.

35. The Second, Third, and Fourth Limbs: The Same Bliss, Varied by Dhyāna

The drenching-figure is varied for the higher dhyānas: a mountain spring for the second, submerged lotuses for the third, and a man wrapped head to foot in white cloth for the fourth — each picturing how that level's happiness pervades the body.

Chinese Source

又於第二喻有差別。山者，喻於無尋伺定；尖頂者，喻於第二靜慮無尋無伺，於所緣境一味勝解；泉者，喻於內等淨支；水軸者，謂水傍流出，水索者，謂水上涌出，此二種喻，如其次第，顯示喜、樂滋潤等言，如前解釋；無不充滿者，當知喻於無間相應。又於第三喻有差別。如嗢鉢羅等離喜之樂，彼相應法及所依身，當知亦爾；水喻離喜無尋伺定，喜發踊躍，由無彼故，喻華胎藏沒在水中。

Ru-Yi Translation

Again, for the second there is a difference in the simile. The mountain illustrates the concentration without applied and sustained thought; its peak illustrates the second dhyāna's single-flavored conviction toward its object, free of applied and sustained thought; the spring illustrates the limb of inner clarity; the water flowing out sideways and the water welling up illustrate, in order, the drenching by joy and by bliss, as explained before; and "nothing left unfilled" illustrates the uninterrupted correspondence [with the body]. Again, for the third there is a difference. As the utpala lotus and the rest [are soaked] in the bliss apart from joy, so too are its associated factors and the body that supports them; the water illustrates the concentration apart from joy, free of applied and sustained thought, and, because joy's leaping exultation is absent, it is illustrated by the flower-buds submerged beneath the water.

Chinese Source

又於第四喻有差別。清淨心者，謂與捨念清淨相應，超過下地諸災患故；鮮白者，謂性是善，自地煩惱無愛味故。……此定亦爾，其心清淨鮮白周遍，一切散動所不能侵，堪忍寒暑乃至他所呵叱惡言，及內身中種種苦受。

Ru-Yi Translation

Again, for the fourth there is a difference. "A purified mind" means associated with pure equanimity-and-mindfulness, having transcended the lower grounds' calamities; "bright-white" means its nature is wholesome, with no relish for its own ground's afflictions. … This concentration too is such: its mind pure, bright-white, and all-pervading, which no scattering can invade; it endures cold and heat, and even others' scolding and abuse, and every kind of painful feeling within the body.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi shows the same figure re-cast for each higher limb. For the second dhyāna, a mountain spring: the mountain is the thoughtless concentration, its peak the single-flavored conviction of the second dhyāna (past applied and sustained thought), the spring the "inner clarity" limb, and the water welling out sideways and upward the joy and bliss soaking the body without a gap. For the third dhyāna, lotuses growing soaked while their buds stay submerged beneath the water: the bliss-apart-from-joy pervades the body, and the submerged buds picture the absence of joy's leaping exultation — for the third dhyāna keeps bliss but has released joy.

For the fourth dhyāna, a man wrapped head to foot in a densely-woven white cloth: the pure equanimity-and-mindfulness that has transcended the lower grounds' three cosmic calamities and eight movements, and that is "bright-white" (wholesome, with no relish for its own afflictions), so pervading and firm that no scattering can invade it — enduring cold, heat, abuse, and bodily pain unmoved. He adds the treatise's further figure of the discerning elder for the accomplished fourth-dhyāna practitioner: thorough in all he does, free of heedlessness, deliberative in weighing and observing, and knowing every increase and decrease — his mind quick and clear.

36. The Fifth Limb, and What Makes This Concentration "Noble"

The fifth limb breaks the dhyāna pattern: it is the reviewing of dependent origination — examining the formations of the three times, and examining even the examining. The treatise then defines what makes the whole a "noble" concentration and why it has exactly five limbs.

Chinese Source

又於第五喻有差別。於所觀相殷勤懇到等，當知已如前釋。謂審觀察三世諸行，於能觀察又復觀察，是此中總義。

Ru-Yi Translation

Again, for the fifth there is a difference in the simile. "Earnestly and thoroughly taking up the mark contemplated," and so on, should be known as already explained [in fascicle 11]. Its general sense here is: closely contemplating the formations of the three times, and, upon the very act of contemplating, contemplating again.

Chinese Source

何等名為聖三摩地？云何建立五支差別？謂四靜慮中，所有聖賢心一境性，及於安立審諦觀察，如是名為聖三摩地。依於四種現法樂住，建立四支；依審觀察緣起法故，又為斷除餘結縛故，建立第五。當知此中二因緣故，建立五支。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is a noble concentration? How are the five limbs established? In the four dhyānas, the noble ones' one-pointedness of mind, and their close, careful contemplation of what is established — this is a noble concentration. On the four pleasant abidings in this very life, the four limbs are established; on the close contemplation of dependent origination, and further, on the severing of the remaining fetters and bonds, the fifth is established. Know that by these two conditions the five limbs are established.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the fifth limb departs from the four-dhyāna pattern: it is the reviewing limb — the very reviewing-mark given back in fascicle 11 (the monk who, earnestly, thoroughly, and without distortion, takes up the object and examines it) — here meaning the close contemplation of the formations of the three times (the twelve links of dependent origination, and the aggregates as impermanent, suffering, empty, and selfless), and, further, examining even the act of examining itself (the after-mind reviewing the fore-mind).

He then draws out what makes the whole a "noble" concentration: the noble ones' one-pointedness of mind resting in the four dhyānas, joined to their careful investigation of what is established — that is, calm wedded to insight. And why five limbs: four are built on the four "pleasant abidings in this very life" (the distinct happiness of each dhyāna), and the fifth on two further grounds — the close contemplation of dependent origination and the severing of whatever fetters and bonds still remain (even a stream-enterer, having cut the view-path afflictions, still has the subtle cultivation-path afflictions to remove). So the noble five-limb concentration is a noble one, stream-enterer and above, resting in the four dhyānas, reviewing dependent origination, and cutting the residual afflictions — calm, insight, and severance in a single practice; the Śāriputra-Abhidharma promises that its accomplishment opens the six superknowledges at will.

37. The Noble Right Concentration, With Cause and Equipment: Five Factors That Lead In

The treatise presents the eightfold path organized around right concentration at its center: the other seven factors serve it as "cause" and "equipment." Five are its cause — right view, thought, speech, action, and livelihood — which lead in sequence from first hearing of the arhats to going forth and purifying one's life.

Chinese Source

復次，云何有因有具聖正三摩地？當知善故，及無漏故，說名為聖。有五道支名此定因，所謂正見、正思惟、正語、正業、正命。具有三種，所謂正見、正精進、正念。此中薄伽梵總說前七道支，與聖正三摩地為因為具。隨其所應，差別當知：謂由前導次第義故，立五為因；於三摩地資助義故，立三為具。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, what is the noble right concentration with cause and with equipment? Know that because it is wholesome and because it is uncontaminated, it is called noble. Five path-factors are named the cause of this concentration: right view, right thought, right speech, right action, and right livelihood. Its equipment is threefold: right view, right effort, and right mindfulness. Here the Blessed One broadly declared the prior seven path-factors to be the cause and the equipment of the noble right concentration; their difference should be known accordingly — because of the sense of leading-in-sequence, five are set up as the cause; because of the sense of assisting the concentration, three are set up as the equipment.

Chinese Source

云何正見等前導次第義？謂先了知世間實有真阿羅漢正行正至，便於出離深生樂欲，獲得正見。次復思惟：何當出離居家迫迮，乃至廣說。從此出家，受學尸羅，修治淨命，是名正語、正業、正命。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the sense in which right view and the rest lead in sequence? First one comes to know that in the world there truly are real arhats of right conduct and right attainment, and so generates a deep desire for release, gaining right view. Next one reflects: "When shall I leave the cramp of household life?" and so on at length. Thereupon one goes forth, takes up the training of the precepts, and purifies one's livelihood — this is right speech, right action, and right livelihood.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that the "noble right concentration with cause and equipment" (有因有具聖正三摩地) is the whole eightfold path seen from the standpoint of right concentration at its center, the other seven factors serving it in two roles. It is "noble" because wholesome and uncontaminated. Five factors are its cause (因) — right view, thought, speech, action, and livelihood — and three its equipment or support (具) — right view, effort, and mindfulness; right view is counted in both roles. "Cause" names these factors as what leads in sequence toward concentration; "equipment" names them as what directly assists it.

He traces the leading-sequence as a life's actual course into practice. Right view begins the moment one learns that there truly are arhats in the world who have walked the path of right conduct to its right attainment, and so conceives a deep wish for release. Right thought follows as the resolve to leave the cramp and confinement of household life. And right speech, right action, and right livelihood follow as the acts of going forth, undertaking the training of the precepts, and purifying one's means of living — the moral foundation that must be laid before concentration proper can arise. So the five are "cause" in the plain sense that they come first and carry one forward to the meditative center.

38. The Equipment That Assists It: Right View, Right Effort, Right Mindfulness

The three "equipment" factors are those that directly assist the concentration: right view maturing through the three wisdoms until it can tell wrong from right in each path-factor, right effort rousing the severance, and right mindfulness accomplishing it — mindfulness being itself a limb of concentration.

Chinese Source

此正見等，於所對治邪見等五猶未能斷。還即依止此五善法，從他聞音，展轉發生聞慧正見。為欲斷除所治法故，又為修習道資糧故，方便觀察。次依聞慧發生思慧，復依思慧發生修慧。

Ru-Yi Translation

This right view and the rest cannot yet sever the five to be countered — wrong view and the rest. Still relying on these five wholesome factors, and hearing the teaching from another, one progressively gives rise to the right view of hearing-wisdom. In order to sever what is to be countered, and to cultivate the provisions for the path, one skillfully investigates: then, upon hearing-wisdom, reflection-wisdom arises, and upon reflection-wisdom, cultivation-wisdom.

Chinese Source

由此正見，於諸邪見，如實了知此是邪見；於諸正見，如實了知此是正見；乃至正命，如實知已。為欲斷除邪見等故，及為圓滿正見等故，發勤精進。若由此故，能斷所治，集能治法，令其圓滿，是名正念。此念即是三摩地分，故亦兼說正三摩地。

Ru-Yi Translation

By this right view, one knows wrong views as they are — "this is wrong view" — and right views as they are — "this is right view" — up through right livelihood, known as it is. In order to sever wrong view and the rest, and to fulfill right view and the rest, one rouses diligent effort. If by this one can sever what is to be countered and gather the countering factors to fullness, this is right mindfulness. This mindfulness is itself a concentration-factor, so right concentration too is implied along with it.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains the "equipment" sense — how right view, right effort, and right mindfulness directly assist the concentration. The five "cause" factors, once laid down, have not yet actually severed their opposites (wrong view and the rest); so, resting on those five wholesome factors and hearing the teaching from a teacher, one develops the right view of hearing-wisdom, then, by skillful investigation aimed at severing the opposites and gathering the path's provisions, reflection-wisdom, and then cultivation-wisdom. Matured through the three wisdoms, right view can now discern wrong from right in every factor — "this is wrong view, this is right view," on up through right livelihood.

Right effort is the diligence roused on that basis, both to sever the wrong factors and to bring the right ones to completion. Right mindfulness is what actually accomplishes the work — severing what is to be countered and gathering the countering factors to fullness — and because mindfulness is itself a limb of concentration, naming it already carries right concentration along with it. So the eightfold path, Cháng Bǎi stresses, is one integrated movement rather than eight separate items: right view leading, right effort and right mindfulness assisting, and all of them converging on right concentration at the center — the reason the Buddha called the seven the "cause and equipment" of the noble right concentration.

39. When Right Concentration Is Fulfilled, and the Three Trainings Complete Together

The treatise marks the two stages at which right concentration is "fulfilled," then folds the whole eightfold path into the three trainings — precepts, mind, and wisdom — all completed at once in cultivating the noble right concentration.

Chinese Source

若是時中，捨邪見等令不復生，修正見等令得圓滿；即於如是方便道中，亦能棄捨邪精進念，兼能修滿正精進念。若於是時，於彼諸法能斷能滿；即於此時，聖正三摩地亦得圓滿。

Ru-Yi Translation

When, at that time, one abandons wrong view and the rest so they arise no more, and cultivates right view and the rest to fullness — then, on that very preparatory path, one is also able to cast off wrong effort and wrong mindfulness, and to cultivate right effort and right mindfulness to fullness. When, at that time, one can sever and can fulfill those factors, then at that very time the noble right concentration too is fulfilled.

Chinese Source

此中由慧為導首，於增上戒先自安處。次聞他音、如理作意，及增上戒學并為依止，於方便道中，發生增上心學及增上慧學。此中正念，名增上心學；正見、正精進，名增上慧學。如是三學，於修聖正三摩地時，皆得圓滿。

Ru-Yi Translation

Herein, with wisdom as the leader, one first settles oneself in the higher training in precepts. Next, with hearing the teaching, sound attention, and the higher training in precepts together as its basis, on the preparatory path one gives rise to the higher training in mind and the higher training in wisdom. Here right mindfulness is called the higher training in mind; right view and right effort are called the higher training in wisdom. These three trainings are all fulfilled at the time of cultivating the noble right concentration.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi marks two stages of "fulfillment." At the preparatory stage one is still in the act of casting off wrong view, wrong effort, and wrong mindfulness while bringing the right ones to fullness; at the fulfillment stage, when that severing-and-fulfilling is complete, the noble right concentration itself stands complete. He then folds the whole eightfold path into the three trainings. Wisdom leads (right view), so one first settles into the higher training in precepts — right speech, action, and livelihood. Then, on that moral base together with hearing the teaching and sound attention, the higher trainings in mind and in wisdom arise: right mindfulness is the higher training in mind (concentration), and right view with right effort the higher training in wisdom. So cultivating the noble right concentration is nothing other than fulfilling all three trainings at once — precepts, concentration, and wisdom perfected together, the eightfold path complete as a single integrated attainment.

40. The Vajra-Like Concentration: The Blow That Ends the Trainee's Path

The vajra-like concentration is the trainee's very last moment — the instant before arhatship. The treatise gives the cluster of reasons it is named for the diamond-thunderbolt, and the exact simile: as vajra is scratched by no gem yet cuts them all, so this concentration is broken by no affliction yet shatters them all.

Chinese Source

復次，云何金剛喻三摩地？謂最後邊學三摩地。此三摩地最第一故，最尊勝故，極堅牢故，上無煩惱能摧伏故，摧伏一切諸煩惱故，是故此定名金剛喻。譬如金剛，其性堅固，諸末尼等不能穿壞，穿壞一切末尼寶等。此定亦爾，故喻金剛。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, what is the vajra-like concentration? The concentration at the very last stage of a trainee. Because this concentration is the foremost, the most excellent, and the utterly firm; because no affliction above it can crush it, and it crushes all afflictions — for these reasons this concentration is called "vajra-like." Just as vajra, firm by nature, cannot be pierced or broken by any maṇi-jewel and the rest, yet pierces and breaks all maṇi-jewels — this concentration too is such, and so is likened to vajra.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the vajra-like concentration (金剛喻三摩地) as the concentration of a trainee's very last moment — the instant before arhatship, when the innate cultivation-path afflictions are severed at their root. He gathers the reasons the treatise gives for the name: it is the foremost and most excellent of concentrations, utterly firm, unassailable by any affliction (nothing "above" it can crush it), and able to crush every affliction beneath it. The simile is exact: as vajra — the diamond-hard "thunderbolt" — is so firm that no other gem can so much as scratch it, while it cuts through them all, so this concentration cannot be broken by any remaining affliction yet shatters them all. It is the decisive blow that ends the trainee's path and opens arhatship — the same vajra concentration named earlier (in fascicles 5 and 11) as the aspirant's final instant, here given its own dedicated treatment among the scriptures' concentrations.

41. The Five Direct-Seeing Attainments: Impurity, Consciousness's Flow, and Severance

Cultivated only by one who has already seen the truths, the five direct-seeing attainments counter the subtle cultivation-path afflictions through a graded contemplation of one's own body and mind — its impurity, the ceaseless flow of consciousness, and the actual reach of one's own liberation.

Chinese Source

復次，云何五現見三摩鉢底？謂諸苾芻，即於此身等，廣說如經。已見諦者修此等至，是故名為現見等至。是諸修道所斷煩惱制伏對治、斷滅對治，及觀察斷，當知此中總略體性。初不淨觀，方便念住以為依止，為令欲貪不現行故，觀察內身種種不淨。第二不淨觀，即彼念住以為依止，乃至觀察骨人之相，為令彼貪不現行故，觀察此身種種不淨；當知齊此，名具觀察一切不淨。後不淨觀，通達法性，觀察此身有如是法，有如是性，乃至廣說。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, what are the five direct-seeing attainments? Monks who, within this very body, [contemplate], as the scripture explains at length. One who has already seen the truths cultivates these attainments, so they are called direct-seeing attainments. Their general nature here is the countering-by-subduing, the countering-by-severing, and the contemplation-of-severance of the afflictions abandoned on the path of cultivation. The first impurity contemplation, resting on the preparatory foundation of mindfulness, contemplates the manifold impurities of the internal body, so that craving-desire may not manifest. The second, resting on that same foundation, contemplates on up to the mark of the skeleton, so that that craving may not manifest — know that this far is called the complete contemplation of all impurity. The later impurity contemplation penetrates the dharma-nature: contemplating that this body has such a nature, such a character, and so on at length.

Chinese Source

觀識流轉者，觀察此識生滅相續。或觀生身展轉相續，謂麤觀察行緣識等；或觀剎那展轉相續，謂細觀察若有貪心、離貪心等品類差別，荏苒過度彼彼日夜、剎那、臘縛、牟呼栗多，於其中間非一眾多種種心識異生異滅。觀察有學未離欲者，俱住二世；已離欲者，唯住他世；阿羅漢果，俱無所住。如是名為觀察於斷。

Ru-Yi Translation

Contemplating the flow of consciousness: contemplating this consciousness's arising-and-perishing continuity — either contemplating the successive continuity of the reborn body (the coarse contemplation of formations conditioning consciousness and the rest), or contemplating momentary succession (the fine contemplation of the varied kinds of mind — with craving, freed of craving, and so on — as, through the passing days and nights, moments and hours, not one but many manifold mind-consciousnesses arise diversely and perish diversely). Contemplating severance: a trainee not yet free of desire dwells in both worlds; one already free of desire dwells only in the other world; at the fruit of arhatship, one dwells in neither. This is called the contemplation of severance.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that the five direct-seeing attainments (五現見三摩鉢底) are cultivated only by one who has already seen the truths — hence "direct-seeing" — to counter the subtle cultivation-path afflictions by subduing them, severing them, and contemplating that severance. They proceed through a graded contemplation of one's own present body and mind. First the impurity of the internal body, to keep sexual craving from arising; then the external impurity, up through the bare skeleton, completing the survey of "all impurity"; then a deeper penetration of the dharma-nature, seeing that the body has just this nature and character by its very constitution.

Then the flow of consciousness: its ceaseless arising and perishing, contemplated coarsely as the twelve-link succession from one life-body to the next (formations conditioning consciousness), and finely as the momentary succession in which, across the passing hours, countless distinct mind-moments — now with craving, now free of it — arise and perish one after another. And finally the contemplation of severance, keyed to how far one has come: a trainee not yet free of desire still dwells in "both worlds" (this life and the next); one free of desire dwells "only in the other world" (no more desire-realm rebirth); and an arhat dwells in neither — no further rebirth at all. The five together are the noble one's direct seeing of his body's impurity, his mind's impermanence, and the exact reach of his own liberation.

42. The No-Perception Attainment

The mastery- and totality-attainments having been covered earlier, the treatise turns to the no-perception attainment — a stilling of mind and mental factors reached by treating perception itself as a disease, with a sharp distinction between cultivating it in this life and being reborn by it.

Chinese Source

勝處等至、遍處等至，如前已說。復次，云何無想三摩鉢底？謂已離遍淨欲，未離上欲，永出離想作意為先，諸心心法滅。問：以何方便入此等至？答：觀想如病、如癰、如箭，入第四靜慮，修背想作意，於所生起種種想中厭背而住，唯謂無想寂靜微妙，於無想中持心而住。如是漸次離諸所緣，心便寂滅。於此生中，亦入亦起。若生於彼，唯入不起。其想若生，便從彼沒。

Ru-Yi Translation

The mastery-attainments and the totality-attainments are as already explained. Furthermore, what is the no-perception attainment? For one who has left the desire of the Universally-Pure [third dhyāna] but not the higher desire, taking as its precondition the attention of permanent escape from perception, the minds and mental factors cease. Question: by what means does one enter this attainment? Answer: contemplating perception as a disease, a boil, an arrow, one enters the fourth dhyāna and cultivates the attention of turning back from perception, wearying of and turning away from the various perceptions that arise, dwelling [in the thought] "only the perception-less is quiescent and subtle," holding the mind in the perception-less. Thus, gradually leaving all objects, the mind becomes quiescent. In this present life one both enters and emerges from it; but if reborn there, one only enters and does not emerge — and when perception arises, one falls from there.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that the no-perception attainment (無想三摩鉢底) belongs to one who has left the third dhyāna's craving but not the fourth's; taking as its precondition the resolve to escape perception permanently, it stills the minds and mental factors. Its method: contemplating perception itself as a disease, a boil, an arrow, one enters the fourth dhyāna and cultivates the "turning-back-from-perception" attention — wearying of every perception that arises and holding the mind on the single thought that "only the perception-less is quiescent and subtle," until, leaving object after object, the mind falls quiet.

He draws the crucial distinction between cultivating it in this life and being reborn by it: cultivated here, one both enters and emerges; but one reborn by it into the No-Perception Heaven only enters — and the moment perception returns, one falls from that heaven. The tradition treats this as a meditative dead-end, mistaken by some non-Buddhists for liberation, since it merely suppresses perception without any insight into no-self; when its long lifespan runs out and perception resumes, rebirth in the lower realms follows. He notes the mastery- and totality-attainments named here were already treated under the eight masteries and ten totalities.

43. The Cessation Attainment: Entering It, and the Order in Which the Formations Still

The cessation attainment — the eighth liberation, the deepest of all — stills mind and mental factors entirely. The treatise gives its two entry-routes, the two things indispensable to it (calm and insight), and the order in which the three formations cease going in and return coming out.

Chinese Source

復次，云何滅盡三摩鉢底？謂已離無所有處欲，暫安住想作意為先，諸心心法滅。問：以何方便入此等至？答：若諸聖者，已離無所有處欲，或依非想非非想處相而入於定，或依滅盡相而入於定。依非想非非想處相而入定者，謂於此二心深生厭捨，非想非非想處進趣所緣皆滅盡故，心便寂滅。依滅盡相而入定者，亦復如是。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, what is the cessation attainment? For one who has left the desire of the sphere of nothingness, taking as its precondition the attention of temporarily abiding in the perception-less, the minds and mental factors cease. Question: by what means does one enter this attainment? Answer: a noble one who has left the desire of the sphere of nothingness enters the concentration either by way of the mark of the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, or by way of the mark of cessation. To enter by the mark of neither-perception-nor-non-perception is to generate a deep weariness and relinquishment toward its two [subtle] minds, so that, all the objects that sphere advances toward being extinguished, the mind falls quiescent. To enter by the mark of cessation is likewise.

Chinese Source

將欲趣入滅盡定時，有二種法多有所作，謂奢摩他、毗鉢舍那。……謂於此義中，八次第定名奢摩他，所有聖慧名毗鉢舍那。於此二中隨闕一種，即不能入滅盡等至；要具此二，方能趣入，是故此二多有所作。問：入滅定時，云何次第滅三種行？答：此有二種，謂行時、住時。若於行時，亦起言說，於初靜慮有此作用，有語行故。若於住時，從第二靜慮已上次第定力，彼三種行次第而滅。當知出時，由逆次第次第而起。

Ru-Yi Translation

When about to enter the cessation attainment, two things have much to do with it: calm and insight. Here the eight successive attainments are the calm, and all the noble wisdom is the insight; lacking either of the two, one cannot enter the cessation attainment — one must possess both to enter, so these two have much to do with it. Question: on entering the cessation attainment, in what order do the three formations cease? Answer: this is of two kinds, the moving-time and the abiding-time. In the moving-time one still gives rise to speech — the first dhyāna has this function, since it has the speech-formation. In the abiding-time, by the force of the successive attainments from the second dhyāna upward, those three formations cease in order. Know that on emerging, they arise in order, in reverse.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi explains that the cessation attainment (滅盡三摩鉢底, the cessation of perception and feeling — the eighth liberation) belongs to a noble one who has left the desire of the sphere of nothingness; taking as its precondition the resolve to abide temporarily in the perception-less, it stills the minds and mental factors entirely — the deepest of all attainments, reached only by non-returners and arhats. One enters it either by way of the fourth formless sphere (deeply wearying of its two remaining subtle minds until their objects are extinguished and the mind falls quiet) or directly by the "mark of cessation." Two things are indispensable: calm (the eight successive attainments, run through in order) and insight (the noble wisdom of no-self); lacking either, one cannot enter. This is exactly what sets it apart from the no-perception attainment — the cessation attainment is the noble path's own, requiring genuine insight, not mere suppression.

He lays out the order in which the three formations still. Going in, they cease in sequence: the speech-formation (applied-and-sustained thought, still active in the first dhyāna, which alone can produce speech) drops first; then, by the power of the second dhyāna and above, the body-formation (the in-and-out breath) and the mind-formation (perception and feeling) cease in turn. Coming out, they return in the reverse order. So the whole descent into cessation and re-ascent from it is a graded quieting and re-quickening of speech, breath, and mind.

44. Two Puzzles of Cessation, and the Three Contacts on Emerging

The cessation attainment raises two classic questions — how consciousness remains in the body when all mind has ceased, and how one enters and exits with no thought of doing so — and the treatise ends it with the three "contacts" a noble one touches on emerging: unmoving, nothingness, and signless.

Chinese Source

問：滅盡定中，諸心心法并皆滅盡，云何說識不離於身？答：由不變壞諸色根中，有能執持轉識種子阿賴耶識不滅盡故，後時彼法從此得生。問：入滅定時，無有分別：我當入定，我當出定。正在定時，心寂滅故，遠離加行；將出定時，心先滅故，亦無作意。云何能入？云何能出？答：先於其心善修治故。若有諸行、諸狀、諸相，能入於定、能出於定，於彼修習、極多修習；由修習故，任運能入、任運能出。

Ru-Yi Translation

Question: in the cessation attainment, all minds and mental factors are wholly extinguished — how then is it said that consciousness is not separate from the body? Answer: because within the unspoiled material faculties there is the storehouse-consciousness, which holds the seeds of the active consciousnesses and is not extinguished; so that afterward those [active] dharmas arise again from it. Question: on entering the cessation attainment there is no thought "I shall enter, I shall exit"; while in it, the mind being quiescent, one is far from any application; and about to emerge, the mind having already ceased, there is no attention either. How can one enter, and how can one exit? Answer: because one has beforehand well cultivated the mind. If there are the practices, states, and marks by which one can enter and emerge, and one cultivates them, cultivates them exceedingly much — then by that cultivation one enters spontaneously and emerges spontaneously.

Chinese Source

云何出滅定時觸三種觸？一、不動觸，二、無所有觸，三、無相觸。謂出定時，多由三境而出於定：一、由有境，二、由境境，三、由滅境。由此三境，於出定時，如其次第觸三種觸。緣於有境而出定時，無有我慢擾動其心，謂此為我而起我慢，或計未來我當有等，乃至廣說，是故說言觸不動觸。緣於境境而出定時，無貪所有、無瞋所有、無癡所有，是故言觸無所有觸。緣於滅境而出定時，於一切相不思惟故，緣無相界，是故說言觸無相觸。

Ru-Yi Translation

How, on emerging from the cessation attainment, does one touch three contacts — the unmoving contact, the nothingness contact, and the signless contact? On emerging, one mostly emerges by way of three objects: the object that is, the object's object, and the object of cessation. By these three, on emerging, one touches the three contacts in order. Emerging by way of the object that is, no self-conceit disturbs the mind — reckoning "this is I" and giving rise to self-conceit, or reckoning "I shall exist in the future," and so on — therefore it is called touching the unmoving contact. Emerging by way of the object's object, there is no greed's having, no hatred's having, no delusion's having — therefore it is called touching the nothingness contact. Emerging by way of the object of cessation, not attending to any sign, one takes the signless realm as object — therefore it is called touching the signless contact.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the two puzzles as classic. First: if all mind and mental factors are extinguished, how can scripture say "consciousness does not leave the body"? Because the material sense-faculties remain unspoiled and hold the storehouse-consciousness (ālayavijñāna), which is not extinguished and retains the seeds of the six active consciousnesses; so when the attainment ends, mind arises again from it. He flags this as one of the classic Yogācāra proofs for the storehouse-consciousness — something must persist through total cessation for life and mind to resume, and that is the ālaya. Second: with no thought "I shall enter" or "I shall exit," and no attention either going in or coming out, how does one enter and exit at all? By prior thorough cultivation: having cultivated the practices, states, and marks that carry one in and out exceedingly much, entering and emerging become spontaneous, requiring no deliberate thought in the moment.

On emerging, one touches three contacts by three objects. By the object that is — the unmoving contact: no self-conceit stirs the mind, no "this is I," no "I shall exist in future." By the object's object — the nothingness contact: there is no greed's "having," no hatred's, no delusion's — nothing is grasped as possessed. By the object of cessation — the signless contact: attending to no sign at all, one rests on the signless realm. Cháng Bǎi reads these three as the mark of a mind rising from the deepest cessation into a peace still free of self, of affliction, and of sign — the aftertaste of the cessation attainment carried back into ordinary awareness.

Fascicle 17 · 瑜伽師地論卷第十七 · The Verses on the Essential Body of the Teaching

Fourteen Topics Distilling the Dharma's Core Content

Fascicle 17 continues directly from fascicle 16's stopping point mid-commentary on the three trainings, then turns to the third and final set of verses in this ground's account of reflection: fourteen topics — beginning with the classic teaching to avoid all evil — distilling the entire teaching's essential content into a single connected sequence of verse and commentary.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 17 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. The Essential-Body Verses Open: Avoid All Evil, Abandon All Desire

Fascicle 17 opens mid-sentence, continuing fascicle 16's prose commentary on the three trainings exactly where it left off — then, partway through, turns to the third and final set of verses in this ground's account of reflection: fourteen topics distilling the entire teaching into a single connected sequence. The first topic opens with Buddhism's most famous single verse, on avoiding evil altogether, and its own reflection on what abandoning desire actually requires.

Chinese Source

已釋意趣義聖教伽他。今當建立體義伽他。宣說法要，是名為體。開顯理趣，是名為義。嗢柁南曰：惡、說、貪、流、怖，類、譽、池、流、貪，作、劬勞、得義，論議十四種。

Ru-Yi Translation

The verses on essential intent have now been explained. Now the verses on the essential body of the teaching should be established. Proclaiming the Dharma's essentials is called the body; disclosing its underlying meaning is called its significance. A summary verse states: evil, teaching, craving, flow, dread, kind, renown, pool, flow, craving, action, toil, attainment of benefit, and discourse — fourteen topics in all.

Chinese Source

於身語意諸所有，一切世間惡莫作，由念正知離諸欲，勿親能引無義苦。

Ru-Yi Translation

Whatever belongs to body, speech, and mind — let none of the world's evil be done; through mindfulness and clear comprehension, abandon every desire; do not draw close to what brings suffering devoid of benefit.

Chinese Source

今此頌中，所言惡者，謂諸惡行。於一切種、一切因緣、一切處所所有惡行皆不應作。云何於一切種不作惡耶？謂由身語意不造眾惡故。云何於一切因緣不作惡耶？謂由貪瞋癡所生諸惡終不造作故。云何於一切處所不作惡耶？謂依有情事處及非有情事處不造眾惡故。

Ru-Yi Translation

In this verse, "evil" refers to evil conduct of every kind. Evil should never be done in any type, from any cause, or in any place whatsoever. What is meant by not doing evil in any type? Never producing evil through body, speech, or mind. What is meant by not doing evil from any cause? Never producing the evils that arise from craving, aversion, and delusion. What is meant by not doing evil in any place? Never producing evil in relation to sentient beings or to non-sentient things.

Chinese Source

云何由念正知遠離諸欲？謂斷事欲及斷煩惱欲故。云何斷事欲？謂如有一，於如來所證正法毗奈耶中得清淨信，了知居家迫迮，猶如牢獄，思求出離……由正信心，捨離家法，趣入非家，然於欲貪猶未永離。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is meant by abandoning desire through mindfulness and clear comprehension? It means severing desire's objects and severing desire's affliction. What is severing desire's objects? It means one who, gaining pure faith in the Dharma and discipline realized by the Tathāgata, recognizes household life to be as confining as a prison, and seeks to leave it... through this pure faith, one abandons household life and enters homelessness — though the craving of desire is not yet entirely gone.

Chinese Source

云何斷煩惱欲？謂彼既出家已，為令欲貪無餘斷故，往趣曠野山林，安居邊際臥具，或住阿練若處，乃至或在空閑靜室。於諸事欲所起一切煩惱欲攝妄分別貪，為對治故，修四念住。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is severing desire's affliction? It means that, having gone forth, in order to eliminate craving entirely, one goes to a wilderness or forest, settles with the most minimal bedding, dwells in a secluded place, or in a quiet, empty room; and in order to counteract every affliction of desire — every false conception of craving arising in connection with desire's objects — one cultivates the four foundations of mindfulness.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies this fourteen-topic summary verse as a genuinely condensed table of contents for everything the rest of fascicles 17-18 will unpack — evil, right teaching, craving, the flow of rebirth, the fear that drives renunciation, and so on through fourteen headings, each pointing toward a specific block of verses and commentary. He reads the opening verse itself as, in effect, the entire Buddhist ethical teaching in four lines: the famous "cease all evil" instruction, glossed here with real precision — evil is barred across every possible category (the three doors of body, speech, and mind), every possible motive (the three root afflictions of craving, aversion, and delusion), and every possible object (whether the evil concerns another sentient being, as in killing or stealing, or a non-sentient thing, as in mishandling property) — leaving no loophole through type, cause, or circumstance.

On abandoning desire, he distinguishes two layers that must both be addressed: desire's actual objects (the concrete situations of household and sensory life) and desire's underlying affliction (the mental habit of craving itself, which can persist long after the objects are physically out of reach). Renunciation — literally leaving home, recognizing ordinary household life as confining as a prison — handles the first layer, but Cháng Bǎi stresses the treatise's own honest admission that ordination alone does not extinguish craving; a newly ordained monastic still carries the mental habit inward, which is exactly why cultivating the four foundations of mindfulness (observing the body, feelings, mind, and mental objects) in a genuinely solitary setting is named as the actual remedy for the second, subtler layer. He notes the treatise goes on, in material this section compresses, to detail how a monastic re-enters a village for alms without losing this hard-won inward stillness — guarding the body from physical danger, guarding the senses from inflaming sights, and keeping mindfulness steady throughout, material the treatise itself cross-references at length to the Śrāvaka Ground's own fuller treatment. With evil conduct and desire's two layers both addressed, the treatise turns to the remainder of this first topic: what genuinely harmful austerity looks like, and why it should not be pursued either.

2. Topic One Closes: Freedom From Desire's Fruit, and the Harm of Self-Torment

Completing the first of fourteen topics, the treatise names what sustained mindfulness and clear comprehension actually accomplish — freedom from the five hindrances, culminating in the first meditative absorption — then turns to the verse's closing line: a direct warning against the non-Buddhist practice of self-mortification, mistaken by its practitioners for a shortcut to liberation.

Chinese Source

又即以彼正知而住為依止故，遠離諸蓋，身心調暢，有所堪能，熾然方便修斷寂靜。彼由如是念及正知為依止故，便能證得煩惱欲斷，遠離諸欲，乃至於初靜慮具足而住。

Ru-Yi Translation

And, resting on that same abiding in clear comprehension, one becomes free of the hindrances; body and mind grow pliant and capable, and one applies oneself with ardent effort to cultivating their cessation, and to stillness. Resting on mindfulness and clear comprehension in this way, one is able to realize the severing of desire's affliction, becoming free of desire, up to and including dwelling fully in the first meditative absorption.

Chinese Source

何等名為引無義苦？謂如有一，若諸沙門、或婆羅門，行自苦行，於現法中，以種種苦自逼自切，周遍燒惱。自謂我今由現法苦所逼惱故，解脫當苦。雖求是事而自煎逼，彼於此事終不能得，然更招集大損惱事。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is meant by bringing on suffering devoid of benefit? It refers to one — whether an ascetic or a brahmin — who practices self-torment, afflicting and scourging themselves in every way in this very life, thinking: through the suffering that afflicts me now, I will be freed from future suffering. Though seeking this goal, and tormenting themselves in pursuit of it, they never actually attain it — and instead bring upon themselves great further harm.

Chinese Source

諸聖弟子，能於如是受用自苦行邊能引非聖無義苦法善了知已，遠而避之，不親、不近，亦不承事。

Ru-Yi Translation

The noble disciples, having thoroughly understood this practice of self-torment as an ignoble teaching bringing suffering devoid of benefit, keep their distance from it, neither drawing close to it, nor associating with it, nor paying homage to those who practice it.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the transition from mindfulness to clear comprehension as marking real progress: mindfulness alone applies the four foundations as a technique, but sustained clear comprehension — continuously and accurately knowing one's own present state — is what actually clears away the five hindrances (craving, ill will, torpor, restlessness, and doubt, already met in fascicle 16's account of concentration), yielding the pliancy and capability that make genuine meditative absorption possible. He notes the verse's own logic is cumulative: mindfulness and clear comprehension together sever desire's underlying affliction, which in turn opens the first meditative absorption — not a separate achievement, but the natural fruit of the very process just described.

On the verse's closing warning, he reads self-mortification as the opposite error from indulgence, but no less mistaken: some non-Buddhist ascetics reasoned that inflicting present suffering on themselves would somehow discharge future suffering in advance, as if pain itself were a kind of currency that could be spent down — a view the treatise flatly rejects, since suffering caused this way accomplishes nothing toward actually uprooting its cause (craving and ignorance) and only adds fresh harm on top of what already exists. He notes the treatise's instruction to noble disciples is unusually direct: not merely to decline this practice personally, but to keep a real distance from those who teach or practice it, neither honoring nor associating with them, since proximity to a compelling but mistaken teaching risks drawing a practitioner toward it. With evil conduct, desire's two layers, and harmful austerity all addressed, the treatise's exposition of the opening verse is complete, and it turns to a further reflection recasting the same ground in terms of what a being's fulfillment actually requires.

3. Topic One's Summary: Two Kinds of Fulfillment Every Being Seeks

Before moving to the second topic, the treatise steps back and recasts everything just covered in terms of what any being's fulfillment actually consists in — a favorable rebirth, or decisive excellence culminating in nirvāṇa — then maps the opening verse's four lines directly onto these two goals, closing the first of the fourteen topics.

Chinese Source

復次，今當略辯上所說義。云何略辯？謂諸有情有二種滿。一、增上生滿，二、決定勝滿。增上生滿者，謂往善趣。決定勝滿者，謂愛盡、離欲、寂滅、涅槃。於此二滿及與障礙，能斷能證，是名略義。

Ru-Yi Translation

Furthermore, let what has been said above now be briefly explained. What is this brief explanation? It is that sentient beings seek two kinds of fulfillment: first, the fulfillment of a favorable rebirth; and second, the fulfillment of decisive excellence. The fulfillment of a favorable rebirth means proceeding to a good realm of rebirth. The fulfillment of decisive excellence means the exhaustion of craving, freedom from desire, cessation, and nirvāṇa. Being able to sever the obstacles to, and realize, both of these fulfillments — this is the brief explanation.

Chinese Source

若於一切種、一切因緣、一切處所不作惡行，彼便能斷增上生滿所有障礙，亦能證得增上生滿。若於受用欲樂行邊及於受用自苦行邊決定遠離，彼便能斷決定勝滿所有障礙，亦能證得決定勝滿。當知是名此中略義。

Ru-Yi Translation

If one refrains from evil conduct of every type, from every cause, in every place, one thereby severs every obstacle to the fulfillment of a favorable rebirth, and also realizes that fulfillment. If one resolutely keeps clear of both the indulgence of sensual pleasure and the practice of self-torment, one thereby severs every obstacle to the fulfillment of decisive excellence, and also realizes that fulfillment. Know that this is the brief explanation given here.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this two-fold framework — favorable rebirth and decisive excellence — as a lens the treatise will apply repeatedly across the remaining thirteen topics, worth understanding precisely here at its first appearance: a favorable rebirth is simply a good outcome within saṃsāra (human or heavenly birth, secured by ordinary ethical conduct), while decisive excellence names the qualitatively different achievement of actually exhausting craving, becoming free of desire, reaching cessation, and realizing nirvāṇa itself — the trajectory that leaves saṃsāra behind rather than merely improving one's position within it. He notes the four technical terms making up "decisive excellence" trace a single deepening process rather than four separate states: exhaustion of craving marks the end of hoping for a better future existence; freedom from desire marks the end of delighting in present sense-objects; cessation marks the ending of whatever afflictions remain beyond craving itself; and nirvāṇa, strictly speaking, names only the state without any remainder at all, after death — so that exhaustion of craving and freedom from desire describe liberation while the body still lives, while cessation and nirvāṇa proper describe its completion.

The closing correspondence he reads as elegantly exact: avoiding evil across every type, cause, and place (the verse's first two lines) removes every obstacle to a favorable rebirth and thereby secures it, while resolutely avoiding both indulgence and self-torment (the verse's last two lines) removes every obstacle to decisive excellence and thereby secures that too — meaning the single opening verse, rightly understood, already contains everything needed for both a good rebirth and full liberation. With this summary, the first of the fourteen topics on the essential body of the teaching — evil conduct and its remedy — is complete, and the treatise turns to the second topic: what should be taught, focused specifically on the five sensory pleasures and why a renunciant's happiness genuinely exceeds an indulgent one's.

4. Topic Two Opens: The Five Sensory Pleasures, and How Craving Traps a Being

The second of the fourteen topics, "what should be spoken of," takes up the five sensory pleasures directly — why they are called this, and how an ordinary being's unexamined attachment to them, left unaddressed, becomes the very engine of samsaric wandering. Four verses frame the topic; this section covers the first, and the reasoning behind why desire deserves to be spoken of at all.

Chinese Source

應說想眾生，依應說安住，不了知應說，而招集生死。

Ru-Yi Translation

Beings conceive what should be spoken of, and dwell resting upon it; not understanding what should be spoken of, they thereby accumulate birth and death.

Chinese Source

今此義中，說妙五欲以為應說。又妙五欲，諸餘沙門、婆羅門等，從施主邊以言求索，故名應說。又諸君主於妙五欲，從僕使等，以言呼召而受用之；由是因緣，亦名應說。又諸受欲者，於妙五欲不能自然善知過患，唯除諸佛及佛弟子，為其宣說彼過患已，乃能了知；由是因緣，亦名應說。

Ru-Yi Translation

Here, the sublime five sensory pleasures are what is meant by "what should be spoken of." Other ascetics and brahmins must ask for them from their donors in words — hence they are called "what should be spoken of." Rulers, too, summon their servants with words in order to enjoy them — by this same reason, they too are called "what should be spoken of." And those who indulge in them cannot, on their own, come to know their faults; only the buddhas and their disciples, by actually teaching those faults, enable this understanding — for this reason too, they are called "what should be spoken of."

Chinese Source

又於諸欲不如實知有眾過患。所謂諸欲無常、虛、偽、空無有實，敗壞之法，猶如幻事誑惑愚夫，甚少愛味，多諸過患。亦不如實了知如是少味多患諸欲出離。

Ru-Yi Translation

Nor do they truly understand desire's many faults — that desires are impermanent, hollow, false, empty of any real substance, subject to decay, deceiving the foolish just as a conjurer's illusion does, offering only the barest sweetness while carrying many faults. Nor do they truly understand how to be free of desires so slight in their sweetness and so heavy with fault.

Chinese Source

彼既如是不見過患、不知出離而受諸欲；由是因緣，便於欲界生為根本所有諸行深起樂著。又復造作生為根本所有業已，受欲界生，生已死滅，生已殞歿。

Ru-Yi Translation

Not seeing these faults, not knowing how to be free of them, one indulges in desire regardless — and by this cause, one becomes deeply attached to whatever conditioned states take rebirth in the desire realm as their root. Having performed the actions rooted in such rebirth, one takes birth in the desire realm again, and having been born, dies; having been born, perishes.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies three distinct reasons the five sensory pleasures earn the name "what should be spoken of": ordinary renunciants and brahmins must actually ask their donors for what sustains them; even a king summons servants with spoken commands to enjoy the same pleasures; and — the deepest reason — no one indulging in sense-pleasure discovers its faults on their own, since the buddhas and their disciples are the ones who must actually put those faults into words for anyone to understand them. Desire, in other words, is a topic that has to be taught, not one that teaches itself.

He traces the verse's causal chain step by step: an ordinary mind first grasps a pleasing object's overall impression, then elaborates it into ever more particular attractive features (he gives the example of noticing a person is beautiful, then admiring the eyes, then the nose, each admiration a fresh layer of the same craving); this generates attachment, indulgence, and eventually — in the treatise's own vivid vocabulary — gluttonous absorption, bewildered bemusement, and stubborn immersion, all simply different names for the same underlying craving intensifying by degrees. Because ordinary beings never see the four faults hidden beneath desire's surface — desires only appear stable but are impermanent; only appear self-directed but are without any real controlling self; only appear clean but are in fact impure; only appear pleasurable but are in fact suffering — they remain trapped by what the verse calls "a conjurer's illusion," and, not knowing the sevenfold sequence of mental cultivation that actually leads out of this trap (already introduced in fascicle 16's account of wisdom), simply continue producing the karma that keeps rebirth in the desire realm running: birth, death, and rebirth in an unbroken cycle powered by nothing more than unexamined attachment to what was never worth the cost. With the first verse's exposition complete, the treatise turns to the second: what happens once a being does come to understand.

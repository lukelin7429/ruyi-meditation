Fascicle 9 · 瑜伽師地論卷第九 · Continuing Directly from Fascicle 8

Why the Same Ten Deeds Shape Even the Fruit on the Tree

Fascicle 8 ended mid-sentence, partway through tracing karma's five stages — not a natural stopping point, simply where the physical scroll divides. This page picks up exactly there: karma's gateways to result, tracing the ten courses of action all the way from ripened destiny, through the specific life-circumstances each one produces in turn, down to the very taste and texture of the physical world a community's shared karma shapes around it.

Translator: Ru-Yi Meditation Center Status: translation in progress Source: Taishō T30, no. 1579, fascicle 9 Commentary guide: Master Cháng Bǎi's lecture notes

1. Karma's Gateways Open: Two Gateways, Five Ways to Give Result

Continuing directly from fascicle 8's break, the treatise opens karmic defilement's fifth item: the gateways through which karma actually delivers its result — first two broad gateways, then the first of these unpacked into five distinct kinds of result a single deed can produce.

Chinese Source

復次，業門云何？此略有二種。一、與果門，二、損益門。與果門者，有五種應知。一、與異熟果，二、與等流果，三、與增上果，四、與現法果，五、與他增上果。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, what are karma's gateways? There are, briefly, two: the gateway of giving result, and the gateway of harm and benefit. The gateway of giving result comprises five kinds, it should be understood: giving a ripened result; giving a result of equal outflow; giving a dominant result; giving a result in this very life; and giving a result through another's dominant influence.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies these five as directly continuing the five results already introduced back in fascicle 5's account of cause, condition, and result — now applied specifically to how the ten courses of action actually deliver their consequences. A ripened result is the rebirth-destiny itself a deed's overall karmic weight determines; a result of equal outflow is a specific life-circumstance that resembles its cause in kind, carried into the very same life a being is reborn into; a dominant result is the shared physical environment a community's collective karma shapes around itself; a present-life result is karma potent enough to ripen within this very life rather than waiting for a future one; and a result through another's dominant influence is a benefit or harm one receives purely because of someone else's karmic power — a buddha's presence in the world, for instance, bringing peace to those who merely encounter and revere him, quite apart from anything the recipient did to earn it directly.

2. Ripened Result Tied to Destiny, and the Ten-Fold Portrait of Equal Outflow

The treatise works through the first two of the five results in vivid concrete detail: ripened result, tracing intense unwholesome karma straight to the hells, and equal-outflow result, a precise ten-fold catalogue matching each unwholesome deed to the specific circumstance it reproduces in the very life it shapes.

Chinese Source

與異熟果者，謂於殺生親近、修習、多修習故，於那落迦中受異熟果。如於殺生，如是於餘不善業道亦爾。是名與異熟果。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for giving a ripened result: by closely engaging in, practicing, and repeatedly practicing killing, one experiences a ripened result in the hells. As with killing, so too with every remaining unwholesome course of action. This is called giving a ripened result.

與等流果者，謂若從彼出，來生此間人同分中，壽量短促，資財匱乏，妻不貞良，多遭誹謗，親友乖離，聞違意聲，言不威肅，增猛利貪，增猛利瞋，增猛利癡，是名與等流果。

As for giving a result of equal outflow: upon emerging from that destiny and being reborn here as a human, one experiences a shortened lifespan; poverty and lack of resources; an unfaithful spouse; frequent, groundless slander; estrangement from friends and family; constantly hearing unwelcome words; speech that carries no real authority or credibility; and an intensified, sharpened craving, hatred, and delusion. This is called giving a result of equal outflow.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi notes the treatise deliberately illustrates only the most severe grade of unwholesome karma here, leading to hell — milder and middling grades of the identical ten unwholesome deeds instead lead to an animal or hungry-ghost rebirth respectively, exactly as karma's stages already established. He then walks through the equal-outflow list as a precise one-to-one correspondence with the ten unwholesome deeds already catalogued in such detail: killing shortens the next life; theft impoverishes it; sexual misconduct produces an unfaithful spouse; false speech invites groundless slander in return; divisive speech estranges one's own friends and family; harsh speech means constantly hearing unwelcome words from others; frivolous speech (whether through wrongful accusation, wrongful teaching, or idle entertainment-talk) produces speech no one finds credible; and covetousness, ill will, and wrong view simply intensify their own kind, craving breeding sharper craving, hatred sharper hatred, delusion sharper delusion — like straightforwardly produces like, "equal outflow" naming exactly this resemblance between a cause and the result it reproduces in kind.

He tells a brief story to underscore the treatise's own point that this reversal is workable: a student once asked his teacher how to actually practice, and was told simply to watch this one thought, moment to moment, and check any unwholesome impulse the instant it arose. Taking this to a fierce extreme, the student struck his own body whenever an unwholesome thought surfaced, bruising himself badly at first — but over time his unwholesome thoughts genuinely grew rarer, his body healed, and he eventually reached genuine realization. Cháng Bǎi's point is not the extremity of the method but the underlying principle it illustrates: wholesome and unwholesome states alike carry this same self-reinforcing, "equal outflow" momentum, so consistent attention to which one is currently active, and deliberately starving the unwholesome of repetition, genuinely works.

3. Dominant Result: How Shared Karma Shapes the Very Taste of the World

The treatise turns to the third result, tracing how a community's habitual unwholesome conduct shapes not merely individual lives but the shared physical environment itself — down to the specific quality of the fruit that grows in it.

Chinese Source

與增上果者，謂由親近、修習、多修習諸不善業增上力故，所感外分光澤尠少，果不充實，果多朽敗，果多變改，果多零落，果不甘美，果不恆常，果不充足，果不便宜，空無果實。當知善業，與此相違。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for giving a dominant result: through the dominant force of closely engaging in, practicing, and repeatedly practicing the unwholesome courses of action, the external world one shares comes to bear little luster; its fruit lacks substance; its fruit frequently rots; its fruit is constantly changing and unstable; its fruit frequently falls and scatters before ripening; its fruit lacks any genuine sweetness; its yield fails to endure; its yield proves insufficient; its yield turns out unfavorable; and it may bear no fruit at all. It should be understood that wholesome karma produces the exact reverse of every one of these.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this ten-part environmental catalogue as, once again, tracking the ten unwholesome deeds in exact sequence: killing produces a world where growing things lack luster, since a habit of taking life leaves its mark even on the vitality of the land itself; theft produces insubstantial fruit, mirroring how theft leaves victims materially hollowed out; sexual misconduct, which corrupts and breaks apart families, produces fruit prone to rot; false speech, whose very nature is instability and unreliability, produces fruit that keeps unpredictably changing; divisive speech, which severs bonds between people, produces fruit that scatters and falls before it can ripen; harsh speech, inherently unpleasant, produces fruit lacking any real sweetness; frivolous speech, purposeless and inconstant, produces an unreliable yield; covetousness, which diminishes what belongs to others, produces an insufficient yield; ill will, which works against others' own wishes, produces an unfavorable yield; and wrong view, which denies cause and effect at the root, produces trees that bear no fruit at all — the most severe outcome, matching the gravity the treatise has consistently assigned wrong view throughout this entire survey.

He draws the same lesson already voiced elsewhere in this fascicle: harming others is, in the deepest sense, harming oneself, since the very world one must go on living in is shaped by exactly this same karma — good returns as good, harm returns as harm, in neither more nor less measure, which is why some classical teachers describe all beings as ultimately "one body." Seen this way, he notes, there is no one left to actually hold a grudge against.

4. Present-Life Result Opens: Eight Kinds of Resolve That Ripen Karma Early

The treatise turns to the fourth result — karma potent enough to ripen within this very life rather than waiting for a future one — opening with two general conditions that intensify a deed's power, then unpacking the first of these, the quality of one's own resolve, into eight distinct kinds.

Chinese Source

與現法果者，有二因緣，善不善業與現法果。一、由欲解故；二、由事故。應知欲解復有八種。一、有顧欲解，二、無顧欲解，三、損惱欲解，四、慈悲欲解，五、憎害欲解，六、淨信欲解，七、棄恩欲解，八、知恩欲解。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for giving a result in this very life: there are two conditions through which wholesome or unwholesome karma ripens within this present life. First, through the quality of one's resolve; second, through the significance of the object involved. Resolve, it should be understood, is of eight kinds: regard-bearing resolve, regard-free resolve, malicious resolve, compassionate resolve, hateful resolve, faithful resolve, ungrateful resolve, and grateful resolve.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the two general conditions as, respectively, the sheer intensity of intention behind a deed and the weight of who or what the deed is actually directed toward — a deed performed with genuine deliberation and conviction, or aimed at someone genuinely deserving of reverence, gratitude, or compassion, carries enough karmic force to ripen quickly rather than waiting across lifetimes. He previews the eight kinds of resolve as a spectrum running from clinging attachment to one's own body and possessions (regard-bearing, which drives unwholesome action) through its wholesome opposite (regard-free, willing to give freely without clinging), through malice and compassion as opposite poles of intention toward others, hatred and faith as opposite postures toward what one encounters, and, finally, ingratitude and gratitude as opposite responses to a debt already owed — each of the eight, he notes, will be unpacked individually as the treatise continues, showing exactly how the specific flavor of a person's resolve, not only the outward deed itself, determines how quickly its karmic weight comes due.

5. Four Kinds of Resolve, Each With Its Own Classical Story

The treatise works through the eight kinds of resolve one by one, pairing each with a vivid traditional story illustrating exactly how that resolve, at sufficient intensity, ripens its karma within the very same life.

Chinese Source

有顧欲解造不善業受現法果者，謂如有一，由增上欲解，顧戀其身、顧戀財物、顧戀諸有，造不善業。無顧欲解所造善業受現法果者，謂如有一，以增上欲解，不顧其身、不顧財物、不顧諸有，造作善業。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for regard-bearing resolve producing unwholesome karma that ripens in this life: this is when someone, through a forceful resolve, clings to their own body, their own wealth, and their own possessions, and so commits an unwholesome deed. As for regard-free resolve producing wholesome karma that ripens in this life: this is when someone, through a forceful resolve, clings to none of these, and so performs a wholesome deed.

損惱欲解造不善業受現法果者，謂如有一，於他有情補特伽羅，以增上品損惱欲解造不善業。慈悲欲解所造善業受現法果者，謂如有一，於他有情補特伽羅，以增上品慈悲欲解造作善業。

As for malicious resolve producing unwholesome karma that ripens in this life: this is when someone, toward another being, acts with a forceful, malicious intent to harm. As for compassionate resolve producing wholesome karma that ripens in this life: this is when someone, toward another being, acts with forceful compassion.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi pairs each of these first four with a classical illustration. For regard-bearing resolve clinging to one's own body: cattle-drovers, famished on a long journey, sliced the tongues from the very cattle they were herding to eat, reasoning the animals weren't truly theirs to keep anyway — and immediately afterward, in an act of terrible poetic justice, their own tongues rotted and dropped away like spoiled fruit. For malicious resolve: a violent man, scolded by his own mother for taking more than his fair share of milk from the family cow, hurled the milk in her face in fury — and immediately broke out in patches of white leprosy exactly matching where the milk had struck her.

For compassionate resolve, he tells the story of a court official, born without full manhood as the fruit of past unwholesome karma, who came upon five hundred cattle being led to be castrated; recognizing his own suffering in theirs, he purchased every one of them to spare them the same fate — and in that very moment of genuine compassion, his own body was restored, a transformation so striking the king who heard of it richly rewarded him with rank and honor on the spot. Cháng Bǎi treats all four stories as making the identical point from different directions: an act performed with sufficiently forceful resolve — whether cruel or compassionate — carries enough weight to ripen its result immediately, within this very life, rather than waiting across the ordinary span of lifetimes karma usually takes to mature.

6. The Remaining Four: Hatred and Faith Toward the Three Jewels, Ingratitude and the Woodcutter's Bear

Chinese Source

憎害欲解造不善業受現法果者，謂如有一，於佛法僧及隨一種尊重處事，以增上品憎害欲解造不善業。淨信欲解所造善業受現法果者，謂如有一，於佛法僧等，以增上品淨信欲解造作善業。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for hateful resolve producing unwholesome karma that ripens in this life: this is when someone, toward the Buddha, the Dharma, the Sangha, or any similarly revered object, acts with a forceful, hateful intent to harm. As for faithful resolve producing wholesome karma that ripens in this life: this is when someone, toward the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha, acts with forceful, genuine faith.

棄恩欲解造不善業受現法果者，謂如有一，於父母所，及隨一種恩造之處，以增上品背恩欲解、欺誑欲解、酷暴欲解造不善業。知恩欲解所造善業受現法果者，謂如有一，於父母等，以增上品知恩欲解、報恩欲解所作善業。

As for ungrateful resolve producing unwholesome karma that ripens in this life: this is when someone, toward their own parents or any similar benefactor, acts with forceful ingratitude, deception, and cruelty. As for grateful resolve producing wholesome karma that ripens in this life: this is when someone, toward their own parents and the like, acts with forceful gratitude and genuine repayment of kindness.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi illustrates hateful resolve with the story of a woodcutter hired to escort a traveling monk, who plotted with his wife to rob and kill him for the presumed silk in his belongings; the monk, sensing the danger, recited a few lines of protective chant, causing a fly to lodge in the would-be killer's nose and never leave — his face soon twisted, his brows and beard fell out, and within a year he had died in confusion and illness, the same affliction passing to his wife when the fly moved on to her. For faithful resolve, he tells of a devout girl who found dog droppings before a sacred shrine and, moved by reverence, cleaned the altar herself and perfumed it with fragrant paste — and was rewarded with a body that ever after exuded the scent of sandalwood and a mouth that spoke with the fragrance of blue lotus.

For ingratitude, Cháng Bǎi recounts one of the most beloved stories in the whole classical repertoire: a woodcutter lost in a snowstorm, near death, was found and sheltered by a great bear — in truth a bodhisattva in animal form — who warmed him, fed him, and cared for him for seven days until the weather cleared, asking only that the woodcutter someday protect the bear's own life in return, just as his had been protected. Descending the mountain, the woodcutter met hunters who offered him two-thirds of the kill if he would reveal the bear's location; he agreed, led them to his benefactor, and the bear was killed — but the instant the woodcutter reached to take his promised share of the meat, both his arms fell from his body as though sliced by an invisible blade, exposing his betrayal to the horrified hunters, who then honored the bear's remains with a proper burial and shrine.

For gratitude, he tells the story of Wang Xiang, a filial son whose stepmother, despite years of unkind treatment, fell ill one winter craving carp — and Wang Xiang, finding the river frozen solid, lay down upon the ice and bared his own body to it until, moved by his sincerity, the ice cracked open and a pair of fish leapt out on their own. Cháng Bǎi treats all eight stories together as the treatise's most memorable demonstration that the specific quality of resolve behind a deed — not merely the deed's outward form — is what determines whether its karmic weight ripens swiftly, within this very life, or waits the ordinary course of lifetimes to come due.

7. The Five Acts of Immediate Retribution, Classified in Technical Detail

The treatise turns to the second condition for a present-life result — the sheer weight of the object involved — opening with the gravest category of deed in the entire Buddhist tradition: the five acts guaranteed to bring immediate rebirth in the Avīci hell, with no intervening destiny at all.

Chinese Source

由事故者，若不善業，於五無間及彼同分中，亦有受現法果者。五無間業者，一、害母，二、害父，三、害阿羅漢，四、破僧，五、於如來所惡心出血。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for the significance of the object: unwholesome karma, when directed at the five objects of immediate retribution or their equivalent class, likewise ripens within this present life. The five acts of immediate retribution are: killing one's mother, killing one's father, killing an arhat, causing a schism in the monastic community, and, with malicious intent, drawing a tathāgata's blood.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi unpacks "immediate" (ānantarya) through its classical five-fold meaning: the destiny itself follows immediately, with certain rebirth in hell and no intervening stop in any other realm; the suffering there is immediate, unbroken by any pause or relief; its duration runs a full cosmic eon without interruption; the being's own life there runs its full term without premature death; and the very form of that hell-body fills the entire space of the hell, however many beings are present at once, without overlap or crowding — five separate senses of "without gap" converging on the single worst destiny the tradition recognizes.

He classifies the five technically: killing one's mother, one's father, or an arhat each complete the full karmic path of killing outright, since the victim actually dies; causing a schism completes the full karmic path of false speech, since it is accomplished through divisive and deceptive words rather than physical violence; and drawing a buddha's blood completes only the preparatory stage of the killing path, never its full completion, since a buddha's body — already free of any further karmic vulnerability — cannot actually be killed at all, no matter the attacker's intent. He also notes the classical distinction within causing schism: dividing the community during the Buddha's own lifetime by setting up a rival teaching (as Devadatta once did) is considered the single gravest form, since it possible only while a buddha still lives to be contradicted, while dividing an ordinary monastic assembly through a procedural dispute, though still severe, is a lesser and more commonly occurring form of the same offense.

8. Equivalent-Class Offenses: Betraying Trust, Exploiting the Vulnerable, Plundering the Sacred

Chinese Source

無間業同分者，謂如有一，於阿羅漢尼及於母所行穢染行。打最後有菩薩。或於天廟、衢路、市肆立殺羊法，流行不絕。或於寄託、得極委重、親友、同心、耆舊等所損害欺誑。或於有苦、貧窮、困乏、無依無怙為作歸依，施無畏已，後返加害，或復逼惱。或劫奪僧門。或破壞靈廟。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for offenses equivalent in class to the acts of immediate retribution: this includes committing an act of sexual defilement against a fully awakened bhikṣuṇī, or against one's own mother; striking a bodhisattva in their final body before buddhahood; establishing, at a shrine, crossroads, or marketplace, a method of ritual slaughter that then spreads and continues unchecked; betraying or deceiving someone who has entrusted oneself with great responsibility — a close friend, a like-minded companion, a respected elder; taking in someone suffering, impoverished, destitute, and utterly without support, granting them refuge and safety, and then turning to harm or further oppress them; plundering the monastic community's own property; or destroying a sacred shrine.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this list as the treatise's own account of what comes closest, in severity, to the five unforgivable acts without technically satisfying their strict definition — sexual violation of an accomplished nun or one's own mother mirrors the gravity of harming an arhat or a parent; striking a being destined for buddhahood in their very last lifetime mirrors harming a tathāgata; institutionalizing ritual slaughter that spreads across a whole community multiplies a single act of killing into an ongoing, self-perpetuating harm; and the remaining items all share a single underlying theme — betraying precisely the trust that made a person vulnerable in the first place, whether a friend's confidence, a stranger's desperate need for shelter, or a community's shared sacred property.

He treats this whole "equivalent class" category as the treatise's way of acknowledging that severity is not a matter of rigid legal technicality alone — an act that falls just short of the five acts' strict definition can still carry comparably devastating weight, particularly when it involves exploiting the very relationship of trust, refuge, or reverence that should have made a being safest. Betrayal of someone who has nowhere else to turn, he stresses, is in some ways a deeper wound than harm from a stranger, precisely because it corrupts the last resort a vulnerable person had left.

9. Converting One's Own Parents, and Honoring Those Fresh From Deep Attainment

The treatise closes present-life result with two further examples of weighty objects: leading one's own parents to genuine faith, and honoring practitioners the very moment they emerge from profound meditative states — with the identical principle reversed for harm.

Chinese Source

若諸善業由事重故受現法果者，謂如有一，母無正信，勸進開化，安置建立於具信中。如無正信，於具信中；如是犯戒，於具戒中；慳吝，於具捨中；惡慧，於具慧中亦爾。如母，父亦爾。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for wholesome karma ripening in this life because of the significance of its object: this is when someone, whose mother lacks genuine faith, guides and enlightens her, establishing her firmly in genuine faith. Just as with faith, so too with leading a mother who violates precepts into upholding them, a stingy mother into genuine generosity, and a mother of poor judgment into genuine wisdom. As with a mother, so too with a father.

或於起慈定者供養承事。如於起慈定者，如是於起無諍定、滅盡定、預流果、阿羅漢果供養承事亦爾。又親於佛所供養承事。如於佛所，如是於學無學僧所亦爾。若即於此尊重事中，與上相違，由損害因緣起不善業，受現法果。

Or one may make offerings to and attend upon someone just emerging from loving-kindness concentration. As with loving-kindness concentration, so too with someone just emerging from the concentration of non-contention, the concentration of cessation, stream-entry, or arhatship. Further, one may personally make offerings to and attend upon a buddha, and, in the same way, upon the community of noble ones still training or already beyond training. Should one instead, toward these very same objects of reverence, act with harmful intent and commit an unwholesome deed, this too ripens within this present life.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads converting one's own parents to genuine faith, ethical conduct, generosity, and wisdom as carrying exceptional weight precisely because of the parent-child bond's own gravity — repaying the debt of birth and upbringing with the far greater gift of setting a parent's own liberation in motion. He explains why the treatise specifies "just emerging" from loving-kindness concentration, non-contention, or cessation attainment rather than simply at any time: a practitioner's mind, freshly returned from such profound absorption, is at its most purified and receptive state, making an offering made at that exact moment carry unusually concentrated merit — the same underlying principle, he notes, that explains why offerings made directly to a buddha or to the noble community carry comparably immense weight. He treats the reversal — that harming these same exalted objects carries an equally intensified unwholesome result — as simply the mirror image of the same governing principle: whatever amplifies a wholesome deed's weight amplifies an unwholesome deed's weight by the identical logic, applied to the identical objects.

10. Result Through Another's Power: Why a Bodhisattva Cannot Always Save

The treatise turns to the fifth and final result — benefit or harm received purely through someone else's dominant karmic force — illustrated through a buddha's protective presence and a bodhisattva's compassionate giving, before candidly explaining the five circumstances in which even a bodhisattva's compassion cannot actually help.

Chinese Source

與他增上果者，謂亦由受現法果業。猶如如來所住國邑，必無疾疫災橫等起。佛神力故，無量眾生無疾、無疫、無有災橫，得安樂住。如佛世尊，如是轉輪聖王及住慈定菩薩亦爾。

Ru-Yi Translation

As for giving a result through another's dominant influence: this too depends on one's own karma capable of ripening in this present life. Just as the very city or country a tathāgata dwells in is certain to be free of plague and calamity, so too, through a buddha's own spiritual power, countless beings there remain free of illness, free of plague, free of disaster, and dwell in genuine peace. As with the Blessed Buddha, so too with a wheel-turning monarch, and with a bodhisattva abiding in loving-kindness concentration.

若諸菩薩以大悲心觀察一切貧窮困苦業天所惱眾生，施以飲食財穀庫藏皆令充足。由此因緣，彼諸眾生得安樂住。

If bodhisattvas, moved by great compassion, observe beings afflicted by poverty and hardship — the working of what might be called their own "karmic providence" — and provide them with food, wealth, grain, and stores until they are genuinely sufficient, then, by reason of this, those beings come to dwell in peace.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi stresses the treatise's own careful qualification here: this benefit genuinely comes from another's overwhelming spiritual power, yet it still only reaches beings who carry some corresponding karmic receptivity of their own — a nation's peace under a virtuous ruler, or under a buddha's presence, is never one-sided charity but a convergence of the great being's power and the ordinary beings' own accumulated merit meeting it halfway.

He turns to the Mahāyānasaṃgraha's own frank explanation of why even a bodhisattva's compassion cannot rescue everyone, illustrated by Maudgalyāyana's own mother, reborn as a hungry ghost — when he offered her a bowl of rice, it burst into flame the instant it touched her mouth, her own karma transforming the gift before she could ever receive it. Five specific conditions can block a bodhisattva's aid entirely: when a being's own karmic obstruction is simply too severe for any external gift to overcome; when material help would actually obstruct the wholesome states that being still needs to develop; when it would prevent a necessary sense of renunciation from ever arising — someone whose poverty alone might finally turn them toward genuine practice, spared that poverty, may never turn at all; when it would let the recipient accumulate yet more unwholesome causes, as wealth given to someone who would only misuse power to harm others; and when it would harm other beings in turn. Cháng Bǎi treats this passage as a genuinely important corrective to any sentimental notion that compassion alone guarantees rescue — even a bodhisattva's power operates within, not against, the deeper logic of karma itself.

11. The Gateway of Harm and Benefit Opens: Eight Ways the Ten Unwholesome Deeds Actually Hurt

With karma's five ways of giving result now complete, the treatise turns to the second of its two great gateways: harm and benefit, opening with eight specific gateways through which the ten unwholesome courses of action actually inflict damage on other beings.

Chinese Source

損益門者，謂於諸有情，依十不善業道，建立八損害門。何等為八？

Ru-Yi Translation

As for the gateway of harm and benefit: with respect to all sentient beings, eight gateways of harm are established based on the ten unwholesome courses of action. What are the eight?

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi previews this second gateway as shifting the lens one more time: where the five results just completed traced karma's consequences back to the person who performed the deed, this new gateway instead classifies the ten unwholesome deeds by the specific kind of harm they inflict on the being they are directed against — a victim's-eye view, in effect, complementing the perpetrator's-eye view the treatise has used throughout. He notes the number eight, rather than ten, already signals that some of the ten courses of action will be grouped together here as inflicting essentially the same category of harm, with the specific eightfold breakdown to follow.

12. The Eight Gateways of Harm Named, and a Mother's Love as Proof No One Wants to Die

The treatise names all eight gateways of harm, tracing bodily, verbal, and mental unwholesome action each to its own specific injury, and grounds the case against killing in a striking parable: even a mother who insists she loves her child more than her own life proves, once truly tested, that self-love runs deeper than any other love.

Chinese Source

一、損害眾生，二、損害財物，三、損害妻妾，四、虛偽友證損害，五、損害助伴，六、顯說過失損害，七、引發放逸損害，八、引發怖畏損害。與此相違，依十善業道，建立八利益門應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

First, harm to beings; second, harm to property; third, harm to a spouse; fourth, harm through false testimony; fifth, harm to one's companions; sixth, harm through openly exposing another's faults; seventh, harm through inducing heedlessness; eighth, harm through instilling fear. It should be understood that, in the reverse direction, the ten wholesome courses of action establish eight gateways of benefit.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps all eight directly onto the ten unwholesome deeds: killing harms beings directly; theft harms property; sexual misconduct harms a spouse; false speech harms through false testimony; divisive speech harms one's companions and friendships; harsh speech harms by openly exposing and mocking another's faults; frivolous speech harms by inducing heedless, purposeless distraction; and craving, hatred, and wrong view together harm by instilling fear — craving making a being anxious never to lose what it wants, hatred making others fear its next outburst, and wrong view corrupting the very ground a being might otherwise stand on with confidence. Each source text then works through a classical, near-uniform pattern for every one of the eight: two general consequences awaiting a human rebirth, and five specific causal links tracing the deed through its full descent from hell, to an animal's body, to a hungry ghost's hunger, and finally back to a marked human trait — killing yielding a short, sickly life; theft yielding poverty and property forever divided among five hostile forces; sexual misconduct yielding an unfaithful spouse and an unruly household; false speech yielding constant slander and being deceived in turn; divisive speech yielding disagreeable, quarrelsome family; harsh speech yielding a life full of unwelcome sounds and disputes; frivolous speech yielding speech no one believes or understands; and craving, hatred, and wrong view yielding, respectively, insatiable want, constant conflict with others, and being born into a family of wrong view with a habitually crooked heart.

He illustrates why killing above all violates something universal with a story the Buddha himself is said to have told: a mother, cradling her beloved infant son before the Buddha, insisted she loved the child more than her own life, more than anything in the world. To show her otherwise, the Buddha conjured four blazing apparitions closing in from every direction — as the flames drew near, the mother instinctively shielded her own face and body with the child, using him as a barrier against the fire, until, confronted with her own action, she confessed: in that instant, all she wanted was to save herself. The Buddha's point, gently made rather than condemning, was simply that every being, without exception, clings to its own life above all else — precisely why, since one's own life is this precious to oneself, another being's life must be exactly as precious to them, and killing therefore violates the one thing every single being, without exception, actually wants most.

13. Karma's Intensification: Six Factors, and a Hierarchy of Severity

The treatise's sixth item traces what actually makes a deed "forceful and extremely weighty" — six distinct factors — before ranking the ten unwholesome deeds and the six wholesome practices against one another in order of relative severity and merit.

Chinese Source

業增上云何？謂猛利極重業。當知此業由六種相。一、加行故；二、串習故；三、自性故；四、事故；五、所治一類故；六、所治損害故。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the intensification of karma? It is a forceful and extremely weighty deed. This weight, it should be understood, arises through six factors: through deliberate effort; through habituation; through the deed's own inherent nature; through the significance of its object; through belonging to a class that has never once been counteracted; and through being counteracted and thereby diminished.

自性故者，謂於綺語，麤惡語為大重罪；於麤惡語，離間語為大重罪；於離間語，妄語為大重罪。於欲邪行，不與取為大重罪；於不與取，殺生為大重罪。於貪欲，瞋恚為大重罪；於瞋恚，邪見為大重罪。又於施性，戒性無罪為勝；於戒性，修性無罪為勝。於聞性，思性無罪為勝；如是等。

As for the deed's own inherent nature: compared to frivolous speech, harsh speech is a graver offense; compared to harsh speech, divisive speech is graver still; compared to divisive speech, false speech is graver yet. Compared to sexual misconduct, theft is graver; compared to theft, killing is graver still. Compared to covetousness, ill will is graver; compared to ill will, wrong view is graver still. Further, compared to the nature of giving, the nature of moral discipline is superior in its freedom from fault; compared to the nature of discipline, the nature of meditative cultivation is superior. Compared to the nature of hearing, the nature of reflection is superior in its freedom from fault; and so on in the same way for cultivation.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the first two factors — deliberate effort and habituation — as, respectively, the sheer intensity of craving, hatred, or delusion (or their wholesome opposites) actually driving a single act, and the compounding weight of having repeated that same act across countless lifetimes in beginningless ignorance; the fifth and sixth as a matched pair, one describing an unwholesome habit left entirely unaddressed until death intensifying without limit, the other describing a wholesome practice — calm-abiding or liberating wisdom — actively counteracting and diminishing that same unwholesome force.

He treats the severity rankings as genuinely instructive rather than arbitrary: among the four verbal offenses, frivolous speech is mildest, harsh speech graver, divisive speech graver still (since it damages a relationship rather than a single exchange), and false speech gravest of all (since it corrupts trust itself); among the three bodily offenses, sexual misconduct is graded below theft, and theft below killing, the taking of life ranked as the most severe bodily harm possible; and among the three mental offenses, covetousness ranks below ill will, which in turn ranks below wrong view — wrong view topping every category the treatise has ranked throughout this entire fascicle, since it alone corrupts a being's grasp of reality itself, poisoning the root from which every other unwholesome choice can spring. On the wholesome side, he reads the ranking of giving below discipline below meditation, and hearing below reflection below cultivation, as tracing a single deepening movement inward: giving still concerns external objects, discipline concerns one's own outward conduct, and meditation concerns the mind's own inner defilement directly — the same movement from surface to root that the ranking of hearing, reflection, and cultivation retraces on the side of wisdom.

14. Karma's Distortion Opens: When Killing the Wrong Target Isn't Quite "Killing" at All

The treatise's seventh item examines three ways karma can go wrong in its very execution — a mismatch between what was intended and what actually occurred — opening with a case that might surprise: killing the wrong person, while still generating serious karmic consequence, does not technically complete the full offense of killing at all.

Chinese Source

業顛倒云何？此有三種應知。一、作用顛倒，二、執受顛倒，三、喜樂顛倒。作用顛倒者，謂如有一，於餘眾生思欲殺害，誤害餘者。當知此中，雖有殺生，無殺生罪；然有殺生種類、殺生相似同分罪生。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the distortion of karma? There are, it should be understood, three kinds: distortion of function, distortion of grasping, and distortion of delight. Distortion of function is when someone, intending to kill one being, mistakenly kills another instead. It should be understood that although a killing has occurred here, it does not constitute the full offense of killing — yet a related offense, of the same general category and closely resembling killing, does still arise.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this opening case as a genuinely careful piece of moral reasoning: the full, technically complete offense of killing requires the object actually killed to be the very object originally intended — kill the wrong target through simple error, and the formal five-part chain (object, intention, defiled mind, effort, and completion) never quite lines up, since the intention and the actual object diverge. Yet he stresses this is no loophole: a life was still genuinely taken through one's own deliberate violent effort, so a serious "same-category, closely resembling" offense still arises and still carries real karmic weight, including an equal-outflow result of a shortened future lifespan — the technical distinction matters for classifying exactly which offense has occurred, not for excusing the harm actually done. He previews the two further distortions still to come: grasping distortion, where a genuine wrongdoing is mistaken in the mind for something else entirely, and delight distortion, where inappropriate pleasure is taken in something that should evoke no pleasure at all.

15. Attacking an Effigy, Two Faces of Moral Confusion, and Taking Wrongdoing as Play

The treatise completes karma's distortion with a case of mistaken belief (attacking a lifeless object thinking it a person), two opposite forms of moral confusion about who deserves protection, and the unsettling case of someone who genuinely enjoys committing wrong.

Chinese Source

若不誤殺其餘眾生，然於非情加刀杖已，謂我殺生。當知此中，無有殺生，無殺生罪；然有殺生種類、殺生相似同分罪生。如殺生業道，如是不與取等一切業道，隨其所應，作用顛倒應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

If, rather than mistakenly killing some other being, one strikes a lifeless object with a blade or club, believing "I have killed" — it should be understood that no killing has genuinely occurred here, and so no offense of killing arises; yet an offense of the same general category, closely resembling killing, still does arise. Just as with the course of killing, so too, correspondingly, with taking what is not given and every other course of action — distortion of function should be understood the same way throughout.

執受顛倒者，謂如有一，起如是見，立如是論：無施無受，乃至廣說一切邪見。彼作是執：畢竟無有能殺、所殺，若不與取乃至綺語，亦無施與、受齋、修褔、受學尸羅；由此因緣，無罪無褔。

Distortion of grasping is when someone holds such a view and establishes such a doctrine: "there is no giving, no receiving" — stated in full detail as every form of wrong view already given. They hold that there is ultimately no one who kills and no one killed, that taking what is not given through frivolous speech likewise involves no giving, no fasting, no accumulated merit, and no undertaking of precepts — and so, by this reasoning, conclude there is neither fault nor merit in anything at all.

又如有一，起如是見，立如是論：若有眾生憎梵、憎天、憎婆羅門。若彼憎惡，唯應殺害。殺彼因緣，唯福無罪。又於彼所起不與取乃至綺語，唯獲福德，無有非福。

Further, others hold such a view and establish such a doctrine: any being who despises Brahmā, the gods, or brahmins deserves nothing but death, and killing such a hateful being produces merit alone, with no fault whatsoever — and that stealing from, or speaking falsely, harshly, divisively, or frivolously toward such a person likewise produces only merit, never any fault at all.

喜樂顛倒者，謂如有一，不善業道現前行時，如遊戲法，極為喜樂。

Distortion of delight is when someone, in the very act of committing an unwholesome deed, takes it as though it were mere sport, and finds it a source of genuine, intense delight.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the non-sentient case as parallel to the mistaken-victim case: real hostile intent and real physical effort were both present, yet the object struck was never actually capable of dying, so the offense again falls short of killing's full technical completion while still generating a lesser environmental consequence — a future rebirth into a world of dull, lusterless produce, echoing the dominant result already catalogued. He treats grasping distortion as gathering the treatise's two starkest moral errors into one category: the nihilist's flat denial that any action carries moral weight at all, already exhaustively refuted across fascicles 6 and 7, and its perverse mirror image — the belief that harming someone who is disliked or who holds different religious convictions is not merely permissible but actively meritorious, a "might makes right" doctrine dressed as piety. And he reads delight distortion as perhaps the most disturbing of the three: not merely committing wrong without remorse, but actively relishing it as entertainment, a moral numbness the treatise flags as its own distinct category of danger, distinguishing indifference to wrongdoing from active enjoyment of it. With these three, karma's distortion — the seventh of karmic defilement's nine items — comes to its close.

16. Karma's Differences Open: Acted, Increased, and Deliberate

The treatise's eighth item opens an extensive classification of karma's many differences, beginning with three foundational pairs — whether a deed was actually carried out, whether it was intensified beyond a bare minimum, and whether it was genuinely deliberate.

Chinese Source

業差別云何？謂有作業，有不作業；有增長業，有不增長業；有故思業，有不故思業。作業者，謂若思業，若思已所起身業、語業。不作業者，謂若不思業，若不思已不起身業、語業。增長業者，謂除十種業。

Ru-Yi Translation

What are the differences among karma? There is karma that is carried out, and karma that is not; karma that is intensified, and karma that is not; karma that is deliberate, and karma that is not. Karma that is carried out is either the volitional act itself, or the bodily and verbal action that volition, once it has occurred, goes on to produce. Karma that is not carried out is the absence of both. Karma that is intensified comprises everything except ten specific exceptions.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies five conditions that together make a deed genuinely "intensified" rather than a bare, minimal instance: careful deliberation beforehand, rather than a merely impulsive act; full completion of the deed rather than an interrupted attempt; the complete absence of any remorse or corrective repentance afterward; the presence of a companion or accomplice sharing in the act, as when theft is compounded by trespass or violence; and, decisively, actually being certain to ripen into a karmic result at all — a deed lacking any one of these five remains merely "carried out" without rising to the intensified category that draws a fuller consequence.

He glosses deliberate karma through five specific origins already implicit throughout this fascicle's account of karma's causes: acting under another's direct command; acting on another's persuasion, mistaking it for one's own genuine benefit; acting from sheer ignorance of what is actually wholesome or unwholesome; acting under the grip of deep-rooted affliction too strong to resist in the moment; and acting from a settled, distorted view that actively endorses the wrongdoing as somehow justified or beneficial — five distinct roads by which a deed becomes fully deliberate, and therefore certain to ripen into its own decisive result, in contrast to non-deliberate karma, whose future ripening remains genuinely uncertain.

17. Thirteen Categories, Forty-Three Kinds: Karma's Full Taxonomic Sweep

The treatise now names, in a single sweeping list, forty-three further kinds of karma — reducible to thirteen broad categories — covering everything from the certainty of ripening to which of the three realms a deed belongs to, in effect a complete taxonomic index of every angle from which karma can be classified.

Chinese Source

如是定異熟業、不定異熟業，異熟已熟業、異熟未熟業，善業、不善業、無記業，律儀所攝業、不律儀所攝業、非律儀非不律儀所攝業，施性業、戒性業、修性業，褔業、非褔業、不動業，順樂受業、順苦受業、順不苦不樂受業，順現法受業、順生受業、順後受業。

Ru-Yi Translation

Thus: karma of determinate ripening and karma of indeterminate ripening; karma already ripened and karma not yet ripened; wholesome, unwholesome, and morally neutral karma; karma bound by moral discipline, karma bound by its absence, and karma bound by neither; karma of the nature of giving, of moral discipline, and of meditative cultivation; meritorious karma, demeritorious karma, and karma leading to the unshakeable formless attainments; karma corresponding to pleasant feeling, to painful feeling, and to neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling; karma ripening in this very life, in the next life, and in some life thereafter.

過去業、未來業、現在業，欲繫業、色繫業、無色繫業，學業、無學業、非學非無學業，見所斷業、修所斷業、無斷業，黑黑異熟業、白白異熟業、黑白黑白異熟業、非黑非白無異熟業能盡諸業，曲業、穢業、濁業，清淨業、寂靜業。

Past, future, and present karma; karma bound to the desire realm, to the form realm, and to the formless realm; karma of those still training, of those beyond training, and of neither; karma abandoned by the path of seeing, by the path of cultivation, and karma requiring no abandonment at all; dark karma with a dark ripening, bright karma with a bright ripening, mixed karma with a mixed ripening, and the karma that is neither dark nor bright, carrying no ripening at all, capable of exhausting every other kind of karma entirely; crooked karma, tainted karma, and turbid karma; and, finally, pure karma and tranquil karma.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this list as less a set of new doctrines than a comprehensive index cross-referencing categories the treatise has already built throughout this fascicle and the ones before it: determinate versus indeterminate ripening recalls the distinction between deliberate and non-deliberate karma just given; the three moral natures and the three kinds of restraint recall the ten courses of action's own basic framework; giving, discipline, and cultivation recall the severity ranking of the previous section; the three realms recall the treatise's very earliest cosmological surveys back in fascicles 4 and 5; and the classical fourfold "dark and bright" scheme — unwholesome karma ripening painfully, wholesome karma ripening pleasantly, a mixture of both, and the fourth and highest category, uncontaminated karma belonging to a liberated mind, which carries no binding ripening at all and is alone capable of actually exhausting every other kind of karma a being has ever accumulated — he flags as the single most soteriologically important item in the whole list, since it names the exact kind of action a practitioner is actually working toward: action that no longer produces any further karmic debt whatsoever.

He notes the closing pair, "crooked, tainted, and turbid" against "pure and tranquil," functions as the whole list's own final summary contrast — every other technical distinction in this taxonomy ultimately sorts into one side or the other of this same basic line, defiled action still caught in self-interest, deception, or turbulence, against purified action genuinely settled and free of it. Rather than explaining all forty-three individually, Cháng Bǎi treats the sheer comprehensiveness of the list itself as the point: karma, examined closely enough, admits of this many independent angles of classification, and a serious practitioner benefits from being able to locate any given deed along all thirteen axes at once, not just the most obvious one.

18. Ten Exceptions That Never Intensify, and What Makes Karma Genuinely Deliberate

The treatise names the ten specific exceptions that keep a deed from ever intensifying into weightier karma, regardless of its outward form — a practically important list, since it identifies exactly which circumstances soften karma's usual force.

Chinese Source

何等為十？一、夢所作業，二、無知所作業，三、無故思所作業，四、不利不數所作業，五、狂亂所作業，六、失念所作業，七、非樂欲所作業，八、自性無記業，九、悔所損業，十、對治所損業。除此十種，所餘諸業名為增長。

Ru-Yi Translation

What are the ten? First, karma performed in a dream; second, karma performed in ignorance; third, karma performed without deliberate intent; fourth, karma performed neither forcefully nor repeatedly; fifth, karma performed while deranged; sixth, karma performed through a lapse of mindfulness; seventh, karma performed unwillingly, at another's command; eighth, karma inherently neutral in nature; ninth, karma diminished through genuine repentance; tenth, karma diminished through an actual antidote applied. Apart from these ten, every remaining kind of karma is called intensified.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads these ten as together identifying exactly what genuine moral responsibility actually requires — consciousness, knowledge, intention, real force, sanity, mindfulness, and willingness — such that karma performed while dreaming, in genuine ignorance, without deliberate intent, only mildly or once, while deranged, through a momentary lapse of mindfulness even in an otherwise careful person, or purely under someone else's command carries correspondingly reduced weight, since one or more of these basic conditions for full responsibility is simply missing. He treats the final two — diminishment through repentance and through an actual antidote — as the treatise's own practical encouragement: swift, genuine repentance after an unwholesome act, and the sustained cultivation of calm-abiding or liberating insight to actually weaken or destroy the underlying afflictive seed, are not merely pious gestures but genuinely effective, named mechanisms by which karma's weight can be reduced after the fact — repentance, he stresses, is not empty ritual but a real and working part of the path.

19. Deliberate Karma Closes, and the Detailed Taxonomy Begins

The treatise closes deliberate and non-deliberate karma with a precise definition, then begins working through the thirteen-category taxonomy named earlier in careful detail, starting with definite versus indefinite ripening and the three moral natures.

Chinese Source

故思業者，謂故思已，若作業、若增長業。不故思業者，謂非故思所作業。順定受業者，謂故思已，若作若增長業。順不定受業者，謂故思已，作而不增長業。

Ru-Yi Translation

Deliberate karma is karma that, having been carefully considered, is then either carried out or further intensified. Non-deliberate karma is karma performed without such deliberate consideration. Karma certain to be experienced is deliberate karma that has been both carried out and intensified. Karma not certain to be experienced is deliberate karma that has been carried out but not intensified.

異熟已熟業者，謂已與果業。異熟未熟業者，謂未與果業。善業者，謂無貪、無瞋、無癡為因緣業。不善業者，謂貪瞋癡為因緣業。無記業者，謂非無貪、無瞋、無癡為因緣，亦非貪瞋癡為因緣業。

Karma already ripened is karma that has already yielded its result. Karma not yet ripened is karma that has not yet yielded its result. Wholesome karma has non-craving, non-hatred, and non-delusion as its cause. Unwholesome karma has craving, hatred, and delusion as its cause. Neutral karma has neither of these as its cause.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi cites the Abhidharma-samuccaya's own threefold account of what makes karma genuinely "certain to be experienced": the act itself may be fixed by prior karmic momentum, occurring within its determined window regardless of anything else, not even a buddha's own power able to prevent it; the actual ripening of a specific result may be fixed once a deed has been both carried out and intensified, exactly as just defined; and the timing of that ripening may itself be fixed, sorting further into ripening in this very life, the next life, or some life beyond — a distinction the treatise will return to shortly. Deliberate but unintensified karma, by contrast, remains genuinely uncertain, its eventual ripening depending on further conditions still to come.

He treats the three moral natures as the simplest and most fundamental sorting of all — wholesome karma rooted in the three wholesome roots already catalogued back in fascicle 3, unwholesome karma rooted in their three opposites, and neutral karma, such as an ordinarily skillful craft performed with a mind neither wholesome nor unwholesome, producing no karmic ripening at either extreme — the baseline distinction every other category in this taxonomy ultimately builds upon.

20. Three Kinds of Restraint, and the Twelve Unrestrained Livelihoods

The taxonomy's fourth category sorts karma by whether it is bound by any code of restraint at all — restrained, unrestrained, or neither — with the unrestrained category anchored to a list of twelve specific occupations built on killing or deceit.

Chinese Source

律儀所攝業者，謂或別解脫律儀所攝業，或靜慮等至果斷律儀所攝業，或無漏律儀所攝業。

Ru-Yi Translation

Karma bound by restraint is karma bound by the pratimokṣa restraint of individual liberation, or by the restraint that suppresses affliction as the fruit of meditative absorption and attainment, or by uncontaminated restraint.

不律儀所攝業者，謂十二種不律儀類所攝諸業。何等十二不律儀類？一、屠羊，二、販雞，三、販豬，四、捕鳥，五、罝兔，六、盜賊，七、魁膾，八、守獄，九、讒剌，十、斷獄，十一、縛象，十二、呪龍。

Karma bound by the absence of restraint is karma belonging to the twelve unrestrained livelihoods. What are the twelve? First, butchering sheep; second, trading in chickens; third, trading in pigs; fourth, trapping birds; fifth, snaring hares; sixth, banditry and theft; seventh, executing condemned prisoners; eighth, guarding a prison; ninth, slandering others for a living; tenth, sentencing prisoners as a magistrate; eleventh, keeping elephants in captivity; twelfth, charming serpents by incantation.

非律儀非不律儀所攝業者，謂除三種律儀業及不律儀類業，所餘一切善、不善、無記業。

Karma bound by neither is every remaining wholesome, unwholesome, or neutral deed, apart from the three kinds of restrained karma and the twelve unrestrained livelihoods.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi distinguishes the three restraints by what actually holds the line against wrongdoing: the pratimokṣa vows taken by any of the sevenfold Buddhist assembly (monks, nuns, novices, and lay disciples) work by a deliberate vow impressed as a seed in the storehouse consciousness; meditative restraint works by concentration's own power suppressing affliction's active manifestation, without uprooting its seed; uncontaminated restraint arises spontaneously alongside the direct realization of the four truths, permanently destroying every seed capable of leading to a bad rebirth. Only the third genuinely severs the root.

He reads the twelve unrestrained livelihoods as a deliberately concrete list — not abstract vice but specific trades a person could actually be born into or fall into, each built on killing (the first five, plus the executioner and the elephant-keeper's habitual cruelty) or on exploiting others through position or deceit (bandit, prison guard, slanderer, judge, snake-charmer). Everything else — the vast ordinary middle of daily conduct, neither vowed to restraint nor structurally built on harm — falls to the third, weakest category: good and bad still arise there, but with none of restraint's settled force behind them.

21. Giving, Precepts, and Meditation, Each Anatomized Fourfold

The taxonomy's fifth category examines karma by its own moral character — giving, precept-keeping, and meditative cultivation — each broken into the same four elements: its cause, its arising, its basis, and its own nature.

Chinese Source

施性業者，謂若因緣、若等起、若依處、若自性。彼因緣者，謂以無貪、無瞋、無癡為因緣。彼等起者，謂無貪、無瞋、無癡俱行，能捨所施物、能起身語業思。彼依處者，謂以所施物及受者為依處。彼自性者，謂思所起能捨所施物身業、語業。如施性業，如是戒性業、修性業隨其所應應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

Karma of the nature of giving has a cause, an arising, a basis, and its own nature. Its cause is non-craving, non-hatred, and non-delusion. Its arising is the intention, occurring together with non-craving, non-hatred, and non-delusion, that is able to relinquish the gift and give rise to the bodily and verbal act. Its basis is the gift itself and its recipient. Its own nature is the bodily and verbal act, arisen from that intention, that actually relinquishes the gift. Karma of the nature of precept-keeping and of meditative cultivation should be understood the same way, each as it applies.

此中戒性業，因緣、等起如前。自性者，謂律儀所攝身語業等。依處者，謂有情、非有情數物。修性因緣者，謂三摩地因緣，即無貪、無瞋、無癡。等起者，謂彼俱行引發定思。自性者，謂三摩地。依處者，謂十方無苦無樂等有情界。

For precept-keeping karma, the cause and arising are as before. Its own nature is the bodily and verbal act bound by restraint. Its basis is both sentient and non-sentient things. For meditative-cultivation karma, the cause is likewise non-craving, non-hatred, and non-delusion, the actual condition for concentration. Its arising is the intention that, occurring together with these three, gives rise to concentration. Its own nature is concentration itself. Its basis is the realm of beings throughout the ten directions who feel neither pain nor pleasure, and, by extension, those who feel pain or pleasure as well.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this fourfold template — cause, arising, basis, own-nature — as a general-purpose anatomy the treatise could in principle apply to any wholesome act, here run across the three classical bases of merit. For giving, the gift and its recipient jointly form the basis, and he notes the recipient class named elsewhere in the treatise spans the suffering (to be relieved), the benefactor (to be repaid), the beloved (to be cherished), and the venerable (the Three Jewels and the genuinely virtuous) — different motives, one single wholesome root. For precept-keeping, he flags that a Buddhist disciple's basis of restraint covers both sentient beings (not to be harmed) and their possessions (not to be taken), while the śrāvaka precepts center on bodily and verbal conduct alone, in contrast to the bodhisattva precepts, where even a single unwholesome thought already constitutes a breach.

For meditative cultivation, he identifies concentration's own nature here as specifically the four immeasurables — loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity — each keyed to a different class of being: loving-kindness wishes happiness on those with neither pain nor pleasure; compassion wishes the suffering relieved of their pain; sympathetic joy rejoices in the happiness of those who have it; and equanimity, resting on the same threefold root of non-craving, non-hatred, and non-delusion, releases delusion toward the neutral, hatred toward the suffering, and craving toward the joyful alike. Unlike the liberating insight developed elsewhere in the path, he stresses, the four immeasurables belong to the category of merit, not of liberation itself — a genuinely good practice, but not by itself the way out of saṃsāra.

22. Merit, Demerit, and the Unmoving Karma of Deep Meditation

The taxonomy's sixth category sorts karma by which of the three realms its result belongs to: meritorious karma bound for a fortunate rebirth, demeritorious karma bound for an unfortunate one, and the "unmoving" karma of deep meditative absorption that alone reaches the form and formless realms.

Chinese Source

褔業者，謂感善趣異熟，及順五趣受善業。非褔業者，謂感惡趣異熟，及順五趣受不善業。不動業者，謂感色無色界異熟，及順色無色界受善業。

Ru-Yi Translation

Meritorious karma brings a ripened result in a fortunate destiny, together with wholesome karma experienced throughout the five destinies. Demeritorious karma brings a ripened result in an unfortunate destiny, together with unwholesome karma experienced throughout the five destinies. Unmoving karma brings a ripened result in the form or formless realm, together with wholesome karma experienced there.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses "unmoving" (不動) as the technical mark distinguishing meditative karma from the ordinary merit of the desire realm: an ordinary wholesome deed can still be redirected by circumstance into any of several possible destinies, but karma born of genuine absorption is fixed to its own level and ripens nowhere else — the first dhyāna undisturbed by external distraction, the second no longer moved even by applied and sustained thought, the third no longer moved by joy itself, the fourth's outer world untouched by the three cosmic calamities and its inner mind untouched by any of the eight kinds of disturbance. He notes that even meritorious karma, for all its genuine goodness, still carries subtle suffering wherever it ripens — a point the treatise established already in its earlier survey of the three realms — so none of these three categories by itself reaches beyond saṃsāra; only the uncontaminated karma named later in this same taxonomy does that.

23. Karma Correlated to Pleasant, Painful, and Neutral Feeling

The seventh category sorts karma by the feeling it brings — pleasant, painful, or neither — mapping each directly onto the realms and meditative levels already surveyed earlier in the fascicle.

Chinese Source

順樂受業者，謂福業及順三靜慮受不動業。順苦受業者，謂非福業。順不苦不樂受業者，謂能感一切處阿賴耶識異熟業，及第四靜慮以上不動業。

Ru-Yi Translation

Karma correlated to pleasant feeling is meritorious karma, together with the unmoving karma of the first three meditative absorptions. Karma correlated to painful feeling is simply demeritorious karma. Karma correlated to neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling is the karma that everywhere brings a ripened result in the storehouse consciousness, together with the unmoving karma of the fourth absorption and beyond.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi points out that the storehouse consciousness itself, whatever karma ripens it, is always felt as neutral — its own accompanying feeling is invariably equanimity, never pleasure or pain — so any karma that ripens specifically as that consciousness already counts as bringing neutral feeling, quite apart from where in the three realms it lands. He adds that the first three absorptions are each described elsewhere as pleasure "bathing the whole body," while the fourth absorption and the four formless spheres above it are marked by a purified equanimity so complete that neither pleasure nor pain any longer touches them — the same three-realm survey the fascicle opened with, now reappearing as a lens on karma itself.

24. Karma Ripening in This Life, the Next, or Some Life Beyond

The eighth category sorts karma by when it ripens, and names the three conditions — force of resolve, force of effort, and the significance of the "field" acted upon — that together determine whether a deed ripens in this very life.

Chinese Source

順現法受業者，謂能感現法果業。順生受業者，謂能感無間生果業。順後受業者，謂能感彼後生果業。

Ru-Yi Translation

Karma ripening in this very life is karma capable of bringing its result within this same lifetime. Karma ripening in the next life is karma capable of bringing its result in the immediately following life, without any gap between. Karma ripening in some later life is karma capable of bringing its result only from the life after next onward.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi draws out the three conditions the commentarial gloss attaches to same-life ripening: strength of resolve (an unusually intense wholesome or unwholesome motivation), strength of effort (a deed carried out without interruption, done earnestly, sustained over time, urged onto others, and rejoiced in when others do the same — all five conditions together), and the significance of the field acted upon (a deed done toward the Three Jewels, a genuine benefactor, one absorbed in loving-kindness or cessation, or one freshly risen from such absorption, ripens with unusual speed, for good or ill). A deed missing all three conditions simply carries its usual course, ripening in the next life or, if even weaker, only in some later life still to come — a distinction he treats as practically important, since it explains why some wrongdoing appears to go unpunished for a lifetime while other, seemingly smaller acts bring visible consequences almost at once.

25. Three Times, Three Realms, and Three Levels of Training

Three further short triads follow in quick succession — karma sorted by whether it lies in the past, present, or future; by which of the three realms it belongs to; and by whether the person who performs it is still in training, beyond training, or neither.

Chinese Source

過去業者，謂住習氣位，或已與果、或未與果業。未來業者，謂未生未滅業。現在業者，謂已造、已思、未謝滅業。

Ru-Yi Translation

Past karma abides in the state of a habitual seed, whether it has already yielded its result or not yet. Future karma has neither yet arisen nor yet ceased. Present karma has already been performed, already been intended, and has not yet passed away.

欲繫業者，謂能感欲界異熟，墮欲界業。色繫業者，謂能感色界異熟，墮色界業。無色繫業者，謂能感無色界異熟，墮無色界業。

Karma bound to the desire realm brings a ripened result there. Karma bound to the form realm brings a ripened result there. Karma bound to the formless realm brings a ripened result there.

學業者，謂若異生、若非異生，學相續中所有善業。無學業者，謂無學相續中所有善業。非學非無學業者，謂除前二，餘相續中所有善、不善、無記業。

Karma of one still in training is every wholesome deed arising in the mind-stream of someone — whether an ordinary being or not — who has entered training. Karma of one beyond training is every wholesome deed arising in the mind-stream of an arhat. Karma of neither is every wholesome, unwholesome, or neutral deed arising in every remaining mind-stream, apart from these two.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats "abides in the state of a habitual seed" as the whole point of the past-karma definition: a completed deed does not simply vanish, but perfumes the storehouse consciousness into a seed that persists there, whether or not its fruit has already ripened — the same seed doctrine the fascicle's own causal apparatus rests on throughout.

On the training triad, he clarifies that "still in training" spans a wide range — from an ordinary person who has first generated the aspiration toward liberation, through the noble stream-enterer, once-returner, and non-returner, all the way up to the very threshold of arhatship — united by the single fact that some further learning still remains before them. Only the arhat, having exhausted every seed of affliction, is properly "beyond training," and only an arhat's every act, wholesome or otherwise ordinary, is genuinely uncontaminated through and through; the trainee's own wholesome deeds, by contrast, still remain a mixture of the uncontaminated moments realized in meditative equipoise and the contaminated wholesome deeds performed once out of it.

26. Karma Abandoned by Seeing, by Cultivation, or Never Needing Abandonment

The twelfth category sorts karma by which stage of the path removes it — the sudden insight of the path of seeing, the gradual work of the path of cultivation, or, for uncontaminated karma itself, no abandonment at all.

Chinese Source

見所斷業者，謂受惡趣不善等業。修所斷業者，謂受善趣善、不善、無記業。無斷業者，謂世出世諸無漏業。

Ru-Yi Translation

Karma abandoned by seeing is unwholesome karma, and the like, that leads to an unfortunate destiny. Karma abandoned by cultivation is wholesome, unwholesome, and neutral karma that leads to a fortunate destiny. Karma needing no abandonment at all is every uncontaminated deed, whether worldly or transcending the world.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi glosses "and the like" as covering several specific items the treatise elsewhere singles out for removal at the very moment of seeing the truths directly: the karma of ever again being born as a woman; the karma of an eighth further rebirth, since a stream-enterer is bound to reach arhatship within at most seven more lives; the karma leading to the non-perception heaven, an attainment only non-Buddhist meditators mistake for liberation; the karma leading to Uttarakuru, a paradise too comfortable to want liberation in; and the residual wholesome karma that would otherwise lead to rebirth as a ghost or animal — all cut cleanly at the single moment of insight, in contrast to the coarser afflictions still requiring the path of cultivation's gradual, repeated work. He treats the third category — needing no abandonment — as itself the goal the first two categories exist to clear the way toward: karma performed with uncontaminated wisdom carries no defiled residue in the first place, so there is simply nothing left in it to remove.

27. Dark and Bright Karma, and the Fourth Kind That Ends Them All

The thirteenth category, one of the taxonomy's richest, sorts karma by a classical fourfold "color" scheme — dark karma with a dark result, bright karma with a bright result, karma mixing both, and the uncontaminated karma that is neither, alone capable of exhausting every other kind of karma a being has ever accumulated.

Chinese Source

黑黑異熟業者，謂非福業。白白異熟業者，謂不動業。黑白黑白異熟業者，謂褔業。有不善業為怨對故，由約未斷非褔業時所有褔業而建立故。

Ru-Yi Translation

Dark karma with a dark ripening is demeritorious karma. Bright karma with a bright ripening is unmoving karma. Mixed karma with a mixed ripening is meritorious karma — established precisely because unwholesome karma still stands as its unresolved counterpart, this naming applying at the stage when demeritorious karma has not yet been eliminated even while meritorious karma is being performed.

非黑非白無異熟業能盡諸業者，謂出世間諸無漏業，是前三業斷對治故。

Karma that is neither dark nor bright, carrying no ripening at all, capable of exhausting every other kind of karma, is transcendent uncontaminated karma — precisely because it serves as the antidote that severs all three of the preceding kinds.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps each of the first three squarely onto destinies already surveyed: dark-dark karma to the various hells, since both its cause and its result are equally unwholesome; bright-bright karma to the four dhyāna heavens, the four formless spheres, and even the palace of the sovereign of the sixth desire heaven, since meditative karma's cause and result are equally wholesome; and mixed karma to every remaining place — the human world, the lower desire heavens, the animal and ghost realms — precisely because ordinary merit there is never pure, always shadowed by unresolved unwholesome karma still lodged in the same mind-stream. He offers a second, psychological reading of "mixed" as well: a deed whose outward form is generous but whose inner motive is calculating (offering a free sample purely to make a sale), or whose inner motive is compassionate but whose outward form is harsh (a teacher's harsh scolding meant only to correct a student's habits) — intention and expression pulling in opposite directions, dark by one measure and bright by the other at once.

He treats the fourth category as the taxonomy's own destination: unlike the first three, which merely differ in color while all still binding a being to some further rebirth, uncontaminated karma alone actively dismantles the very mechanism of ripening — not one more shade added to the palette, but the wisdom that empties the palette out entirely, which is why the treatise calls it capable of "exhausting every other kind of karma" rather than merely adding a fourth color to the first three.

28. Crooked, Tainted, and Turbid Karma, Against Pure and Tranquil — the Taxonomy Closes

The thirteenth category's final five terms close the entire forty-three-fold taxonomy: three names for karma corrupted by wrong view, set against the two names — pure and tranquil — that describe the deeds of those with genuine, settled confidence in the Dharma.

Chinese Source

曲業者，謂諸外道善不善業。穢業者，謂即曲業亦名穢業。又有穢業，謂此法異生於聖教中顛倒見者、住自見取者、邪決定者、猶預覺者所有善不善業。濁業者，謂即曲業、穢業亦名濁業。又有濁業，謂此法異生於聖教中不決定者、猶預覺者所有善不善業。

Ru-Yi Translation

Crooked karma is the wholesome and unwholesome karma of every non-Buddhist teacher. Tainted karma is this same crooked karma, so named again; and there is a further tainted karma — the wholesome and unwholesome karma of one who, though within this very teaching, holds a distorted view of the sacred doctrine, clings fixedly to their own opinion, has settled into a wrong conclusion, or remains caught in doubt. Turbid karma is this same crooked and tainted karma, so named yet again; and there is a further turbid karma — the wholesome and unwholesome karma of one who, though within this very teaching, remains undecided about the sacred doctrine or caught in doubt.

又有差別，唯於外道法中有此三業。由邪解行義故，名曲；由此為依，能障所起諸功德義故，名穢；能障通達真如義故，名濁；應知。清淨業者，謂此法異生於聖教中正決定者、不猶預覺者所有善業。寂靜業者，謂住此法非異生者，一切聖者所有學無學業。

On a further reading, these three belong to non-Buddhist teachings alone. They are named crooked because they arise from wrongful understanding and wrongful conduct; named tainted because, on this same basis, they obstruct whatever wholesome quality would otherwise arise; and named turbid because they obstruct the realization of suchness itself — so it should be understood. Pure karma is the wholesome karma of one who, within this very teaching, holds a settled and correct conviction about the sacred doctrine, free of doubt. Tranquil karma is the karma, whether still in training or beyond it, of every noble one who dwells in this teaching and is no longer an ordinary being at all.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads these five terms as offering two different diagnoses of the same underlying condition, layered one atop the other. The first reading extends "crooked" beyond non-Buddhist teachers to a Buddhist practitioner who hears the genuine teaching but mishandles it: mistaking the wrong thing for the Dharma, stubbornly preferring one's own opinion over the teaching itself, collapsing into a nihilistic misreading of emptiness that denies causality altogether, or simply never settling into confidence — four distinct ways of falling short even while formally inside the tradition. The second, narrower reading restricts all three terms to non-Buddhist teachers alone, on the reasoning that these particular distorted views belong to no genuinely internal school; on this reading, a Buddhist practitioner's own shortfall is named only by the pair "tainted" and "turbid," reserving "crooked" for non-Buddhist doctrine specifically.

He treats the closing gloss — crooked names wrongful understanding-and-conduct itself, tainted names the obstruction of good qualities that follows from it, turbid names the deeper obstruction of realizing suchness — as showing the three are not three separate faults but three depths of a single one, each building on the last. Against this stands the practitioner of settled, undoubting confidence (pure) and the noble one whose mind is genuinely at rest, free of affliction's disturbance (tranquil) — a final contrast that, he notes, closes out the entire forty-three-item taxonomy on the simplest possible note: everything above ultimately sorts into confusion or clarity, disturbance or peace.

29. Karma's Faults — Seven Kinds of Harm, and Karmic Defilement Closes

Karmic defilement's ninth and final item names seven faults common to every unwholesome deed, worked out through killing as the example, then points to two further, more extensive scriptural accounts — ten faults from breaking a precept, and the many faults the Buddha named for breaking any of the five lay precepts.

Chinese Source

業過患云何？當知略說有七過患。謂殺生者殺生為因，能為自害、能為他害、能為俱害，生現法罪、生後法罪、生現法後法罪，受彼所生身心憂苦。

Ru-Yi Translation

What are karma's faults? Briefly, there are seven. Taking killing as the cause: it can harm oneself, it can harm another, it can harm both; it generates guilt in this very life, guilt in a later life, or guilt in both this life and a later one; and it brings the resulting suffering of body and mind.

云何能為自害？謂為害生發起方便，由此因緣，便自被害，若被繫縛，若遭退失，若被訶毀；然彼不能損害於他。云何能為他害？謂即由此所起方便，能損害他。由此因緣，不自被害乃至訶毀。云何能為俱害？謂即由此所起方便，能損害他。由此因緣，復被他害，若被繫縛乃至訶毀。

What is harming oneself? One sets out to take a life, and by this very cause is oneself harmed in turn — seized and bound, stripped of one's own merit, or reviled — without ever having actually harmed the other. What is harming another? By this same undertaking, one does harm the other, and by this cause is not oneself harmed, bound, or reviled at all. What is harming both? By this same undertaking, one does harm the other, and by this same cause is in turn harmed oneself as well — seized, bound, or reviled in turn.

云何生現法罪？謂如能為自害。云何生後法罪？謂如能為他害。云何生現法後法罪？謂如能為俱害。云何受彼所生身心憂苦？謂為害生發起方便，而不能成六種過失，又不能辦隨欲殺事，彼由所欲不會因緣，便受所生身心憂苦。

What is generating guilt in this very life? It is as in harming oneself. What is generating guilt in a later life? It is as in harming another. What is generating guilt in both? It is as in harming both. And what is the resulting suffering of body and mind? One sets out to take a life, yet accomplishes none of the six faults just named, nor even succeeds in the killing itself — and simply from this failure to obtain what one sought, suffering of body and mind arises all the same.

又有十種過患，依犯尸羅。如經廣說應知。又有四種不善業道，及飲諸酒以為第五。依犯事善男學處，佛薄伽梵說多過患應知。廣說如闡地迦經。

There are, further, ten faults tied to breaking moral discipline, as the sūtras explain at length. And there are, further, the four unwholesome courses of action — killing, taking what is not given, sexual misconduct, and false speech — together with the drinking of intoxicants as a fifth: on the breach of any of these five lay precepts, the Blessed Buddha spoke of many faults, as explained at length in the Chanti-jia Sūtra.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the seven faults as a deliberately exhaustive grid rather than a simple list: every unwholesome deed lands on one of three targets (self, other, or both) and generates guilt across one of three time-spans (this life, a later life, or both), and whichever cell of that grid a given act falls into, it still produces the seventh and final fault — the immediate suffering of body and mind that accompanies even a failed attempt, since the afflicted intention behind the deed is itself already a burning, whether or not the deed ever succeeds. He stresses the case of harming oneself in particular: an unsuccessful killer still suffers arrest, loss of standing, and public censure, all without ever having actually taken the other's life — proof that unwholesome karma wounds its own agent first, regardless of outcome.

He points to the ten faults of broken discipline (detailed in the treatise's own later Śrāvaka Stage) as tracing every stage from a corrupt motive at the very moment of ordination through to the eventual collapse of the precept altogether, and to the fivefold lay precepts' own scriptural elaboration as showing that karmic consequence, once set in motion, cannot simply be evaded — closing with the point that a genuine bodhisattva, meeting an unwelcome circumstance, looks first to their own past karma rather than blaming external causes. With this, karmic defilement (業雜染), the second of the three defilements opened at the very start of fascicle 8, is now complete in its entirety — its full ninefold analysis (nature, classification, causes, stages, gateways, intensification, distortion, differences, and now faults) fully covered across two fascicles. Only the third and final defilement, rebirth defilement (生雜染), remains before the whole triad first named in fascicle 8's opening lines is closed.

30. Rebirth Defilement Opens: Four Aspects, and Fifty-Eight Places to Be Born

The third and final defilement, rebirth defilement (生雜染), opens with a fourfold outline — difference, hardship, uncertainty, and transmigration — then begins the first aspect, difference, by surveying the full extent of where a being can possibly be born: three realms, five destinies and four modes of birth, and fifty-eight specific places in all.

Chinese Source

云何生雜染？謂由四種相應知。一、由差別故；二、由艱辛故；三、由不定故；四、由流轉故。生差別者，當知復有五種。一、界差別，二、趣差別，三、處所差別，四、勝生差別，五、自身世間差別。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is rebirth defilement? It should be understood through four aspects: difference, hardship, uncertainty, and transmigration. Difference itself has five further kinds: difference of realm, difference of destiny, difference of place, difference of superior birth, and difference of one's own world.

界差別者，謂欲界及色無色界生差別。趣差別者，謂於五趣四生差別。處所差別者，謂欲界中有三十六處生差別，色界中有十八處生差別，無色界中有四處生差別，如是總有五十八生。

Difference of realm is the difference between birth in the desire realm and birth in the form or formless realm. Difference of destiny is the difference among the five destinies and the four modes of birth. Difference of place is thirty-six places of birth in the desire realm, eighteen in the form realm, and four in the formless realm — fifty-eight places of birth in all.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this fourfold outline as the treatise's own map of the whole remainder of the fascicle: difference surveys the sheer variety of what a rebirth can be; hardship dwells on how much suffering the ordinary process of being born and dying has already cost every being, across countless past lives; uncertainty shows that not even parents, pleasure, place, or companions can be counted on to repeat from one life to the next; and transmigration — by far the largest of the four, since it houses the treatise's full twelve-link analysis of dependent origination — explains the actual mechanism driving the whole cycle.

He itemizes the desire realm's thirty-six places exactly as catalogued back in fascicle 4's own realm-survey (the eight hot and eight cold hells, the ghost realm, the asura realm, the four continents with their eight intermediate continents, and the six heavens), and the form realm's eighteen as the three places of each of the first three dhyānas plus the fourth dhyāna's nine — the three common to both ordinary beings and non-returners, the five pure-abode heavens reserved for non-returners alone, and, at the very summit, the palace of Maheśvara, dwelling place of the tenth-stage bodhisattvas — with the four formless spheres completing the full fifty-eight. He flags this as a genuinely comprehensive inventory: every place a being has ever been or ever will be born, named once and located precisely within the three realms already surveyed at length earlier in the fascicles.

31. Superior Birth: Black, White, and Neither, Across Every Realm

Difference of superior birth sorts the fortunate rebirths within each realm still further — three grades among humans by the color of their past karma, three kinds of desire-realm god, three kinds of form-realm god, and three kinds of formless-realm god — closing the fivefold difference with a brief note on the sheer scale of other world-systems.

Chinese Source

勝生差別者，謂欲界人中有三勝生。一、黑勝生生。謂如有一，生旃荼羅家，若卜羯娑家，若造車家，若竹作家，若生所餘下賤貧窮乏少財物飲食等家，如是名為人中薄福德者。二、白勝生生。謂如有一，生剎帝利大富貴家，若婆羅門大富貴家，若諸長者大富貴家，若生所餘豪貴大富多諸財穀庫藏等家，如是名為人中勝褔德者。三、非黑非白勝生生。謂如有一，非前二種生處中家者。

Ru-Yi Translation

Superior birth among humans in the desire realm is of three kinds. First, a dark superior birth: one born into a family of outcaste executioners, or of latrine-cleaners, or of cart-makers, or of bamboo-workers, or into any other family that is base, poor, and short of goods and food — one of meager merit among humans. Second, a bright superior birth: one born into a great and wealthy warrior-caste family, or a great and wealthy brahmin family, or a great and wealthy elder's family, or into any other eminent, wealthy family rich in grain and treasure — one of superior merit among humans. Third, a superior birth neither dark nor bright: one born into a household falling between these first two.

又欲界天中亦有三種勝生。一、非天生，二、依地分生，三、依虛空宮殿生。又色界中有三種勝生。一者、異生無想天生，二者、有想天生，三者、淨居天生。又無色界中有三勝生。一、無量想天生，二、無所有想天生，三、非想非非想天生。自身世間差別者，謂於十方無量世界中，有無量有情無量生差別應知。

Among desire-realm gods there are likewise three kinds of superior birth: birth as an asura, birth resting on the earth, and birth resting on a palace in open space. Among form-realm gods there are three: birth in the non-perception heaven, reserved for non-Buddhist ordinary beings; birth in a heaven of perception; and birth in a pure abode. Among formless-realm gods there are three: birth in the sphere of limitless perception, birth in the sphere of nothing whatsoever, and birth in the sphere of neither perception nor non-perception. Difference of one's own world is this: throughout the limitless world-systems of the ten directions, there are limitless beings and limitless differences of birth — so it should be understood.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the three human grades as directly proportioned to the mixture of merit and demerit carried over from a past life — never pure black or pure white in practice, since even the wealthiest family still suffers and even the poorest still has some ease, but sorted by which color visibly predominates in a given birth's outward circumstances. Among desire-realm gods, he notes the asura is listed here as its own kind of "heavenly" superior birth despite the asura's characteristic belligerence, while the remaining five heavens split simply by whether their palace rests on solid ground (the Four Kings and the Thirty-Three) or floats free in open space above it (the four higher heavens) — a distinction the fascicle's own earlier cosmology, back in fascicles 4 and 5, first established at length.

Among form-realm gods, he flags the non-perception heaven's inclusion as pointed: it is technically the loftiest rebirth the fourth dhyāna can produce, yet it is reserved for non-Buddhist meditators alone, since a being who mistakes the mere cessation of perception for liberation itself has by definition missed the actual path — a warning already sounded in fascicle 6's survey of the sixteen non-Buddhist doctrines. The five pure abodes, by contrast, house only genuine non-returners. With the brief closing note on other world-systems' own limitless variety, this closes difference of superior birth, and with it the entire fivefold difference of birth (realm, destiny, place, superior birth, and one's own world) — the first of rebirth defilement's four aspects.

32. The Hardship of Birth: Oceans of Blood, Tears, and Mother's Milk

Rebirth defilement's second aspect, hardship, quotes the Buddha directly to give saṃsāra's sheer duration a visceral, physical measure: the blood shed across countless past lives as an animal or a human already exceeds the volume of the four great oceans — and so, the Buddha adds, do the tears wept over lost kin, and the milk once drunk from a mother's breast.

Chinese Source

生艱辛者，如薄伽梵說：汝等長時馳騁生死，身血流注過四大海。所以者何？汝等長夜，或生象、馬、駝、驢、牛、羊、雞、鹿等眾同分中，汝等於彼多被斫截身諸支分，令汝身血極多流注。如於象等眾同分中，人中亦爾。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the hardship of birth? As the Blessed One said: for a long time you have run and raced through birth and death, and the blood that has poured from your bodies already exceeds the four great oceans. Why is this so? Through the long night of saṃsāra you have again and again been born among the ranks of elephants, horses, camels, donkeys, cattle, sheep, chickens, and deer, and among them your limbs have again and again been cut and severed, so that the blood pouring from your bodies has been immense. And exactly as among these ranks of elephants and the rest, so too has it been for you among humans.

又復汝等於長夜中，喪失無量父母兄弟姊妹親屬，又復喪失種種財寶諸資生具，令汝洟淚極多流注，如前血量。如血、洟淚，如是當知所飲母乳其量亦爾。如是等類生艱辛苦無量差別應知。

And through that same long night, you have lost countless parents, brothers, sisters, and kin, and have lost as well every kind of treasure and possession — so that the tears you have shed likewise pour forth in a volume equal to the blood already told. And as with the blood and the tears, so too, it should be understood, is the volume of the mother's milk you have drunk. Such is the immeasurable, varied hardship of birth, and it should be understood accordingly.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads this not as poetic exaggeration but as a claim the treatise attributes to the Buddha's own unobstructed knowledge of every being's past lives — a scale of suffering ordinary memory could never access, disclosed precisely so that it can do the work ordinary memory cannot: puncture the complacent sense that this present life's troubles are the whole of one's suffering. He notes the deliberate symmetry of the three measures — blood shed through violence suffered as an animal and a human alike, tears shed over the loss of every kind of loved one and possession, and milk drunk at the very start of each of those countless births — tracing the entire arc of a life, from its most vulnerable beginning to its most violent ending, repeated without any discoverable first occurrence. The teaching's intended effect, he stresses, is not despair but urgency: only by seeing the true scale of what has already been endured does the wish to actually exit the cycle become real rather than merely notional.

33. Uncertain Birth: Every Being Was Once Your Parent, and a Single Life's Bones Outstrip a Mountain

Rebirth defilement's third aspect, uncertainty, shows that nothing about a rebirth can be counted on to recur — not one's parents, not whether a given experience will be pleasant or painful, not one's place, and not one's companions — closing with the Buddha's own vivid measure of a single being's remains piled up across just one eon.

Chinese Source

生不定者，如薄伽梵說：假使取於大地所有一切草木根莖枝葉等，截為細籌，如四指量，計算汝等長夜展轉所經父母，如是眾生曾為我母，我亦長夜曾為彼母；如是眾生曾為我父，我亦長夜曾為彼父。如是算計，四指量籌速可窮盡，而我不說汝等長夜所經父母其量邊際。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is uncertain birth? As the Blessed One said: suppose one took every root, stem, branch, and leaf of every plant and tree on this great earth, and cut them all into fine tallies four fingers in length, and used them to count every parent you have had through that long night of rebirth — such beings have each been my mother, and I too have through that long night been their mother; such beings have each been my father, and I too have through that long night been their father. Reckoned this way, the tallies, four fingers each, would swiftly be exhausted — yet I could not tell you the outer limit of how many parents you have had through that long night.

又復說言：汝等有情自所觀察，長夜展轉，成就第一極重憂苦今得究竟。汝等當知，我亦曾受如是大苦。如苦，樂亦爾。又復說言：我觀大地，無少處所可得汝等長夜於此處所未曾經受無量生死。

And further: you beings, examining for yourselves, will find that through that long night you have again and again undergone the very heaviest suffering, and only now have arrived at its actual end. You should know that I too once bore just such great suffering. And as with suffering, so too with pleasure. And further: I survey this great earth, and can find not the smallest place where you have not, through that long night, undergone countless births and deaths already.

又復說言：我觀世間有情，不易可得長夜流轉不為汝等若母，若父、兄弟、姊妹，若軌範師，若親教師，若餘尊重，若等尊重。又如說言：若一補特伽羅於一劫中所受身骨，假使有人為其積集不爛壞者，其聚量高王舍城側廣博脅山。

And further: I survey the beings of this world, and can find hardly any who, through that long night of transmigration, have not at some time been your mother, your father, your brother, your sister, your preceptor, your ordination master, or someone else deserving reverence, or an equal to be revered. And it is further said: were someone to gather up, unspoiled, all the bones a single person receives across the bodies of just one single eon, the resulting pile would stand as high as Mount Vipula beside Rājagṛha.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads the four-finger tally as a deliberately impossible thought experiment — every plant on earth reduced to counting-sticks, exhausted long before the true number of one's own past parents could be reached — designed to defeat the mind's instinct to treat "countless" as a mere figure of speech rather than an actual, if incomprehensible, magnitude. He draws out the ethical payoff explicitly: since virtually every being encountered has, across that same immeasurable past, at some point stood in the place of one's own mother, father, or teacher, the natural response is not indifference to strangers but the same reverence owed to kin — the same teaching, he notes, that stands behind the Lotus Sūtra's Bodhisattva Never Disparaging, who bowed to every person he met as a future buddha.

On the closing measure of a single being's bones outstripping a mountain within just one eon — a vanishing fraction of the beginningless past already surveyed — he treats it as the aspect's summary image: pleasure and pain, parents and strangers, all cycle without any fixed pattern to rely on, and the sheer physical residue of that cycling, when actually reckoned, dwarfs anything ordinary intuition would guess. With this, the third aspect, uncertainty of birth, is complete; only the fourth and by far the largest aspect, transmigration (生流轉) — the treatise's full twelve-link analysis of dependent origination — remains before rebirth defilement, and the whole threefold defilement first named at fascicle 8's opening, can close.

34. Transmigration Opens: A Nine-Part Outline, and the Three Aspects of Dependent Origination

Rebirth defilement's fourth and by far largest aspect, transmigration (生流轉), opens by identifying transmigration with dependent origination itself, then lays out a nine-part outline in verse before beginning with dependent origination's own essential nature — the three-part shape by which a past cause gives rise to a present result, which then either continues the cycle forward or turns toward liberation.

Chinese Source

云何生流轉？謂自身所有緣起，當知此即說為流轉。云何緣起？嗢柁南曰：體門義差別，次第難釋詞，緣性分別緣，攝諸經為後。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is transmigration? It is dependent origination as it operates within one's own being — this, it should be understood, is what is called transmigration. What is dependent origination? The summary verse says: essence, gateway, meaning, and differences; sequence, the hard questions, and etymology; conditioning-nature and the analysis of conditions; and, last, what the sūtras themselves say of it.

云何緣起體？若略說，由三種相建立緣起。謂從前際，中際生；從中際，後際生；中際生已，若趣流轉，若趣清淨究竟。

What is the essence of dependent origination? Briefly, dependent origination is established through three aspects: the present arising from the past; the future arising from the present; and, once the present has arisen, its either continuing onward in transmigration or turning toward the final end of purification.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi flags the nine-part outline named in the summary verse as without real parallel elsewhere in Buddhist literature for its sheer thoroughness — essence, the sense-gateways through which it operates, its underlying meaning, its many differences, its fixed sequence, a set of hard questions it must answer, the etymology of "dependent origination" itself, its relationship to the four kinds of condition named earlier in the fascicle's own causal analysis, a link-by-link breakdown, and finally a review of how various sūtras themselves describe it — a comprehensiveness he reads as marking this as one of the treatise's central teachings, on par with the self-refutation of fascicle 6 or the ten causes of fascicle 5.

On the threefold essence, he underscores that this is the same twelve-link chain already implied throughout the treatise's causal apparatus, now given its own dedicated, systematic treatment: a being's present existence (the "middle time") is the direct product of ignorance and karma accumulated in a past life (the "prior time"); that present existence, in turn, either generates the causes for yet another future life (the "later time"), continuing the wheel exactly as before, or — for the one who has actually taken up the path — turns instead toward dependent origination's own cessation, culminating in nirvāṇa. He treats this branching as the whole point of the analysis to come: the same twelve links that explain how ordinary beings remain bound also show, precisely and mechanically, the point at which the chain can be broken.

35. From Karma-Bound Consciousness to a New Body in the Womb

The detailed walkthrough of "the present arising from the past" begins: ignorance-driven karma leaves consciousness itself karma-bound, carrying its seed unbroken until death; then, at the moment of dying, twin cravings — for a self and for a place — draw that same consciousness into a new womb, where it unfolds through the eight classical embryonic stages already named in earlier fascicles, now explained specifically as cause-consciousness ripening into result-consciousness.

Chinese Source

由此隨業識，乃至命終流轉不絕，能為後有相續識因。此識將生果時，由內外貪愛正現在前以為助伴，從彼前際既捨命已，於現在世自體得生。

Ru-Yi Translation

By this, consciousness becomes karma-bound consciousness, flowing unbroken all the way to the end of life, and thereby serving as the cause of the continuing consciousness of a further existence. As this consciousness is about to give rise to its result, inward and outward craving stand immediately present as its companion, and once life in that former existence has been relinquished, one's own being arises anew in the present life.

在母腹中，以因識為緣，相續果識前後次第而生，乃至羯羅藍等位差別而轉。於母胎中，相續果識與名色俱，乃至衰老，漸漸增長。爾時感生受業名色與異熟果。

Within the mother's womb, taking cause-consciousness as condition, the continuing result-consciousness arises in due sequence, unfolding in turn through the kalala stage and the other differentiated stages that follow. Within the womb, this continuing result-consciousness occurs together with name-and-form, and gradually develops all the way through to old age. At every such stage, it should be understood that karma ripening in the very next life is what produces both this name-and-form and this ripened result.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi clarifies the terminology: the same storehouse consciousness is called "karma-bound consciousness" once a fresh deed has perfumed it with a new seed, "cause-consciousness" while that seed still abides in potential, and "result-consciousness" once it has actually ripened into a new life — one single continuous stream, named differently only according to which phase of its own unfolding is being described. He singles out the two cravings drawing consciousness into its next womb — the inward craving that simply cannot tolerate the prospect of no further existence at all, and the outward craving drawn toward whatever future circumstance karma has already fixed as its destination — as the same mechanism the treatise's much earlier account of conception, back in fascicle 1, first described from the experiential side; this passage now supplies its formal causal name.

He notes the eight embryonic stages (kalala, arbuda, peśī, ghana, praśākhā, the stage of hair/nails, the stage of the faculties, and the stage of full bodily form) and the subsequent arc through infancy, childhood, youth, and old age were already traced in physical, developmental detail across fascicles 1 and 2; here the same ground is revisited only to make one specific causal point — that name-and-form and the ripening consciousness arise strictly together, neither one ever appearing without the other, which is the very thesis the next link in the chain, "name-and-form conditions the six sense-bases," exists to unpack.

36. Consciousness and Name-and-Form, Bundled Like Two Reeds

The treatise turns to explain why consciousness and name-and-form are said to condition each other mutually rather than in one direction only — using the classical image of two bundled reeds, neither able to stand without the other — then extends the whole picture to the four modes of birth and the three kinds of karma that determine which realm a being is born into.

Chinese Source

又此異熟識即依名色而轉，由必依託六依轉故，是故經言名色緣識。由識執受諸根，墮相續法方得流轉；故此二種依止於識相續不斷。由此道理，於現在世，識緣名色，名色緣識，猶如束蘆，乃至命終相依而轉。如是名為從前際，中際諸行緣起生；中際生已，流轉不絕。

Ru-Yi Translation

Further, this ripening consciousness operates only in dependence on name-and-form, since it must operate resting on the six sense-bases — this is why the sūtras say name-and-form conditions consciousness. And because consciousness grasps and sustains the sense-faculties, they fall into the pattern of a continuing process and are thereby able to transmigrate — so these two, in turn, depend on consciousness to continue unbroken. By this same reasoning, in the present life, consciousness conditions name-and-form and name-and-form conditions consciousness, exactly like two bundled reeds, depending on each other all the way to the end of life. This, then, is what is meant by dependent origination's arising from the past into the present, and by the present, once arisen, continuing to flow on unbroken.

當知此中依胎生者，說流轉次第。若卵生濕生者，除處母胎，餘如前說。若於有色有情聚中，謂欲色界受化生者，諸根決定圓滿而生，與前差別。若於無色界，以名為依，及色種子為依，識得生起；以識為依，名及色種子轉。從此種子，色雖斷絕，後更得生；與前差別。

It should be understood that the sequence of transmigration just given describes those born from the womb. For those born from an egg or from moisture, the whole account applies just the same, apart from the stage of dwelling in a mother's womb. Among embodied beings born by spontaneous transformation — desire-realm and form-realm beings alike — every faculty arises at once, fully complete, unlike the gradual unfolding just described. In the formless realm, consciousness arises resting on name and on the seed of form; and, in turn, name and the seed of form continue resting on consciousness. Though form's actual manifestation is thereby interrupted there, it will arise again later from this same seed — this is how the formless realm differs from the rest.

又由福業，生欲界人天；由非福業，生諸惡趣；由不動業，生色無色界。云何不生？由不生故，趣清淨究竟。

Further: by meritorious karma one is born among the humans and gods of the desire realm; by demeritorious karma one is born into the unfortunate destinies; by unmoving karma one is born into the form or formless realm. And what is non-arising? It is by such non-arising that one turns toward the final end of purification.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the bundled-reeds image as making a precise point: consciousness cannot exist floating free of a body, and a body's faculties cannot remain alive and functioning without consciousness sustaining them — remove either reed and the whole bundle collapses, exactly as a living being's psychophysical continuity collapses the instant either side is missing. He flags that the commentarial tradition reads "consciousness" in this particular pairing two different ways depending on which vehicle's teaching is in view: read through the śrāvaka path's own vocabulary, before the storehouse consciousness (ālayavijñāna) had been formally established as a distinct teaching, "consciousness" here means the six operating consciousnesses collectively; read through the fully developed Yogācāra account this treatise itself teaches elsewhere, it is specifically the eighth, storehouse consciousness that grasps and sustains the sense-faculties without interruption from conception to death, making the six active consciousnesses instead a further, dependent layer arising from it.

On the differences by mode of birth, he notes womb-birth's gradual unfolding (already traced through the eight embryonic stages in the previous section) is in fact the exception rather than the rule across the full range of possible existence: egg- and moisture-born beings skip only the womb stage; spontaneously arising beings — every desire-realm and form-realm god — appear with every faculty already complete, with no developmental period at all; and formless-realm beings dispense with physical form altogether, retaining only its dormant seed, ready to manifest again the moment a future rebirth calls for a body once more. He closes by underscoring the three-line summary linking meritorious, demeritorious, and unmoving karma directly to their destinations as the fascicle's own recap of ground already covered across fascicles 4 through 9, now folded explicitly into the twelve-link chain — with the single word "non-arising" left as a placeholder for the cessation side of the teaching, to be unpacked once the forward-flowing side of dependent origination is fully told.

37. Two Kinds of Ignorance, and the Projecting Cause That Plants Five Seeds for the Next Life

The treatise now turns from "past to present" to "present to future," identifying the two results any present being already carries from past karma, then naming the specific ignorance — not knowing the present result and not knowing the coming one — that drives fresh karma to project five seeds (name-and-form, the six bases, contact, and feeling) toward a further existence.

Chinese Source

謂中際已生補特伽羅，受二種先業果。謂受內異熟果，及境界所生受增上果。此補特伽羅或聞非正法故，或先串習故，於二果愚。由愚內異熟果故，於後有生苦不如實知；由迷後有後際無明增上力故，如前於諸行若作若增長。

Ru-Yi Translation

A person who has already arisen in the present receives two results from past karma: the inward ripened result, and the dominant result arising from contact with the outward environment. Such a person, whether from hearing teachings that are not the genuine Dharma, or from prior habituation, remains ignorant of both these results. Through ignorance of the inward ripened result, one fails to truly know the suffering of a further existence; and through this same delusion about that further existence — ignorance regarding what is yet to come — one continues, exactly as before, either performing or intensifying karmic formations.

由此新所作業，故說此識名隨業識。即於現法中，說無明為緣故行生，行為緣故識生。此識於現法中名為因識，能攝受後生果識故。又即此識是後有名色種子之所隨逐，此名色種子是後有六處種子之所隨逐，此六處種子是後有觸種子之所隨逐，此觸種子是後有受種子之所隨逐。如是總名於中際中後有引因應知。

By this newly performed karma, this consciousness is called karma-bound consciousness. It is in this way that, within the present existence, ignorance conditions the arising of formations, and formations condition the arising of consciousness. Within the present existence this consciousness is called cause-consciousness, since it is able to gather in the result-consciousness of a further birth. And this same consciousness is followed by the seed of a further existence's name-and-form; that seed of name-and-form is followed by the seed of the six sense-bases of a further existence; that seed of the six sense-bases is followed by the seed of contact; and that seed of contact is followed by the seed of feeling. All of this, taken together, should be understood as the projecting cause laid down within the present existence for a further existence.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats the two results — the inward ripened result (one's own body and mind, whatever its health or fortune) and the outward dominant result (the shared physical environment one experiences it in) — as simply the treatise's own earlier fivefold-result and karma-classification material, now explicitly folded into the twelve-link framework. The critical move, he stresses, is what happens next: rather than recognizing both results as impermanent and selfless, ordinary ignorance mistakes the present body for "myself, the one experiencing this," and remains equally clueless about what a next existence might hold — and it is precisely this second, forward-facing ignorance ("later-existence ignorance") that drives fresh karmic formations, exactly repeating the very first link of the whole chain, only now within the present life rather than the past one.

He unpacks "cause-consciousness" and its five trailing seeds as the treatise's technical account of what actually gets planted by that fresh karma: not a single undifferentiated potential, but a graduated sequence of seeds — name-and-form, the six sense-bases, contact, and feeling — each one, in the commentarial gloss, "trailing" the one before it only in the sense of the order they will unfold in during some later life, not because they are laid down at different moments now; all five are perfumed into the storehouse consciousness together, in a single stroke, at the very moment karma is performed. He offers Venerable Miàojìng's dumpling illustration to make the driving force vivid: simply hearing the word "dumpling" at night, with no dumpling anywhere near, is enough to make the mouth water — proof of how deeply ingrained, automatic, and largely unconscious this same craving-grasping machinery already is, running underneath ordinary daily life long before any grand act of wrongdoing is required to keep the twelve-link chain turning.

38. Craving, Grasping, and Becoming: The Generating Cause That Waters the Seed

Having named the projecting cause that plants the seeds, the treatise now explains the generating cause that actually ripens them: feeling gives rise to craving, craving hardens into grasping (of four distinct kinds), and grasping's moisture turns the previously projected seeds into "becoming" — karma now ripe and ready to produce the very next life.

Chinese Source

由先異熟果愚引後有已，又由第二境界所生受果愚故，起緣境界受愛。由此愛故，或發欲求，或發有求；或執欲取，或執見、戒及我語取。由此愛取和合資潤，令前引因轉名為有。即是後有生因所攝。

Ru-Yi Translation

Having already, through ignorance of the inward ripened result, projected a further existence, one is further, through ignorance of the result arising from the outward environment, moved to crave the feeling that contact with that environment produces. Through this craving, one gives rise either to craving for sensory desire or craving for further existence; and one clings either to the grasping of sensory desire, or to the grasping of views, of precepts and vows, or of the doctrine of a self. Through the moistening union of this craving and grasping, the previously projected cause is transformed and named "becoming" — and this, precisely, is what is meant by the generating cause of a further existence.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi traces the sequence as a tightening spiral: bare feeling, arising from contact with the world, would be karmically inert on its own — it is only the second layer of ignorance (mistaking that feeling as something genuinely worth pursuing or avoiding) that turns it into craving. Craving then splits two ways: craving for sensory desire, aimed at the desire realm's pleasures, and craving for further existence, aimed at rebirth in the form or formless realm through meditative attainment. Craving, sustained, hardens into one of four kinds of grasping — grasping at sensory desire itself; grasping at one's own views as supreme (the same view-grasping already anatomized across the sixteen non-Buddhist doctrines in fascicles 6–7); grasping at precepts and vows, mistaking some external rule of conduct as itself the cause of liberation; and grasping at the very notion of a self, clinging to "I" as though it named something real rather than a mere manner of speaking.

The pivotal technical move, he stresses, is what craving and grasping actually do to the seeds already laid down by the projecting cause: they moisten them, the way water moistens a dormant seed in the ground, converting what was until now mere dormant potential — the projected seeds of name-and-form, the six bases, contact, and feeling — into "becoming" (有), karma now primed and certain to ripen into an actual next existence. He notes one small but real difference from Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi's own account: that later Yogācāra text names formations themselves as beginning the seed-set moistened into becoming, while this treatise names only the five links from consciousness through feeling — a minor variation in scope around the same underlying doctrine. With craving, grasping, and becoming now covered, this closes the generating cause, and with it both halves of the mechanism — projecting cause and generating cause together — by which the present existence gives rise to the next.

39. From Death to the Next Birth, and Why Grief Belongs at Birth, Not Death

The treatise now completes the ordinary picture — how the projected and moistened seeds actually manifest as a further existence, whether gradually or all at once — then restates the whole present-life sequence from contact through old age, sickness, and death in a single unbroken formula, closing with the striking observation that birth, not death, is where suffering's root actually lies.

Chinese Source

從此無間，命既終已，隨先引因所引識等受最為後，此諸行生，或漸或頓。如是於現法中，無明觸所生受為緣故愛，愛為緣故取，取為緣故有，有為緣故生，生為緣故老病死等諸苦差別。或於生處次第現前，或復種子隨逐應知。

Ru-Yi Translation

Immediately following this, once life has ended, the formations belonging to consciousness and the rest of the seeds trailing from the earlier projecting cause arise — either gradually or all at once. And so, within the present existence: feeling arisen from ignorance-tainted contact conditions craving; craving conditions grasping; grasping conditions becoming; becoming conditions birth; and birth conditions the varied sufferings of old age, sickness, and death. These may appear in fixed sequence at the very place of birth, or they may instead remain as trailing seeds still awaiting their occasion — so it should be understood.

如是於中際中，無明緣行等、受緣愛等為因緣故，後際諸行生。

Thus, within the present existence, it is by ignorance conditioning formations and the rest, and by feeling conditioning craving and the rest, taken together as cause and condition, that the formations of a further existence arise.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi maps "gradual" onto womb- and egg-birth, where a being's own faculties unfold in the four stages of conception, equal growth, emergence, and gradual maturing already described earlier in the fascicle, and "sudden" onto moisture- and spontaneous birth, where the full body appears complete in a single instant. He treats the closing formula — feeling, craving, grasping, becoming, birth, old age, sickness, death — as the treatise's own compressed restatement of everything the two causes chapter has just spent several sections unpacking link by link, now run through once more as a single continuous chain, with old age and sickness explicitly said to sometimes remain dormant as unmanifested seed (a form-realm or desire-heaven god may carry no visible signs of aging at all, yet still carries the seed for it, ready to ripen the moment a future rebirth calls for it).

He draws out the treatise's own striking reframing at this point: ordinary people weep at death and rejoice at birth, but from the standpoint of the twelve links, birth is the actual root cause and death merely one of its several inevitable consequences, alongside aging and sickness — so if grief is to be felt anywhere, birth is the more logically consistent place for it, since it is birth alone that can be prevented through the path, while death, once birth has already occurred, cannot be avoided by any being whatsoever. With this, the ordinary, unliberated side of "the present existence generating a further one" is complete; the treatise now turns to the alternative — the one who instead turns this same chain toward its own cessation.

40. Turning Toward Purification: Hearing the Dharma, and an Arhat's Karma That Even Prison Bars Could Not Undo

Against the ordinary forward flow, the treatise now describes the alternative path: one who has already gathered the provisions for practice hears the genuine teaching, reflects soundly on the four noble truths, and thereby gives rise to right view, advancing in sequence toward the purified insight of a trainee and finally an arhat — illustrated by a classical story showing that even an arhat's already-ripened karma cannot simply be waved away.

Chinese Source

復有先集資糧，於現法中，從他聞音，及於二果諸行，若於彼因、彼滅、彼趣滅行如理作意，由此如理作意為緣，正見得生，從此次第得學無學清淨智見。

Ru-Yi Translation

There is, further, one who has already gathered the provisions for practice, who in the present existence hears the teaching from another, and who applies sound reasoning attention to the two results just described, to their cause, to their cessation, and to the practice leading to that cessation. By this sound reasoning attention as condition, right view arises; and from this, in due sequence, one attains the purified wisdom and insight of a trainee, and then of one beyond training.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi identifies the four objects of sound reasoning attention here as none other than the four noble truths in disguise: the two results (the ripened body and the environment it experiences) are simply suffering itself; their cause is craving and grasping, the origin of suffering; their cessation is nirvāṇa; and the practice leading to that cessation is the path — so this passage restates the entire Buddhist path in the twelve-link chain's own vocabulary. He traces the resulting progression exactly as elsewhere in the treatise: sound reasoning attention gives rise to a still-contaminated right view at the level of preparatory practice, culminating in the single moment of uncontaminated insight that marks stream-entry, then advancing through once-returner and non-returner to the final, complete purification of arhatship — trainee and non-trainee wisdom differing, he stresses, only in degree of completeness, not in kind.

He relates the Mahāvibhāṣā's account of an arhat wrongly imprisoned as a caution against assuming attainment erases karma's own momentum: a monk dyeing robes was falsely accused of having slaughtered a cattle-owner's missing ox — the dye vat mistaken, through the sheer force of the accuser's own ripening karma, for blood and hide — and was jailed for years, his very existence forgotten by his own monastery, his psychic powers rendered useless against karma already in motion, until the debt was paid and he was freed, later performing wonders that left the king who had unknowingly imprisoned him in awed repentance. Cháng Bǎi pairs this with a second story from the same source: a stream-enterer in the arhat's company, hearing of the king's fault, allowed a single flash of resentment on the arhat's behalf — never voicing it, never acting on it — yet a guardian spirit, sensing that thought, avenged it by burying the king's entire city in a flood. He draws the moral sharply: only a fully liberated arhat's mind, in or out of meditation, never stirs even a single ill-willed thought; a lesser trainee's mind, though vastly improved, can still leak — and its consequences, carried out by forces well beyond the trainee's own intention, can be entirely disproportionate to how faint the original thought was.

41. When Ignorance Ceases, the Whole Chain Ceases: Wisdom-Liberation and Mind-Liberation

The treatise now gives dependent origination's most celebrated formula in reverse: exactly as each link in the forward chain conditions the next, so the severing of ignorance and craving unravels the entire sequence link by link, culminating in the classical twin liberations — liberation through wisdom, which ends ignorance, and liberation through mind, which ends craving.

Chinese Source

由此智見，無明及愛永斷無餘。由此斷故，於彼所緣不如實知無明觸所生受亦復永斷。由此斷故，永離無明，於現法中證慧解脫。若於無明觸所生受相應心中所有貪愛，即於此心得離繫故，貪愛永滅，於現法中證心解脫。

Ru-Yi Translation

By this wisdom and insight, ignorance and craving are severed without remainder. By this severing, the feeling arisen from ignorance-tainted contact toward its object, no longer known truly, is likewise severed forever. By this severing, ignorance is forever left behind, and liberation through wisdom is realized in this very existence. And the craving present in the mind associated with feeling arisen from ignorance-tainted contact, once that mind is released from this bondage, is forever extinguished, and liberation through mind is realized in this very existence.

設彼無明不永斷者，依於識等受最為後所有諸行後際應生；由無明滅故，更不復起，得無生法。是故說言：無明滅故，行滅；次第乃至異熟生觸滅故，異熟生受滅。

Were that ignorance not forever severed, the formations belonging to consciousness and the rest, down to feeling, would still arise in a further existence; but because ignorance has ceased, they no longer arise at all, and the state of non-arising is attained. This is why it is said: because ignorance ceases, formations cease; and so in sequence, down to: because ripened contact ceases, ripened feeling ceases.

於現法中無明滅故，無明觸滅；無明觸滅故，無明觸所生受滅；無明觸所生受滅故，愛滅；愛滅故，如前得無生法。由此故說取等惱最為後諸行永滅。

Within the present existence, because ignorance ceases, ignorance-tainted contact ceases; because ignorance-tainted contact ceases, the feeling arisen from it ceases; because that feeling ceases, craving ceases; and because craving ceases, the state of non-arising is attained just as before. This is why it is said that grasping and the rest, down to every remaining formation, are forever extinguished.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi presents this as the twelve-link chain's own mirror image: every forward link named across this fascicle's long causal exposition — ignorance to formations, formations to consciousness, all the way to feeling — now runs in exact reverse, each cessation causing the next, until nothing remains to project a further existence at all. He distinguishes the two resulting liberations precisely: wisdom-liberation is what results specifically from ignorance's own extinction — the practitioner now sees rather than merely believes that the aggregates are selfless; mind-liberation is what results from craving's extinction — the practitioner's mind no longer clings to any of the three realms' pleasures, whatever it encounters. An arhat, he notes, achieves both simultaneously and completely, whereas the phrase "wisdom-liberated only" describes certain arhats in the canon who lack the deeper meditative absorptions but have nonetheless fully uprooted both ignorance and craving at the level that matters for liberation itself.

He flags the "grasping and the rest" clause as silently completing the whole remaining chain by implication — grasping, becoming, birth, old age, sickness, death, sorrow, lamentation, suffering, and distress all fall away together the instant craving is cut, since every one of them was only ever craving's own downstream consequence. This reverse formula, he adds, is among the most frequently cited passages in the entire Āgama corpus precisely because of its symmetry: it proves the forward chain is not fate but mechanism, and a mechanism, unlike fate, can be dismantled at any one of its links once the practitioner knows exactly where to apply the wisdom that does the dismantling.

42. Two Nirvāṇas: Liberation Still Wearing a Body, and Liberation Wearing None

The treatise now defines nirvāṇa itself in two stages — the nirvāṇa with residue, realized the moment craving and ignorance are severed while the body born of past karma still lives out its natural span, and the nirvāṇa without residue, realized when that final body's consciousness and every feeling quietly cease at death, never to arise again.

Chinese Source

如是於現法中諸行不轉；由不轉故，於現法中，於有餘依界證得現法涅槃。彼於爾時，唯餘清淨識緣名色，名色緣識；乃至有識身在，恆受離繫受，非有繫受。此有識身乃至先業所引壽量恆相續住。若壽量盡，便捨識所持身。此命根後所有命根無餘永滅，更不重熟。

Ru-Yi Translation

Thus, within the present existence, these formations no longer operate; and because they no longer operate, one realizes, within the present existence, nirvāṇa already attained in this very life, within the nirvāṇa-realm with residue remaining. At that time there remains only a purified consciousness conditioning name-and-form, and name-and-form conditioning consciousness; and for as long as this embodied consciousness endures, one constantly experiences only feeling released from bondage, never feeling still bound. This embodied consciousness continues on for exactly as long as the lifespan drawn by past karma lasts. Once that lifespan is exhausted, the body sustained by consciousness is relinquished, and this life-faculty, once ended, ceases entirely without remainder, never to ripen again.

又復此識與一切受任運滅故，所餘因緣先已滅故，不復相續，永滅無餘，是名無餘依涅槃界，究竟寂靜處。亦名趣求涅槃者，於世尊所梵行已立，究竟涅槃。

And further, once this consciousness and every feeling cease of their own accord, and since every other supporting cause has already ceased as well, nothing continues on — a final cessation without remainder. This is called the nirvāṇa-realm without residue remaining, the place of ultimate tranquility. One who has reached it is also called one who has attained the goal of nirvāṇa: the holy life has been established under the Blessed One, and nirvāṇa is now complete.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi frames the two nirvāṇas by what is left over: with residue remaining, an arhat's body — the ripened fruit of karma sown before liberation — still lives out its allotted span, so name-and-form and consciousness still condition each other exactly as before, only now purified, since every feeling that arises is felt free of bondage rather than tangled in craving; without residue remaining, once that final lifespan is spent, no further body ever arises to replace it, and consciousness and feeling alike simply cease, with no further cause left standing to produce anything more. He cites the treatise's own later fourfold description of an arhat's residual tranquility — tranquility from suffering (the palm tree cut at the root, incapable of ever sprouting again), tranquility from affliction (craving, hatred, and delusion all permanently exhausted), tranquility from ever again harming a being (no further unwholesome deed possible), and tranquility of equanimity (seeing, hearing, and every other sense contact met with neither joy nor sorrow, only settled mindfulness) — as filling out exactly what "purified" consciousness looks like while the body still lives.

He closes with Venerable Miàojìng's own pointed question to students: do you actually want a state with no eyes, no ears, no colors, no sounds, no friends, no family — nothing at all? Most honestly admit they do not, still comfortable in the world of the six senses and their objects. Cháng Bǎi treats the honesty of that answer as itself instructive: genuinely delighting in nirvāṇa, rather than merely assenting to it as a doctrine, requires having actually realized selflessness — an aspiration far harder to make sincere than to state.

43. Dependent Origination's Essence, Fully Established — the First of Nine Gateways Closes

A brief closing statement gathers the three aspects just explained — past giving rise to present, present giving rise to future, and the present's fork toward either continued transmigration or purification — into a single definition of dependent origination's own essence, closing the very first of the nine topics named in the fascicle's opening summary verse.

Chinese Source

如是已說由三種相建立緣起，謂從前際，中際生；從中際，後際生；又於中際，若流轉，若清淨；是名緣起體性。

Ru-Yi Translation

Thus it has been explained that dependent origination is established through three aspects: the present arising from the past; the future arising from the present; and, within the present itself, either continuing in transmigration or turning toward purification. This is called the essence of dependent origination.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi treats this single closing line as deliberately anticlimactic by design — after the treatise's long, technical unfolding of two lifetimes' worth of causes and results, the ordinary forward chain, and the full path through wisdom-liberation, mind-liberation, and both nirvāṇas, the essence itself turns out to be stated in one sentence, because everything else was simply that one sentence's own detailed unpacking. He notes this closes 緣起體 (dependent origination's essence), the first of the nine topics named in the summary verse that opened transmigration's fourth aspect — essence, gateway, meaning, differences, sequence, the hard questions, etymology, conditioning-nature, and the sūtras' own testimony — with essence now fully established, and eight further topics, including the treatise's promised gateway-by-gateway and link-by-link analysis, still ahead before rebirth defilement itself, and the whole three-part defilement first opened in fascicle 8, can finally close.

44. Eight Gateways Where Dependent Origination Operates, from a Single Thought to a Whole World-System

The treatise's second topic, gateway, shows that dependent origination is not confined to the twelve-link chain alone but operates through eight distinct domains — from a single act of seeing, through a crop ripening in a field, to an entire world-system's formation and collapse, and finally to the very gateway by which the whole cycle can be purified.

Chinese Source

緣起門云何？謂依八門，緣起流轉。一、內識生門，二、外稼成熟門，三、有情世間死生門，四、器世間成壞門，五、食任持門，六、自所作業增上勢力受用隨業所得愛非愛果門，七、威勢門，八、清淨門。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the gateway of dependent origination? Dependent origination flows through eight gateways. First, the gateway of an inward consciousness arising; second, the gateway of an outward crop ripening; third, the gateway of a sentient being's death and birth; fourth, the gateway of a physical world's formation and destruction; fifth, the gateway of the four foods sustaining life; sixth, the gateway of one's own accumulated karma's dominant power determining what desirable or undesirable result is experienced; seventh, the gateway of mastery; eighth, the gateway of purification.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi cross-references this list directly to the Sūtra on the Foremost Gateway to Dependent Origination, matching each of the eight to its own sūtra passage: the first is simply eye and form meeting to produce eye-consciousness, and the contact, feeling, and craving that follow — dependent origination operating inside a single moment of perception; the second is a grain of rice, given field, water, and season, sprouting and ripening into food; the third is the full twelve-link chain already explained at length, tracing an entire lifetime's death and rebirth; the fourth is the shared karma of countless beings jointly producing and eventually destroying a world-system, the same cosmology surveyed in fascicle 4; the fifth is the four foods — edible, contact, mental intention, and consciousness — sustaining a body once it exists; the sixth is wholesome and unwholesome deeds respectively producing a fortunate or unfortunate destiny; the seventh is a meditator's mastery over concentration making wished-for accomplishments actually manifest, exactly as intended, without deviation; and the eighth is hearing genuine teaching and applying sound reasoning attention until ignorance itself is extinguished, the very same cessation formula given in the previous section, now identified as its own distinct gateway.

He treats the sheer breadth of this list as making a single point: dependent origination is not one narrow doctrine about rebirth alone, but the treatise's general account of how absolutely anything — a thought, a crop, a life, a galaxy, a meal, a destiny, a meditative power, or liberation itself — comes to be, each instance following the identical logic of causes and conditions, however different the domains appear on the surface.

45. Eight Layers of "Dependent Origination's Meaning" — Answering Every Rival School at Once

The treatise's third topic peels back what "dependent origination" actually means through eight successive layers, each one closing off a specific rival philosophical claim — no controlling self, yet not static; momentary, yet not without brief continuity; dependent, yet with no real agency in the parts; and, running through it all, an unbroken, unmixed, self-made causal thread.

Chinese Source

緣起義云何？謂離有情義，是緣起義；於離有情復無常義，是緣起義；於無常復暫住義，是緣起義；於暫住復依他義，是緣起義；於依他離作用義，是緣起義；於離作用復因果相續不斷義，是緣起義；於因果相續不斷，復因果相似轉義，是緣起義；於因果相似轉，復自業所作義，是緣起義。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is the meaning of dependent origination? The meaning of being free of any controlling being is the meaning of dependent origination. Free of any such being, and further impermanent — this too is the meaning of dependent origination. Impermanent, and further briefly persisting — this too is the meaning of dependent origination. Briefly persisting, and further dependent on other conditions — this too is the meaning of dependent origination. Dependent on other conditions, yet devoid of any real agency within them — this too is the meaning of dependent origination. Devoid of real agency, and yet cause and result continue unbroken — this too is the meaning of dependent origination. Continuing unbroken, and further with cause and result corresponding to each other — this too is the meaning of dependent origination. Corresponding to each other, and further each being the product of one's own karma alone — this too is the meaning of dependent origination.

問：為顯何義建立緣起耶？答：為顯因緣所攝染汙、清淨義故。

Question: To reveal what purpose is dependent origination established? Answer: To reveal the defiled and the purified meanings both contained within causes and conditions.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi reads these eight layers as a deliberately sequenced refutation of eight distinct rival positions, each building on the one before: free of any controlling being denies both the Vaiśeṣika's independent self and the Buddhist Vātsīputrīya sect's own quasi-self, since there is neither an agent who acts nor a recipient who experiences the result — only volition doing the acting and contact-born feeling doing the experiencing. Impermanent denies the Sāṃkhya's changeless primal nature and certain Buddhist schools' claim that dependent origination itself is an unconditioned, eternal law. Briefly persisting denies the Sammitīya's claim that a conditioned thing's four characteristic phases are not truly momentary. Dependent on other conditions denies the naturalists' claim that things arise spontaneously, needing no causal support at all. Devoid of real agency denies the Sarvāstivāda's claim that a condition itself performs some genuine causal action, when in truth — as the treatise elsewhere puts it, using the same bundled-reeds image already met earlier in the fascicle — a conditioned thing is neither self-made, other-made, both, nor uncaused, only conventionally described as if it acted.

Unbroken continuity denies both the nihilist's claim that cause and result simply stop and the Sautrāntika's claim that ripening cause and ripened result are never truly simultaneous, leaving a gap; matching cause and result denies the doctrine (already refuted in fascicle 6 as "ritual killing is the true Dharma") that an unwholesome cause like slaughter could ever produce a wholesome result like heaven; and self-made denies both the nihilist who denies causation altogether and the "unequal-cause" theorists who claim one being's actions could ripen as another's result — karma, he stresses, is always and only one's own. He closes by noting the treatise's own answer to why any of this needed establishing at all: not abstract philosophy for its own sake, but precisely to make visible, within the single fact of causes and conditions, both the defiled chain that keeps a being bound and the purified chain that sets it free — the same fork between transmigration and cessation that has run through the entire chapter.

46. Nineteen Faces of Ignorance, the First Link Anatomized in Full

The treatise's fourth topic, differences, now works through each of the twelve links in turn, beginning with ignorance itself — and rather than treating it as one undifferentiated darkness, names nineteen distinct varieties, each a specific, nameable way the mind fails to know something it needs to know.

Chinese Source

於前際無知云何？謂於過去諸行起不如理分別，謂我於過去為曾有耶？為曾無耶？曾何體性？曾何種類？所有無知。於後際無知云何？謂於未來諸行起不如理分別，謂我於未來為當有耶？為當無耶？當何體性？當何種類？所有無知。於前後際無知云何？謂於內起不如理猶預，謂何等是我？我為何等？今此有情從何所來？於此沒已當往何所？所有無知。

Ru-Yi Translation

What is ignorance regarding the past? Unsound reflection on past formations — was I, or was I not; what nature did I have; what kind of being was I — all this not-knowing. What is ignorance regarding the future? The same unsound reflection turned toward what is yet to come. What is ignorance regarding both past and future together? Inward, unsound uncertainty — what is this "I"; what is "I" itself; where has this being come from, and where will it go once it perishes here — all this not-knowing.

於內無知，謂於各別諸行起不如理作意，謂之為我所有無知。於外無知，謂於外非有情數諸行起不如理作意，謂為我所所有無知。於內外無知，謂於他相續諸行起不如理分別，謂怨親中所有無知。於業無知，謂於諸業起不如理分別，謂有作者所有無知。於異熟無知，謂於異熟果所攝諸行起不如理分別，謂有受者所有無知。於業異熟無知，謂於業及果起不如理分別所有無知。

Ignorance regarding what is inward mistakes one's own aggregates for "I." Ignorance regarding what is outward mistakes non-sentient things for "mine." Ignorance regarding both mistakes another's aggregates as enemy or friend. Ignorance regarding karma mistakes there to be an agent who acts. Ignorance regarding ripened result mistakes there to be a recipient who experiences it. Ignorance regarding karma-and-result together is simply confusion about how the two relate at all.

於佛無知、於法無知、於僧無知者：謂於佛菩提、於正法善說性、於僧正行，或不思惟、或邪思惟、或由放逸、或由疑惑、或由毀謗所有無知。於苦無知云何？謂於苦是苦性，或不思惟、或邪思惟、或由放逸、或由疑惑、或由毀謗所有無知。如於苦，當知於集、滅、道無知亦爾。

Ignorance regarding the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṅgha — toward the Buddha's own awakening, the well-taught nature of the true Dharma, and the Saṅgha's genuine conduct — arises through simply not reflecting, through wrongful reflection, through heedlessness, through doubt, or through outright disparagement. Ignorance regarding suffering is this same failure applied to suffering's own nature as suffering; and, exactly as with suffering, so too with origin, cessation, and the path.

於因無知云何？謂起不如理分別，或計無因，或計自在、世性、士夫、中間等不平等因所有無知。如於因無知，於從因所生諸行亦爾。又彼無罪故名善，有罪故名不善；有利益故名應修習，無利益故名不應修習；黑故名有罪，白故名無罪；雜故名有分。於六觸處如實通達無知云何？謂增上慢者，於所證中顛倒思惟所有無知。如是略說十九種無知。

Ignorance regarding cause is unsound reflection that posits either no cause at all, or an unequal cause — a sovereign lord, primal nature, a cosmic person, or an intermediate creator; and exactly the same ignorance applies to the formations arising from that cause. These, too, are called wholesome when blameless and unwholesome when blameworthy; worth cultivating when beneficial and not worth cultivating when not; dark when blameworthy, bright when blameless, and mixed when both are intermingled. Ignorance regarding true realization of the six fields of contact is the distorted thinking of one who, overestimating their own attainment, mistakes what has not truly been realized for what has. These, briefly stated, are the nineteen kinds of ignorance.

Reading note — after Master Cháng Bǎi

Master Cháng Bǎi groups the nineteen into recognizable clusters: three concern the self across time (past, future, and both at once — the very same "was I, will I be, what am I" questions the treatise's own opening survey of self-view, back in fascicle 6's refutation of the sixteen doctrines, already dismantled at length); three concern the boundary of self and other (inward aggregates mistaken for "I," outward objects mistaken for "mine," another's aggregates mistaken as friend or foe); three concern karma and its ripening (a false agent, a false recipient, and confusion about how deed and result actually connect); nine concern the Three Jewels and the Four Noble Truths, each following an identical fivefold pattern of failure (never reflecting, reflecting wrongly, growing heedless, doubting, or outright slandering); two concern causation itself (positing either no cause or the wrong kind of cause, for both the cause and its resulting formations); and one, standing apart from the rest, names the specific failure of the "overreacher" — a meditator who mistakes a genuine but lesser attainment for full realization, still unable to tell a truly purified contact with the six senses from one merely temporarily calmed by concentration.

He stresses the entire nineteen-fold list is not academic hairsplitting but a diagnostic map: since ordinary ignorance rarely announces itself as such, naming its nineteen specific disguises — mistaking self, mistaking ownership, mistaking agency, doubting the Three Jewels, misreading the Four Truths, misattributing causation, and overestimating one's own progress — gives a practitioner nineteen concrete places to actually look for it, rather than being left to search for a single abstract "delusion" with no specific shape at all.

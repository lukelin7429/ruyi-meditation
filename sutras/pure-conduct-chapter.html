Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A bodhisattva who skillfully applies the mind — 善用其心 — turns every single act of an ordinary day, from putting on a robe to washing a bowl, into a wish for the awakening of all beings; nothing is too small to be practice, and nothing is left outside the path.

Where it sits in the Avataṃsaka

The Avataṃsaka Sūtra (華嚴經, the "Flower Garland Sūtra") is one of the largest and most cosmic scriptures in the Mahāyāna canon — an ocean of interpenetrating worlds, radiant Buddhas, and the fifty-two stages of the bodhisattva path. Inside that vastness, the Chapter of Pure Conduct is something different: a close, practical, almost domestic text. It has no cosmic vision, no other-world Buddha. It simply asks — and then answers, 141 times — how a single day of an ordinary practitioner's life can be turned entirely toward the liberation of others.

This intimacy of scale is exactly why the chapter has always circulated more widely, and been chanted more often, than almost anything else in the Avataṃsaka. It is the one part of this enormous sūtra you can actually carry into a kitchen, a hallway, or a hospital room.

A text older than the sūtra it lives in

The Chapter of Pure Conduct did not start out as a chapter. It circulated independently for centuries before it was folded into the Avataṃsaka — the clearest sign of how prized it was on its own. The earliest known Chinese translation is Lokakṣema's 《諸菩薩求佛本業經》 (2nd century CE, Eastern Han); Zhi Qian of the Wu kingdom translated it again in the 3rd century as 《菩薩本業經》, in the 〈願行品〉 section, and it was Zhi Qian who first cast the material into the verse form used ever since. Two later translators then absorbed it into the full Avataṃsaka: Buddhabhadra placed it as chapter 7 of the 60-fascicle version (418–420 CE), and Śikṣānanda placed it as chapter 11 of the 80-fascicle version (completed 699 CE) — the translation used on this page, and the most widely read of the four surviving versions.

The question: what Sudhīmati asks

The chapter opens with Bodhisattva Sudhīmati (智首, literally "wisdom foremost") putting ten sets of questions to Mañjuśrī — around a hundred qualities in all — asking how a bodhisattva can obtain each one. The questions climb from the most basic (flawless conduct) to the most exalted (the ten powers of a Buddha):

9十王護持the reverence and protection of ten kinds of kings, divine and human
10十種為依、十種超勝being a refuge, a torch, a guide for all beings — and being foremost among all beings

It is a formidable list — the entire résumé of a Buddha, in outline. The drama of the chapter is that Mañjuśrī does not answer it point by point. He answers it with a single principle, and then shows what that principle looks like at breakfast.

The answer: "skillfully applying the mind"

Mañjuśrī opens by praising the question — 善哉！佛子，汝今為欲多所饒益，多所安隱，哀愍世間，利樂天人，問如是義, "Excellent, child of the Buddha — you ask this wishing to benefit many, bring peace to many, have compassion for the world, and bring welfare to gods and humans." Then he collapses the entire hundred-item list into one sentence:

若諸菩薩善用其心，則獲一切勝妙功德。
"If a bodhisattva skillfully applies the mind, every supreme and wondrous virtue follows."

Everything else in the chapter — all 141 verses — is Mañjuśrī making good on that claim, scene by scene. Nothing further is asked of the reader: no austerity, no special circumstance, no monastery required to start. The entire path is contained in how the mind meets each moment. This is also why the chapter closes, after the last verse, by circling back to the very same phrase, now in the past tense: a bodhisattva who has practiced this way — 若諸菩薩如是用心，則獲一切勝妙功德 — becomes unshakable by the whole world, including gods, māras, and even the more limited attainments of the Śrāvaka and Pratyekabuddha vehicles.

The form: one thing seen, one wish made

Each of the 141 verses follows the same four-part shape, tightened by Zhi Qian into verse form and kept ever since:

  1. An ordinary moment — a scene from daily life: waking, dressing, walking a road, seeing a bridge, eating, going to sleep.
  2. The turn — the fixed phrase 當願眾生, "the wish: may all beings…" This hinge is what makes the chapter a practice and not a poem. Nothing is only noticed; everything is dedicated.
  3. The wish — usually two matched four-character clauses, drawing some quality out of the concrete image: a bridge becomes a wish to carry all beings across; muddy water becomes a wish to wash away affliction; a locked door becomes a wish to enter the gate of the Dharma.
  4. Nothing more. No verse explains itself. The four lines are the whole practice — see, turn, wish, move on to the next thing.

A well-known example, from Section VI below: seeing a bridge on the road, 若見橋道，當願眾生，廣度一切，猶如橋梁 — "may all beings be widely carried across, like a bridge." The genius of the form is how ordinary the trigger is and how large the wish becomes.

The nine sections

The 141 verses have no headings in the sūtra itself, but the great Huayan commentator Chengguan (澄觀, 738–839 CE), National Master Qingliang, divided them into nine movements in his 《華嚴經疏鈔》, tracing a single day of a monastic's life from home to sleep. That division, still used in study and chanting today, structures the text on this page:

Notice the shape of the day this traces: home, ordination, meditation, dressing and washing, setting out, the long walk into town noticing everything along the way (by far the largest section — over a third of the whole chapter), begging house to house, the meal, chanting and bowing before returning, and finally sleep. The mundane and the monastic are not separated; the text simply keeps walking, and keeps turning what it sees into a vow.

Why the small things get so much attention

Western readers often expect a sacred text to reserve its intensity for extraordinary moments — a vision, a crisis, a death. This chapter does the opposite. It gives exactly the same four-line treatment to seeing a beautiful sunrise and to using the toilet, to entering deep meditation and to putting on socks. That is the point, not an oversight. If liberation is only available in rare, elevated states, then most of a human life — the eating, dressing, walking, waiting — is wasted time. The Chapter of Pure Conduct refuses that. Every object the eye lands on, every task the hand performs, is treated as sufficient material for the entire bodhisattva path, provided the mind turns it toward all beings. This is also why the chapter has always resonated beyond monastic life: a layperson chanting these verses over a sink of dishes is doing exactly what the text asks.

How to actually use this text

  1. Read one section at a time. The chapter is not meant to be absorbed in one sitting; it is meant to be lived in, section by section, day by day.
  2. Borrow the form for your own day. The deepest use of this text is not memorization but imitation — noticing an ordinary moment in your own life and silently completing it with the wish: may all beings… Boiling water, opening a laptop, waiting at a red light are all fair game; the sūtra's own list was never meant to be exhaustive.
  3. Chant a section before or after the activity it describes. Section I suits a quiet moment at home; the verses on washing (Section IV) suit standing at a sink; the closing verses on sleep (Section IX) suit the edge of the bed.

Two questions readers often ask

"Isn't wishing something for other people, silently, just sentiment — does it actually do anything?" In the Mahāyāna, a formally made wish directed at the awakening of others is not decoration; it is puṇya-pariṇāmanā, the dedication or "turning-over" of merit — a technical act with real weight in the tradition, not unlike setting an intention that shapes everything that follows it. The chapter trains this dedication until it becomes reflexive: not something remembered occasionally, but the mind's default response to whatever it meets.

"Why does a text about compassion for all beings spend so much time on physical routines — teeth, robes, toilets?" Because the alternative is a compassion that only shows up for special occasions, which is to say, a compassion that is mostly absent. By anchoring the wish to the most repeated, least dramatic actions of a day, the chapter makes universal benevolence into a habit of attention rather than an occasional mood — something closer to what contemporary readers might recognize as mindfulness, but aimed outward, at the welfare of others, rather than only inward.

Key terms

Jìngxíng 淨行 — "pure conduct" or "purifying practice." Conduct made pure not by withdrawing from ordinary activity but by turning every act, through a wish, toward the benefit of all beings.
dāng yuàn zhòngshēng 當願眾生 — "the wish: may all beings…" The hinge phrase repeated in all 141 verses, turning a bare observation into a dedication.
gāthā 偈頌 — a verse, typically four lines of four characters in the Chinese of this text. The literary unit of the whole chapter.
shàn yòng qí xīn 善用其心 — "skillfully applying the mind." Mañjuśrī's one-sentence answer to Sudhīmati's hundred questions, and the thesis the rest of the chapter demonstrates.
Sudhīmati 智首菩薩 — "Wisdom Foremost," the bodhisattva who asks the opening questions.
Mañjuśrī 文殊師利菩薩 — the bodhisattva of wisdom, who answers with the 141 verses.
pariṇāmanā 迴向 — dedication, the "turning over" of merit or intention toward the awakening of others; the technical act each verse performs.
bodhisattva 菩薩 — one bound for full buddhahood who practises for the sake of all beings.

I  ·  Living at Home 明菩薩在家時願
Verses 1–11 — the bodhisattva still living as a householder.
1
菩薩在家，當願眾生，知家性空，免其逼迫。

A bodhisattva living at home — the wish: may all beings know that "home" has no fixed nature of its own, and so be freed from its pressures.

2
孝事父母，當願眾生，善事於佛，護養一切。

Serving parents with filial devotion — the wish: may all beings serve the Buddhas well, and protect and nurture all beings.

3
妻子集會，當願眾生，怨親平等，永離貪著。

Gathered with spouse and children — the wish: may all beings see friend and foe as equal, forever free of craving and attachment.

4
若得五欲，當願眾生，拔除欲箭，究竟安隱。

On obtaining the five desired pleasures — the wish: may all beings pull out the arrow of desire, and reach final peace.

5
伎樂聚會，當願眾生，以法自娛，了伎非實。

At a gathering of music and entertainment — the wish: may all beings take their joy in the Dharma alone, understanding that such performances are not real.

6
若在宮室，當願眾生，入於聖地，永除穢欲。

Dwelling in a fine chamber — the wish: may all beings enter the ground of the noble ones, and forever remove impure desire.

7
著瓔珞時，當願眾生，捨諸偽飾，到真實處。

Putting on jeweled ornaments — the wish: may all beings cast off every false adornment, and arrive at the place of truth.

8
上升樓閣，當願眾生，升正法樓，徹見一切。

Climbing up to a high tower — the wish: may all beings ascend the tower of the true Dharma, and see all things through and through.

9
若有所施，當願眾生，一切能捨，心無愛著。

Giving something in charity — the wish: may all beings be able to give up everything, their minds free of clinging attachment.

10
眾會聚集，當願眾生，捨眾聚法，成一切智。

Gathered together in a crowd — the wish: may all beings let go of the ways of the crowd, and accomplish the wisdom that knows all things.

11
若在厄難，當願眾生，隨意自在，所行無礙。

Caught in difficulty and misfortune — the wish: may all beings move as freely as they wish, unobstructed in all they do.

II  ·  Going Forth 明創出家時願
Verses 12–27 — leaving the household, entering the monastery, and taking ordination.
12
捨居家時，當願眾生，出家無礙，心得解脫。

Leaving the household — the wish: may all beings go forth without hindrance, their minds attaining liberation.

13
入僧伽藍，當願眾生，演說種種，無乖諍法。

Entering the monastery grounds — the wish: may all beings expound every kind of teaching without conflict or discord.

14
詣大小師，當願眾生，巧事師長，習行善法。

Approaching one's teachers, senior and junior — the wish: may all beings skillfully serve their teachers and elders, and practice what is good and wholesome.

15
求請出家，當願眾生，得不退法，心無障礙。

Requesting to go forth — the wish: may all beings attain the Dharma that never regresses, their minds free of hindrance.

16
脫去俗服，當願眾生，勤修善根，捨諸罪軛。

Taking off lay clothing — the wish: may all beings diligently cultivate wholesome roots, and cast off every yoke of wrongdoing.

17
剃除鬚髮，當願眾生，永離煩惱，究竟寂滅。

Shaving off hair and beard — the wish: may all beings forever leave the afflictions behind, and reach ultimate stillness.

18
著袈裟衣，當願眾生，心無所染，具大仙道。

Putting on the monastic robe — the wish: may all beings' minds be stained by nothing, and be complete in the way of the great sage.

19
正出家時，當願眾生，同佛出家，救護一切。

At the very moment of going forth — the wish: may all beings go forth just as the Buddha did, and rescue and protect all beings.

20
自歸於佛，當願眾生，紹隆佛種，發無上意。

Taking refuge in the Buddha — the wish: may all beings carry on the Buddha's lineage, and give rise to the unsurpassed intention.

21
自歸於法，當願眾生，深入經藏，智慧如海。

Taking refuge in the Dharma — the wish: may all beings enter deeply into the treasury of the scriptures, their wisdom as vast as the sea.

22
自歸於僧，當願眾生，統理大眾，一切無礙。

Taking refuge in the Saṅgha — the wish: may all beings guide and govern the great assembly, unobstructed in everything.

23
受學戒時，當願眾生，善學於戒，不作眾惡。

Undertaking to study the precepts — the wish: may all beings learn the precepts well, and do no wrong of any kind.

24
受阇梨教，當願眾生，具足威儀，所行真實。

Receiving the ācārya's instruction — the wish: may all beings be complete in dignified conduct, and true in everything they do.

25
受和尚教，當願眾生，入無生智，到無依處。

Receiving the preceptor's instruction — the wish: may all beings enter the wisdom of the unborn, and arrive at the place beyond all reliance.

26
受具足戒，當願眾生，具諸方便，得最勝法。

Receiving the full ordination precepts — the wish: may all beings be complete in every skillful means, and attain the most excellent Dharma.

27
若入堂宇，當願眾生，升無上堂，安住不動。

Entering the hall — the wish: may all beings ascend the unsurpassed hall, and dwell there unmoving.

III  ·  Sitting in Meditation 明修禪定時願
Verses 28–32 — spreading the sitting mat, taking the meditation posture, and entering concentration.
28
若敷床座，當願眾生，開敷善法，見真實相。

Spreading out the sitting mat — the wish: may all beings unfold every wholesome quality, and see the mark of reality as it truly is.

29
正身端坐，當願眾生，坐菩提座，心無所著。

Sitting upright with body composed — the wish: may all beings sit upon the seat of awakening, their minds clinging to nothing.

30
結跏趺坐，當願眾生，善根堅固，得不動地。

Taking the cross-legged posture — the wish: may all beings' wholesome roots grow firm, and reach the ground that cannot be moved.

31
修行於定，當願眾生，以定伏心，究竟無餘。

Practicing meditative concentration — the wish: may all beings subdue the mind through concentration, down to the very last trace.

32
若修於觀，當願眾生，見如實理，永無乖諍。

Practicing insight — the wish: may all beings see reality exactly as it is, forever free of contention.

IV  ·  Deportment at the Dwelling 明住處進止威儀時願
Verses 33–46 — rising from meditation, dressing, cleaning the teeth, and washing.
33
捨跏趺坐，當願眾生，觀諸行法，悉歸散滅。

Rising from the cross-legged seat — the wish: may all beings observe that all conditioned things return to scattering and cessation.

34
下足住時，當願眾生，心得解脫，安住不動。

Setting the foot down to stand — the wish: may all beings' minds attain liberation, and abide unmoving.

35
若舉於足，當願眾生，出生死海，具眾善法。

Lifting the foot to walk — the wish: may all beings rise out of the ocean of birth and death, complete in every wholesome quality.

36
著下裙時，當願眾生，服諸善根，具足慚愧。

Putting on the lower robe — the wish: may all beings be clothed in every wholesome root, complete in conscience and humility.

37
整衣束帶，當願眾生，檢束善根，不令散失。

Straightening the robe and tying the sash — the wish: may all beings hold their wholesome roots in check, and let none of them scatter or be lost.

38
若著上衣，當願眾生，獲勝善根，至法彼岸。

Putting on the upper robe — the wish: may all beings obtain the most excellent wholesome roots, and reach the far shore of the Dharma.

39
著僧伽梨，當願眾生，入第一位，得不動法。

Putting on the great outer robe — the wish: may all beings enter the foremost stage, and attain the Dharma that does not move.

40
手執楊枝，當願眾生，皆得妙法，究竟清淨。

Taking the willow twig in hand — the wish: may all beings all attain the wondrous Dharma, and reach ultimate purity.

41
嚼楊枝時，當願眾生，其心調淨，噬諸煩惱。

Chewing the willow twig — the wish: may all beings' minds become tamed and pure, and may they bite through every affliction.

42
大小便時，當願眾生，棄貪瞋癡，蠲除罪法。

Relieving oneself — the wish: may all beings cast away greed, hatred, and delusion, and cleanse away every unwholesome thing.

43
事訖就水，當願眾生，出世法中，速疾而往。

Going to the water when finished — the wish: may all beings move swiftly ahead in the Dharma that transcends the world.

44
洗滌形穢，當願眾生，清淨調柔，畢竟無垢。

Washing away the body's impurity — the wish: may all beings be pure and gentle, and in the end utterly without stain.

45
以水盥掌，當願眾生，得清淨手，受持佛法。

Rinsing the hands with water — the wish: may all beings obtain pure hands, to receive and uphold the Buddha's teaching.

46
以水洗面，當願眾生，得淨法門，永無垢染。

Washing the face with water — the wish: may all beings attain the pure gate of the Dharma, forever free of every stain.

V  ·  Setting Out to Teach and Beg 明乞食教化時願
Verses 47–49 — taking up the staff and bowl, and setting out on the road.
47
手執錫杖，當願眾生，設大施會，示如實道。

Taking the monk's staff in hand — the wish: may all beings hold a great feast of giving, and reveal the path exactly as it is.

48
執持應器，當願眾生，成就法器，受天人供。

Holding the alms bowl — the wish: may all beings become true vessels of the Dharma, worthy of offerings from gods and humans.

49
發趾向道，當願眾生，趣佛所行，入無依處。

Setting foot on the road — the wish: may all beings walk the path the Buddha walked, and enter the place beyond all reliance.

VI  ·  On the Road 明在路見聞諸事時願
Verses 50–104 — by far the longest section: the road itself, everything seen along it, and every kind of person met on the way.
50
若在於道，當願眾生，能行佛道，向無餘法。

On the road — the wish: may all beings be able to walk the Buddha's path, and turn toward the Dharma that leaves nothing behind.

51
涉路而去，當願眾生，履淨法界，心無障礙。

Setting off along the road — the wish: may all beings tread the pure Dharma-realm, their minds free of every obstruction.

52
見升高路，當願眾生，永出三界，心無怯弱。

Seeing a road rising uphill — the wish: may all beings forever rise out of the three realms, their minds free of timidity.

53
見趣下路，當願眾生，其心謙下，長佛善根。

Seeing a road descending — the wish: may all beings' minds grow humble and low, nurturing the Buddha's wholesome roots.

54
見斜曲路，當願眾生，捨不正道，永除惡見。

Seeing a crooked, winding road — the wish: may all beings abandon wrong paths, and forever remove wrong views.

55
若見直路，當願眾生，其心正直，無諂無誑。

Seeing a straight road — the wish: may all beings' minds be honest and upright, free of flattery and deceit.

56
見路多塵，當願眾生，遠離塵坌，獲清淨法。

Seeing a dusty road — the wish: may all beings stay far from the dust that settles on them, and obtain the pure Dharma.

57
見路無塵，當願眾生，常行大悲，其心潤澤。

Seeing a road free of dust — the wish: may all beings constantly practice great compassion, their minds moist with kindness.

58
若見險道，當願眾生，住正法界，離諸罪難。

Seeing a dangerous road — the wish: may all beings abide in the true Dharma-realm, and leave behind every wrongdoing and difficulty.

59
若見眾會，當願眾生，說甚深法，一切和合。

Seeing an assembly gathered — the wish: may all beings speak the profound Dharma, and bring everyone into harmony.

60
若見大柱，當願眾生，離我諍心，無有忿恨。

Seeing a great pillar — the wish: may all beings leave behind the quarrelsome mind of self, and hold no resentment.

61
若見叢林，當願眾生，諸天及人，所應敬禮。

Seeing a grove of trees — the wish: may all beings become someone that gods and humans alike should revere and bow to.

62
若見高山，當願眾生，善根超出，無能至頂。

Seeing a high mountain — the wish: may all beings' wholesome roots rise so high that none can reach their peak.

63
見棘刺樹，當願眾生，疾得翦除，三毒之刺。

Seeing a thorn tree — the wish: may all beings swiftly cut away the thorns of the three poisons.

64
見樹葉茂，當願眾生，以定解脫，而為蔭映。

Seeing lush leaves on a tree — the wish: may all beings, through concentration and liberation, become a shade for others.

65
若見華開，當願眾生，神通等法，如華開敷。

Seeing flowers in bloom — the wish: may all beings' supernormal powers and other qualities open and bloom like flowers.

66
若見樹華，當願眾生，眾相如華，具三十二。

Seeing blossoms on a tree — the wish: may all beings' every feature bloom like a flower, complete with the thirty-two marks.

67
若見果實，當願眾生，獲最勝法，證菩提道。

Seeing fruit on a tree — the wish: may all beings obtain the most excellent Dharma, and realize the path of awakening.

68
若見大河，當願眾生，得預法流，入佛智海。

Seeing a great river — the wish: may all beings enter the stream of the Dharma, and flow into the ocean of the Buddha's wisdom.

69
若見陂澤，當願眾生，疾悟諸佛，一味之法。

Seeing a marsh or reservoir — the wish: may all beings swiftly awaken to the one taste shared by the teaching of every Buddha.

70
若見池沼，當願眾生，語業滿足，巧能演說。

Seeing a pond — the wish: may all beings' speech be complete and full, skillful in setting forth the teaching.

71
若見汲井，當願眾生，具足辯才，演一切法。

Seeing a well being drawn from — the wish: may all beings be complete in eloquence, able to expound every teaching.

72
若見湧泉，當願眾生，方便增長，善根無盡。

Seeing a spring welling up — the wish: may all beings' skillful means increase, their wholesome roots without end.

73
若見橋道，當願眾生，廣度一切，猶如橋梁。

Seeing a bridge on the road — the wish: may all beings ferry everyone widely across, just as a bridge does.

74
若見流水，當願眾生，得善意欲，洗除惑垢。

Seeing flowing water — the wish: may all beings gain wholesome intention and aspiration, and wash away the stain of confusion.

75
見修園圃，當願眾生，五欲圃中，耘除愛草。

Seeing a garden being tended — the wish: may all beings, in the garden of the five desires, weed out the grass of craving.

76
見無憂林，當願眾生，永離貪愛，不生憂怖。

Seeing a sorrow-free grove — the wish: may all beings forever leave behind craving, and give rise to no fear or grief.

77
若見園苑，當願眾生，勤修諸行，趣佛菩提。

Seeing a garden park — the wish: may all beings diligently cultivate every practice, and advance toward the Buddha's awakening.

78
見嚴飾人，當願眾生，三十二相，以為嚴好。

Seeing someone richly adorned — the wish: may all beings be adorned instead with the thirty-two marks.

79
見無嚴飾，當願眾生，捨諸飾好，具頭陀行。

Seeing someone without adornment — the wish: may all beings give up every fine adornment, and be complete in ascetic practice.

80
見樂著人，當願眾生，以法自娛，歡愛不捨。

Seeing someone absorbed in pleasure — the wish: may all beings take their own joy in the Dharma, delighting in it without ever letting go.

81
見無樂著，當願眾生，有為事中，心無所樂。

Seeing someone without attachment to pleasure — the wish: may all beings' minds take no delight in anything conditioned.

82
見歡樂人，當願眾生，常得安樂，樂供養佛。

Seeing someone joyful — the wish: may all beings constantly find peace and joy, and delight in making offerings to the Buddha.

83
見苦惱人，當願眾生，獲根本智，滅除眾苦。

Seeing someone in pain and distress — the wish: may all beings obtain fundamental wisdom, and extinguish every suffering.

84
見無病人，當願眾生，入真實慧，永無病惱。

Seeing someone free of illness — the wish: may all beings enter true wisdom, forever free of the affliction of sickness.

85
見疾病人，當願眾生，知身空寂，離乖諍法。

Seeing someone sick — the wish: may all beings know the body to be empty and still, and leave behind quarrelsome ways.

86
見端正人，當願眾生，於佛菩薩，常生淨信。

Seeing someone handsome and fine — the wish: may all beings constantly give rise to pure faith in the Buddhas and bodhisattvas.

87
見醜陋人，當願眾生，於不善事，不生樂著。

Seeing someone unattractive — the wish: may all beings take no delight or attachment in unwholesome things.

88
見報恩人，當願眾生，於佛菩薩，能知恩德。

Seeing someone repaying a kindness — the wish: may all beings know and recognize the kindness of the Buddhas and bodhisattvas.

89
見背恩人，當願眾生，於有惡人，不加其報。

Seeing someone who has turned against a kindness — the wish: may all beings never repay evil with evil, even toward those who do wrong.

90
若見沙門，當願眾生，調柔寂靜，畢竟第一。

Seeing a monk — the wish: may all beings be gentle and still, foremost in the end.

91
見婆羅門，當願眾生，永持梵行，離一切惡。

Seeing a brahmin — the wish: may all beings forever uphold pure conduct, and leave behind every evil.

92
見苦行人，當願眾生，依於苦行，至究竟處。

Seeing an ascetic — the wish: may all beings, relying on ascetic practice, arrive at the ultimate place.

93
見操行人，當願眾生，堅持志行，不捨佛道。

Seeing someone of disciplined conduct — the wish: may all beings hold firmly to their resolve and conduct, and never abandon the Buddha's path.

94
見著甲冑，當願眾生，常服善鎧，趣無師法。

Seeing someone wearing armor — the wish: may all beings always wear the armor of goodness, and advance toward the teacherless Dharma.

95
見無鎧仗，當願眾生，永離一切，不善之業。

Seeing someone without armor or weapons — the wish: may all beings forever leave behind every unwholesome deed.

96
見論議人，當願眾生，於諸異論，悉能摧伏。

Seeing people in debate — the wish: may all beings be able to overcome every wrong doctrine.

97
見正命人，當願眾生，得清淨命，不矯威儀。

Seeing someone who lives by right livelihood — the wish: may all beings obtain a pure livelihood, without pretense in their conduct.

98
若見於王，當願眾生，得為法王，恆轉正法。

Seeing a king — the wish: may all beings become kings of the Dharma, forever turning the wheel of the true teaching.

99
若見王子，當願眾生，從法化生，而為佛子。

Seeing a prince — the wish: may all beings be born of the Dharma, and become children of the Buddha.

100
若見長者，當願眾生，善能明斷，不行惡法。

Seeing an elder — the wish: may all beings judge wisely and clearly, and do nothing unwholesome.

101
若見大臣，當願眾生，恆守正念，習行眾善。

Seeing a minister of state — the wish: may all beings constantly guard right mindfulness, and practice every good thing.

102
若見城郭，當願眾生，得堅固身，心無所屈。

Seeing a city wall — the wish: may all beings obtain an unbreakable body, their minds bending to nothing.

103
若見王都，當願眾生，功德共聚，心恆喜樂。

Seeing a royal capital — the wish: may all beings gather every virtue together, their minds forever joyful.

104
見處林藪，當願眾生，應為天人，之所歎仰。

Seeing someone dwelling in the forest wilds — the wish: may all beings become someone gods and humans praise and look up to.

VII  ·  Begging in the Village 明至聚落乞食時願
Verses 105–128 — entering the village door to door, receiving food, and eating the meal.
105
入里乞食，當願眾生，入深法界，心無障礙。

Entering the village to beg for food — the wish: may all beings enter the deep Dharma-realm, their minds free of every obstruction.

106
到人門戶，當願眾生，入於一切，佛法之門。

Arriving at someone's door — the wish: may all beings enter every gate of the Buddha's teaching.

107
入其家已，當願眾生，得入佛乘，三世平等。

Having entered the house — the wish: may all beings enter the Buddha-vehicle, in which past, present, and future are equal.

108
見不捨人，當願眾生，常不捨離，勝功德法。

Seeing someone who will not give — the wish: may all beings never let go of the Dharma of supreme virtue.

109
見能捨人，當願眾生，永得捨離，三惡道苦。

Seeing someone able to give — the wish: may all beings forever be free of the suffering of the three lower realms.

110
若見空缽，當願眾生，其心清淨，空無煩惱。

Seeing an empty bowl — the wish: may all beings' minds be pure, empty of every affliction.

111
若見滿缽，當願眾生，具足成滿，一切善法。

Seeing a full bowl — the wish: may all beings be complete and full in every wholesome quality.

112
若得恭敬，當願眾生，恭敬修行，一切佛法。

On being treated with respect — the wish: may all beings respectfully practice every teaching of the Buddha.

113
不得恭敬，當願眾生，不行一切，不善之法。

On not being treated with respect — the wish: may all beings do nothing that is unwholesome.

114
見慚恥人，當願眾生，具慚恥行，藏護諸根。

Seeing someone with a sense of shame — the wish: may all beings be complete in conscience and restraint, guarding well the sense faculties.

115
見無慚恥，當願眾生，捨離無慚，住大慈道。

Seeing someone shameless — the wish: may all beings abandon shamelessness, and dwell in the way of great loving-kindness.

116
若得美食，當願眾生，滿足其願，心無羨欲。

On receiving delicious food — the wish: may all beings' wishes be fulfilled, their minds free of envy and craving.

117
得不美食，當願眾生，莫不獲得，諸三昧味。

On receiving unappetizing food — the wish: may all beings all obtain the flavor of every meditative absorption.

118
得柔軟食，當願眾生，大悲所熏，心意柔軟。

On receiving soft food — the wish: may all beings be permeated by great compassion, their minds and hearts made soft.

119
得麤澀食，當願眾生，心無染著，絕世貪愛。

On receiving coarse, rough food — the wish: may all beings' minds cling to nothing, cutting off every worldly craving.

120
若飯食時，當願眾生，禪悅為食，法喜充滿。

While eating a meal — the wish: may all beings take the joy of meditation as their food, and be filled with the joy of the Dharma.

121
若受味時，當願眾生，得佛上味，甘露滿足。

While tasting the food — the wish: may all beings obtain the Buddha's supreme flavor, and be filled with the nectar of the deathless.

122
飯食已訖，當願眾生，所作皆辦，具諸佛法。

When the meal is finished — the wish: may all beings accomplish everything they set out to do, complete in every teaching of the Buddha.

123
若說法時，當願眾生，得無盡辯，廣宣法要。

When teaching the Dharma — the wish: may all beings gain inexhaustible eloquence, and widely proclaim the essentials of the teaching.

124
從舍出時，當願眾生，深入佛智，永出三界。

Leaving the house — the wish: may all beings enter deeply into the Buddha's wisdom, and forever rise out of the three realms.

125
若入水時，當願眾生，入一切智，知三世等。

Entering the water — the wish: may all beings enter the wisdom that knows all things, and understand past, present, and future as equal.

126
洗浴身體，當願眾生，身心無垢，內外光潔。

Bathing the body — the wish: may all beings be free of every stain in body and mind, radiant and clean within and without.

127
盛暑炎毒，當願眾生，捨離眾惱，一切皆盡。

In the fierce heat of summer — the wish: may all beings let go of every affliction, until every one of them is exhausted.

128
暑退涼初，當願眾生，證無上法，究竟清涼。

As the heat retreats and coolness first arrives — the wish: may all beings realize the unsurpassed Dharma, and reach ultimate coolness.

VIII  ·  After the Meal: Chanting and Veneration 明食訖禮誦時願
Verses 129–138 — reciting scripture, seeing the Buddha, and circling and bowing before a stūpa.
129
諷誦經時，當願眾生，順佛所說，總持不忘。

Chanting the sūtras — the wish: may all beings follow what the Buddha taught, holding it all in mind and never forgetting.

130
若得見佛，當願眾生，得無礙眼，見一切佛。

On seeing the Buddha — the wish: may all beings obtain an unobstructed eye, and see every Buddha.

131
諦觀佛時，當願眾生，皆如普賢，端正嚴好。

Gazing carefully at the Buddha — the wish: may all beings all be like Samantabhadra, upright and finely adorned.

132
見佛塔時，當願眾生，尊重如塔，受天人供。

Seeing a Buddha stūpa — the wish: may all beings be revered like a stūpa, and receive offerings from gods and humans.

133
敬心觀塔，當願眾生，諸天及人，所共瞻仰。

Regarding a stūpa with a reverent mind — the wish: may all beings be looked up to by gods and humans together.

134
頂禮於塔，當願眾生，一切天人，無能見頂。

Bowing the head at a stūpa — the wish: may all beings' crowns be beyond the sight of any god or human at all.

135
右繞於塔，當願眾生，所行無逆，成一切智。

Circling a stūpa clockwise — the wish: may all beings meet no obstruction in what they do, and accomplish the wisdom that knows all things.

136
繞塔三匝，當願眾生，勤求佛道，心無懈歇。

Circling a stūpa three times — the wish: may all beings diligently seek the Buddha's path, their minds never slackening or resting.

137
讚佛功德，當願眾生，眾德悉具，稱歎無盡。

Praising the Buddha's virtues — the wish: may all beings be complete in every virtue, praised without end.

138
讚佛相好，當願眾生，成就佛身，證無相法。

Praising the Buddha's marks and features — the wish: may all beings accomplish the Buddha's body, and realize the Dharma beyond all marks.

IX  ·  Waking and Sleeping 明晝夜寢覺時願
Verses 139–141 — washing the feet, lying down to rest, and the moment of waking. The final section of the chapter.
139
若洗足時，當願眾生，具神足力，所行無礙。

Washing the feet — the wish: may all beings be complete in the power of supernormal feet, unobstructed in everything they do.

140
以時寢息，當願眾生，身得安隱，心無動亂。

Lying down to rest at the proper time — the wish: may all beings' bodies find peace, their minds free of agitation.

141
睡眠始寤，當願眾生，一切智覺，周顧十方。

Waking from sleep — the wish: may all beings, with the awakening of all-knowing wisdom, look out across the ten directions.

Self-check quiz

Twelve questions, covering the background and all nine sections. Click an answer to see immediate feedback. No score is recorded.

Question 1 of 12
What does the title 淨行 (Jìngxíng, "Pure Conduct") point to?
Correct: C. The chapter's 141 verses apply the same treatment to washing hands, walking a road, and sitting in meditation — nothing is excluded from practice; conduct becomes "pure" through the wish that accompanies it, not through withdrawal from daily life.
Question 2 of 12
Who asks the opening questions, and who answers with the 141 verses?
Correct: B. Sudhīmati puts about a hundred questions to Mañjuśrī, who collapses them into a single principle — "skillfully applying the mind" — and then demonstrates it verse by verse.
Question 3 of 12
Mañjuśrī's one-sentence answer to Sudhīmati's long list of questions is:
Correct: D. This single sentence is the thesis of the entire chapter. The 141 verses that follow are not a new topic — they are Mañjuśrī showing exactly what "skillfully applying the mind" looks like, moment by moment, through a single day.
Question 4 of 12
What is the fixed hinge phrase repeated in every one of the 141 verses?
Correct: A. This is the hinge that turns a bare observation into a dedication (pariṇāmanā) — the technical act each verse performs, and the reason the text is a practice rather than a description.
Question 5 of 12
Before it became a chapter of the Avataṃsaka Sūtra, this text:
Correct: C. Lokakṣema translated an early version in the 2nd century; Zhi Qian translated it again in the 3rd century and cast it into verse form. It was only later absorbed into the 60-fascicle (5th c.) and then 80-fascicle (7th c.) Avataṃsaka as a chapter.
Question 6 of 12
In Section I (Living at Home), verse 3 — "Gathered with spouse and children" (妻子集會) — pairs with which wish?
Correct: B. The verse does not ask the householder to leave the scene — it asks the mind to meet it differently: seeing spouse and children with the same even-handedness extended to everyone, rather than through the grip of attachment.
Question 7 of 12
In Section II (Going Forth), what does verse 18 — "putting on the monastic robe" (著袈裟衣) — wish for?
Correct: C. 袈裟 (kāṣāya) is the monastic robe; the wish moves straight past the garment itself to what it stands for — a mind free of every stain, complete in the Buddha's own way.
Question 8 of 12
Section II moves through refuge in the Three Jewels — the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṅgha (自歸於佛、自歸於法、自歸於僧). What does "taking refuge in the Dharma" wish for?
Correct: A. 深入經藏，智慧如海 — refuge in the Dharma is paired with a wish for depth of study and breadth of wisdom, not mere reverence from a distance.
Question 9 of 12
In Section III (Sitting in Meditation), verse 30 pairs the cross-legged posture with a wish for "wholesome roots" to grow firm and reach 不動地. What is notable about this phrase?
Correct: C. The chapter often lets a physical image double as a doctrinal one — sitting still becomes a wish for the stage of practice where realization itself no longer wavers.
Question 10 of 12
Section VI (On the Road) is by far the longest section — 55 of the chapter's 141 verses. What does it consist of?
Correct: B. Nearly 40% of the entire chapter is spent on one walk into town — proof that the text finds as much material for practice in a mountain, a well, or a stranger's face as anywhere else.
Question 11 of 12
In Section VII (Begging in the Village), verse 119 — receiving coarse, unpleasant food (得麤澀食) — pairs with which wish?
Correct: A. Good food and bad food get the identical treatment across verses 116–119 — each becomes an occasion to notice attachment or aversion arising, and to wish it released.
Question 12 of 12
The chapter's very last verse (141), "waking from sleep" (睡眠始寤), wishes that all beings gain:
Correct: D. 一切智覺，周顧十方 — even the most physically ordinary moment of a day, the instant of waking, is treated as sufficient material for the largest possible wish. It is a fitting way for the chapter to end.

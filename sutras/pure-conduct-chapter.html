Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A bodhisattva who skillfully applies the mind — 善用其心 — turns every single act of an ordinary day, from putting on a robe to washing a bowl, into a wish for the awakening of all beings; nothing is too small to be practice, and nothing is left outside the path.

Where it sits in the Avataṃsaka

The Avataṃsaka Sūtra (華嚴經, the "Flower Garland Sūtra") is one of the largest and most cosmic scriptures in the Mahāyāna canon — an ocean of interpenetrating worlds, radiant Buddhas, and the fifty-two stages of the bodhisattva path. Inside that vastness, the Chapter of Pure Conduct is something different: a close, practical, almost domestic text. It has no cosmic vision, no other-world Buddha. It simply asks — and then answers, 141 times — how a single day of an ordinary practitioner's life can be turned entirely toward the liberation of others.

This intimacy of scale is exactly why the chapter has always circulated more widely, and been chanted more often, than almost anything else in the Avataṃsaka. It is the one part of this enormous sūtra you can actually carry into a kitchen, a hallway, or a hospital room.

A text older than the sūtra it lives in

The Chapter of Pure Conduct did not start out as a chapter. It circulated independently for centuries before it was folded into the Avataṃsaka — the clearest sign of how prized it was on its own. The earliest known Chinese translation is Lokakṣema's 《諸菩薩求佛本業經》 (2nd century CE, Eastern Han); Zhi Qian of the Wu kingdom translated it again in the 3rd century as 《菩薩本業經》, in the 〈願行品〉 section, and it was Zhi Qian who first cast the material into the verse form used ever since. Two later translators then absorbed it into the full Avataṃsaka: Buddhabhadra placed it as chapter 7 of the 60-fascicle version (418–420 CE), and Śikṣānanda placed it as chapter 11 of the 80-fascicle version (completed 699 CE) — the translation used on this page, and the most widely read of the four surviving versions.

The question: what Sudhīmati asks

The chapter opens with Bodhisattva Sudhīmati (智首, literally "wisdom foremost") putting ten sets of questions to Mañjuśrī — around a hundred qualities in all — asking how a bodhisattva can obtain each one. The questions climb from the most basic (flawless conduct) to the most exalted (the ten powers of a Buddha):

6七覺分、三解脫門the seven factors of awakening, and the three gates of liberation — emptiness, signlessness, wishlessness

8十智力the ten powers of knowledge of a Buddha — karma across the three times, the unobstructed divine eye…
9十王護持the reverence and protection of ten kinds of kings, divine and human
10十種為依、十種超勝being a refuge, a torch, a guide for all beings — and being foremost among all beings

It is a formidable list — the entire résumé of a Buddha, in outline. The drama of the chapter is that Mañjuśrī does not answer it point by point. He answers it with a single principle, and then shows what that principle looks like at breakfast.

The answer: "skillfully applying the mind"

Mañjuśrī opens by praising the question — 善哉！佛子，汝今為欲多所饒益，多所安隱，哀愍世間，利樂天人，問如是義, "Excellent, child of the Buddha — you ask this wishing to benefit many, bring peace to many, have compassion for the world, and bring welfare to gods and humans." Then he collapses the entire hundred-item list into one sentence:

若諸菩薩善用其心，則獲一切勝妙功德。
"If a bodhisattva skillfully applies the mind, every supreme and wondrous virtue follows."

Everything else in the chapter — all 141 verses — is Mañjuśrī making good on that claim, scene by scene. Nothing further is asked of the reader: no austerity, no special circumstance, no monastery required to start. The entire path is contained in how the mind meets each moment. This is also why the chapter closes, after the last verse, by circling back to the very same phrase, now in the past tense: a bodhisattva who has practiced this way — 若諸菩薩如是用心，則獲一切勝妙功德 — becomes unshakable by the whole world, including gods, māras, and even the more limited attainments of the Śrāvaka and Pratyekabuddha vehicles.

The form: one thing seen, one wish made

Each of the 141 verses follows the same four-part shape, tightened by Zhi Qian into verse form and kept ever since:

  1. An ordinary moment — a scene from daily life: waking, dressing, walking a road, seeing a bridge, eating, going to sleep.
  2. The turn — the fixed phrase 當願眾生, "the wish: may all beings…" This hinge is what makes the chapter a practice and not a poem. Nothing is only noticed; everything is dedicated.
  3. The wish — usually two matched four-character clauses, drawing some quality out of the concrete image: a bridge becomes a wish to carry all beings across; muddy water becomes a wish to wash away affliction; a locked door becomes a wish to enter the gate of the Dharma.
  4. Nothing more. No verse explains itself. The four lines are the whole practice — see, turn, wish, move on to the next thing.

A well-known example, from later in the chapter (not yet translated on this page — see the map below): seeing a bridge on the road, 若見橋道，當願眾生，廣度一切，猶如橋梁 — "may all beings be widely carried across, like a bridge." The genius of the form is how ordinary the trigger is and how large the wish becomes.

The nine sections

The 141 verses have no headings in the sūtra itself, but the great Huayan commentator Chengguan (澄觀, 738–839 CE), National Master Qingliang, divided them into nine movements in his 《華嚴經疏鈔》, tracing a single day of a monastic's life from home to sleep. That division, still used in study and chanting today, structures the text on this page:

Notice the shape of the day this traces: home, ordination, meditation, dressing and washing, setting out, the long walk into town noticing everything along the way (by far the largest section — over a third of the whole chapter), begging house to house, the meal, chanting and bowing before returning, and finally sleep. The mundane and the monastic are not separated; the text simply keeps walking, and keeps turning what it sees into a vow.

Why the small things get so much attention

Western readers often expect a sacred text to reserve its intensity for extraordinary moments — a vision, a crisis, a death. This chapter does the opposite. It gives exactly the same four-line treatment to seeing a beautiful sunrise and to using the toilet, to entering deep meditation and to putting on socks. That is the point, not an oversight. If liberation is only available in rare, elevated states, then most of a human life — the eating, dressing, walking, waiting — is wasted time. The Chapter of Pure Conduct refuses that. Every object the eye lands on, every task the hand performs, is treated as sufficient material for the entire bodhisattva path, provided the mind turns it toward all beings. This is also why the chapter has always resonated beyond monastic life: a layperson chanting these verses over a sink of dishes is doing exactly what the text asks.

How to actually use this text

  1. Read one section at a time. The chapter is not meant to be absorbed in one sitting; it is meant to be lived in, section by section, day by day.
  2. Borrow the form for your own day. The deepest use of this text is not memorization but imitation — noticing an ordinary moment in your own life and silently completing it with the wish: may all beings… Boiling water, opening a laptop, waiting at a red light are all fair game; the sūtra's own list was never meant to be exhaustive.
  3. Chant a section before or after the activity it describes. Section I suits a quiet moment at home; the verses on washing (in Section IV, forthcoming) suit standing at a sink; the closing verses on sleep (Section IX, forthcoming) suit the edge of the bed.

Two questions readers often ask

"Isn't wishing something for other people, silently, just sentiment — does it actually do anything?" In the Mahāyāna, a formally made wish directed at the awakening of others is not decoration; it is puṇya-pariṇāmanā, the dedication or "turning-over" of merit — a technical act with real weight in the tradition, not unlike setting an intention that shapes everything that follows it. The chapter trains this dedication until it becomes reflexive: not something remembered occasionally, but the mind's default response to whatever it meets.

"Why does a text about compassion for all beings spend so much time on physical routines — teeth, robes, toilets?" Because the alternative is a compassion that only shows up for special occasions, which is to say, a compassion that is mostly absent. By anchoring the wish to the most repeated, least dramatic actions of a day, the chapter makes universal benevolence into a habit of attention rather than an occasional mood — something closer to what contemporary readers might recognize as mindfulness, but aimed outward, at the welfare of others, rather than only inward.

Key terms

Jìngxíng 淨行 — "pure conduct" or "purifying practice." Conduct made pure not by withdrawing from ordinary activity but by turning every act, through a wish, toward the benefit of all beings.
dāng yuàn zhòngshēng 當願眾生 — "the wish: may all beings…" The hinge phrase repeated in all 141 verses, turning a bare observation into a dedication.
gāthā 偈頌 — a verse, typically four lines of four characters in the Chinese of this text. The literary unit of the whole chapter.
shàn yòng qí xīn 善用其心 — "skillfully applying the mind." Mañjuśrī's one-sentence answer to Sudhīmati's hundred questions, and the thesis the rest of the chapter demonstrates.
Sudhīmati 智首菩薩 — "Wisdom Foremost," the bodhisattva who asks the opening questions.
Mañjuśrī 文殊師利菩薩 — the bodhisattva of wisdom, who answers with the 141 verses.
pariṇāmanā 迴向 — dedication, the "turning over" of merit or intention toward the awakening of others; the technical act each verse performs.
bodhisattva 菩薩 — one bound for full buddhahood who practises for the sake of all beings.

The text

Chinese source: Śikṣānanda's 699 CE translation, Taishō T279, fascicle 14 (大方廣佛華嚴經, CBETA), the version chanted across East Asia today. The nine section divisions follow Chengguan's 《華嚴經疏鈔》. English translation made fresh for Ru-Yi Meditation Center, kept plain and close to the Chinese. Sections I–II are complete; the remaining sections are mapped with their Chinese section titles and verse counts and will be added as translation continues.
I  ·  Living at Home 明菩薩在家時願
Verses 1–11 — the bodhisattva still living as a householder.
1
菩薩在家，當願眾生，知家性空，免其逼迫。

A bodhisattva living at home — the wish: may all beings know that "home" has no fixed nature of its own, and so be freed from its pressures.

2
孝事父母，當願眾生，善事於佛，護養一切。

Serving parents with filial devotion — the wish: may all beings serve the Buddhas well, and protect and nurture all beings.

3
妻子集會，當願眾生，怨親平等，永離貪著。

Gathered with spouse and children — the wish: may all beings see friend and foe as equal, forever free of craving and attachment.

4
若得五欲，當願眾生，拔除欲箭，究竟安隱。

On obtaining the five desired pleasures — the wish: may all beings pull out the arrow of desire, and reach final peace.

5
伎樂聚會，當願眾生，以法自娛，了伎非實。

At a gathering of music and entertainment — the wish: may all beings take their joy in the Dharma alone, understanding that such performances are not real.

6
若在宮室，當願眾生，入於聖地，永除穢欲。

Dwelling in a fine chamber — the wish: may all beings enter the ground of the noble ones, and forever remove impure desire.

7
著瓔珞時，當願眾生，捨諸偽飾，到真實處。

Putting on jeweled ornaments — the wish: may all beings cast off every false adornment, and arrive at the place of truth.

8
上升樓閣，當願眾生，升正法樓，徹見一切。

Climbing up to a high tower — the wish: may all beings ascend the tower of the true Dharma, and see all things through and through.

9
若有所施，當願眾生，一切能捨，心無愛著。

Giving something in charity — the wish: may all beings be able to give up everything, their minds free of clinging attachment.

10
眾會聚集，當願眾生，捨眾聚法，成一切智。

Gathered together in a crowd — the wish: may all beings let go of the ways of the crowd, and accomplish the wisdom that knows all things.

11
若在厄難，當願眾生，隨意自在，所行無礙。

Caught in difficulty and misfortune — the wish: may all beings move as freely as they wish, unobstructed in all they do.

II  ·  Going Forth 明創出家時願
Verses 12–27 — leaving the household, entering the monastery, and taking ordination.
12
捨居家時，當願眾生，出家無礙，心得解脫。

Leaving the household — the wish: may all beings go forth without hindrance, their minds attaining liberation.

13
入僧伽藍，當願眾生，演說種種，無乖諍法。

Entering the monastery grounds — the wish: may all beings expound every kind of teaching without conflict or discord.

14
詣大小師，當願眾生，巧事師長，習行善法。

Approaching one's teachers, senior and junior — the wish: may all beings skillfully serve their teachers and elders, and practice what is good and wholesome.

15
求請出家，當願眾生，得不退法，心無障礙。

Requesting to go forth — the wish: may all beings attain the Dharma that never regresses, their minds free of hindrance.

16
脫去俗服，當願眾生，勤修善根，捨諸罪軛。

Taking off lay clothing — the wish: may all beings diligently cultivate wholesome roots, and cast off every yoke of wrongdoing.

17
剃除鬚髮，當願眾生，永離煩惱，究竟寂滅。

Shaving off hair and beard — the wish: may all beings forever leave the afflictions behind, and reach ultimate stillness.

18
著袈裟衣，當願眾生，心無所染，具大仙道。

Putting on the monastic robe — the wish: may all beings' minds be stained by nothing, and be complete in the way of the great sage.

19
正出家時，當願眾生，同佛出家，救護一切。

At the very moment of going forth — the wish: may all beings go forth just as the Buddha did, and rescue and protect all beings.

20
自歸於佛，當願眾生，紹隆佛種，發無上意。

Taking refuge in the Buddha — the wish: may all beings carry on the Buddha's lineage, and give rise to the unsurpassed intention.

21
自歸於法，當願眾生，深入經藏，智慧如海。

Taking refuge in the Dharma — the wish: may all beings enter deeply into the treasury of the scriptures, their wisdom as vast as the sea.

22
自歸於僧，當願眾生，統理大眾，一切無礙。

Taking refuge in the Saṅgha — the wish: may all beings guide and govern the great assembly, unobstructed in everything.

23
受學戒時，當願眾生，善學於戒，不作眾惡。

Undertaking to study the precepts — the wish: may all beings learn the precepts well, and do no wrong of any kind.

24
受阇梨教，當願眾生，具足威儀，所行真實。

Receiving the ācārya's instruction — the wish: may all beings be complete in dignified conduct, and true in everything they do.

25
受和尚教，當願眾生，入無生智，到無依處。

Receiving the preceptor's instruction — the wish: may all beings enter the wisdom of the unborn, and arrive at the place beyond all reliance.

26
受具足戒，當願眾生，具諸方便，得最勝法。

Receiving the full ordination precepts — the wish: may all beings be complete in every skillful means, and attain the most excellent Dharma.

27
若入堂宇，當願眾生，升無上堂，安住不動。

Entering the hall — the wish: may all beings ascend the unsurpassed hall, and dwell there unmoving.

Self-check quiz

Eight questions, covering the background and Sections I–II. Click an answer to see immediate feedback. No score is recorded.

Question 1 of 8
What does the title 淨行 (Jìngxíng, "Pure Conduct") point to?
Correct: C. The chapter's 141 verses apply the same treatment to washing hands, walking a road, and sitting in meditation — nothing is excluded from practice; conduct becomes "pure" through the wish that accompanies it, not through withdrawal from daily life.
Question 2 of 8
Who asks the opening questions, and who answers with the 141 verses?
Correct: B. Sudhīmati puts about a hundred questions to Mañjuśrī, who collapses them into a single principle — "skillfully applying the mind" — and then demonstrates it verse by verse.
Question 3 of 8
Mañjuśrī's one-sentence answer to Sudhīmati's long list of questions is:
Correct: D. This single sentence is the thesis of the entire chapter. The 141 verses that follow are not a new topic — they are Mañjuśrī showing exactly what "skillfully applying the mind" looks like, moment by moment, through a single day.
Question 4 of 8
What is the fixed hinge phrase repeated in every one of the 141 verses?
Correct: A. This is the hinge that turns a bare observation into a dedication (pariṇāmanā) — the technical act each verse performs, and the reason the text is a practice rather than a description.
Question 5 of 8
Before it became a chapter of the Avataṃsaka Sūtra, this text:
Correct: C. Lokakṣema translated an early version in the 2nd century; Zhi Qian translated it again in the 3rd century and cast it into verse form. It was only later absorbed into the 60-fascicle (5th c.) and then 80-fascicle (7th c.) Avataṃsaka as a chapter.
Question 6 of 8
In Section I (Living at Home), verse 3 — "Gathered with spouse and children" (妻子集會) — pairs with which wish?
Correct: B. The verse does not ask the householder to leave the scene — it asks the mind to meet it differently: seeing spouse and children with the same even-handedness extended to everyone, rather than through the grip of attachment.
Question 7 of 8
In Section II (Going Forth), what does verse 18 — "putting on the monastic robe" (著袈裟衣) — wish for?
Correct: C. 袈裟 (kāṣāya) is the monastic robe; the wish moves straight past the garment itself to what it stands for — a mind free of every stain, complete in the Buddha's own way.
Question 8 of 8
Section II moves through refuge in the Three Jewels — the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṅgha (自歸於佛、自歸於法、自歸於僧). What does "taking refuge in the Dharma" wish for?
Correct: A. 深入經藏，智慧如海 — refuge in the Dharma is paired with a wish for depth of study and breadth of wisdom, not mere reverence from a distance.

Itivuttaka · Discourse 38

Thoughts

Vitakkasutta

Setting
None given — no narrative frame, as with almost every discourse in this collection
Speaker
The Buddha alone, describing his own recurring thoughts before addressing his listeners directly
Form
An extended prose statement in several parts, restated in three unusually image-rich verse stanzas
Position
The Book of the Twos, Chapter Two, the 1st of its discourses
Length
1–2 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the longest and most densely poetic of this run of ten discourses

A rare glimpse of the Buddha’s own recurring thoughts

Most Itivuttaka discourses instruct mendicants directly about their own practice. This one is different in focus, if not in form: it describes what regularly occupies the Buddha’s own mind — two thoughts, of sanctuary and of seclusion — before turning, in its second half, to ask the same two habits of thought of his listeners. It closes with some of the most sustained, image-rich verse in this stretch of the collection, and opens the Book of the Twos’ second chapter.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Two thoughts often occur to the Realized One: the thought of sanctuary, born from loving kindness and non-harm, and the thought of seclusion, born from having already given up what is unskillful — and the same two thoughts are then urged on mendicants as well, though phrased for them as an open task rather than a settled fact.

Two loves, two thoughts

The discourse’s structure pairs a disposition with the thought it produces: because the Realized One “loves kindness and delights in it” (an orientation toward non-affliction), the thought “I shall not hurt any creature firm or frail” often arises. Because he “loves seclusion and delights in it,” the thought “what is unskillful has been given up” arises. Each thought follows directly from an already-established disposition, not from effort applied in the moment.

The disciples get a question, not a statement

The parallel passage addressed to mendicants does not simply repeat the Buddha’s finished thought. His non-harm thought is repeated to them nearly unchanged (“I shall not hurt any creature…”), but his seclusion thought — already completed for him (“has been given up”) — becomes, for them, three open questions: “What is unskillful? What is not given up? What should I give up?” The shift from settled statement to active inquiry marks the real difference between an awakened teacher’s condition and a student’s still-ongoing task.

The verse’s portrait

The closing verses move well beyond the two thoughts themselves into an extended portrait: “the bearer of the unbearable,” one who has “crossed over,” “disposed of Māra,” “gone beyond old age” — dense, honorific language less common in these short prose-and-verse pairs.

The mountain simile

The final image compares the Buddha, having “ascended the Temple of Truth” — a palace or tower built, figuratively, of the Dhamma itself — to someone standing on a rocky mountain peak who can see the people scattered below, here “swamped with sorrow, overcome by rebirth and old age.” It is one of the more visually vivid closing images among these ten discourses, and a fitting way to open the Book of the Twos’ second chapter.

Key terms

vitakka — “thought”: the discourse’s title word, referring here specifically to two recurring, characteristic thoughts rather than to thought in general.
khema — “sanctuary, safety, security”: the first of the two thoughts, arising from non-harm.
paviveka — “seclusion”: the second thought, arising from having already given up the unskillful.
abyāpajjha — “non-affliction, freedom from ill will”: the disposition said to give rise to the thought of sanctuary.
the Temple of Truth — the verse’s image for the vantage point from which the Buddha, likened to someone on a mountaintop, looks upon suffering beings.

The text

The discourse in full — the Buddha’s own two recurring thoughts, extended to his listeners, and a closing run of image-rich verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Two thoughts, mendicants, often occur to the Realized One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha: the thought of sanctuary, and that of seclusion. The Realized One loves kindness and delights in it, so this thought often occurs to him: ‘Through this behavior, I shall not hurt any creature firm or frail.’

§3The Realized One loves seclusion and delights in it, so this thought often occurs to him: ‘What is unskillful has been given up.’

§4So, mendicants, you too should love kindness and delight in it, then this thought will often occur to you: ‘Through this behavior, I shall not hurt any creature firm or frail.’

§5You too should love seclusion and delight in it, then this thought will often occur to you: ‘What is unskillful? What is not given up? What should I give up?’” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§6“Two thoughts occur to him,
the Realized One, the bearer of the unbearable:
first mentioned was thought of sanctuary,
then the second made clear was seclusion.

§7Dispeller of darkness, the great seer has crossed over:
the attained, the master, the undefiled.
In the midst of it all, he is freed in the ending of craving;
that sage bears his final body.
He has disposed of Māra, I declare, and gone beyond old age.

§8Standing high on a rocky mountain,
you can see the people all around.
In just the same way, the all-seer, so intelligent,
having ascended the Temple of Truth,
rid of sorrow, looks upon the people
swamped with sorrow, overcome by rebirth and old age.”

§9This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

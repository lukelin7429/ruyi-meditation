Itivuttaka · Iti 96

Yoked to Sensual Pleasures

The Book of the Threes · Chapter Five

Book
The Book of the Threes · Chapter Five
Frame
None — addressed generally to “mendicants,” with no location, listener, or story given
Form
A short threefold classification in prose, followed by one restating verse
Length
~1 minute to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a clean, memorable scheme

Why this discourse, ninety-sixth

Three kinds of person are distinguished here purely by which of two “yokes” still binds them — sensual pleasure, and the pull toward rebirth — with full freedom arriving only once both are gone.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Three kinds of person are distinguished purely by which of two “yokes” still binds them — sensual pleasure and the pull toward rebirth — with full freedom arriving only when both are gone.

Two yokes, three people

The scheme is compact: “one yoked to both sensual pleasures and rebirth is a returner, who comes back to this place. One unyoked from sensual pleasures but still yoked to rebirth is a non-returner, who comes not back to this place. One unyoked from both sensual pleasures and rebirth is a perfected one, who has ended defilements.” It is worth noting honestly that this discourse maps only three positions relative to these two yokes; it does not, within this text, spell out a separate stage prior to the “returner” it names — the discourse’s own three-part scheme is simpler than the fuller traditional ladder of awakening it clearly echoes.

The verse restates the same three positions

“Yoked to both sensual pleasures and the desire to be reborn in a future life; sentient beings continue to transmigrate, with ongoing birth and death.” Those “who’ve given up sensual pleasures without attaining the end of defilements, and are still yoked to being reborn, are said to be non-returners.” And those “who have cut off doubt, and ended conceit and future lives, they’re the ones in this world who’ve truly crossed over, having reached the ending of defilements.”

A fuller ladder, for context

Readers curious about the wider traditional scheme this discourse’s simpler two-yoke, three-person version echoes — running from stream-enterer, through once-returner and non-returner, up to the perfected one (arahant) — can find that fuller progression laid out at length elsewhere, comparing the escalating value of generosity, ethical conduct, and meditation across each stage. This discourse, by contrast, keeps its focus narrow: exactly two yokes, exactly three resulting positions.

Key terms

the two yokes — sensual pleasure (kāma) and continued rebirth or existence (bhava) — the two bonds this discourse measures a person against.
“returner” — bound by both yokes; comes back to this place.
“non-returner” — freed from sensual pleasure but still yoked to rebirth; does not come back to this place — a term traditionally used for the third of four stages of awakening.
“perfected one” — unyoked from both; has ended defilements (arahant).
the fuller four-stage ladder — the wider traditional scheme of stream-enterer, once-returner, non-returner, and arahant, which this discourse’s simpler three-person version echoes without spelling out every stage.

The text

The discourse in full: the threefold classification and the entire restating verse. No location or listener is named in the source. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1.1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2.1–2.5“Mendicants, one yoked to both sensual pleasures and rebirth is a returner, who comes back to this place. One unyoked from sensual pleasures but still yoked to rebirth is a non-returner, who comes not back to this place. One unyoked from both sensual pleasures and rebirth is a perfected one, who has ended defilements.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3.1–3.4“Yoked to both sensual pleasures
and the desire to be reborn in a future life;
sentient beings continue to transmigrate,
with ongoing birth and death.

§4.1–4.4Those who’ve given up sensual pleasures
without attaining the end of defilements,
and are still yoked to being reborn,
are said to be non-returners.

§5.1–5.4Those who have cut off doubt,
and ended conceit and future lives,
they’re the ones in this world who’ve truly crossed over,
having reached the ending of defilements.”

§6.1This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

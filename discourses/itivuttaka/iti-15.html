Itivuttaka · The Book of the Ones · Chapter Two

The Fetter of Craving

The Book of the Ones, Chapter Two

Audience
Mendicants in general — no named individual, no location, no occasion is given
Form
A single prose declaration followed by a two-stanza verse, all held within one continuous quotation
Length
Under a minute to read
Pairing
Companion to Iti 14 (“The Shroud of Ignorance”) — together, craving and ignorance are the two forces the tradition names as sustaining the entire cycle of transmigration
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — a single image, stated and then developed in verse

Why this discourse, fifteenth

Immediately after naming ignorance as the shroud that covers beings most thoroughly, the Itivuttaka turns to a second image for a second cause: craving as a fetter, binding beings to the same long wandering. Where Iti 14 pictured something that covers, this discourse pictures something that binds and travels alongside — craving as a fellow traveler on a journey no one chose to take.

Reading guide

A fetter, not a shroud

The shift in image from the previous discourse is deliberate and worth noticing. A shroud covers; a fetter (saṃyojana) binds and restrains movement while still permitting a kind of forced motion — exactly the paradox the verse develops, where craving is described not as something that stops a person but as a “partner” that travels with them precisely because they are bound to it. The prose statement itself is direct: “I do not see a single fetter, fettered by which people wander and transmigrate for a long time like the fetter of craving.”

Craving as companion, not merely obstacle

The verse’s central image is unusually intimate for this kind of spare text: “Craving is a person’s partner as they transmigrate on this long journey.” A partner accompanies; it is close, familiar, perhaps even trusted. That framing captures something the more clinical language of “fetter” alone might miss — craving doesn’t simply block or restrain from outside, it rides along, state to state, precisely because it has been mistaken for something worth keeping.

“From this state to another”

The verse’s next line names the mechanism directly: “They go from this state to another, but don’t escape transmigration.” Movement alone, in other words, isn’t freedom. A being can change states, circumstances, even entire realms of rebirth, and still remain exactly as bound as before, because what binds them — craving itself — has travelled along unchanged.

The second stanza: knowing the danger

The verse turns, in its second half, from diagnosis to response: “Knowing this danger, that craving is the cause of suffering — rid of craving, free of grasping, a mendicant would wander mindful.” Notably, the verb “wander” recurs here too, but transformed: the aimless wandering of transmigration becomes, for the mendicant who has seen craving’s danger clearly, a different kind of wandering altogether — free of grasping, undertaken mindfully rather than compelled.

Reading it beside Iti 14

Taken together, these two discourses name what the wider tradition treats as the two principal forces sustaining rebirth: ignorance, clouding what is seen, and craving, pulling toward what is wanted. Neither discourse explains how the two relate to each other technically; each simply gives its own cause the fullest possible weight, in its own image, before moving on.

Key terms

taṇhā — craving, the subject of this discourse, named as a fetter binding beings to prolonged rebirth.
saṃyojana — “fetter,” the discourse’s central image, distinct from the “shroud” image used for ignorance in Iti 14.
“a person’s partner” — the verse’s intimate image for craving, accompanying a being through the long journey of transmigration.
“from this state to another” — the verse’s observation that mere change of circumstance, without giving up craving, does not amount to escape.
wandering, transformed — the verse’s closing image of a mendicant who, free of craving and grasping, “would wander mindful” — the same verb used for aimless transmigration, now describing something deliberate.

The text

The discourse in full. The verse’s two stanzas are held within a single continuous quotation in the source text, reproduced here as one block. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, I do not see a single fetter, fettered by which people wander and transmigrate for a long time like the fetter of craving. Fettered by craving, people wander and transmigrate for a long time.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3–4“Craving is a person’s partner
as they transmigrate on this long journey.
They go from this state to another,
but don’t escape transmigration.

Knowing this danger,
that craving is the cause of suffering—
rid of craving, free of grasping,
a mendicant would wander mindful.”

§5This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

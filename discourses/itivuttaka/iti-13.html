Itivuttaka · The Book of the Ones · Chapter Two

Complete Understanding of Disdain

The Book of the Ones, Chapter Two

Audience
Mendicants in general — no named individual, no location, no occasion is given
Form
A single terse prose statement followed by a four-couplet verse restating the identical point
Length
Under a minute to read
Series
The last of seven “complete understanding” discourses (Iti 7–13), closing the run that began with all things and moved through conceit, greed, hate, delusion, and anger
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — plain and very short, though the claim it makes is total

Why this discourse, thirteenth

This is the last of a run of seven discourses that share one formula and one purpose: naming a mental state, one at a time, and insisting that suffering cannot end without directly knowing it, completely understanding it, losing one’s taste for it, and giving it up. Disdain closes the list — less commonly discussed today than greed, hate, or anger, but treated here with exactly the same weight.

Reading guide

Closing the set

With this discourse the run that opened at Iti 7 (“Complete Understanding of All”) and continued through conceit, greed, hate, delusion, and anger comes to an end. Structurally, nothing changes: the same opening and closing attributions, the same two-part demand, the same verse shape. What changes, one final time, is the target. Disdain gets exactly the same treatment as greed or hate — a sign that the compilers of this collection did not treat some defilements as more serious than others by giving them more elaborate discourses; each gets the identical, complete formula.

What “disdain” names

The English word here translates a state of mind belonging to the same family as anger, denigration, envy, and rivalry — the kind of secondary corruption of the heart that shows up in lists throughout the canon describing subtler ways a mind turns against others, short of open hostility. Rather than guess at fine distinctions the brief text itself doesn’t draw, it is enough to notice what the discourse does with it: disdain is treated as serious enough to warrant the identical total claim made for anger and for delusion — that failing to deal with it thoroughly means suffering cannot end.

The demand, restated one more time

As in the two discourses before it, understanding alone is explicitly ruled insufficient. The text requires directly knowing and completely understanding disdain together with dispassion toward it and actually giving it up. Read across all seven discourses in this set, that pairing is the constant: each defilement must be both seen clearly and let go of, not merely diagnosed.

The verse: an ending, echoed one final time

The verse repeats, once more, the shape used throughout this set: overcome by disdain, beings go to a bad place; understood by the discerning and given up, they never return to this world. There is a kind of quiet emphasis in reading these final lines as the close of a longer sequence — six discourses (Iti 8–13, following on from Iti 7) have now each ended on this identical note, applied every time to something different.

What comes next

Having closed out this list of specific mental states, the Itivuttaka moves on: the following discourse, Iti 14, steps back to a single overarching cause — ignorance itself — described as the one shroud that keeps beings transmigrating longer than any other.

Key terms

disdain — the state of mind this discourse addresses, belonging to a family of “secondary” mental corruptions alongside anger, denigration, envy, and rivalry.
pariññā — “complete understanding,” the same exhaustive knowing demanded throughout this set of seven discourses.
virāga — “dispassion,” paired again with giving up as the required second condition.
duggati — “a bad place,” the destination named for those overcome by disdain.
Iti 7–13 — the full run this discourse closes: all things, conceit, greed, hate, delusion, anger, and disdain, each treated with the identical formula.

The text

The discourse in full. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, without directly knowing and completely understanding disdain, without dispassion for it and giving it up, you can’t end suffering. By directly knowing and completely understanding disdain, having dispassion for it and giving it up, you can end suffering.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“When overcome by disdain
beings go to a bad place.
Having rightly understood that disdain,
the discerning give it up.
Once they’ve given it up,
they never return to this world.”

§4This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

Itivuttaka · The Book of the Twos · Discourse 41

Bereft of Wisdom

Paññāparihīnasutta

Setting
None given — no place, audience, or occasion; the discourse opens directly with the formula “This was said by the Buddha…”
Position
14th of 22 discourses in the Book of the Twos
Form
A twofold prose statement (bereft / not bereft) followed by three verses restating the same point
Length
About 1 minute to read
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — simple in outline, but its verses compress a great deal into very few lines

Why this discourse, forty-first

What does it actually mean to be destitute? This short discourse answers with a single, deliberately provocative claim: the truly “bereft” are not the poor or the sick, but those who lack noble wisdom — a lack that costs them their happiness now and, the text says, their next rebirth. It is one of the plainest statements in the Itivuttaka of wisdom’s stakes, stated first in prose and then compressed further into three tight verses.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

To lack noble wisdom is to be genuinely destitute — living unhappily now and facing a bad rebirth — while to possess it is to be happy now and headed for a good rebirth; and wisdom is called “best in the world” because, beyond improving this life, it alone penetrates through to a right understanding of the very ending of rebirth and continued existence.

No narrative frame

Like almost every discourse in the Itivuttaka, this one gives no place, no audience, and no occasion. It does not open on a journey or a question from a visitor; it opens directly with the collection’s own signature formula, “This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard,” and moves straight into the teaching itself, stated once in prose and then again, more densely, in verse. Whatever occasioned it (if anything did) is simply not part of what has come down to us.

Truly bereft

The opening line reframes destitution: “Those sentient beings are truly bereft, mendicants, who are bereft of noble wisdom.” The word rendered “bereft” (parihīna, “diminished, deprived, fallen away from”) is not applied here to money, health, or status, but to wisdom specifically — and the text is careful to state the cost on both sides of the ledger at once: such a person “lives unhappily in this very life, with distress, anguish, and fever,” and “when the body breaks up, after death, can expect a bad rebirth.” The mirror image follows immediately for the person not bereft of wisdom: happy now, free of distress, and headed for a good rebirth. The teaching yokes present psychological wellbeing to future destiny in a single, symmetrical statement.

Habituated to name and form

The first verse widens the lens from the individual to “the world with its gods” as a whole, calling it “bereft of wisdom, habituated to name and form, imagining this is truth.” Nāmarūpa, “name and form,” is standard shorthand in early Buddhist thought for the mental and physical constituents of experience — the basic materials any being takes itself and its world to be made of. The verse’s charge is that ordinary experience, left unexamined, mistakes this given material for reality itself (“imagining this is truth”); being “bereft of wisdom,” on this reading, is not simply ignorance of some fact but a settled habituation to taking appearance at face value.

Wisdom that penetrates

The second verse states the positive claim directly: “Wisdom is best in the world, as it leads to penetration, through which one rightly understands the ending of rebirth and continued existence.” The verb translated “penetration” (nibbedhagaminī, “leading to a breaking-through”) marks wisdom as something more than accumulated information: it is specifically what cuts through the habituation to name-and-form named in the previous verse, and what alone reaches as far as “the ending of rebirth and continued existence” — not merely a better life within the round of rebirth, but its termination.

Envied by gods and humans

The closing verse turns to the Buddhas themselves: “Gods and humans envy them, the Buddhas, ever mindful, of laughing wisdom, bearing their final body.” The Pali behind “laughing wisdom” is a compound, hāsapaññā — literally “joyful” or “exultant” wisdom — an unusual pairing of two words not often placed together, and Sujato’s “laughing” keeps that unusual flavor rather than smoothing it into a blander “joyful.” “Bearing their final body” is the standard marker for an arahant: this life is the last one. The discourse ends, in other words, exactly where its opening line pointed — not at a doctrine about wisdom in the abstract, but at concrete figures who have carried the teaching all the way through.

Key terms

parihīna — “bereft, diminished, fallen away from”: the discourse’s opening word, applied here specifically to noble wisdom rather than to any ordinary form of poverty or loss.
nāmarūpa — “name and form”: the mental and physical constituents of experience; the first verse calls the unwise world “habituated” to taking this as ultimate truth.
nibbedhagāminī — “leading to penetration”: the quality that makes wisdom “best in the world,” described as reaching all the way to the ending of rebirth.
jātibhavaparikkhaya — “the ending of rebirth and continued existence”: what wisdom’s penetration is said to rightly understand — the discourse’s outer horizon.
hāsapaññā — “laughing” or joyful wisdom: the closing verse’s distinctive compound describing what gods and humans envy in the Buddhas.

The text

The discourse in full: the twofold prose statement and all three verses. Nothing has been abridged — this short teaching is given complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Those sentient beings are truly bereft, mendicants, who are bereft of noble wisdom. They live unhappily in this very life—with distress, anguish, and fever—and when the body breaks up, after death, they can expect a bad rebirth. Those sentient beings are not bereft who are not bereft of noble wisdom. In this very life they’re happy—free of distress, anguish, and fever—and when the body breaks up, after death, they can expect a good rebirth.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“See the world with its gods, bereft of wisdom, habituated to name and form, imagining this is truth.

§4Wisdom is best in the world, as it leads to penetration, through which one rightly understands the ending of rebirth and continued existence.

§5Gods and humans envy them, the Buddhas, ever mindful, of laughing wisdom, bearing their final body.”

§6This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

Itivuttaka · Discourse 58

Craving

The Book of the Threes, Chapter One

Setting
None given — like almost every Itivuttaka discourse, no place, audience, or occasion is named
Form
A brief prose statement naming three cravings, followed by a two-stanza verse contrasting bondage to craving with crossing over
Segments
18 (iti58:1.1–5.1)
Length
Under 1 minute to read
Parallel
No complete Chinese Itivuttaka survives for comparison; no specific parallel is asserted here
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — brief, but its threefold craving is the same formula that defines the second noble truth

Why this discourse, fifty-eighth

Iti 58 names the three cravings — for sensual pleasures, for existence, and for nonexistence — that appear elsewhere in the canon as the formal definition of the second noble truth, the origin of suffering. Here they are given the Itivuttaka’s spare treatment: a one-sentence enumeration, then a verse that moves from beings “yoked by Māra’s yoke” and caught in endless transmigration to those who have “truly crossed over” by giving up exactly this craving.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

There are three cravings — for sensual pleasures, for existence, and for nonexistence — and beings bound by them are “yoked by Māra’s yoke,” continuing to transmigrate, while those who have given up all three “have truly crossed over” and reached the ending of the defilements.

The second noble truth, in miniature

This exact threefold division — craving for sensual pleasures, craving for existence, craving for nonexistence — is the standard definition of taṇhā given as the second noble truth, the origin of suffering, in the canon’s most famous teaching on the subject. Seeing it appear here, stripped of any narrative context, is a reminder that Iti 58 is not offering a minor or secondary observation about craving; it is restating, in the Itivuttaka’s characteristically compressed form, one of the most load-bearing doctrinal formulas in the entire tradition.

Craving for nonexistence

The first two cravings are intuitive: wanting pleasure, wanting to continue existing. The third, craving for nonexistence, is easy to misread as simple pessimism or a wish for annihilation, but in context it names a specific, subtle craving — the wish, arising precisely because existence is painful, that this self simply stop, rather than the wish to see existence and its causes clearly and thereby be free of them. Craving for nonexistence is still craving; it still keeps the wheel turning, just aimed in the opposite direction from craving for existence.

Yoked, then crossed over

The verse’s two stanzas are built as a clean contrast. The first describes beings “bound by craving,” their minds “full of desire for rebirth in this or that state,” “yoked by Māra’s yoke,” finding “no sanctuary,” continuing to transmigrate through “ongoing birth and death.” The second describes exactly the opposite condition for those who have “given up craving” and are “rid of craving for rebirth in this or that state”: they have “truly crossed over,” having “reached the ending of defilements.” The verse does not describe a partial or gradual softening of craving; it presents only two conditions, bound or crossed over, with nothing in between named.

Key terms

craving for sensual pleasures — the first of the three (kāma-taṇhā), ordinary craving for pleasant experience.
craving for existence — the second (bhava-taṇhā), the wish for being to continue.
craving for nonexistence — the third (vibhava-taṇhā), a subtler craving for this self to simply cease, distinct from clear insight into impermanence.
Māra’s yoke — the image of bondage the verse uses for beings still bound by craving, tying them to “ongoing birth and death.”
“truly crossed over” — the verse’s description of those who have given up craving, reaching “the ending of the defilements.”

The text

The discourse in full — brief prose and a two-stanza verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, there are these three cravings. What three? Craving for sensual pleasures, craving for existence, and craving for nonexistence. These are the three cravings.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“Bound by craving, minds full of desire
for rebirth in this or that state,
yoked by Māra’s yoke, these people
find no sanctuary from the yoke.
Sentient beings continue to transmigrate,
with ongoing birth and death.

§4Those who have given up craving,
rid of craving for rebirth in this or that state,
they in this world have truly crossed over,
having reached the ending of defilements.”

§5This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

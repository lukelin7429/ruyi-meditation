Itivuttaka · The Book of the Twos · Discourse 46

The Benefits of Training

Sikkhānisaṁsasutta

Setting
None given — no place, audience, or occasion; the discourse opens directly with the Itivuttaka's own formula
Position
19th of 22 discourses in the Book of the Twos
Form
An imperative prose exhortation, the same "two results" formula as Iti 45, and a two-verse restatement
Length
About 1 minute to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — concise and formulaic, built from four paired qualities

Why this discourse, forty-sixth

A compact instruction for how to live, structured around four roles: training as one’s benefit, wisdom as overseer, freedom as core, and mindfulness as ruler. Like its immediate neighbors in this part of the book, it closes with the same promise of one of two results, and verses that picture the finished task as having "disposed of Māra" and "gone beyond old age."

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Living with training as one's benefit, wisdom as overseer, freedom as core, and mindfulness as ruler leads to one of two results — enlightenment in this very life, or, if residue remains, non-return — and the sage who completes this training is pictured as having disposed of Māra and gone beyond both old age and death.

No narrative frame

As with almost every discourse in this collection, no place, audience, or occasion is given; the teaching opens directly with the Itivuttaka's formula.

Four roles for one life

The opening line assigns four different functions to four different qualities in a single sentence: "Mendicants, live with training as benefit, with wisdom as overseer, with freedom as core, and with mindfulness as ruler." Each metaphor is distinct — training (sikkhā) is what one gains from; wisdom is what oversees or supervises the rest; freedom (vimutti) is named as the very core or essence of the life described; mindfulness is called the ruler, the governing authority over it all. The four are not simply listed as a set of separate virtues, but assigned specific, differentiated jobs within a single structure.

The same "two results" formula

As in the discourse immediately before this one (Iti 45) and the one immediately after (Iti 47), the promise attached is identical: "A mendicant who lives in this way can expect one of two results: enlightenment in this very life, or if there's residue left behind, non-return." This repeated formula across three consecutive discourses suggests a deliberate grouping — retreat, this fourfold way of living, and wakeful meditation (in Iti 47) are each presented as sufficient, on their own, to reach one of the same two outcomes.

Disposing of Māra, going beyond old age

The first verse pictures the completed task: "The training fulfilled, not liable to decline, overseen by wisdom, seer of rebirth's end; that sage bears their final body; they have disposed of Māra, I declare, and gone beyond old age." Māra, the personification of death and hindrance throughout the early texts, is here something to be "disposed of," not merely avoided. The second verse turns this description into a direct exhortation: "Therefore be always enjoying absorption, immersed in samādhi, energetic, seers of rebirth's end. Having overcome Māra and his armies, mendicants, go beyond birth and death." The move from description to imperative is the verse's own structure — what the sage has already done becomes what the listening mendicants are told to do.

Key terms

sikkhānisaṁsā — "training as benefit": the first of the discourse's four roles, describing training as what one gains from.
paññuttarā — "wisdom as overseer": wisdom placed in a supervisory role over the rest of the practice.
vimuttisārā — "freedom as core": freedom named as the very essence of the life described, not merely its outcome.
satādhipateyyā — "mindfulness as ruler": mindfulness given the role of governing authority.
Māra — the personification of death and hindrance in early Buddhist texts; here something the finished sage is said to have "disposed of."

The text

The discourse in full: the fourfold exhortation, the two-results formula, and both verses. Bilara <j> join-markers have been removed for clean reading. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, live with training as benefit, with wisdom as overseer, with freedom as core, and with mindfulness as ruler. A mendicant who lives in this way can expect one of two results: enlightenment in this very life, or if there’s residue left behind, non-return.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“The training fulfilled, not liable to decline, overseen by wisdom, seer of rebirth’s end; that sage bears their final body; they have disposed of Māra, I declare, and gone beyond old age.

§4Therefore be always enjoying absorption, immersed in samādhi, energetic, seers of rebirth’s end. Having overcome Māra and his armies, mendicants, go beyond birth and death.”

§5This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

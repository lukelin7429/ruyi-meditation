Itivuttaka · Book of the Threes, Chapter Two · Iti 65

Good Conduct

Sucaritasutta

Setting
None given — no place, audience beyond “mendicants,” or occasion, in keeping with the Itivuttaka’s spare form throughout
Speakers
The Buddha alone; the Itivuttaka’s bipartite form never includes an interlocutor
Form
A one-line list of three items, restated and extended in a single two-stanza verse — the exact mirror of the previous discourse
Length
Under a minute to read the passage itself
Parallels
No complete Chinese Itivuttaka survives; a partial parallel collection exists in Tibetan translation, with scattered individual parallels across the Āgamas, none specifically identified for this short discourse
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — the plainest possible statement of a very familiar three-part scheme

Why this discourse

This discourse is, almost word for word, the previous one with every value reversed. Where Bad Conduct named three doors of wrongdoing and ended in hell for a “witless” person, this one names the same three doors used well and ends in heaven for a “wise” one. Reading the two side by side is more instructive than reading either alone: it shows, almost like a diagram, how the compilers of this collection built teachings in matched, symmetrical pairs.

Reading guide

Three doors, reversed

The prose statement names three kinds of good conduct (sucarita): “by way of body, speech, and mind” (kāyasucarita, vacīsucarita, manosucarita) — the identical threefold division used one discourse earlier for bad conduct, now applied to its positive counterpart. Where that discourse’s prose was diagnostic, naming a problem, this one is prescriptive, naming its resolution.

The verse: giving up, not just avoiding

Notice the verse’s exact phrasing: it does not simply say “good conduct by body, speech, and mind.” It says “having given up bad conduct by way of body… speech, and mind, and whatever else is corrupt” — conduct is framed as an active renunciation of the very same wrongdoing named in the previous sutta, not simply a separate, unrelated virtue. Then comes the same reversal seen in the prose: “not having done bad deeds, and having done many good, when their body breaks up, that wise person is reborn in heaven.” Sappañña, “wise,” is the exact opposite of duppañña, “witless,” used in the mirrored line of the previous discourse.

A matched pair, read together

Placed side by side, Bad Conduct and Good Conduct are close to being a single teaching split into two discourses: identical three-part structure, identical two-stanza verse form, every specific term inverted — bad becomes good, done becomes given up, witless becomes wise, hell becomes heaven. This kind of deliberate pairing is a genuine feature of how the Itivuttaka’s short chapters are organized, not an accident of translation or arrangement.

Part of a larger quartet

This discourse is also the second of four in this chapter built on the “three, by way of body, speech, and mind” template — followed immediately by Purity and Sagacity, which keep the same three-part structure but shift their outcome away from heaven and hell altogether, toward a state described as having “given up everything.” Seen as a set of four, the chapter appears to be working through variations on a single grammatical mold.

The form itself

As throughout this collection, Good Conduct opens with “This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard,” states its teaching in prose, restates it in verse, and closes with a near-mirror of the opening line. No place, occasion, or listener is named beyond “mendicants” — nothing narrative frames it at all.

Key terms

sucarita — “good conduct,” of three kinds: by body, speech, and mind — the direct counterpart of duccarita in the previous discourse.
kāya-, vacī-, manosucarita — good conduct “by way of body,” “by way of speech,” and “by way of mind” respectively.
“having given up” — the verse’s framing of good conduct as an active renunciation of the very wrongdoing named in the previous discourse, not a separate virtue.
sappañña — “wise,” literally endowed with wisdom (paññā); the exact opposite of duppañña, “witless,” used in Iti 64.
sagga — heaven; the stated destination when the cumulative pattern of good conduct predominates.

The text

The discourse in full — a one-line prose list of three kinds of good conduct, followed by a two-stanza verse describing their cumulative result. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

The teaching

§2“Mendicants, there are these kinds of good conduct. What three? Good conduct by way of body, speech, and mind. These are the three kinds of good conduct.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

The verse

§3“Having given up bad conduct by way of body, speech, and mind, and whatever else is corrupt;

§4not having done bad deeds, and having done many good, when their body breaks up, that wise person is reborn in heaven.”

§5This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

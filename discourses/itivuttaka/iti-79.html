Itivuttaka · The Book of the Threes · Chapter Three

Decline

Parihānasutta

Setting
Not stated — no place or occasion is given
Speakers
The Buddha alone; no interlocutor appears
Form
A matched pair of three-item prose lists, restated in a two-stanza verse
Length
~1 minute to read
Book
The Book of the Threes, Chapter Three — the closing discourse of this chapter
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — plain, practical advice aimed at trainees still in training

Why this discourse, the seventy-ninth

This is the last discourse in the chapter, and it closes on a note of blunt, practical advice for anyone still in training: three ordinary habits, named twice each — once as causes of decline, once, in their absence, as the conditions for not declining. There is no doctrine to unpack here, only a short list worth checking against one’s own daily habits.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Three things lead to the decline of a mendicant trainee — relishing work, relishing talk, and relishing sleep — and their absence, symmetrically, are the three things that do not lead to decline.

A trainee, specifically

The discourse is careful to specify who this applies to: a sekha, a “trainee,” someone who has entered the path but has not yet completed it. This isn’t a general warning against work, conversation, or rest as such — the concern is relishing, ārāma, an eager, absorbed delight in these three activities, of exactly the kind that would pull a trainee’s attention and energy away from practice at a stage where that attention still needs protecting.

Stated twice, mirror-image

The prose states its point twice, once in the negative and once in the positive, using nearly identical language both times: “relishes work, talk, and sleep” leads to decline; a trainee who “doesn’t relish work, talk, and sleep” does not decline. Nothing else is added on either side — the discourse trusts the plain mirroring to make its point without further argument.

“Few duties, being wakeful not restless”

The verse adds one useful reframe: it calls the state to avoid “restless” (uddhata) rather than simply busy, and recommends instead having “few duties” and being “wakeful, not restless.” Both versions of the trainee — the one in decline and the one who isn’t — are measured against the same stated goal: capacity for “the highest awakening,” called incapable for one and capable for the other, in exactly parallel language.

Key terms

sekha — “trainee” — someone who has entered the path but not yet completed it; the discourse’s stated audience.
parihāna — “decline” — the discourse’s Pali title, and the specific risk the three habits are said to produce.
kammārāma … bhassārāma … niddārāma — “relishing work … relishing talk … relishing sleep” — the three named habits, repeated verbatim in both the decline and non-decline formulas.
uddhata — “restless” — the verse’s term for the state produced by relishing these three things, contrasted with being wakeful and having few duties.
sambodhimuttama — “the highest awakening” — the goal both versions of the verse measure the trainee’s capacity against.

The text

The discourse in full: the two matched prose lists, the two-stanza verse, and the fixed closing line — the last discourse in this chapter of the Book of the Threes. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“These three things lead to the decline of a mendicant trainee. What three? It’s when a mendicant relishes work, talk, and sleep. These three things lead to the decline of a mendicant trainee.

§3These three things don’t lead to the decline of a mendicant trainee. What three? It’s when a mendicant doesn’t relish work, talk, and sleep. These three things don’t lead to the decline of a mendicant trainee.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§4“Restless, they relish
work, talk, and sleep.
Such a mendicant is incapable
of experiencing the highest awakening.

§5That’s why one ought have few duties,
being wakeful not restless.
Such a mendicant is capable
of experiencing the highest awakening.”

§6This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

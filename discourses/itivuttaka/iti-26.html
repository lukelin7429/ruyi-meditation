Itivuttaka · Discourse 26

Giving

Dānasutta

Collection
Book of the Ones (Ekakanipāta), Chapter Three — Iti 26 of 27 in this book
Setting
No location or interlocutor is given — only the Itivuttaka’s stock opening and closing formula
Form
A prose statement built as a counterfactual (“if beings knew…”), then a longer verse extending the same claim into a picture of heavenly rebirth
Length
~1 minute to read
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — direct and vividly concrete

Why this discourse, twenty-sixth

This discourse does not tell mendicants they ought to be generous. It makes a different, sharper claim: that stinginess survives only because people do not truly understand what generosity produces — and that if they did understand it as clearly as the Buddha says he does, they would not be able to eat a single meal without sharing it, down to the very last mouthful.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

If sentient beings knew, as the Buddha says he does, the fruit of giving and sharing, they would not eat without first giving, the stain of stinginess would not occupy their minds, and they would share even their last mouthful of food, so long as there was someone there to receive it.

A claim about knowledge, not virtue

What is notable here is the shape of the argument. The discourse does not say “you should be generous” as a moral instruction to be followed by an act of will against contrary inclination. It says, in effect: stinginess persists only because its true results are not known clearly enough. “It is because sentient beings do not know, as I do, the fruit of giving and sharing, that they eat without first giving, and the stain of stinginess occupies their minds.” Ignorance, not a fixed character flaw, is presented as the operative cause — which implies that clearer seeing, not just stronger willpower, is what would actually change the behavior.

The last mouthful

The discourse’s most vivid detail is also its most demanding: “they would not eat without sharing even their last mouthful, their last morsel, so long as there was someone to receive it.” This is not a call to give away surplus or abundance; it names the single hardest case — the very last bite of food a person has — and claims that full understanding of giving’s fruit would make even that worth sharing. It is a deliberately extreme test case, not a realistic expectation for daily practice, and it functions the way such extreme cases often do in this literature: by naming the limit, it clarifies how far the principle is being pushed, even if ordinary generosity falls well short of it.

The verse: from motive to outcome

Where the prose stays with the psychology of giving — the stain of stinginess, the clarity that dissolves it — the verse follows the act through to its outcome. Rid of stinginess, “with clear and confident heart,” the generous person gives “in season to the noble ones, where a gift is very fruitful.” Having given food in abundance to those worthy of a religious donation, “after passing from the human realm, the givers go to heaven,” where they “enjoy all the pleasures they desire.” The verse does not abandon the epistemic framing established by the prose; it simply completes the picture the prose gestures toward — this is what “the fruit of giving and sharing” that beings do not fully know actually consists of.

Left unspecified: to whom

It is worth noting what the discourse does not pin down. The opening prose speaks only of “someone to receive it” — any recipient at all is enough to make the last-morsel test meaningful. Only the verse narrows this to “the noble ones” and “those worthy of a religious donation” as the setting where a gift is described as “very fruitful.” The discourse as a whole moves from a very broad claim about the psychology of stinginess and generosity to a more specific picture of especially fruitful giving, without ever claiming that only the second is meritorious.

Key terms

dānasaṃvibhāgassa vipākaṃ — “the fruit and result of giving and sharing”: the specific knowledge the Buddha claims to have and ordinary beings lack.
macchera(mala) — “the stain of stinginess”: the mental obstruction the discourse says would simply have no foothold if giving’s fruit were truly understood.
carimo ālopo, carimaṃ kabaḷaṃ — “the last mouthful, the last morsel”: the discourse’s extreme test case for genuine generosity.
dakkhiṇeyya — “those worthy of an offering”: the recipients the verse specifies as making a gift especially fruitful.
paṭiggahaka — “a recipient”: the more general term used in the prose, where any recipient at all is enough for the last-morsel test to apply.

The text

The discourse in full: the counterfactual prose statement about knowledge and stinginess, and the verse extending it into a picture of heavenly rebirth. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, if sentient beings only knew, as I do, the fruit of giving and sharing, they would not eat without first giving, and the stain of stinginess would not occupy their minds. They would not eat without sharing even their last mouthful, their last morsel, so long as there was someone to receive it. It is because sentient beings do not know, as I do, the fruit of giving and sharing, that they eat without first giving, and the stain of stinginess occupies their minds.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“If sentient beings only knew
how great is the fruit
of giving and sharing
as taught by the great seer!

§4Rid of the stain of stinginess,
with clear and confident heart,
they would duly give to the noble ones,
where a gift is very fruitful.

§5Having given food in abundance
to those worthy of a religious donation,
after passing from the human realm,
the givers go to heaven.

§6And when they have arrived there in heaven,
they enjoy all the pleasures they desire.
The generous enjoy the
fruit of giving and sharing.”

§7This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

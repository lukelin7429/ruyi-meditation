Itivuttaka · The Book of the Threes · Iti 90

The Best Kinds of Confidence

“This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.”

Setting
None given — no place, audience, or occasion is named
Form
A prose statement arguing, one at a time, why each of three objects of confidence is "the best," followed by a two-stanza verse
Length
~2 minutes to read
Book
The Book of the Threes (Iti 60–99); the opening discourse of Chapter Five
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a clear, if repetitive, structure built around a single refrain

Why this discourse, ninetieth

This discourse opens the Book of the Threes' fifth chapter with a straightforward but carefully argued claim: confidence in the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Saṅgha isn't simply traditional or convenient — each of the three is argued, in turn, to actually be the best of its entire category, among all beings, all things conditioned or unconditioned, and all communities. The refrain that follows each argument is almost mathematical: confidence in the best yields, as its result, the best.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Confidence in the Buddha (the best of all sentient beings), in the teaching of fading away (the best of all things conditioned or unconditioned), and in the Saṅgha (the best of all communities) are the three best kinds of confidence, because “having confidence in the best, the result is the best” — a refrain repeated identically for all three.

Three claims to be “the best,” each argued specifically

Rather than simply asserting the superiority of the three refuges, the discourse gives each one a distinct argument for its claim to be “the best.” The Buddha is called “the best of all sentient beings—be they footless, with two feet, four feet, or many feet; with form or formless; with perception or without perception or with neither perception nor non-perception” — a comprehensive sweep through every conceivable category of living being, leaving nothing outside the comparison. The teaching of “fading away” (the Dhamma) is called “the best of all things whether conditioned or unconditioned,” unpacked immediately as “the quelling of vanity, the removing of thirst, the abolishing of clinging, the breaking of the round, the ending of craving, fading away, cessation, extinguishment” — a cascade of near-synonyms for the same cessation, piled up rather than merely named once. The Saṅgha is called “the best of all communities and groups,” described in the canon's standard formula as “the four pairs, the eight individual persons,” worthy of offerings, hospitality, religious donation, greeting with cupped palms, and standing as “the supreme field of merit for the world.”

A refrain, repeated exactly

After each of the three arguments, the text closes with the identical two-line refrain: “Those who have confidence in [the Buddha / the teaching of fading away / the Saṅgha] have confidence in the best. Having confidence in the best, the result is the best.” The repetition isn't incidental — it is the discourse's actual argument, restated three times so the logical shape becomes unmistakable: the object of one's confidence determines the character of the result, and an object that is genuinely “the best” of its category yields a correspondingly superior result. This is less an appeal to loyalty or tradition than a kind of proportional reasoning about where confidence is well placed.

The Dhamma described without being named directly

It's worth noticing that the second kind of confidence is never called simply “the Dhamma” in the prose — it's “fading away” (virāga), immediately glossed through eight near-synonymous phrases for the ending of craving and clinging. Among the three objects of confidence, this is the only one described entirely in terms of a process or an absence — not a person (like the Buddha) or a community (like the Saṅgha), but a cessation. The discourse's choice to describe it this way, rather than simply naming "the teaching" as an abstract body of doctrine, keeps the focus on what the teaching actually points toward and accomplishes, rather than treating it as a body of propositions to have confidence in for their own sake.

The verse: confidence that pays dividends

The closing verse restates all three objects of confidence in compressed form, then adds a claim the prose doesn't make explicitly: that “giving gifts to the best, the best of merit grows: the best lifespan, beauty, fame, reputation, happiness, and strength.” The verse pairs confidence with generosity directly — it isn't only about where one places trust internally, but about where one directs material offerings, and ties that choice to concrete, worldly benefits alongside the spiritual ones. The final line completes the thought for anyone who takes this path: “when they become a god or human, they rejoice at reaching the best” — whichever kind of favorable rebirth follows, the outcome is framed as continuous with, not separate from, the confidence and giving that produced it.

Key terms

the three best kinds of confidence — confidence in the Buddha, in the teaching of fading away (the Dhamma), and in the Saṅgha.
fading away (virāga) — the discourse's name for the Dhamma in this context, unpacked as "the quelling of vanity, the removing of thirst, the abolishing of clinging… cessation, extinguishment."
the four pairs, the eight individual persons — the standard canonical description of the noble Saṅgha, called here "the supreme field of merit for the world."
"having confidence in the best, the result is the best" — the discourse's exact refrain, repeated after each of the three arguments.
the best lifespan, beauty, fame, reputation, happiness, and strength — the concrete results the verse says grow from giving gifts to the best, alongside the spiritual result of confidence itself.

The text

The discourse in full: the prose argument for each of the three best kinds of confidence, and the two-stanza closing verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, these three kinds of confidence are the best. What three?

Mendicants, the Realized One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha, is said to be the best of all sentient beings—be they footless, with two feet, four feet, or many feet; with form or formless; with perception or without perception or with neither perception nor non-perception. Those who have confidence in the Buddha have confidence in the best. Having confidence in the best, the result is the best.

§3Fading away is said to be the best of all things whether conditioned or unconditioned. That is, the quelling of vanity, the removing of thirst, the abolishing of clinging, the breaking of the round, the ending of craving, fading away, cessation, extinguishment. Those who have confidence in the teaching of fading away have confidence in the best. Having confidence in the best, the result is the best.

§4The Saṅgha of the Realized One’s disciples is said to be the best of all communities and groups. It consists of the four pairs, the eight individual persons. This is the Saṅgha of the Buddha’s disciples that is worthy of offerings dedicated to the gods, worthy of hospitality, worthy of a religious donation, worthy of greeting with cupped palms, and is the supreme field of merit for the world. Those who have confidence in the Saṅgha have confidence in the best. Having confidence in the best, the result is the best. These are the three best kinds of confidence.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§5“For those who, knowing the best teaching,
base their confidence on the best—
confident in the best Awakened One,
supremely worthy of a religious donation;

§6confident in the best teaching,
the bliss of fading and stilling;
confident in the best Saṅgha,
the supreme field of merit—

§7giving gifts to the best,
the best of merit grows:
the best lifespan, beauty,
fame, reputation, happiness, and strength.

§8An intelligent person gives to the best,
settled on the best teaching.
When they become a god or human,
they rejoice at reaching the best.”

§9This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

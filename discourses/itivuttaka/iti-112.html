Itivuttaka · Discourse 112

The World

Lokasutta

Setting
No narrative frame — no time, place, or listener is named
Speakers
The Buddha, addressing the mendicants collectively
Form
A fourfold structure mirroring the four noble truths, applied to "the world"; four separate explanations of the epithet “Tathāgata”; and a seven-stanza closing verse
Length
~2 minutes to read
Position
13th and final discourse of the Chapter on the Brahmin’s Offering of the Teaching, the sole chapter of the Book of the Fours — and the last of all 112 discourses in the Itivuttaka
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — dense in its middle section, expansive in its closing verse

Why this discourse

The last of 112 discourses turns, at the very end, from small practical matters — rag-robes, honest speech, walking postures, honoring parents — to the widest possible frame: the world itself, and the one who is said to have fully understood it. Four separate reasons are given for the title “Tathāgata,” each closing on the same refrain, before the longest verse in this set of twelve discourses turns the whole thing into a hymn.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

“The world has been understood by the Realized One, and he is detached from the world” — a claim repeated for the world’s origin, its cessation, and the practice leading to its cessation, before the discourse gives four separate reasons why he is called “Tathāgata,” the Realized One, at all.

The four truths, one more time — now about “the world”

The opening lines follow a structure this same chapter has already used twice, in Iti 102 and Iti 103, applied there to suffering: something is understood, its origin is understood and given up, its cessation is understood and realized, and the practice leading to that cessation is understood and developed. Here the object is not named as “suffering” at all, but as “the world” (loka) — “the world has been understood… the origin of the world has been understood and given up… the cessation of the world has been understood and realized… the practice that leads to the cessation of the world has been understood and developed.” The parallel is exact enough that it amounts to an equation: whatever “the world” means in this discourse, it is being treated as functionally identical to dukkha itself — the same four-part structure, one final time, at the widest possible scale.

Four reasons for a single name

The discourse then asks, in effect, what “Tathāgata” actually means, and gives four separate answers, each closing on the same refrain: “that’s why he’s called the ‘Realized One’.”

Basis for the nameWhat is said
Total understandingWhatever is seen, heard, thought, known, attained, sought, or explored by the mind, in this whole world with its gods, Māras, and Divinities — all of it has been understood by him.
Word matches realityFrom the night of his awakening to the night of his final extinguishment, everything he speaks, says, and expresses is real, not otherwise.
Word matches deedHe does as he says, and says as he does.
Unrivaled in the worldIn this whole world, he is the vanquisher, the unvanquished, the universal seer, the wielder of power.

No single one of these four is offered as the whole explanation; together they cover comprehension, truthfulness across an entire teaching career, integrity between word and action, and a final claim of unrivaled standing — four independent grounds converging on the same title.

The verse: a hymn to the Tathāgata

Where most discourses in this chapter close with a compact two- or three-stanza verse, this one runs to seven — the longest in this set of twelve, and noticeably more exalted in tone. It moves from a restatement of detachment (“directly knowing the whole world as it is… he is detached from the whole world, uninvolved with the whole world”) to a description of the released mind (“reached ultimate peace: extinguishment, fearing nothing from any quarter”), and then outward, to how others respond: gods and humans who have gone to him for refuge “come together and revere him, even the deities revere him,” calling him, in the verse’s own words, “the best of tamers,” “the seer among the peaceful,” “the foremost of liberators,” and “the most excellent of guides across.” The verse’s final line is a plain, unadorned claim: “in the world with its gods, he has no rival.”

Closing the collection

This is the last of 112 discourses in the Itivuttaka, and its own content marks the fact in its own quiet way, without any explicit farewell. Having spent eleven chapters’ worth of short teachings on rag-robes, honest speech among mendicants, the postures of the body, and the debt owed to one’s own parents, the collection’s final word turns fully toward the teacher himself — not as a display of grandeur, but as one more instance of the same thing this whole book has been doing all along: stating, as exactly as possible, what is actually true. Following the verse, the source text adds a line that is not part of the poem at all, closing not only this discourse but the entire collection: “The So it Was Said is finished.” Nothing more is said after that. A collection built from a hundred and twelve short, spare restatements of the same handful of concerns ends the way it began each time — with a plain, closing formula, and nothing appended to dress it up.

Key terms

loka — “the world”: the discourse’s subject and its own Pāli title, treated here through the same fourfold structure elsewhere applied to suffering.
tathāgata — “the Realized One”: the epithet given four separate explanations across this discourse, each closing with the same refrain.
visaṁyutta — “detached” or disjoined: what the Realized One is said to be from the world, even while having fully understood it.
anupādisesā nibbānadhātu — “the no-residue element of extinguishment”: named as the point marking the end of the period across which everything the Tathāgata says is said to be real.
brahmacakka — “the divine wheel,” which the verse says the Buddha “turns… in all the world with its gods” — the same wheel-turning image used of the first teaching of the four truths.

The text

The discourse in full, given complete: the fourfold structure applied to “the world,” the four explanations of the name “Tathāgata,” the seven-stanza closing verse, and the line marking the end of the entire collection. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, the world has been understood by the Realized One; and he is detached from the world. The origin of the world has been understood by the Realized One; and he has given up the origin of the world. The cessation of the world has been understood by the Realized One; and he has realized the cessation of the world. The practice that leads to the cessation of the world has been understood by the Realized One; and he has developed the practice that leads to the cessation of the world.

§3In this world—with its gods, Māras, and Divinities, this population with its ascetics and brahmins, its gods and humans—whatever is seen, heard, thought, known, attained, sought, and explored by the mind, all that has been understood by the Realized One. That’s why he’s called the ‘Realized One’.

§4From the night when the Realized One awakens to the supreme perfect awakening until the night he becomes fully extinguished—in the element of extinguishment with no residue—everything he speaks, says, and expresses is real, not otherwise. That’s why he’s called the ‘Realized One’.

§5The Realized One does as he says, and says as he does. Since this is so, that’s why he’s called the ‘Realized One’.

§6In this world—with its gods, Māras, and divinities, this population with its ascetics and brahmins, gods and humans—the Realized One is the vanquisher, the unvanquished, the universal seer, the wielder of power. That’s why he’s called the ‘Realized One’.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§7–9“Directly knowing the whole world as it is, and everything in it, he is detached from the whole world, uninvolved with the whole world. That attentive one is the vanquisher of all who is released from all ties. He has reached ultimate peace: extinguishment, fearing nothing from any quarter. He is the Buddha, with defilements ended, untroubled, with doubts cut off. He has attained the end of all deeds, freed with the ending of attachments.

§10–13That Blessed One is the Buddha, he is the supreme lion, in all the world with its gods, he turns the divine wheel. And so those gods and humans, who have gone to the Buddha for refuge, come together and revere him, even the deities revere him: ‘Tamed, he is the best of tamers, peaceful, he is the seer among the peaceful, liberated, he is the foremost of liberators, crossed over, he is the most excellent of guides across.’ And so they revere him, great of heart and intrepid. In the world with its gods, he has no rival.”

§14This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

The collection’s own closing words. Following this verse, the source text adds a line, not itself part of the verse, marking the end not only of this discourse but of the entire Itivuttaka: “The So it Was Said is finished.”

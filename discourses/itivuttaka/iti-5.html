Disdain

Makkhasutta

Why this discourse names a quieter fault

Disdain is a subtler target than greed, hate, or even anger — it names not a surge of wanting or aversion but a settled contempt for others, a habit of belittling their qualities or achievements. Iti 5 gives it exactly the same weight and the same formula as the louder defilements before it, which is itself worth noticing: the text treats a quiet, socially respectable-seeming fault as no less serious an obstacle than greed or hate.

Reading guide

Makkha: contempt, denigration, disdain

The Pali term makkha is usually rendered as disdain, denigration, or contempt — specifically the habit of dismissing or belittling others' good qualities, contributions, or achievements rather than acknowledging them honestly. It is a quieter fault than the ones named so far in this chapter: no dramatic surge of wanting, no flash of temper, just a persistent unwillingness to give credit where it is due.

Why a “quiet” fault gets the same treatment

What is notable is that the text gives disdain the exact same structural weight as greed, hate, delusion, and anger before it: the same guarantee, the same six-line verse. There is no hint here that disdain is a lesser concern simply because it is less dramatic. If anything, a fault this socially unremarkable — the kind of quiet contempt that rarely draws attention or correction from others — may be easier to overlook in oneself precisely because it does not announce itself the way anger or greed do.

A small irregularity, preserved honestly

Readers checking this page against the source translation may notice one small oddity: the opening line reads “give up one thing. and I guarantee” — a period followed by a lowercase “and,” rather than the comma used in the parallel line of every other sutta in this set. This is a minor irregularity present in the published source text itself, not an error introduced here; in keeping with the commitment to verbatim reproduction, it has been left exactly as found rather than silently corrected.

Disdain among related faults

Elsewhere in the canon, disdain is grouped together with several related qualities — among them the conceit taken up two suttas later in this same chapter (Iti 6) — as part of a longer list of subtler corruptions of the mind, distinct from the three foundational roots but no less worth uprooting.

Key terms

makkha — “disdain, denigration, contempt”: the habit of dismissing or belittling others' good qualities rather than acknowledging them
mūla — “root”: the term for the three foundational unwholesome causes (greed, hate, delusion); disdain is a related but distinct, subtler corruption of mind
anāgāmitā — “non-return”: the same guarantee held out here as for every factor in this set of six teachings
pajānanti — “they rightly understand”: the verse’s description of how the discerning come to relinquish disdain, as with the other factors in this chapter
the period quirk — a small irregularity in the source text at 2.1–2.2, where the sentence break falls differently than in the parallel line of Iti 1–4 and 6; preserved verbatim here rather than silently corrected

The text

The discourse in full: the opening frame, the Buddha’s one-sentence teaching, the verse that restates it, and the closing frame. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral). A minor punctuation irregularity present in the source (noted in the reading guide) is preserved exactly as published.

The teaching

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, give up one thing. and I guarantee you non-return. What one thing? Disdain is the one thing. Give it up, and I guarantee you non-return.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

The verse

§3“When overcome by disdain
beings go to a bad place.
Having rightly understood that disdain,
the discerning give it up.
Once they’ve given it up,
they never return to this world.”

§4This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

Why this discourse, second of a set of six

Iti 2 repeats Iti 1's structure almost word for word, changing only the name of the factor at stake: hate in place of greed. That exactness is itself the point worth noticing here. Rather than reading this as a second, separate story, it helps to read it as the second entry in a short, deliberate catalogue — one where the repetition is doing real work, training the listener to isolate one quality of mind at a time rather than lumping every unskillful state together.

Reading guide

Same shape, one word changed

Compare this discourse line by line against Iti 1 and the overlap is almost total: the same guarantee, the same six-line verse, even the same rhythm of “when overcome by ___, beings go to a bad place.” The only substitution is the defilement itself. This is not laziness in the text — it is a rhetorical device. By holding everything else constant and swapping a single variable, the collection turns six independent-seeming suttas into something closer to a structured checklist a listener could actually use: greed, then hate, then delusion, and on through anger, disdain, and conceit, each isolated in turn for direct inspection.

Hate as the second root

Where greed (lobha) pulls a mind toward something, hate (dosa) pushes a mind away from something — aversion, ill will, ill-tempered rejection of what is unwanted. Named second among the three traditional unwholesome roots (after greed, before delusion), dosa is the opposite psychological mechanism to greed but is said to distort perception and motivate harmful action in much the same fundamental way.

What the guarantee might be pointing at

The reward on offer is again anāgāmitā, non-return. In the traditional model of the ten fetters that bind a person to repeated rebirth, ill will (usually named byāpāda or paṭigha) is one of the two fetters whose complete abandonment is specifically what distinguishes a non-returner from those at the two stages below. Whether “hate” in this terse formula is meant as a precise equivalent of that specific fetter, or as a broader, more everyday name for the same underlying tendency, the text does not spell out — and it is worth resisting the urge to force too tight an equation between a four-word teaching and a technical list found elsewhere. What can be said plainly is that the pairing of hate with non-return here sits comfortably alongside that broader traditional picture.

Reading it as part of a set

Taken with Iti 1, this sutta suggests a reading strategy: rather than treating each of the first six teachings in this chapter as a complete lesson on its own, read them side by side as a single extended teaching in six short movements, each isolating exactly one obstacle.

Key terms

dosa — “hate”: aversion, ill will, hostility; the second of the three unwholesome roots, alongside greed and delusion
byāpāda / paṭigha — “ill will” / “repulsion”: terms used elsewhere for the specific fetter said to be abandoned by a non-returner — closely related to, though not necessarily identical in scope with, the everyday term dosa used here
duggati — “bad place, place of loss”: what the verse says awaits beings overcome by hate, mirroring the same phrase used of greed in Iti 1
anāgāmitā — “non-return”: the guarantee held out for giving up hate, as for greed — not full liberation, but a specific stage short of it
ekadhammaṃ — “one thing”: the recurring structural claim across Iti 1–6, that a single factor, fully relinquished, suffices for the stated result

The text

The discourse in full: the opening frame, the Buddha’s one-sentence teaching, the verse that restates it, and the closing frame. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The teaching

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, give up one thing and I guarantee you non-return. What one thing? Hate is the one thing. Give it up, and I guarantee you non-return.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

The verse

§3“When overcome by hate
beings go to a bad place.
Having rightly understood that hate,
the discerning give it up.
Once they’ve given it up,
they never return to this world.”

§4This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

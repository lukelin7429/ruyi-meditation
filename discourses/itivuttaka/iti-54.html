Itivuttaka · Discourse 54

Searches (1st)

The Book of the Threes, Chapter One

Setting
None given — like almost every Itivuttaka discourse, no place, audience, or occasion is named
Form
A brief prose statement naming three searches, followed by a single verse reusing the same four-part formula found at Iti 52
Segments
16 (iti54:1.1–5.1)
Length
Under 1 minute to read
Parallel
No complete Chinese Itivuttaka survives for comparison; no specific parallel is asserted here
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a short, formulaic teaching, though the third “search” is worth pausing over

Why this discourse, fifty-fourth

Iti 54 turns the same lens used on feeling (Iti 52–53) onto a different target: searching itself. Three searches are named — for sensual pleasures, for continued existence, and, notably, for a spiritual path — and the same understand-cause-cessation-path formula from Iti 52 is applied to them. That the third search, the search for a spiritual path, is itself something to be understood and eventually ended is the discourse’s sharpest point: even the search that looks like the solution is, in the end, still a search.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

There are three searches — for sensual pleasures, for continued existence, and for a spiritual path — and a mendicant who understands them, their cause, their ending, and the path to that ending is, in the verse’s words, “hungerless, quenched” — the identical description used for feeling in Iti 52.

Three searches, not two

The first two searches are unsurprising: wanting sensual pleasure, and wanting one’s existence to continue (in this life or a future one). The third is the discourse’s point of interest: “the search for a spiritual path” is named as a search in exactly the same sense as the other two, subject to the same analysis, the same cause, and the same ending. This is not a claim that spiritual practice is worthless — the discourse itself is part of a spiritual path — but a claim about searching as an activity: even a search aimed at something wholesome remains, structurally, a form of seeking driven by what is lacking, and it too is meant to be understood and eventually laid to rest rather than pursued forever.

The same formula, a different target

The verse here is word for word the same shape used for feeling at Iti 52: “understands searches, the cause of searches, where they cease, and the path that leads to their ending,” ending in a mendicant who is “hungerless, quenched.” Seeing the identical four-part method applied to two entirely different topics — feeling and searching — is itself instructive: the Book of the Threes is demonstrating a repeatable analytical tool, not offering a list of unrelated observations.

A quiet textual detail

In the source text used here, the sentence “These are the three searches” is not closed with a quotation mark before the narration resumes — unlike the equivalent line in several neighboring discourses (Iti 52, Iti 55, Iti 56), where the closing quotation mark is present. This reading guide reproduces the text exactly as given rather than silently correcting it; it does not change the meaning of the passage.

Key terms

the search for sensual pleasures — the first search, ordinary craving for pleasant experience.
the search for continued existence — the second, the wish for one’s being to go on, in this life or beyond it.
the search for a spiritual path — the third, named here as a search in the same sense as the other two, and subject to the same ending.
the four-part formula — the thing itself, its cause, its cessation, and the path to cessation; identical in shape to the formula applied to feeling at Iti 52.
“hungerless, quenched” — the verse’s closing description, word for word the same phrase used to close Iti 52.

The text

The discourse in full — brief prose and a single verse, reproduced exactly as it appears in the source, including one place where a closing quotation mark is absent. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, there are these three searches. What three? The search for sensual pleasures, the search for continued existence, and the search for a spiritual path. These are the three searches. The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“Stilled, aware,
a mindful disciple of the Buddha
understands searches,
the cause of searches,

§4where they cease,
and the path that leads to their ending.
With the ending of searches, a mendicant
is hungerless, quenched.”

§5This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

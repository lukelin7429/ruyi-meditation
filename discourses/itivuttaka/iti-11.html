Itivuttaka · The Book of the Ones · Chapter Two

Complete Understanding of Delusion

The Book of the Ones, Chapter Two

Audience
Mendicants in general — no named individual, no location, no occasion is given anywhere in the text
Form
A single terse prose statement, spoken once, followed by a four-couplet verse restating the identical point
Length
Under a minute to read
Series
The fifth of seven “complete understanding” discourses (Iti 7–13), each applying an identical formula in turn to conceit, greed, hate, delusion, anger, and disdain
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — plain and very short, though the claim it makes is total

Why this discourse, eleventh

This text has almost nothing in it by way of scene-setting, and that is precisely the point. It names one thing — delusion — states as flatly as possible that suffering cannot end without fully knowing and abandoning it, and stops. There is no visitor, no dialogue, no place-name. What the Itivuttaka offers instead of narrative is repetition: the same claim once in prose, then once again in verse, as though saying a thing twice, in two different registers, were itself a way of driving it home.

Reading guide

A form built for memory, not narrative

Nearly every discourse in the Itivuttaka follows the same shape, and this one shows it with almost nothing else added: an opening attribution (“This was said by the Buddha… that is what I heard”), a short prose statement of the teaching, a bridge line (“The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said”), a verse that says the same thing again in different words, and a closing attribution mirroring the opening. There is no brahmin, no householder, no place name, no story of any kind. That absence is not a gap in the record; it is how this genre works. The Itivuttaka’s short units were built to be carried in memory and repeated, and repetition — prose, then verse, then done — is the technique doing that work here.

Delusion, named alone

Early Buddhist teaching regularly names three roots of unwholesome action: greed (lobha), hate (dosa), and delusion (moha). Elsewhere in this same chapter of the Itivuttaka, greed and hate each receive their own discourse built on this identical formula (Iti 9 and Iti 10). Here it is delusion’s turn, isolated and addressed on its own terms, without being folded into a longer discussion of the other two. The discourse does not define delusion further or illustrate it with an example; it simply asserts what is at stake in failing to deal with it.

Two verbs, not one

The statement doesn’t say merely that you must understand delusion to end suffering. It pairs two distinct operations: “directly knowing and completely understanding” on one side, and “dispassion… and giving it up” on the other. The first is cognitive — seeing delusion clearly and completely, not partially or theoretically. The second is a change in orientation and then in behavior — losing one’s appetite for delusion and actually relinquishing it. The discourse’s structure insists both operations are required together; knowing about delusion without also becoming dispassionate toward it and giving it up is, by this formula, not enough to end suffering.

The verse: a bad place, and no return

The verse restates the prose claim through consequence rather than definition. Overcome by delusion, “beings go to a bad place” — the discourse doesn’t elaborate on what that place is, but the phrase throughout this literature marks an unhappy destination after death. The second half turns the picture around: once delusion is rightly understood and given up, “they never return to this world.” That closing line is worth sitting with. It doesn’t promise a better rebirth; it describes an end to the entire cycle of coming back at all — the strongest claim this short text has room to make.

Reading it beside its neighbors

This discourse makes the most sense read as one unit in a set. Iti 9 and Iti 10 apply the same formula to greed and hate; Iti 12 and Iti 13, immediately following, apply it to anger and disdain. Read in sequence, the pattern itself becomes part of the teaching: the same total claim, made again and again, for each defilement in turn, refusing to suggest that any one of them might be safe to leave unexamined.

Key terms

moha — delusion, the third of the three unwholesome roots alongside greed and hate; the subject of this discourse in isolation.
pariññā — “complete understanding,” the exhaustive, non-partial knowing the discourse demands, distinct from simply being told about a thing.
virāga — “dispassion,” the fading of appetite or attachment that the discourse pairs with understanding as a second, necessary condition.
duggati — “a bad place,” the unhappy destination the verse names as the result of being overcome by delusion.
“never return to this world” — the verse’s closing phrase, describing not merely a better rebirth but an end to the returning itself.

The text

The discourse in full — all of it, since nothing here is longer than this. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, without directly knowing and completely understanding delusion, without dispassion for it and giving it up, you can’t end suffering. By directly knowing and completely understanding delusion, having dispassion for it and giving it up, you can end suffering.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“When overcome by delusion
beings go to a bad place.
Having rightly understood that delusion,
the discerning give it up.
Once they’ve given it up,
they never return to this world.”

§4This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

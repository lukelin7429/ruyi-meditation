Itivuttaka · Book of the Threes, Chapter Two · Iti 64

Bad Conduct

Duccaritasutta

Setting
None given — no place, audience beyond “mendicants,” or occasion, in keeping with the Itivuttaka’s spare form throughout
Speakers
The Buddha alone; the Itivuttaka’s bipartite form never includes an interlocutor
Form
A one-line list of three items, restated and extended in a single two-stanza verse
Length
Under a minute to read the passage itself
Parallels
No complete Chinese Itivuttaka survives; a partial parallel collection exists in Tibetan translation, with scattered individual parallels across the Āgamas, none specifically identified for this short discourse
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — the plainest possible statement of a very familiar three-part scheme

Why this discourse

This is one of the most basic ethical claims in the entire canon, reduced to its barest form: there are three doors through which conduct happens — body, speech, and mind — and bad conduct through any of them, uncorrected by good deeds, leads at death to hell. Nothing here is subtle; the discourse’s real interest is structural. It is the first of a matched pair, its very next companion in the collection restating the same three-part scheme with every value flipped — and together with two further discourses just after it, it opens a short run of four suttas in this chapter built on exactly the same body-speech-mind template.

Reading guide

Three doors, one conduct

The prose could not be more direct: there are three kinds of bad conduct (duccarita), “by way of body, speech, and mind” (kāyaduccarita, vacīduccarita, manoduccarita). This threefold division of action — what one does, says, and thinks or intends — is one of the most basic organizing schemes in the entire tradition’s account of ethics, and this discourse states it with no further gloss at all.

The verse: cumulative bad, single result

The verse does not multiply the three into three separate outcomes. Instead, it piles them into a single cumulative portrait — “having done bad things by way of body, speech, and mind, and whatever else is corrupt” — and adds a further, distinct failing: not having done good deeds at all, on top of having done many bad ones. The result is stated once, for the whole pattern together: “when their body breaks up, that witless person is reborn in hell.” The Pali word for “witless” here, duppañña, literally means one lacking wisdom (paññā) — tying this discourse’s ethical failure back to the language of insight used two suttas earlier in The Eye.

Its mirror twin, next

What makes this discourse worth reading alongside its immediate neighbor is how exactly it is mirrored there. Good Conduct, the very next sutta in the collection, restates this same structure — three doors, a cumulative verse, a single result — with every term flipped: good conduct instead of bad, giving up wrongdoing instead of doing it, a wise person instead of a witless one, heaven instead of hell. Read together, the pair reads almost like a single teaching split into a negative and positive half.

Where this fits: a quartet of body-speech-mind suttas

This discourse also opens a short run of four suttas in this chapter — Bad Conduct, Good Conduct, Purity, and Sagacity — all four built on the identical “three, by way of body, speech, and mind” template, though the last two shift from conduct and its rebirth-consequences to purity and sagehood, ending not in heaven or hell but in a state described as having “given up everything.” Seeing the four side by side makes the compiler’s method visible: the same grammatical mold, filled four different ways.

The form itself

As throughout this collection, Bad Conduct opens with “This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard,” states its teaching in prose, restates it in verse, and closes with a near-mirror of the opening line. No place, occasion, or listener is named beyond “mendicants” — nothing narrative frames it at all.

Key terms

duccarita — “bad conduct” or misconduct, of three kinds: by body, speech, and mind.
kāya-, vacī-, manoduccarita — misconduct “by way of body,” “by way of speech,” and “by way of mind” respectively; the three doors through which action happens.
kusala / akusala — “skillful” and “unskillful” deeds; the verse contrasts not having done the former with having done much of the latter.
duppañña — “witless,” literally lacking wisdom (paññā); the verse’s description of the person reborn in hell.
niraya — hell, or a place of loss; the stated destination when the cumulative pattern of bad conduct goes uncorrected.

The text

The discourse in full — a one-line prose list of three kinds of bad conduct, followed by a two-stanza verse describing their cumulative result. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

The teaching

§2“Mendicants, there are these three kinds of bad conduct. What three? Bad conduct by way of body, speech, and mind. These are the three kinds of bad conduct.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

The verse

§3“Having done bad things by way of body, speech, and mind, and whatever else is corrupt;

§4not having done good deeds, and having done many bad, when their body breaks up, that witless person is reborn in hell.”

§5This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

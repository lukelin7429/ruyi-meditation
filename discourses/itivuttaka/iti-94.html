Itivuttaka · Iti 94

Examination

The Book of the Threes · Chapter Five

Book
The Book of the Threes · Chapter Five
Frame

Form

Length
Under 1 minute to read
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — brief, but its closing image is left unexplained

Why this discourse, ninety-fourth

This is the shortest discourse in the entire set — two sentences of prose and a single quatrain. Its claim is precise: when a mendicant examines in such a way that consciousness neither scatters outward, sticks inward, nor grows anxious through grasping, the very origin of future suffering simply has nowhere left to arise.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

When examination keeps consciousness from scattering outward, sticking inward, or growing anxious through grasping, the very origin of future suffering — rebirth, old age, and death — has nowhere to arise.

The shortest discourse in this set

Honesty is worth stating plainly here: this is the most compressed discourse among the ten covered in this batch — just two sentences of prose plus a single four-line verse. It names no location, no audience, no story. It reads almost like a note-to-self, or a formula meant to be turned over in meditation rather than unpacked in a lecture.

Consciousness, examined

The instruction names three ways consciousness should not behave under examination: it should be “neither scattered nor diffused externally” (that is, not chasing after external objects), “nor stuck internally” (not clinging inward either, perhaps to inner states or a sense of self), and the person “would not be anxious because of grasping” — a phrase that recurs across the early texts for the distress that follows when something clung to changes, is threatened, or is lost. When all three conditions hold, the discourse says, “there is no coming to be of the origin of suffering — of rebirth, old age, and death in the future.”

Seven chains, left unnamed

The closing verse speaks of “one who has given up seven chains, a mendicant who has cut the cord” — but the discourse itself never lists what those seven chains are. This is worth flagging honestly rather than guessing: the number seven for binding qualities or tendencies does appear elsewhere in the broader tradition, but this particular short verse leaves the list unspecified, trusting the listener to already know it or simply making its point without spelling out the details. It is a clear example of the compressed, allusive style this collection is capable of at its most minimal.

Key terms

viññāṇa, consciousness — the faculty examined here for whether it scatters outward, sticks inward, or grows anxious through grasping.
anxious because of grasping — a recurring early-text phrase for the distress that follows when something clung to changes, is threatened, or is lost.
“seven chains” — an unenumerated group of bonds mentioned only in the closing verse; this discourse itself does not list them.
“cut the cord” — the verse’s image for a mendicant who has severed whatever still ties them to further rebirth.
transmigration (saṃsāra) — the ongoing movement through repeated rebirth, said here to be finished for such a person.

The text

The discourse in full — the entirety of it, being extremely brief. No location or listener is named in the source. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1.1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2.1–2.4“A mendicant should examine in whatever way such that, while examining, consciousness would neither be scattered nor diffused externally, nor stuck internally, and they would not be anxious because of grasping. When consciousness is neither scattered nor diffused externally, nor stuck internally, and they are not anxious because of grasping, there is no coming to be of the origin of suffering—of rebirth, old age, and death in the future.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3.1–3.4“For one who has given up seven chains,
a mendicant who has cut the cord,
transmigration through births is finished,
there’ll be no more future lives for them.”

§4.1This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

Elements of Extinguishment

Nibbānadhātusutta

Position
17th of 22 discourses in the Book of the Twos
Form

Length
About 1–2 minutes to read

Why this discourse, forty-fourth

This short discourse is the standard canonical source for a distinction later Buddhist thought returns to repeatedly: two "elements of extinguishment," one that describes a perfected one still alive in the world, senses intact, and one described only as a felt experience that "will become cool right here." The text is precise about what it says and, notably, restrained about what it does not say — a restraint this guide tries to match rather than resolve on the text’s behalf.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

There are two elements of extinguishment (nibbānadhātu): one "with residue," describing a perfected one whose greed, hate, and delusion have ended even though the five senses still function and still register pleasant and unpleasant experience, and one "with no residue," described only in the text's own spare terms as a state in which everything felt, no longer relished, "will become cool right here."

No narrative frame

As with almost every discourse in this collection, no place, audience, or occasion is given; the text opens directly with the Itivuttaka's formula and proceeds straight into its definitions.

The element with residue

The first element is defined through the standard formula for an arahant: "a perfected one, with defilements ended, who has completed the spiritual journey, done what had to be done, laid down the burden, achieved their heart's goal, utterly ended the fetter of continued existence, and is rightly freed through enlightenment." The text then adds something specific: "Their five sense faculties still remain. So long as their senses have not gone they continue to experience the agreeable and disagreeable, to feel pleasure and pain." What has changed is not the presence of sense experience itself but what happens inside it — "the ending of greed, hate, and delusion in them is called the element of extinguishment with residue." A perfected one, on this description, still sees, hears, and feels; greed, hate, and delusion simply no longer arise in response.

The element with no residue

The second element is introduced with the identical arahant-formula, then defined in a single, spare sentence: "For them, everything that's felt, being no longer relished, will become cool right here. This is called the element of extinguishment with no residue." The text does not itself elaborate on timing or location beyond the phrase "right here" (idheva). Tradition has long connected this second element to what happens at the death of an arahant, when the aggregates that supported the first kind of experience cease entirely — and the verse below supports that reading by calling it "what pertains to what follows this life." But it is worth being precise about what the sutta itself states and what interpretation supplies: the text's own wording is brief and restrained, and this guide aims to stay close to it rather than build further past what it actually says.

The verses, restated

The closing verses restate the same distinction in slightly different terms, without adding a positive further description. The element "with residue" is called what "pertains to this life... though the leash to existence has ended"; the element "with no residue" is called what "pertains to what follows this life, where all states of existence cease." The final lines describe those who have understood "the unconditioned state" as having minds freed, the leash to existence ended, and as having "given up all states of existence" — language that, again, describes an ending rather than characterizing some further condition beyond it.

Key terms

nibbānadhātu — "element of extinguishment": the discourse's own term for each of the two states it distinguishes.
sa-upādisesa — "with residue": the element in which the five senses still function and still register pleasant and unpleasant, while greed, hate, and delusion have ended.
anupādisesa — "with no residue": the element described only as what remains once everything felt, no longer relished, "becomes cool."
rāgakkhaya, dosakkhaya, mohakkhaya — "the ending of greed, hate, and delusion": what specifically defines the element with residue.
sīti (bhavissanti) — "will become cool": the verb the text uses for what happens to felt experience in the element with no residue, rather than any more elaborate description.

The text

The discourse in full: both definitions and the two-verse restatement. Bilara <j> join-markers in the verse lines have been removed for clean reading. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“There are, mendicants, these two elements of extinguishment. What two? The element of extinguishment with residue, and the element of extinguishment with no residue.

§3And what is the element of extinguishment with residue? It’s when a mendicant is a perfected one, with defilements ended, who has completed the spiritual journey, done what had to be done, laid down the burden, achieved their heart’s goal, utterly ended the fetter of continued existence, and is rightly freed through enlightenment. Their five sense faculties still remain. So long as their senses have not gone they continue to experience the agreeable and disagreeable, to feel pleasure and pain. The ending of greed, hate, and delusion in them is called the element of extinguishment with residue.

§4And what is the element of extinguishment with no residue? It’s when a mendicant is a perfected one, with defilements ended, who has completed the spiritual journey, done what had to be done, laid down the burden, achieved their heart’s goal, utterly ended the fetter of continued existence, and is rightly freed through enlightenment. For them, everything that’s felt, being no longer relished, will become cool right here. This is called the element of extinguishment with no residue. These are the two elements of extinguishment.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§5“These two elements of extinguishment have been explained by the Clear-eyed One, the unattached, the unaffected. One element pertains to this life—that with residue though the leash to existence has ended; and that with no residue, which pertains to what follows this life, where all states of existence cease.

§6Those who have fully understood the unconditioned state—their minds freed, the leash to existence ended—attained to the heart of the Dhamma, they delight in ending, the unaffected ones have given up all states of existence.”

§7This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

Itivuttaka · Discourse 22

The Benefits of Love

Mettasutta

Collection

Setting
No location or interlocutor is given beyond “mendicants” — but unusually for this collection, the body of the teaching is autobiographical
Form
The Buddha recounts his own past lives in the first person, then closes with a short verse restating the point
Length
~2 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — simple in its claim, but dense with cosmological detail

Why this discourse, twenty-second

Most of the Itivuttaka’s short teachings are impersonal statements: “mendicants, there are these two things…” This one is different. Here the Buddha steps out of the third person and tells mendicants about his own past — eons spent as a radiant brahmā, as Sakka lord of gods, as a wheel-turning monarch — and then asks, almost as if surprised by his own good fortune, what deed of his could possibly account for it. The answer he gives himself grounds an entire cosmology of long, exceptional rebirths in three ordinary-sounding practices, one of which is simply developing a mind of love.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Buddha recalls, from his own experience, that developing a mind of love for seven years kept him from returning to this world for seven full eons of cosmic contraction and expansion, during which he was reborn as Great Brahmā, as Sakka lord of gods thirty-six times, and as a wheel-turning monarch many hundreds of times — and when he asks himself what deed produced this, he answers: giving, self-control, and restraint.

“Don’t fear good deeds”

The discourse opens with an unusual imperative: mendicants should not be afraid of puñña, good or meritorious deeds. It is worth pausing on why this needs saying at all. Elsewhere the tradition warns constantly against clinging, craving, and the pursuit of pleasant results — so an instruction not to fear the accumulation of good karma can sound, out of context, like an invitation to attachment. The text heads this off directly: “good deeds” is simply “a term for happiness, for what is likable, desirable, and agreeable.” The Buddha is not asking anyone to crave results; he is correcting an opposite, equally mistaken worry — that ethical and generous action is somehow suspect or dangerous. It plainly is not.

An unusually personal recollection

Almost none of the Itivuttaka’s 112 discourses contain a narrative frame, and this one has no interlocutor or setting either — but it is still, in its content, one of the collection’s most vividly autobiographical passages. The Buddha recounts, in the first person, a specific practice: developing a mind of love (mettacittaṃ) for seven years. The stated result is startling in scale — not returning to this world for “seven eons of the cosmos contracting and expanding.” As each eon contracted, he says, he went to “the realm of streaming radiance”; as it expanded, he was reborn in “an empty mansion of divinity,” becoming Great Brahmā: “the vanquisher, the unvanquished, the universal seer, the wielder of power.” He then adds, almost as an aside, that within those same eons he was also Sakka, lord of gods, thirty-six times, and a wheel-turning monarch — a “just and principled king” ruling to all four quarters and possessing the seven treasures — “many hundreds of times,” not to mention lesser regional kingship, which he waves off as too minor to enumerate.

Asking the question himself

What makes the passage distinctive is the Buddha turning the question on himself: “Of what deed of mine is this the fruit and result, that I am now so mighty and powerful?” The text does not let the extraordinary cosmology stand as spectacle; it insists on tracing it back to cause. His own answer names three things: dāna (giving), dama (self-control or self-mastery), and saññama (restraint). The mettā practice described just above is folded into this frame — developing a mind of love is itself a form of the self-control and restraint being credited, alongside outright generosity.

The verse: three things, not one

The closing verse restates the three causes slightly differently, in terms a listener might find easier to practice directly: “giving and moral conduct, developing a mind of love.” This is worth noting precisely, since the discourse’s title emphasizes love specifically, yet the text itself credits three factors jointly, not love alone. What earns love its place in the title, most likely, is the vividness of its illustration — seven years of practice mapped directly onto seven full eons of exceptional rebirth is the passage’s most striking single detail, even though generosity and ethical conduct are named as co-equal causes throughout.

Not a promise, a recollection

It is also worth being precise about the kind of claim being made. This is not a general assurance that anyone who practices love for seven years will become Great Brahmā; it is the Buddha recalling, from his own direct memory of past lives, one specific case — his own. The scale of the account (thirty-six times as Sakka, many hundreds of times as a wheel-turning monarch) functions less as a formula to replicate and more as testimony to how far-reaching these three ordinary practices can be, coming from someone claiming to have seen the results firsthand across an almost unimaginable span of cosmic time.

Key terms

mettacitta — “a mind of love”: the specific practice the Buddha says he developed for seven years, credited with seven eons of exceptional rebirth.
dāna, dama, saññama — “giving, self-control, restraint”: the three deeds the Buddha names, in the prose, as the cause of his power; the verse restates them as giving, moral conduct, and developing a mind of love.
ābhassara — “streaming radiance”: the realm the Buddha says he went to as each eon contracted, a recurring feature of early Buddhist cosmology.
suññaṃ brahmavimānaṃ — “an empty mansion of divinity”: the unoccupied Brahmā palace he is reborn into as each eon expands, becoming Mahābrahmā.
cakkavatti — “wheel-turning monarch”: the ideal righteous universal ruler, a role the Buddha says he held “many hundreds of times,” complete with the seven treasures and dominion over all four quarters.

The text

The discourse in full: the Buddha’s recollection of his own past lives and the deeds behind them, and the closing verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, don’t fear good deeds. For ‘good deeds’ is a term for happiness, for what is likable, desirable, and agreeable. I recall undergoing for a long time the likable, desirable, and agreeable results of good deeds performed over a long time. Having developed a mind of love for seven years, for seven eons of the cosmos contracting and expanding I didn’t return to this world again. As the eon contracted I went to the realm of streaming radiance. As it expanded I was reborn in an empty mansion of divinity.

§3There I was the Divinity, the Great Divinity, the vanquisher, the unvanquished, the universal seer, the wielder of power. I was Sakka, lord of gods, thirty-six times. Many hundreds of times I was a king, a wheel-turning monarch, a just and principled king. My dominion extended to all four sides, I achieved stability in the country, and I possessed the seven treasures. Not to mention regional kingship!

§4Then I thought, ‘Of what deed of mine is this the fruit and result, that I am now so mighty and powerful?’ Then I thought, ‘It is the fruit and result of three kinds of deeds: giving, self-control, and restraint.’”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§5“One should practice only good deeds,
whose happy outcome stretches ahead.
Giving and moral conduct,
developing a mind of love:

§6having developed these
three things yielding happiness,
that astute one is reborn
in a happy, pleasing world.”

§7This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

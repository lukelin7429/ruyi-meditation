Why this discourse invites a second look

On its surface, Iti 3 is the third in a run of near-identical teachings: give up delusion, the Buddha says, and non-return is guaranteed — exactly the promise made for greed and hate in the two suttas before it. But delusion occupies a different place than greed or hate in how the tradition elsewhere describes the stages of awakening, and a careful reader will notice the tension rather than smooth past it. This reading guide raises that tension honestly, as a genuine question the text leaves open, rather than resolving it with false certainty.

Reading guide

The same formula, a third time

Word for word, this discourse follows Iti 1 and Iti 2 exactly, substituting “delusion” for “greed” and “hate.” The prose statement, the guarantee of non-return, the six-line verse — all repeat the pattern precisely. Delusion (moha) is the third of the traditional three unwholesome roots, so its inclusion here completes a natural set: greed, hate, delusion, treated one after another with identical treatment.

A genuine tension worth naming

Elsewhere in the canon, the standard model of the ten fetters binding beings to rebirth holds that full delusion — ignorance itself, the deepest root of not seeing things as they are — is the very last fetter to fall away, cut only at the point of complete awakening (arahantship), not at the earlier stage of non-return. Yet here, exactly as with greed and hate before it, giving up “delusion” is paired with the same reward: non-return, not full liberation. Three suttas in a row promising an identical result for three factors that, elsewhere, are said to drop away at different points along the path is a real feature of the text worth sitting with, not explaining away.

How might this be held?

Nothing here should be read as claiming a settled answer. One honest possibility is that “delusion” in this terse, formulaic setting names something more targeted — the degree of not-understanding that still ties a mind to the sense-desire realm specifically — rather than every trace of ignorance in its most subtle, technical sense. Another honest possibility is simply that the Itivuttaka’s compact, matched-formula style is not attempting fine-grained technical precision about fetter theory at all; its purpose may be closer to a memorable statement of principle than a rigorous map of stages. Both readings are reasonable. What matters for a careful reader is noticing the tension exists, rather than assuming the text has resolved it for you.

Why this matters for practice

Whatever the correct technical reading, the practical instruction is the same as in Iti 1 and Iti 2: delusion, like greed and hate, is something to be rightly understood and then let go of — not indulged, not merely managed, but investigated closely enough that letting go becomes possible.

Key terms

moha — “delusion”: confusion, not-seeing, failure to understand things as they are; the third of the three unwholesome roots, after greed and hate
avijjā — “ignorance”: a closely related term used elsewhere for the fetter said to be cut only at full awakening — raising the tension this reading guide discusses
dasa saṃyojanā — “the ten fetters”: the traditional list of bonds cut in stages across the path to awakening, the framework against which this sutta’s promise is worth comparing
anāgāmitā — “non-return”: the guarantee held out here for giving up delusion, the same reward promised for greed and hate
pajānanti — “they rightly understand”: the verse’s insistence that giving up delusion, like greed, follows from genuinely seeing it, not simply suppressing it

The text

The discourse in full: the opening frame, the Buddha’s one-sentence teaching, the verse that restates it, and the closing frame. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The teaching

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, give up one thing and I guarantee you non-return. What one thing? Delusion is the one thing. Give it up, and I guarantee you non-return.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

The verse

§3“When overcome by delusion
beings go to a bad place.
Having rightly understood that delusion,
the discerning give it up.
Once they’ve given it up,
they never return to this world.”

§4This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

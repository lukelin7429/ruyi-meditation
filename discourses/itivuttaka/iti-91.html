Why this discourse, ninety-first

Living on alms is called “the last of lifestyles” here — the plainest, most exposed livelihood a person can take up, one the world itself curses. But taking it up is not the same as fulfilling it. This discourse draws that distinction with a single unsparing image: a mendicant who still covets sensual pleasure and harbors ill will, despite the robe, is like a firebrand pulled from a funeral pyre — burning at both ends, fouled with dung in the middle, useless as timber anywhere at all.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Living on alms is called “the last of lifestyles” — a station beneath which there is nowhere left to fall — and going forth into it only accomplishes anything if the person genuinely releases sensual craving and ill will along with lay life; a mendicant who keeps the robe but not the renunciation is like a funeral firebrand, too fouled for any ordinary use.

No story, only a warning

Nearly every discourse in the Itivuttaka forgoes the machinery familiar from the longer collections — no location is named, no listener is introduced, no question prompts the teaching. This one is typical of the form: it opens with the Buddha addressing “mendicants” in the abstract, and it closes the same way. Whatever occasion prompted it, if there was one, has not survived in this text; only the saying has. Readers used to the Majjhima or Digha Nikāyas, with their named towns and named interlocutors, should expect something plainer here — a saying meant to stand on its own.

The last of lifestyles

“This is the last of lifestyles, mendicants, namely relying on alms,” the discourse opens. “The world curses you: ‘You beggar, walking bowl in hand!’” There is no attempt to soften this. Alms-mendicancy is presented as the most exposed, least dignified livelihood available — and precisely because of that, the discourse insists, “earnest gentlemen take it up for a good reason.”

Right reasons and wrong reasons for going forth

The text is unusually specific about which motives don’t count: not to escape a summons by a king, not to escape a summons for a bandit, not because of debt, not out of fear, and not simply “in order to make a living.” The genuine motive, instead, is this: “I’m swamped by rebirth, old age, and death; by sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress. I’m swamped by suffering, mired in suffering. Hopefully I can make an end to this entire mass of suffering.” Only that reason, the discourse implies, actually justifies taking up “the last of lifestyles.”

The firebrand no one can use

The discourse’s central image describes someone who has gone forth for the right reason but hasn’t followed through: they “covet sensual pleasures,” are “infatuated, full of ill will and malicious intent,” unmindful, with “straying minds and undisciplined faculties.” Such a person, the text says, is “like a firebrand for lighting a funeral pyre, burning at both ends, and smeared with dung in the middle” — unusable as timber whether in the village (that is, for lay purposes) or in the wilderness (for ascetic purposes). They’ve “missed out on the pleasures of the lay life,” the discourse concludes, “and haven’t fulfilled the goal of the ascetic life” — caught, in other words, between two worlds and serving neither.

Three verses, not one

Where many Itivuttaka discourses restate their point in a single closing quatrain, this one adds two further stanzas, extending rather than merely repeating the warning: one on wearers of the ocher cloth who remain “unrestrained and wicked” and are, on that account, “reborn in hell”; and a closing image — that it would be better for an immoral, unrestrained person to swallow a scorching iron ball than to eat alms food they have not, in the discourse’s terms, earned through genuine practice. It is worth being honest that this is a harsher and more extended close than the collection’s usual single-verse restatement, and it reads as a deliberate escalation rather than a gentler summary.

Key terms

piṇḍapāta — living on alms, called here “the last of lifestyles”: the most stripped-down of all livelihoods available to a person.
wrong reasons for going forth — escaping a king’s or bandit’s summons, debt, fear, or simply the need to make a living — each explicitly rejected here as a valid motive.
the firebrand simile — a stick used for lighting a cremation pyre, burning at both ends and fouled with dung in the middle; useless as timber anywhere, in village or wilderness alike.
the ocher cloth — the dyed robe of the renunciate, worn in the added verses by some described as “unrestrained and wicked” despite it.
the iron ball — swallowing a red-hot iron ball, offered in the closing verse as preferable to living immorally off alms one hasn’t earned through practice.

The text

The discourse in full: the prose statement, and all three verse stanzas that follow it. No location, speaker introduction, or listener is given in the source. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1.1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2.1–2.7“This is the last of lifestyles, mendicants, namely relying on alms. The world curses you: ‘You beggar, walking bowl in hand!’ Yet earnest gentlemen take it up for a good reason. Not to escape a summons by a king or a summons for a bandit, or because you were in debt or in fear, or in order to make a living. Rather, because they think: ‘I’m swamped by rebirth, old age, and death; by sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress. I’m swamped by suffering, mired in suffering. Hopefully I can make an end to this entire mass of suffering.’ That’s how this gentleman has gone forth.

§2.8–2.10Yet they covet sensual pleasures; they’re infatuated, full of ill will and malicious intent. They are unmindful, lacking situational awareness and immersion, with straying minds and undisciplined faculties. Suppose there was a firebrand for lighting a funeral pyre, burning at both ends, and smeared with dung in the middle. It couldn’t be used as timber either in the village or the wilderness. I say that individual is just like this. They’ve missed out on the pleasures of the lay life, and haven’t fulfilled the goal of the ascetic life.”

§2.11–2.12The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3.1–3.4“They’ve left behind the pleasures of the lay life,
and miss out on the goal of the ascetic life.
Ruining it, they throw it away,
and perish like a funeral firebrand.

§4.1–4.4Many who wear a scrap of ocher cloth
are unrestrained and wicked.
Being wicked, they are reborn in hell
due to their bad deeds.

§5.1–5.4It’d be better for the immoral and unrestrained
to eat an iron ball,
scorching, like a burning flame,
than to eat the nation’s alms.”

§6.1This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

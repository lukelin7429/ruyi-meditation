Bright Things

Sukkadhammasutta

Position
15th of 22 discourses in the Book of the Twos
Form

Length
About 1 minute to read
Difficulty

Why this discourse, forty-second

Two unglamorous inner qualities — conscience and prudence — are credited here with holding ordinary social life together at all. Strip them away, this discourse claims, and the very distinctions that structure a household or a community collapse; keep them, and not only does the world stay recognizable, but the same two qualities are said to carry a mendicant all the way to the end of rebirth.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Conscience and prudence are named as the two "bright things" that protect the world — without them, the text says, the ordinary distinctions of family and respect would collapse into a kind of moral indistinction, like animals; with them, both a functioning society and, in the verse, one's own progress toward the end of birth and death are made possible.

No narrative frame

As with almost every Itivuttaka discourse, no place, audience, or occasion is given. The text moves directly from the opening formula, "This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard," into the teaching itself.

Two bright things that protect the world

"These two bright things, mendicants, protect the world. What two? Conscience and prudence." Hirī (conscience) and ottappa (prudence) are a standing pair throughout the early texts — hirī usually glossed as an inner sense of shame at wrongdoing rooted in self-respect, ottappa as an outward-facing wariness of wrongdoing rooted in concern for its consequences and for others. Here they are given a strikingly broad job: not merely personal virtues, but the two things that keep "the world" itself functioning as a recognizable moral order.

What would be lost without them

The text is concrete about the stakes: "If these two bright things did not protect the world, there would be no recognition of the status of mother, aunts, or wives and partners of tutors and respected people." Without conscience and prudence, in other words, the very distinctions that structure family and household life — who may not be treated as a sexual partner, who commands particular respect — would simply cease to be recognized. "The world would become dissolute, like goats and sheep, chickens and pigs, and hounds and jackals" — a blunt image of undifferentiated animal life, offered not as a claim about animals themselves but as a picture of what human society would collapse into without these two restraining qualities.

Bright roots, lost or established

The verse restates the same claim as a contrast of outcomes. Those in whom conscience and prudence "are never found at all" have "lost their bright roots" and simply "fare on in birth and death" — carried along by the ordinary momentum of the round, rather than making any progress out of it. Those in whom the two "are always rightly established," by contrast, "thrive in the spiritual life" and, "being at peace," will not be reborn again. The move from the first half of the discourse to the second is worth noticing: conscience and prudence begin as the glue of ordinary social recognition, and end as qualities the verse credits with ending rebirth altogether for the person who keeps them "rightly established."

Key terms

hirī — conscience: an inner sense of shame at wrongdoing, rooted in self-respect; the first of the two "bright things."
ottappa — prudence: an outward-facing wariness of wrongdoing, rooted in concern for its consequences; the second "bright thing," always paired with hirī.
sukkā dhammā — "bright things" or "bright qualities": the discourse's own name for conscience and prudence, taken together.
sukkamūla — "bright root": what the verse says is lost by someone in whom conscience and prudence are never found.
"like goats and sheep…" — the discourse's image for a world without conscience or prudence: not a claim about animals, but a picture of undifferentiated social collapse.

The text

The discourse in full: the single prose statement and its two-verse restatement. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“These two bright things, mendicants, protect the world. What two? Conscience and prudence. If these two bright things did not protect the world, there would be no recognition of the status of mother, aunts, or wives and partners of tutors and respected people. The world would become dissolute, like goats and sheep, chickens and pigs, and hounds and jackals. But because the two bright things protect the world, there is recognition of the status of mother, aunts, and wives and partners of tutors and respected people.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“Those in whom conscience and prudence are never found at all, have lost their bright roots, and fare on in birth and death.

§4Those in whom conscience and prudence are always rightly established, thrive in the spiritual life; being at peace, they will not be reborn again.”

§5This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

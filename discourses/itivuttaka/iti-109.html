Itivuttaka · Discourse 109

A River

Nadīsotasutta

Setting
No location or time given; the discourse opens directly with the simile itself rather than a fronted "Mendicants,"
Speakers
The Buddha, teaching a simile addressed throughout to the mendicants (named mid-sentence in the Pāli, though not at the very front of the English rendering)
Form
An extended simile, then a term-by-term decoding of nine of its elements, then a closing verse
Length
~2 minutes to read
Position
10th of 13 discourses in the Chapter on the Brahmin’s Offering of the Teaching, the sole chapter of the Book of the Fours (Iti 100–112)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the longest and most structurally involved teaching in this set of twelve

Why this discourse

The most extended simile in this chapter, and the only one the Buddha decodes piece by piece rather than leaving to stand on its own. A person drifts on a river current that feels “nice and pleasant” toward a lake, downstream, thick with waves, whirlpools, and crocodiles. A man with clear eyes, standing on the bank, calls out a warning. Then, one term at a time, the Buddha explains exactly what every element of the scene stands for.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A person drifting on a river current that seems “nice and pleasant” is warned by a clear-eyed man on the bank that a dangerous, crocodile-infested lake lies downstream — and the Buddha then explains that every element of this scene names something specific in the structure of craving and its escape.

A simile, then its key

Unlike most similes in the early texts, which are left for the listener to work out, this one comes with its own glossary. Having told the story — the current, the warning, the swimmer turning to paddle upstream — the Buddha says plainly, “I’ve made up this simile to make a point,” and then supplies the point term by term: the river current is craving; its being “nice and pleasant” is the six interior sense fields (eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind); the downstream lake is the five lower fetters; the danger of waves is anger and distress; the whirlpool is the five kinds of sensual stimulation; the crocodiles and monsters in the lake are, in the text’s own words, “ladies” (mātugāma); paddling “against the stream” is renunciation; the physical effort of hands and feet is rousing energy; and the clear-eyed man on the bank who gives the warning is the Realized One himself — the Buddha.

A pleasant surface, a dangerous end

The simile’s structure carries its own argument: the danger is not visible from inside the current. The person being swept along experiences the ride as “nice and pleasant” — that is precisely what the six senses feel like from the inside — and it takes an outside observer, someone standing apart on solid ground, to see what lies ahead and to name it. Nothing about the current itself signals the coming lake; the warning has to come from elsewhere, and the swimmer’s response — paddling against the very current that feels good — only makes sense once the danger has actually been pointed out.

An honest note on the simile’s language

One detail deserves to be named plainly rather than passed over: the crocodiles and monsters threatening the swimmer in the downstream lake are glossed, in this translation, as “ladies” — mātugāma, literally “womenfolk.” This is a simile addressed to a monastic audience that the tradition assumed, by default, to be male, and the image reflects that specific audience and its specific temptations rather than a general claim about women as such. Read in its own context, it is one item in a list of dangers named for a particular listener, not a stand-alone verdict on half of humanity.

The far shore, named at the end

The closing verse follows the swimmer past the moment of turning back: having given up sensual pleasures “in pain,” aspiring to “the future sanctuary from the yoke,” such a person, “with deep understanding and heart well freed,” experiences “universal liberation.” The verse’s final line gives this person a title that closes the whole simile’s arc: having completed the spiritual journey and “gone to the end of the world,” they are called “one who has gone beyond” — no longer in the current at all.

Key terms

nadīsota — “the river current”: a term, the Buddha says, for craving itself; also the discourse’s own Pāli title.
orambhāgiya-saṁyojana — “the five lower fetters”: what the downstream lake is said to stand for.
nekkhamma — “renunciation”: what paddling “against the stream” is said to stand for.
vīriyārambha — “rousing energy”: what the physical effort of paddling with hands and feet is said to stand for.
mātugāma — literally “womenfolk,” rendered “ladies” here: the term the simile uses for the crocodiles and monsters in the downstream lake, addressed to this collection’s assumed male monastic audience.

The text

The discourse in full, given complete: the simile, the Buddha's own term-by-term decoding of it, and the closing verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Suppose a person was being carried along by a river current that seemed nice and pleasant. If a person with clear eyes saw them, they’d say: ‘My man, even though the river current carrying you along seems nice and pleasant, downstream there is a lake with waves and whirlpools, saltwater crocodiles and monsters. When you reach there it will result in death or deadly pain.’ Then, when they heard what was said, they’d paddle against the stream using their hands and feet.

§3I’ve made up this simile to make a point. And this is the point. ‘River current’ is a term for craving.

§4‘Seeming nice and pleasant’ is a term for the six interior sense fields.

§5‘A downstream lake’ is a term for the five lower fetters.

§6‘Danger of waves’ is a term for anger and distress.

§7‘Whirlpool’ is a term for the five kinds of sensual stimulation.

§8‘Saltwater crocodiles and monsters’ is a term for ladies.

§9‘Against the stream’ is a term for renunciation.

§10‘Paddling with hands and feet’ is a term for rousing energy.

§11‘A person with clear eyes’ is a term for the Realized One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§12“In pain they’d give up sensual pleasures, aspiring to the future sanctuary from the yoke. With deep understanding and heart well freed, they’d experience universal liberation. That knowledge master who has completed the spiritual journey, and gone to the end of the world, is called ‘one who has gone beyond’.”

§13This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

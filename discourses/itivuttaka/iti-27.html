Itivuttaka · Discourse 27

The Meditation on Love

Mettābhāvanāsutta

Collection
Book of the Ones (Ekakanipāta), Chapter Three — Iti 27, the final discourse of this book’s 27
Setting
No location or interlocutor is given — only the Itivuttaka’s stock opening and closing formula
Form
Three parallel similes in prose, each repeating the same refrain, followed by a longer four-part verse
Length
~2 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — repetitive and image-rich, with some Vedic ritual vocabulary

Why this discourse, twenty-seventh — the last of the Book of the Ones

The Book of the Ones closes not with a warning or a summary, but with an escalating series of comparisons, each insisting that a single inner quality — the heart’s release through love — outshines every other form of merit-making so completely that all of it, gathered together, is not worth a sixteenth part. Moonlight against starlight, sunrise against darkness, the morning star at dawn: three different pictures of the same overwhelming claim, before the verse takes direct aim at the era’s most lavish royal sacrifices and finds them wanting too.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Of all the grounds for making worldly merit, none is worth a sixteenth part of the heart’s release by love, which “shines and glows and radiates” the way moonlight outshines starlight, the way an autumn sunrise dispels every trace of darkness, and the way the morning star shines at the crack of dawn.

The last discourse of its book

This is the twenty-seventh and final discourse of the Ekakanipāta, the Book of the Ones — the underlying source text marks it as such, closing the chapter and the book together before moving on to the paired teachings of the Book of the Twos. It is worth noting honestly that this closing, structural fact sits at the level of the collection’s organization rather than being stated within the translated text of the discourse itself. Even so, it is a fitting note to end on: having spent this chapter on single, isolated qualities — a pure mind, diligence, lying, giving — the book’s final entry names the one quality it treats as outshining every other ground for merit combined.

Three similes, one refrain

The prose builds its claim through repetition rather than argument. Three times, an image of light is offered — the moon over the stars, the sun over “all the darkness of space,” the morning star at the crack of dawn — and three times the identical refrain follows: “of all the grounds for making worldly merit, none are worth a sixteenth part of the heart’s release by love. Surpassing them, the heart’s release by love shines and glows and radiates.” This kind of exact, incantatory repetition is common in the more expansive discourses of this collection, and its effect here is cumulative: each simile does not so much add new information as deepen the same single claim.

The verse takes aim at royal sacrifice

The verse does something the prose does not: it names specific, real practices and ranks them explicitly below a mind developed with love. It describes “royal potentates” who conquered the whole land and traveled the country “sponsoring sacrifices” — the horse sacrifice, the human sacrifice, the “casting of the yoke-pin,” the “royal soma drinking,” and the “unimpeded” — a set of grand Vedic royal rites, the human sacrifice among them, that a conquering king of the era might sponsor as the ultimate expression of merit and legitimacy. The verse’s judgment is direct: “these are not worth a sixteenth part of the mind developed with love, as starlight cannot rival the moon.” Naming these rites explicitly, rather than speaking only in generalities about “worldly merit,” makes the comparison concrete and, in the case of human sacrifice, pointedly severe.

The closing line: love as non-violence in practice

The verse’s final quatrain draws out the practical implication directly: “Don’t kill or cause others to kill, don’t conquer or encourage others to conquer, with love for all living creatures — you’ll have no enmity for anyone.” Placed immediately after the catalogue of royal sacrifices and conquest, this line reads as a deliberate contrast: the conquering kings just described built their merit through conquest and, in the case of the human sacrifice, killing; the mind of love this discourse has spent its whole length praising instead refuses killing and conquest altogether. The Book of the Ones ends, in other words, not on a note of cosmic scale for its own sake, but on an explicitly non-violent, universally applied loving-kindness — “mettaṃso sabbabhūtesu,” love extended toward all beings without exception.

An echo of an earlier discourse in the same book

Love also opened one of the more personal moments earlier in this same chapter: Iti 22, where the Buddha traced his own eons of exceptional rebirth partly to seven years of developing a mind of love. Read side by side, the two discourses bookend the theme within a single book — Iti 22 offers testimony from direct experience of what love’s cultivation can produce, and this closing discourse states, as an explicit ranking, just how far that cultivation outstrips every other form of merit-making, however grand.

Key terms

mettā cetovimutti — “the heart’s release, or liberation of mind, through love”: the single quality this discourse ranks above every other ground for merit.
soṃasiṃ kalaṃ — “a sixteenth part”: the discourse’s recurring fraction, repeated three times in the prose and twice more in the verse, measuring how far love outstrips its rivals.
opadhikā puññakiriyavatthūni — “grounds for worldly (attachment-based) merit-making”: the whole category of ordinary meritorious acts that love is said to surpass.
assamedha, purisamedha — “the horse sacrifice, the human sacrifice”: two of the grand Vedic royal rites the verse names explicitly and ranks below a mind developed with love.
Ekakanipāta — “the Book of the Ones”: the section of the Itivuttaka this discourse closes, the collection’s first of four books.

The text

The discourse in full: the three parallel similes and the closing verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral). The bilara source marks a join between two verse segments (rendered here simply as continuous text) in the sacrifice list.

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, of all the grounds for making worldly merit, none are worth a sixteenth part of the heart’s release by love. Surpassing them, the heart’s release by love shines and glows and radiates.

§3It’s like how the radiance of all the stars is not worth a sixteenth part of the moon’s radiance. Surpassing them, the moon’s radiance shines and glows and radiates. In the same way, of all the grounds for making worldly merit, none are worth a sixteenth part of the heart’s release by love. Surpassing them, the heart’s release by love shines and glows and radiates.

§4It’s like the last month of the rainy season, in autumn, when the heavens are sunny and cloudless. And as the sun is rising to the firmament, having dispelled all the darkness of space, it shines and glows and radiates. In the same way, of all the grounds for making worldly merit, none are worth a sixteenth part of the heart’s release by love. Surpassing them, the heart’s release by love shines and glows and radiates.

§5It’s like how, at the crack of dawn, the Morning Star shines and glows and radiates. In the same way, of all the grounds for making worldly merit, none are worth a sixteenth part of the heart’s release by love. Surpassing them, the heart’s release by love shines and glows and radiates.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§6“A mindful one who develops
limitless love
weakens the fetters,
seeing the ending of attachments.

§7Loving just one creature with a hateless heart
makes you a good person.
Sympathetic for all creatures,
a noble one creates abundant merit.

§8The royal potentates conquered this land
and traveled around sponsoring sacrifices—
horse sacrifice, human sacrifice,
the sacrifices of the ‘casting of the yoke-pin’, the ‘royal soma drinking’, and the ‘unimpeded’.

§9These are not worth a sixteenth part
of the mind developed with love,
as starlight cannot rival the moon.

§10Don’t kill or cause others to kill,
don’t conquer or encourage others to conquer,
with love for all living creatures—
you’ll have no enmity for anyone.”

§11This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

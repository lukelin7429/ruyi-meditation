Itivuttaka · Book of the Threes, Chapter Two · Iti 67

Sagacity

Moneyyasutta

Setting
None given — no place, audience beyond “mendicants,” or occasion, in keeping with the Itivuttaka’s spare form throughout
Speakers
The Buddha alone; the Itivuttaka’s bipartite form never includes an interlocutor
Form
A one-line list of three items, restated in a single verse stanza closely echoing the previous discourse
Length
Under a minute to read the passage itself
Parallels
No complete Chinese Itivuttaka survives; a partial parallel collection exists in Tibetan translation, with scattered individual parallels across the Āgamas, none specifically identified for this short discourse
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — brief and plain, best read alongside its close companion

Why this discourse

Moneyya, “sagacity” or sagehood, comes from the same root as muni, “sage” — the word behind the Buddha’s own epithet Sakyamuni, “sage of the Sakyans.” This discourse names three kinds of sagacity, by body, speech, and mind, in a structure and even a verse-shape almost identical to the purity discourse just before it. The two together read less like separate teachings than two related words — purity and sagehood — applied to the very same threefold pattern.

Reading guide

A sage’s three doors

“There are these three kinds of sagacity,” the prose says: sagacity of body, speech, and mind (kāyamoneyya, vacīmoneyya, manomoneyya). Moneyya is built from muni, “sage” — the same word used of solitary, silent forest renunciants throughout the canon, and later applied to the Buddha himself as an epithet. Sagacity here is not simply cleverness; it names the settled, restrained quality traditionally associated with such a figure.

Nearly the same verse as its neighbor

The verse’s shape closely tracks the previous discourse’s: “a sage in body, a sage in speech, a sage undefiled in mind; a sage, blessed with sagacity, has washed off all bad things, they say.” “Washed off all bad things” (ninhātapāpaka) stands in almost the same position, and means almost the same thing, as Purity’s closing “has given up everything” — two related images (washing away impurity; giving up entirely) for what is, in substance, a single accomplishment.

Purity and sagacity, read as a pair

Set beside Purity, this discourse looks less like an independent teaching and more like the same idea named twice, once through the image of cleanliness (soceyya) and once through the image of the settled sage (moneyya). Both use the identical body-speech-mind structure, both keep their verse to a single stanza, and both close without mentioning heaven, hell, or any future rebirth — a clear departure from the two conduct-discourses that opened this short run.

The form itself

As throughout this collection, Sagacity opens with “This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard,” states its teaching in prose, restates it in a single verse stanza, and closes with a near-mirror of the opening line. No place, occasion, or listener is named beyond “mendicants” — nothing narrative frames it at all.

Key terms

moneyya — “sagacity” or sagehood, built from muni (sage); of three kinds: body, speech, and mind.
muni — “sage”: a settled, restrained figure, later applied to the Buddha as an epithet (as in Sakyamuni).
kāya-, vacī-, manomoneyya — sagacity “of body,” “of speech,” and “of mind” respectively.
ninhātapāpaka — “has washed off all bad things”: the verse’s closing image, closely paralleling “has given up everything” in the previous discourse.
manomoneyya — “sagacity of mind”: called “undefiled” (anāsava) in the verse, echoing the previous discourse’s language for purity of heart.

The text

The discourse in full — a one-line prose list of three sagacities, followed by a single verse stanza closely echoing the previous discourse’s shape. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

The teaching

§2“Mendicants, there are these three kinds of sagacity. What three? Sagacity of body, speech, and mind. These are the three kinds of sagacity.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

The verse

§3“A sage in body, a sage in speech, a sage undefiled in mind; a sage, blessed with sagacity, has washed off all bad things, they say.”

§4This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

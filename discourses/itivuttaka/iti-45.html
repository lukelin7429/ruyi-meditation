Retreat

Paṭisallānasutta

Form

Difficulty

Why this discourse, forty-fifth

A short, direct summons to solitude: enjoy retreat, love retreat, and pair it with inner serenity, meditative absorption, and discernment. This discourse promises, for the mendicant who lives this way, one of exactly two results — a formula that recurs, word for word, across several discourses in this part of the book.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A mendicant who enjoys retreat, stays committed to inner serenity of the heart, does not neglect absorption, is endowed with discernment, and frequents empty huts can expect one of two results: enlightenment in this very life, or, if some residue remains, non-return.

No narrative frame

As with almost every discourse in this collection, no place, audience, or occasion is given; the teaching opens directly with the Itivuttaka's formula.

Love retreat, but not alone

The opening line is a direct address: "Enjoy retreat, mendicants, love retreat." What follows immediately qualifies what that solitude is for: "Be committed to inner serenity of the heart, don't neglect absorption, be endowed with discernment, and frequent empty huts." Retreat (paṭisallāna) here is not solitude for its own sake, but the setting in which serenity (samatha), meditative absorption (jhāna), and discernment (vipassanā) are meant to be cultivated together — four elements named in a single breath rather than as separate options.

The "two results" formula

The promise attached to this practice uses a formula that recurs, almost word for word, in the next two discourses of this book (Iti 46 and Iti 47): "A mendicant who enjoys retreat can expect one of two results: enlightenment in this very life, or if there's residue left behind, non-return." The two outcomes named are the two highest of the traditional four stages of awakening — full enlightenment (arahantship) in this very life, or, short of that, non-return (anāgāmitā), meaning no further rebirth into the sensual realm. The formula does not promise a lesser result short of these two; it names only the top two rungs as what can be expected from this practice.

The verses: peace, discernment, and diligence

The first verse describes the resulting state of mind directly: "With minds at peace, alert, mindful and meditative, they rightly discern the Dhamma, unconcerned for sensual pleasures." The second verse turns to what protects that state: "Delighting in diligence, peaceful, seeing fear in negligence, such a one can't decline, and has drawn near to extinguishment." The verse's own logic is worth noting — it is not retreat alone, but retreat joined with diligence and a clear-eyed "fear" of negligence, that the text credits with making decline impossible and extinguishment close at hand.

Key terms

paṭisallāna — retreat or seclusion: the discourse's opening summons, paired here with serenity, absorption, and discernment rather than solitude alone.
cetosamatha — serenity or calm of the heart: the first of the four qualities named alongside retreat.
jhāna — absorption: the meditative state the text says should not be neglected.
vipassanā — discernment or insight: the analytical counterpart to serenity, named as the third quality.
anāgāmitā — non-return: the second of the "two results," meaning no further rebirth into the sensual realm, named alongside full enlightenment in this very life.

The text

The discourse in full: the exhortation, the two-results formula, and both verses. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Enjoy retreat, mendicants, love retreat. Be committed to inner serenity of the heart, don’t neglect absorption, be endowed with discernment, and frequent empty huts. A mendicant who enjoys retreat can expect one of two results: enlightenment in this very life, or if there’s residue left behind, non-return.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“With minds at peace, alert, mindful and meditative, they rightly discern the Dhamma, unconcerned for sensual pleasures.

§4Delighting in diligence, peaceful, seeing fear in negligence, such a one can’t decline, and has drawn near to extinguishment.”

§5This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

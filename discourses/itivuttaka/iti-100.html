The Brahmin’s Offering of the Teaching

Why this discourse, one-hundredth

This discourse opens an entirely new book of the Itivuttaka. The Buddha calls himself a “brahmin” in a wholly re-defined sense — devoted to charity, bearing his final body — and describes his disciples as his true, mouth-born children, before repeating and extending the familiar comparison that giving the teaching always outranks giving material things.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Buddha calls himself a “brahmin” in an entirely re-defined, non-hereditary sense — one devoted wholly to charity, bearing his final body — and describes his disciples as his true, mouth-born children, heirs to the teaching rather than to property, before repeating (and for the first time extending to four) the recurring comparison that giving, sharing, supporting, or offering the teaching always outranks doing the same with material things.

Opening the next book

It’s worth marking plainly: this discourse is the very first of The Book of the Fours, immediately following the close of The Book of the Threes at Iti 99 — a genuine book boundary within the Itivuttaka. Per its own source heading, the chapter (vagga) this discourse opens is titled “The Chapter on the Brahmin’s Offering of the Teaching,” taking its name directly from this very discourse.

“I am a brahmin”

The Buddha describes himself with this redefined term at the outset: “I, mendicants, am a brahmin, committed to charity, always open-handed, bearing my final body, a healer, a surgeon.” It is a striking image, folding generosity, finality (no further rebirth ahead), and something like a physician’s care into a single self-description.

“Born from my mouth”

He describes his disciples in equally striking terms: “you are my true-born sons, born from my mouth, born of the teaching, created by the teaching, heirs in the teaching, not in things of the flesh.” This is worth pausing on: almost identical language — “born from his mouth,” “heir to principle” — appears elsewhere in the canon in a much longer narrative, where the Buddha answers two former brahmins who’d been insulted by their own caste-fellows for abandoning a claim to be literally “born from the Divinity’s mouth.” There, the Buddha offers genuine spiritual descent through realization as the only claim that actually holds up. Here, that same image is used directly and without the surrounding story, describing his own disciples as such heirs.

Four comparisons, not three

The discourse then repeats a threefold comparison already familiar from Iti 98, the last discourse of the previous book — gift, sharing, and support, each contrasting “things of the flesh” against “the teaching,” with the teaching always ranked better — but adds a fourth: “there are these two offerings. An offering of things of the flesh and an offering of the teaching. The better of these two offerings is an offering of the teaching.” It’s a small but real difference worth noticing between the two discourses: the same structure, extended by one.

The closing verse

The discourse ends by returning to compassion rather than comparison: “the Realized One, compassionate for all living creatures, unstintingly offers teaching. Sentient beings revere him, first among gods and humans, who has gone beyond rebirth.”

Key terms

“brahmin,” redefined — here the Buddha applies the term to himself, based on charity and final liberation rather than birth or lineage.
“born from my mouth” — the discourse’s description of true disciples as the Buddha’s spiritual children; almost identical language appears elsewhere, in the Buddha’s answer to two brahmins insulted for abandoning a literal claim to birth from a Divinity’s mouth.
the four comparisons — gift, sharing, support, and (newly, here) offering, each contrasting “things of the flesh” with “the teaching.”
“a healer, a surgeon” — part of the Buddha’s self-description here, alongside being “committed to charity” and “bearing my final body.”
“my final body” — the traditional description of an arahant’s or Buddha’s last physical existence, with no further rebirth to follow.

The text

The discourse in full: the Buddha's self-description, the fourfold comparison, and the closing verse. No location or listener is named in the source. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1.1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2.1–2.2“I, mendicants, am a brahmin, committed to charity, always open-handed, bearing my final body, a healer, a surgeon. You are my true-born sons, born from my mouth, born of the teaching, created by the teaching, heirs in the teaching, not in things of the flesh.

§3.1–3.3There are these two gifts. A gift of things of the flesh and a gift of the teaching. The better of these two gifts is the gift of the teaching.

§4.1–4.3There are these two kinds of sharing. Sharing things of the flesh and sharing the teaching. The better of these two kinds of sharing is sharing the teaching.

§5.1–5.3There are these two kinds of support. Support in things of the flesh and support in the teaching. The better of these two kinds of support is support in the teaching.

§6.1–6.3There are these two offerings. An offering of things of the flesh and an offering of the teaching. The better of these two offerings is an offering of the teaching.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§7.1–7.4“The Realized One, compassionate for all living creatures,
unstintingly offers teaching.
Sentient beings revere him, first among gods and humans,
who has gone beyond rebirth.”

§8.1This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

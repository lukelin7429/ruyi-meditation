Itivuttaka · The Book of the Ones · Chapter Two

Harmony in the Saṅgha

The Book of the Ones, Chapter Two

Audience
Mendicants in general — no named individual, no location, no occasion given
Form
A single prose declaration followed by a six-line verse restating the same point
Length
Under a minute to read
Pairing
Second of a matched contrast with Iti 18 — that discourse named schism as harmful to gods and humans; this one names harmony as beneficial to them
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — brief, and the positive counterpart to the discourse just before it

Why this discourse, nineteenth

Set directly against Iti 18, this discourse takes the identical claim structure and inverts every term: what was harmful becomes beneficial, what undermined confidence now builds it, what ended in hell now ends in heaven. Read on its own, it is a simple, positive statement about the value of a harmonious community. Read beside its neighbor, it completes a deliberate two-part contrast.

Reading guide

A near-perfect mirror

Set this discourse’s opening beside Iti 18’s and the construction is unmistakable: “One thing, mendicants, arises in the world for the welfare and happiness of the people, for the people, for the benefit, welfare, and happiness of gods and humans.” Every term that was negative in Iti 18 — detriment, suffering, harm — has its positive counterpart here: welfare, happiness, benefit. The rhetorical question-and-answer repeats too: “What one thing? Harmony in the Saṅgha.”

What harmony actually looks like

Just as Iti 18 named concrete behaviors of a split community, this discourse names concrete behaviors of a harmonious one, stated as a direct negation of the earlier list: “When the Saṅgha is in harmony, they don’t argue, insult, block, or forsake each other.” The picture given is deliberately unglamorous — not ecstatic unity, simply the absence of the specific breakdowns named in the discourse before it.

Confidence, restored

Where schism failed to inspire confidence in outsiders and caused some existing confidence to waver, harmony does the reverse: “This inspires confidence in those without it, and increases confidence in those who have it.” The effect on the wider world, gods and humans included, is treated as symmetrical with its opposite — a community’s internal state radiates outward either way.

The verse: happiness, support, and heaven

The verse’s language softens where Iti 18’s hardened: “A Saṅgha in harmony is happy, as is support for those in harmony.” Where the schismatic “took a stand against the teaching” and “favored factions,” the one who creates harmony “takes a stand on the teaching” and “favors harmony” — ruining no sanctuary rather than ruining one. The closing couplet inverts Iti 18’s hell directly: “After creating harmony in the Saṅgha, they rejoice in heaven for an eon.”

Reading the pair as one teaching

Neither discourse, alone, states the full picture as clearly as the two together do. Placed back to back, they present a single teaching in two halves: the same community, the same causes (a stand taken, a faction favored or harmony favored), and two entirely opposite outcomes, stated with matching structure and weight.

Key terms

sāmaggi — “harmony” or concord, the subject of this discourse, named as the direct counterpart to schism in Iti 18.
“for the welfare… of gods and humans” — the discourse’s wide scale of benefit, mirroring the wide scale of harm claimed for schism.
“don’t argue, insult, block, or forsake” — the discourse’s description of a harmonious Saṅgha, stated as a direct negation of Iti 18’s list.
“favoring harmony” — the verse’s description of one who creates concord, set against “favoring factions” in Iti 18.
an eon in heaven — the verse’s stated consequence for one who creates harmony, the direct inversion of Iti 18’s eon in hell.

The text

The discourse in full. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“One thing, mendicants, arises in the world for the welfare and happiness of the people, for the people, for the benefit, welfare, and happiness of gods and humans. What one thing? Harmony in the Saṅgha. When the Saṅgha is in harmony, they don’t argue, insult, block, or forsake each other. This inspires confidence in those without it, and increases confidence in those who have it.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“A Saṅgha in harmony is happy,
as is support for those in harmony.
Taking a stand on the teaching,
favoring harmony, they ruin no sanctuary.
After creating harmony in the Saṅgha,
they rejoice in heaven for an eon.”

§4This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

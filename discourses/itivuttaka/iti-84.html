Itivuttaka · The Book of the Threes · Iti 84

For the Welfare of the People

“This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.”

Setting
None given — no place, audience, or occasion is named
Form
A single prose statement identifying three kinds of individuals, followed by a two-stanza verse
Length
~1–2 minutes to read
Book
The Book of the Threes (Iti 60–99), Chapter Four
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a repetitive, formulaic structure that rewards close reading of what's identical versus what differs

Why this discourse, eighty-fourth

It would be easy to assume that only a Buddha teaches with real authority, and that anyone else — however awakened, however advanced — is merely relaying secondhand material. This discourse pointedly resists that hierarchy. It names three kinds of people who "arise in the world for the welfare and happiness of the people": a fully awakened Buddha, an arahant disciple, and even a trainee still working toward full awakening — and then describes all three using the identical phrase, word for word, for what they teach and why it matters.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Three kinds of individuals arise in the world out of sympathy for it, for the welfare and happiness of gods and humans: a fully awakened Buddha, an arahant disciple, and a trainee disciple who is still practicing — and the discourse describes the teaching each one gives in exactly the same words.

Three individuals, one repeated formula

The prose section moves through its three individuals with almost mechanical precision. First comes “a Realized One… a fully awakened Buddha,” given the fullest string of titles — “accomplished in knowledge and conduct, holy, knower of the world, supreme guide for those fit for training, teacher of gods and humans, awakened, blessed.” Second comes a mendicant who is “a perfected one, with defilements ended… rightly freed through enlightenment” — an arahant, someone who has completed the path for themselves. Third comes, more surprisingly, “a disciple of that Teacher” who is merely “a trainee, a learned practitioner with precepts and observances intact” — someone still on the path, not yet fully awakened. What is striking is that after naming each individual, the text describes what they do in identical language: each one “teaches Dhamma that’s good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, meaningful and well-phrased,” and each one “reveals a spiritual practice that’s entirely full and pure.” The sentence naming each as arising “for the welfare and happiness of the people, out of sympathy for the world, for the benefit, welfare, and happiness of gods and humans” is likewise repeated verbatim for all three.

Why include the trainee at all?

Placing an unawakened trainee in the same category as the Buddha and an arahant is a deliberate flattening, not an oversight. The discourse doesn't claim the trainee's realization equals the Buddha's — the earlier string of titles applied only to the Buddha makes that clear enough. What it claims is that the trainee's function, when they teach the Dhamma faithfully and live it with precepts intact, is the same kind of act, arising from the same sympathy, serving the same welfare. This has practical implications for the value the tradition places on ordinary teaching and ordinary practice: a mendicant who has not yet finished the path but nonetheless teaches accurately and keeps their precepts is not merely marking time until someone more qualified arrives — they are already doing the thing this discourse says matters.

The verse: a caravan and its followers

The closing verse arranges the three in sequence — “the Teacher is the first, the great seer, following whom is the evolved disciple, and then a trainee, a practitioner” — and calls them collectively “beacons proclaiming the teaching” who “fling open the door to freedom from death, freeing many from yokes.” The final stanza shifts to address the listener directly: those who follow the path “taught by the unsurpassed caravan leader” and remain diligent “make an end of suffering in this very life.” The caravan-leader image casts the Buddha as the one who has already made the crossing and knows the route, with the arahant and trainee functioning as guides along the same road rather than separate destinations.

A discourse about lineage, not just doctrine

Read as a whole, this sutta is less about the content of any particular teaching and more about the chain of people through whom a teaching keeps reaching new listeners — Buddha to arahant to trainee, each still teaching. It offers a quiet argument for why the tradition can outlast any single teacher: the same sympathy and the same basic activity recur at every stage of the path, not only at its completion.

Key terms

a Realized One (Tathāgata) — the fully awakened Buddha, given the fullest list of titles: accomplished in knowledge and conduct, holy, knower of the world, supreme guide, teacher of gods and humans, awakened, blessed.
a perfected one (arahant) — a mendicant "with defilements ended," who has completed the path, laid down the burden, and is "rightly freed through enlightenment."
a trainee (sekha) — "a learned practitioner with precepts and observances intact," still on the path but included as the third of the three who teach for the world's welfare.
"good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end" — the identical description applied to the teaching given by all three individuals, without distinction.
the unsurpassed caravan leader — the verse's image for the Buddha, whose path, when followed diligently, ends suffering "in this very life."

The text

The discourse in full: the three individuals who arise for the world's welfare, described in matching language, and the closing two-stanza verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“These three individuals, mendicants, arise in the world for the welfare and happiness of the people, out of sympathy for the world, for the benefit, welfare, and happiness of gods and humans. What three?

It’s when a Realized One arises in the world, perfected, a fully awakened Buddha, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, holy, knower of the world, supreme guide for those fit for training, teacher of gods and humans, awakened, blessed. He proclaims a teaching that’s good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, meaningful and well-phrased. And he reveals a spiritual practice that’s entirely full and pure. This is the first individual who arises in the world for the welfare and happiness of the people, out of sympathy for the world, for the benefit, welfare, and happiness of gods and humans.

§3Furthermore, it’s when a mendicant is a perfected one, with defilements ended, who has completed the spiritual journey, done what had to be done, laid down the burden, achieved their heart’s goal, utterly ended the fetter of continued existence, and is rightly freed through enlightenment. They teach Dhamma that’s good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, meaningful and well-phrased. And they reveal a spiritual practice that’s entirely full and pure. This is the second individual who arises in the world for the welfare and happiness of the people, out of sympathy for the world, for the benefit, welfare, and happiness of gods and humans.

§4Furthermore, it’s when a disciple of that Teacher is a trainee, a learned practitioner with precepts and observances intact. They teach Dhamma that’s good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, meaningful and well-phrased. And they reveal a spiritual practice that’s entirely full and pure. This is the third individual who arises in the world for the welfare and happiness of the people, out of sympathy for the world, for the benefit, welfare, and happiness of gods and humans. These are the three individuals who arise in the world for the welfare and happiness of the people, out of sympathy for the world, for the benefit, welfare, and happiness of gods and humans.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§5“The Teacher is the first, the great seer,
following whom is the evolved disciple,
and then a trainee, a practitioner,
learned, with precepts and observances intact.

§6These three are first among gods and humans,
beacons proclaiming the teaching!
They fling open the door to freedom from death,
freeing many from yokes.

§7Following the path so well taught
by the unsurpassed caravan leader,
those who are diligent in the Holy One’s teaching
make an end of suffering in this very life.”

§8This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

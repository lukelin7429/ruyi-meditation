Itivuttaka · Discourse 52

Feelings (1st)

The Book of the Threes, Chapter One

Setting
None given — like almost every Itivuttaka discourse, no place, audience, or occasion is named

Segments
16 (iti52:1.1–5.1)
Length
Under 1 minute to read
Parallel

Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — the simplest form this collection uses: name the three, then apply a standard four-part analysis

Why this discourse, fifty-second

Iti 52 is the first of a pair (with Iti 53 immediately following) built around the most basic classification of feeling in the early texts: pleasant, painful, and neutral. Rather than describing feelings in detail, this discourse does something the Book of the Threes will repeat several times — it takes a threefold list and runs it through a fixed four-part formula: understand the thing, its cause, its ending, and the path to that ending. The same formula returns, word for word, in the discourses on searches (Iti 54) and defilements (Iti 56).

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

There are three feelings — pleasant, painful, and neutral — and a mendicant who understands them, their cause, their ending, and the path to that ending is, in the verse’s words, “hungerless, quenched.”

The simplest possible threefold list

Feeling (vedanā) is analyzed at great length elsewhere in the canon — by bodily versus mental origin, by connection to the senses, by worldly versus spiritual character. This discourse doesn’t attempt any of that. It states the most basic three-part division — pleasant, painful, neutral — and moves immediately to what matters: not classifying feeling further, but seeing it clearly enough to be free of it.

A recurring four-part formula

The verse doesn’t describe feelings directly; it describes a disciple who “understands feelings, the cause of feelings, where they cease, and the path that leads to their ending.” This four-part shape — the thing itself, its cause, its cessation, the path to that cessation — echoes the structure of the four noble truths, applied here to a single topic rather than to suffering as a whole. The Book of the Threes reuses this exact formula, almost word for word, for searches at Iti 54 and for defilements at Iti 56. Seeing the pattern repeat across three different topics is itself part of what this discourse teaches: the analysis is a method, not a one-off observation about feeling.

“Hungerless, quenched”

The verse’s closing description is worth sitting with: not someone who feels nothing, but someone “hungerless” — free of the craving that ordinarily seizes on pleasant feeling and recoils from painful feeling — and “quenched,” the standard image for the extinguishing of the fires of greed, hatred, and delusion. Iti 53, immediately following, gives the more detailed instructions for how each of the three feelings should specifically be regarded in order to arrive at this same quenching.

Key terms

pleasant feeling — the first of the three (sukha vedanā), the feeling ordinarily met with craving and clinging.
painful feeling — the second (dukkha vedanā), ordinarily met with aversion.
neutral feeling — the third, neither painful nor pleasant (adukkhamasukhā vedanā), easiest of the three to overlook.
the four-part formula — understanding a thing, its cause, its cessation, and the path to its cessation; reused for searches (Iti 54) and defilements (Iti 56).
“hungerless, quenched” — the verse’s description of a mendicant who has fully understood feeling this way; the same phrase closes Iti 54 and Iti 56.

The text

The discourse in full — brief prose and a single verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, there are these three feelings. What three? Pleasant, painful, and neutral feeling. These are the three feelings.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“Stilled, aware,
a mindful disciple of the Buddha
understands feelings,
the cause of feelings,

§4where they cease,
and the path that leads to their ending.
With the ending of feelings, a mendicant
is hungerless, quenched.”

§5This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

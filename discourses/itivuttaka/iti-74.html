A Child

Puttasutta

Setting

The Buddha alone; no interlocutor appears
Form

Length
~2 minutes to read

★★☆☆☆ — a plainly stated, symmetrical comparison

Why this discourse, the seventy-fourth

Three kinds of children, sorted not by talent or fortune but by a single measure: has this child gone for refuge and kept the five precepts, relative to their own parents? The discourse runs the same sentence three times with the variables swapped, producing a small, exact grid of possibilities before the verse draws its conclusion.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

There are three kinds of children in the world — one who betters their birth, one who equals it, and one who fails it — and which kind a child is depends entirely on whether that child has gone for refuge to the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha, and kept the five precepts, relative to whether their parents have done the same.

A grid, not a hierarchy of birth

Read carefully, the three cases form a small logical grid. A child “betters their birth” when the parents have not gone for refuge and are “immoral, of bad character,” but the child has and is ethical. A child “equals their birth” when both parents and child have gone for refuge and kept the precepts alike. A child “fails their birth” when the parents have gone for refuge and are ethical, but the child has not and is not. Notice what never appears: a case where neither parents nor child have gone for refuge. The discourse isn’t interested in ranking families by birth or class at all — only in tracking whether ethical and refuge-taking conduct is being carried forward, matched, or dropped from one generation to the next.

The five precepts, named in full each time

All three cases spell out the same five items in identical language: not killing living creatures, not stealing, not committing sexual misconduct, not lying, and not consuming intoxicants. The repetition is deliberate — and typical of how this discourse, like much of the Itivuttaka, prefers to restate a formula in full each time rather than abbreviate it, so that each of the three cases stands as a complete, self-sufficient statement.

“Disgracing their family”

The verse is blunt about which outcome the “astute” actually wish for: a child who betters or at least equals their birth, “not one who fails their birth, disgracing their family.” The final stanza then describes such children in more general terms — “faithful, accomplished in ethics, bountiful, rid of stinginess” — and closes with an image of brightness: “like the moon freed from clouds, they shine in the assemblies.”

Key terms

atijāta — “betters their birth” — a child more ethical and refuge-taking than their own parents.
anujāta — “equals their birth” — a child matching their parents’ refuge and conduct.
avajāta — “fails their birth” — a child less ethical and refuge-taking than their own parents; the discourse’s Pali title, Puttasutta, simply means “on children.”
saraṇa gata — “gone for refuge” — to the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha; the recurring measure applied to both parent and child in all three cases.
sīlavā kalyāṇadhammo — “ethical, of good character” — the discourse’s fixed description for whichever party, parent or child, is keeping the five precepts.

The text

The discourse in full: the three-part comparison between children and their parents, given complete for all three cases since each mirrors the others exactly, followed by the two-stanza verse and the fixed closing line. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“These three kinds of children are found in the world. What three? One who betters their birth, one who equals their birth, one who fails their birth.

§3And how does a child better their birth? It’s when a child’s parents have not gone for refuge to the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha. They kill living creatures, steal, commit sexual misconduct, lie, and consume intoxicants of beer, wine, and liquor. They’re immoral, of bad character. But their child has gone for refuge to the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha. They don’t kill living creatures, steal, commit sexual misconduct, lie, or consume intoxicants of beer, wine, and liquor. They’re ethical, of good character. That’s how a child betters their birth.

§4And how does a child equal their birth? It’s when a child’s parents have gone for refuge to the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha. They don’t kill living creatures, steal, commit sexual misconduct, lie, or consume intoxicants of beer, wine, and liquor. They’re ethical, of good character. And their child has gone for refuge to the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha. They don’t kill living creatures, steal, commit sexual misconduct, lie, or consume intoxicants of beer, wine, and liquor. They’re ethical, of good character. That’s how a child equals their birth.

§5And how does a child fail their birth? It’s when a child’s parents have gone for refuge to the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha. They don’t kill living creatures, steal, commit sexual misconduct, lie, or consume intoxicants of beer, wine, and liquor. They’re ethical, of good character. But their child has not gone for refuge to the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha. They kill living creatures, steal, commit sexual misconduct, lie, and consume intoxicants of beer, wine, and liquor. They’re immoral, of bad character. That’s how a child fails their birth. These are the three kinds of children found in the world.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§6“The astute wish for a child
who betters or equals their birth;
not one who fails their birth,
disgracing their family.

§7These are the children in the world
who become lay devotees;
faithful, accomplished in ethics,
bountiful, rid of stinginess.
Like the moon freed from clouds,
they shine in the assemblies.”

§8This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

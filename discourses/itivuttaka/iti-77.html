Fragile

Bhidurasutta

under 1 minute to read

Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — among the shortest and plainest discourses in the entire collection

Why this discourse, the seventy-seventh

One sentence of prose, one stanza of verse, and nothing else — this is one of the briefest discourses in the whole Itivuttaka. Three claims about impermanence are stated flatly, with no simile and no argument, and the verse simply describes what someone who has taken those three claims seriously goes on to do.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

This body is fragile, consciousness is liable to fade away, and all attachments are impermanent, suffering, and perishable.

Three claims, no argument

Unlike several of the other discourses in this set, this one offers no simile, no list of examples, and no comparison to weigh one thing against another. It simply states three things as fact: the body is bhidura, fragile or breakable; consciousness itself is virāgadhamma, liable to fade or lose its color; and every upadhi, every attachment, is impermanent, suffering, and subject to change. The discourse doesn’t try to persuade — it assumes these claims will be recognized as true once stated, and moves directly to what follows from them.

What follows from taking it seriously

The verse describes the consequence: someone who genuinely knows the body is fragile, knows consciousness has faded, and “sees the danger in attachments” goes on to “go beyond birth and death.” The verb matters — not merely believing these three claims intellectually, but seeing danger in attachments as a live perception, is what the verse presents as doing the actual work.

“They await their time”

The verse’s final image is notably calm: someone who has “attained ultimate peace” and is fully “evolved” simply “awaits their time.” There is no urgency or anxiety in the picture — having taken fragility and impermanence fully on board, the discourse suggests, produces not dread but a kind of settled waiting.

Key terms

bhidura — “fragile” or “breakable” — the discourse’s Pali title, and its description of the body specifically.
virāgadhamma — “liable to fade away” — the discourse’s description of consciousness (viññāṇa) itself, not only the body.
upadhī — “attachments” — described here as aniccā dukkhā vipariṇāmadhammā, impermanent, suffering, and subject to change.
jātimaraṇa — “birth and death” — what the verse says is gone beyond by one who sees the danger in attachments.
paramaṁ santiṁ — “ultimate peace” — the state the verse says has been attained by the one who “awaits their time.”

The text

The discourse in full — one of the shortest in this set: a single prose sentence, a single verse stanza, and the fixed closing line. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“This body is fragile, mendicants, consciousness is liable to fade away, and all attachments are impermanent, suffering, and perishable.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“Knowing that the body is fragile,
that consciousness is faded,
and seeing the danger in attachments,
they go beyond birth and death.
Having attained ultimate peace,
evolved, they await their time.”

§4This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

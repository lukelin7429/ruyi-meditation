Itivuttaka · Discourse 102

The Ending of Defilements

Āsavakkhayasutta

Setting
No narrative frame — no time, place, or listener is named
Speakers
The Buddha, addressing the mendicants collectively
Form
A single conditional claim about knowledge and liberation, then a three-stanza verse tracing the stages from training to freedom
Length
<1 minute to read
Position
3rd of 13 discourses in the Chapter on the Brahmin’s Offering of the Teaching, the sole chapter of the Book of the Fours (Iti 100–112)
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — compact, but assumes familiarity with the four noble truths

Why this discourse

A short, almost formulaic claim: the ending of the defilements (āsavakkhaya) happens only for someone who knows and sees — specifically, who knows and sees suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path. It is a discourse about what liberation depends on, phrased as flatly as an equation, then walked once more through a verse that adds the missing middle step: knowledge of ending comes first, and only then does the deeper knowledge of freedom follow, in the very same life.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

“The ending of the defilements is for one who knows and sees, not for one who does not know or see” — and what must be known and seen, the Buddha specifies immediately, is nothing exotic: suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path leading to its cessation.

Not effort alone, but knowledge

The discourse’s structure is almost argumentative: it states a claim, then asks its own follow-up question — “knows and sees what?” — before answering. This has the effect of ruling something out before it can even be proposed: the ending of the āsavas (the deep-seated defilements or “influxes” that the tradition treats as the very last things to go before full awakening) is not achieved by willpower, ritual, or duration of practice as such. It is achieved by a specific act of understanding, directed at a specific object — the four truths already named across the Itivuttaka’s earlier discourses on ascetics and brahmins (Iti 103, standing right next to this one, restates the same four truths from a different angle).

Two knowledges, not one

The verse adds a detail the prose alone doesn’t supply: a sequence. “As a trainee trains, following the straight road, first they know about ending; enlightenment follows in the same lifetime.” There are two distinct moments of knowing named here — the “knowledge of ending” (khaya-ñāṇa) that arises at the moment the defilements actually end, and then a further, retrospective “knowledge of freedom” (aññā) that arises for the person who is now freed by that very ending. The text is careful not to collapse these into a single event: the ending happens, and then the fact of having been freed by it becomes known as its own further realization.

Not for the lazy or the unlearned

The verse’s closing lines are its bluntest: “Not by the lazy, or by fools who don’t understand, may this be realized — extinguishment, the release from all ties.” Diligence and understanding are named together as the twin preconditions, ruling out both the person who simply doesn’t try and the person who tries without the right understanding. Nothing here promises that effort by itself is sufficient; the entire discourse has been built to make exactly that point.

Key terms

āsava — usually rendered “defilements” or “influxes”: the deep undercurrents of craving, view, and ignorance whose complete ending marks full awakening.
jānato passato — “for one who knows and sees”: the discourse’s repeated qualifier, naming exactly the condition under which the āsavas end.
khayasmiṁ paṭhamaṁ ñāṇaṁ — “first, knowledge of ending”: the first of the verse’s two named knowledges, arising as the defilements actually end.
aññā — the further, “supreme knowledge of freedom” that follows the knowledge of ending, for one now freed through enlightenment.
saṁyojana — the “fetters,” whose ending the verse names directly alongside the arising of this final knowledge.

The text

The discourse in full, given complete: the claim about knowledge and the ending of defilements, and the three-stanza verse that follows it. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, I say that the ending of defilements is for one who knows and sees, not for one who does not know or see. For one who knows and sees what? The ending of defilements is for one who knows and sees suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path. The ending of the defilements is for one who knows and sees this.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3–5“As a trainee trains, following the straight road, first they know about ending; enlightenment follows in the same lifetime. Then to one freed through enlightenment the knowledge of ending arises, the supreme knowledge of freedom, with the ending of the fetters. Not by the lazy, or by fools who don’t understand may this be realized—extinguishment, the release from all ties.”

§6This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

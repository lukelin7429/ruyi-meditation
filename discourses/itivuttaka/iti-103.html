Itivuttaka · Discourse 103

Ascetics and Brahmins

Samaṇabrāhmaṇasutta

Setting
No narrative frame — no time, place, or listener is named
Speakers
The Buddha, addressing the mendicants collectively
Form
A two-part contrast — those who understand the four truths and those who don't — followed by a four-stanza verse restating the same contrast
Length
~1 minute to read
Position
4th of 13 discourses in the Chapter on the Brahmin’s Offering of the Teaching, the sole chapter of the Book of the Fours (Iti 100–112)
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a clear two-sided contrast

Why this discourse

A blunt redefinition: anyone, ascetic or brahmin by title, who does not truly understand suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path does not count — in the Buddha’s own words, as a true ascetic or brahmin at all, whatever their robes or lineage claim. The verse then draws out the practical stakes: those without this understanding cannot make an end of anything, and simply keep being reborn and growing old.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Whether someone genuinely counts as an ascetic or a brahmin, the Buddha says, has nothing to do with title or appearance and everything to do with whether they truly understand suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path — realized, moreover, “with their own insight,” not merely accepted secondhand.

A redefinition, stated as a denial

The discourse is built as a flat denial before it becomes an affirmation. Of those who don’t understand the four truths, the Buddha says: “I don’t deem them as true ascetics and brahmins” — regardless, the text implies, of whatever status the titles “samaṇa” or “brāhmaṇa” carry elsewhere. “Those venerables don’t realize the goal of life as an ascetic or brahmin, and don’t live having realized it with their own insight.” Only afterward does the positive half arrive, phrased in an exact mirror of the negative: those who do understand these four truths, the Buddha does regard as true ascetics and brahmins, precisely because they have realized that same goal for themselves.

What’s at stake: making an end, or not

The verse doesn’t simply repeat the prose’s categories — it adds a consequence. Those without this understanding, it says, “lack the heart’s release, as well as the release by wisdom” and, because of that, are “unable to make an end” — they “continue to be reborn and grow old.” Those who do understand are “endowed with the heart’s release, as well as the release by wisdom,” and so are able to make an end: they “don’t continue to be reborn and grow old.” The two kinds of release named here — of heart and of wisdom — are given as a pair throughout the early texts, standing respectively for freedom achieved through concentration and freedom achieved through insight; both, this verse insists, depend on the same prior understanding of the four truths.

A companion to the discourse beside it

Iti 102, immediately preceding this one, made a nearly identical claim about who can end the defilements: “one who knows and sees” the same four truths. This discourse applies that exact standard to a specific pair of social titles — ascetic and brahmin — that a fourth-to-third-century-BCE Indian audience would have recognized as claims to the highest possible religious authority. Placed back to back, the two discourses make the same point twice from different angles: real spiritual attainment is not a matter of category or title, but of whether the four truths have actually been seen.

Key terms

samaṇa — “ascetic”: a wandering renunciant, one of the two titles this discourse redefines by realization rather than by role.
brāhmaṇa — “brahmin”: a member of the priestly class by birth, the other title this discourse redefines the same way.
sāmaññattha / brahmaññattha — “the goal of life as an ascetic” and “as a brahmin”: what the text says must be realized with one’s own insight, not merely held as a title.
cetovimutti — “the heart’s release,” liberation associated with concentration; named in the verse alongside its counterpart below.
paññāvimutti — “release by wisdom,” liberation associated with insight; the verse says both depend on understanding the four truths.

The text

The discourse in full, given complete: the redefinition of a true ascetic or brahmin, and the four-stanza verse that follows it. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, there are ascetics and brahmins who don’t truly understand about suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path. I don’t deem them as true ascetics and brahmins. Those venerables don’t realize the goal of life as an ascetic or brahmin, and don’t live having realized it with their own insight.

§3There are ascetics and brahmins who truly understand about suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path. I regard them as true ascetics and brahmins. Those venerables realize the goal of life as an ascetic or brahmin, and live having realized it with their own insight.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§4–5“There are those who don’t understand suffering and suffering’s cause, and where all suffering cease with no residue left behind. And they do not know the path that leads to the stilling of suffering. They lack the heart’s release, as well as the release by wisdom. Unable to make an end, they continue to be reborn and grow old.

§6–7But there are those who understand suffering and suffering’s cause, and where all suffering cease with no residue left behind. And they understand the path that leads to the stilling of suffering. They’re endowed with the heart’s release, as well as the release by wisdom. Able to make an end, they don’t continue to be reborn and grow old.”

§8This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

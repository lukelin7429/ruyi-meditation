Itivuttaka · The Book of the Twos · Discourse 43

Free of Rebirth

Ajātasutta

Setting
None given — no place, audience, or occasion; the discourse opens directly with the Itivuttaka's own formula
Position
16th of 22 discourses in the Book of the Twos
Form
A single prose argument followed by a three-verse restatement
Length
About 1 minute to read
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — brief but conceptually dense, touching directly on Nibbāna; best read with restraint rather than over-interpretation

Why this discourse, forty-third

This is the Itivuttaka’s version of one of the canon’s most quoted passages: the claim that "there is... freedom from rebirth, freedom from what has been produced, made, and conditioned." Its argument is careful and, notably, defensive rather than descriptive — it establishes that such a freedom must exist, on pain of there being no escape at all, without going on to characterize positively what it is beyond that bare fact.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

There is freedom from rebirth and from everything produced, made, and conditioned — and the text’s own argument for this is not a positive description of what that freedom is like, but a conditional one: without it, no escape from the conditioned could ever be found, and since an escape evidently is found, such a freedom must exist.

No narrative frame

As with almost every discourse in this collection, no place, audience, or occasion is given. The teaching opens directly with the Itivuttaka's formula and proceeds straight into the statement itself.

An argument, not a description

The prose is compact and repeats itself by design: "There is, mendicants, freedom from rebirth, freedom from what has been produced, made, and conditioned. If there were no freedom from rebirth... then you would find no escape here from rebirth... But since there is freedom from rebirth... an escape is found." Notice what this argument does and does not do. It does not describe the unconditioned positively — what it is, where it is, what it is like. It argues, instead, from necessity: an escape from the conditioned realm is evidently found (elsewhere the tradition describes exactly this, in the awakening of arahants), and this would be impossible unless something unconditioned existed to escape to. The four terms doing the work — unborn, unproduced, unmade, unconditioned — are all negations, denying that this freedom arose, came into being, was constructed, or depends on conditions, rather than affirmations of some positive further nature.

The conditioned realm, described

The verses turn first to what the escape is an escape from: "What's reborn, produced, and arisen, made, conditioned, not lasting, wrapped in old age and death, frail, a nest of disease, whose inception is the leash to food: that's not fit to delight in." The phrase "the leash to food" (a compressed image for how conditioned existence depends on nutriment to continue) closes the list of what makes this realm precisely the kind of thing one would want an escape from — not evil in some dramatic sense, but structurally unstable, ageing, and diseased by nature.

The escape, named but not elaborated

Against that backdrop, the escape is called "peaceful, beyond the scope of logic, everlasting... the sorrowless, stainless state, the cessation of all painful things, the blissful stilling of conditions." Even here, at its most expansive, the description stays close to negation and cessation — sorrowless, stainless, a stilling — rather than offering a positive picture of some further place or substance. Given how easily a reader could import a metaphysical picture of their own into a passage like this, the discourse itself is worth reading carefully on its own restrained terms: it establishes that an unconditioned escape exists and names what it is not troubled by, without going further than that.

Key terms

ajāta, abhūta, akata, asaṅkhata — "unborn, unarisen, unmade, unconditioned": four negations naming the freedom this discourse argues must exist.
nissaraṇa — "escape": what the argument concludes must be possible, given that an unconditioned freedom exists.
āhāranettippabhava — "whose inception is the leash to food": the verse's compressed image for how conditioned existence depends on nutriment.
atakkāvacara — "beyond the scope of logic": how the verse describes the escape — not reachable by reasoning alone.
saṅkhārūpasama — "the stilling of conditions": the verse's closing description of the escape's peace.

The text

The discourse in full: the prose argument and all three verses. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“There is, mendicants, freedom from rebirth, freedom from what has been produced, made, and conditioned. If there were no freedom from rebirth, freedom from what has been produced, made, and conditioned, then you would find no escape here from rebirth, from what has been produced, made, and conditioned. But since there is freedom from rebirth, freedom from what has been produced, made, and conditioned, an escape is found from rebirth, from what has been produced, made, and conditioned.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“What’s reborn, produced, and arisen, made, conditioned, not lasting, wrapped in old age and death, frail, a nest of disease,

§4whose inception is the leash to food: that’s not fit to delight in. The escape from that is peaceful, beyond the scope of logic, everlasting,

§5where nothing is reborn or arisen, the sorrowless, stainless state, the cessation of all painful things, the blissful stilling of conditions.”

§6This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

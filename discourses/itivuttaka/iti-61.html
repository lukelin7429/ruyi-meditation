Itivuttaka · Book of the Threes, Chapter Two · Iti 61

The Eye

Cakkhusutta

Setting
None given — no place, audience beyond “mendicants,” or occasion, in keeping with the Itivuttaka’s spare form throughout
Speakers
The Buddha alone; the Itivuttaka’s bipartite form never includes an interlocutor
Form
A one-sentence list of three items, stated in prose, then restated and quietly extended in two short verse stanzas
Length
Under a minute to read the passage itself
Parallels
No complete Chinese Itivuttaka survives; a partial parallel collection exists in Tibetan translation, with scattered individual parallels across the Āgamas, none specifically identified for this short discourse
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — the three terms are simply named, not explained; a little background fills them out

Why this discourse

This is one of the shortest discourses in the entire Pāli Canon, and also one of the most quietly ambitious: in barely two sentences of prose it proposes that “eye” can mean three quite different things — ordinary sight, a meditatively developed clairvoyance, and the direct wisdom that ends suffering — and treats all three as belonging to a single continuum. Later commentarial literature would expand this into a still more elaborate list of “five eyes” associated with a fully awakened Buddha. Here, in its earliest and plainest form, the list has just three members, and the verse adds something the prose never quite says outright: that these three eyes arise in sequence, one opening the way to the next.

Reading guide

Three eyes, one word

“Eye” (cakkhu) is doing metaphorical work from the very first line. The discourse does not open by talking about seeing in the ordinary sense and then branching into extended meanings; it simply announces, flatly, that there are three eyes, as though this were as uncontroversial as saying there are three seasons. Only the list itself — the eye of flesh, the eye of clairvoyance, the eye of wisdom — reveals that only the first of the three has anything to do with the organ in your head.

What the three eyes are

The eye of flesh (maṁsacakkhu) is ordinary physical sight, the only one of the three that everyone already has. The eye of clairvoyance (dibbacakkhu, literally the “divine eye”) is one of the higher knowledges (abhiññā) said to be developed through deep meditation — traditionally described elsewhere in the canon as the capacity to see beings passing away and reappearing according to their deeds. The eye of wisdom (paññācakkhu) is called “supreme” (anuttara) in the verse, and refers to the direct, liberating insight that the other two eyes, however remarkable, do not by themselves provide.

The verse’s addition: a path, not just a list

A reader could be forgiven for expecting the verse simply to repeat the prose’s three-item list in more decorative language, and its first stanza does exactly that. But the second stanza does something the prose never claims: it puts the three eyes in causal order. “The arising of the eye of flesh is the path to the clairvoyant eye,” it says — and then, when knowledge arises, the supreme eye of wisdom appears, and whoever gains that eye is released from all suffering. The prose lists three parallel things; the verse turns them into three rungs of a single ladder, with ordinary sight, oddly enough, standing at the bottom of a path that ends in liberation.

A seed for a longer list

This compact, three-eye formula is often read as an early seed of the later, more elaborate “five eyes” (pañcacakkhu) catalogue that appears in post-canonical commentarial literature, which adds a universal eye and a Buddha-eye to the three named here. Nothing in this short discourse itself makes that claim; it is worth knowing about mainly so a reader doesn’t mistake the fuller five-eye list, when encountered elsewhere, for something this particular sutta already contains.

The form itself

Like every discourse in this collection, The Eye opens with “This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard,” states its teaching once in prose, restates it in verse, and closes with a near-mirror of the opening line. There is no audience named beyond the standing address “mendicants,” no place, no occasion — nothing narrative at all. The teaching itself is asked to carry the whole weight of the discourse, which is very much the Itivuttaka’s ordinary way of working.

Key terms

cakkhu — “eye”: here a metaphor for three different modes of seeing, only the first of which is literal.
maṁsacakkhu — “the eye of flesh”: ordinary physical sight, the first and most basic of the three.
dibbacakkhu — “the divine eye” or clairvoyance: a meditatively developed higher knowledge, traditionally described elsewhere as perceiving beings’ rebirth according to their deeds.
paññācakkhu — “the eye of wisdom”: called “supreme” in the verse, the direct insight said to release one from all suffering.
purisuttamo — “the supreme person”: the verse’s epithet for the Buddha as the one who taught the three eyes.

The text

The discourse in full — two sentences of prose followed by two brief verse stanzas. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

The teaching

§2“Mendicants, there are these three eyes. What three? The eye of the flesh, the eye of clairvoyance, and the eye of wisdom. These are the three eyes.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

The verse

§3“The eye of flesh, the clairvoyant eye, the supreme eye of wisdom: these three eyes were taught by the supreme person.

§4The arising of the eye of flesh is the path to the clairvoyant eye. When knowledge arises—the supreme eye of wisdom—one who gains that eye is released from all suffering.”

§5This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

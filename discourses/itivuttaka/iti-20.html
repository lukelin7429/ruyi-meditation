A Corrupted Mind

The Book of the Ones, Chapter Two

Audience
Mendicants in general — no named individual is the subject; the “individual whose mind is corrupted” is spoken of generically, not named or described further
Form
A first-person prose account of the Buddha comprehending such a mind, followed by a three-stanza verse restating the same point
Length
About a minute to read — the longest of this run of ten discourses
Pairing
Answered by its counterpart, Iti 21, “A Pure Mind” — immediately following, outside the scope of this page
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a direct, first-person claim about the link between mental state and rebirth

Why this discourse, twentieth

This discourse is spoken in the first person in a way most of this chapter is not: “When I’ve comprehended the mind of an individual whose mind is corrupted, I understand…” No name is given for this individual, no story is told about them — the claim is about a category of mental state, generically described, and what the Buddha says he can know about someone in that state.

Reading guide

A claim about comprehending, not judging

The discourse’s framing is worth noticing carefully. It doesn’t say the Buddha condemns or punishes a person with a corrupted mind; it says that having comprehended such a mind, a specific understanding follows: “If this individual were to die right now, they would be placed in hell as if delivered there.” The claim is diagnostic, not moralizing — a description of where a particular mental state leads, stated as directly as the text can state it.

“As if delivered there”

This phrase carries real weight. It doesn’t describe rebirth in hell as a distant, uncertain possibility contingent on a future judgment; it describes something closer to an already-determined consequence, as automatic and immediate as being carried to a destination by someone else’s hand. The text asks its own question and answers it directly: “Why is that? Because their mind is corrupted.”

Mental state at the moment of death

The discourse’s closing prose line generalizes the point beyond this one hypothetical individual: “Corruption of mind is the reason why some sentient beings, when their body breaks up, after death, are reborn in a place of loss, a bad place, the underworld, hell.” The specific timing matters here — the state of mind described is one held at the point of death, not necessarily a permanent trait, though the text doesn’t explore how such a state might arise or be reversed within this discourse itself.

The verse: three stanzas, one claim repeated

Unlike most of the discourses in this chapter, the verse here runs to three stanzas rather than one or two, and it takes its time. The first names the setting almost narratively — “the Buddha explained this matter in the mendicants’ presence” — before the second and third stanzas restate the prose’s claim nearly word for word: die now, be reborn in hell, “for their mind is corrupted,” “cast down as surely as if they’d been carried off and put there.” The repetition across three stanzas, rather than the usual one, gives this particular claim unusual emphasis within the chapter.

What answers it

This discourse’s natural counterpart, Iti 21 (“A Pure Mind”), lies just beyond the ten discourses gathered on this site and is built, structurally, by another set of hands entirely — but its existence is worth noting here. Just as Iti 18 and Iti 19 balanced schism against harmony, this discourse’s account of a corrupted mind leading to hell is answered, immediately next, by an account of a pure mind leading to heaven.

Key terms

a corrupted mind — the discourse’s subject, a mental state comprehended by the Buddha and linked directly to rebirth in a place of loss.
“as if delivered there” — the discourse’s image for how automatic and immediate the consequence of dying with a corrupted mind is described to be.
the moment of death — the specific timing the discourse names: the mind’s state “when their body breaks up, after death” is what the claim concerns.
a place of loss, the underworld, hell — the discourse’s own stacked terms for the destination named as a result of a corrupted mind.
Iti 21, “A Pure Mind” — the discourse immediately following this one, presenting the opposite case; beyond the scope of this page but its natural counterpart.

The text

The discourse in full. The verse’s three stanzas are held within a single continuous quotation in the source text, reproduced here as one block. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, when I’ve comprehended the mind of an individual whose mind is corrupted, I understand: ‘If this individual were to die right now, they would be placed in hell as if delivered there.’ Why is that? Because their mind is corrupted. Corruption of mind is the reason why some sentient beings, when their body breaks up, after death, are reborn in a place of loss, a bad place, the underworld, hell.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3–5“Knowing an individual’s
corrupted mind,
the Buddha explained this matter
in the mendicants’ presence.

If that individual
were to die at this time,
they’d be reborn in hell,
for their mind is corrupted.

That sort is cast down as surely
as if they’d been carried off and put there.
For corruption of mind is the reason
sentient beings go to a bad place.”

§6This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

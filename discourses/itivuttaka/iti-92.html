Itivuttaka · Iti 92

The Corner of the Cloak

The Book of the Threes · Chapter Five

Book
The Book of the Threes · Chapter Five
Frame
None — addressed generally to “mendicants,” with no location, listener, or story given
Form
Two contrasted images (physical closeness versus distance) in prose, followed by two verses restating and extending the point
Length
~1 minute to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a simple, memorable contrast

Why this discourse, ninety-second

What makes a follower close to the Buddha? Not, this discourse insists, physical proximity. A mendicant who literally holds the hem of his robe while still gripped by craving and ill will is, in every way that matters, far from him. One who lives a hundred leagues away but has let go of that craving is close — because to see the teaching is to see the Buddha, and to miss it is to miss him, regardless of distance.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A mendicant who literally clings to the hem of the Buddha’s robe while still gripped by craving and ill will is, in every way that matters, far from him; one who lives a hundred leagues away but has let go of craving is close — because to see the teaching is to see the Buddha, and to miss the teaching is to miss him, regardless of physical proximity.

Two mendicants, two distances

The discourse sets up its point with a clean pair of images. First: “suppose a mendicant were to hold the corner of my cloak and follow behind me step by step” — yet this mendicant “covets sensual pleasures,” is “infatuated, full of ill will and malicious intent,” unmindful, with “straying minds and undisciplined faculties.” Despite the physical closeness, “they are far from me, and I from them.” Second, the reverse: “suppose a mendicant were to live a hundred leagues away” but does not covet sensual pleasures, is not infatuated or full of ill will, and has “established mindfulness, situational awareness and immersion, with unified mind and restrained faculties.” Despite the distance, “they are close to me, and I to them.”

Seeing the teaching, seeing the Buddha

The reason given for both cases is identical: “that mendicant does not see the teaching. Not seeing the teaching, they do not see me” — or, in the second case, the reverse: “that mendicant sees the teaching. Seeing the teaching, they see me.” It is worth noting, honestly, that a very similar equation between seeing the Dhamma and seeing the Buddha appears elsewhere in the canon, in the story of the monk Vakkali, who was so devoted to gazing at the Buddha’s physical form that the Buddha had to redirect him toward the teaching itself. Here the same equation is simply stated as a flat, unconditional principle, without any individual’s story attached to it — a difference worth noticing between this collection’s spare style and the fuller narrative treatments found elsewhere.

The lake free of wind

The closing verses restate the point in image after image: the one “full of desire and distress” may follow closely behind, “yet see how distant they are” — “the stirred from the still, the burning from the quenched, the greedy from the greedless.” By contrast, “an astute person who has understood and directly known the teaching, grows calm, as unperturbed as a lake free of wind.” The final lines draw the whole discourse together with a simple principle of likeness: “the still to the still, the quenched to the quenched, the greedless to the greedless” — closeness, in other words, is a matter of what one has in common with the Buddha, not where one stands relative to him.

Key terms

“seeing the teaching, they see me” — the discourse’s central claim; a related idea is developed at greater length elsewhere in the story of the monk Vakkali.
a hundred leagues (yojana) — a traditional long-distance unit, used here purely to make the point that physical remoteness is irrelevant to closeness with the Buddha.
santa, “still” — the calm, unperturbed state contrasted throughout the verse with agitation and craving.
nibbuta, “quenched” — extinguished, cooled; used of a mind freed from the metaphorical burning of craving and hatred.
like draws to like — the verse’s closing structural device, pairing the still with the still, the quenched with the quenched, the greedless with the greedless.

The text

The discourse in full: both contrasted images and both closing verses. No location or listener is named in the source. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1.1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2.1–2.6“Mendicants, suppose a mendicant were to hold the corner of my cloak and follow behind me step by step. Yet they covet sensual pleasures; they’re infatuated, full of ill will and malicious intent. They are unmindful, lacking situational awareness and immersion, with straying minds and undisciplined faculties. Then they are far from me, and I from them. Why is that? Because that mendicant does not see the teaching. Not seeing the teaching, they do not see me.

§3.1–3.8Suppose a mendicant were to live a hundred leagues away. Yet they do not covet sensual pleasures; they’re not infatuated, or full of ill will and malicious intent. They have established mindfulness, situational awareness and immersion, with unified mind and restrained faculties. Then they are close to me, and I to them. Why is that? Because that mendicant sees the teaching. Seeing the teaching, they see me.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§4.1–4.6“One full of desire and distress
may follow close behind,
yet see how distant they are—
the stirred from the still,
the burning from the quenched,
the greedy from the greedless.

§5.1–5.4An astute person who has understood
and directly known the teaching,
grows calm, as unperturbed
as a lake free of wind.

§6.1–6.4See how close they are—
the still to the still,
the quenched to the quenched,
the greedless to the greedless.”

§7.1This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

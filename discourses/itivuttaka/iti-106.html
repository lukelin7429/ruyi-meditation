Itivuttaka · Discourse 106

With Divinity

Sabrahmakasutta

Setting
No narrative frame — no time, place, or listener is named
Speakers
The Buddha, addressing the mendicants collectively
Form
Four parallel claims about a family that honors its parents, an explanation of the terms used, and a three-stanza verse
Length
~1 minute to read
Position
7th of 13 discourses in the Chapter on the Brahmin’s Offering of the Teaching, the sole chapter of the Book of the Fours (Iti 100–112)
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — direct and unusually warm in tone

Why this discourse

A single word, redirected: “Brahmā” — ordinarily the highest of the gods, or the impersonal absolute of brahminical cosmology — is here simply a term (adhivacana) for one’s own mother and father. A family where children honor their parents at home, this discourse says, already lives with Divinity, with the elder gods, with the first tutors, and with those worthy of the offerings ordinarily reserved for the gods.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

“A family where the children honor their parents in their home is said to live with Divinity” — and the same family is said, in three further parallel formulas, to live with “the elder gods,” with “the first tutors,” and with “those worthy of offerings dedicated to the gods,” because each of these four terms, the Buddha explains directly, is simply another name for one’s own mother and father.

Four titles, one referent

The discourse states its four formulas first, then immediately explains all four at once: “‘Divinity’ is a term for your parents. ‘Elder gods’ is a term for your parents. ‘First tutors’ is a term for your parents. ‘Worthy of offerings dedicated to the gods’ is a term for your parents.” Each title captures a slightly different facet of what a parent is: Brahmā for a kind of originating, presiding status; “elder gods” (pubbadevatā) for a household deity present before any other; “first tutors” for the plain fact that parents are literally the first teachers anyone has; and “worthy of offerings” for the idea that whatever devotion is due to the gods is, in an ordinary household, actually owed to the two people who raised you.

The reason given

Having named all four titles, the text asks its own question — “Why is that?” — and answers it plainly, without any appeal to ritual obligation or scriptural authority: “Parents are very helpful to their children, they raise them, nurture them, and show them the world.” The justification is entirely practical and observational. Nothing here depends on parents being owed reverence by cosmic decree; it rests on what parents concretely, verifiably do — the sheer scale of unglamorous labor involved in raising a person from infancy to the point where they can navigate the world at all.

What honoring them looks like

The verse turns the explanation into a set of instructions, and they are strikingly bodily and domestic: “an astute person would revere them and honor them with food and drink, clothes and bedding, anointing and bathing, and by washing their feet.” This is not devotion at a distance — not prayer or ritual offering to a god who cannot be touched — but direct physical care of the kind any household could actually provide. The verse’s closing lines promise a double reward for this: praise “in this life by the astute,” and rejoicing in heaven after death — the same heavenly destination, notably, that the gods being compared to parents are themselves said to inhabit.

Key terms

Brahmā — ordinarily the high god of brahminical cosmology; used here, unusually, as a direct epithet (adhivacana) for one’s own parents, not the deity itself.
pubbadevatā — “the elder” or “earlier gods”: the second of the four titles applied to parents.
pubbācariya — “the first tutors”: the third title, naming parents as literally a child’s earliest teachers.
āhuneyya — “worthy of oblation,” ordinarily said of those deserving ritual offerings; the fourth title applied here to parents.
bahukāra — “very helpful”: the reason given for all four titles — that parents raise, nurture, and show their children the world. The same word supplies the Pāli title of the discourse immediately following, Iti 107.

The text

The discourse in full, given complete: the four formulas, the explanation of the terms, and the closing verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, a family where the children honor their parents in their home is said to live with Divinity. A family where the children honor their parents in their home is said to live with the elder gods. A family where the children honor their parents in their home is said to live with the first tutors. A family where the children honor their parents in their home is said to live with those worthy of offerings dedicated to the gods.

§3‘Divinity’ is a term for your parents. ‘Elder gods’ is a term for your parents. ‘First tutors’ is a term for your parents. ‘Worthy of offerings dedicated to the gods’ is a term for your parents. Why is that? Parents are very helpful to their children, they raise them, nurture them, and show them the world.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§4–6“Parents are said to be ‘Divinity’ and ‘first tutors’. They’re worthy of offerings dedicated to the gods from their children, for they love their offspring. Therefore an astute person would revere them and honor them with food and drink, clothes and bedding, anointing and bathing, and by washing their feet. Because they serve their parents like this, they’re praised in this life by the astute, and they depart to rejoice in heaven.”

§7This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

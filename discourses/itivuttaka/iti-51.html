Why this discourse, fifty-first

Iti 51 opens the meditative material of the Book of the Threes with a claim that rewards slow reading: release is not a single leap but a passage through three “elements” — form, formlessness, and cessation — and only the last of the three is where freedom actually occurs. The formless is not the goal; it is a place the verse specifically warns against getting stuck in. As with nearly every discourse in this collection, no audience or occasion is named — the teaching stands on its own, in prose and then in verse.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Three specialized “elements” — form, formlessness, and cessation — mark stages of meditative depth on the way to release, and the verse is careful to specify that only the third, cessation, is where release itself takes place.

Not the familiar elements

“Element” (dhātu) is one of the most overloaded words in the early texts, and this discourse uses it in a specifically meditative sense that has nothing to do with the physical elements — earth, water, fire, air — analyzed elsewhere in discussions of the body. It is worth being precise about which triad is meant, because a very similarly named one exists: elsewhere, “another three elements” refers to the sensual, form, and formless realms of rebirth. This discourse’s triad — the elements of form, formlessness, and cessation — describes something different: three depths of meditative experience culminating not in a further realm of rebirth but in cessation itself.

Comprehending, not stuck, released

The first stanza chooses its verbs with care. One comprehends the element of form — not rejecting it outright, but understanding it fully, presumably through the four form-sphere absorptions. One is not stuck in the formless — language that treats the formless attainments as a known hazard, a refined and peaceful place a meditator can mistake for the destination. Only in cessation is one released, and only there does the verse call someone a “conqueror of death.” The three verbs trace an ascending seriousness: comprehend, don’t get stuck, be released.

The second stanza’s addition

The first stanza could almost stand alone, but the second adds something specific: it names what is directly experienced as “the element free of death,” and it says the Buddha teaches this — the “undefiled” and “fully awakened” one showing others “the sorrowless, stainless state.” Cessation, in other words, is not annihilation being described from the outside; it is a state that can be directly experienced, and having been experienced, taught.

Key terms

the element of form — the first of the three, corresponding to the fine-material sphere of meditative absorption; the verse says it should be “comprehended,” not merely entered.
the formless — the second element, the formless meditative attainments; notably, the only one of the three the verse specifically warns against being “stuck” in.
the element of cessation — the third and only one in which the verse locates actual release; those released here are called “conquerors of death.”
“the element free of death” — the second stanza’s name for what is directly experienced in cessation — a description of the deathless, not of extinction.
“the sorrowless, stainless state” — what the fully awakened Buddha is said to teach, closing the discourse’s second stanza.

The text

The discourse in full — nine lines of prose and verse, given complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, there are these three elements. What three? The elements of form, formlessness, and cessation. These are the three elements.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“Comprehending the element of form,
not stuck in the formless,
those who are released in cessation,
they are conquerors of death.

§4Having directly experienced the element free of death,
free of attachments;
having realized relinquishment
of attachments, the undefiled
fully awakened Buddha teaches
the sorrowless, stainless state.”

§5This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

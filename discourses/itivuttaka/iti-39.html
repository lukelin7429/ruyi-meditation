Itivuttaka · Discourse 39

Teaching

Desanāsutta

Setting
None given — no narrative frame, as with almost every discourse in this collection
Speaker
The Buddha alone
Form
A prose statement describing a method in two steps, restated in a matching verse
Position
The Book of the Twos, Chapter Two, the 2nd of its discourses
Length
< 1 minute to read
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — brief and self-referential, describing method rather than content

The Buddha’s teaching method, in two steps

Rather than describing a doctrine, this short discourse describes a method — the two-step approach the Buddha is said to use whenever he teaches the Dhamma at all: first, showing what is evil to be evil; only then, urging disillusionment, dispassion, and freedom from it. It is less a specific teaching than a description of how every specific teaching proceeds.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Realized One’s teaching always has two approaches: seeing evil as evil, and then, having seen it, becoming disillusioned, dispassionate, and freed from it.

Diagnosis before treatment

The order matters: the discourse insists that clear identification comes first (“see evil as evil”), and only afterward does the second approach — the actual work of disillusionment and release — become possible. The sequence cannot be skipped; on this account, no remedy is offered before the problem is named plainly.

Not new content, but a stated method

What is notable here is that the discourse does not specify what “evil” (pāpa) refers to in any particular case; it describes the shape every application takes, whatever the specific unskillful quality identified. This makes it more a statement about pedagogical method than a teaching in its own right — an unusually self-referential, almost meta, discourse for such a short text.

Compassion as the stated motive

The verse specifies why the Buddha teaches this way at all: he is “compassionate for all beings.” The two-step method is not framed as a logical structure alone, but as the shape a teaching necessarily takes when its actual purpose is to help someone let go of something harmful.

A short discourse describing a large pattern

Though brief, this teaching describes something that recurs throughout the wider canon: discourses on craving, on the aggregates, on views, and elsewhere consistently move from an act of clear-eyed identification to the disillusionment and release that identification makes possible. This short text simply names that recurring shape directly.

Key terms

desanā — “teaching, exposition”: the discourse’s title word, referring to the Buddha’s method of instruction rather than to a particular doctrine.
pāpa — “evil, what is bad”: the object of the method’s first step, left unspecified in this short text.
nibbindati — “become disillusioned, disenchanted”: the first of the effects named for the method’s second step.
virajjati — “become dispassionate, free of desire”: the second effect, echoed again in the closing verse.
sabbabhūtānukampin — “compassionate for all beings”: the verse’s stated motive for why the Buddha teaches in this two-step way at all.

The text

The discourse in full — a description of the Buddha’s two-step teaching method and its restating verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, the Realized One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha has two approaches to teaching Dhamma. What two? ‘See evil as evil’—this is the first approach to teaching Dhamma. ‘Having seen evil as evil, be disillusioned, dispassionate, and freed from it’—this is the second approach to teaching Dhamma. The Realized One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha has these two approaches to teaching Dhamma.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“See the two approaches for
explaining the Dhamma
used by the Realized One, the Buddha,
compassionate for all beings:

§4see that that is evil,
and be dispassionate towards it.
Then, with a mind free of desire,
you’ll make an end of suffering.”

§5This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

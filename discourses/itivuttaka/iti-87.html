Itivuttaka · The Book of the Threes · Iti 87

Destroyers of Sight

“This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.”

Setting
None given — no place, audience, or occasion is named
Form
Two matched prose lists (three harmful thoughts, three skillful thoughts) followed by a single-stanza verse
Length
~1 minute to read
Book
The Book of the Threes (Iti 60–99), Chapter Four
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a clean, symmetrical structure, easy to follow

Why this discourse, eighty-seventh

Most of the Itivuttaka's threes name a single set of three things. This discourse names two sets of three, deliberately mirrored against each other — three thoughts that destroy sight and three that create it — using exactly the same descriptive apparatus for both, so the contrast reads almost like a ledger with two matching columns.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Thoughts of sensuality, malice, and cruelty destroy sight, vision, and knowledge and block wisdom, while their direct opposites — thoughts of renunciation, good will, and harmlessness — create sight, vision, and knowledge and grow wisdom, and a mendicant's task is simply to think the second set and get rid of the first.

Two lists, identically described

The discourse's structure couldn't be more symmetrical. The three unskillful thoughts — “sensuality, malice, and cruelty” — are called “destroyers of sight, vision, and knowledge. They block wisdom, they’re on the side of distress, and they don’t lead to extinguishment.” The three skillful thoughts — “renunciation, good will, and harmlessness” — are described with the mirrored apparatus: “creators of sight, vision, and knowledge. They grow wisdom, they’re on the side of solace, and they lead to extinguishment.” Every phrase in the first description has a matched opposite in the second: destroyers/creators, block/grow, distress/solace, don't lead to/lead to extinguishment. Nothing is left asymmetrical; the two sets are constructed as precise mirror images.

Sensuality, malice, cruelty — and their opposites

The specific three-and-three named here recur throughout the canon as a standard pairing (most famously worked out at length in the discourse on Two Kinds of Thought). Sensuality's opposite is renunciation — not suppression of desire but a active turning away from craving sense pleasure. Malice's opposite is good will, an active wish for others' wellbeing rather than merely the absence of ill will. Cruelty's opposite is harmlessness, which in the canon typically implies active compassion for suffering beings, not simple non-interference. In each pair, the wholesome side isn't presented as a neutral default but as its own distinct orientation of mind, requiring its own cultivation.

“Destroyers of sight”: an unusual metaphor

Describing unskillful thoughts as literally destroying “sight, vision, and knowledge” is a striking metaphor: the danger of sensuality, malice, and cruelty isn't only that they're unpleasant or unethical, but that indulging them actively damages one's capacity to see clearly at all. The skillful thoughts, by contrast, don't merely avoid this damage; they positively “create” the same sight, vision, and knowledge. Thought, on this account, isn't a neutral medium through which perception passes unaffected — certain patterns of thinking actively degrade or build the very faculty of clear seeing.

The verse: rain on dust

The closing verse condenses the entire teaching into direct instruction — “think the three skillful thoughts, and get rid of the unskillful” — before offering its single image: “quelling such thoughts and considerations, like rain on the dust, with a heart calmed of thought, you’ll touch the state of peace right here.” Dust, stirred by wind, obscures and irritates until rain settles it; the simile suggests that unskillful thought is similarly always ready to rise up and cloud things again unless actively settled, and that the settling itself — not the mere absence of wind — is what brings clarity. “Right here” (diṭṭheva dhamme in similar formulas elsewhere) marks the promised peace as available in this very life, not deferred to some future existence.

Key terms

the three destroyers — thoughts of sensuality, malice, and cruelty, said to destroy sight, vision, and knowledge, block wisdom, and never lead to extinguishment.
the three creators — thoughts of renunciation, good will, and harmlessness, said to create sight, vision, and knowledge, grow wisdom, and lead to extinguishment.
renunciation (nekkhamma) — the direct opposite of sensuality: an active turning away from craving for sense pleasure, not mere abstinence.
good will (avyāpāda) — the opposite of malice: an active wish for others' wellbeing.
harmlessness (avihiṃsā) — the opposite of cruelty: active compassion for suffering beings, not simple non-interference.

The text

The discourse in full: the two mirrored lists of three thoughts each, and the closing verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, these three unskillful thoughts are destroyers of sight, vision, and knowledge. They block wisdom, they’re on the side of distress, and they don’t lead to extinguishment. What three? Thoughts of sensuality, malice, and cruelty. These are the three unskillful thoughts that are destroyers of sight, vision, and knowledge. They block wisdom, they’re on the side of distress, and they don’t lead to extinguishment.

§3These three skillful thoughts are creators of sight, vision, and knowledge. They grow wisdom, they’re on the side of solace, and they lead to extinguishment. What three? Thoughts of renunciation, good will, and harmlessness. These are the three skillful thoughts that are creators of sight, vision, and knowledge. They grow wisdom, they’re on the side of solace, and they lead to extinguishment.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§4“Think the three skillful thoughts,
and get rid of the unskillful.
Quelling such thoughts and considerations,
like rain on the dust,
with a heart calmed of thought,
you’ll touch the state of peace right here.”

§5This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

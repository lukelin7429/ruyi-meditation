Itivuttaka · Discourse 34

Keen

Ātāpīsutta

Setting
None given — no narrative frame, as with almost every discourse in this collection
Speaker
The Buddha alone
Form
A two-part conditional prose statement, restated in a longer two-stanza verse
Position
The Book of the Twos, Chapter One, the 7th of its ten discourses
Length
< 1 minute to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — simple claim, but the verse packs in several additional technical terms

Effort as a precondition, not a virtue on the side

This discourse makes an unusually strong claim: without being ātāpī (keen, ardent) and ottāpī (prudent, morally sensitive to wrongdoing), a mendicant simply cannot achieve awakening — not that it becomes harder, but that it becomes impossible. Paired with its positive counterpart in the same breath, the teaching treats sustained effort not as one virtue among many but as an actual precondition for the goal itself.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Without keen effort and moral sensitivity, awakening, extinguishment, and the supreme sanctuary from the yoke are all said to be simply out of reach for a mendicant; with them, all three become achievable.

Three names for one goal

Notice three terms named together for what keen effort makes possible: sambodhi (awakening), nibbāna (extinguishment), and “the supreme sanctuary from the yoke” (anuttara yogakkhema) — a phrase built from yoga (yoke, bond) and khema (safety, security) that recurs elsewhere in the canon as another name for the same final freedom. The discourse does not distinguish these three as separate attainments; naming all three together is a way of emphasizing how total the achievement is.

The verse’s fuller diagnosis

Where the prose names only two qualities (ātāpī, ottāpī), the verse’s negative half lists several more failings together: laziness, lack of energy, “dullness and drowsiness” (thīna-middha, one of the five hindrances), lack of conscience, lack of regard for others. The positive half of the verse likewise adds mindfulness, alertness, and meditative absorption to the original pair — suggesting keen effort was never meant to stand alone, but as part of a wider cluster of qualities working together.

“In this very life”

Notably, the verse’s closing promise is not for some future rebirth but idheva, “right here” — supreme awakening reachable now, in this very life, for one who cuts “the fetter of birth and old age” through sustained, alert effort.

Its place among its neighbors

As the seventh of Chapter One’s ten discourses, this is also the point where the chapter’s focus shifts from the hell-and-heaven accounting of the previous three discourses (Iti 31–33) to the internal discipline that actually gets a practitioner there.

Key terms

ātāpī — “keen, ardent”: literally one who burns, from the same root, tapati, that gives Iti 31’s atapanīya its opposite valence — burning as effort here, rather than the burn of remorse there.
ottāpī — “prudent”: moral dread of wrongdoing, traditionally paired with hirī (conscience) as the two qualities said elsewhere in the canon to guard the world.
thīnamiddha — “dullness and drowsiness”: one of the five hindrances, named in the verse among the failings of a mendicant lacking keen effort.
yogakkhema — “sanctuary, security from the yoke”: one of several names in the canon for final liberation, built from yoga (bond, yoke) and khema (safety).
idheva — “in this very life, right here”: the verse’s insistence that supreme awakening is not deferred to some future existence.

The text

The discourse in full — a two-part conditional statement and its restating two-stanza verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, without being keen and prudent a mendicant can’t achieve awakening, extinguishment, and the supreme sanctuary from the yoke. But if a mendicant is keen and prudent they can achieve awakening, extinguishment, and the supreme sanctuary from the yoke.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“Neither keen nor prudent,
lazy, lacking energy,
full of dullness and drowsiness,
unconscientious, lacking regard for others,
such a mendicant is incapable
of experiencing the highest awakening.

§4One who is mindful, alert, meditative,
keen, prudent, and diligent,
having cut the fetter of birth and old age,
would experience supreme awakening in this very life.”

§5This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

Itivuttaka · Discourse 24

A Heap of Bones

Aṭṭhipuñjasutta

Collection
Book of the Ones (Ekakanipāta), Chapter Three — Iti 24 of 27 in this book
Setting
No location or interlocutor is given — only the Itivuttaka’s stock opening and closing formula
Form
A single arresting image stated in prose, then a longer verse that names the mountain, then pivots to the four noble truths
Length
~1 minute to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a vivid image plus a compact reference to the four noble truths

Why this discourse, twenty-fourth

Some teachings argue their point; this one simply makes a number too large to hold in the mind. A single person, wandering through just one eon of repeated birth and death, would leave behind a pile of bones the size of an actual mountain — and that mountain, the verse insists, is not a figure of speech but a real, nameable peak. Having made saṃsāra’s scale felt rather than merely stated, the discourse then turns, in its final lines, to how that scale can be brought to an end.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

One person, transmigrating for a single eon, would amass a heap of bones as large as Mount Vepulla itself — and yet, the verse continues, whoever sees the four noble truths clearly will make an end of all this suffering within at most seven more lives.

A number made physical

The prose statement is blunt and almost clinical: “one person roaming and transmigrating for an eon would amass a heap of bones the size of this Mount Vepulla, if they were gathered together and not lost.” No simile is offered to soften it — the claim is stated as if it were simply a fact about accumulation over time. What makes it land is the phrase “if they were gathered together and not lost”: of course they are lost, scattered across countless past bodies in countless past births, which is precisely why the number is normally invisible. The discourse briefly makes it visible.

Not a metaphorical mountain

The verse takes the further, more striking step of naming the mountain specifically: Vepulla, “higher than the Vulture’s Peak, near the Mountainfold of the Magadhans.” This is a real, identifiable peak in the ring of hills surrounding Rājagaha, the ancient capital of Magadha — the same cluster of mountains, including Vulture’s Peak, that appears repeatedly across the early texts as a setting for teachings. By anchoring the image to an actual landmark the audience could have walked to, the discourse insists that this is not a poetic exaggeration but a claim about real physical accumulation, however impossible it would be to verify.

From an overwhelming image to a practical exit

Having built up this almost unbearable picture of accumulated remains, the verse does not leave the listener there. It pivots directly: “But then, with right understanding, one sees the noble truths — suffering, suffering’s origin, suffering’s transcendence, and the noble eightfold path that leads to the stilling of suffering.” The heap of bones is not presented as a permanent, inescapable feature of existence; it is the visible measure of what continues only in the absence of this specific seeing. The four truths are offered not as abstract doctrine but as the one thing capable of interrupting an otherwise unimaginably vast process.

“Seven times at most”

The closing lines promise that such a person, having roamed on at most seven more times, “makes an end of suffering, with the ending of all fetters.” This phrase — roaming on seven times at most — is the canon’s standard way of describing someone who has entered the stream leading irreversibly to liberation, guaranteed no more than seven further rebirths. Paired here with “the ending of all fetters,” which elsewhere marks the final goal itself, the verse seems less concerned with pinning down one precise technical stage than with promising a definite, bounded endpoint — seven rebirths at the very most — to a process that, moments earlier, had just been measured in mountains.

Key terms

kappa — “eon”: the vast unit of cosmic time in which even a single person’s bones are said to rival a mountain.
Vepullo pabbato — “Mount Vepulla”: a real, named peak in the ring of hills around Rājagaha, given as the literal scale of the heap of bones.
ariyasaccāni — “the noble truths”: suffering, its origin, its transcendence, and the path leading to its stilling — named here as what must be seen to end the cycle.
sattakkhattuṃ paramaṃ — “seven times at most”: the canon’s stock phrase for someone guaranteed no more than seven further rebirths.
saṃyojana — “fetters”: the bonds whose complete ending, in the verse’s final line, marks the true end of suffering.

The text

The discourse in full: the prose statement, and the verse that names the mountain before pivoting to the four noble truths. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, one person roaming and transmigrating for an eon would amass a heap of bones the size of this Mount Vepulla, if they were gathered together and not lost.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“If the bones of a single individual
for a single eon were gathered up,
they’d make a pile the size of a mountain:
so said the great seer.

§4And this is declared to be
as huge as Mount Vepulla,
higher than the Vulture’s Peak
near the Mountainfold of the Magadhans.

§5But then, with right understanding,
one sees the noble truths—
suffering, suffering’s origin,
suffering’s transcendence,
and the noble eightfold path
that leads to the stilling of suffering.

§6After roaming on seven times at most,
that individual
makes an end of suffering,
with the ending of all fetters.”

§7This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

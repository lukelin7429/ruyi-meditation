Itivuttaka · The Book of the Ones · Discourse 10

Complete Understanding of Hate

Dosapariññāsutta

Setting
None given — no place, audience, or occasion is named
Speaker
The Buddha, addressing “mendicants” collectively
Form
A one-sentence prose statement, restated by a six-line verse
Position
Book of the Ones, Chapter One, Iti 10 — hate revisited under the “complete understanding” formula, closing this chapter
Length
under 1 minute to read
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — simple wording; useful as a capstone for the whole chapter

Why this discourse closes the chapter

Hate first appeared at Iti 2, given up in exchange for non-return. Here, at the close of this opening chapter, it returns for a second treatment — directly known, completely understood, grown dispassionate toward, and given up, in order to end suffering — completing a pattern shared with greed at Iti 1 and Iti 9. With this sutta, the chapter’s ten teachings complete a clear, deliberate arc.

Reading guide

Closing a matched pair

Iti 9 revisited greed; Iti 10 revisits hate. Together they complete a symmetry: of the six factors named in Iti 1–6 (greed, hate, delusion, anger, disdain, conceit), exactly two — greed and hate — along with conceit, are given a second, more demanding treatment before this chapter ends. Delusion, anger, and disdain are not revisited. Whether that omission is meaningful or simply reflects how the chapter happened to be assembled, the text itself does not say — but the pattern is there for a careful reader to notice.

Same reused verse strategy

As with greed at Iti 9, the verse given here is identical, word for word, to the verse already used for hate at Iti 2: “When overcome by hate, beings go to a bad place...” The prose teaching has been upgraded to the fourfold “complete understanding” formula; the verse is carried over exactly. This is the second instance of the same pattern seen at Iti 9, reinforcing the idea that these verses function as freestanding, general statements rather than as text bound tightly to one specific surrounding formula.

The chapter’s arc, looking back

Read start to finish, this opening chapter moves from six single-factor teachings built around the promise of non-return (Iti 1–6), through a discourse establishing a fuller, more demanding fourfold method applied first to the totality of experience itself (Iti 7), and finally to three specific factors revisited under that fuller method (Iti 8, 9, 10). The chapter’s shape is not accidental repetition; it is a structured intensification, ending not with a new list of defilements but with a deeper way of addressing three already named.

What comes next

The next discourse, Iti 11, begins the second chapter of the Book of the Ones, moving beyond the roots and near-roots examined so far to new territory — a natural point to pause and take stock of what this first chapter, taken as a whole, has actually offered: not a long narrative, but ten short, precisely matched lessons in how a small number of core obstacles are named, understood, and let go of.

Key terms

dosa — “hate”: the factor first named at Iti 2, examined here a second time under the fuller “complete understanding” formula
pariññā — “complete understanding”: the deeper knowing this sutta requires of hate, beyond the simple “give it up” instruction of Iti 2
the reused verse — this sutta’s verse is identical, word for word, to the verse given for hate at Iti 2, matching the same pattern seen with greed at Iti 9
dukkhassa anta — “the end of suffering”: the reward named here, in place of the non-return promised at Iti 2 for the same factor
the chapter’s arc — Iti 1–6 (single factors, non-return) → Iti 7 (the all, the fourfold method) → Iti 8–10 (conceit, greed, hate revisited, ending suffering)

The text

The discourse in full: the opening frame, the Buddha’s one-sentence teaching, the verse that restates it (identical in wording to the verse given for hate at Iti 2), and the closing frame. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The teaching

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, without directly knowing and completely understanding hate, without dispassion for it and giving it up, you can’t end suffering. By directly knowing and completely understanding hate, having dispassion for it and giving it up, you can end suffering.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

The verse

§3“When overcome by hate
beings go to a bad place.
Having rightly understood that hate,
the discerning give it up.
Once they’ve given it up,
they never return to this world.”

§4This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

Itivuttaka · The Book of the Threes · Iti 82

The Cry of the Gods

“This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.”

Setting
None given — no place, audience, or occasion is named
Form
A prose statement naming three occasions of a divine "cry," followed by a three-stanza verse of praise
Length
~1 minute to read
Book
The Book of the Threes (Iti 60–99), Chapter Four
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — simple in structure, built around a single sustained battle metaphor

Why this discourse, eighty-second

Three moments in a mendicant's life, this discourse says, are dramatic enough that the gods themselves cry out — not out of alarm, but recognition. The image running through the entire text is martial: going forth is joining a battle, sustained meditation practice is fighting that battle, and awakening is winning it. It's an unusual register for a tradition often associated with quietism, and worth taking at face value: the practice is described here as a real contest, with a real opponent, and a real victory to be had.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

On three occasions — going forth, developing the qualities that lead to awakening, and finally realizing freedom — the gods cry out in recognition, because each stage represents real progress in a battle against Māra that the verse insists has actually been won.

Three cries, one escalating battle

The prose section lists the three occasions in strict escalation. First, when a noble disciple “shaves off their hair and beard, dresses in ocher robes, and goes forth from the lay life to homelessness” — the standard formula for ordination throughout the canon — the gods cry: “This noble disciple intends to join battle with Māra!” The going-forth itself is framed as a declaration of intent, nothing more; the fight hasn't started yet. Second, when that disciple actually “meditates pursuing the development of the seven qualities on the side of awakening,” the cry shifts tense: “This noble disciple is joining battle with Māra!” Now the engagement is underway. Third, when the disciple “realizes the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom in this very life,” ending the defilements through direct insight, the cry completes the arc: “This noble disciple has won victory in battle, establishing himself as foremost in battle!”

Who is Māra here?

The text doesn't pause to explain Māra, treating the figure as already familiar — the personification of death, temptation, and everything that keeps beings bound to repeated rebirth. The verse makes the identification explicit: the disciple has “defeated the army of death,” and their liberation is “unhindered” because “they see nothing in them by means of which they might fall under the sway of Death.” The battle, in other words, is not against an external army but against everything in the disciple's own mind that death and rebirth could still get a grip on. Winning means becoming a target death can no longer find purchase on.

Why the gods, and why a cry?

The detail that it is specifically the gods who cry out — and that they do so “on occasion,” not constantly — signals that these three moments are understood, even from a heavenly vantage point, as genuinely remarkable. The verse extends this further: the gods don't just cry out, they pay homage, addressing the victorious disciple directly as “O thoroughbred” and marveling that a battle so hard to win has actually been won. This is one of relatively few Itivuttaka passages where non-human beings appear at all within the teaching itself (rather than merely as its audience elsewhere in the canon), and their appearance here functions as a kind of independent confirmation: even beings with vastly longer lives and greater power recognize what the disciple has achieved as extraordinary.

Three stages practitioners can locate themselves within

Read plainly, this discourse offers three checkpoints rather than a single finish line: the decision to leave lay life and commit to practice, the sustained work of actually developing the qualities that lead toward awakening, and the completion of that work in liberating insight. Each stage is treated as worth its own recognition, not merely as a preliminary to the next. For any practitioner, the middle stage — the ongoing cultivation, still underway, not yet finished — is where most of a life is actually spent, and the discourse validates it as already “joining battle,” not merely preparation for a fight that hasn't begun.

Key terms

Māra — personification of death and the forces that bind beings to repeated existence; the unnamed opponent throughout this discourse's battle imagery.
going forth — shaving hair and beard, donning ocher robes, and leaving lay life for homelessness; the first of the three occasions, marked here as merely the declared intent to fight.
the seven qualities on the side of awakening — the qualities a disciple develops during the "ongoing battle" stage; traditionally identified with the seven factors of awakening (bojjhaṅga), part of the larger set of thirty-seven qualities said to support awakening.
undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom — the discourse's description of full awakening, realized "in this very life" through the ending of defilements by one's own direct insight.
the army of death — the verse's image for what has been defeated by the one whose liberation is unhindered, because there is nothing left in them for death to take hold of.

The text

The discourse in full: the three occasions on which the gods cry out, and the three-stanza verse of praise that follows. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, these three cries are uttered among the gods on occasion. What three?

When a noble disciple shaves off their hair and beard, dresses in ocher robes, and goes forth from the lay life to homelessness, the gods cry out: ‘This noble disciple intends to join battle with Māra!’ This is the first occasion a cry is uttered among the gods.

§3Furthermore, when a noble disciple meditates pursuing the development of the seven qualities on the side of awakening, the gods cry out: ‘This noble disciple is joining battle with Māra!’ This is the second occasion a cry is uttered among the gods.

§4Furthermore, when a noble disciple realizes the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom in this very life, and they live having realized it with their own insight due to the ending of defilements, the gods cry out: ‘This noble disciple has won victory in battle, establishing himself as foremost in battle!’ This is the third occasion a cry is uttered among the gods. These are the three cries that are uttered among the gods on occasion.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§5–6“Seeing the winner of the battle—
a disciple of the Buddha,
great of heart, intrepid—
even the deities revere them:

‘Homage to you, O thoroughbred!
You won a battle hard to win!
Having defeated the army of death,
your liberation is unhindered.’

§7And so the deities revere the one,
who has achieved their heart’s desire.
For they see nothing in them by means of which
they might fall under the sway of Death.”

§8This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

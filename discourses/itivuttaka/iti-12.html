Itivuttaka · The Book of the Ones · Chapter Two

Complete Understanding of Anger

The Book of the Ones, Chapter Two

Audience
Mendicants in general — no named individual, no location, no occasion is given
Form
A single terse prose statement followed by a four-couplet verse restating the identical point
Length
Under a minute to read
Series
The sixth of seven “complete understanding” discourses (Iti 7–13), repeating the same formula used for delusion immediately before it
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — plain and very short, though the claim it makes is total

Why this discourse, twelfth

This discourse repeats, almost word for word, the discourse just before it — only the defilement named has changed, from delusion to anger. That repetition is not filler. Reading the “complete understanding” set as a run, rather than picking out any single entry, is part of how the Itivuttaka makes its point: the same total demand applies again and again, defilement after defilement, without exception.

Reading guide

The same claim, a different target

Set this discourse beside Iti 11 and the relationship is immediate: identical opening and closing attributions, an identical two-part demand (“directly knowing and completely understanding… dispassion for it and giving it up”), and an identical verse shape (overcome → bad place; understood and given up → never return). Only one word changes across the whole discourse: delusion becomes anger. The Itivuttaka is willing to spend an entire discourse on what is, structurally, a single substitution — because the point isn’t variety of expression, it’s coverage. Each defilement gets its own full statement, not a footnote to another one.

Anger among the fetters

Anger (kodha) is not one of the three classic unwholesome roots (greed, hate, delusion) treated as its own trio elsewhere in this same chapter; it belongs instead to a wider family of “secondary” corruptions of the mind — anger, hostility, denigration, rivalry, envy, stinginess, and the like — that show up together in lists throughout the canon. This discourse and the one following it (on disdain) draw on that broader family, extending the “complete understanding” treatment beyond the three roots to name specific, recognizable states of mind that a practitioner is likely to catch in themselves directly.

A minor irregularity, left as found

One small textual detail is worth noting honestly rather than silently smoothing over: in the source text, the discourse’s closing quotation mark after “you can end suffering” is missing where a matching mark appears in the otherwise identical Iti 11 and Iti 13. It doesn’t change the meaning, and this reading guide reproduces the text exactly as it was received rather than silently correcting it.

The verse: the same shape again

As in Iti 11, the verse doesn’t define anger or describe what it feels like; it states consequences. Overcome by anger, “beings go to a bad place.” Once anger is rightly understood by “the discerning” and given up, “they never return to this world” — the same total claim of an end to the cycle of rebirth, not merely an improved one.

Reading it beside its neighbors

Taken with Iti 9, 10, 11, and 13, this discourse is one entry in a five-part run applying an identical formula to greed, hate, delusion, anger, and disdain in turn. Read on its own, it risks looking repetitive; read as part of the set, the repetition is the argument — no defilement is exempt from the same demand.

Key terms

kodha — anger, the subject of this discourse; one of a family of “secondary” mental corruptions distinct from the three root defilements.
pariññā — “complete understanding,” the same exhaustive knowing demanded in Iti 11, now applied to anger.
virāga — “dispassion,” paired again with giving up as the second required condition.
duggati — “a bad place,” the destination named for those overcome by anger.
the repeated formula — the discourse’s near-total identity with Iti 11 and Iti 13, differing only in which defilement is named.

The text

The discourse in full. Note that the source text’s closing quotation mark is missing after “suffering” in the second paragraph below — reproduced here exactly as received rather than silently corrected. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, without directly knowing and completely understanding anger, without dispassion for it and giving it up, you can’t end suffering. By directly knowing and completely understanding anger, having dispassion for it and giving it up, you can end suffering.

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“When overcome by anger
beings go to a bad place.
Having rightly understood that anger,
the discerning give it up.
Once they’ve given it up,
they never return to this world.”

§4This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

Itivuttaka · Discourse 29

Living in Happiness

Sukhavihārasutta

Collection
Book of the Twos (Dukanipāta), Chapter One — Iti 29 of this book
Setting
No location or interlocutor is given — only the Itivuttaka’s stock opening and closing formula
Form
A prose statement naming two qualities, then a two-quatrain verse restating the same point — term for term, the reverse of the discourse immediately before it
Length
< 1 minute to read
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — short, direct, and best read alongside its companion

Why this discourse, twenty-ninth

Read on its own, this discourse is a simple, almost cheerful claim: guard the senses, eat in moderation, and life goes well, now and after death. Read immediately after Iti 28, which it follows without a break, it is something more exact than that — the same sentence, the same structure, the same six sense doors, with every single term flipped from its negative to its positive form.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A mendicant possessing two qualities — guarding the doors of the senses, and moderation in eating — lives happily in this very life, “without distress, anguish, and fever,” and can expect a good rebirth when the body breaks up after death.

The deliberate mirror of Iti 28

This discourse cannot really be read in isolation from the one immediately before it. Every element of Iti 28’s description of an unhappy life reappears here in reversed form: “not guarding the sense doors” becomes “guarding the sense doors”; “eating too much” becomes “moderation in eating”; “distress, anguish, and fever” becomes “without distress, anguish, and fever”; a bad rebirth becomes a good one. Even the verse’s six sense doors — eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind — are listed in the same order, now described as “well guarded” rather than left unguarded. This exact, line-by-line reversal is itself worth noticing as a deliberate literary device, not an accident of compilation: the Itivuttaka uses this same technique elsewhere in this very chapter — Iti 20 and Iti 21, a corrupted mind and a pure mind, form an identical matched pair earlier in the collection.

Two ordinary disciplines, not a rare attainment

It is worth being honest about how modest these two qualities are. Neither is a description of deep meditative absorption, nor of some special insight into ultimate truths; both are basic, describable behaviors — restraint at the six sense doors, and simply eating in moderation. The discourse’s claim is correspondingly modest and concrete: these two ordinary disciplines, sustained, are said to be sufficient on their own to make the difference between a life of continuous inner “burning” and a life free of it.

“Not burning in body, not burning in mind”

The verse’s closing image mirrors Iti 28’s precisely: where that discourse ended with a person “burning in body, burning in mind, by day or by night,” this one ends with someone “not burning in body, not burning in mind, by day or by night” — living, instead, in happiness. The parallel is exact enough that reading the two verses side by side is close to reading a single poem with its terms inverted partway through.

Reading the pair together

Taken as a matched set, Iti 28 and Iti 29 make a case that is easy to state but not always easy to act on: that ordinary, unglamorous restraint at the senses and at the table is not a minor detail of monastic discipline, but one of the more direct levers available for whether a life, day to day, is lived in suffering or in happiness — independent of anything more dramatic.

Key terms

indriyesu guttadvāratā — “guarding the doors of the senses”: the positive counterpart to Iti 28’s “not guarding,” the first of the two named qualities here.
bhojane mattaññutā — “moderation in eating”: the second quality, the positive counterpart to Iti 28’s “eating too much.”
sukha vihāra — “dwelling, or living, in happiness”: this discourse’s title and its stated present-life consequence.
avighātaṃ, anupāyāsaṃ, apariḷāhaṃ — “without distress, anguish, and fever”: the mirror-image of Iti 28’s three-part description of suffering.
a matched pair — the discourse’s defining feature: read with Iti 28, it forms a term-for-term reversal, a device the Itivuttaka also uses for Iti 20 and Iti 21 earlier in the collection.

The text

The discourse in full: the prose naming the two qualities, and the verse describing their felt result — the precise positive mirror of Iti 28. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, when a mendicant has two qualities they live happily in this very life—without distress, anguish, and fever—and when the body breaks up, after death, they can expect a good rebirth. What two? Guarding the sense doors and moderation in eating. When a mendicant possesses these two qualities they live happily in this very life—without distress, anguish, and fever—and when the body breaks up, after death, they can expect a good rebirth.”

The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“Eye, ear, nose,
tongue, body, and likewise mind:
a mendicant who makes these
sense doors well guarded—

§4eating in moderation,
restrained in the sense faculties—
reaps happiness
both physical and mental.

§5Not burning in body,
not burning in mind,
by day or by night
such a person lives in happiness.”

§6This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

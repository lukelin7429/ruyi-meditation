Itivuttaka · Discourse 53

Feelings (2nd)

The Book of the Threes, Chapter One

Setting
None given — like almost every Itivuttaka discourse, no place, audience, or occasion is named
Form
A prose statement giving a specific instruction for each of the three feelings, followed by a verse restating the point
Segments
20 (iti53:1.1–5.1)
Length
Under 1 minute to read
Parallel
No complete Chinese Itivuttaka survives for comparison; no specific parallel is asserted here
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — short, but each of its three instructions repays careful thought, especially the claim that pleasant feeling “should be seen as suffering”

Why this discourse, fifty-third

Where Iti 52 named the three feelings and applied a general formula, Iti 53 gets specific: it gives one instruction per feeling — pleasant feeling should be seen as suffering, painful feeling as a dart, neutral feeling as impermanent — and ties correct application of all three to cutting off craving and making an end of suffering. It is one of this collection’s clearest single-paragraph statements of how insight actually operates: not by adding a new feeling, but by seeing the three that are already there correctly.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The same three feelings named in Iti 52 — pleasant, painful, neutral — are here given one instruction each: see pleasant feeling as suffering, painful feeling as a dart, neutral feeling as impermanent, and in doing so, cut off craving and make an end of suffering.

Three feelings, three specific instructions

This is the discourse’s real content, and it rewards being read slowly. Painful feeling as a “dart” is the least surprising of the three — pain hurts, and the canon elsewhere develops this exact image (a second, mental “dart” that an untrained mind adds to an already painful first one). Neutral feeling as “impermanent” is also fairly direct: the peaceful, easy-to-overlook feeling is still a conditioned, passing state, not a stable resting place. The genuinely demanding instruction is the first: pleasant feeling “should be seen as suffering.” Not painful, not neutral — pleasant. The point is not that pleasure feels bad while it lasts, but that anything which must end, and which is chased and clung to because it will end, functions as suffering precisely because of that impermanence and that clinging.

What seeing this rightly accomplishes

The discourse doesn’t leave the three instructions as abstractions; it states a result. A mendicant who has seen the three feelings this way is called someone who “sees rightly, has cut off craving, cast off the fetters, and by rightly comprehending conceit has made an end of suffering.” Three specific results are packed together: craving cut off (the response to pleasant feeling), the fetters cast off (the bonds that keep one bound to further rebirth), and conceit — the subtle sense of “I am” that can survive even after craving has weakened — rightly comprehended. The verse closes by calling this “slipping one’s yokes,” using the image of a draft animal freed from what has been binding it to its labor.

A pair with Iti 52

Read together, Iti 52 and Iti 53 move from the general to the specific: Iti 52 says a disciple understands feeling, its cause, its ending, and the path to that ending; Iti 53 spells out exactly what that understanding looks like, feeling by feeling. Neither discourse asks a practitioner to stop feeling pleasure, pain, or neutrality — only to stop mistaking any of the three for something to be grasped, resisted, or ignored.

Key terms

pleasant feeling as suffering — the discourse’s most demanding instruction: not that pleasure feels bad, but that its impermanence and the clinging it invites make it function as suffering.
painful feeling as a dart — the image at the center of SN 36.6, where a trained mind is said to feel only the first, unavoidable dart of pain, not a self-inflicted second one.
neutral feeling as impermanent — the easiest feeling to overlook, named here as no more stable a resting place than the other two.
conceit — the subtle sense of “I am”; the discourse specifies that this too must be “rightly comprehended,” not only craving cut off.
“slipped their yokes” — the verse’s closing image, of a draft animal freed from what has bound it to further labor.

The text

The discourse in full — its longest single sentence in this pair, given complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha, the Perfected One: that is what I heard.

§2“Mendicants, there are these three feelings. What three? Pleasant, painful, and neutral feeling. Pleasant feeling should be seen as suffering. Painful feeling should be seen as a dart. Neutral feeling should be seen as impermanent. When a mendicant has seen these three feelings in this way, they’re called a noble mendicant who sees rightly, has cut off craving, cast off the fetters, and by rightly comprehending conceit has made an end of suffering.” The Buddha spoke this matter. On this it is said:

§3“A mendicant who sees pleasure as pain,
and suffering as a dart,
and that peaceful, neutral feeling
as impermanent

§4sees rightly.
And when freed in regards to that,
that peaceful sage, with perfect insight,
has truly slipped their yokes.”

§5This too is a matter that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

Udāna · Ud 6.1

Surrendering the Life Force

Āyusaṅkhārossajjanasutta

Setting
Near Vesālī: an alms round in the city, then the Cāpāla Shrine, one of several shrines the Buddha names as “lovely”
Speakers
The Buddha, Venerable Ānanda, and Māra the Wicked
Form
Narrative: a threefold hint given and missed, an uninvited visitor, a short exchange, then a four-line verse
Length
~4 minutes to read
Parallel
The same episode is narrated at greater length within DN 16, the Mahāparinibbāna Sutta, as part of the Buddha’s final year
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — short in length, heavy in what it describes

Why this heartfelt saying

Ud 6.1 opens the sixth chapter of the Udāna with the single heaviest moment the whole collection contains: the passage in which, by the text’s own account, the Buddha consciously and deliberately fixes the timing of his own death. Nothing here is dramatized. There is no farewell address in this short text, no crowd, no final instruction — only an afternoon at a shrine outside Vesālī, three missed chances, an uninvited visitor, and four lines of verse.

Reading guide

An ordinary morning, an unusual invitation

The sutta opens the way a great many suttas open: the Buddha robes up, takes his bowl, and goes into Vesālī for alms. Nothing marks this morning as different from countless others — until, back from the round, he tells Ānanda to bring his sitting cloth: “Let’s go to the Cāpāla Shrine for the day’s meditation.” Ānanda follows him there and they sit down. Only then does the text signal that something else is happening.

The hint, given three times

Seated at the shrine, the Buddha remarks that Vesālī is lovely, and names six of its shrines as lovely in turn — the Udena, Gotamaka, Seven Maidens, Many Sons, Sārandada, and Cāpāla. He then says that anyone who has fully developed the four bases of psychic power could, if they wished, “live for the proper lifespan or what’s left of it,” and that he himself has that mastery. The text is explicit that this is deliberate: Ānanda “didn’t get it, even though the Buddha dropped such an obvious hint, such a clear sign.” He is expected to beg the Buddha to remain — and does not. The Buddha repeats the same words a second and a third time. Each time, the same silence. The text offers its own explanation for why: Ānanda’s mind, it says, “was as if possessed by Māra.”

Māra’s request

The Buddha then simply dismisses Ānanda — “Go now, Ānanda, at your convenience” — who withdraws to sit under a nearby tree. Almost immediately, Māra the Wicked appears and asks, in near-identical language repeated across four conditions, that the Buddha “now be fully extinguished.” His case is built entirely on the Buddha’s own earlier words: long ago, the Buddha had said he would not pass away until he had monk, nun, layman, and laywoman disciples who were competent, learned, and able to teach and defend the Dhamma, and until the spiritual path itself was “successful and prosperous, extensive, popular, widespread.” Māra states, and the Buddha does not dispute, that every one of these conditions now holds. It is worth pausing on how unusual this scene is: elsewhere in the canon Māra typically works to obstruct or undermine the teaching’s success. Here, ironically, it is the very success of that teaching — cited by Māra himself — that supplies his argument for asking the Buddha to leave the world now rather than later.

“Surrendering the life force”

The Buddha’s reply is brief: “Relax, Wicked One. The full extinguishment of the Realized One will be soon. Three months from now the Realized One will be fully extinguished.” The text then states, without elaboration, that at the Cāpāla Shrine the Buddha, “mindful and aware, surrendered the life force” — and that a great earthquake and thunder followed. The text does not explain the mechanics of this act, and this reading guide will not invent an explanation it doesn’t give. What it reports is simple and exact: a deliberate, aware act, performed immediately after the exchange with Māra, that fixes the countdown to the Buddha’s parinibbāna at three months. Readers wanting the fuller narrative context — what happens before and after, across the Buddha’s final months — will find this same episode retold within the much longer DN 16.

The heartfelt saying

The verse that closes the sutta is dense even by the Udāna’s standards: “Comparing the incomparable with the creation of prolonged life, the sage surrendered the life force. Happy inside, serene, he shattered self-creation like a suit of armor.” Whatever else it does, the verse frames the moment as a weighing — “comparing the incomparable” — set against a choice for prolonged life, and describes the outcome not as loss but as a kind of unburdening: “self-creation,” shattered like armor removed, met with an inner happiness the text calls serene rather than grim. It is a striking image to close on: something usually worn for protection, broken open, from the inside, by someone at peace.

Key terms

āyusaṅkhāra — “life force” or “life-formation” — the vitality the text says the Buddha “surrendered” (ossaji) at the Cāpāla Shrine, fixing the interval to his full extinguishment at three months.
cattāro iddhipādā — the four bases of psychic power — named as what makes it possible for one who has mastered them to “live for the proper lifespan or what’s left of it,” if they wish. The text states the Buddha had mastered them but does not enumerate them individually here.
Cāpāla Cetiya — the Cāpāla Shrine, one of six shrines near Vesālī the Buddha calls “lovely” in the same breath as he drops his hint, and the location of the entire episode.
Māra — “the Wicked One,” who here does something he rarely does elsewhere in the canon: formally requests the Buddha’s death, citing the Buddha’s own earlier conditions for it as now fulfilled.
parinibbāna — rendered here as “full extinguishment” — the state Māra asks for five times over, and which the Buddha confirms will occur “three months from now.”

The text

The full episode: the threefold hint at the Cāpāla Shrine, Māra’s request, the Buddha’s reply, and the heartfelt saying that follows. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Vesālī, at the Great Wood, in the hall with the peaked roof. Then the Buddha robed up in the morning and, taking his bowl and robe, entered Vesālī for alms. Then, after the meal, on his return from almsround, he addressed Venerable Ānanda: “Ānanda, get your sitting cloth. Let’s go to the Cāpāla Shrine for the day’s meditation.”

§2“Yes, sir,” replied Ānanda. Taking his sitting cloth he followed behind the Buddha. Then the Buddha went up to the Cāpāla Shrine, where he sat on the seat spread out. When he was seated he said to Venerable Ānanda:

§3“Ānanda, Vesālī is lovely. And the Udena, Gotamaka, Seven Maidens, Many Sons, Sārandada, and Cāpāla Shrines are all lovely. Whoever has developed and cultivated the four bases of psychic power—made them a vehicle and a basis, kept them up, consolidated them, and properly implemented them—may, if they wish, live for the proper lifespan or what’s left of it. The Realized One has developed and cultivated the four bases of psychic power, made them a vehicle and a basis, kept them up, consolidated them, and properly implemented them. If he wished, the Realized One could live for the proper lifespan or what’s left of it.”

§4But Ānanda didn’t get it, even though the Buddha dropped such an obvious hint, such a clear sign. He didn’t beg the Buddha, “Sir, may the Blessed One please remain for the proper lifespan! May the Holy One please remain for the proper lifespan! That would be for the welfare and happiness of the people, out of sympathy for the world, for the benefit, welfare, and happiness of gods and humans.” For his mind was as if possessed by Māra. For a second time … and for a third time, the Buddha said to Ānanda:

§5“Ānanda, Vesālī is lovely. And the Udena, Gotamaka, Seven Maidens, Many Sons, Sārandada, and Cāpāla Shrines are all lovely. Whoever has developed and cultivated the four bases of psychic power—made them a vehicle and a basis, kept them up, consolidated them, and properly implemented them—may, if they wish, live for the proper lifespan or what’s left of it. The Realized One has developed and cultivated the four bases of psychic power, made them a vehicle and a basis, kept them up, consolidated them, and properly implemented them. If he wished, the Realized One could live for the proper lifespan or what’s left of it.”

§6But Ānanda didn’t get it, even though the Buddha dropped such an obvious hint, such a clear sign. He didn’t beg the Buddha, “Sir, may the Blessed One please remain for the proper lifespan! May the Holy One please remain for the proper lifespan! That would be for the welfare and happiness of the people, out of sympathy for the world, for the benefit, welfare, and happiness of gods and humans.” For his mind was as if possessed by Māra.

§7Then the Buddha said to Venerable Ānanda, “Go now, Ānanda, at your convenience.” “Yes, sir,” replied Ānanda. He rose from his seat, bowed, and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on his right, before sitting at the root of a tree close by.

§8And then, not long after Ānanda had left, Māra the Wicked went up to the Buddha, stood to one side, and said to him:

§9–10“May the Blessed One now be fully extinguished! May the Holy One now be fully extinguished! Now is the time for the full extinguishment of the Buddha. Sir, you once made this statement: ‘Wicked One, I shall not be fully extinguished until I have monk disciples who are competent, educated, self-assured, learned, have memorized the teachings, and practice in line with the teaching. Not until they practice properly, living in line with the teaching. Not until they’ve learned their own tradition, and explain, teach, assert, establish, disclose, analyze, and make it clear. Not until they can legitimately and completely refute the doctrines of others that come up, and teach with a demonstrable basis.’ Today you do have such monk disciples. May the Blessed One now be fully extinguished! May the Holy One now be fully extinguished! Now is the time for the full extinguishment of the Buddha. Sir, you once made this statement: ‘Wicked One, I shall not be fully extinguished until I have nun disciples who are competent, educated, self-assured, learned …’ … Today you do have such nun disciples.

§11–12‘Wicked One, I shall not be fully extinguished until I have layman disciples who are competent, educated, self-assured, learned …’ Today you do have such layman disciples. ‘Wicked One, I shall not be fully extinguished until I have laywoman disciples who are competent, educated, self-assured, learned …’ Today you do have such laywoman disciples. May the Blessed One now be fully extinguished! May the Holy One now be fully extinguished! Now is the time for the full extinguishment of the Buddha.

§13Sir, you once made this statement: ‘I shall not be fully extinguished until my spiritual path is successful and prosperous, extensive, popular, widespread, and well proclaimed wherever there are gods and humans.’ Today your spiritual path is successful and prosperous, extensive, popular, widespread, and well proclaimed wherever there are gods and humans. May the Blessed One now be fully extinguished! May the Holy One now be fully extinguished! Now is the time for the full extinguishment of the Buddha.”

§14When this was said, the Buddha said to Māra, “Relax, Wicked One. The full extinguishment of the Realized One will be soon. Three months from now the Realized One will be fully extinguished.”

§15So at the Cāpāla Shrine the Buddha, mindful and aware, surrendered the life force. When he did so there was a great earthquake, awe-inspiring and hair-raising, and thunder cracked the sky.

§16Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§17“Comparing the incomparable with the creation of prolonged life, the sage surrendered the life force. Happy inside, serene, he shattered self-creation like a suit of armor.”

How many times does the Buddha give Ānanda the hint about being able to live out the full lifespan?

What does Ānanda fail to do each time?

Who approaches the Buddha soon after Ānanda withdraws?
Correct: C. Māra appears, stands to one side, and makes his request.

How does the Buddha reply to Māra?

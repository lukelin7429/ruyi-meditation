Udāna · Discourse 1.8

With Saṅgāmaji

Saṅgāmajisutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speaker
The Buddha, describing what he alone sees, using clairvoyance, of the episode’s final moment
Theme
A former wife brings their child to test Saṅgāmaji’s resolve three times; his silence and the Buddha’s verse locate true brahmin-hood in equanimity toward the hardest possible bond
Length
~1 minute to read
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — emotionally the most demanding discourse in this thread

The hardest test in this thread

Every discourse so far in this run has tested the meaning of “brahmin” against something — a ritual, a name, poverty, fear. This one tests it against a bond most people would consider the very last thing to give up: a former spouse, and one’s own child, placed directly in front of you.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

When Venerable Saṅgāmaji’s former wife brings their young son to him three times, asking him to raise the boy, and he answers each time only with silence, the Buddha — watching, unseen, with clairvoyance — describes this as an equanimity neither glad at her coming nor grieved at her leaving, and declares it the substance of brahmin-hood.

A wife's plea, met three times with silence

Saṅgāmaji has arrived at Sāvatthī to see the Buddha. His former wife, hearing of his arrival, comes to the Jeta Grove with their young son and finds him seated at the root of a tree in meditation. Three times, in almost identical language, she says: “Ascetic, please raise this little boy.” Three times, the text tells us plainly, “Saṅgāmaji kept silent.” The threefold repetition — a recognizable narrative device used elsewhere in the canon to mark deliberateness — makes clear this isn’t hesitation or shock; it is a sustained, chosen non-response.

The child set down, then taken back

When silence doesn’t move him, she escalates: she puts the boy down directly in front of Saṅgāmaji, saying, “This is your child, ascetic. Please raise him.” Even now, the text says, “Saṅgāmaji neither looked at the boy nor spoke to him.” This is the discourse’s hardest moment to sit with — a father, physically confronted with his own son, offering nothing at all in response, not even a glance.

An unseen witness

What happens next is reported only because the Buddha is said to see it with “clairvoyance that is purified and superhuman”: the mother, walking away, looks back, sees Saṅgāmaji still ignoring the boy, concludes — reasonably, from what she can see — “this ascetic doesn’t even want his child,” and returns to pick him up before leaving for good. No one else in the story witnesses this return. We know of it only through the Buddha’s report, layered into the very telling of the episode.

The verse's answer: neither glad nor grieved

The Buddha’s heartfelt saying doesn’t comment on the boy’s welfare or resolve the story’s obvious emotional difficulty. It states, simply: “when she came he was not glad, when she left he did not grieve. Victorious in battle, freed from chains, that’s who I declare a brahmin.” The equanimity described is total — extending equally to her arrival and her departure, to gaining the sight of his child and losing it again.

A hard case, deliberately chosen

It is worth asking why this particular scenario, of everything that could illustrate non-attachment, was chosen for this thread. An abstract stranger, or a minor inconvenience, would have made the same doctrinal point far more comfortably. Placing a former spouse and one’s own child at the center instead means the verse’s claim is being tested against the single hardest case its author could plausibly construct — which is presumably exactly why it belongs here, in a chapter otherwise populated with taunting spirits and streets full of the poor.

Key terms

Saṅgāmaji — the mendicant at the center of this discourse, whose silence toward his former wife and son forms its central scene.
the threefold repetition — a recognizable narrative convention in the canon, here used to mark that Saṅgāmaji’s silence is deliberate, not merely surprised.
“victorious in battle, freed from chains” — the verse’s closing imagery, describing equanimity as a kind of hard-won victory rather than mere indifference.
clairvoyance (here, the Buddha’s) — the means by which the mother’s return for the child is witnessed and reported at all, unseen by anyone else in the story.
“not glad… not grieved” — the verse’s precise description of equanimity, applied equally to the wife’s arrival and her departure.

The text

The discourse in full: the setting, the threefold plea and silence, the unseen return, and the closing verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The setting

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time Venerable Saṅgāmaji had arrived at Sāvatthī to see the Buddha. His former wife heard that he had arrived, and went to the Jetavana, taking their boy.

Three requests, met with silence

§2Now at that time Venerable Saṅgāmaji was sitting at the root of a tree for the day’s meditation. Then his former wife went up to him and said, “Ascetic, please raise this little boy.” When she said this, Saṅgāmaji kept silent.

§3For a second time she said, “Ascetic, please raise this little boy.” For a second time, Saṅgāmaji kept silent.

§4For a third time she said, “Ascetic, please raise this little boy.” For a third time, Saṅgāmaji kept silent.

The child set down, then taken back

§5Then she put down the boy in front of Saṅgāmaji, saying, “This is your child, ascetic. Please raise him.”

§6But Saṅgāmaji neither looked at the boy nor spoke to him. Then his former wife went a little distance away. Looking back, she saw Saṅgāmaji ignoring the boy, and thought, “This ascetic doesn’t even want his child.” She returned to pick up the boy, then left. With clairvoyance that is purified and superhuman, the Buddha saw how Saṅgāmaji’s former wife went back for the child.

The heartfelt saying

§7Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§8“When she came he was not glad,
when she left he did not grieve.
Victorious in battle, freed from chains,
that’s who I declare a brahmin.”

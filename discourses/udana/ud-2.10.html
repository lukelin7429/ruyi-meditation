Udāna · Heartfelt Sayings 2.10

With Bhaddiya

Bhaddiyasutta

Setting
Near Anupiyā, in a mango grove
Speaker
The Buddha, questioning Venerable Bhaddiya (son of Kāṭīgodhā) after other mendicants report his behavior
Occasion
Bhaddiya, a mendicant and former king, is repeatedly heard exclaiming “Oh, what bliss!” alone in the wilderness; summoned by the Buddha, he explains why
Form
Extended narrative — rumor, summons, direct question, Bhaddiya’s own explanation — closing in a four-line verse
Length
About 3 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — closes the chapter with its most surprising reversal

Why this heartfelt saying, closing the chapter

The chapter closes with a case of mistaken assumption, resolved simply by asking. A mendicant who was once a king keeps exclaiming “Oh, what bliss!” when alone — and his fellow mendicants assume the worst. What he actually says, once asked directly, inverts the entire premise of royal security.

Reading guide

A misread exclamation

This closing discourse of the chapter turns on a misunderstanding almost resolved by simply asking. Venerable Bhaddiya, identified as “son of Kāṭīgodhā” and, the story reveals, formerly a king, is repeatedly heard — alone in the wilderness, at the foot of a tree, in an empty dwelling — exclaiming “Oh, what bliss! Oh, what bliss!” Other mendicants, overhearing this, draw what seems like the obvious conclusion: he must be dissatisfied with the spiritual life, homesick for the pleasures of royalty he gave up.

The Buddha asks directly

Rather than confronting Bhaddiya themselves or simply speculating further, the mendicants report what they’ve heard to the Buddha, who sends for him and asks him outright, twice — first whether it’s true he says this, and then why he says it. Bhaddiya’s answer overturns the mendicants’ assumption completely. As a king, he explains, his guard was “well organized within and without the royal compound, within and without the city, and within and without the country” — an elaborate, comprehensive security. And yet, guarded so thoroughly, he “remained fearful, scared, suspicious, and nervous.” Now, alone in places that would seem far more exposed and dangerous — the wilderness, an empty hut — he is none of those things: “I live relaxed, unruffled, surviving on charity, my heart free as a wild deer.”

Bliss located precisely

What makes Bhaddiya’s explanation land is its precision. He doesn’t offer a vague comparison of monastic contentment against royal luxury; he directly inverts the expected relationship between security and safety. Maximum external protection, as king, produced constant internal fear. Minimal external protection, as a homeless mendicant, produces its opposite — not because danger has vanished, but because something in how he relates to it has changed.

“Even the gods can’t see them”

The heartfelt saying. “They who hide no anger within, passed beyond any kind of existence; happy, free from fear and sorrow — even the gods can’t see them.”

The Buddha’s closing heartfelt saying doesn’t restate Bhaddiya’s story directly; it generalizes from it, and its own logic runs deeper than Bhaddiya’s account of dropping bodyguards: freedom from anger and from clinging to “existence” (bhava) itself — not merely a change of external circumstance — is what produces the fearlessness he’s describing, in a final, more radical form. The verse’s last line is genuinely strange and worth sitting with: someone “free from fear and sorrow” in this sense becomes invisible even to the gods (devā nānubhavanti dassanāya). Across the canon, gods are typically depicted as powerful and far-seeing compared to human beings; to say that even they cannot see such a person suggests a kind of freedom that has stepped entirely outside the categories — including divine ones — by which beings are normally located, watched, and known. It’s a fitting note to end this chapter’s series of ordinary human scenes — kings, snakes, grieving parents, business disputes — on: the most complete freedom described in the whole chapter is also the hardest to picture.

Key terms

Bhaddiya Kāṭīgodhāyaputta — “Bhaddiya, son of Kāṭīgodhā,” the mendicant at the center of this discourse, a former king now living as a homeless renunciant.
udānaṅ udānesi — “expressed a heartfelt sentiment,” used first of Bhaddiya’s own repeated exclamation, before the Buddha’s own closing udāna at the end.
migabhūtena cetasā — “with a heart/mind like a wild deer,” Bhaddiya’s own image for his present freedom, unguarded and unafraid.
kopa — “anger” or “resentment,” named in the closing verse as what someone free of fear and sorrow no longer hides within.
bhavābhava — “[any kind of] existence,” what the verse says such a person has gone beyond, tying Bhaddiya’s personal fearlessness to a wider release from continued existence.

The text

The heartfelt saying in full: Bhaddiya’s repeated exclamation, the mendicants’ assumption, his own explanation, and the Buddha’s closing verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1.1–1.4So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Anupiya in a mango grove. Now at that time, Venerable Bhaddiya son of Kāḷīgodhā, even in the wilderness, at the foot of a tree, or in an empty dwelling, frequently expressed this heartfelt sentiment: “Oh, what bliss! Oh, what bliss!”

§2.1–2.6Several mendicants heard him and thought, “Without a doubt, Venerable Bhaddiya leads the spiritual life dissatisfied. It’s when recalling the pleasures of royalty he formerly enjoyed as a layperson that, even in the wilderness, at the foot of a tree, or in an empty dwelling, he frequently expresses this heartfelt sentiment: ‘Oh, what bliss! Oh, what bliss!’”

§3.1Then those mendicants went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and told him what was happening.

§4.1–4.3So the Buddha addressed one of the monks, “Please, monk, in my name tell the mendicant Bhaddiya that the teacher summons him.”

§5.1–5.3“Yes, sir,” that monk replied. He went to Bhaddiya and said to him, “Reverend Bhaddiya, the teacher summons you.” “Yes, reverend,” Bhaddiya replied. He went to the Buddha, bowed, and sat down to one side. The Buddha said to him:

§6.1–6.3“Is it really true, Bhaddiya, that even in the wilderness, at the foot of a tree, or in an empty dwelling, you frequently express this heartfelt sentiment: ‘Oh, what bliss! Oh, what bliss!’?” “Yes, sir.”

§7.1–7.7“But why do you say this?” “Formerly, as a layperson ruling the land, my guard was well organized within and without the royal compound, within and without the city, and within and without the country. But although I was guarded and defended in this way, I remained fearful, scared, suspicious, and nervous. But these days, even when alone in the wilderness, at the foot of a tree, or in an empty dwelling, I’m not fearful, scared, suspicious, or nervous. I live relaxed, unruffled, surviving on charity, my heart free as a wild deer. It is for this reason that, even in the wilderness, at the foot of a tree, or in an empty dwelling, I frequently expressed this heartfelt sentiment: ‘Oh, what bliss! Oh, what bliss!’”

§8.1Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§9.1–9.4“They who hide no anger within, passed beyond any kind of existence; happy, free from fear and sorrow— even the gods can’t see them.”

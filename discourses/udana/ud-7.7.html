Udāna · Discourse 7.7

The Ending of Proliferation

Papañcakhayasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Occasion
The Buddha sits reviewing his own giving up of the judgments driven by mental proliferation — no other figure appears
Form
A brief narrative frame with no dialogue at all, closed by a four-line inspired utterance (udāna)
Length
<1 minute to read
Notable feature
A rare first-person occasion — the Buddha reflecting on his own state rather than observing someone else
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — the underlying concept, papañca, is one of the more abstract and debated terms in the early texts

Why this discourse, the Buddha alone

Every other discourse so far in this chapter has the Buddha observing someone else — a disciple meditating, mendicants clinging to pleasure, a community mocking one of its own. Here, uniquely, there is no one else in the scene at all. The Buddha simply sits, reviewing something in himself, and the resulting verse is correspondingly turned inward: not a correction, not praise for another, but a description of what it is to have nothing left in the mind that proliferates.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Sitting alone, the Buddha reviews his own complete letting go of the “judgments driven by the proliferation of perceptions” and expresses, in verse, what remains for someone who has passed beyond that entire process: a mind the world cannot scorn, because it no longer offers anything to seize on.

A discourse with no second figure

Every frame story in this chapter up to this point has involved at least one other person — a disciple, mendicants, a community. This discourse breaks that pattern entirely. The opening states only that “the Buddha was sitting reviewing his own giving up of the judgments driven by the proliferation of perceptions” — a private, internal act of reflection, witnessed by no one, reported anyway because the text’s interest is in the utterance that follows rather than any external drama.

What "proliferation" means

Papañca, translated here as “proliferation,” names the mind’s tendency to take a bare sense experience and elaborate it — spinning out judgments, associations, and a sense of “me” and “mine” that were never present in the original contact. The compound the text uses, “judgments driven by the proliferation of perceptions” (papañcasaññāsaṅkhā), names this whole chain: perception feeding proliferation, proliferation feeding judgment. This same term is treated at much greater length in MN 18, where a series of monks and finally the Buddha himself trace the entire process from raw sense contact through to the “battles, brawls, and disputes” that unchecked proliferation eventually produces.

Beyond the halter and cross-bar

The verse names the state that results when this whole chain has been given up: “there is no proliferation remaining in those who have passed beyond the halter and cross-bar.” A halter and cross-bar are restraints — farm equipment for controlling draft animals — used here as an image for whatever holds ordinary minds in place and directs them. To have passed beyond both is to be free not just of specific desires but of the whole apparatus of guided, driven thinking.

The closing lines make an unusual, almost paradoxical claim: “the sage who lives without craving is never scorned by the world with its gods.” Elsewhere in the canon the Buddha and his disciples are mocked, doubted, and opposed by plenty of people. This reading guide takes the line to describe something narrower and more precise: that a mind with nothing left to proliferate from offers the world nothing to fasten a scornful judgment onto — not that scorn never occurs, but that it cannot land where there is no remaining self-narrative to receive it.

A rare vantage point

Because this is one of the few Udāna discourses where the Buddha is both the subject and the sole figure present, it offers something the other suttas in this chapter, built around disciples, cannot: a description, in the Buddha’s own voice, of what his own completed freedom is like from the inside, rather than how it appears to an outside observer.

Key terms

papañca (proliferation) — the mind’s tendency to elaborate raw sense experience into judgments, associations, and a sense of self, treated at length in MN 18.
papañcasaññāsaṅkhā (judgments driven by the proliferation of perceptions) — the fuller compound naming the whole chain from perception through proliferation to judgment, which the Buddha here reviews having given up.
yoga and palligha (halter and cross-bar) — farm restraints for draft animals, used as an image for whatever ordinarily holds and directs the mind; the verse describes passing beyond both.
taṇhā (craving) — named directly as the trait absent from “the sage who lives without craving,” the discourse’s closing description of the awakened mind.
paccavekkhamāno (reviewing) — the same reflective term used of Koṇḍañña in Ud 7.6, here applied uniquely to the Buddha’s own reflection on himself.

The text

The discourse in full — a private moment of self-reflection, closed by a four-line inspired utterance. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A private review

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time the Buddha was sitting reviewing his own giving up of the judgments driven by the proliferation of perceptions.

§2Then, understanding his own giving up of the judgments driven by the proliferation of perceptions, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

The verse

§3“There is no proliferation remaining in those
who have passed beyond the halter and cross-bar.
The sage who lives without craving
is never scorned by the world with its gods.”

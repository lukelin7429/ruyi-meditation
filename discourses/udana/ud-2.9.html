Udāna · Heartfelt Sayings 2.9

With Visākhā

Visākhāsutta

Setting
Sāvatthī, in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s Mother, in the Eastern Monastery (Pubbārāma)
Speaker
The Buddha, addressing Visākhā
Occasion
Visākhā has some unspecified business with King Pasenadi of Kosala that did not turn out as she wanted
Form
Brief narrative and dialogue, then a compact four-line verse
Length
Under 2 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a short scene with a clear, generalizable saying

Why this heartfelt saying, ninth of the chapter

Visākhā — one of the most prominent laywomen patrons in the entire canon — arrives frustrated by an unresolved matter with King Pasenadi. The Buddha doesn’t take her side or settle the dispute; he names the shape of the frustration itself, in four lines about control, authority, and the “yokes” that bind anyone caught in something shared.

Reading guide

An unnamed frustration

The discourse doesn’t specify what business Visākhā — one of the most prominent laywomen patrons in the canon, known elsewhere for her generosity and for building the very monastery, the Eastern Monastery’s stilt longhouse, where this scene takes place — had with King Pasenadi. All the text says is that “the king’s settlement did not meet her expectations.” Whatever it was, the Buddha’s question when she arrives in the middle of the day is simple and direct: “where are you coming from in the middle of the day?” — and her answer just repeats the narrator’s own description of the situation back to him.

Control and authority, named plainly

The heartfelt saying. “All under another’s control is suffering, all under one’s own authority is pleasing; what’s shared is stressful for both parties, for yokes are hard to escape.”

The saying doesn’t address the specifics of Visākhā’s dealings with the king at all — it names the general structure of any situation like it. Being subject to someone else’s decision (paravasa) is painful regardless of the particular outcome; having authority over one’s own affairs (issariya) is what’s pleasant. And the verse goes further than a simple contrast between control and subjection: it adds that anything jointly held or shared between two parties — like Visākhā’s unresolved business, presumably involving property or an agreement with the king — is stressful for both sides, because such “yokes” are hard to escape from once entered.

Not a verdict on the king or the dispute

It would be easy to read this as taking Visākhā’s side against Pasenadi, but the verse doesn’t frame it that way. Both parties to something shared are named as stressed by it — “sādhāraṇe vihaññanti,” those with something shared are troubled — not just the party who came away dissatisfied. The teaching isn’t about who was right in this particular dealing with the king; it’s about the nature of joint or contested control itself, regardless of which side of it a person is on.

A pattern, not a solution

As with several of the shorter sayings in this chapter, the verse offers no advice about what Visākhā should do next about the king’s decision. It names the shape of her frustration — being under another’s control — and generalizes it into something anyone entangled in a shared arrangement will recognize, rather than resolving the specific dispute that prompted it.

Key terms

Visākhā Migāramātā — “Visākhā, Migāra’s mother,” one of the most prominent laywomen in the canon and a major donor associated with the Eastern Monastery where this scene is set.
paravasa — “under another’s control,” named directly as suffering (dukkha) in the verse’s opening line.
issariya — “authority” or “sovereignty [over oneself],” named as pleasing (sukha) in contrast to paravasa.
sādhāraṇa — “shared” or “held in common,” the condition the verse says is stressful for both parties involved.
yoga — “yoke,” the verse’s closing image for a shared bond or entanglement, described as “hard to escape.”

The text

The heartfelt saying in full: Visākhā’s unresolved business with the king and the Buddha’s reply. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1.1–1.4So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother in the Eastern Monastery. Now at that time Visākhā was caught up in some business with King Pasenadi. But the king’s settlement did not meet her expectations.

§2.1–2.5Then, in the middle of the day, she went to the Buddha, bowed, and sat down. The Buddha said to her: “So, Visākhā, where are you coming from in the middle of the day?” “Sir, I am caught up in some business with King Pasenadi. But the king’s settlement did not meet my expectations.”

§3.1Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§4.1–4.4“All under another’s control is suffering, all under one’s own authority is pleasing; what’s shared is stressful for both parties, for yokes are hard to escape.”

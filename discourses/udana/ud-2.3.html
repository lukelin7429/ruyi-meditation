Udāna · Heartfelt Sayings 2.3

A Stick

Daṇḍasutta

Setting
On the road between Sāvatthī and Jeta’s Grove, during the Buddha’s alms round
Speaker
The Buddha, prompted simply by seeing an event, not by any question or conversation
Occasion
The Buddha sees a group of boys hitting a snake with a stick
Form
A two-sentence narrative frame, then two mirrored four-line verses
Length
Under 2 minutes to read
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — the shortest and plainest occasion in this chapter

Why this heartfelt saying, third of the chapter

Nothing here is elaborate: the Buddha, walking for alms, sees a group of boys hitting a snake with a stick. What follows is two mirrored, four-line stanzas built on a single premise so simple it’s easy to walk past — every creature, human or not, wants the same thing you do.

Reading guide

The shortest kind of occasion

Several of the Udāna’s heartfelt sayings are triggered by a whole scene of conversation or crisis; this one needs almost none. The frame is two sentences: the Buddha, walking for alms between Sāvatthī and the Jeta Grove, “saw the boys hitting the snake.” That’s all. No dialogue, no petition, no debate. What follows is not an intervention — the text doesn’t say the Buddha stops the boys or speaks to them — it’s simply what he says afterward, “understanding this matter.”

A mirror, not a lecture

The heartfelt saying. “Creatures love happiness, so if you harm them with a stick in search of your own happiness, after death you’ll find no happiness. Creatures love happiness, so if you don’t harm them with a stick in search of your own happiness, after death you will find happiness.”

The saying’s structure is exactly symmetrical, the same four lines repeated with only the middle line flipped. There’s no scolding of the boys directly, and no threat aimed at any particular person. The verse states a general principle in the plainest possible form: whoever wants happiness for themselves and inflicts harm on others in pursuit of it undermines the very thing they’re chasing.

Love of happiness as the shared premise

What makes the logic work is the opening premise, repeated in both verses: “creatures love happiness” (sukhakāmāni bhūtāni) — not just human beings, but “creatures” generally, which is exactly the point being made about a snake being struck with a stick. If every being, human or animal, shares that same wish, then harming any of them for one’s own comfort is a direct contradiction: pursuing your own happiness while producing suffering for a being that wants happiness just as much as you do. The verse doesn’t argue that harmlessness is a rule to be obeyed; it treats it as a straightforward consequence of taking seriously what every creature already wants.

Deeds returning to their doer

The closing lines of each stanza — “after death you’ll find no happiness” and “after death you will find happiness” — place the consequence beyond the immediate scene, in the register of kamma (deeds and their results) that runs through the whole canon. The verse doesn’t specify what mechanism connects the stick to the future outcome; it simply states the shape of the pattern, cause matching consequence, harm matching harm’s absence.

Key terms

daṇḍa — “a stick” or “rod,” giving the discourse its title and the concrete image around which the verse is built.
sukhakāma — “one who loves/desires happiness,” the word used of “creatures” generally, including the snake being struck.
bhūta — “creature” or “being,” the verse’s deliberately broad term, not limited to humans.
vihiṃsati — “harms” or “hurts,” the verb describing what the boys do to the snake and what the verse warns against.
pecca — “after death,” “having passed on,” marking that the verse’s stated consequence belongs to the wider frame of rebirth and kamma.

The text

The heartfelt saying in full: a brief scene on the alms round and the two mirrored verses it prompts. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1.1–1.5So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time, between Sāvatthī and the Jeta Grove, several boys were hitting a snake with a stick. Then the Buddha robed up in the morning and, taking his bowl and robe, entered Sāvatthī for alms. He saw the boys hitting the snake.

§2.1Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§3.1–3.4“Creatures love happiness, so if you harm them with a stick in search of your own happiness, after death you’ll find no happiness.

§4.1–4.4Creatures love happiness, so if you don’t harm them with a stick in search of your own happiness, after death you will find happiness.”

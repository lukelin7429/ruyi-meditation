Udāna · Heartfelt Sayings 2.1

With Mucalinda

Mucalindasutta

Setting
Uruvelā, at the root of the Mucalinda tree by the Nerañjarā River, in the first days after the Buddha’s awakening
Speaker
The Buddha, unprompted by any question; Mucalinda the serpent king acts, but the text records no words of his
Occasion
A seven-day out-of-season storm arises while the newly awakened Buddha sits unmoving; the dragon king Mucalinda shelters him
Form
Narrative frame, then a four-line heartfelt saying in two matched verses
Length
About 2 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a famous, simple story building to a compact, layered statement

Why this heartfelt saying, first of the second chapter

No image from the Buddhist canon is more instantly recognizable than this one: a serpent king’s raised hood sheltering a seated Buddha through a seven-day storm. It opens the Udāna’s second chapter, but its setting reaches back to the very first days after the awakening itself. What the Buddha says once the storm has passed, though, has nothing to do with serpents, storms, or shelter at all — it’s a compact, four-line ranking of what happiness actually is, ending in the hardest kind of letting go.

Reading guide

A story before the saying

Ud 2.1 opens the Muccalindavagga, the Udāna’s second chapter, but its setting reaches back to the very beginning: Uruvelā, “at the root of the Mucalinda tree on the bank of the Nerañjarā River,” in the days just after the Buddha’s awakening. He is seated cross-legged, unmoving, for seven days, “experiencing the bliss of freedom.” A storm blows up out of season — seven days of cold wind, rain, and overcast sky. Mucalinda, a nāga king (a serpent or dragon king in the cosmology of the texts), leaves his home, coils around the Buddha’s body seven times, and spreads his hood overhead, thinking only: “May the Buddha not be hot or cold, nor be bothered by flies, mosquitoes, wind, sun, or reptiles.”

A courtesy repaid, then withdrawn

When the storm clears, Mucalinda unwinds himself, and — hiding his serpent form — appears as a young brahmin, standing before the Buddha with cupped palms in veneration. It’s a striking small detail: a being from entirely outside the human and mendicant orders extends real care to the newly awakened teacher, then quietly steps back into a conventional, respectful human posture once the danger has passed. The image of the Buddha seated beneath Mucalinda’s raised hood is among the most recognizable in Buddhist art across Southeast Asia, appearing in bronze and stone from Cambodia to Thailand to Sri Lanka.

Four lines, an ascending ladder

The heartfelt saying. “Seclusion is happiness for the contented who see the teaching they have learned. Kindness for the world is happiness for one who’d not harm a living creature. Dispassion for the world is happiness for one who has gone beyond sensual pleasures. But dispelling the conceit ‘I am’ is truly the ultimate happiness.”

What the Buddha says afterward is not about serpents or storms at all. It’s an udāna in the fullest sense — a spontaneous exclamation “understanding this matter,” not an answer to any question and not direct commentary on the rescue he has just received. The four lines build in a clear progression, and they don’t simply repeat “happiness” four times — they rank it: contentment in seclusion; harmlessness toward other creatures; freedom from craving for sensual pleasure; and finally, undoing the subtle conceit “I am” (asmimāna) that can persist even in someone who has let go of much else. Each line moves further inward, from a mind’s relationship to solitude, to its relationship with other beings, to its relationship with pleasure, to its relationship with itself.

Bodily care, unstated by the point

There’s an implicit contrast worth noticing between the story and the saying it introduces. Mucalinda’s care is entirely physical — shelter from cold, heat, insects, sun. It’s a real kindness, freely given, and the Buddha doesn’t refuse it or comment on it. But when he does speak, none of the four lines mention warmth, dryness, or physical comfort at all. The happiness he describes is available with or without a nāga king’s protection. This isn’t a rejection of Mucalinda’s gesture — the text simply doesn’t editorialize about it one way or the other — but the udāna quietly redirects attention from what circumstance can provide toward what circumstance can’t touch.

Key terms

Mucalinda (nāgarājā) — the “serpent/dragon king” who leaves his home to shelter the newly awakened Buddha through a seven-day storm.
udāna — a “heartfelt saying” or spontaneous utterance — the text states the Buddha speaks only “understanding this matter,” never as a reply to a question.
vimuttisukha — “the bliss of freedom,” what the Buddha is described as experiencing during his seven days of unmoving meditation before the storm.
asmimāna — the conceit “I am,” the subtle residue of self-reference the saying names as the final thing to dispel for “truly the ultimate happiness.”
viveka — “seclusion,” the very first and most basic happiness the verse names, available to “the contented who see the teaching they have learned.”

The text

The heartfelt saying in full: the seven-day storm, Mucalinda’s shelter, and the closing verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1.1–1.3So I have heard. At one time, when he was first awakened, the Buddha was staying in Uruvelā at the root of the Mucalinda tree on the bank of the Nerañjarā River. There the Buddha sat cross-legged for seven days without moving, experiencing the bliss of freedom.

§2.1–2.3Just then a large storm blew up out of season, bringing seven days of precipitation, with cold winds and overcast skies. Mucalinda, the dragon king, came out from his abode, encircled the Buddha’s body with seven coils and spread his large hood over his head, thinking, “May the Buddha not be hot or cold, nor be bothered by flies, mosquitoes, wind, sun, or reptiles.”

§3.1–3.2When seven days had passed, the Buddha emerged from that state of immersion. When he knew the heavens were clear and cloudless, Mucalinda unwrapped his coils from the Buddha’s body. Hiding his own form, he manifested in the form of a brahmin youth. He stood in front of the Buddha, venerating him with cupped palms.

§4.1Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§5.1–5.4“Seclusion is happiness for the contented who see the teaching they have learned. Kindness for the world is happiness for one who’d not harm a living creature.

§6.1–6.4Dispassion for the world is happiness for one who has gone beyond sensual pleasures. But dispelling the conceit ‘I am’ is truly the ultimate happiness.”

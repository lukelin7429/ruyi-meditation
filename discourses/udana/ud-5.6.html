Udāna · Soṇavagga · Discourse 5.6

With Soṇa

Soṇasutta

Setting
Beginning near Kuraraghara in Avanti, ending near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove
Figures
The layman (later mendicant) Soṇa of the Sharp Ears, his mentor Venerable Mahākaccāna, Venerable Ānanda, and the Buddha
Occasion
Soṇa’s long path from lay attendant to ordained mendicant, and his first meeting with the Buddha
Length
~5 minutes to read
Chapter
Soṇavagga (Ud 5.1–5.10), the fifth of the Udāna’s eight chapters — named for Ud 5.6, “With Soṇa”
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — a longer travel-and-encounter narrative in two stages

Why this heartfelt saying

Soṇa asks to go forth three times before his teacher agrees. Once ordained, it takes three years just to find enough monks in the region to complete his full ordination. And when he finally meets the Buddha face to face, what impresses the Buddha most isn’t a feat of meditation — it’s Soṇa reciting, word for word, an entire collection of sixteen discourses.

Reading guide

A mentor who says “not yet” — twice

Soṇa is a lay attendant to Venerable Mahākaccāna in the Avanti region. Twice, in private retreat, the same thought arises for him: “it’s not easy for someone living at home to lead the spiritual life utterly full and pure, like a polished shell… Why don’t I shave off my hair and beard, dress in ocher robes, and go forth?” Twice he brings this to Mahākaccāna, and twice his teacher talks him out of it — not by discouraging renunciation itself, but by pointing out how hard the monastic life actually is (“eating in one part of the day and sleeping alone”) and suggesting Soṇa instead practice a modified version of it while remaining a layperson. Both times, “Soṇa’s aspiration to go forth died down.” Only on the third arising of the same thought does Mahākaccāna finally agree.

The hardest ordination in the canon

What happens next is a detail easy to read past too quickly: “the southern region, including Avanti, was short of monks. It took three years and much struggle and difficulty before Venerable Mahākaccāyana was able to assemble from here and there a Sangha consisting of ten monks” — the minimum required for a full ordination. Three years between going forth as a novice and being fully ordained, simply because there weren’t enough monks nearby to form a quorum. It’s a small, logistical detail that says a great deal about how thin and scattered the monastic community still was in regions far from the teaching’s center.

An honored guest

Once ordained, Soṇa wants something simple: to see the Buddha in person, having “only heard reports.” He travels to Sāvatthī, delivers Mahākaccāna’s respectful greeting, and is received with a quiet but unmistakable mark of esteem — the Buddha has Ānanda prepare a shared dwelling for them both. Ānanda reads the gesture correctly: “When the Buddha orders me to prepare lodgings for a specific mendicant, he wishes to stay in the same dwelling with that mendicant.”

A recitation of the Chapter of the Eights

At dawn, the Buddha asks Soṇa to “speak some Dhamma… as you feel inspired,” and Soṇa “intoned all sixteen discourses in the Chapter of the Eights.” This detail is more than local color: the “Chapter of the Eights” is the Aṭṭhakavagga, which survives today as the fourth chapter of the Sutta Nipāta — meaning this scene shows a named collection of texts already being memorized and transmitted as a fixed set, likely quite early in the tradition’s history. The Buddha’s praise is specific and technical: “you have learned the sixteen discourses… well, you have applied the mind and remembered it well. You are a good speaker, with a polished, clear, and articulate voice.”

Why it took so long

The Buddha then asks how many years Soṇa has been ordained — only one — and, gently, why it took him so long to get here. Soṇa’s answer is disarmingly honest: “I have long seen the drawbacks of sensual pleasures, yet living in a house is cramped, with many duties and much to do.” Seeing the problem clearly and actually escaping it, the sutta suggests, are two different achievements, separated by years. The closing verse speaks in the Buddha’s own voice about exactly this gap: “Seeing the danger of the world, I understood the truth without attachments. The Noble One does not delight in evil, the Pure One does not delight in evil” — naming the destination Soṇa, after three refusals, three years, and a long journey, has finally reached.

Key terms

Soṇa Koḷivisa — “of the Sharp Ears,” a lay attendant to Mahākaccāna who becomes a mendicant only after asking three times; the sutta’s namesake and its Pali title, Soṇasutta.
Mahākaccāna — Soṇa’s teacher, remembered in the tradition as foremost among the Buddha’s disciples in explaining brief statements in detail; here shown carefully testing Soṇa’s resolve before granting it.
“rains” (vassa) — the rainy-season retreat used to count monastic seniority; Soṇa has kept “one rains” when he meets the Buddha, despite the long delay in reaching ordination.
the Chapter of the Eights — the Aṭṭhakavagga, sixteen discourses which Soṇa recites from memory in full, and which survive today as the fourth chapter of the Sutta Nipāta.
“cramped, with many duties” — sambādho gharāvāso, the standard early Buddhist description of household life used to explain the difficulty of renunciation, echoed almost verbatim in Soṇa’s own account of his delay.

The text

The discourse in full: Soṇa’s threefold request to go forth, his three-year wait for ordination, his journey to meet the Buddha, and his recitation of the Chapter of the Eights. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time Venerable Mahākaccāna was staying in the land of the Avantis near Kuraraghara on Steep Mountain. And the layman Soṇa of the Sharp Ears was Mahākaccāna’s attendant.

§2Then as Soṇa was in private retreat this thought came to his mind, “As I understand Master Mahākaccāna’s teachings, it’s not easy for someone living at home to lead the spiritual life utterly full and pure, like a polished shell. Why don’t I shave off my hair and beard, dress in ocher robes, and go forth from lay life to homelessness?”

§3–4Then Soṇa went up to Mahākaccāna, bowed, sat down to one side, and told him what he was thinking. Then he said, “May Master Mahākaccāna please give me the going forth!”

§5When this was said, Mahākaccāna said to him, “It’s hard to lead the spiritual life as long as you live, eating in one part of the day and sleeping alone. Come now, Soṇa, while remaining a layperson just as you are, devote yourself to the instructions of the Buddhas, leading the spiritual life at suitable times, eating in one part of the day and sleeping alone.” Then Soṇa’s aspiration to go forth died down.

§6For a second time, while in private retreat the thought came to Soṇa that he should go forth, but the outcome was the same.

§7–8For a third time, as Soṇa was in private retreat this thought came to his mind, “As I understand Master Mahākaccāna’s teachings, it’s not easy for someone living at home to lead the spiritual life utterly full and pure, like a polished shell. Why don’t I shave off my hair and beard, dress in ocher robes, and go forth from lay life to homelessness?” For a third time, Soṇa went up to Mahākaccāna, bowed, sat down to one side, and told him what he was thinking. Then he said, “May Master Mahākaccāna please give me the going forth!”

§9Then Mahākaccāna gave Soṇa the going forth. Now at that time the southern region, including Avanti, was short of monks. It took three years and much struggle and difficulty before Venerable Mahākaccāyana was able to assemble from here and there a Sangha consisting of ten monks and give Venerable Soṇa the full ordination.

§10Then as Venerable Soṇa was in private retreat this thought came to his mind, “I have not personally seen the Buddha. I have only heard reports that that Blessed One is like this or like that. If my mentor allows, I should go to see that Blessed One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha.”

§11–13Then in the late afternoon, Soṇa came out of retreat, went up to Mahākaccāna, bowed, sat down to one side, and told him what he was thinking. Then Mahākaccāna said, “Good, good, Soṇa! Go to see the Blessed One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha. You will see that Blessed One who is impressive and inspiring, with peaceful faculties and heart, attained to the highest self-control and serenity, like an elephant with tamed, guarded, and controlled faculties. On seeing him, in my name bow with your head to his feet. Ask him if he is healthy and well, nimble, strong, and living comfortably, saying, ‘Sir, my mentor Venerable Mahākaccāna bows with his head to your feet. He asks if you are healthy and well, nimble, strong, and living comfortably.’”

§14Saying, “Yes, sir,” Soṇa welcomed and agreed with Mahākaccāna’s words. He rose from his seat, bowed, and respectfully circled Mahākaccāna, keeping him on his right. Then he set his lodgings in order and, taking his bowl and robe, set out for Sāvatthī. Eventually he came to Sāvatthī and Jeta’s Grove. He went up to the Buddha, bowed, and sat down to one side. Soṇa said to the Buddha, “Sir, my mentor Venerable Mahākaccāna bows with his head to your feet. He asks if you are healthy and well, nimble, strong, and living comfortably.”

§15“I hope you’re keeping well, mendicant; I hope you’re all right. And I hope you have arrived from your journey unwearied, having had no trouble getting almsfood.” “I’m keeping well, Blessed One; I’m all right. And I have arrived from my journey unwearied, having had no trouble getting almsfood.”

§16Then the Buddha said to Venerable Ānanda, “Prepare lodgings for this visiting mendicant.” Then Venerable Ānanda thought, “When the Buddha orders me to prepare lodgings for a specific mendicant, he wishes to stay in the same dwelling with that mendicant. The Buddha wishes to stay together with Venerable Soṇa.” He prepared lodgings for Soṇa in the same dwelling where the Buddha was staying.

§17The Buddha spent much of the night sitting meditation in the open. Then he rose from his seat, washed his feet and entered the dwelling. Venerable Soṇa did the same. Then the Buddha rose at the crack of dawn and addressed Soṇa, “Speak some Dhamma, mendicant, as you feel inspired.”

§18“Yes, sir,” replied Soṇa. He intoned all sixteen discourses in the Chapter of the Eights. When Soṇa finished his intoning, the Buddha applauded, saying, “Good, good, mendicant! You have learned the sixteen discourses of the Chapter of the Eights well, you have applied the mind and remembered it well. You are a good speaker, with a polished, clear, and articulate voice that expresses the meaning. How many rains have you been ordained, mendicant?” “I have one rains, Blessed One.” “But why did it take you so long to make it?” “Sir, I have long seen the drawbacks of sensual pleasures, yet living in a house is cramped, with many duties and much to do.”

§19Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§20“Seeing the danger of the world,
I understood the truth without attachments.
The Noble One does not delight in evil,
the Pure One does not delight in evil.”

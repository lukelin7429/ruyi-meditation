Udāna · Soṇavagga · Discourse 5.5

Sabbath

Uposathasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother, in the Eastern Monastery
Figures
Venerable Ānanda, Venerable Mahāmoggallāna, an unnamed unethical monk, and the Buddha
Occasion
On the sabbath (uposatha) night, the Buddha three times declines to recite the monastic code
Length
~6 minutes to read
Chapter
Soṇavagga (Ud 5.1–5.10), the fifth of the Udāna’s eight chapters — named for Ud 5.6, “With Soṇa”
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — the longest and most structurally elaborate text in this chapter

Why this heartfelt saying

Three times Ānanda asks the Buddha to recite the monastic code to the assembled Saṅgha; three times, silence, until the Buddha finally says why: “the assembly is not pure.” What follows is a small, decisive act by Mahāmoggallāna, a permanent change to how the code will be recited from that night on, and eight elaborate reasons — borrowed from what titans are said to love about the ocean — for why mendicants love this teaching and training.

Reading guide

Three requests, two silences

It is the sabbath (uposatha) night, and the Buddha is sitting with the Saṅgha, waiting to recite the monastic code as usual. Three times, across the three watches of the night, Ānanda respectfully asks him to begin. The first two times, “the Buddha kept silent” — no explanation given. Only on the third request, as dawn is stirring, does the Buddha finally answer: “Ānanda, the assembly is not pure.” The repetition and the silences do real work here — they let the problem announce itself gradually, and let the reader feel Ānanda’s puzzlement build across a full night before the reason is given.

Moggallāna takes matters into his own hands

Venerable Mahāmoggallāna, wondering who the Buddha could mean, scans the minds of everyone present and finds the one person who doesn’t belong: “that unethical individual, of bad qualities, filthy, with suspicious behavior, underhand, no true ascetic or spiritual practitioner — though claiming to be one.” He asks the man to leave, once, twice, a third time; each time, silence. Finally Moggallāna “took that individual by the arm, ejected him out the gate, and bolted the door.” The Buddha’s reaction is telling: not disapproval of the physical removal, but amazement that it had to come to that — “how that futile man waited to be taken by the arm!”

A permanent change, announced on the spot

With the assembly now pure, Ānanda asks again for the recitation — and instead gets something much larger than a simple “yes.” The Buddha announces: “From this day forth, monks, I will not perform the sabbath or recite the monastic code. Now you should perform the sabbath and recite the monastic code. It’s impossible, monks, it can’t happen that a Realized One could recite the monastic code in an impure assembly.” A single night’s awkward silence becomes the origin point for an enduring change in how the community governs itself — the mendicants themselves, not the Buddha personally, take over the recitation from here on.

Eight things titans love about the ocean, eight things mendicants love about the teaching

The Buddha then explains the underlying principle with an extended simile, matching each of eight qualities the titans (asuras) are said to love about the ocean to a corresponding quality of the Dhamma and training that mendicants love in exactly the same way:

What titans love about the oceanWhat mendicants love about the teaching
It slants and slopes gradually, with no abrupt precipiceAwakening comes through gradual training, progress, and practice — not abruptly
It is consistent and never overflows its boundariesA training rule, once laid down, is kept even at the cost of life
It doesn’t tolerate a corpse, but quickly strands it on the shoreThe Saṅgha doesn’t tolerate an unethical individual, but quickly gathers and expels them
Great rivers entering it lose their names and are simply “the ocean”All four classes, on going forth, lose their former names and are simply “ascetics who follow the Sakyan”
It never empties or fills, for all the world’s streams and rainsThe element of extinguishment never empties or fills, however many are fully extinguished in it
It has just one taste, the taste of saltThis teaching and training has just one taste, the taste of freedom
It is full of many kinds of treasuresThe teaching is full of treasures: the four kinds of mindfulness meditation, the four right efforts, the four bases of psychic power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven awakening factors, and the noble eightfold path
Many great beings live in it, some vast in sizeGreat beings live in the teaching: the stream-enterer, once-returner, non-returner, and perfected one, along with those practicing to realize each fruit

This is an original organizing table for this reading guide, not a translation; the full verbatim text of all eight pairs, exactly as spoken, is given in full in the text below.

The closing verse: what stays covered gets soaked

The heartfelt saying that ends the sutta returns, in miniature, to the night’s opening problem: “The rain saturates things that are covered up; it doesn’t saturate things that are open. Therefore you should open up a covered thing, so the rain will not saturate it.” The unethical monk who sat silently through three requests to leave was, in exactly this sense, a covered thing — his real condition stayed hidden until Moggallāna’s mind-reading and direct confrontation opened it up. The verse turns one night’s specific incident into a general principle about concealment and its cost.

Key terms

uposatha — the sabbath or observance day, on which the Saṅgha traditionally gathers to recite the monastic code (Pātimokkha); the occasion for this entire sutta and the source of its title, Uposathasutta.
“the assembly is not pure” — the Buddha’s terse explanation for his silence — the trigger for Moggallāna’s mind-reading and the unethical monk’s expulsion.
the eight ocean qualities — a set of similes comparing what titans (asuras) love about the ocean to eight corresponding qualities mendicants love about the Dhamma and training, from gradual training to the four kinds of great beings.
the element of extinguishment — nibbānadhātu — described, like the ocean, as never emptying or filling however many beings become fully extinguished in it.
“the rain saturates things that are covered up” — the closing verse’s image, linking the night’s concealed wrongdoing to a general principle: what stays hidden is what gets soaked.

The text

The discourse in full: three requests and two silences, Moggallāna’s intervention, the Buddha’s announcement, the eight-fold ocean simile in full, and the closing verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother in the Eastern Monastery. Now, at that time it was the sabbath, and the Buddha was sitting surrounded by the Saṅgha of monks.

§2And then, as the night was getting late, in the first watch of the night, Venerable Ānanda rose from his seat, arranged his robe over one shoulder, raised his cupped palms toward the Buddha and said, “Sir, the night is getting late. It is the first watch of the night, and the Saṅgha of monks has been sitting long. Please, sir, may the Buddha recite the monastic code to the monks.” But when he said this, the Buddha kept silent.

§3For a second time, as the night was getting late, in the middle watch of the night, Ānanda rose from his seat, arranged his robe over one shoulder, raised his cupped palms toward the Buddha and said, “Sir, the night is getting late. It is the middle watch of the night, and the Saṅgha of monks has been sitting long. Please, sir, may the Buddha recite the monastic code to the monks.” But for a second time the Buddha kept silent.

§4For a third time, as the night was getting late, in the last watch of the night, as dawn stirred, bringing joy to the night, Ānanda rose from his seat, arranged his robe over one shoulder, raised his cupped palms toward the Buddha and said, “Sir, the night is getting late. It is the last watch of the night and dawn stirs, bringing joy to the night. Please, sir, may the Buddha recite the monastic code to the monks.” “Ānanda, the assembly is not pure.”

§5Then Venerable Mahāmoggallāna thought, “Who is the Buddha talking about?” Then he focused on encompassing the minds of everyone in the Saṅgha. He saw that unethical individual, of bad qualities, filthy, with suspicious behavior, underhand, no true ascetic or spiritual practitioner—though claiming to be one—rotten inside, festering, and depraved, sitting in the middle of the Saṅgha. When he saw him he rose from his seat, went up to him and said, “Get up, reverend. The Buddha has seen you. You can’t live in communion with the monks.” But when he said this, that individual kept silent.

§6For a second time and a third time, he asked that monk to leave. But for a third time that individual kept silent.

§7Then Venerable Mahāmoggallāna took that individual by the arm, ejected him out the gate, and bolted the door. Then he went up to the Buddha, and said to him, “I have ejected that individual. The assembly is pure. Please, sir, may the Buddha recite the monastic code to the monks.” “It’s incredible, Moggallāna, it’s amazing, how that futile man waited to be taken by the arm!”

§8Then the Buddha said to the monks, “From this day forth, monks, I will not perform the sabbath or recite the monastic code. Now you should perform the sabbath and recite the monastic code. It’s impossible, monks, it can’t happen that a Realized One could recite the monastic code in an impure assembly.

§9Seeing these eight incredible and amazing things the titans love the ocean. What eight?

§10The ocean gradually slants, slopes, and inclines, with no abrupt precipice. This is the first thing the titans love about the ocean.

§11Furthermore, the ocean is consistent and doesn’t overflow its boundaries. This is the second thing the titans love about the ocean.

§12Furthermore, the ocean doesn’t accommodate a carcass, but quickly carries it to the shore and strands it on the beach. This is the third thing the titans love about the ocean.

§13Furthermore, when they reach the ocean, all the great rivers—that is, the Ganges, Yamunā, Aciravatī, Sarabhū, and Mahī—lose their names and clans and are simply reckoned as ‘the ocean’. This is the fourth thing the titans love about the ocean.

§14Furthermore, for all the world’s streams that reach it, and the showers that fall from the sky, the ocean never empties or fills up. This is the fifth thing the titans love about the ocean.

§15Furthermore, the ocean has just one taste, the taste of salt. This is the sixth thing the titans love about the ocean.

§16Furthermore, the ocean is full of many kinds of treasures, such as pearls, gems, beryl, conch, quartz, coral, silver, native gold, rubies, and emeralds. This is the seventh thing the titans love about the ocean.

§17Furthermore, many great beings live in the ocean, such as leviathans, leviathan-gulpers, leviathan-gulper-gulpers, titans, dragons, and centaurs. In the ocean there are life-forms a hundred leagues long, or even two hundred, three hundred, four hundred, or five hundred leagues long. This is the eighth thing the titans love about the ocean. Seeing these eight incredible and amazing things the titans love the ocean.

§18In the same way, seeing eight incredible and amazing things, mendicants, the mendicants love this teaching and training. What eight?

§19The ocean gradually slants, slopes, and inclines, with no abrupt precipice. In the same way in this teaching and training the penetration to enlightenment comes from gradual training, progress, and practice, not abruptly. This is the first thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training.

§20The ocean is consistent and doesn’t overflow its boundaries. In the same way, when a training rule is laid down for my disciples they wouldn’t break it even for the sake of their own life. This is the second thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training.

§21The ocean doesn’t accommodate a carcass, but quickly carries it to the shore and strands it on the beach. In the same way, the Saṅgha doesn’t accommodate an individual who is unethical, of bad qualities, filthy, with suspicious behavior, underhand, no true ascetic or spiritual practitioner—though claiming to be one—rotten inside, festering, and depraved. But they quickly gather and expel them. Even if such an individual is sitting in the middle of the Saṅgha, they’re far from the Saṅgha, and the Saṅgha is far from them. This is the third thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training.

§22Furthermore, when they reach the ocean, all the great rivers—that is, the Ganges, Yamunā, Aciravatī, Sarabhū, and Mahī—lose their names and clans and are simply reckoned as ‘the ocean’. In the same way, when they go forth from the lay life to homelessness, all four classes—aristocrats, brahmins, peasants, and menials—lose their former names and clans and are simply reckoned as ‘ascetics who follow the Sakyan’. This is the fourth thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training.

§23For all the world’s streams that reach it, and the showers that fall from the sky, the ocean never empties or fills up. In the same way, though several mendicants become fully extinguished in the element of extinguishment with no residue, the element of extinguishment never empties or fills up. This is the fifth thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training.

§24The ocean has just one taste, the taste of salt. In the same way, this teaching and training has one taste, the taste of freedom. This is the sixth thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training.

§25The ocean is full of many kinds of treasures, such as pearls, gems, beryl, conch, quartz, coral, silver, native gold, rubies, and emeralds. In the same way, this teaching and training is full of many kinds of treasures, such as the four kinds of mindfulness meditation, the four right efforts, the four bases of psychic power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven awakening factors, and the noble eightfold path. This is the seventh thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training.

§26Many great beings live in the ocean, such as leviathans, leviathan-gulpers, leviathan-gulper-gulpers, titans, dragons, and centaurs. In the ocean there are life-forms a hundred leagues long, or even two hundred, three hundred, four hundred, or five hundred leagues long. In the same way, great beings live in this teaching and training, and these are those beings. The stream-enterer and the one practicing to realize the fruit of stream-entry. The once-returner and the one practicing to realize the fruit of once-return. The non-returner and the one practicing to realize the fruit of non-return. The perfected one, and the one practicing for perfection. This is the eighth thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training. Seeing these eight incredible and amazing things, the mendicants love this teaching and training.”

§27Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§28“The rain saturates things that are covered up;
it doesn’t saturate things that are open.
Therefore you should open up a covered thing,
so the rain will not saturate it.”

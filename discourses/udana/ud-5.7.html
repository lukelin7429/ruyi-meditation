Udāna · Soṇavagga · Discourse 5.7

With Revata the Doubter

Kaṅkhārevatasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Figures
Venerable Revata the Doubter and the Buddha
Occasion
The Buddha silently observes Revata meditating nearby
Length
~1 minute to read
Chapter
Soṇavagga (Ud 5.1–5.10), the fifth of the Udāna’s eight chapters — named for Ud 5.6, “With Soṇa”
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — a single observed moment and a four-line verse

Why this heartfelt saying

The Buddha says nothing to Revata directly. He simply watches a monk nicknamed “the Doubter” sitting cross-legged, reviewing his own purification, having already traversed the very doubt his name commemorates — and turns the sight into a verse about what actually resolves uncertainty.

Reading guide

A nickname earned, then outgrown

This sutta introduces Venerable Revata by his epithet: “the Doubter” (Kaṅkhā-Revata). But the moment the text captures him in is not doubt — it’s the far side of it. He is “sitting not far from the Buddha, cross-legged, with his body straight, reviewing his own purification by traversing doubt.” The nickname and the scene sit in quiet tension: whatever uncertainty once defined him, the text finds him here in the act of having gotten past it, checking his own clarity rather than struggling for it.

What kind of doubt?

The verse specifies the scope with some precision: “Any doubts about this life or the next, about one’s own experiences or those of another.” That is a comprehensive sweep — not merely doctrinal uncertainty about a teaching, but uncertainty about one’s own present existence, what comes after death, and even how to weigh one’s own experience against someone else’s account of theirs. It is the kind of doubt that could unsettle a practitioner from every direction at once.

Meditation as what actually resolves it

The verse credits a specific method for dissolving all of this: “those who meditate give them all up, keenly practicing the spiritual life.” Not debate, not persuasion, not simply being told the right answer — meditators (jhāyino) are named as the ones who let go of doubt, and they do it through the same “keen practice” that Ud 5.2’s verse recommends in light of impermanence. Revata, watched in silence, becomes a living example of the process the verse describes in the abstract.

Key terms

kaṅkhā — “doubt” — the quality named in Revata’s own epithet, Kaṅkhā-Revata, “Revata the Doubter,” and in the sutta’s Pali title, Kaṅkhārevatasutta.
“traversing doubt” — vitiṇṇakaṅkha, a standard formula in the canon for the clarity that marks a stream-enterer or higher attainment; here shown as something Revata reviews in his own meditation.
jhāyino — “those who meditate” — the verse’s specific answer to who actually gives up doubt, naming meditative practice rather than argument.
“this life or the next” — one half of the verse’s comprehensive scope of doubt, paired with uncertainty about “one’s own experiences or those of another.”
the cross-legged posture — “cross-legged, with his body straight” — the same meditation posture the Buddha observes in Cūḷapanthaka in Ud 5.10, later in this same chapter.

The text

The discourse in full: the Buddha observes Revata meditating, and speaks a short verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time Venerable Revata the Doubter was sitting not far from the Buddha, cross-legged, with his body straight, reviewing his own purification by traversing doubt.

§2The Buddha saw him meditating there.

§3Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§4“Any doubts about this life or the next,
about one’s own experiences or those of another:
those who meditate give them all up,
keenly practicing the spiritual life.”

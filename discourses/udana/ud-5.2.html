Udāna · Soṇavagga · Discourse 5.2

Short-lived

Appāyukasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Figures
Venerable Ānanda and the Buddha
Occasion
Ānanda, coming out of retreat, remarks on how short-lived the Buddha’s own mother was
Length
~1 minute to read
Chapter
Soṇavagga (Ud 5.1–5.10), the fifth of the Udāna’s eight chapters — named for Ud 5.6, “With Soṇa”
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — one short exchange, one four-line verse

Why this heartfelt saying

Ānanda states a fact that could easily read as a private sorrow — the Buddha’s mother died seven days after giving birth to him — and the Buddha neither deflects it nor lingers on it personally. He confirms it, generalizes it into a pattern true of every being intent on awakening, and turns straight to what the fact demands of the living: urgency.

Reading guide

An exclamation, not a question

Ānanda comes out of retreat in the late afternoon, goes to the Buddha, and doesn’t ask anything. He states his amazement directly: “It’s incredible, sir, it’s amazing! How short-lived was the Blessed One’s mother! For seven days after the Blessed One was born, his mother passed away and was reborn in the host of joyful gods.” (Sujato renders the destination simply as “the host of joyful gods”; tradition identifies this as the Tusita heaven, though the sutta itself doesn’t use that name.) The text never names the mother either — tradition remembers her as Māyā — this udāna is interested in the pattern, not the biography.

A fixed rule, not a private grief

The Buddha’s reply is striking for what it doesn’t do: he doesn’t comment on his own loss at all. Instead he confirms and generalizes: “That’s so true, Ānanda! For the mothers of beings intent on awakening are short-lived. Seven days after the beings intent on awakening are born, their mothers pass away and are reborn in the host of joyful gods.” This is presented as a rule that holds for every bodhisatta’s mother, not an event unique to his own family history — the specific becomes an instance of a much older, larger pattern.

From one death to every death

The verse then widens the lens again, from bodhisattas’ mothers to every living being: “Whether born or to be born, all depart, leaving the body behind. The skillful, understanding that all is lost, would keenly practice the spiritual life.” The logic moves outward in three steps across the whole text — one mother, then a fixed pattern for all such mothers, then the flat universal fact that everyone born eventually leaves the body behind. The conclusion isn’t mourning; it’s urgency. Because loss is certain and total (“all is lost”), the skillful response is to practice keenly now, not to grieve at length over what was always going to happen.

Characteristic Udāna directness

It would have been easy for an editor to omit this detail about the Buddha’s own mother, or to surround it with more consolation. This collection instead states it plainly, in one exchange, and moves immediately to what the fact teaches. That directness — no lingering, no special pleading even about the Buddha’s own family — is characteristic of the Udāna’s brief, observational style, visible again in several of this chapter’s other short scenes.

Key terms

the Blessed One’s mother — unnamed in this sutta; tradition remembers her as Māyā, who according to this text died seven days after childbirth and was reborn among “the host of joyful gods.”
the host of joyful gods — Sujato’s rendering of the destination; commonly identified in the wider tradition with the Tusita heaven, though this sutta itself does not use that name.
bodhisatta — “a being intent on awakening” — the term behind the Buddha’s generalization that this seven-day pattern holds for every such being’s mother, not only his own.
“leaving the body behind” — the verse’s core image for death, applied without exception to everyone “born or to be born.”
“understanding that all is lost” — the pivot phrase connecting the fact of impermanence to the verse’s practical demand: keen practice, not delay.

The text

The discourse in full: Ānanda’s remark, the Buddha’s confirmation, and the closing verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Then in the late afternoon, Venerable Ānanda came out of retreat and went to the Buddha. He bowed, sat down to one side, and said to him: “It’s incredible, sir, it’s amazing! How short-lived was the Blessed One’s mother! For seven days after the Blessed One was born, his mother passed away and was reborn in the host of joyful gods.”

§2“That’s so true, Ānanda! For the mothers of beings intent of awakening are short-lived. Seven days after the beings intent on awakening are born, their mothers pass away and are reborn in the host of joyful gods.”

§3Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§4“Whether born or to be born,
all depart, leaving the body behind.
The skillful, understanding that all is lost,
would keenly practice the spiritual life.”

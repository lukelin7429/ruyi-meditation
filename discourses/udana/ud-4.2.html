Udāna · Meghiyavagga · Discourse 4.2

Restless

Uddhatasutta

Setting
The sal forest of the Mallas at Upavattana, near Kusinārā
Present
The Buddha, observing several unnamed mendicants nearby
Form
No dialogue at all — the Buddha simply observes and then speaks a heartfelt verse
Length
Under 1 minute to read
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — short and direct, though its compressed verse repays a slow reading

Why this heartfelt saying

There is no conversation in this discourse at all — no question asked, no reply given, not even a name attached to the mendicants involved. The Buddha simply notices a group of careless monks staying nearby and, seeing them, speaks a verse. It is one of the starkest examples in the Udāna of the genre’s basic shape: something is seen, and a heartfelt saying follows, with no resolution supplied.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A body left unguarded, a view gone wrong, and a mind overcome by dullness and drowsiness together deliver a person into Māra’s power — and the remedy the verse offers is just as compact: guard the mind, make right thought your habitat, keep right view in front, and understand how things rise and fall.

A silent rebuke

The setting is precise and, for readers of the wider canon, resonant: the sal forest of the Mallas at Upavattana, near Kusinārā — the very place where the Buddha’s life would eventually end. Nothing in this short discourse mentions that fact, and it should not be read into the text, but the location is worth noting all the same. What the text does say is that “several mendicants were staying not far from the Buddha in a wilderness hut,” and it describes them at some length: “restless, insolent, fickle, scurrilous, loose-tongued, unmindful, lacking situational awareness and immersion, with straying minds and undisciplined faculties.” The Buddha “saw those mendicants staying nearby.” That is the entire narrative frame. No one is addressed, corrected, or even named; the verse follows directly from what the Buddha sees.

Four failures, one fall

The verse’s first half reads almost like a diagnosis: “Unguarded in body, ruined by wrong view, overcome with dullness and drowsiness, you fall under Māra’s sway.” Each phrase names a distinct failure — a body not restrained, a view that has gone astray, and a mind so dull and drowsy it can no longer notice either — and together they describe exactly the kind of carelessness just attributed to the nearby mendicants.

The remedy, in four parts

The second half answers point for point: “That’s why you should guard the mind, with right thoughts your habitat, and right view at the fore. Having understood rise and fall, a mendicant who has overcome dullness and drowsiness would abandon all bad destinies.” Guarding the mind answers the unguarded body; right thought as one’s habitat and right view at the fore answer the ruined view; and understanding “rise and fall” — the impermanence of conditioned things — is what allows dullness and drowsiness themselves to be overcome.

Restlessness and drowsiness together

It is worth noticing that although the mendicants are introduced as “restless” — the word that gives this discourse its title — the verse’s actual target is “dullness and drowsiness,” a different hindrance altogether. Read together, the discourse suggests these are not opposites but companions: a mind that has never learned to guard itself can be scattered and unmoored one moment, dull and heavy the next. Both are simply what an unguarded mind does.

Key terms

uddhata — “restless”: the word used to describe the mendicants, and the title of this discourse.
thīna-middha — dullness and drowsiness, one of the five hindrances, and the specific obstacle the closing verse names as needing to be overcome.
Māra — the personification of death and temptation; the verse says an unguarded person “falls under Māra’s sway.”
sammādiṭṭhi — right view, which the verse says should be “at the fore” (purekkhāra) of a guarded mind.
udayabbaya — “rise and fall,” the arising and passing away of conditioned phenomena; understanding it is what the verse credits with overcoming dullness and drowsiness.

The text

The discourse in full: the Buddha’s silent observation of a group of careless mendicants near Kusinārā, and the two-part verse this occasions. There is no dialogue in this short text. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the sal forest of the Mallas at Upavattana near Kusinārā. Now at that time several mendicants were staying not far from the Buddha in a wilderness hut. They were restless, insolent, fickle, scurrilous, loose-tongued, unmindful, lacking situational awareness and immersion, with straying minds and undisciplined faculties.

§2The Buddha saw those mendicants staying nearby.

§3Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§4“Unguarded in body, ruined by wrong view, overcome with dullness and drowsiness, you fall under Māra’s sway.

§5That’s why you should guard the mind, with right thoughts your habitat, and right view at the fore. Having understood rise and fall, a mendicant who has overcome dullness and drowsiness would abandon all bad destinies.”

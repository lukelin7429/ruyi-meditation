Udāna · Meghiyavagga · Discourse 4.9

With Upasena

Upasenasutta

Setting
Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground
Present
Venerable Upasena, son of Vaṅgantā, in private retreat, and the Buddha (reading his mind)
Form
An interior monologue, confirmed rather than corrected, closed by an eight-line verse
Length
About 1 minute to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — brief, but its confident first-person voice rewards careful attention

Why this heartfelt saying

Alone in retreat, Venerable Upasena counts off, in his own words, everything that has gone right for him: his teacher, his training, his companions, his own attainment — concluding simply, “My life has been good, and my death will be good.” The Buddha, reading this thought directly, does not correct it. He confirms it, in verse.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Reviewing his own life in private retreat, Upasena declares himself thoroughly fortunate — a good teacher, a well-explained teaching, good companions, fulfilled precepts, a unified mind, and the ending of the defilements — and the Buddha, perceiving this thought, answers not with correction but with a verse confirming exactly this kind of person’s freedom from grief.

An inventory of good fortune

As Venerable Upasena, son of Vaṅgantā, sits alone in retreat, a specific train of thought comes to him, quoted directly and at length: “I’m so fortunate, so very fortunate! My Teacher is the Blessed One, perfected, a fully awakened Buddha. Well explained is the teaching and training in which I have gone forth from the lay life to homelessness. My spiritual companions are ethical and of good character. I have fulfilled the precepts. My mind is unified and serene. I am a perfected one who has ended the defilements. I am of great might and power.” He concludes: “My life has been good, and my death will be good.”

A confidence the Buddha does not correct

What is notable about this discourse is what does not happen. Elsewhere in the canon, a mendicant’s claim to have “ended the defilements” might be tested, qualified, or corrected. Here, the Buddha, “knowing Venerable Upasena’s train of thought” by direct perception, simply speaks a verse — and the verse affirms rather than challenges what Upasena has just claimed about himself. The discourse offers this confidence as accurate, not as something to be examined for pride or self-deception.

The verse: no grief, in life or at death

The Buddha’s response comes in two stanzas. The first: “One who has no guilt in life, nor grief at facing death: that attentive one who has seen the truth, grieves not among the grieving.” The second draws the conclusion to its end: “For the mendicant with peaceful mind, who has cut off craving for continued existence, transmigration through births is finished, there’ll be no more future lives for them.” The verse’s structure mirrors Upasena’s own closing thought — “my life has been good, and my death will be good” — confirming that a guiltless life and a fearless death are exactly what should be expected from someone who has, as the verse says, “cut off craving for continued existence.”

A quiet, confident close

Nothing in this discourse involves narrative incident, dialogue, or crisis; it is entirely interior, moving from one mendicant’s private self-assessment to the Buddha’s silent confirmation of it. Placed near the end of the vagga, alongside Ud 4.7 and Ud 4.10’s similarly still portraits of Sāriputta, it adds Upasena’s own first-person voice to this collection’s recurring interest in what a fully settled mind actually sounds like from the inside.

Key terms

khīṇāsava — “one whose defilements are ended,” a standard epithet for an arahant; Upasena applies it to himself directly in his private reflection.
diṭṭhapada — “one who has seen the truth” or “seen the path,” describing the attentive one in the verse who does not grieve among the grieving.
bhavataṇhā — craving for continued existence; the verse specifies it is this, cut off, that ends future rebirth for the peaceful-minded mendicant.
santacitta — a peaceful or stilled mind, the quality the closing verse attributes to the mendicant who will have no more future lives.
punabbhava — “future existence” or rebirth; the verse’s final word states plainly that there is none left for such a mendicant.

The text

The discourse in full: Upasena’s private reflection on his own good fortune and attainment, and the two-stanza verse the Buddha speaks in response, perceiving his thought directly. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground. Then as Venerable Upasena son of Vaṅgantā was in private retreat this thought came to his mind: “I’m so fortunate, so very fortunate! My Teacher is the Blessed One, perfected, a fully awakened Buddha. Well explained is the teaching and training in which I have gone forth from the lay life to homelessness. My spiritual companions are ethical and of good character. I have fulfilled the precepts. My mind is unified and serene. I am a perfected one who has ended the defilements. I am of great might and power. My life has been good, and my death will be good.”

§2Then, knowing that Venerable Upasena’s train of thought, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§3“One who has no guilt in life, nor grief at facing death: that attentive one who has seen the truth, grieves not among the grieving.

§4For the mendicant with peaceful mind, who has cut off craving for continued existence, transmigration through births is finished, there’ll be no more future lives for them.”

Udāna · Meghiyavagga · Discourse 4.3

The Cowherd

Gopālakasutta

Setting
Wandering through the land of the Kosalans, under a roadside tree
Present
The Buddha, a large Saṅgha of mendicants, and an unnamed cowherd
Form
A two-part narrative — teaching and hospitality, then sudden violence — closed by a short verse
Length
About 2 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a plain story with an unsettling, deliberately unexplained turn

Why this heartfelt saying

A cowherd feeds the Buddha and his entire Saṅgha with his own hands, is taught by the Buddha twice in one day, and is dead within hours — killed by a man the text never names, for a reason it never gives. The Buddha’s response supplies no explanation of kamma, no comfort, and no closure: only a verse about how dangerous an undirected mind can be, more dangerous than any hater or enemy.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

After a generous cowherd who has just fed the Buddha and the whole Saṅgha is killed without warning or explanation, the Buddha’s heartfelt saying does not address the killing directly at all: it simply states that a wrongly directed mind will do a person more harm than a hater or an enemy ever could.

An invitation, twice fulfilled

The Buddha is wandering through Kosala with a large Saṅgha when he leaves the road to sit at the root of a tree. A cowherd approaches, is taught, and is “educated, encouraged, fired up, and inspired” by the Buddha’s talk. In gratitude he invites the Buddha and the entire Saṅgha to a meal the next day; the Buddha “consented with silence,” the traditional wordless way of accepting an invitation. The following day the cowherd has “plenty of thick milk-rice prepared in his own home, with fresh ghee,” and serves the assembled mendicants “with his own hands.” After the meal, the Buddha teaches him a second time, then rises to leave.

An abrupt, unexplained death

The text’s next sentence is jarring in its plainness: “soon after the Buddha had left, the cowherd was killed by a certain man in the gap between village perimeters.” No name is given for the killer, no motive is offered, and no connection is drawn between this death and anything the cowherd did, gave, or heard that day. Several mendicants report the killing to the Buddha; nothing in the text suggests they asked why, or that the Buddha explained why. The discourse is notably austere here — it declines to supply the kind of narrative closure a reader might expect.

The verse’s real target

The Buddha’s heartfelt saying, given “understanding this matter,” does not mention the cowherd, the killer, or kamma explicitly at all: “A wrongly directed mind would do you more harm than a hater to the hated, or an enemy to their foe.” Read in context, the verse’s point is not really about the cowherd’s death as an isolated event; it redirects attention to something more universal — the ordinary listener’s own mind, and the particular danger of a mind that has gone in a wrong direction. An external hater or enemy is at least a known, bounded threat; a wrongly directed mind of one’s own is unbounded and ever-present.

Three suttas, one thread

Placed third in its vagga, after Meghiya’s account of a mind harassed by unwanted thoughts and the verse warning careless mendicants against falling under Māra’s sway, this discourse continues a loose thread: each, in its own register, concerns the direction and guarding of the mind, rather than external circumstance, as the decisive factor in a person’s wellbeing.

Key terms

micchāpaṇihitaṁ cittaṁ — a wrongly directed or misapplied mind, the central image of the closing verse.
gopālaka — cowherd, the discourse’s central figure and the source of its title.
sandassesi samādapesi samuttejesi sampahaṁsesi — the Buddha’s fourfold formula for a Dhamma talk: he “educated, encouraged, fired up, and inspired” his listener, applied to the cowherd twice in this discourse.
tuṇhībhāva — “consented with silence,” the traditional wordless way the Buddha accepts an invitation to a meal.
udāna — heartfelt utterance, the genre itself; here spoken in direct response to news of an unexplained, violent death.

The text

The discourse in full: the cowherd’s teaching and invitation, the meal he serves with his own hands, his sudden death, and the Buddha’s closing verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was wandering in the land of the Kosalans together with a large Saṅgha of mendicants. And then the Buddha left the road, went to the root of a certain tree, and sat down on the seat spread out.

§2Then a certain cowherd went up to the Buddha, bowed, and sat down to one side. The Buddha educated, encouraged, fired up, and inspired him with a Dhamma talk.

§3Then the cowherd said to the Buddha, “Sir, would the Buddha, together with the mendicant Saṅgha, please accept tomorrow’s meal from me?” The Buddha consented with silence. Then, knowing that the Buddha had consented, the cowherd rose from his seat, bowed, and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on his right, before leaving.

§4And when the night had passed the cowherd had plenty of thick milk-rice prepared in his own home, with fresh ghee. Then he had the Buddha informed of the time, saying, “Sir, it’s time. The meal is ready.”

§5The Buddha robed up, took his bowl and robe and, together with the Sangha of monks, went to the house of that cowherd, where he sat down on the prepared seat in the dining hall. Then the cowherd served and satisfied the mendicant Saṅgha headed by the Buddha with his own hands with a thick milk-rice and fresh ghee. When the Buddha had eaten and washed his hand and bowl, the cowherd took a low seat and sat to one side. The Buddha then instructed, inspired, and gladdened him with a teaching, after which he got up and left. But soon after the Buddha had left, the cowherd was killed by a certain man in the gap between village perimeters.

§6Then several mendicants went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and told him what had happened.

§7Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§8“A wrongly directed mind would do you more harm than a hater to the hated, or an enemy to their foe.”

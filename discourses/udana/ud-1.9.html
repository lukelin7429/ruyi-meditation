Udāna · Discourse 1.9

Dreadlocked Ascetics

Jaṭilasutta

Setting
Near Gayā, on Gayā Head, during the cold spell of the January winter
Speaker
The Buddha, watching a group of ascetics bathe; no question is asked of him in this discourse
Theme
Closing this thread’s run: ritual bathing believed to purify is set against a verse locating purity in truth and principle instead
Length
< 1 minute to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — brief, with a clear and pointed contrast

Purity, tested against water

Like Ud 1.6, this discourse offers no dialogue at all — only an observed scene and a verse. Where Mahākassapa’s alms round tested brahmin-hood against poverty, this scene, closing the six-discourse thread that opened at Ud 1.4, tests it against a specific, concrete ritual practice: repeated bathing for purification.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Watching a group of dreadlocked ascetics plunge repeatedly into the freezing Gayā river, believing “through this there is purity,” the Buddha responds with a verse stating flatly that purity doesn’t come from water at all, but from truth and principle.

Bathing against the winter cold

The scene is set with unusual sensory precision for this collection: “the cold spell when the snow falls in the January winter,” at Gayā Head, where “many dreadlocked ascetics plunged in and out of the Gayā river.” Alongside this repeated plunging, they also “served the sacred flame” — fire-worship paired with ritual bathing — all of it undertaken, the text tells us directly, in the belief that “through this there is purity.”

A silent witness, once again

As in Ud 1.6, there is no dialogue here at all. The Buddha “saw them plunging in and out,” and that observation alone is what prompts the verse. Two of the six discourses in this thread — this one and the Mahākassapa episode — share this structure: no question, no debate, simply a scene observed and a heartfelt saying spoken in response to it.

The verse's answer: truth and principle, not water

The verse could hardly be more direct: “purity doesn’t come from water, no matter how many people bathe there. One who has truth and principle, they are pure, they are brahmins.” It doesn’t deny that the ascetics are sincere, or soften the contrast for tact. It states the claim and its opposite side by side, in as few words as the verse allows.

A recurring canonical concern

The rejection of ritual bathing as a path to genuine purification is a concern that surfaces more than once in early Buddhist texts, generally aimed at the same underlying point this verse makes: that external practices, however sincerely performed, cannot substitute for an internal change of character. This discourse gives that broader concern one of its most compact statements — purity paired not with water but with the twin qualities of truth (sacca) and principle (dhamma).

Closing the six-discourse thread

With this discourse, the run that opened at Ud 1.4 comes to a close. Across six scenes — a muttering ascetic, ten approaching elders, an alms round among the poor, a taunting spirit, a former wife’s visit, and now a company of ritual bathers — the same claim has been tested against ritual utterance, birth, poverty, fear, family attachment, and now water itself. Each time, the verdict lands in the same place: on inner qualities, not external circumstance or practice.

Key terms

jaṭila — “dreadlocked ascetics,” the group observed in this discourse practicing ritual bathing and fire-worship.
Gayā Head — the location of this episode, on the Gayā river, a site associated in the broader tradition with ascetic practice.
the sacred flame — the fire-worship the ascetics are described as serving alongside their repeated bathing.
sacca and dhamma — “truth” and “principle,” the two qualities the verse names as the actual basis of purity.
ritual purification, critiqued — the belief “through this there is purity” attributed to the ascetics, and directly contradicted by the verse.

The text

The discourse in full: the setting, the observed ritual bathing, and the closing verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The setting

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Gayā on Gayā Head. Now at that time—during the cold spell when the snow falls in the January winter—many dreadlocked ascetics plunged in and out of the Gayā river. Plunging and showering, they served the sacred flame, thinking: “Through this there is purity.”

§2The Buddha saw them plunging in and out.

The heartfelt saying

§3Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§4“Purity doesn’t come from water,
no matter how many people bathe there.
One who has truth and principle,
they are pure, they are brahmins.”

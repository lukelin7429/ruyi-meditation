Udāna · Discourse 8.6

The Layfolk of Pāṭali Village

Pāṭaligāmiyasutta

Setting
A guest house at Pāṭali Village in the land of the Magadhans, then the bank of the Ganges River
Speakers
The Buddha; the lay followers of Pāṭali Village; Venerable Ānanda; the Magadhan ministers Sunidha and Vassakāra
Form
An extended narrative combining a prose teaching, a prophecy, and a closing heartfelt sentiment (udāna) in verse
Length
~7 minutes to read
Historical note
Pāṭaliputta, prophesied here, became the historical Mauryan capital, present-day Patna
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a clear, structured narrative with an ethical teaching at its center

Why this discourse

This discourse folds together an ordinary evening of lay teaching — five plain drawbacks of unethical conduct, five plain benefits of ethical conduct — with two much stranger episodes: a vision of deities settling on building sites, and a river crossed as easily as a person might bend an arm. It is worth reading for the contrast between its two registers as much as for either one alone.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

In a village guest house at Pāṭali, the Buddha teaches the assembled lay followers five drawbacks of failing in ethics and five benefits of accomplishing in it; then, seeing with clairvoyance the deities who have taken possession of the very building sites two Magadhan ministers are using for a new citadel, he prophesies that this settlement will become a great trading city facing three threats — fire, flood, and dissension — before crossing the flooded Ganges by psychic power and closing with a verse on those who have “crossed over.”

The guest house, and the five and five

The layfolk of Pāṭali Village prepare their guest house with real care — carpets, seats, a water jar, an oil lamp — and the seating itself is described precisely: the Buddha against the central column facing east, the mendicants against the west wall facing him, the laypeople against the east wall facing him from the other side. What follows is a plain, symmetrical teaching: five drawbacks for someone who fails in ethics (lost wealth through negligence, a bad reputation, timidity in any assembly, dying with a sense of being lost, and rebirth in a place of loss), matched one-for-one against five benefits for someone who succeeds in it (wealth gained through diligence, a good reputation, boldness in any assembly, dying without that same sense of being lost, and rebirth in a heavenly realm). Nothing here depends on rank, caste, or ritual status; the entire teaching turns on conduct alone.

Deities, and a prophecy about a great city

After the layfolk leave, the discourse shifts registers abruptly. The Magadhan ministers Sunidha and Vassakāra are building a citadel at Pāṭali Village to guard against the Vajjis, and the text reports that “thousands of deities were taking possession of building sites,” with rulers and ministers of various ranks unknowingly inclined to build wherever deities of a matching rank had already settled. The Buddha, seeing this with clairvoyance, remarks to Ānanda that it is “as if they were building the citadel in consultation with the gods of the thirty-three,” and then makes a specific prediction: this place will become “the chief city” as far as trade and civilization extend — named as the Pāṭaliputta trade center — but will face three recurring threats: fire, flood, and dissension. Readers of later Indian history will recognize Pāṭaliputta as the city that did, in fact, become the Mauryan imperial capital.

A meal, and the naming of a gate

Sunidha and Vassakāra, unaware of the prophecy just made about their own citadel, invite the Buddha and the Saṅgha for a meal and serve them personally. Afterward the Buddha offers a short verse of appreciation about dedicating merit to the deities of a place one dwells in. The ministers, for their part, quietly decide to name the gate the Buddha leaves by “the Gotama Gate,” and the ford where he will cross the Ganges “the Gotama Ford” — an entirely ordinary human gesture of honor, occurring alongside events the text has just described as involving thousands of unseen deities.

Crossing the Ganges, and the closing verse

At the river, described as so full “a crow could drink from it,” ordinary people are busy searching for boats, dinghies, or materials to tie a raft. The Buddha and the Saṅgha, by contrast, simply vanish from the near shore and appear on the far one, “as easily as a strong person would extend or contract their arm.” Watching the people still working to cross, the Buddha speaks the discourse’s closing heartfelt sentiment: those who have “built a bridge and left the marshes behind” have already crossed, while others are still tying their raft. The verse turns the entire episode — village teaching, prophecy, and river crossing alike — toward a single image of the difference between having crossed over and still preparing to.

Key terms

sīla — “ethics”: the single variable the five drawbacks and five benefits both turn on, independent of caste or rank.
the five and five — wealth, reputation, confidence in assemblies, a settled mind at death, and destination after death, each paired for the unethical and the ethical person in turn.
Pāṭaliputta — the “trade center” the Buddha prophesies here, later the historical capital of the Mauryan empire, present-day Patna.
the three threats — fire, flood, and dissension: the specific dangers the Buddha names for the future city, alongside his prediction of its greatness.
the Gotama Gate, the Gotama Ford — names the ministers privately decide to give the places the Buddha passes through, an ordinary gesture of honor set beside an extraordinary crossing.

The text

The discourse in full, given complete and without abbreviation. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Arrival at Pāṭali Village

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was wandering in the land of the Magadhans together with a large Saṅgha of mendicants when he arrived at the village of Pāṭali. The lay followers of Pāṭali Village heard that he had arrived. So they went to see him, bowed, sat down to one side, and said to him, “Sir, please consent to come to our guest house.” The Buddha consented with silence.

§2Then, knowing that the Buddha had consented, the lay followers of Pāṭali Village rose from their seat, bowed, and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on their right. Then they went to the guest house, where they spread carpets all over, prepared seats, set up a water jar, and placed an oil lamp. Then they went back to the Buddha, bowed, stood to one side, and told him of their preparations, saying: “Please, sir, come at your convenience.”

§3In the morning, the Buddha robed up and, taking his bowl and robe, went to the guest house together with the Saṅgha of mendicants. Having washed his feet he entered the guest house and sat against the central column facing east. The Saṅgha of mendicants also washed their feet, entered the guest house, and sat against the west wall facing east, with the Buddha right in front of them. The lay followers of Pāṭali Village also washed their feet, entered the guest house, and sat against the east wall facing west, with the Buddha right in front of them. Then the Buddha addressed them:

The five drawbacks, the five benefits

§4–8“Householders, there are these five drawbacks for an unethical person because of their failure in ethics. What five? Firstly, an unethical person loses great wealth on account of negligence. This is the first drawback. Furthermore, an unethical person gets a bad reputation. This is the second drawback. Furthermore, an unethical person enters any kind of assembly timid and embarrassed, whether it’s an assembly of aristocrats, brahmins, householders, or ascetics. This is the third drawback. Furthermore, an unethical person feels lost when they die. This is the fourth drawback. Furthermore, an unethical person, when their body breaks up, after death, is reborn in a place of loss, a bad place, the underworld, hell. This is the fifth drawback. These are the five drawbacks for an unethical person because of their failure in ethics.

§9–13There are these five benefits for an ethical person because of their accomplishment in ethics. What five? Firstly, an ethical person gains great wealth on account of diligence. This is the first benefit. Furthermore, an ethical person gets a good reputation. This is the second benefit. Furthermore, an ethical person enters any kind of assembly bold and self-assured, whether it’s an assembly of aristocrats, brahmins, householders, or ascetics. This is the third benefit. Furthermore, an ethical person dies not feeling lost. This is the fourth benefit. Furthermore, when an ethical person’s body breaks up, after death, they’re reborn in a good place, a heavenly realm. This is the fifth benefit. These are the five benefits for an ethical person because of their accomplishment in ethics.”

§14The Buddha spent much of the night educating, encouraging, firing up, and inspiring the lay followers of Pāṭali Village with a Dhamma talk. Then he sent them off, “The night is getting late, householders. Please go at your convenience.” And then the lay followers of Pāṭali Village approved and agreed with what the Buddha said. They rose from their seat, bowed, and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on their right, before leaving. Soon after they left the Buddha entered a private cubicle.

Deities, and a prophecy about a great city

§15Now at that time the Magadhan ministers Sunidha and Vassakāra were building a citadel at Pāṭali Village to keep the Vajjis out. At that time thousands of deities were taking possession of building sites in Pāṭali Village. Illustrious rulers or royal chief ministers inclined to build houses at sites possessed by illustrious deities. Middling rulers or royal chief ministers inclined to build houses at sites possessed by middling deities. Lesser rulers or royal chief ministers inclined to build houses at sites possessed by lesser deities.

§16–17With clairvoyance that is purified and superhuman, the Buddha saw those deities taking possession of building sites in Pāṭali Village, and the people building houses in accord with the station of the deities. The Buddha rose at the crack of dawn and addressed Ānanda, “Ānanda, who is building a citadel at Pāṭali Village?” “Sir, the Magadhan chief ministers Sunidha and Vassakāra are building a citadel to keep the Vajjis out.” “It’s as if they were building the citadel in consultation with the gods of the thirty-three. With clairvoyance that is purified and superhuman, I saw those deities taking possession of building sites. Illustrious rulers or royal chief ministers inclined to build houses at sites possessed by illustrious deities. Middling rulers or royal chief ministers inclined to build houses at sites possessed by middling deities. Lesser rulers or royal chief ministers inclined to build houses at sites possessed by lesser deities. As far as the civilized region extends, as far as the trading zone extends, this will be the chief city: the Pāṭaliputta trade center. But Pāṭaliputta will face three threats: from fire, flood, and dissension.”

A meal, and the naming of a gate

§18–19Then the Magadhan ministers Sunidha and Vassakāra approached the Buddha, and exchanged greetings with him. When the greetings and polite conversation were over, they stood to one side and said, “Would Mister Gotama together with the mendicant Saṅgha please accept today’s meal from me?” Then, knowing that the Buddha had consented, they went to their own guest house, where they had delicious fresh and cooked foods prepared. Then they had the Buddha informed of the time, saying, “It’s time, worthy Gotama, the meal is ready.”

§20Then the Buddha robed up in the morning and, taking his bowl and robe, went to their guest house together with the mendicant Saṅgha, where he sat on the seat spread out. Then Sunidha and Vassakāra served and satisfied the mendicant Saṅgha headed by the Buddha with their own hands with delicious fresh and cooked foods.

§21When the Buddha had eaten and washed his hand and bowl, Sunidha and Vassakāra took a low seat and sat to one side. The Buddha expressed his appreciation with these verses:

§22–24“In the place he makes his dwelling,
having fed the astute
and the virtuous here,
the restrained spiritual practitioners,
he should dedicate an offering
to the deities there.
Venerated, they venerate him;
honored, they honor him.
After that they have sympathy for him,
like a mother for the child at her breast.
A man beloved of the deities
always sees nice things.”

§25When the Buddha had expressed his appreciation to Sunidha and Vassakāra with these verses, he rose from his seat and left.

§26–27Sunidha and Vassakāra followed behind the Buddha, thinking, “The gate through which the ascetic Gotama exits today shall be named the Gotama Gate. The ford at which he crosses the Ganges River shall be named the Gotama Ford.” Then the gate through which the Buddha exited was named the Gotama Gate. Then the Buddha came to the Ganges River. Now at that time the Ganges was full to the brim so a crow could drink from it.

Crossing the Ganges, and the closing verse

§27–28Wanting to cross from the near to the far shore, some people were seeking a boat, some a dinghy, while some were tying up a raft. But, as easily as a strong person would extend or contract their arm, the Buddha, together with the mendicant Saṅgha, vanished from the near shore and landed on the far shore. He saw all those humans wanting to cross over.

§29Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§30“Those who cross a deluge or stream
have built a bridge and left the marshes behind.
While some people are still tying a raft,
intelligent people have crossed over.”

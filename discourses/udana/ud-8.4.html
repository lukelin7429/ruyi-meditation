Udāna · Discourse 8.4

About Extinguishment (4th)

Catutthanibbānapaṭisaṁyuttasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery, closing the same recurring setting as Ud 8.1–8.3
Speakers
The Buddha, addressing the assembled mendicants collectively
Form
A short prose heartfelt sentiment (udāna), structured as a seven-link causal chain rather than a list of negations
Length
<1 minute to read
Position
Fourth and last of the four consecutive “About Extinguishment” discourses opening the Pāṭaligāmiyavagga
Difficulty
★★★★★ — a tightly linked chain, easy to skim past without tracing each step

Why this discourse

The fourth and final “About Extinguishment” passage takes a different shape from its three predecessors. Rather than negating a list of categories (Ud 8.1), naming a single mental event (Ud 8.2), or arguing from necessity (Ud 8.3), it traces a chain: one condition giving rise to the next, giving rise to the next, seven links in all, from dependence and agitation down to the end of suffering. Read closely, it shows a process rather than only stating a conclusion.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

In seven linked steps, the Buddha traces how dependence produces agitation while independence does not; how the absence of agitation yields tranquility; how tranquility removes inclination; and how, once inclination is gone, coming and going, passing away and reappearing, and finally this life, the next life, and anything between the two, all cease — concluding, as in Ud 8.1, “just this is the end of suffering.”

Same setting, fourth and final pass

The frame repeats once more, word for word: the ongoing Dhamma talk, the attentive mendicants, the heartfelt sentiment arising from “understanding this matter.” This is the fourth time in a row the collection has used the identical narrative setting, changing only the content of what the Buddha says — a structure that itself suggests four attempts at approaching one difficult subject from four different angles, rather than four unrelated teachings.

The chain, link by link

Unlike its three predecessors, this passage is built as a sequence of conditional steps, each one setting up the next: dependence produces agitation; independence removes it; the absence of agitation is tranquility; tranquility removes inclination; the absence of inclination removes coming and going; the absence of coming and going removes passing away and reappearing; and the absence of passing away and reappearing removes this life, the next life, and anything in between. Seven links, each stated as following necessarily from the one before it.

Dependent and independent

The chain’s starting point is a contrast between the “dependent” (nissita) and the “independent” (anissita) — someone leaning on, relying on, or attached to something, versus someone who is not. This is a psychological starting point rather than a cosmological one: the passage begins not with a description of nibbāna’s nature but with a description of a mind’s relationship to what it clings to, and only then follows that relationship’s consequences all the way down to the end of suffering.

From agitation to tranquility to no inclination

The first three links form a recognizable psychological progression: reliance produces a kind of restlessness or agitation (calitaṁ); its absence is tranquility (passaddhi); and tranquility, in turn, removes “inclination” (nati) — the same kind of leaning or tilting negated by the word “uninclined” in Ud 8.2. The chain, in other words, moves from an observable state of mind (agitated or calm) toward the same subtle quality Ud 8.2 named directly.

No this life, the next, or between the two

The final links extend the chain outward from psychology into the entire framework of rebirth: no coming and going, then no passing away and reappearing, then, in the passage’s most sweeping phrase, “no this life or the next or between the two” — explicitly ruling out not just this world and a next world, but any intermediate state between them. The chain closes exactly where Ud 8.1 closes: “just this is the end of suffering,” the one positive statement earned by everything that precedes it.

How this differs from its three predecessors

Ud 8.1 negates a list of categories all at once. Ud 8.2 names a single achievement in compressed verse. Ud 8.3 argues from necessity, in conditional form. Ud 8.4 alone traces a process: it shows how getting from ordinary, dependent experience to the end of suffering actually proceeds, step by step, rather than simply asserting that the endpoint exists or describing what it is not. Read as the close of a four-part sequence, it adds something the other three do not attempt — a path, however compressed, rather than only a destination.

The same restraint, once more

Even here, the passage does not describe tranquility, non-inclination, or the end of coming and going in positive experiential terms — it names them and moves directly to the next link in the chain. The four “About Extinguishment” discourses, taken together, use negation, compressed verse, conditional argument, and causal chain as four different tools, and every one of them stops short of offering the kind of positive metaphysical picture a reader might reach for. That consistency, across four quite different literary forms, looks less like coincidence and more like a considered choice.

Key terms

nissita / anissita — “dependent / independent”: leaning or relying on something, versus not; the chain’s psychological starting point.
calitaṁ — “agitation”: the first consequence of dependence in the chain, removed by independence.
passaddhi — “tranquility”: what remains when agitation is absent; a term also used elsewhere for one of the seven factors of awakening.
nati — “inclination”: the same leaning or tilting quality negated directly by “uninclined” (anata) in Ud 8.2, here removed as a further link in the chain.
idha, huraṁ, ubhayamantarena — “this life, the next, or between the two”: the chain’s final, most sweeping negation, closing off every temporal location a rebirth could occupy.

The text

The discourse in full, given complete and without abbreviation. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time the Buddha was educating, encouraging, firing up, and inspiring the mendicants with a Dhamma talk about extinguishment. And those mendicants were paying attention, applying the mind, concentrating wholeheartedly, and actively listening.

§2Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§3“For the dependent there is agitation. For the independent there’s no agitation. When there’s no agitation there is tranquility. When there is tranquility there’s no inclination. When there’s no inclination, there’s no coming and going. When there’s no coming and going, there’s no passing away and reappearing. When there’s no passing away and reappearing there’s no this life or the next or between the two. Just this is the end of suffering.”

Udāna · Discourse 1.10

With Bāhiya

Bāhiyasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery — the endpoint of a journey that began far away, at Suppāraka on the ocean shore
Speaker
The Buddha, teaching Bāhiya of the Bark Cloth directly, on the street, while on his own alms round
Theme
A mistaken sense of attainment is corrected, an urgent journey follows, and a single brief instruction on bare perception brings immediate, complete liberation — followed within minutes by Bāhiya’s sudden death
Length
~3 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — the longest and philosophically densest discourse in this opening chapter

One of the most famous teachings in the whole canon

This is the discourse the whole rest of the chapter has been building toward without announcing it: the closing episode of the Udāna’s first ten suttas, and one of the single most quoted passages anywhere in the Pali Canon. A man who believes himself already liberated is corrected by a stranger, races across the subcontinent without a night’s rest, and receives — while the Buddha is mid-almsround, having twice already said “it’s not the time” — a teaching so brief it can be recited from memory, and so immediately effective that he is fully freed before the Buddha has even finished walking away. He is dead within minutes of hearing it. Given both its length and its stature, this reading guide takes it more slowly and more fully than the nine discourses before it.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Bāhiya, urgently seeking a teacher after a deity corrects his false sense of attainment, is given a single brief instruction by the Buddha — “in the seen will be merely the seen… in the known will be merely the known” — is freed from all defilement on the spot, and dies minutes later, having, in the Buddha’s own words, become “fully quenched.”

A mistaken certainty

The discourse opens not with a request for teaching but with a private, unspoken conviction. Bāhiya, living at Suppāraka on the ocean shore, is “honored, respected, revered, venerated, and esteemed,” provided with robes, almsfood, lodging, and medicine. In private retreat, the thought comes to him: “I am one of those in the world who are perfected or on the path to perfection.” Nothing in the text suggests this is spoken aloud or tested against anyone else’s judgment — it is simply what reverence and material comfort have allowed him to conclude about himself, alone.

A stranger's honest correction

A deity, described as a former relative of Bāhiya, intervenes “having sympathy and wanting what’s best for him” — and what that sympathy produces is not encouragement but direct contradiction: “Bāhiya, you’re not a perfected one, nor on the path to perfection. You don’t have the practice by which you might become” one. The deity doesn’t soften this or explain further; it simply names the actual teacher Bāhiya needs to find, in a city Bāhiya has apparently never visited. Kindness, in this scene, takes the form of disruption rather than comfort.

A journey without a single night's rest

What follows is described with real physical urgency. Suppāraka lay on the western coast; Sāvatthī, where the Buddha was staying, lay far to the north. Bāhiya leaves “right away,” and the text notes specifically that he sojourns “no more than a single night in any place” for the entire distance. The intensity of the journey mirrors the intensity that has suddenly opened up inside him — from settled contentment to something closer to desperation, in the space of a single conversation.

Three refusals, then insistence

Arriving at last, Bāhiya finds the Buddha already out on his alms round and rushes to him, bowing at his feet and asking, twice, to be taught. Both times the Buddha declines: “it’s not the time, Bāhiya, so long as I have entered an inhabited area for almsfood.” It is Bāhiya’s third appeal that changes the outcome, and its reasoning is stark: “you never know, sir, when life is at risk, either the Buddha’s or my own.” The line will prove literally true for Bāhiya within the same episode — and it is presumably this note of real urgency, not mere persistence, that finally moves the Buddha to teach him standing in the street.

The teaching itself: “in the seen, merely the seen”

The instruction is remarkably compact: train so that in the seen there is merely the seen, in the heard merely the heard, in the thought merely the thought, in the known merely the known. Four doors of experience — sight, hearing, thought, and cognition generally — are each named and each reduced to bare registering, with nothing added on top. The Buddha then traces the consequence: trained this way, “you won’t be ‘by that’ ” — not identified with or defined by the experience; and “not ‘by that’ ” leads to not being “‘in that’ ” — no longer situated inside the experience as a self having it; and being “not ‘in that’ ” means not being “in this life or the next or between the two.” The final line states the result plainly: “just this is the end of suffering.” Read as a whole, the instruction describes a stripping-away, step by step, of everything the mind ordinarily builds on top of bare perception — the sense of a self standing behind the seeing, receiving the seen, carrying it forward into an identity that continues in time.

Awakened, then killed

The text reports the effect immediately and without qualification: “due to this brief Dhamma teaching… Bāhiya’s mind was right away freed from defilements by not grasping.” Then, in the very same breath of narration, comes the discourse’s most jarring turn: the Buddha leaves, and “soon after,” before anything further can happen, “a cow with a baby calf charged at Bāhiya and took his life.” The text offers no transition, no foreshadowing beyond Bāhiya’s own words about not knowing “when life is at risk,” and no commentary bridging the two events. Complete liberation and sudden, ordinary death are simply set side by side, unexplained — the discourse does not suggest that awakening protects a person from an incidental accident, nor does it dwell on the apparent unfairness of the timing.

“Fully quenched” — the Buddha's verdict

When the mendicants report Bāhiya’s cremation and ask where he has been reborn, the Buddha’s answer bypasses the question of rebirth altogether: “Bāhiya was astute. He practiced in line with the teaching, and did not trouble me about the teachings. Bāhiya of the Bark Cloth has become fully quenched.” The remark about not troubling the Buddha with further questions is worth pausing on — it credits Bāhiya’s achievement partly to how directly and completely he simply acted on what he was told, without requiring elaboration.

The closing verse: describing what awakening is not

The verse that follows describes the state Bāhiya has reached entirely through negation. Water, earth, fire, and air — the four elements used throughout the canon as the basic constituents of the physical world — have “no footing” there; no star shines, the sun sheds no light, the moon does not glow, and yet “no darkness is found.” Every ordinary reference point a listener might use to picture some place or condition is named and then withdrawn, refusing any positive, describable image. Only in the second half does the verse turn toward what can be said: a sage who finds understanding through their own sagacity is released “from forms and formless, from pleasure and pain” — freedom described, at last, not as a location but as a release from every pair of opposites experience can offer.

One of the shortest paths to awakening in the entire canon

What makes this discourse exceptional within the Udāna, and within the wider canon, is its sheer compression. There is no described lifetime of gradual training here, no sequence of meditative stages spelled out. A single, brief spoken instruction, given almost reluctantly in the middle of an alms round, is immediately understood and immediately liberating — for a listener whose journey to receive it, and whose insistence in the face of two refusals, turn out to have been exactly as urgent as his own words claimed.

Key terms

Bāhiya of the Bark Cloth — the wandering ascetic at the center of this discourse, named for the bark-fiber garment he wore; honored at Suppāraka before his journey to see the Buddha.
“merely the seen… merely the known” — the core instruction: training the four doors of experience — sight, hearing, thought, cognition — to register nothing beyond bare occurrence.
“not ‘by that’ … not ‘in that’ ” — the instruction’s description of the successive removal of a constructed self standing behind or within experience.
parinibbuta — “fully quenched”: the Buddha’s own term for what Bāhiya achieved, bypassing any question of where he was reborn.
the four elements’ “no footing” — the closing verse’s method of describing Nibbāna entirely by negating every familiar physical and cosmic reference point.

The text

The discourse in full: Bāhiya’s mistaken certainty, the deity’s correction, the urgent journey, the threefold request, the teaching itself, Bāhiya’s sudden death, and the closing verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A mistaken thought

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time Bāhiya of the Bark Cloth was residing by Suppāraka on the ocean shore, where he was honored, respected, revered, venerated, and esteemed. And he received robes, almsfood, lodgings, and medicines and supplies for the sick. Then as he was in private retreat this thought came to his mind, “I am one of those in the world who are perfected or on the path to perfection.”

A deity's warning

§2Then a deity who was a former relative of Bāhiya, having sympathy and wanting what’s best for him, approached him and said: “Bāhiya, you’re not a perfected one, nor on the path to perfection. You don’t have the practice by which you might become a perfected one or one on the path to perfection.”

§3“So who then are those in the world who are perfected or on the path to perfection?” “In the northern lands there is a city named Sāvatthī. There that Blessed One is now staying, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha. He is a perfected one and teaches the Dhamma for the sake of perfection.”

A journey without rest

§4Impelled by that deity, Bāhiya left Suppāraka right away. Sojourning no more than a single night in any place, he made his way to Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery in the Jeta Grove at Sāvatthī. At that time several mendicants were walking mindfully in the open air. Bāhiya approached them and said, “Sirs, where is the Blessed One at present, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha? For I want to see him.” “He has entered an inhabited area for almsfood, Bāhiya.”

Three urgent requests

§5Then Bāhiya rushed out of the Jeta Grove and entered Sāvatthī, where he saw the Buddha walking for alms. He was impressive and inspiring, with peaceful faculties and heart, attained to the highest self-control and serenity, like an elephant with tamed, guarded, and controlled faculties. Bāhiya went up to the Buddha, bowed down with his head at the Buddha’s feet, and said, “Sir, let the Blessed One teach me the Dhamma! Let the Holy One teach me the Dhamma! That would be for my lasting welfare and happiness.” The Buddha said this: “It’s not the time, Bāhiya, so long as I have entered an inhabited area for almsfood.”

§6For a second time, Bāhiya said, “But you never know, sir, when life is at risk, either the Buddha’s or my own. Let the Blessed One teach me the Dhamma! Let the Holy One teach me the Dhamma! That would be for my lasting welfare and happiness.” For a second time, the Buddha said, “It’s not the time, Bāhiya, so long as I have entered an inhabited area for almsfood.”

§7For a third time, Bāhiya said, “But you never know, sir, when life is at risk, either the Buddha’s or my own. Let the Blessed One teach me the Dhamma! Let the Holy One teach me the Dhamma! That would be for my lasting welfare and happiness.”

The teaching

§8“In that case, Bāhiya, you should train like this: ‘In the seen will be merely the seen; in the heard will be merely the heard; in the thought will be merely the thought; in the known will be merely the known.’ That’s how you should train. When you have trained in this way, you won’t be ‘by that’. When you’re not ‘by that’, you won’t be ‘in that’. When you’re not ‘in that’, you won’t be in this life or the next or between the two. Just this is the end of suffering.”

Freed on the spot

§9Then, due to this brief Dhamma teaching of the Buddha, Bāhiya’s mind was right away freed from defilements by not grasping.

A sudden death

§10And when the Buddha had given Bāhiya this brief advice he left. But soon after the Buddha had left, a cow with a baby calf charged at Bāhiya and took his life.

§11Then the Buddha wandered for alms in Sāvatthī. After the meal, on his return from almsround, he exited the city together with several mendicants and saw that Bāhiya had passed away. He said to the monks, “Mendicants, pick up Bāhiya’s corpse. Having lifted it onto a cot and carried it, cremate it and build a monument. Mendicants, one of your spiritual companions has passed away.”

§12“Yes, sir,” replied those mendicants. They did as the Buddha asked, then returned to the Buddha and said, “Sir, Bāhiya’s corpse has been cremated and a monument built for him. Where has he been reborn in his next life?” “Mendicants, Bāhiya was astute. He practiced in line with the teaching, and did not trouble me about the teachings. Bāhiya of the Bark Cloth has become fully quenched.”

The heartfelt saying

§13Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§14–15“Where water and earth,
fire and air have no footing—
there no star does shine,
nor does the sun shed its light;
there the moon glows not,
yet no darkness is found.

And when a sage, a brahmin, finds understanding
through their own sagacity,
then, from forms and formless,
from pleasure and pain they are released.”

§16This too is a heartfelt saying that was spoken by the Blessed One: that is what I heard.

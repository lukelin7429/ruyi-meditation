Udāna · Discourse 7.10

King Udena

Utenasutta

Setting
Near Kosambī, at Ghosita’s Monastery
Occasion
A fire destroys King Udena’s royal compound while he is away, killing five hundred women led by Queen Sāmāvatī; mendicants ask the Buddha where they were reborn
Form
A brief report followed by a direct question and answer, closed by a four-line inspired utterance (udāna)
Length
~1 minute to read
What the text does not say
No cause of the fire is given, and no other figures (such as a rival co-wife) are named or blamed — see the reading guide below
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — plain in its events, but raises a real question about tragedy and attainment

Why this discourse, closing the chapter

This is the only discourse in the chapter to report a mass death, and it is worth being exact about what the text actually contains, since the story of King Udena and Queen Sāmāvatī is elaborated at great length in later commentarial literature — rival wives, a plot involving deliberate arson, extended biographies of the women who died. None of that appears in this sutta. What the canonical text itself gives is far sparer: a fire, a death toll, a name, and a question the mendicants themselves ask about what became of the dead.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Told that five hundred women, led by Queen Sāmāvatī, have died in a fire while King Udena was away, the Buddha states plainly that some had already reached the first three stages of awakening and that “none of those laywomen died without some fruit of the practice” — then adds a verse about a world caught up in delusion that only looks, to those still in it, like it makes sense.

What the text actually reports

The frame is brief and matter-of-fact: “while King Udena was visiting a park, his royal compound burned down. Five hundred women died, with Queen Sāmāvatī at their head.” That is the entire account of the event itself — no cause of the fire, no perpetrator, no account of how or why it happened. Mendicants on their ordinary almsround hear of it, report it to the Buddha as they would any other piece of news, and then ask him a direct question: “Sir, where have those laywomen been reborn in the next life?”

This reading guide deliberately stops here rather than filling in the well-known later tradition surrounding Sāmāvatī’s death, which involves a rival co-wife, a deliberate plot, and a much longer cast of characters. Whatever their historical basis, those details belong to commentarial material composed later and are not part of this canonical text. Presenting them as though this sutta told that fuller story would misrepresent what the discourse actually says.

An answer about attainment, not about the fire

The Buddha’s answer addresses only the mendicants’ actual question — not why the fire happened, but what became of those who died in it: “among those laywomen there were stream-enterers, once-returners, and non-returners.” These are the first three of the four stages of awakening recognized across the early texts, each marking a different degree of progress toward full liberation. The Buddha’s summary judgment is stated as an assurance rather than a claim about every individual’s specific attainment: “none of those laywomen died without some fruit of the practice.” The text does not itemize who reached which stage, or say anything about Sāmāvatī specifically beyond naming her as first among the dead.

A world that seems to make sense

The closing verse turns from the specific event to a general reflection on how delusion operates: “the world is caught up in delusion, but is looked on as making sense.” This is not a comment on the fire’s cause but on ordinary perception generally — the world’s apparent coherence and stability is itself, the verse suggests, part of what keeps beings caught. “The fool caught up in attachment is surrounded by darkness” and experiences things “as if eternal,” while “for one who sees, there is nothing” permanent to hold onto. Read against the fire that opens the discourse, the verse offers no explanation of tragedy, no theodicy; it offers instead a description of two different ways the same impermanent world can be seen.

A quiet close to the chapter

As the final discourse in the Cūḷavagga, this sutta ends the chapter on an unresolved note rather than a triumphant one — genuine loss, a plain question about what such loss means for people the mendicants evidently respected, and an answer that offers assurance without pretending the tragedy did not happen. It is a fitting, sober counterpart to the miracle at the well in Ud 7.9 immediately before it.

Key terms

Sāmāvatī — named in the text only as “Queen Sāmāvatī,” first among the five hundred women who died; the text gives no further biographical detail about her.
sotāpanna, sakadāgāmī, anāgāmī (stream-enterer, once-returner, non-returner) — the first three of the four stages of awakening recognized in the early texts, named here as attainments some of the dead women had reached.
“some fruit of the practice” — the Buddha’s summary assurance about all the laywomen who died, stated generally rather than itemized individual by individual.
sammohaṁ (delusion) — the state the verse says the world is “caught up in,” even as it “is looked on as making sense” by those still within it.
“for one who sees, there is nothing” — the verse’s closing contrast to the world’s deceptive appearance of permanence, describing how the same impermanent world looks to someone free of delusion.

The text

The discourse in full — a brief report, a direct question, and the Buddha’s answer, closed by a six-line inspired utterance. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A fire, and a question

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Kosambī, in Ghosita’s Monastery. Now at that time, while King Udena was visiting a park, his royal compound burned down. Five hundred women died, with Queen Sāmāvatī at their head.

§2Then several mendicants robed up in the morning and, taking their bowls and robes, entered Kosambī for alms. Then, after the meal, when they returned from almsround, they went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and told him had happened. They asked the Buddha, “Sir, where have those laywomen been reborn in the next life?”

An answer about attainment

§3“Among those laywomen there were stream-enterers, once-returners, and non-returners. None of those laywomen died without some fruit of the practice.”

§4Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

The verse

§5“The world is caught up in delusion,
but is looked on as making sense.
The fool caught up in attachment
is surrounded by darkness.
It seems as if eternal,
but for one who sees, there is nothing.”

Udāna · Ud 6.3

The Buddha’s Reviewing

Paccavekkhaṇasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speakers
None — a wholly private moment, with no dialogue or listener recorded
Form
The barest structural pattern in the Udāna: a one-sentence frame, then four lines of verse
Length
Under 1 minute to read
Note
Among the shortest texts in the entire Pali Canon
Difficulty
★★★★★ — brief in length, genuinely difficult to pin down in meaning

Why this heartfelt saying

At two sentences of narration and four lines of verse, Ud 6.3 is among the shortest texts anywhere in the Pali Canon. It records no conversation and no audience — only the Buddha sitting alone at Jeta’s Grove, reviewing his own mind, and a saying compressed almost to the point of riddle. This reading guide tries to stay honest about that compression rather than resolving it into something tidier than the text actually offers.

Reading guide

A private review

The entire narrative frame is a single sentence: the Buddha is “sitting reviewing his own giving up of many bad, unskillful qualities, and the many skillful qualities he had fully developed.” No one asks him a question. No one is described as present. Unlike almost every other sutta in this chapter — where a king arrives, or mendicants report some disturbance, or a visitor appears — this one has no external occasion at all. The only event is an internal one: an act of deliberate self-review.

The saying itself

What follows is genuinely compressed: “What was before then was not; what before was not then was. It never was, nor will it be, nor is it found today.” Read against the sentence that precedes it, a cautious first pass is possible: the “bad, unskillful qualities” that were once present are now simply absent, and the “skillful qualities” once absent are now fully present — a description of a mind that has been completely remade. But the second half of the verse pushes further than that reading comfortably accounts for. “It never was, nor will it be, nor is it found today” does not read like a report of one quality replacing another; it reads more like a claim about something that, from the standpoint the Buddha now occupies, was never really there to begin with — as if the entire question of “was, is, or will be” no longer quite applies.

Reading it honestly

This guide will not claim more certainty about that second reading than it deserves. The text supplies no further explanation, no interlocutor to ask a clarifying question, and no narrative payoff of the kind that closes most of the other suttas in this chapter. What can be said plainly is this: the verse is built on paradox rather than plain description, and it seems designed to resist being paraphrased into something less strange than it is. Given the choice between forcing a single confident reading and leaving the difficulty visible, this guide chooses the latter — which is, admittedly, an unusual thing for a reading guide to do, but seems truer to what a two-line, unwitnessed utterance can actually bear.

Key terms

paccavekkhaṇa — “reviewing” or “reflecting upon” — the sutta’s title, and the exact act described: the Buddha privately surveying which qualities he has abandoned and which he has developed.
akusala dhammā — “bad, unskillful qualities” — what the text says the Buddha reviews himself as having given up, in the plural and unspecified.
kusalā dhammā — “skillful qualities” — what he reviews himself as having “fully developed,” again left unspecified.
udāna — the genre term for the whole collection: a spontaneous “heartfelt saying.” Here it arises from no dialogue at all — only silent self-reflection, one of the clearest examples in the collection of the form functioning exactly as its name suggests.
“it never was, nor will it be” — the sutta’s closing line, and its central difficulty: a claim that resists a single settled reading.

The text

The complete text: a one-sentence frame, and the four-line verse that follows it. Nothing else is recorded. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time the Buddha was sitting reviewing his own giving up of many bad, unskillful qualities, and the many skillful qualities he had fully developed.

§2Then, knowing the many bad, unskillful qualities that he had given up and the many skillful qualities he had fully developed, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§3“What was before then was not; what before was not then was. It never was, nor will it be, nor is it found today.”

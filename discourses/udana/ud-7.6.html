Udāna · Discourse 7.6

The Ending of Craving

Taṇhāsaṅkhayasutta

Setting

Why this discourse, a name earned decades earlier

The monk at the center of this discourse carries an unusual honorific: aññāsi, “who understood.” It was given to him, according to the tradition, the moment he grasped the Buddha’s very first teaching at Varanasi — a moment celebrated elsewhere in this reading series (see SN 56.11). This sutta shows him much later, simply sitting near the Buddha, quietly reviewing a freedom that name only partially described when it was first given.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Watching Venerable Aññāsikoṇḍañña sit quietly, reviewing his own freedom through the ending of craving, the Buddha speaks a verse describing a plant with no root, ground, or leaves left for anything to grow from — praised, it says, even by the gods and by the Divinity himself.

Reviewing, not attaining

Unlike the Bhaddiya discourses earlier in this chapter, nothing is newly achieved here. The text is explicit about the tense: Koṇḍañña is “reviewing the freedom through the ending of craving” (paccavekkhamāno), a term the canon uses for looking back over something already attained, not for attaining it in the moment. He sits “cross-legged, with his body straight” — the standard description of formal seated meditation — simply confirming, for himself, a freedom that is already his.

A name from long before

The text names him “Koṇḍañña Who Understood,” using the honorific aññāsi that, according to the wider tradition, was first applied to him at the very founding of the Saṅgha: he was the first of the Buddha’s companions to grasp the teaching at Varanasi, prompting the Buddha’s own exclamation, “Koṇḍañña has understood! Koṇḍañña has understood!” That founding moment is told in full elsewhere (see SN 56.11 in this reading series). This discourse offers no narrative bridge between that early event and this later scene — only the same honorific name, attached now to a much more settled, complete kind of understanding.

No root, no ground, no leaves

The verse’s central image is botanical: “there is no root or ground or leaves for them, so where would creepers sprout from?” A creeper needs all three — a root to draw from, ground to anchor in, and leaves to grow toward — and craving is pictured the same way throughout the canon, as something that needs conditions to keep spreading. Removing all three conditions at once, rather than merely trimming what has already grown, is the verse’s way of describing complete, rather than partial, freedom.

The closing lines shift from image to reputation: “who is worthy to criticize them? Even the gods praise them, and by the Divinity, too, they’re praised.” “The Divinity” (Brahmā) names the highest deity of the Brahmanical cosmology the early texts operate within — praise from this figure functions as the highest conceivable outside endorsement, placed here beside the praise of ordinary gods to underline just how uncontested this freedom is.

A quiet counterpart

Read beside Ud 7.8, the next-but-one discourse in this chapter, a pattern becomes visible: several suttas in the Cūḷavagga simply show the Buddha watching a senior disciple meditate, and responding in verse to what he observes, without any dialogue at all. This discourse and Ud 7.8 (about Mahākaccāna) share almost the same frame, down to the sitting posture described, suggesting a deliberate grouping of “the Buddha watches a disciple meditate” scenes within this chapter.

Key terms

Aññāsikoṇḍañña — “Koṇḍañña Who Understood,” traditionally the first of the Buddha’s disciples to grasp the Dhamma, at the founding teaching told in SN 56.11.
taṇhāsaṅkhayavimutti (freedom through the ending of craving) — the specific attainment Koṇḍañña is described as reviewing, not newly reaching, in this discourse.
paccavekkhamāno (reviewing) — a canonical term for looking back over an attainment already achieved, distinguishing this quiet scene from the sudden liberations described elsewhere in this chapter.
mūla, chamā, paṇṇā (root, ground, leaves) — the three conditions a creeper needs to grow, all named as absent in the verse’s image of craving completely uprooted.
Brahmā (“the Divinity”) — the highest deity of the cosmology the early texts assume; the verse names praise from this figure as the highest conceivable outside endorsement.

The text

The discourse in full — a brief narrative frame with no recorded dialogue, closed by a six-line inspired utterance. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Reviewing a freedom already won

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time Venerable Koṇḍañña Who Understood was sitting not far from the Buddha, cross-legged, with his body straight, reviewing the freedom through the ending of craving.

§2The Buddha saw him meditating there.

§3Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

The verse

§4“There is no root or ground or leaves for them,
so where would creepers sprout from?
That attentive one is released from bonds:
who is worthy to criticize them?
Even the gods praise them,
and by the Divinity, too, they’re praised.”

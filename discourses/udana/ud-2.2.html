Udāna · Heartfelt Sayings 2.2

Kings

Rājasutta

Setting
Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speaker
The Buddha, addressing a group of mendicants after overhearing their conversation
Occasion
Mendicants argue over which of two kings, Bimbisāra of Magadha or Pasenadi of Kosala, has greater wealth and power
Form
Narrative frame, a brief correction, then a four-line heartfelt saying
Length
About 2 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a simple scene with a sharply pointed comparison

Why this heartfelt saying, second of the chapter

A group of mendicants, gathered after their almsround, fall into an argument that could happen anywhere: which of two wealthy, powerful neighbors has more? The Buddha’s response corrects the question before answering anything about kings at all — and then supplies a comparison so lopsided it makes the original argument look almost quaint.

Reading guide

An unfinished argument

The frame is almost comic in its ordinariness: a cluster of mendicants, gathered in the assembly hall after their almsround meal, fall into a very human argument — which of two kings has the greater fortune, dominion, forces, and might, Seniya Bimbāsāra of Magadha or Pasenadi of Kosala? Both kings appear elsewhere across the canon as real patrons and interlocutors of the Buddha, so this isn’t an abstract dispute; it’s the kind of comparison anyone might make about two prominent, wealthy neighbors. The conversation is left “unfinished” when the Buddha arrives from his retreat in the late afternoon and asks what they’d been discussing.

Not what you gave up the household life for

The Buddha’s response is direct and a little pointed: “it is not appropriate for you gentlemen who have gone forth out of faith from the lay life to homelessness to talk about such things.” He doesn’t explain at length why; instead he gives a simple, twofold instruction for what mendicants sitting together should do instead — “discuss the teachings or keep noble silence.” The implicit point is about proportion: measuring one king’s treasury against another’s is exactly the kind of worldly comparison the going-forth was meant to leave behind.

A comparison of a different order

The heartfelt saying. “Neither the pleasures of the senses, nor even divine happiness, is worth even a sixteenth part of the happiness of craving’s end.”

Having redirected the mendicants away from one comparison, the Buddha’s heartfelt saying supplies a different one — and a far more extreme mismatch than Bimbāsāra versus Pasenadi. It isn’t sensual pleasure against divine pleasure that’s being ranked (impressive enough a contrast on its own, human wealth against heavenly wealth) — both together, the verse says, don’t add up to a sixteenth part of the happiness that comes from the ending of craving (tañhākkhaya). The “sixteenth part” is a stock canonical fraction for something almost too small to register; the point isn’t a precise ratio but the scale of the gap.

The comparison the mendicants should have had

Read against its frame, the saying does double duty. It corrects the specific error — comparing two kings’ wealth is not a fitting topic — while replacing it with the comparison that actually matters for someone who has gone forth: not which worldly fortune is greater, but how small even the greatest worldly and heavenly fortune is next to freedom from craving.

Key terms

rāja — “king”; the discourse’s frame turns on a comparison between two named kings, Bimbāsāra and Pasenadi.
ariyo tuñhībhāvo — “noble silence,” the second of the two things the Buddha says mendicants gathered together should do instead of idle comparison.
kāmasukha — “the pleasure of the senses,” the first term in the saying’s steep comparison.
dibba sukha — “divine happiness,” heavenly pleasure, the second term compared and still found wanting.
tañhākkhaya — “the ending of craving,” the happiness the verse holds up as incomparably greater than either sensual or divine pleasure.

The text

The heartfelt saying in full: the mendicants’ unfinished argument and the Buddha’s reply. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1.1–1.5So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time, after the meal, on return from almsround, several mendicants sat together in the assembly hall and this discussion came up among them: “Which of these two kings has greater wealth, riches, treasury, dominion, vehicles, forces, might, and power: King Seniya Bimbisāra of Magadha or King Pasenadi of Kosala?” At that point the conversation among those mendicants was left unfinished.

§2.1–2.3Then in the late afternoon, the Buddha came out of retreat, went to the assembly hall, sat down on the seat spread out, and addressed the mendicants: “Mendicants, what were you sitting talking about just now? What conversation was left unfinished?”

§3.1So the mendicants told him what they had been talking about when the Buddha arrived. The Buddha said,

§4.1–4.3“Mendicants, it is not appropriate for you gentlemen who have gone forth out of faith from the lay life to homelessness to talk about such things. When you’re sitting together you should do one of two things: discuss the teachings or keep noble silence.”

§5.1Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§6.1–6.4“Neither the pleasures of the senses, nor even divine happiness, is worth even a sixteenth part of the happiness of craving’s end.”

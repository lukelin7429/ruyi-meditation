Udāna · Heartfelt Sayings 2.4

Esteem

Sakkārasutta

Setting
Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speaker
The Buddha, addressing mendicants who report an incident to him
Occasion
The Buddha and the Saṅgha receive abundant honor and support; other wanderers, receiving none, turn to abuse and harassment
Form
Narrative frame, then a compact four-line verse
Length
About 2 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the verse is more compressed and abstract than most in this chapter

Why this heartfelt saying, fourth of the chapter

Most of this chapter’s heartfelt sayings grow out of suffering or a chance encounter. This one grows out of success: the Buddha and his community are receiving unusual honor and support, and other religious wanderers, receiving none, turn to open hostility. What the Buddha says in response has almost nothing to do with strategy for handling rivals — it reframes what being struck by praise or blame even is.

Reading guide

Success as a problem

This heartfelt saying is unusual among the ten in this chapter because its trigger isn’t suffering, grief, or a chance encounter — it’s success. The Buddha and the mendicant Saṅgha are, the text says plainly, “honored, respected, revered, venerated, and esteemed,” and well provided with robes, almsfood, lodging, and medicine. Wanderers who follow other religious paths receive none of this. Rather than simply describing an imbalance, the text goes on to its consequence: those wanderers, “unable to bear the esteem of the mendicant Saṅgha,” turn to abuse — insulting, harassing, and troubling mendicants wherever they find them, in village or wilderness.

Reported, not asked about

Several mendicants bring this to the Buddha directly, simply telling him what has been happening. There’s no question posed — no “what should we do,” no request for a strategy for dealing with hostile rivals. The Buddha’s heartfelt saying that follows isn’t operational advice about handling harassment; it’s addressed to something underneath the situation entirely.

Contact, not ownership

The heartfelt saying. “When struck by pleasure and pain in the village or wilderness, regard it not as self or alien. Contacts strike because of attachment; how would contacts strike one free of attachment?”

The verse doesn’t tell mendicants how to respond to the wanderers’ hostility. It reframes what “being struck” even is. Being honored is a kind of contact; being abused is another. The verse’s instruction is to regard neither “as self or alien” — not to claim it as confirmation of who you are, and not to treat the person delivering it as simply an enemy to be resisted. What makes any contact — praise or blame, esteem or abuse — land with force is attachment (upadhi); remove that, and the verse asks, rhetorically, how contact could still strike at all.

The pattern the story sets up

The surrounding narrative gives the abstract verse its edge. It would be easy to read “regard it not as self or alien” as detached advice from a position of comfort, but the frame makes clear the Buddha’s own community is the one currently on the receiving end of both extremes — first great esteem, then, indirectly, the resentment it provokes in others. The teaching isn’t offered from outside the situation; it’s offered from inside it.

Key terms

sakkāra — “esteem” or “honor,” giving the discourse its title; what the Buddha and Saṅgha receive and the other wanderers don’t.
lābha — “gain” or material support (robes, almsfood, lodging, medicine), listed alongside honor as what flows to the well-regarded.
phassa — “contact,” the verse’s central term for any experience — pleasant or painful — striking a person.
upadhi — “attachment” or “acquisition,” what the verse identifies as the actual cause of contact’s power to strike; without it, contact has nothing to land on.
“not as self, nor as alien” — the verse’s twofold instruction (nevattato no parato) for how to regard being struck by pleasure or pain.

The text

The heartfelt saying in full: the Saṅgha’s esteem, the other wanderers’ hostility, and the Buddha’s reply. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1.1–1.6So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time the Buddha was honored, respected, revered, venerated, and esteemed. And he received robes, almsfood, lodgings, and medicines and supplies for the sick. And the mendicant Saṅgha was also honored, respected, revered, venerated, and esteemed. And they received robes, almsfood, lodgings, and medicines and supplies for the sick. But the wanderers who followed other religions were not honored, respected, revered, venerated, and esteemed. And they didn’t receive robes, almsfood, lodgings, and medicines and supplies for the sick. Then those wanderers who followed other religions, unable to bear the esteem of the mendicant Sangha, abused, attacked, harassed, and troubled the mendicants in the village and the wilderness.

§2.1Then several mendicants went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and told him what had happened.

§3.1Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§4.1–4.4“When struck by pleasure and pain in the village or wilderness, regard it not as self or alien. Contacts strike because of attachment; how would contacts strike one free of attachment?”

Udāna · Discourse 7.5

Another Discourse with Bhaddiya the Dwarf

Aparalakuṇḍakabhaddiyasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Occasion
The Buddha sees Bhaddiya approached from a distance, “despised by most of the mendicants” for his looks, and corrects the assembly directly
Form
A short exchange with the assembled mendicants, followed by a four-line inspired utterance (udāna)
Length
~1 minute to read
Companion
Third of three discourses in this chapter centered on Bhaddiya, after Ud 7.1 and Ud 7.2
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — direct in its social point, but the chariot verse is a compressed riddle-like image

Why this discourse, prejudice named and corrected

The first two Bhaddiya discourses in this chapter left one detail quietly unaddressed: what his fellow mendicants actually thought of him. This discourse names it outright. The Buddha describes Bhaddiya, in the hearing of the assembly, exactly as they see him — “ugly, unsightly, deformed” — and then states, without hedging, that this same monk is “very mighty and powerful” and has “realized the supreme end of the spiritual path.”

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Watching Bhaddiya approach from a distance, ridiculed by his own community for his appearance, the Buddha states plainly that this is the same monk who has already reached the highest goal of the path — and follows it with a verse comparing him to a flawless, one-spoked chariot rolling on, untroubled.

Seen, and mocked, from a distance

This discourse does something the previous two Bhaddiya suttas did not: it describes, in the Buddha’s own words to the assembly, exactly how Bhaddiya was regarded by his fellow mendicants. As he approaches “closely following several mendicants,” the Buddha sees him “ugly, unsightly, deformed, and despised by most of the mendicants,” and asks the assembled monks directly: “Do you see this monk coming” — described in exactly those terms? Their answer is a simple, uncomfortable “yes, sir.” The text does not soften or explain away the community’s prejudice; it states it plainly, through the Buddha’s own description, before correcting it.

The correction

Having named what the mendicants see, the Buddha immediately reframes it: “that mendicant is very mighty and powerful. It’s not easy to find an attainment that he has not already attained.” He goes further, using the canon’s standard formula for full awakening — the goal “for which gentlemen rightly go forth from the lay life to homelessness,” achieved “in this very life” through Bhaddiya’s “own insight.” The point is made without any softening of the initial, blunt description of his appearance: the correction is not that Bhaddiya is secretly handsome, but that his appearance and his attainment have nothing to do with one another, and the assembly’s judgment mistook the former for a measure of the latter.

A faultless, one-spoked chariot

The verse that follows turns to a striking, almost riddle-like image: “with faultless parts and white canopy, the one-spoked chariot rolls on.” A chariot is ordinarily judged by its craftsmanship and its wheels; a “one-spoked” wheel breaks the expected picture entirely, suggesting something that should not function by ordinary standards yet moves regardless — a fitting image for a monk whose outward form drew scorn while his inward attainment was complete. “See it come, untroubled, with stream cut, unbound” closes the verse by naming what actually matters: not the vehicle’s conventional appearance, but that its stream (of craving) is cut and it travels unbound.

Closing a small cycle

Taken with Ud 7.1 and Ud 7.2, this sutta completes a three-part sequence: first, Bhaddiya’s liberation, quietly achieved; second, his teacher still treating him as unfinished; third, his community judging him by appearance while he is, invisibly to them, already complete. Across all three, the Buddha never announces Bhaddiya’s attainment as a general public matter — he responds to specific moments of misperception, correcting each one as it arises rather than issuing one sweeping declaration.

Key terms

mahiddhika mahānubhāva (mighty and powerful) — the Buddha’s description of Bhaddiya, directly contradicting the assembly’s dismissive view based on his appearance.
“the supreme end of the spiritual path” — the canon’s standard formula for arahantship, “realized… in this very life” through one’s “own insight,” here applied to Bhaddiya despite his community’s low opinion of him.
ekāra ratha (one-spoked chariot) — the verse’s central image: a chariot that should not function by ordinary craftsmanship standards, yet “rolls on” regardless — a picture of attainment independent of outward form.
chinnasota (stream cut) — the same image of craving as a flowing stream, here cut, used elsewhere in this chapter (see Ud 7.2) to describe liberation.
Lakuṇḍaka Bhaddiya — “Bhaddiya the Dwarf,” here explicitly named by the Buddha as “despised by most of the mendicants” for his looks, yet fully liberated.

The text

The discourse in full — the Buddha's exchange with the assembled mendicants, closed by a four-line inspired utterance. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Seen from a distance

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Then Venerable Bhaddiya the Dwarf, closely following several mendicants, approached the Buddha.

§2The Buddha saw Venerable Bhaddiya coming off in the distance—ugly, unsightly, deformed, and despised by most of the mendicants. The Buddha addressed the mendicants:

Named, and corrected

§3“Mendicants, do you see this monk coming—ugly, unsightly, deformed, and despised by most of the mendicants?” “Yes, sir.”

§4“That mendicant is very mighty and powerful. It’s not easy to find an attainment that he has not already attained. And he has realized the supreme end of the spiritual path in this very life. He lives having achieved with his own insight the goal for which gentlemen rightly go forth from the lay life to homelessness.”

§5Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

The verse

§6“With faultless parts and white canopy,
the one-spoked chariot rolls on.
See it come, untroubled,
with stream cut, unbound.”

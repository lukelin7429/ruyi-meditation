Udāna · Meghiyavagga · Discourse 4.5

A Bull Elephant

Nāgasutta

Setting
Kosambī, at Ghosita’s Monastery, then the forest at Pārileyyaka
Present
The Buddha, alone, and a bull elephant who leaves his own herd
Form
Two mirrored accounts of crowding and withdrawal, joined by a closing verse
Length
About 3 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a simple, almost fable-like structure with real emotional weight

Why this heartfelt saying

Crowded and uncomfortable among monks, nuns, laypeople, rulers, and rival ascetics, the Buddha quietly packs his bowl and robe and walks away alone, telling no one. A bull elephant, crowded in nearly identical language by his own herd, makes the same decision independently — and the two accounts, told in deliberately mirrored terms, meet at Pārileyyaka. This version of the story mentions no dispute among the monks at all — only the plain burden of being surrounded.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Buddha’s own quiet decision to leave a crowded monastery without telling anyone is echoed, almost sentence for sentence, by a bull elephant’s decision to leave his crowded herd — and the closing verse treats the two departures as, quite literally, of one mind.

Crowded, in suffering and discomfort

At Kosambī, the text says, the Buddha “lived crowded by monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen; by rulers and their chief ministers, and monastics of other religions and their disciples,” and adds, in words repeated twice for emphasis, “Crowded, he lived in suffering and discomfort.” It is worth being precise about what this discourse does and does not say: it names only the burden of crowding itself — it does not mention any quarrel or dispute among the mendicants, a detail sometimes associated with this period elsewhere in the tradition but simply absent from this particular text.

Leaving without a word

The Buddha’s response is strikingly solitary for a teacher normally surrounded by students and seekers: after his almsround, he “set his lodgings in order himself,” and then, “without informing his attendants or taking leave of the mendicant Saṅgha,” set out alone for Pārileyyaka, “with no companion.” He settles “in a protected forest grove, at the foot of a sacred sal tree.”

A parallel discovery

The text then turns, without transition, to a bull elephant living “crowded by other males, females, younglings, and cubs” — eating grass they have trampled, drinking water they have muddied, jostled by females after his bath. The description of his discomfort uses the same repeated phrase as the Buddha’s: “Crowded, he lived in suffering and discomfort.” Independently, the elephant thinks almost the Buddha’s exact thought — “Why don’t I live alone, withdrawn from the herd?” — leaves the herd, and travels to the very grove where the Buddha now sits. There he serves him: clearing vegetation from the ground, and using his trunk to set out water for drinking and washing.

Two minds, one verse

Once both are settled, each independently reflects on the relief of the change — again in matching language, now shifted from suffering to “happiness and comfort.” The Buddha, “understanding his own seclusion and knowing that elephant’s train of thought,” speaks the verse: “The giant elephant, with tusks like draft-poles, agrees heart to heart with the spiritual giant, since each finds joy in the woods alone.” It is worth noticing what the elephant’s presence complicates about the idea of “solitude”: he does not leave the Buddha alone, but attends on him without being asked. What both have escaped is not company as such, but crowding — the friction of being surrounded on every side.

Key terms

Pārileyyaka — the forest the Buddha withdraws to, and this discourse’s second setting; traditionally remembered as the site of this very episode.
ākiṇṇa — “crowded,” the operative word repeated for both the Buddha and the elephant, describing an oppressive surrounding-by-others rather than mere company.
nāga — a word meaning both “elephant” and, as an epithet, a spiritually great or formidable being; the closing verse’s pun — “this agreement of nāga with nāga” — plays on both senses at once.
pavivitta / seclusion — the value that drives both departures, though neither account frames it as total isolation: the elephant actively serves the Buddha once both have withdrawn.
bhaddasāla — the “sacred sal tree” at whose foot the Buddha settles in the protected grove at Pārileyyaka.

The text

The discourse in full: the Buddha’s departure from a crowded Kosambī, the bull elephant’s parallel departure from his own herd, and the Buddha’s closing verse. This version of the story mentions no dispute among the mendicants — only the burden of crowding. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Kosambī, in Ghosita’s Monastery. Now at that time Buddha lived crowded by monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen; by rulers and their chief ministers, and monastics of other religions and their disciples. Crowded, he lived in suffering and discomfort. Then he thought, “These days I live crowded by monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen; by rulers and their chief ministers, and monastics of other religions and their disciples. Crowded, I live in suffering and discomfort. Why don’t I live alone, withdrawn from the group?”

§2Then the Buddha robed up in the morning and, taking his bowl and robe, entered Kosambī for alms. After the meal, on his return from almsround, he set his lodgings in order himself. Taking his bowl and robe, without informing his attendants or taking leave of the mendicant Saṅgha, he set out to go wandering alone towards Pārileyyaka, with no companion. When he eventually arrived, he stayed in a protected forest grove, at the foot of a sacred sal tree.

§3A certain bull elephant was also living crowded by other males, females, younglings, and cubs. He ate the grass they’d trampled, and they ate the broken branches he dragged down. He drank muddy water, and after his bath the female elephants bumped into him. Crowded, he lived in suffering and discomfort. Then he thought, “These days I live crowded by other males, females, younglings, and cubs. I eat the grass they’ve trampled, and they eat the broken branches I’ve dragged down. I drink muddy water, and after my bath the female elephants bump into me. Crowded, I live in suffering and discomfort. Why don’t I live alone, withdrawn from the herd?”

§4So he left the herd and went to Pārileyyaka, where he approached the Buddha in the protected forest grove at the foot of a sacred sal tree. There he attended on the Buddha, clearing the vegetation from the place where the Buddha stayed, and using his trunk to set out water for drinking and washing.

§5Then as the Buddha was in private retreat this thought came to his mind, “Formerly I lived crowded by monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen; by rulers and their chief ministers, and monastics of other religions and their disciples. Crowded, I live in suffering and discomfort. But now I live uncrowded by monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen; by rulers and their chief ministers, and monastics of other religions and their disciples. Being uncrowded, I live in happiness and comfort.”

§6And to the bull elephant also this thought came to mind, “Formerly I lived crowded by other males, females, younglings, and cubs. I ate the grass they’d trampled, and they ate the broken branches I’d dragged down. I drank muddy water, and after my bath the female elephants bumped into me. Crowded, I lived in suffering and discomfort. But now I live uncrowded by other males, females, younglings, and cubs. I eat untrampled grass, and other elephants don’t eat the broken branches I have dragged down. I don’t drink muddy water, and the female elephants don’t bump into me after my bath. Being uncrowded, I live in happiness and comfort.”

§7Then, understanding his own seclusion and knowing that elephant’s train of thought, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§8“The giant elephant, with tusks like draft-poles, agrees heart to heart with the spiritual giant, since each finds joy in the woods alone.”

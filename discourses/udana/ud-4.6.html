Udāna · Meghiyavagga · Discourse 4.6

The Alms-Gatherer

Piṇḍolasutta

Setting
Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Present
The Buddha and Venerable Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja, seated nearby
Form
Pure observation — no dialogue — followed by a six-line verse
Length
Under 1 minute to read
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — short and plain, a compact checklist of monastic discipline

Why this heartfelt saying

The Buddha says nothing at all to Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja — he simply watches him sit, cross-legged and upright, embodying an entire catalogue of austere qualities, and turns what he sees into six lines summing up, in miniature, what he calls “the instruction of the Buddhas.”

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Watching Venerable Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja meditate nearby, embodying a long list of disciplined, austere qualities at once, the Buddha distills what he sees into a six-line verse that functions as a compressed summary of the monastic training as a whole.

A portrait in one sentence

The discourse’s entire narrative content is a single, densely packed description. Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja is “sitting not far from the Buddha, cross-legged, with his body straight.” The text then lists what he is: “one who lived in the wilderness, ate only almsfood, wore rag robes, and owned just three robes.” It adds a second cluster of qualities: “of few wishes, content, secluded, aloof, and energetic, an advocate of austerities, dedicated to the higher mind.” The Buddha “saw him meditating there” — and that is the entire narrative. No question is asked, and no answer given; the portrait itself is the occasion for the verse.

Piṇḍola, “the alms-gatherer”

This elder’s title in the discourse’s own name, Piṇḍola, points to one specific practice named in his portrait — eating only almsfood (piṇḍapātika) — among several ascetic observances (dhutaṅga) he is said to keep: wilderness-dwelling, rag robes, and owning only the minimum three robes. He is further called “an advocate of austerities,” marking him as someone who not only practiced these observances but spoke up for them.

The verse as a summary

The closing verse reads like a compressed table of contents for the whole of monastic discipline: “Not speaking ill nor doing harm; restraint in the monastic code; moderation in eating; staying in remote lodgings; commitment to the higher mind — this is the instruction of the Buddhas.” Each line corresponds to something already visible in Piṇḍola’s portrait — his ethical restraint, his moderate almsfood diet, his wilderness lodging, his dedication to the higher mind — generalized from this one elder’s example into a five-part statement the verse explicitly calls the teaching of every Buddha, not only this one.

A companion piece

The very next discourse in this vagga (Ud 4.7) repeats almost this exact scene with Sāriputta in the same role, using much of the same language for his qualities, though without the specific ascetic observances named for Piṇḍola. Read together, the two suttas function as a matched pair: two different elders, observed in near-identical postures, each occasioning his own brief verse on what that same stillness and discipline amounts to.

Key terms

piṇḍapātika — “alms-food eater,” one who eats only what is received on almsround; the practice giving this elder, and this discourse, their name.
dhutavāda — “advocate of austerities,” describing Piṇḍola as a spokesman for the ascetic observances (dhutaṅga), not merely a practitioner of them.
appiccho santuṭṭho — “of few wishes, content,” a paired quality shared with Sāriputta’s portrait in the following discourse, Ud 4.7.
adhicitta — the “higher mind,” the object of the dedication (anuyutta) attributed to Piṇḍola and named again in the closing verse’s final line.
pātimokkha-saṁvara — restraint in accordance with the monastic code, the verse’s second line and a core element of monastic ethical discipline.

The text

The discourse in full: the Buddha’s silent observation of Venerable Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja and the six-line verse it occasions. There is no dialogue in this short text. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time Venerable Bhāradvāja the Alms-Gatherer was sitting not far from the Buddha, cross-legged, with his body straight. He was one who lived in the wilderness, ate only almsfood, wore rag robes, and owned just three robes. He was of few wishes, content, secluded, aloof, and energetic, an advocate of austerities, dedicated to the higher mind.

§2The Buddha saw him meditating there.

§3Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§4“Not speaking ill nor doing harm; restraint in the monastic code; moderation in eating; staying in remote lodgings; commitment to the higher mind— this is the instruction of the Buddhas.”

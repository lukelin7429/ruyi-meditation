Udāna · Heartfelt Sayings 2.8

Suppavāsā

Suppavāsāsutta

Setting
Near Kuṇḍiyā, in the Kuṇḍadhāna Grove
Speaker
The Buddha; also featuring Suppavāsā the Koliyan, her husband, Venerable Mahāmoggallāna, Venerable Sāriputta, and Suppavāsā’s newborn son
Occasion
Suppavāsā, pregnant for seven years and in obstructed labor for seven days, endures severe pain through three reflections; the Buddha blesses her and she safely gives birth, then hosts a seven-day almsgiving
Form
The longest and most elaborate narrative in this chapter, built from several linked scenes, closing in a four-line verse
Length
About 5–6 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — more moving parts than any other discourse in this chapter

Why this heartfelt saying, eighth of the chapter

Seven years pregnant, seven days in obstructed labor, sustained by three specific reflections through severe pain — Suppavāsā’s story is the longest and most eventful in this chapter, running through a blessing, a delicate negotiation over hospitality, and a newborn’s startling first words. The heartfelt saying that closes it reads back over everything that came before with a single, quiet warning.

Reading guide

Seven years, seven days

This is by far the longest of the ten heartfelt sayings in this chapter, and its numbers are stated with a kind of ritual repetition: Suppavāsā the Koliyan has been pregnant for seven years, and in obstructed labor for seven days. Enduring “painful, sharp, severe, acute feelings,” she holds onto three specific reflections — that the Buddha teaches for the ending of just this kind of suffering, that the Saṅgha practices well for the ending of just this kind of suffering, and that extinguishment (Nibbāna) is where suffering like this isn’t found at all. These aren’t abstract doctrines to her in this moment; the text presents them as what she is actively using to endure.

A message relayed, twice

Suppavāsā sends her husband to the Buddha with her greeting and her exact words, repeated by the narrator nearly verbatim as the husband delivers them and again as he reports back — a stylistic feature typical of oral transmission, but one that also has the effect of slowing the story down around her suffering, giving it weight through repetition rather than rushing past it. The Buddha’s response, when it comes, is a simple blessing: “May Suppavāsā the Koliyan be happy and healthy! May she give birth to a healthy child!” The text states that the moment he speaks, she does exactly that.

A gift, carefully negotiated

What follows is a small, very human episode about hospitality and prior commitments. Grateful, Suppavāsā wants to host the Buddha and the Saṅgha for seven days of meals — but another lay follower, a supporter of Venerable Mahāmoggallāna, has already invited them for the very next day. Rather than simply overriding the earlier invitation, the Buddha sends Mahāmoggallāna to negotiate: the other lay follower is initially reluctant, wanting a guarantee of “wealth, life, and faith” before yielding his place, and only relents once Mahāmoggallāna clarifies he can vouch for the first two but not the third — a nice, understated moment where even a generous concession is worked out through ordinary human bargaining, not compelled.

A child who speaks like a teacher

The story’s strangest moment comes at its end. Suppavāsā’s newborn, brought to bow before Sāriputta, is asked the ordinary pleasantry, “I hope you’re keeping well… I hope you are not in pain.” His answer is startling: “How could I be keeping well? How could I be getting by? For seven years I lived in a pot of blood.” Suppavāsā’s response to this uncanny exchange is not alarm but joy — “my child is conversing with the General of the Dhamma!” — and when the Buddha asks if she’d want another child like this, she answers she’d want seven more.

The saying, against everything that came before

The heartfelt saying. “Pain in the guise of pleasure, the disliked in the guise of the liked, suffering in the guise of happiness, overpower the negligent.”

Coming immediately after Suppavāsā’s wish for seven more children exactly like this one — a wish born directly out of nine years combined of pregnancy and dangerous labor — the verse reads less as commentary on her specifically than as a wider observation the whole story has just illustrated from several angles: an ordeal endured for the sake of a longed-for child; hospitality eagerly sought after near-death; even an infant’s report of suffering met with delight rather than concern. None of these responses is condemned outright, but the verse names, plainly, how easily what looks like pleasure can carry pain inside it, for anyone not paying close attention.

Key terms

Suppavāsā Koliyaputtā — the discourse’s central figure, a Koliyan laywoman whose extraordinarily long and difficult pregnancy and labor frame the whole story.
dukkha — “suffering” or “pain,” what Suppavāsā endures and what her three sustaining reflections are all oriented toward the ending of.
Nibbāna — “extinguishment,” named directly in Suppavāsā’s third reflection as “so very blissful, where such suffering as this is not found.”
pāṭibhoga — “guarantor” or “one who vouches,” the term used in the negotiation between Mahāmoggallāna and the other lay follower over wealth, life, and faith.
pamatta — “negligent” or “heedless,” the word describing who is overpowered by pain disguised as pleasure in the closing verse.

The text

The heartfelt saying in full: Suppavāsā’s ordeal, the Buddha’s blessing, the negotiated meal offering, her son’s startling words, and the closing verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1.1–1.7So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Kuṇḍiyā in the Kuṇḍadhāna Grove. Now at that time Suppavāsā the Koliyan had been pregnant for seven years, and in obstructed labor for seven days. While suffering painful, sharp, severe, acute feelings, three thoughts helped her endure: “Oh! The Blessed One is indeed a fully awakened Buddha, who teaches the Dhamma for giving up suffering such as this. Oh! The Saṅgha of the Buddha’s disciples is indeed practicing well, who practice for giving up suffering such as this. Oh! Extinguishment is so very blissful, where such suffering as this is not found.”

§2.1–2.9Then Suppavāsā addressed her husband, “Please, master, go to the Buddha, and in my name bow with your head to his feet. Ask him if he is healthy and well, nimble, strong, and living comfortably. And then say: ‘Suppavāsā the Koliyan has been pregnant for seven years, and in obstructed labor for seven days. While suffering painful, sharp, severe, acute feelings, three thoughts help her endure: “Oh! The Blessed One is indeed a fully awakened Buddha, who teaches the Dhamma for giving up suffering such as this. Oh! The Saṅgha of the Buddha’s disciples is indeed practicing well, who practice for giving up suffering such as this. Oh! Extinguishment is so very blissful, where such suffering as this is not found.”’”

§3.1“Excellent idea,” he replied. He went to the Buddha and told him of his wife’s struggles. The Buddha said:

§4.1–4.2“May Suppavāsā the Koliyan be happy and healthy! May she give birth to a healthy child!” As soon as he spoke, Suppavāsā, happy and healthy, gave birth to a healthy child.

§5.1–5.5Saying “Yes, sir,” the Koliyan gentleman approved and agreed with what the Buddha said. He rose from his seat, bowed, and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on his right. Then he returned to his own house. He saw that his wife, happy and healthy, had given birth to a healthy child, and thought, “Oh lord, how incredible, how amazing! The Realized One has such psychic power and might! For as soon as he spoke, Suppavāsā, happy and healthy, gave birth to a healthy child.” He became uplifted and overjoyed, full of rapture and happiness.

§6.1–6.8Then Suppavāsā addressed her husband, “Please, master, go to the Buddha, and in my name bow with your head to his feet. And then say, ‘Suppavāsā the Koliyan, who was pregnant for seven years, and in obstructed labor for seven days, is now happy and healthy and has given birth to a healthy child. She invites the mendicant Saṅgha headed by the Buddha to a meal for seven days. Sir, would the Buddha please accept seven meals from Suppavāsā?’”

§7.1“Excellent idea,” he replied. He went to the Buddha, told him the good news, and conveyed his wife’s invitation.

§9.1–9.9Now at that time a certain lay follower had already invited the Sangha of monks headed by the Buddha for the meal on the following day. That lay follower was Venerable Mahāmoggallāna’s supporter. Then the Buddha addressed Venerable Mahāmoggallāna, “Please, Moggallāna, go to that lay follower and say to him, ‘Suppavāsā the Koliyan, who was pregnant for seven years, and in obstructed labor for seven days, is now happy and healthy and has given birth to a healthy child. She invites the mendicant Saṅgha headed by the Buddha to a meal for seven days. Let Suppavāsā make seven meals, afterwards you can make yours.’ He is your supporter.”

§10.1“Yes, sir,” replied Mahāmoggallāna. He went to that lay follower and conveyed the Buddha’s request.

§11.1–11.6“If, sir, Master Mahāmoggallāna can guarantee me three things— wealth, life, and faith— then let Suppavāsā make seven meals, afterwards I shall make mine.” “I can guarantee you two things— wealth and life. But as for faith, you alone are the guarantor.”

§12.1–12.3“If, sir Master Mahāmoggallāna can guarantee me two things— wealth and life— then let Suppavāsā make seven meals, afterwards I shall make mine.”

§13.1–13.3Having persuaded that lay follower, Mahāmoggallāna went to the Buddha and said, “I’ve persuaded the lay follower. Let Suppavāsā make seven meals, afterwards he shall make his.”

§14.1–14.2For seven days Suppavāsā served and satisfied the Buddha with her own hands with delicious fresh and cooked foods. And she made her little boy bow to the Buddha and the mendicant Sangha.

§15.1–15.4Then Sāriputta said to the boy, “I hope you’re keeping well, little boy; I hope you’re all right. I hope that you are not in pain.” “How could I be keeping well? How could I be getting by? For seven years I lived in a pot of blood.”

§16.1–16.5Then Suppavāsā, thinking, “My child is conversing with the General of the Dhamma!” was uplifted and overjoyed, full of rapture and happiness. Knowing this, the Buddha said to her, “Would you like to have another child like this?” “Sir, I would like to have seven more children like this!”

§17.1Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§18.1–18.4“Pain in the guise of pleasure, the disliked in the guise of the liked, suffering in the guise of happiness, overpower the negligent.”

Udāna · Discourse 7.1

Bhaddiya the Dwarf (1st)

Paṭhamalakuṇḍakabhaddiyasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Occasion
Sāriputta teaches Venerable Bhaddiya the Dwarf; the Buddha silently witnesses the result
Form
A brief narrative frame with no recorded dialogue, closed by a single four-line inspired utterance (udāna)
Length
<1 minute to read
Narrative frame
Minimal — the Buddha never addresses Bhaddiya directly in this discourse; he only perceives what has happened
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — short and plain on the surface, but its imagery of “crossing the flood” repays unpacking

Why this discourse, first of the Cūḷavagga

This is the opening discourse of the Udāna’s seventh chapter, the Cūḷavagga (“Lesser Chapter”), and it introduces a figure the chapter keeps returning to: Venerable Bhaddiya, known throughout the canon simply as “the Dwarf” for his short stature. Nothing here explains why his teacher, Sāriputta, was working so hard on him, or what “in many ways” involved. The text gives only the barest frame — a Dhamma talk, a mind freed, the Buddha noticing — and lets a four-line verse carry the weight of what actually happened.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

While Sāriputta is still working to encourage a monk he takes to be a trainee, that monk’s mind quietly reaches full freedom — and the Buddha marks the moment not with praise or instruction, but with a spare verse about being utterly unbound, “above, below, everywhere.”

A teacher, and a student further along than expected

The sutta opens with almost no context: Sāriputta, the Buddha’s chief disciple in wisdom, is “educating, encouraging, firing up, and inspiring” Venerable Bhaddiya the Dwarf “in many ways with a Dhamma talk.” This four-verb formula (sandasseti samādapeti samuttejeti sampahaṁseti in Pali) is a stock phrase used throughout the canon to describe skilled, patient teaching. Nothing marks this as an unusual occasion — it reads like an ordinary training session.

Freed by not grasping

Then, without any further exchange recorded, “Bhaddiya’s mind was freed from defilements by not grasping.” This phrase — freedom achieved anupādāya, by letting go of clinging rather than by acquiring anything — is the canon’s standard formula for describing arahantship, full awakening. The text does not dramatize it. It simply states that it happened, mid-lesson, apparently without Sāriputta’s awareness.

What the Buddha does next is characteristic of this whole collection: he does not intervene, correct, or announce anything. He simply “saw what had happened” — and only then, privately understanding the significance, does he speak, not to Bhaddiya or Sāriputta, but as a heartfelt utterance to himself and whoever else is present to hear it.

Above, below, everywhere free

The verse itself is dense and worth slowing down for. “Above, below, everywhere free” uses a totality formula common across the canon — up, down, and all around — to describe a freedom with no direction left unexamined and no corner of experience still held onto. “Not contemplating ‘I am this’” names the specific thing that has dropped away: asmimāna, the subtle conceit of identifying with any experience as a self, which the tradition treats as the last fetter to fall even after other defilements have gone.

The final image, “he has crossed the flood not crossed before, so as to not be reborn,” draws on one of the canon’s recurring pictures of the deep, ordinary difficulty of the spiritual path: four floods — of sensuality, of continued existence, of views, and of ignorance — that sweep beings through the round of rebirth. To say Bhaddiya has crossed a flood “not crossed before” is to say this is not one more incremental step but something genuinely unprecedented for him: complete, not partial.

Why a dwarf, and why here

This is the first of three discourses in the Cūḷavagga built around Bhaddiya (see also Ud 7.2 and 7.5), and read together they form a small, pointed cycle. Here his attainment is announced almost in passing. In Ud 7.5, later in the same chapter, the text confronts head-on the fact that his fellow monks judged him by his appearance — “despised by most of the mendicants” for being “ugly, unsightly, deformed” — despite exactly the freedom recorded here. The quiet placement of that fact in this first sutta, without comment, sets up the more explicit correction to come.

Key terms

Lakuṇḍaka Bhaddiya — “Bhaddiya the Dwarf,” a monk whose short stature drew ridicule from other mendicants (see Ud 7.5) but who reaches full liberation here, quietly, in the middle of a lesson meant to encourage him further.
anupādāya vimutti — “freed… by not grasping”: the canon’s standard formula for arahantship, describing liberation as a release of clinging rather than an acquisition.
ogha (the flood) — the four floods of sensuality, continued existence, views, and ignorance, pictured as torrents sweeping beings through repeated rebirth; full awakening is described as crossing all four.
“I am this” (asmimāna) — the subtle conceit of identifying with experience as a self; the verse names its absence, not any external attainment, as the mark of true freedom.
udāna — “inspired utterance”: a spontaneous verse spoken not to instruct a listener but to express the significance of what the Buddha has just witnessed, the genre that gives this whole collection its name.

The text

The discourse in full — a brief narrative frame with no recorded dialogue, closed by the Buddha’s four-line inspired utterance. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A teacher, and a mind quietly freed

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time Venerable Sāriputta was educating, encouraging, firing up, and inspiring Venerable Bhaddiya the Dwarf in many ways with a Dhamma talk.

§2Then after being taught like this Bhaddiya’s mind was freed from defilements by not grasping.

§3The Buddha saw what had happened.

§4Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

The verse

§5“Above, below, everywhere free,
not contemplating ‘I am this’.
Freed like this, he has crossed the flood
not crossed before, so as to not be reborn.”

Udāna · Soṇavagga · Discourse 5.10

With Cūḷapanthaka

Cūḷapanthakasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Figures
Venerable Cūḷapanthaka and the Buddha
Occasion
The Buddha silently observes Cūḷapanthaka meditating nearby
Length
~1 minute to read
Chapter
Soṇavagga (Ud 5.1–5.10), the fifth of the Udāna’s eight chapters — named for Ud 5.6, “With Soṇa”
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — the closing text of the chapter, a single observed moment and a six-line verse

Why this heartfelt saying

Like Ud 5.7 earlier in this same chapter, nothing is said aloud here at all — only a mendicant sitting in meditation, mindfulness established, and the Buddha watching before he speaks. This is the last of the Soṇavagga’s ten heartfelt sayings, and it closes the chapter on exactly the note of quiet, sustained steadiness it opened by other means.

Reading guide

A familiar meditation posture

Cūḷapanthaka is described “sitting not far from the Buddha, cross-legged, his body set straight, and mindfulness brought to the present.” That last phrase — mindfulness established “to the present” or “in front” (parimukhaṁ satiṁ upaṭṭhapetvā) — is the same formula that opens the instructions of the Satipatthana Sutta’s foundational meditation posture: cross-legged, body straight, mindfulness established. Readers who know that text will recognize this as more than incidental description; it identifies exactly what kind of practice Cūḷapanthaka is engaged in.

Steady across all postures

The verse doesn’t stop at sitting. It extends steadiness outward: “Steady in body, steady in mind, standing, sitting or lying down.” Mindfulness of this kind, the verse implies, isn’t confined to a formal session on a cushion — it is meant to travel with a person through every ordinary posture of the day, a round-the-clock discipline rather than an occasional exercise.

Vanishing from the King of Death

The verse’s closing image is striking: “a mendicant focusing on this mindfulness gains an ever higher distinction. And when they have done so, they vanish from the King of Death.” This isn’t a claim of literal invisibility, but of going beyond the reach of Māra, or Death, understood as ruler of the unliberated — the traditional image for what full liberation means in relation to the ongoing cycle death governs.

What this text doesn’t say

It’s worth being clear about what this brief scene does and doesn’t include. Cūḷapanthaka is well known elsewhere in the tradition — in later commentarial literature, not in this sutta itself — for a memorable story of early slow-wittedness overcome through a simple, persistent exercise the Buddha is said to have given him. None of that appears here. This Udāna text shows him already an accomplished meditator, mindfulness fully established, with no reference to any earlier struggle. The two pictures aren’t in conflict — they simply come from different layers of the tradition, and this page keeps them separate rather than importing the commentarial story into a text that doesn’t contain it.

Key terms

Cūḷapanthaka — “Little Path,” the mendicant observed meditating here; later commentarial literature (not this sutta) also remembers him for a story of early slow learning overcome through diligence.
parimukhaṁ satiṁ upaṭṭhapetvā — “mindfulness brought to the present” or “established in front” — the same phrase that opens the meditation instructions of the Satipatthana Sutta.
the four postures — iriyāpatha — standing, sitting, and lying down are named explicitly in the verse as postures across which steadiness should extend.
the King of Death — Maccurājā, the traditional epithet for Māra or Death as ruler over the unliberated; “vanishing” from this ruler is the verse’s image for liberation.
the cross-legged posture — shared word for word with Ud 5.7’s description of Revata the Doubter, linking the chapter’s two silent, observed meditators.

The text

The discourse in full, closing the Soṇavagga: the Buddha observes Cūḷapanthaka meditating, and speaks the chapter’s final verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time Venerable Cūḷapanthaka was sitting not far from the Buddha, cross-legged, his body set straight, and mindfulness brought to the present.

§2The Buddha saw him meditating there.

§3Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§4“Steady in body, steady in mind,
standing, sitting or lying down:
a mendicant focusing on this mindfulness
gains an ever higher distinction.
And when they have done so,
they vanish from the King of Death.”

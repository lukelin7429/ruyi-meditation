Udāna · Heartfelt Sayings 2.7

An Only Son

Ekaputtakasutta

Setting
Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speaker
The Buddha, addressing several lay followers who arrive in visible mourning
Occasion
A lay follower’s only, beloved child has died; several lay followers arrive at midday with wet clothes and hair
Form
Brief narrative and dialogue, then an eight-line verse in two matched stanzas
Length
About 2 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a grief-centered scene with the longest verse so far in this chapter

Why this heartfelt saying, seventh of the chapter

Several lay followers arrive with wet clothes and hair — visible signs of grief. A father’s only child has died. The Buddha’s response is the longest heartfelt saying so far in this chapter: eight lines, in two matched stanzas, naming a vulnerability the text says belongs to “hosts of gods and most human beings” alike.

Reading guide

Arriving in grief

The detail that opens this discourse is small but vivid: several lay followers come to the Buddha in the middle of the day “with wet clothes and hair.” The Buddha asks why, and the answer is immediate and plain: a certain lay follower’s only child, “dear and beloved,” has died. There is no elaborate lament recorded, no philosophical question posed by the grieving father — the text lets the fact and the visible signs of mourning speak for themselves before the Buddha responds.

Two states, in two stanzas

The heartfelt saying. “Hosts of gods and most human beings are bound to what seems dear and pleasant. Gloomy and exhausted, they fall under the sway of the King of Death. The diligent, who day and night give up what seems pleasant, dig out the root of gloom — Death’s bait so hard to escape.”

The heartfelt saying is the longest in this chapter, and it’s built in two clearly matched halves. The first names the ordinary condition, shared by gods and “most human beings”: being “bound to what seems dear and pleasant,” and as a consequence, “gloomy and exhausted,” falling “under the sway of the King of Death.” The second names an alternative: “the diligent, who day and night give up what seems pleasant, dig out the root of gloom — Death’s bait so hard to escape.” The two halves aren’t a comparison between grieving and not grieving as personal failures or successes; they describe two different relationships to what is loved, one that most beings, human and divine alike, share, and one available through sustained diligence.

Naming the mechanism, not condemning the grief

It’s worth noticing what the verse doesn’t do: it doesn’t rebuke the mourning lay followers for their tears, and it doesn’t instruct them, in this text, to stop grieving. What it offers instead is closer to a diagnosis than to a directive — attachment to “what seems dear and pleasant” is what makes loss land as devastation, a mechanism the verse calls “Death’s bait” (maccuno āmisa), something that catches nearly everyone precisely because it is genuinely dear. “Digging out the root of gloom” is described as the diligent person’s task, not a switch anyone can flip mid-grief.

Gods included

One detail sharpens the point: the verse specifies that this vulnerability belongs to “hosts of gods and most human beings” alike (devakāyā puthu manussā ca). Loss and attachment aren’t framed as a peculiarly human weakness to be embarrassed about; they’re described as close to universal across realms of rebirth, which is part of why the path out — sustained diligence in relinquishing what seems pleasant — is treated as something rare and hard-won rather than simply obvious advice.

Key terms

ekaputtaka — “only child” or “only son,” giving the discourse its title.
piyarūpa — “what seems dear/pleasant,” the object of attachment named twice in the verse as the source of both bondage and, when relinquished, freedom.
Maccurāja — “the King of Death,” the power under whose sway the attached, grieving majority are said to fall.
appamatta — “diligent” or “heedful,” describing those who, day and night, give up what seems pleasant.
maccuno āmisa — “Death’s bait,” the verse’s image for attachment to the dear and pleasant — something hard to escape precisely because it is so appealing.

The text

The heartfelt saying in full: lay followers arriving in mourning and the Buddha’s eight-line reply. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1.1–1.3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time a certain lay follower’s dear and beloved only child passed away.

§2.1–2.2Then in the middle of the day several lay followers with wet clothes and hair went up to the Buddha, bowed, and sat down to one side. The Buddha said to them: “Why, lay followers, have you come here in the middle of the day with wet clothes and hair?”

§3.1–3.3The lay follower replied, “Sir, my dear and beloved only child has passed away. That’s why we came here in the middle of the day with wet clothes and hair.”

§4.1Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§5.1–5.4“Hosts of gods and most human beings are bound to what seems dear and pleasant. Gloomy and exhausted, they fall under the sway of the King of Death.

§6.1–6.4The diligent, who day and night give up what seems pleasant, dig out the root of gloom— Death’s bait so hard to escape.”

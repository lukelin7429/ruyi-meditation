Udāna · Soṇavagga · Discourse 5.3

With Suppabuddha the Leper

Suppabuddhakuṭṭhisutta

Setting
Near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground
Figures
Suppabuddha, a leper; the Buddha; several unnamed mendicants
Occasion
A poor, sick man approaches a crowd hoping for food, and stays to hear the Buddha teach
Length
~5 minutes to read
Chapter
Soṇavagga (Ud 5.1–5.10), the fifth of the Udāna’s eight chapters — named for Ud 5.6, “With Soṇa”
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — a full narrative arc: conversion, sudden death, and a karmic backstory

Why this heartfelt saying

Suppabuddha only comes to the crowd because he thinks food is being handed out. He stays because there isn’t any and the teaching is happening anyway. Within one sitting he becomes a stream-enterer — and within the hour, walking away, he is killed by a charging cow. This udāna sits with that sequence without flinching, and then explains, unhurried, exactly why it happened this way.

Reading guide

A hungry man in the wrong place

Suppabuddha is introduced without ceremony: “a poor person, destitute, and pitiful,” living with leprosy in Rājagaha. He sees a crowd seated in the distance and thinks, reasonably enough, “Surely some fresh and cooked foods will be distributed there… Hopefully I’ll get something to eat.” Only when he arrives does he discover his mistake: it’s the Buddha teaching Dhamma to a large assembly, no food in sight. His decision to stay anyway — “Why don’t I also listen to the teaching?” — is presented as almost an afterthought, not a moment of religious longing. The sutta doesn’t sentimentalize his motives, and that honesty is part of what makes the scene work.

The Buddha reads the room

What follows is a set-piece the tradition uses elsewhere too: the Buddha scans the assembly’s minds, identifies who is ready, and delivers a graduated teaching aimed precisely at that person — giving, ethical conduct, and heaven; the drawbacks of sensual pleasure; the benefit of renunciation; and finally, once Suppabuddha’s mind is “ready, supple, without hindrances, elated, and confident,” the four noble truths. The text’s own simile for what happens next is exact: “Just as a clean cloth rid of stains would properly absorb dye,” the vision of the Dhamma arises in him on the spot — “Everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease.” Suppabuddha goes for refuge as a lay follower, using the same formula of gratitude (“righting the overturned…”) found elsewhere in the canon for a listener whose confusion has genuinely lifted.

Refuge, then sudden death

The very next line is abrupt on purpose: “But soon after Suppabuddha had left, a cow with a baby calf charged at him and took his life.” No transition, no warning, no editorializing. Mendicants come to the Buddha asking where he has been reborn, plainly anxious about a man who died moments after his conversion. The Buddha’s answer is immediate and unqualified: with three fetters ended, Suppabuddha is a stream-enterer, “not liable to be reborn in the underworld, assured, destined for awakening.” The abrupt timing turns out to be beside the point — what mattered had already happened, in the moments before the cow appeared.

Karma explained, not merely asserted

A mendicant then asks the harder question: why was Suppabuddha a leper, poor and pitiful, in the first place? The Buddha’s answer reaches back to a much earlier life, when Suppabuddha was a financier’s son who encountered Tagarasikhī, a paccekabuddha (“Independent Buddha”), entering the city for alms. Mistaking him for something contemptible — “Who is this leper wandering about with his leper’s robe?” — he spat on the ground and turned his back. The result was proportioned in two parts: many years in hell, and then, as “a residual result of that same deed,” a life as an actual leper once he returned to the human realm. But the story doesn’t end there: “owing to faith in the teaching… he undertook ethics, learning, generosity, and wisdom,” and this same difficult life led, at its close, to rebirth “in the company of the gods of the thirty-three,” where he “outshines the other gods in beauty and glory.” A single lifetime of disadvantage, in other words, was neither pure punishment nor a dead end — it was also the setting in which he built the very qualities that carried him past it.

The closing verse

The heartfelt saying that closes the sutta reads almost like advice Suppabuddha himself might have used decades earlier: “As a clear-eyed man would avoid rough paths, so long as strength is found; an astute person in the living world, would shun bad deeds.” It is a plain, practical caution, delivered only after the listener has seen in full detail exactly what one careless act of contempt can cost — and exactly how much a difficult life can still be turned toward something good.

Key terms

kuṭṭhī — “leper” — Suppabuddha’s condition, and part of the sutta’s own Pali title, Suppabuddhakuṭṭhisutta, “the discourse with Suppabuddha the leper.”
paccekabuddha — an “Independent Buddha” — Tagarasikhī, the figure Suppabuddha insulted in a past life, who awakened without a teacher and without founding a following.
the clean-cloth simile — the text’s image for the arising of the Dhamma-eye: “Just as a clean cloth rid of stains would properly absorb dye,” so the truth takes hold in a mind made ready.
three fetters — identity view, doubt, and misapprehension of precepts and observances; their ending is what defines Suppabuddha, at his death, as a stream-enterer.
the graduated talk — the Buddha’s step-by-step teaching sequence for a receptive listener: giving, ethical conduct, and heaven; the drawbacks of sensuality; the benefit of renunciation; then the four noble truths.

The text

The discourse in full: Suppabuddha’s hope for food, his conversion, his sudden death, and the Buddha’s explanation of the karma behind both his hardship and his final destination. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground. At that time in Rājagaha there was a leper called Suppabuddha. He was a poor person, destitute, and pitiful. Now, at that time the Buddha was teaching Dhamma, surrounded by a large assembly.

§2Suppabuddha saw the crowd seated off in the distance and thought, “Surely some fresh and cooked foods will be distributed there. Why don’t I approach the crowd? Hopefully I’ll get something to eat.”

§3So he approached the crowd where he saw the Buddha teaching Dhamma, surrounded by a large assembly. When he saw this he thought, “There’s no food being distributed here. The ascetic Gotama is teaching Dhamma in an assembly. Why don’t I also listen to the teaching?” Right there he sat down to one side, thinking, “I also will listen to the teaching.”

§4Then the Buddha focused on encompassing the minds of everyone in the assembly, thinking, “Who here is capable of understanding the teaching?” He saw Suppabuddha sitting in the assembly, and thought, “He is capable of understanding the teaching.” He gave a step by step talk especially for Suppabuddha on giving, ethical conduct, and heaven. He explained the drawbacks of sensual pleasures, so sordid and corrupt, and the benefit of renunciation. When the Buddha knew that Suppabuddha’s mind was ready, supple, without hindrances, elated, and confident, he revealed the teaching unique to the Buddhas: suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path. Just as a clean cloth rid of stains would properly absorb dye, in that very seat the stainless, immaculate vision of the Dhamma arose in Suppabuddha: “Everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease.”

§5Then Suppabuddha saw, attained, understood, and fathomed the Dhamma. He went beyond doubt, got rid of indecision, and became self-assured and independent of others regarding the Teacher’s instructions. He rose from his seat and went to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and said:

§6“Excellent, sir! Excellent! As if he were righting the overturned, or revealing the hidden, or pointing out the path to the lost, or lighting a lamp in the dark so people with clear eyes can see what’s there, the Buddha has made the teaching clear in many ways. I go for refuge to the Buddha, to the teaching, and to the mendicant Saṅgha. From this day forth, may the Buddha remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.”

§7After Suppabuddha had been educated, encouraged, fired up, and inspired with a Dhamma talk by the Buddha, he rose from his seat, bowed, and respectfully circled the Buddha before leaving. But soon after Suppabuddha had left, a cow with a baby calf charged at him and took his life.

§8Then several mendicants went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and said, “The leper named Suppabuddha, after being educated, encouraged, fired up, and inspired with a Dhamma talk by the Buddha, has passed away. Where has he been reborn in his next life?”

§9“Mendicants, Suppabuddha was astute. He practiced in line with the teaching, and did not trouble me about the teachings. With the ending of three fetters, Suppabuddha is a stream-enterer, not liable to be reborn in the underworld, assured, destined for awakening.”

§10When he said this, one of the mendicants said to the Buddha, “What is the cause, sir, what is the reason why Suppabuddha became a leper, a poor person, destitute, and pitiful?”

§11“Once upon a time, mendicants, Suppabuddha used to be a financier’s son right here in Rājagaha. On his way to visit a park, he saw Tagarasikhī, an Independent Buddha, entering the city for alms. When he saw this he thought, “Who is this leper wandering about with his leper’s robe?” Before leaving, he spat on the ground and turned his left side to Tagarasikhī. As a result of that deed he burned in hell for many years, for many hundreds, many thousands, many hundreds of thousands of years. And as a residual result of that same deed, he became a leper right here in Rājagaha, a poor person, destitute and pitiful. But owing to faith in the teaching and training proclaimed by the Realized One, he undertook ethics, learning, generosity, and wisdom. After undertaking these things, when his body broke up, after death, he was reborn in a good place, a heavenly realm, in the company of the gods of the thirty-three. There he outshines the other gods in beauty and glory.”

§12Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§13“As a clear-eyed man would avoid rough paths,
so long as strength is found;
an astute person in the living world,
would shun bad deeds.”

Udāna · Discourse 8.9

With Dabba (1st)

Paṭhamadabbasutta

Setting
Near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground
Speakers
The Buddha and Venerable Dabba the Mallian
Form
A brief narrative of a mendicant’s final passing, closing with a four-line heartfelt verse (udāna)
Length
~2 minutes to read
Position
First of two consecutive discourses about Dabba’s passing; Ud 8.10 retells the same event to the wider assembly
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — a vivid narrative with a precisely constructed closing verse

Why this discourse

Dabba the Mallian simply announces that it is time for his full extinguishment, receives the Buddha’s unadorned permission, and then, rising into the air, brings his own life to an end in a display involving fire and leaving nothing behind. The verse that follows is worth reading with real care: it names, one by one, exactly the five components early Buddhist thought uses to describe a living person, and states that each one, in turn, has ended.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Dabba the Mallian tells the Buddha it is time for his full extinguishment, is told simply to do as he sees fit, and then rises into the air, enters and withdraws from the fire element, and passes away while seated cross-legged in midair, his body burning away without leaving even ash or soot — prompting a verse naming the ending of body, perception, feeling, choices, and consciousness in turn.

A request, and a simple assent

What stands out first is how little ceremony surrounds the request itself. Dabba approaches, bows, sits down, and states plainly: “Holy One, it is the time for my full extinguishment.” The Buddha’s reply is equally brief: “Please, Dabba, do as you see fit.” No further instruction, no final teaching, no elaborate farewell — the entire exchange is two short lines, treating Dabba’s readiness as something requiring no further discussion.

Rising into the air, entering the fire element

Dabba then rises from his seat, bows, circles the Buddha respectfully, and rises into the air, where “sitting cross-legged in midair, [he] entered and withdrew from the fire element before becoming fully extinguished.” The result is described precisely: his body, burning and combusting, left “neither ashes nor soot to be found” — compared directly to ghee or oil burning completely away, leaving no residue at all. The simile is chosen carefully: ghee and oil are substances that, when they burn, leave behind nothing solid, unlike wood or a body under ordinary circumstances.

The closing verse: five things named, five things ended

The heartfelt sentiment that follows is compact but exact: “the body is broken up, perception has ceased, all feelings have become cool; choices are stilled, and consciousness come to an end.” Read against the standard early Buddhist analysis of a person into five aggregates — form (here, the body), feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness — the verse names every one of the five, in a slightly different order, and states that each has ended. This is not incidental phrasing; it is a precise, structured statement using the exact analytical vocabulary the tradition uses elsewhere to describe what a living being consists of.

What the verse does, and does not, claim

As with the “About Extinguishment” passages earlier in this vagga, it is worth noticing what the verse does not say. It does not describe Dabba as going anywhere, becoming anything, or continuing in some altered form. It states, in the same disciplined register found throughout this collection’s treatment of the subject, that each of the five components used to describe a person has ceased — body, perception, feeling, choices, consciousness — without characterizing what, if anything, remains to be said about a person once the terms ordinarily used to describe one no longer apply.

Key terms

tejodhātu — “fire element”: the meditative element Dabba enters and withdraws from immediately before his final passing.
parinibbāyi — “became fully extinguished”: the verb marking the moment of final passing, distinct from ordinary death.
the five aggregates (pañcakkhandha) — body, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness: the standard early Buddhist analysis of a living person, each one named as ending in the closing verse.
saṅkhārā — “choices” or volitional formations: rendered here as “stilled,” one of the five aggregates named in the verse.
Dabba the Mallian — a senior mendicant, elsewhere remembered for his skill in organizing lodgings and meal rosters for the Saṅgha; his manner of passing is unique among named disciples in the Canon.

The text

The discourse in full, given complete and without abbreviation. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground. Then Venerable Dabba the Mallian went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and said to him: “Holy One, it is the time for my full extinguishment.” “Please, Dabba, do as you see fit.”

§2Then Dabba rose from his seat, bowed and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on his right. Then he rose into the air and, sitting cross-legged in midair, entered and withdrew from the fire element before becoming fully extinguished.

§3Then when he was fully quenched while sitting cross-legged in midair, his body burning and combusting left neither ashes nor soot to be found. It’s like when ghee or oil blaze and burn, and neither ashes nor soot are found.

§4Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§5“The body is broken up, perception has ceased,
all feelings have become cool;
choices are stilled,
and consciousness come to an end.”

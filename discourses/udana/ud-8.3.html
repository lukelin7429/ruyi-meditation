Udāna · Discourse 8.3

About Extinguishment (3rd)

Tatiyanibbānapaṭisaṁyuttasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery, in the same recurring setting as Ud 8.1–8.2
Speakers
The Buddha, addressing the assembled mendicants collectively
Form
A short prose heartfelt sentiment (udāna), built as a conditional argument rather than a list of negations
Length
<1 minute to read
Position
Third of four consecutive “About Extinguishment” discourses; contains the canon’s best-known “there is an unborn…” statement
Difficulty
★★★★★ — brief, logically structured, and just as easy to over-interpret as its neighbors

Why this discourse

This short passage is probably the second-most quoted statement on nibbāna in the Canon, after Ud 8.1. Where that passage negates at length, this one argues — using a genuine conditional structure, “if there were no X, then no escape would be possible; but since there is X, escape is found.” It is worth reading closely as an argument, not simply as another description, since its shape tells you something about what kind of claim is actually being made.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Buddha states that there is freedom from rebirth and from what has been produced, made, and conditioned, then argues for it in conditional form: if this freedom did not exist, no escape from rebirth and conditioned existence would be possible — but since it does exist, an escape is found.

Same setting, third pass

The frame is again identical to Ud 8.1 and Ud 8.2: an ongoing Dhamma talk on extinguishment, an attentive assembly, a heartfelt sentiment arising from “understanding this matter.” Three passages in a row, three different rhetorical strategies applied to what is recognizably the same subject.

Four negated terms

The Pali behind “freedom from rebirth, freedom from what has been produced, made, and conditioned” is a set of four compact negations: ajātaṁ (unborn), abhūtaṁ (not become, unarisen), akataṁ (unmade), and asaṅkhataṁ (unconditioned). Sujato’s translation renders the set as “freedom from rebirth” plus “what has been produced, made, and conditioned” — four ways of denying that this thing arose, came into being, was constructed, or depends on conditions, the four terms that between them cover just about every way early Buddhist thought describes something coming to exist.

An argument, not only a description

What makes this passage distinctive among the four “About Extinguishment” discourses is its explicit logical structure: “if there were no… then you would find no escape… but since there is… an escape is found.” This is a conditional argument, stated twice — once in the negative (what would follow from its absence) and once in the affirmative (what follows from its presence). The passage is not simply describing a state; it is reasoning toward the conclusion that liberation is genuinely possible, by pointing out what would have to be true for that possibility to make sense at all.

What kind of claim is this?

It matters that the argument concludes only “an escape is found,” not a description of what the escape is like from the inside. The passage secures a single point: given that conditioned existence is what it is (produced, made, dependent, subject to rebirth), a way out is only conceivable if something not of that kind exists. It does not go on to say what that something is positively like, any more than Ud 8.1 does. The restraint is the same; only the mode of argument differs.

Relation to the Buddha’s own account of seeking

This same vocabulary appears in the Buddha’s account of his own pre-awakening search, recorded at MN 26: seeking “the unborn, the unaging, the unailing, the deathless, the sorrowless, the unstained,” and eventually reaching it. Reading Ud 8.3 alongside that passage is useful precisely because it shows the same term, ajātaṁ (“unborn”), functioning in both a first-person narrative of a search and this later, impersonal statement of its logical necessity — two different literary settings converging on the same claim.

A caution against building a metaphysics on it

As with Ud 8.1, it is tempting to read this passage as establishing a metaphysical entity — an unconditioned Absolute standing apart from the conditioned world, waiting to be found. The text itself does not go that far. Its logical form is defensive rather than constructive: it argues that something unconditioned must exist, on pain of liberation being impossible, without characterizing that something beyond the bare fact of its not being born, produced, made, or conditioned. Whatever positive picture a reader supplies beyond that is their own addition, not the text’s claim.

Key terms

ajātaṁ — “unborn”: rendered by Sujato as “freedom from rebirth”; the same term used of what the Buddha describes seeking at MN 26.
abhūtaṁ, akataṁ, asaṅkhataṁ — “not become, unmade, unconditioned”: three further negations, rendered together by Sujato as “what has been produced, made, and conditioned,” denied of this freedom.
nissaraṇa — “escape”: what the passage argues is only possible because an unconditioned freedom exists; the argument’s conclusion, not its premise.
the conditional form — “if there were no… then no escape… but since there is… an escape is found”: a logical structure distinguishing this passage from the pure negation of Ud 8.1 and the compressed verse of Ud 8.2.
saṅkhata / asaṅkhata — “conditioned / unconditioned”: the basic Abhidhamma-era distinction this passage is among the earliest and clearest canonical sources for.

The text

The discourse in full, given complete and without abbreviation. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time the Buddha was educating, encouraging, firing up, and inspiring the mendicants with a Dhamma talk about extinguishment. And those mendicants were paying attention, applying the mind, concentrating wholeheartedly, and actively listening.

§2Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

§3“There is, mendicants, freedom from rebirth, freedom from what has been produced, made, and conditioned. If there were no freedom from rebirth, freedom from what has been produced, made, and conditioned, then you would find no escape here from rebirth, from what has been produced, made, and conditioned. But since there is freedom from rebirth, freedom from what has been produced, made, and conditioned, an escape is found from rebirth, from what has been produced, made, and conditioned.”

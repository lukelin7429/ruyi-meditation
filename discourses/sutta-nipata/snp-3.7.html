Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 3.7

With Sela

Selasutta

Setting
Wandering through the land of the Aṅguttarāpans, arriving at their town of Āpaṇa, with a large Saṅgha of 1,250 mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha; the matted-hair ascetic Keṇiya; the brahmin Sela, a master of the three Vedas; Sela’s three hundred young students
Form
A layered narrative — a threefold invitation, a physical verification of the “marks of a great man,” a verse debate on kingship, and a mass ordination
Length
~16 minutes to read
Parallel
Preserved almost word for word in the Majjhima Nikāya as MN 92, also titled the Selasutta
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — a vivid story, though it leans on brahmanical lore about bodily “marks” that’s unfamiliar to most modern readers

Why this discourse

Few discourses in the Sutta Nipāta tell as complete a story as this one: an ascetic’s generous invitation, a scholar-brahmin’s skepticism, a body examined for thirty-two legendary marks, and a debate about what “kingship” really means, all resolving in a single afternoon’s mass ordination of three hundred students. At its center is a question that mattered enormously in ancient India and still has bite today: when someone is recognized as extraordinary, what should society expect from them — dominion, or something else entirely? Sela arrives expecting to find, at best, a future emperor. The Buddha’s answer — “I am a king, Sela, the supreme king of the teaching” — redefines the terms of the conversation entirely.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Physical greatness, verified mark by mark, points to two possible destinies — universal monarch or Buddha — but the Buddha, confronted with a brahmin eager to crown him, insists that his actual sovereignty is of a different order altogether: he “rolls forth the wheel of the teaching,” not the wheel of conquest.

An ascetic’s hospitality, three times offered

The sutta opens with Keṇiya, a matted-hair ascetic — the term describes a class of non-Buddhist renunciants recognizable by their hair — who hears of the Buddha’s arrival at Āpaṇa with 1,250 mendicants and is moved simply by hearing the standard formula of the Buddha’s reputation recited in full. He invites the entire Saṃgha to a meal. The Buddha twice declines with the same practical objection: the Saṃgha is large, and Keṇiya is “devoted to the brahmins” — a gentle hint that the Buddha assumes brahmin generosity has already been spent elsewhere. Only on the third asking does he consent, by silence, the standard narrative signal throughout the canon for acceptance. Keṇiya then organizes an entire household’s labor around the meal: ovens dug, wood chopped, water jars set, a pavilion built by his own hands.

The brahmin Sela hears the news

Sela is introduced with real specificity: master of the three Vedas together with their vocabularies, ritual, phonology, grammar, and “the testaments as fifth,” well versed in cosmology and — crucially — in the marks of a great man, and currently teaching three hundred young students. Passing Keṇiya’s hermitage mid-preparation, he assumes a wedding, a sacrifice, or a royal visit. What he hears instead is a name: the awakened one. Sela makes Keṇiya repeat the word twice, as if testing whether it was really said. Then, alone with his thoughts, he recalls a maxim handed down from “brahmins of the past, elderly and senior, the tutors of tutors”: a being who possesses the thirty-two marks of a great man has exactly two possible futures, and no others.

Two destinies, thirty-two marks

The doctrine Sela recites is precise: possessing the marks, a great man who stays at home becomes a wheel-turning monarch, ruling by principle over all four quarters, backed by seven treasures and a thousand valiant sons — conquering “without rod or sword.” Or, if he goes forth from lay life, he becomes a Buddha, one who has “cast off the world’s veil.” This same doctrine of the two-destiny great man appears repeatedly across the early texts (see DN 30, on the marks themselves, and DN 26, on the wheel-turning monarch, both already on this site) — evidence of how deeply this idea was shared between the Buddhist and brahmanical worlds, even while each side drew very different conclusions from it.

Verifying the marks

Sela examines the Buddha’s body and finds every mark present but two: he cannot confirm whether the genitals are sheathed in a foreskin, or gauge the true size of the tongue — both marks that ordinary observation can’t settle without either indecency or a physically implausible display. The text handles this with startling directness: the Buddha, reading Sela’s doubt, uses psychic power to resolve the first and simply demonstrates the second, stretching his tongue to stroke his ear holes, nostrils, and entire forehead. It is one of the few moments in the early canon where a physical wonder is deployed this specifically — not to awe a crowd, but to settle a scholar’s precise technical doubt.

Sela’s praise, and a claim of kingship

Convinced the marks are all present, but still uncertain whether presence of the marks alone proves awakening, Sela recalls another maxim — that a true Buddha “reveals himself when praised” — and extols the Buddha on the spot in verse, admiring his radiance, and then, quite directly, urging him toward the throne he seems so obviously built for: “you’re fit to be a king… may you reign as king of kings, lord of mankind!” The Buddha’s reply reframes the entire encounter: “I am a king, Sela… the supreme king of the teaching. By the teaching I roll forth the wheel which cannot be rolled back.” Sela presses further, in the idiom of court and army: if you are a king, who is your general, the one who keeps your wheel rolling? The Buddha names Sāriputta — “taking after the Realized One, keeps it rolling on” — before stating his own credentials in the driest possible terms: “I have known what should be known, and developed what should be developed, and given up what should be given up: and so, brahmin, I am a Buddha.”

A mass ordination

Sela does not merely accept the answer; he turns immediately to his own three hundred students, telling them that whoever endorses the Buddha’s teaching should go forth with him right there, on the spot, and that those who don’t wish to may simply leave. Every one of them asks to be ordained. It is one of the largest single ordinations recorded anywhere in the early texts, and the Buddha’s consent is characteristically understated: “the going forth isn’t in vain for one who trains with diligence.” The meal Keṇiya had spent the previous section preparing finally arrives — served, notably, with the Buddha’s new monastic recruits presumably among the assembly — and the Buddha repays the hospitality with a short verse in Keṇiya’s honor, ranking the Saṃgha itself as “the foremost” object of merit-seeking generosity, alongside the sun among blazes and the ocean among rivers.

Eight days to full awakening

The sutta’s final movement compresses an entire spiritual trajectory into a single line: Sela and his assembly, “living alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute,” realize the goal of the spiritual life “in this very life” and become arahants. Their closing verses date the transformation with unusual precision — “this is the eighth day since we went for refuge… in these seven days… we’ve become tamed in your teaching” — and end with an image of physical reverence matching the physical verification that opened the story: three hundred newly liberated monks, standing with cupped palms raised, asking the Buddha to stretch out his feet so that they, as the text puts it, might let “these giants bow to the Teacher.”

Key terms

the thirty-two marks — physical characteristics (mahāpurisa lakkhaṇa) that brahmanical lore held to indicate a “great man” destined to become either a wheel-turning monarch or a Buddha; treated in detail in DN 30.
cakkavatti — the “wheel-turning monarch,” a universal ruler who conquers “by principle, without rod or sword” and possesses seven treasures; the subject of DN 26.
“the supreme king of the teaching” — the Buddha’s own re-description of his authority, deliberately borrowing and subverting the vocabulary of kingship Sela offers him.
matted-hair ascetic (jaṭila) — Keṇiya’s classification — a type of non-Buddhist renunciant recognizable by unshaven, knotted hair, common in this period.
MN 92 — the Majjhima Nikāya’s parallel version of this same story, textually very close to the Sutta Nipāta’s account.

The text

The discourse in full: Keṇiya’s invitation, Sela’s scrutiny of the thirty-two marks, the verse exchange on kingship, the mass ordination, and the closing verses eight days later. This is one of the longer and most narratively developed suttas in the collection, reproduced complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The ascetic Gotama’s reputation

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was wandering together with a large Saṅgha of 1,250 mendicants in the land of the Aṅguttarāpans when he arrived at a town of theirs named Āpaṇa. The matted-hair ascetic Keṇiya heard: “It seems the ascetic Gotama—a Sakyan, gone forth from a Sakyan family—has arrived at Āpaṇa, together with a large Saṅgha of 1,250 mendicants. He has this good reputation: ‘That Blessed One is perfected, a fully awakened Buddha, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, holy, knower of the world, supreme guide for those fit for training, teacher of gods and humans, awakened, blessed.’ He has realized with his own insight this world—with its gods, Māras, and divinities, this population with its ascetics and brahmins, gods and humans—and he makes it known to others. He proclaims a teaching that’s good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, meaningful and well-phrased. And he reveals a spiritual practice that’s entirely full and pure. It’s good to see such perfected ones.”

An invitation, thrice repeated

§2So Keṇiya approached the Buddha and exchanged greetings with him. When the greetings and polite conversation were over, he sat down to one side. The Buddha educated, encouraged, fired up, and inspired him with a Dhamma talk. Then he said to the Buddha, “Would Mister Gotama together with the mendicant Saṅgha please accept tomorrow’s meal from me?” When he said this, the Buddha said to him, “The Saṅgha is large, Keṇiya; there are 1,250 mendicants. And you are devoted to the brahmins.”

§3–4For a second time, Keṇiya asked the Buddha to accept a meal offering. “Never mind that the Saṅgha is large, with 1,250 mendicants, and that I am devoted to the brahmins. Would Mister Gotama together with the mendicant Saṅgha please accept tomorrow’s meal from me?” And for a second time, the Buddha gave the same reply. For a third time, Keṇiya asked the Buddha to accept a meal offering. “Never mind that the Saṅgha is large, with 1,250 mendicants, and that I am devoted to the brahmins. Would Mister Gotama together with the mendicant Saṅgha please accept tomorrow’s meal from me?” The Buddha consented with silence. Then, knowing that the Buddha had consented, Keṇiya rose from his seat and went to his own hermitage. There he addressed his friends and colleagues, relatives and kin, “My friends and colleagues, relatives and kin: please listen! The ascetic Gotama together with the mendicant Saṅgha has been invited by me for tomorrow’s meal. Please help me out with the manual preparations.” “Yes, worthy sir,” they replied. Some dug ovens, some chopped wood, some washed dishes, some set out a water jar, and some spread out seats. Meanwhile, Keṇiya set up the pavilion himself.

The brahmin Sela hears the news

§5–6Now at that time the brahmin Sela was residing in Āpaṇa. He had mastered the three Vedas, together with their vocabularies and ritual performance, their phonology and word classification, and the testaments as fifth. He knew them word-by-word, and their grammar. He was well versed in cosmology and the marks of a great man. And he was teaching three hundred young students to recite the hymns. And at that time Keṇiya was devoted to Sela. Then Sela, while going for a walk escorted by the three hundred young students, approached Keṇiya’s hermitage. He saw the preparations going on, and said to Keṇiya, “Keṇiya, is your son or daughter being married? Or are you setting up a big sacrifice? Or has King Seniya Bimbisāra of Magadha been invited for tomorrow’s meal?”

§7“There is no marriage, Sela, and the king is not coming. Rather, I am setting up a big sacrifice. The ascetic Gotama has arrived at Āpaṇa, together with a large Saṅgha of 1,250 mendicants. He has this good reputation: ‘That Blessed One is perfected, a fully awakened Buddha, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, holy, knower of the world, supreme guide for those fit for training, teacher of gods and humans, awakened, blessed.’ He has been invited by me for tomorrow’s meal together with the mendicant Saṅgha.” “Worthy Keṇiya, did you say ‘the awakened one’?” “I said ‘the awakened one’.” “Worthy Keṇiya, did you say ‘the awakened one’?” “I said ‘the awakened one’.”

§8Then Sela thought, “It’s hard to even find the word ‘awakened one’ in the world. The thirty-two marks of a great man have been handed down in our hymns. A great man who possesses these has only two possible destinies, no other. If he stays at home he becomes a king, a wheel-turning monarch, a just and principled king. His dominion extends to all four sides, he achieves stability in the country, and he possesses the seven treasures. He has the following seven treasures: the wheel, the elephant, the horse, the jewel, the woman, the householder, and the commander as the seventh treasure. He has over a thousand sons who are valiant and heroic, crushing the armies of his enemies. After conquering this land girt by sea, he reigns by principle, without rod or sword. But if he goes forth from the lay life to homelessness, he becomes a perfected one, a fully awakened Buddha, who has cast off the world’s veil. “But Keṇiya, where is the Blessed One at present, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha?”

Verifying the marks of a great man

§9When he said this, Keṇiya pointed with his right arm and said, “There, worthy Sela, at that line of blue forest.” Then Sela, together with his young students, approached the Buddha. He said to his young students, “Come quietly, gentlemen, tread gently. For the Buddhas are intimidating, like a lion living alone. When I’m consulting with the ascetic Gotama, don’t interrupt. Wait until I’ve finished speaking.”

§10–11Then Sela went up to the Buddha, and exchanged greetings with him. When the greetings and polite conversation were over, he sat down to one side. and scrutinized the Buddha’s body for the thirty-two marks of a great man. He saw all of them except for two, which he had doubts about: whether the private parts were sheathed in a foreskin, and the largeness of the tongue. Then it occurred to the Buddha, “Sela sees all the marks except for two, which he has doubts about: whether the private parts are sheathed in a foreskin, and the largeness of the tongue.” The Buddha used his psychic power to will that Sela would see his private parts sheathed in a foreskin. And he stuck out his tongue and stroked back and forth on his ear holes and nostrils, and covered his entire forehead with his tongue.

§12Then Sela thought, “The ascetic Gotama possesses the thirty-two marks completely, lacking none. But I don’t know whether or not he is an awakened one. I have heard that brahmins of the past who were elderly and senior, the tutors of tutors, said, ‘Those who are perfected ones, fully awakened Buddhas reveal themselves when praised.’ Why don’t I extoll him in his presence with fitting verses?” Then Sela extolled the Buddha in his presence with fitting verses:

Sela’s praise, in verse

§13–14“O Blessed One, your body’s perfect,
you’re radiant, handsome, lovely to behold;
golden colored,
with teeth so white; you’re strong.
The characteristics
of a handsome man,
the marks of a great man,
are all found on your body.

§15–16Your eyes are clear, your face is fair,
you’re formidable, upright, majestic.
In the midst of the Saṅgha of ascetics,
you shine like the sun.
You’re a mendicant fine to see,
with skin that shines like lustrous gold.
But with such excellent appearance,
what do you want with the ascetic life?

§17–18You’re fit to be a king,
a wheel-turning monarch, chief of charioteers,
victorious in the four quarters,
lord of the Black Plum Tree Land.
Aristocrats, nobles, and kings
ought follow your rule.
Gotama, may you reign
as king of kings, lord of mankind!”

Two kinds of kingship

§19“I am a king, Sela”,
said the Buddha,
“the supreme king of the teaching.
By the teaching I roll forth the wheel
which cannot be rolled back.”

§20–21“You claim to be awakened,”
said Sela the brahmin,
“the supreme king of the teaching.
‘I roll forth the teaching’:
so you say, Gotama.
Then who is your general,
the disciple who follows the Teacher’s way?
Who keeps rolling the wheel
of the teaching you rolled forth?”

§22–23“By me the wheel was rolled forth,”
said the Buddha,
“the supreme wheel of the teaching.
Sāriputta, taking after the Realized One,
keeps it rolling on.
I have known what should be known,
and developed what should be developed,
and given up what should be given up:
and so, brahmin, I am a Buddha.

§24–26Dispel your doubt in me—
make up your mind, brahmin!
The sight of a Buddha
is hard to find again.
I am a Buddha, brahmin,
the supreme surgeon,
one of those whose appearance in the world
is hard to find again.
A manifestation of divinity, unequaled,
crusher of Māra’s army;
having subdued all my opponents,
I rejoice, fearing nothing from any quarter.”

Sela and his students go forth

§27–28“Pay heed, good fellows, to what
is spoken by the Clear-eyed One.
The surgeon, the great hero,
roars like a lion in the jungle.
A manifestation of divinity, unequaled,
crusher of Māra’s army;
who would not be inspired by him,
even one born in a dark class?

§29–31Those who wish may follow me;
those who don’t may go.
Right here, I’ll go forth in his presence,
the one of such outstanding wisdom.”
“Sir, if you endorse
the teaching of the Buddha,
we’ll also go forth in his presence,
the one of such outstanding wisdom.”
“These three hundred brahmins
with cupped palms held up, ask:
‘May we lead the spiritual life
in your presence, Blessed One?’”

§32“The spiritual life is well explained,”
said the Buddha,
“apparent in the present life, immediately effective.
Here the going forth isn’t in vain
for one who trains with diligence.”

§33And the brahmin Sela together with his assembly received the going forth, the ordination in the Buddha’s presence. And when the night had passed Keṇiya had delicious fresh and cooked foods prepared in his own hermitage. Then he had the Buddha informed of the time, saying, “It’s time, worthy Gotama, the meal is ready.” Then the Buddha robed up in the morning and, taking his bowl and robe, went to Keṇiya’s hermitage, where he sat on the seat spread out, together with the Saṅgha of mendicants.

The meal, and a verse of appreciation

§34Then Keṇiya served and satisfied the mendicant Saṅgha headed by the Buddha with his own hands with delicious fresh and cooked foods. When the Buddha had eaten and washed his hand and bowl, Keṇiya took a low seat and sat to one side. The Buddha expressed his appreciation with these verses:

§35–36“The foremost of sacrifices is the offering to the sacred flame;
the Sāvitrī is the foremost of prayer;
of humans, the king is the foremost;
the ocean’s the foremost of rivers;
the foremost of stars is the moon;
the sun is the foremost of blazes;
for those who sacrifice seeking merit,
the Saṅgha is the foremost.”

A return, eight days later

§37–38When the Buddha had expressed his appreciation to Keṇiya the matted-hair ascetic with these verses, he rose from his seat and left. Then Venerable Sela and his assembly, living alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute, soon realized the supreme end of the spiritual path in this very life. They lived having achieved with their own insight the goal for which gentlemen rightly go forth from the lay life to homelessness. And Venerable Sela together with his assembly became perfected. Then Sela with his assembly went to see the Buddha. He arranged his robe over one shoulder, raised his cupped palms toward the Buddha, and said:

§39–40“This is the eighth day since
we went for refuge, O Clear-eyed One.
In these seven days, Blessed One,
we’ve become tamed in your teaching.
You are the Buddha, you are the Teacher,
you are the sage who has overcome Māra;
you have cut off the underlying tendencies,
you’ve crossed over, and you bring humanity across.

§41–42You have transcended attachments,
your defilements are shattered;
you are a lion, free of grasping,
with fear and dread given up.
These three hundred mendicants
stand with cupped palms raised.
Stretch out your feet, great hero:
let these giants bow to the Teacher.”

Question 1 of 10
Where does this discourse take place, and who is traveling with the Buddha?
Correct: A. The scale of the traveling Saṅgha is part of why Keṇiya’s offer to feed everyone is such a notable act of generosity.
Question 2 of 10
Who is Keṇiya, and what does he do at the start of the sutta?
Correct: B. The Buddha accepts only on the third asking, by silence — the canon’s standard way of marking consent.
Question 3 of 10
Who is Sela, and what is he known for?
Correct: C. His technical expertise in the “marks of a great man” is exactly why he is qualified to scrutinize the Buddha’s body.
Question 4 of 10
According to the brahmanical doctrine Sela recalls, what are the two possible destinies of someone who possesses all thirty-two marks of a great man?
Correct: D. This same two-destiny doctrine about the marks appears elsewhere in the canon, including DN 30 and DN 26.
Question 5 of 10
Which two marks does Sela have doubts about, and how are they resolved?
Correct: A. The Buddha stretches his tongue to stroke his ear holes, nostrils, and entire forehead to settle Sela’s doubt directly.
Question 6 of 10
In his praise-verses, what does Sela urge the Buddha to become?
Correct: B. Sela’s assumption sets up the Buddha’s pointed reply about what kind of “king” he actually is.
Question 7 of 10
How does the Buddha respond to being urged toward worldly kingship?
Correct: C. This reframes “kingship” from political dominion to teaching authority — a wheel of Dhamma rather than a wheel of conquest.
Question 8 of 10
When Sela asks who “keeps rolling” the wheel the Buddha has set in motion, who does the Buddha name?
Correct: D. Sāriputta is repeatedly singled out in the early texts as the disciple most closely associated with carrying the teaching forward.
Question 9 of 10
What happens to Sela and his three hundred students after the verse exchange?
Correct: A. It is one of the largest single mass ordinations recorded anywhere in the early Buddhist texts.
Question 10 of 10
What do Sela’s closing verses, delivered eight days later, ask the Buddha to do?
Correct: B. The image closes the sutta with an act of physical reverence that mirrors the physical scrutiny with which the story began.

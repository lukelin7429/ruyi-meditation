Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 3.1

Going Forth

Pabbajjāsutta

Setting
On the road to Rājagaha and up Mount Paṇḍava, in the years before the awakening — the future Buddha is still an unenlightened wandering ascetic
Speakers
A narrator recounting the young ascetic Gotama’s journey, and King Bimbisāra of Magadha, who questions him directly
Form
A brief narrative poem in twenty verses — among the oldest connected accounts in the canon of the future Buddha’s own going forth, long before the elaborated legend of the four sights and the night ride from the palace
Length
~4 minutes to read
Northern parallel
The Sutta Nipāta as a whole stands outside the four Chinese Āgamas; no direct Āgama counterpart to this poem is asserted here, though the same King Bimbisāra later reappears, after the awakening, in the Vinaya’s own account of the Buddha’s early ministry
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — plain narrative verse, but biographically central

Why this discourse, first of the Great Chapter

The Mahāvagga opens not with a teaching but with a story: a young ascetic named Gotama, not yet awakened, walking the alms round in Rājagaha with such evident composure that a king climbs down from his own longhouse roof to go and find him. What King Bimbisāra offers him — a life of pleasure and, by clear implication, the rank of a well-born commander — is precisely what he has already left behind. His answer is unadorned: he has seen the danger in sensual pleasures, and renunciation is his refuge. It is one of the plainest, least embellished tellings anywhere in the canon of the choice that, according to the tradition, made everything else in this collection possible.

Reading guide

The poem in one sentence

A young ascetic, not yet the Buddha, leaves a cramped household life for the open space of homelessness, walks to Rājagaha, and is offered wealth and standing by its king — which he declines, explaining plainly that he has seen the danger in sensual pleasures and is going on to strive.

Not yet the Buddha

The narrator’s opening line is worth pausing on: “I shall extol going forth with the example of the Clear-eyed One” (v.1). “Clear-eyed One” (cakkhumā) is an epithet normally reserved for the Buddha after his awakening, yet it is applied here, retrospectively, to a period before any awakening had occurred. The poem is telling the story from the far side of the events it describes — a completed life looking back at its own beginning — while the events themselves unfold in real time, with a young man who does not yet know how his striving will end.

Leaving a cramped life

The reason for going forth is given in a single compressed image: “This life at home is cramped, a realm of dirt” against “the life of one gone forth is like an open space” (v.2). Seeing this, he went forth. What follows immediately is not a description of a palace or a family left behind — the poem never mentions either — but a description of ethical purification: he shunned bad deeds of body, left verbal misconduct behind, and purified his livelihood (v.3). The going forth is presented, from its very first moment, as a moral undertaking as much as a change of address.

Into Rājagaha, and up the mountain

The scene shifts to Rājagaha, “the Mountainfold of the Magadhans,” where the ascetic goes on the alms round, “replete with excellent marks” (v.4). From atop his own longhouse, King Bimbisāra notices him and is struck enough to order messengers to follow and learn where he is staying (v.5–8). The description of the ascetic himself on his rounds is spare and exact: wandering indiscriminately for alms, sense doors guarded, aware and mindful, his bowl quickly filled (v.9) — a small but telling detail, since a mendicant’s visible bearing was often read, then as now, as evidence of inner discipline. He leaves the city for Mount Paṇḍava, and the messengers report back that he sits “like a tiger or a bull, like a lion in a mountain cave” (v.10–12).

The king’s offer

Bimbisāra comes in person, by chariot as far as the road allows and then on foot (v.13–14), and after the courtesies, addresses him directly: “You are young, just a youth… endowed with beauty and stature, like an aristocrat of good birth in glory at the army’s head, surrounded by a troop of elephants. I offer you pleasures — enjoy them! But please tell me of your birth” (v.16–17). It is worth being precise about what the verses actually say: Bimbisāra does not formally appoint him to any office. He looks at this composed, evidently well-born young stranger, assumes he must be the sort of person born to lead troops, and offers him pleasures to enjoy — an invitation whose social logic points toward wealth and command even if no specific rank is named. Later commentarial tradition makes the offer explicit, elaborating it into a direct proposal of half his kingdom or a place at the head of his army; the poem itself is content with the king’s admiring assumption and a simple offer of enjoyment.

Gotama’s answer

The reply answers the question actually asked — birth — before addressing the offer. He is from the Himalayan foothills, from a wealthy and strong land among the Kosalans; his clan is “named for the Sun,” and he is a Sakyan by birth (v.18–19). Having identified his lineage plainly, he explains why none of it, nor the pleasures on offer, appeals to him: “I have gone forth from that family — I do not yearn for sensual pleasure” (v.19). The poem’s closing lines are its clearest statement of motive, and they double as a hinge into the very next discourse in this collection: “Seeing the danger in sensual pleasures, seeing renunciation as sanctuary, I shall go on to strive; that is where my mind delights” (v.20).

What this poem does not say

Readers coming from the familiar legend — the four sights, the sleeping musicians, the sorrowing father, the wife Yasodharā and infant son Rāhula, the horse Kanthaka and the charioteer Channa riding out by night — will notice how much of that is simply absent here. This poem, among the oldest connected biographical fragments in the Pali canon, names no father, no wife, no son, no companion, and no dramatic departure scene. It moves directly from the household being “cramped” to the ascetic already on the road to Rājagaha. The elaborated narrative familiar from later tradition and popular retelling grew up around and beyond this spare account; MN 26, in the Buddha’s own first-person recollection, is closer in spirit and equally restrained, describing parents “crying with tearful faces” but no more.

Key terms

pabbajjā — “going forth”: the act of leaving household life for homelessness, the discourse’s title and subject.
cakkhumā — “Clear-eyed One,” an epithet for the Buddha applied retrospectively in the poem’s opening line, even though the events it introduces take place before any awakening.
Bimbisāra — the king of Magadha who notices the ascetic Gotama from his longhouse roof; he reappears, after the awakening, as one of the Buddha’s foremost lay patrons.
Rājagaha & Mount Paṇḍava — the Magadhan capital where the alms round takes place, and the mountain on its outskirts where the ascetic settles to meditate.
“named for the Sun” — the Sakyan clan’s claimed solar lineage (Ādicca-bandhu, “Kinsman of the Sun,” is used elsewhere in the canon as an epithet of the Buddha himself), given here as the answer to Bimbisāra’s question about birth.
“seeing the danger” — (ādīnava) the poem’s closing rationale for renunciation: not aversion for its own sake, but a clear-eyed assessment of what sensual pleasure costs.

The text

The discourse in full, all twenty verses. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral). Verse numbers below follow the traditional stanza divisions.

§1“I shall extol going forth
with the example of the Clear-eyed One,
the course of inquiry that led to
his choice to go forth.

§2‘This life at home is cramped,
a realm of dirt.’
‘The life of one gone forth is like an open space.’
Seeing this, he went forth.

§3Having gone forth, he shunned
bad deeds of body.
And leaving verbal misconduct behind,
he purified his livelihood.

§4The Buddha went to Rājagaha,
the Mountainfold of the Magadhans.
He betook himself for alms,
replete with excellent marks.

§5Bimbisāra saw him
while standing atop his longhouse.
Noticing that he was endowed with marks,
he said the following:

§6‘Pay heed, sirs, to this one,
handsome, majestic, radiant;
accomplished in deportment,
he looks just a plough’s length in front.

§7Eyes downcast, mindful,
unlike one from a low family.
Let the king’s messengers run out,
and find where the mendicant will go.’

§8The messengers sent out
followed right behind, thinking
‘Where will the mendicant go?
Where shall he find a place to stay?’

§9Wandering indiscriminately for alms,
sense doors guarded and well restrained,
aware and mindful,
his bowl was quickly filled.

§10Having wandered for alms,
the sage left the city.
He betook himself to Mount Paṇḍava,
thinking, ‘Here is the place I shall stay.’

§11Seeing that he had arrived at a place to stay,
the messengers withdrew nearby,
but one of them returned
to inform the king.

§12‘Great king, the mendicant
is on the east flank of Mount Paṇḍava.
There he sits, like a tiger or a bull,
like a lion in a mountain cave.’

§13Hearing the messenger’s report,
the aristocrat set out
hurriedly in his fine chariot
towards Mount Paṇḍava.

§14He went as far as vehicles could go,
then dismounted from his chariot,
approached on foot,
and reaching him, drew near.

§15Seated, the king greeted him
and made polite conversation.
When the courtesies were over,
he said the following:

§16‘You are young, just a youth,
a lad in the prime of life.
You are endowed with beauty and stature,
like an aristocrat of good birth

§17in glory at the army’s head,
surrounded by a troop of elephants.
I offer you pleasures—enjoy them!
But please tell me of your birth.’

§18‘Up north, O King,
on the slopes of the Himalayas,
full of wealth and strength, lies the land
of one whose abode is among the Kosalans.

§19Their clan is named for the Sun,
they are Sakyans by birth.
I have gone forth from that family—
I do not yearn for sensual pleasure.

§20Seeing the danger in sensual pleasures,
seeing renunciation as sanctuary,
I shall go on to strive;
that is where my mind delights.’”

Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 3.8

The Dart

Sallasutta

Setting
No narrative frame is given — unusually for the canon, the sutta opens directly with the teaching itself, without “So I have heard” or any named place or audience
Speakers
An unnamed teaching voice, traditionally attributed to the Buddha; no interlocutor appears
Form
Twenty verses of sustained, unbroken argument — a single continuous meditation on mortality and grief
Length
~4 minutes to read
Resonance
Thematically close to the Dhammapada’s chapter on old age (Dhp 11, already on this site) and to the canon’s many teachings on the five daily recollections
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — plain language, but emotionally direct in a way few suttas are

Why this discourse

“The Dart” is one of the shortest suttas in the Mahāvagga, and also one of the most quietly devastating. It offers no story, no questioner, no brahmin to convert — only twenty verses building, image by image, toward a single unflinching claim: that grief, however natural, helps no one, changes nothing, and only adds a second wound to a first. The image that gives the sutta its name — sorrow as a dart lodged in the body, to be deliberately drawn out rather than nursed — has become one of the most widely quoted in the entire Sutta Nipāta, still recited today at Buddhist funerals across many traditions.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Death comes to everyone without exception, grief cannot reach or help the dead, and someone who wants their own happiness should deliberately pluck out the dart of sorrow rather than let it fester.

A teaching without a frame

Unlike almost every other sutta in this collection, the Sallasutta has no opening narrative at all — no “so I have heard,” no place name, no audience identified. It simply begins mid-argument, as though picking up a thought already in progress: “Unforeseen and unknown is the extent of this mortal life — hard and short and bound to pain.” The effect is to make the teaching feel less like advice given to someone in particular and more like a plain description of how things are, addressed to anyone who will ever lose anyone.

Mortality without exception

The opening verses stack up images of universal fragility: ripe fruit always in danger of falling, clay pots destined to break, mortals “young and old, foolish and wise” alike going “under the sway of death.” The sutta is careful to make this point without exception — not simply that people die, but that no wisdom, no virtue, and no relationship can intervene: “a father cannot protect his son, nor relatives their kin.” The verse comparing mortals “led away one by one, like a cow to the slaughter” is one of the starkest images anywhere in the early texts.

Why grief fails to help the dead

Having established that death cannot be prevented, the sutta turns to a second, sharper claim: that grief, once death has happened, accomplishes nothing. “Not by weeping and wailing will you find peace of heart. It just gives rise to more suffering, and distresses your body.” The reasoning is almost clinical — the mourner “grows thin and pale,” hurts only themselves, and “it does nothing to help the dead.” This isn’t a claim that loss shouldn’t be felt; it’s a claim that the specific response of prolonged lamentation compounds harm without addressing it — a distinction the sutta returns to more than once.

The uncertainty at the heart of loss

One of the sutta’s subtlest moves comes in its middle verses: “For whatever you imagine it is, it turns out to be something else. Such is separation: see the way of the world!” Loss, on this account, is disorienting not just because someone is gone but because our expectations about permanence were mistaken from the start — a note that connects this short sutta to the canon’s broader teaching on impermanence (anicca), even though that word itself never appears here.

Extinguishing grief, pulling out the dart

The closing verses offer two images for how grief should be handled, not suppressed but actively released: put out like “a blazing refuge with water,” or blown away “like the wind a tuft of cotton.” Then comes the sutta’s signature image, the one that gives it its title: “One who seeks their own happiness would pluck out the dart from themselves — the wailing and moaning, and sadness inside.” The final verse names the result plainly: “overcoming all sorrow, one is sorrowless and quenched” — quenched (nibbuto) being the same word at the root of nibbāna itself.

Key terms

salla — the “dart” or arrow of the title — here a metaphor for grief itself, lodged in the body and requiring deliberate removal.
“like a cow to the slaughter” — one of the sutta’s starkest images for universal mortality, describing mortals led away one by one while relatives watch and wail.
“for whatever you imagine it is, it turns out to be something else” — the sutta’s account of why loss disorients — not only absence itself, but the exposure of a mistaken assumption of permanence.
nibbuto — “quenched” or “cooled” — the sutta’s closing description of the sorrowless state, sharing its root with nibbāna.

The text

The discourse in full: twenty verses, given complete, with no narrative frame to abridge. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Mortality without exception

§1–2Unforeseen and unknown
is the extent of this mortal life—
hard and short
and bound to pain.
There is no way that
those born will not die.
On reaching old age death follows:
such is the nature of living creatures.

§3–4As ripe fruit
are always in danger of falling,
so mortals once born
are always in danger of death.
As clay pots
made by a potter
all end up being broken,
so is the life of mortals.

§5–6Young and old,
foolish and wise—
all go under the sway of death;
all are destined to die.
When those overcome by death
leave this world for the next,
a father cannot protect his son,
nor relatives their kin.

Why grief fails to help the dead

§7–8See how, while relatives look on,
wailing profusely,
mortals are led away one by one,
like a cow to the slaughter.
And so the world is stricken
by old age and by death.
That is why the attentive do not grieve,
for they understand the way of the world.

§9–10For one whose path you do not know—
not whence they came nor where they went—
you lament in vain,
seeing neither end.
If a bewildered person,
lamenting and self-harming,
could extract any good from that,
then those who see clearly would do the same.

§11–12For not by weeping and wailing
will you find peace of heart.
It just gives rise to more suffering,
and distresses your body.
Growing thin and pale,
you hurt yourself.
It does nothing to help the dead:
your lamentation is in vain.

§13–14Unless a personage gives up grief,
they fall into suffering all the more.
Bewailing those whose time has come,
you fall under the sway of grief.
See, too, other folk departing,
passing on according to their deeds;
fallen under the sway of death,
beings flounder while still here.

The uncertainty at the heart of loss

§15–16For whatever you imagine it is,
it turns out to be something else.
Such is separation:
see the way of the world!
Even if a human lives
a hundred years or more,
they are parted from their family circle,
they leave this life behind.

§17Therefore, having learned from the Perfected One,
dispel lamentation.
Seeing the dead and departed, think:
“I cannot escape this.”

Pulling out the dart

§18As one would extinguish
a blazing refuge with water,
so too an attentive one—a wise,
astute, and skilled person—
would swiftly blow away grief that comes up,
like the wind a tuft of cotton.

§19–20One who seeks their own happiness
would pluck out the dart from themselves—
the wailing and moaning,
and sadness inside.
With dart plucked out, unattached,
having found peace of mind,
overcoming all sorrow,
one is sorrowless and quenched.

Question 1 of 10
What is unusual about how this sutta opens, compared to most others in the canon?
Correct: A. The effect is to present the teaching as a plain description of how things are, rather than advice tailored to one person.
Question 2 of 10
According to the opening verses, can any wisdom, virtue, or relationship prevent death?
Correct: B. Mortality is presented as applying without exception, to “young and old, foolish and wise” alike.
Question 3 of 10
What simile does the sutta use for how mortals are led toward death while relatives look on?
Correct: C. It is one of the starkest and most memorable images anywhere in the early Buddhist texts.
Question 4 of 10
What does the sutta claim about the effect of weeping and wailing on the person who is grieving?
Correct: D. The sutta’s claim is not that loss shouldn’t be felt, but that prolonged lamentation compounds harm without addressing it.
Question 5 of 10
What does the sutta mean by, “For whatever you imagine it is, it turns out to be something else”?
Correct: A. This connects the sutta to the canon’s broader teaching on impermanence, even though the word anicca itself never appears.
Question 6 of 10
What two images does the sutta use for how grief should be actively released, rather than suppressed?
Correct: B. Both images suggest deliberate, active release rather than passive endurance.
Question 7 of 10
What is the sutta’s title image, and what does it represent?
Correct: C. “One who seeks their own happiness would pluck out the dart from themselves — the wailing and moaning, and sadness inside.”
Question 8 of 10
What word does the final verse use to describe the state reached by overcoming sorrow, and what does it share a root with?
Correct: D. The closing image of being “sorrowless and quenched” ties grief’s resolution directly to the vocabulary of liberation itself.
Question 9 of 10
Does the sutta claim that grief should never be felt at all?
Correct: A. The sutta’s target is specifically self-harming, prolonged lamentation, not the initial experience of loss.

Correct: B. Both images appear early in the sutta to establish universal fragility before the argument turns to grief itself.

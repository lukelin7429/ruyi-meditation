Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 1.11

Victory Over Desire for the Body

Vijayasutta

Setting
No prose frame at all — the discourse opens and closes entirely in verse, with no narrative occasion given
Speakers
Traditionally the Buddha, addressed to no one named within the verses
Form
Fourteen verses of continuous, unbroken contemplation — a single sustained argument moving from anatomy to the charnel ground to liberation
Length
~2 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No complete Chinese Āgama counterpart is known to survive for this discourse specifically, consistent with the Sutta Nipāta as a whole
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — unflinching, anatomical language in service of a precise meditative purpose

Why this discourse, the eleventh

Few texts in the canon are as deliberately unglamorous as this one. In fourteen short verses, the body is taken apart piece by piece — bones, organs, the “nine streams” of ordinary bodily discharge, a hollow skull “filled with brains” — and then followed all the way to the charnel ground, where dogs, jackals, crows, and worms finish what illness or age began. This is not morbidity for its own sake. The discourse names its own purpose in the final verse: pride and contempt toward others, it says, are themselves “a failure to see” what any body, one’s own included, actually is.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A sustained, anatomically explicit contemplation of the body as it actually is — organs, discharge, and eventual corpse — offered as a direct antidote to bodily desire, vanity, and the pride or contempt people build on the body’s appearance.

Verses 1–2: movement, and what conceals

The discourse opens almost clinically, cataloguing the body’s basic motions — “walking and standing, sitting and lying down, extending and contracting the limbs.” Verse 2 immediately names the problem the rest of the poem exists to solve: “linked together by bones and sinews, plastered over with flesh and hide, and covered by the skin, the body is not seen as it is.” The skin, in other words, is doing rhetorical work here as much as biological work — it is what makes ordinary seeing incomplete, hiding the actual composition of what people find attractive.

Verses 3–7: what the skin conceals

What follows is a deliberately unsparing inventory: “guts and belly, liver and bladder, heart and lungs, kidney and spleen,” then “spit and snot, sweat and fat, blood and synovial fluid, bile and grease.” The image of “nine streams” of “filth… always flowing” — discharge from the eyes, ears, nostrils, mouth, and body — extends the same logic to ordinary, everyday bodily function, not just to illness or death. The section closes on the head itself, “hollow… all filled with brains,” and a pointed verdict: “governed by ignorance, the fool thinks it’s lovely.” The target of the whole catalogue is named directly here — not the body itself, but the ignorance that finds it straightforwardly beautiful without reckoning with any of this.

Verses 8–9: the charnel ground

The poem then follows the body to its end: “bloated and livid, dumped in a charnel ground, the relatives forget it,” devoured in turn by “dogs, jackals, wolves, and worms,” then “crows and vultures, and any other creatures there.” Charnel-ground contemplation of this kind was, and in some traditions still is, a literal meditation practice, undertaken at actual sites where the dead were exposed, precisely to make bodily impermanence undeniable rather than theoretical.

Verses 10–12: what a wise mendicant does with this

The turn from description to practice is explicit: “a wise mendicant here, having heard the Buddha’s words, fully understands it, for they see it as it is.” Verse 11 states the underlying principle as a compact formula — “as this is, so is that, as that is, so is this” — applying everything just said about the corpse in the charnel ground equally to one’s own living body, and equally to every other living body one might be tempted to desire or admire. On this basis, the verse says, one would “blanch desire for the body inside and out.” The result, in verse 12, is not despair but liberation: “rid of desire and lust,” the wise mendicant “has found the peace free of death, extinguishment, the state that does not pass” — the same body-contemplation that seems so bleak on its surface is presented here as a direct route to the deathless.

Verses 13–14: the real target is pride

The closing verses make the discourse’s actual purpose unmistakable. Verse 13 restates the whole catalogue in a single line — “this two-legged body is dirty and stinking, full of different carcasses, and oozing all over the place” — then adds the observation that gives the poem its point: “but still it is cherished!” The final verse names exactly what that cherishing produces when left unexamined: “if, on account of such a body, someone prides themselves or looks down on others—what is that but a failure to see?” The whole unsparing inventory, in other words, is not aimed at self-disgust for its own sake. It is aimed specifically at the arrogance and contempt that bodily vanity generates between people — and it ends by naming that arrogance, plainly, as a failure of accurate perception.

Key terms

“the body is not seen as it is” — the discourse’s opening diagnosis in verse 2: the skin conceals the body’s actual composition from ordinary perception.
the nine streams — the discharge points of the ordinary living body (eyes, ears, nostrils, mouth, and skin), cited as evidence against bodily idealization even before death enters the picture.
charnel-ground contemplation — a meditation practice, historically undertaken at literal sites where the dead were exposed, that this discourse’s verses 8–9 draw on directly.
“as this is, so is that” — the formula in verse 11 applying everything said about a corpse equally to one’s own living body and to every other body.
“a failure to see” — the discourse’s closing verdict on bodily pride and contempt, naming the real target of the entire contemplation.

The text

All fourteen verses in full, exactly as they stand with no narrative frame at either end. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Movement, and what conceals

Verse 1Walking and standing,
sitting and lying down,
extending and contracting the limbs:
these are the movements of the body.

Verse 2Linked together by bones and sinews,
plastered over with flesh and hide,
and covered by the skin,
the body is not seen as it is.

What the skin conceals

Verse 3It’s full of guts and belly,
liver and bladder,
heart and lungs,
kidney and spleen,

Verse 4spit and snot,
sweat and fat,
blood and synovial fluid,
bile and grease.

Verse 5Then in nine streams
the filth is always flowing.
There is muck from the eyes,
wax from the ears,

Verse 6and snot from the nostrils.
The mouth sometimes vomits
bile and sometimes phlegm.
And from the body, sweat and waste.

Verse 7Then there is the hollow head
all filled with brains.
Governed by ignorance,
the fool thinks it’s lovely.

The charnel ground

Verse 8And when it lies dead,
bloated and livid,
dumped in a charnel ground,
the relatives forget it.

Verse 9It’s devoured by dogs,
by jackals, wolves, and worms.
It’s devoured by crows and vultures,
and any other creatures there.

What a wise mendicant does with this

Verse 10A wise mendicant here,
having heard the Buddha’s words,
fully understands it,
for they see it as it is.

Verse 11“As this is, so is that,
as that is, so is this.”
They’d blanch desire for the body
inside and out.

Verse 12That wise mendicant here
rid of desire and lust,
has found the peace free of death,
extinguishment, the state that does not pass.

The real target is pride

Verse 13This two-legged body is dirty and stinking,
full of different carcasses,
and oozing all over the place—
but still it is cherished!

Verse 14And if, on account of such a body,
someone prides themselves
or looks down on others—
what is that but a failure to see?

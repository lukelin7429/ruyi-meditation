Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 1.1

The Serpent

Uragasutta

Setting
None given — unlike almost everything that follows it in the collection, the text supplies no location, no audience, and no occasion
Speakers
None named. The seventeen verses stand as direct teaching, traditionally placed first in the Sutta Nipāta and in the Khuddaka Nikāya’s verse collections generally
Form
Continuous verse — seventeen four-line verses, each closing with the same refrain
Length
~4 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — short and plainly built, but the imagery is dense and the “near shore and the far” line is deliberately left unglossed

Why this discourse, the first

Nothing introduces this text. No mendicant approaches, no brahmin asks a question, no location is named — the Sutta Nipāta simply begins, verse after verse, with a snake shedding its skin. That image, repeated seventeen times without variation in its final two lines, gives the discourse its name and gives an entire genre of Buddhist verse literature its opening gesture: renunciation is not violent rejection but something closer to a snake’s own biology, a natural outgrowing of what no longer fits. Anger, greed, craving, and conceit are each, in turn, described as sloughed off rather than fought off — and the discourse closes by widening from named defilements to something more structural: the underlying tendencies themselves, and the five hindrances that block calm and clarity in the first place.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Seventeen verses, each ending in the same refrain, use the image of a snake shedding its worn-out skin to describe a mendicant letting go — in turn — of anger, greed, craving, conceit, attachment to future lives, hidden resentment, restless mental activity, and finally the underlying tendencies and hindrances themselves.

A collection opened without introduction

Every other discourse translated for this site opens by telling you where the Buddha was staying and who he was speaking to, even when that framing is brief. The Uragasutta supplies none of it. There is no narrator, no named speaker, no place, no listener. The seventeen verses simply proceed, each stating a quality relinquished and then repeating: “such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far, as a serpent its old worn-out skin.” The commentarial tradition and later collections read these verses as spoken by the Buddha, but the text itself makes no such claim — it is worth noticing, reading the collection’s first page, that this restraint is deliberate rather than accidental.

The refrain: a snake shedding its skin

A snake does not fight its old skin off; it simply grows past it, and the skin comes away because it no longer fits. That is the discourse’s entire argument in a single image, repeated with only the opening couplet changing from verse to verse. The phrase “the near shore and the far” is left unglossed by the text itself — it has been read, at different points in the tradition, as this life and the next, or as the internal and external sense fields, or more broadly as any pair of opposed positions a person might cling to. The discourse does not resolve this ambiguity, and neither does this guide; what is clear is that the mendicant described sheds both sides of whatever pair is meant, not simply trading one shore for another.

Verses 1–4: four things cut off entirely

The first four verses proceed through anger (driven out “as with medicine a snake’s spreading venom”), greed (“cut off entirely, like a lotus plucked flower and stalk”), craving (dried up “that swift-flowing stream”), and conceit (swept away “as a fragile bridge of reeds by a great flood”). Each image supplies its own logic: venom is drawn out, not simply endured; a lotus is removed root and stem, not merely trimmed; a stream is dried at its source; a flimsy reed bridge cannot survive a real flood. The differences between the four similes matter as much as their shared refrain.

Verses 5–7: a future with nothing to find, an interior with nothing hidden

Verse 5 turns from present defilements to future rebirth: such a person “finds no worth” in future lives, the way “an inspector of fig trees finds no flower” — because fig trees, in the region’s common knowledge, produce fruit without any visible flowering stage first. Verse 6 turns inward: no anger is hidden away, stored for later. Verse 7 addresses something more subtle still — the “mental vibrations” that produce restlessness are “cleared away, internally clipped off entirely.”

Verses 8–13: neither running too far nor running back

A new refrain-within-the-refrain appears here: “they have not run too far nor run back.” Verse 8 glosses this as having “gone beyond all this proliferation” — the compulsive elaboration of experience into more experience. Verses 9 through 13 then repeat the same opening line five times, each time adding one clause: knowing that nothing in the world is what it seems, and being free, in turn, of greed, lust, hate, and delusion. The cumulative repetition is itself part of the teaching — each verse adds exactly one term to what has come before, building a list rather than simply restating a claim.

Verses 14–17: roots, distress, entanglement, and hindrance

The closing verses move from named defilements to the underlying structures that produce them: no underlying tendencies remain, and the unskillful roots are gone (v.14); nothing born of distress remains to pull such a person back to “the near shore” (v.15); no entangling “snarl” grows that would bind them to a new life (v.16); and, in the final verse, the five hindrances themselves have been given up, leaving someone “untroubled, rid of indecision, free of thorns.” The discourse ends, that is, not with a dramatic climax but with a kind of inventory completed.

Key terms

uraga — “serpent”: the discourse’s name, drawn from its refrain image of a snake shedding its old skin, repeated in all seventeen verses.
“the near shore and the far” — a phrase the text leaves deliberately unglossed; read variously in the tradition as this world and the next, or as the internal and external sense fields, or more generally as any pair of positions a person might cling to.
proliferation — the restless elaboration of raw experience into further thought and reaction, named directly in verse 8 as what such a mendicant has “gone beyond.”
underlying tendencies — latent dispositions toward greed, aversion, and delusion that persist beneath the level of active thought or emotion, named in verse 14 as fully absent in the person described.
the five hindrances — the five qualities (including indecision, named explicitly) that the final verse describes as “given up,” leaving the mind “free of thorns.”

The text

All seventeen verses of the Uragasutta, given in full, without omission or paraphrase. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1When anger surges, they drive it out,
as with medicine a snake’s spreading venom.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 2They’ve cut off greed entirely,
like a lotus plucked flower and stalk.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 3They’ve cut off craving entirely,
drying up that swift-flowing stream.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 4They’ve swept away conceit entirely,
as a fragile bridge of reeds by a great flood.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 5In future lives they find no worth,
as an inspector of fig trees finds no flower.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 6They hide no anger within,
passed beyond any kind of existence.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 7Their mental vibrations are cleared away,
internally clipped off entirely.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 8They have not run too far nor run back,
but have gone beyond all this proliferation.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 9They have not run too far nor run back,
for they know that nothing in the world is what it seems.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 10They have not run too far nor run back,
knowing nothing is what it seems, free of greed.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 11They have not run too far nor run back,
knowing nothing is what it seems, free of lust.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 12They have not run too far nor run back,
knowing nothing is what it seems, free of hate.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 13They have not run too far nor run back,
knowing nothing is what it seems, free of delusion.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 14They have no underlying tendencies at all,
and are rid of unskillful roots,
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 15They have nothing born of distress at all
to make them to return to the near shore.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 16No snarl grows in them at all,
that would shackle them to a new life.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

Verse 17They’ve given up the five hindrances,
untroubled, rid of indecision, free of thorns.
Such a mendicant sheds the near shore and the far,
as a serpent its old worn-out skin.

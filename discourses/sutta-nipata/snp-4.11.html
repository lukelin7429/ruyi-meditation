Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 4.11

Quarrels and Disputes

Kalahavivādasutta

Setting
Not stated in the verse itself; no location or occasion is named
Speakers
An unnamed questioner and the Buddha, in a rapid back-and-forth of paired verses
Form
Continuous philosophical verse, minimal narrative frame — a chain of eight linked questions, each answer supplying the material for the next question
Length
~4 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A version of much of the Aṭṭhakavagga survives in the Chinese Yizu Jing (義足經, T198), translated in the 3rd century CE, though exact correspondence to this individual sutta is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★★★★ — a tightly linked causal chain, among the most conceptually demanding verse in the collection

Why this discourse, the eleventh

This discourse works like a chain of “but why?” questions a child might ask, except each answer here is precise enough to become the next question’s premise. Quarrels trace back to what we hold dear; what we hold dear traces back to desire; desire traces back to pleasant and unpleasant feeling; feeling traces back to contact; and contact, finally, traces back to name-and-form — the basic fact of there being an experiencing body and mind at all. Scholars have long noted how closely this sequence tracks the standard analysis of dependent origination (paṭiccasamuppāda) found throughout the Nikāyas, particularly its middle links of contact, feeling, and craving. Whether this verse chain is an ancestor of that later systematic formula or an independent parallel arrived at by the same reasoning, it shows the causal analysis of suffering already at work in some of the oldest verse the canon preserves.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Quarrels, grief, and stinginess trace back through what we hold dear, then desire, then pleasant and unpleasant feeling, then contact, and finally to name-and-form itself — and a sage who sees this whole dependency chain, rather than arguing over any single link in it, is freed and stops entering disputes altogether.

Eight questions, one chain

The discourse is built as a sequence of paired verses: a question, then an answer that supplies exactly the material needed for the next question. It opens by asking where quarrels and disputes, lamentation, sorrow, stinginess, conceit, arrogance, and slander come from, and receives a first answer — “from what we hold dear” — that is immediately treated as itself needing explanation. The questioner keeps pressing back, one step at a time: where does what we hold dear come from? Desire. Where does desire come from? Pleasant and unpleasant feeling. Where does feeling come from? Contact. Where does contact come from? Name-and-form. At each stage the answer is not a final resting point but the premise for the next “why.”

The middle links: feeling, evaluation, and the volatile pair

A pivotal verse observes that “seeing the manifestation and vanishing of forms, a personage makes evaluations in the world” — the mind doesn’t just register pleasant and unpleasant feeling passively, it actively judges and interprets what it perceives, and anger, lies, and indecision all arise “when that pair” — pleasure and pain — “is present.” The chain then narrows further: pleasure and pain themselves spring from contact, and when contact is absent they simply do not occur. Possessiveness, in a parallel side-branch, is traced to wishing rather than to feeling directly — a second thread the discourse weaves in alongside the main one.

Name-and-form, and how it vanishes

The chain bottoms out at contact arising from “name and form” (nāma-rūpa) — the basic combination of a physical body and the mental factors that engage with it, the minimal condition for any experience at all. Asked how form “vanishes” — how this whole chain can be interrupted rather than merely described — the answer turns unexpectedly subtle: not through ordinary perception, and not through distorted perception, but also not through lacking perception altogether or perceiving something that has already vanished. This points to a specific, exacting meditative state rather than simple inattention or blankness, and the discourse adds that “judgments due to proliferation” (papañca) themselves spring from perception — naming perception as the point where the whole chain of elaboration either continues or stops.

An occasion with no residue

The final exchange asks directly whether this analysis represents the highest possible purity, or whether something further remains. The answer distinguishes two claims: some genuinely consider this the highest extent of purity, but others, “claiming to be experts,” speak of something beyond even this — “an occasion when there is no residue,” a traditional description of final nibbāna, in which nothing at all remains to continue. The closing verse ties the whole chain together: knowing that these states are dependent, and knowing specifically what each one depends on, the inquiring sage is freed and “enters no dispute” — not by winning an argument about which link matters most, but by seeing the entire dependency structure clearly enough that there is nothing left to argue about.

Key terms

kalahavivāda — literally “quarrels and disputes,” the discourse’s title and its opening question, traced across the chain of verses to increasingly fundamental causes.
name-and-form (nāma-rūpa) — the basic combination of a physical body and the mental factors that engage with it — the point at which the discourse’s causal chain bottoms out, and a standard link in the broader analysis of dependent origination.
proliferation (papañca) — the mind’s tendency to elaborate perception into judgment, comparison, and conceptual sprawl; the discourse names it as springing directly from perception itself.
“an occasion when there is no residue” — a traditional description of final nibbāna at the death of one who is fully awakened, when no conditions remain to give rise to further existence.
dependent origination (paṭiccasamuppāda) — the standard Nikāya formula tracing suffering through a chain of conditions including contact, feeling, and craving; this discourse’s causal chain closely parallels its middle links, though the exact historical relationship between the two is not settled here.

The text

The discourse in full, all sixteen verses of question and answer. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1“Where do quarrels and disputes come from?
And lamentation and sorrow, and stinginess?
What of conceit and arrogance, and slander too—
tell me please, where do they come from?”

Verse 2“Quarrels and disputes come from what we hold dear,
as do lamentation and sorrow, stinginess,
conceit and arrogance.
Quarrels and disputes are linked to stinginess,
and when disputes have arisen there is slander.”

Verse 3“So where do things held dear in the world spring from?
And the lusts that are loose in the world?
Where spring the hopes and aims
a man has for the next life?”

Verse 4“What we hold dear in the world spring from desire,
as do the lusts that are loose in the world.
From there spring the hopes and aims
a man has for the next life.”

Verse 5“So where does desire in the world spring from?
And evaluations, too, where do they come from?
And anger, lies, and indecision,
and other things spoken of by the Ascetic?”

Verse 6“What they call pleasure and pain in the world—
based on that, desire comes about.
Seeing the manifestation and vanishing of forms,
a personage makes evaluations in the world.

Verse 7Anger, lies, and indecision—
these things are, too, when that pair is present.
One who is indecisive should train in the path of knowledge;
it is from knowledge that the Ascetic speaks of these things.”

Verse 8“Where do pleasure and pain spring from?
When what is absent do these things not occur?
And also, on the topic of manifesting and vanishing—
tell me where they spring from.”

Verse 9“Pleasure and pain spring from contact;
when contact is absent they do not occur.
And on the topic of manifesting and vanishing—
I tell you they spring from there.”

Verse 10“So where does contact in the world spring from?
And possessions, too, where do they come from?
When what is absent is there no possessiveness?
When what vanishes do contacts not strike?”

Verse 11“Name and form cause contact;
possessions spring from wishing;
when wishing is absent there is no possessiveness;
when form vanishes, contacts don’t strike.”

Verse 12“Form vanishes for one proceeding how?
And how do happiness and suffering vanish?
Tell me how they vanish;
I think we ought to know these things.”

Verse 13“Without normal perception or distorted perception;
not lacking perception, nor perceiving what has vanished.
Form vanishes for one proceeding thus;
for judgments due to proliferation spring from perception.”

Verse 14“Whatever I asked you have explained to me.
I ask you once more, please tell me this:
Do some astute folk here say that this is the highest extent
of purity of the spirit?
Or do they say it is something else?”

Verse 15“Some astute folk do say that this is the highest extent
of purity of the spirit.
But some of them, claiming to be experts,
speak of an occasion when there is no residue.

Verse 16Knowing that these states are dependent,
and knowing what they depend on, the inquiring sage,
having understood, is freed, and enters no dispute.
The attentive do not proceed to life after life.”

Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 2.2

Putrefaction

Āmagandha Sutta

Setting
No narrative frame in the verses; the discourse opens directly on a dialogue in progress
Speakers
An unnamed questioner and a teacher repeatedly addressed as “Kassapa”; the closing lines refer to the teacher simply as “the Buddha” and “the sage free of putrefaction”
Form
Fourteen verses of dialogue — a challenge about diet, met with a redefinition of “putrefaction” as unethical conduct, closing in narration
Length
~3 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a single sustained argument, made vivid through a long, escalating list

Why this discourse

The sutta opens on what looks like an accusation: someone eating “delicious food, nicely cooked and prepared… a dish of fine rice” is told, bluntly, “you eat putrefaction.” The word is āmagandha — raw stench, the smell of rotting flesh — and the accusation trades on an old ascetic idea that eating meat, or rich food generally, is a kind of moral filth. What makes the discourse worth reading is the reversal that follows. Asked directly what he actually means by “putrefaction,” the teacher answers with a long, unflinching list that has nothing to do with diet at all — killing, cruelty, theft, deceit, adultery — and closes by insisting that no amount of fasting, nudity, matted hair, or ritual purity will ever substitute for it.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

“Putrefaction” — moral stench, the thing that actually contaminates a life — has nothing to do with what a person eats, and everything to do with how they treat other beings.

An accusation about food

The dialogue opens mid-argument. Someone praises the simple diet of “the good” — millet, wild grains, broomcorn, greens, tubers, and squashes, “properly obtained,” without lying to get it — and then contrasts that simplicity with the teacher’s own meal: “delicious food, nicely cooked and prepared, and offered by others… a dish of fine rice.” The charge lands directly: “Kassapa, you eat putrefaction.” The follow-up makes the accusation sharper still — the rice, it turns out, was “well prepared with the flesh of fowl,” despite Kassapa having apparently once said “putrefaction is not appropriate for me.” The questioner presses: so what, exactly, do you take putrefaction to be?

The answer: a long list with nothing to do with meat

Kassapa’s answer is immediate and unambiguous: “killing creatures, mutilation, murder, abduction; stealing, lying, cheating and fraud, learning crooked spells, adultery: this is putrefaction, not eating meat.” That closing line — “this is putrefaction, not eating meat” — becomes a refrain, repeated at the end of six consecutive verses, each adding to the catalogue: unbridled sensuality and nihilism; harsh betrayal of friends and stinginess; anger, jealousy, and boastfulness; debt-evasion and business fraud; cruelty and indifference to others’ suffering; and finally, in the most severe verse, greed and hostility so entrenched that “those beings pass into darkness, falling headlong into hell.” The list moves, deliberately, from the mundane (lying, cheating) to the psychologically corrosive (jealousy, arrogance) to the catastrophic (cruelty that leads to a bad rebirth) — and not one item on it involves what anyone had for dinner.

Not ritual purity either

Having dismissed diet as the measure of purity, the sutta then closes off every other conventional shortcut in a single dense verse: “not fish or flesh or fasting, nudity or baldness, or dreadlocks or dirt, not rough hides or serving the sacred flame, or the many endless mortifications in the world, not hymns or oblations, sacrifices or seasonal observances, will cleanse a mortal not free of doubt.” This is a wide net — it catches the ascetic’s self-mortification and the brahmin’s ritual sacrifice in the same breath, treating them as two versions of the same mistake: substituting an external practice for the internal work of removing doubt and unwholesome intention.

What actually cleanses

The positive teaching, when it finally arrives, is comparatively brief: guarding the “streams of sense impressions,” wandering with the faculties conquered, standing on the teaching, delighting in sincerity and tenderness. The wise, the verse says, “have slipped their chains and given up all suffering; they don’t cling to the seen and the heard.” After the length and specificity of the list of what putrefaction actually is, this final description of the alternative is almost understated — as if the sutta trusts that, once the false target (meat-eating) has been thoroughly cleared away, the real target barely needs elaboration.

A conversion

The closing verses shift into narration: the Buddha “explained this matter to him again and again, until the master of hymns understood it,” the teaching “illustrated with colorful verses by the sage free of putrefaction, unattached, hard to trace.” The questioner — described only as “the master of hymns,” suggesting a brahmin steeped in ritual learning — responds not with further argument but with humility: he bows and “right away begs to go forth,” asking to be ordained. The sutta ends, in other words, exactly where its logic points: not with the questioner conceding a debating point, but with him abandoning the framework — diet, ritual, external purity — that generated the original accusation in the first place.

Key terms

āmagandha — literally “raw stench” or “putrefaction,” the meat-smell some ascetics treated as a marker of moral impurity; the sutta’s title and its central, contested term.
“this is putrefaction, not eating meat” — the refrain closing six consecutive verses, each time following a fresh list of unethical conduct.
Kassapa — the name by which the teacher is addressed throughout the verses; the closing narration calls him simply “the Buddha” and “the sage.”
“master of hymns” — the questioner, identified only by this epithet, suggesting a brahmin versed in ritual chant who ultimately asks to go forth.

The text

All fourteen verses, complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

An accusation about food

Verse 1“The good eat properly obtained
millet, wild grains, broomcorn,
greens, tubers, and squashes.
They don’t lie to get what they want.

Verse 2But when you eat delicious food,
nicely cooked and prepared, and offered by others,
enjoying a dish of fine rice,
Kassapa, you eat putrefaction.

Verse 3‘Putrefaction is not appropriate for me’—
so you said, O brahmin only by kin.
Yet here you are enjoying a dish of fine rice,
well prepared with the flesh of fowl.
I’m asking you this, Kassapa:
what do you take to be putrefaction?”

The answer: a list with nothing to do with meat

Verse 4“Killing creatures, mutilation, murder, abduction;
stealing, lying, cheating and fraud,
learning crooked spells, adultery:
this is putrefaction, not eating meat.

Verse 5People here with unbridled sensuality,
greedy for tastes, mixed up in impurity,
nihilists, immoral, intractable:
this is putrefaction, not eating meat.

Verse 6Tough, callous calumniators,
pitiless and arrogant betrayers of friends,
misers who never give anything:
this is putrefaction, not eating meat.

Verse 7Anger, vanity, obstinacy, contrariness,
deceit, jealousy, boastfulness,
haughtiness, intimacy with the wicked:
this is putrefaction, not eating meat.

Verse 8The ill-behaved, debt-evaders, informants,
business cheats and con-artists,
vile men committing sin:
this is putrefaction, not eating meat.

Verse 9People here who can’t stop harming living creatures,
taking from others, intent on hurting,
immoral, cruel, harsh, lacking regard for others:
this is putrefaction, not eating meat.

Verse 10Greedy, hostile, aggressive to others,
and addicted to evil—those beings pass into darkness,
falling headlong into hell:
this is putrefaction, not eating meat.

Not ritual purity either

Verse 11Not fish or flesh or fasting,
nudity or baldness, or dreadlocks or dirt,
not rough hides or serving the sacred flame,
or the many endless mortifications in the world,
not hymns or oblations, sacrifices or seasonal observances,
will cleanse a mortal not free of doubt.

What actually cleanses

Verse 12Guarding the streams of sense impressions, wander with faculties conquered,
standing on the teaching, delighting in sincerity and tenderness.
The wise have slipped their chains and given up all suffering;
they don’t cling to the seen and the heard.”

A conversion

Verse 13The Buddha explained this matter to him again and again,
until the master of hymns understood it.
It was illustrated with colorful verses
by the sage free of putrefaction, unattached, hard to trace.

Verse 14Having heard the fine words of the Buddha,
that are free of putrefaction, casting aside all suffering;
humbled, he bowed to the Realized One,
and right away begged to go forth.

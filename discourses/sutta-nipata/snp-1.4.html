Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 1.4

With Bhāradvāja the Farmer

Kasibhāradvājasutta

Setting
Near the brahmin village of Ekanāḷa in the Southern Hills of Magadha, on a working farm, at plowing and sowing season
Speakers
The Buddha and the brahmin Bhāradvāja the Farmer, who is harnessing roughly five hundred plows for the season
Form
Prose frame with verse teaching — a prose narrative opens and closes the discourse; the central exchange, including the Buddha’s description of his own “farming,” is in verse
Length
~6 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a clear extended metaphor, plus a vivid closing image of milk-rice hissing in water

Why this discourse, the fourth

A brahmin named Bhāradvāja, mid-harvest with five hundred plows in the field, sees the Buddha standing for alms and delivers the obvious challenge: I plow and sow, and then I eat — you should do the same before you eat. It is a fair question, asked by a man who can see for himself that the person in front of him owns no yoke, no plow, no oxen. The Buddha’s answer does not dispute the premise that food should be earned; it reframes what earning looks like, turning faith into seed, wisdom into a yoke, mindfulness into a plowshare and goad, and describing a harvest — “freedom from death” — that no literal field produces. The farmer’s response to being persuaded is immediate and physical: he asks to be ordained on the spot, and is.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A brahmin farmer challenges the Buddha to plow and sow before eating, exactly as any real worker must, and the Buddha answers by describing the entire spiritual path as its own form of farming — faith the seed, wisdom the yoke and plough, mindfulness the plowshare and goad, energy the draft animal, and freedom from death the harvest — a description that persuades Bhāradvāja to seek ordination immediately.

A direct challenge, not a hostile one

The frame is concrete and unusually specific for this collection: the Buddha is staying near the brahmin village of Ekanāḷa, in the Southern Hills of Magadha, and Bhāradvāja the Farmer has “harnessed around five hundred plows,” since it is sowing season. He is in the middle of distributing food to his workers when he notices the Buddha standing silently for alms. His challenge is not an insult; it states a genuinely reasonable expectation of his time and place: “I plough and sow, ascetic, and then I eat. You too should plough and sow, then you may eat.” When the Buddha replies that he too plows and sows, Bhāradvāja does the obvious thing — he looks for the equipment. “I don’t see the worthy Gotama with a yoke or plow or plowshare or goad or oxen, yet he says: ‘I too plough and sow.’”

The farming verses

Asked to explain, the Buddha answers entirely in verse, mapping each element of Bhāradvāja’s literal equipment onto a quality of practice: “faith is my seed, fervor my rain, and wisdom is my yoke and plough. Conscience is my draft-pole, mind my strap, mindfulness my plowshare and goad.” The mapping continues into conduct — “guarded in body and speech, I restrict my intake of food” — and into two further tools, “truth as my scythe” for reaping and “gentleness” as the release at the end of the labor. Energy itself becomes the draft animal, “transporting me to sanctuary from the yoke,” moving forward without turning back, toward a place “where there is no sorrow.” The verses close by naming the harvest directly: “that’s how to do the farming that has freedom from death as its fruit. When you finish this farming you’re released from all suffering.”

A gift refused, then accepted differently

Persuaded, Bhāradvāja pours milk-rice into a bronze cup and offers it, declaring the Buddha a genuine farmer who deserves the food. The Buddha refuses this particular gift on a specific principle: “food enchanted by a verse isn’t fit for me to eat… the Buddhas cast aside things enchanted with verses.” The distinction matters — it is not the food itself that is refused, but food offered as if payment for, or in response to, the verses just spoken. He directs Bhāradvāja to offer such things through ordinary giving instead, describing himself, without ceremony, as “the field for the seeker of merit” — extending the discourse’s central metaphor one step further, from the giver’s side of the exchange.

An image that answers a question

Told to dispose of the rejected milk-rice where nothing grows or into water with no living creatures, Bhāradvāja drops it into water — and it “sizzled and hissed, steaming and fuming,” compared directly to an iron cauldron heated all day and then plunged into water. The image is startling precisely because it is unexplained: no cause is stated, no miracle claimed outright, only the visible fact reported and left for Bhāradvāja — and the reader — to register. It is this, more than the verses themselves, that leaves him “shocked and awestruck.”

Ordination, the same day

Bhāradvāja’s response uses the same formula of genuine, immediate understanding found elsewhere in this collection — “as if he were righting the overturned, or revealing the hidden…” — but goes further than most: rather than simply going for refuge as a layperson, he asks for ordination on the spot and receives it. The discourse’s closing lines report, without elaboration, that he soon after “realized the supreme end of the spiritual path in this very life” and became one of the perfected — a farmer whose harvest, in the end, matched the metaphor exactly.

Key terms

Ekanāḷa — the brahmin village in the Southern Hills of Magadha near which this exchange takes place, during the working season for sowing.
the farming verses — the Buddha’s extended mapping of literal farming equipment onto qualities of practice: faith as seed, wisdom as yoke and plough, mindfulness as plowshare and goad, energy as draft animal, freedom from death as harvest.
“food enchanted by a verse” — the specific category of offering the Buddha declines, on the principle that food should not be given as if in payment for teaching just delivered.
“the field for the seeker of merit” — how the Buddha describes himself to Bhāradvāja once redirecting the gift, extending the discourse’s central farming metaphor to the act of giving itself.

The text

The complete Kasibhāradvājasutta — prose narrative and verse teaching together, exactly as they appear in sequence. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A farmer’s challenge

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the land of the Magadhans in the Southern Hills near the brahmin village of Ekanāḷa. Now at that time the brahmin Bhāradvāja the Farmer had harnessed around five hundred plows, it being the season for sowing. Then the Buddha robed up in the morning and, taking his bowl and robe, went to where Bhāradvāja the Farmer was working. Now at that time Bhāradvāja the Farmer was distributing food. Then the Buddha went to where the distribution was taking place and stood to one side.

§2–3Bhāradvāja the Farmer saw him standing for alms and said to him, “I plough and sow, ascetic, and then I eat. You too should plough and sow, then you may eat.”

“I too plough and sow, brahmin, and then I eat.” “I don’t see the worthy Gotama with a yoke or plow or plowshare or goad or oxen, yet he says: ‘I too plough and sow, brahmin, and then I eat.’”

§4–5Then Bhāradvāja the Farmer addressed the Buddha in verse:

“You claim to be a farmer,
but I don’t see you farming.
Tell me your farming when asked,
so I can recognize your farming.”

The farming verses

§6–9“Faith is my seed, fervor my rain,
and wisdom is my yoke and plough.
Conscience is my draft-pole, mind my strap,
mindfulness my plowshare and goad.

Guarded in body and speech,
I restrict my intake of food.
I use truth as my scythe,
and gentleness is my release.

Energy is my harnessed beast,
transporting me to sanctuary from the yoke.
It goes without turning back
where there is no sorrow.

That’s how to do the farming
that has freedom from death as its fruit.
When you finish this farming
you’re released from all suffering.”

A gift offered, and redirected

§10Then Bhāradvāja the Farmer poured milk-rice in a large bronze cup and presented it to the Buddha: “May the worthy Gotama eat the milk-rice! The worthy one is a farmer. For the worthy Gotama does the farming that has freedom from death as its fruit.”

§11–12“Food enchanted by a verse isn’t fit for me to eat. That’s not the principle of those who see, brahmin. The Buddhas cast aside things enchanted with verses. Since there is such a principle, brahmin, that’s how they live.

Serve with other food and drink the consummate one, the great seer, with defilements ended and remorse stilled. For he is the field for the seeker of merit.”

§13“Then, worthy Gotama, to whom should I give the milk-rice?” “Brahmin, I don’t see anyone in this world—with its gods, Māras, and Divinities, this population with its ascetics and brahmins, its gods and humans—who can properly digest this milk-rice, except for the Realized One or one of his disciples. Well then, brahmin, throw out the milk-rice where there is little that grows, or drop it into water that has no living creatures.”

The milk-rice hisses

§14So Bhāradvāja the Farmer dropped the milk-rice in water that had no living creatures. And when the milk-rice was placed in the water, it sizzled and hissed, steaming and fuming. Suppose there was an iron cauldron that had been heated all day. If you placed it in the water, it would sizzle and hiss, steaming and fuming. In the same way, when the milk-rice was placed in the water, it sizzled and hissed, steaming and fuming.

Ordination, the same day

§15Then Bhāradvāja the Farmer, shocked and awestruck, went up to the Buddha, bowed down with his head at the Buddha’s feet, and said, “Excellent, worthy Gotama! Excellent! As if he were righting the overturned, or revealing the hidden, or pointing out the path to the lost, or lighting a lamp in the dark so people with clear eyes can see what’s there, worthy Gotama has made the teaching clear in many ways. I go for refuge to the worthy Gotama, to the teaching, and to the mendicant Saṅgha. May I receive the going forth, the ordination in the worthy Gotama’s presence?”

§16And Bhāradvāja the Farmer received the going forth, the ordination in the Buddha’s presence. Not long after his ordination, Venerable Bhāradvāja, living alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute, soon realized the supreme end of the spiritual path in this very life. He lived having achieved with his own insight the goal for which gentlemen rightly go forth from the lay life to homelessness. He understood: “Rebirth is ended; the spiritual journey has been completed; what had to be done has been done; there is nothing further for this place.” And Venerable Bhāradvāja became one of the perfected.

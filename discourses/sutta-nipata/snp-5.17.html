Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 5.17

The Questions of Piṅgiya

Piṅgiyamānavapucchā

Setting
The Pāsāṇaka Shrine in Magadha, within Bāvarī’s frame narrative (see Snp 5.1)
Questioner
Piṅgiya, the sixteenth and last of Bāvarī’s student-brahmins
Form
Four verses in two matched exchanges, both urgent requests from an elderly questioner
Length
~1–2 minutes to read
Position
Sixteenth and last of the individual questions in the Pārāyanavagga
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — direct and personal, closing the sequence of sixteen questions

Why this discourse

Piṅgiya is the last of the sixteen student-brahmins to question the Buddha, and he is also, like Bāvarī himself, old — “old, feeble, and pallid,” his eyes unclear and his hearing faint. His request has none of the leisure some of the other questions carry: he asks the Buddha directly not to let him “perish while still confused.” His satisfaction with the Buddha’s answer is deep enough that his own devotional verses go on to fill the next two discourses, closing the entire Pārāyanavagga.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Seeing how people are harmed and tormented through attachment first to form and then to craving itself, Piṅgiya is told, twice, in nearly matching language: give up what is causing the harm, and being diligent about it, so as not to be reborn.

An old man’s urgency

Piṅgiya opens without any of the formal epithets or elaborate comparisons found in some of the other fifteen questions: “I am old, feeble, and pallid, my eyes unclear, my hearing faint — don’t let me perish while still confused.” It is worth remembering that Piṅgiya is Bāvarī’s own contemporary in age, not a young student sent on his teacher’s behalf while Bāvarī himself stays comfortably at a distance; both men are old, and both are racing against the same clock. His request is exactly what that urgency would produce: not curiosity about doctrine in the abstract, but “explain the teaching so that I may understand the giving up of rebirth and old age here” — now, in what time remains.

First exchange: give up form

The Buddha’s first answer is specific in a way that feels almost pointed given Piṅgiya’s failing eyesight: “seeing them being harmed on account of forms, those negligent folk are inflicted on account of forms — therefore, Piṅgiya, being diligent, give up form so as not to be reborn.” Whether or not the choice of “form” (rūpa) as the specific target is meant to resonate with an old man whose own eyes are failing, the answer names something concrete: attachment to what is seen — and, by extension, to the whole sensory and visible world — as a specific, nameable source of harm to be released, not merely endured.

Second exchange: give up craving

Piṅgiya then makes an unusual claim about his own life: in all ten directions — the four quarters, the intermediate points, above and below — “there’s nothing at all in the world that you’ve not seen, heard, thought, or cognized.” This is not boastful so much as a statement of completion: he has, in effect, already lived a full worldly life of experience, and repeats his same urgent request. The Buddha’s second answer widens the target from form specifically to craving in general: “observing mankind sunk in craving, tormented, mired in old age; therefore, Piṅgiya, being diligent, give up craving so as not to be reborn.”

A satisfied student, and what follows

Unlike the other fifteen questions, which each stand as self-contained discourses, Piṅgiya’s response to this teaching is significant enough that it is not recorded here at all — it becomes the subject of the two discourses that close the entire Pārāyanavagga. In Snp 5.18, a narrator’s voice reports that all sixteen questioners were satisfied; in Snp 5.19, Piṅgiya himself, reciting what he has understood for his teacher Bāvarī, becomes the final individual voice of the entire Sutta Nipāta. His question here, urgent and personal, opens directly onto the collection’s close.

Key terms

rūpa — “form,” the specific target of the first answer given to Piṅgiya; attachment to the seen and visible, named as a source of harm to release.
the ten directions — the four quarters, the four intermediate points, above and below; Piṅgiya uses this complete list to claim he has already seen, heard, thought, and cognized everything the world has to offer.
Bāvarī’s contemporary — unlike most of the other fifteen questioners, Piṅgiya shares Bāvarī’s own advanced age, which the reading guide connects to the discourse’s particular urgency.
the last of sixteen — Piṅgiya is the final individual questioner; his satisfaction with this teaching carries over directly into the two closing discourses of the whole vagga.

The text

The discourse in full — four verses across two matched exchanges. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1“I am old, feeble, and pallid,”
said Venerable Piṅgiya,
“my eyes unclear, my hearing faint—
don’t let me perish while still confused.
Explain the teaching so that I may understand
the giving up of rebirth and old age here.”

§2“Seeing them being harmed
on account of forms,”
replied the Buddha,
“those negligent folk are inflicted
on account of forms—
therefore, Piṅgiya, being diligent,
give up form so as not to be reborn.”

§3“The four quarters, the intermediate directions,
below, and above: in these ten directions
there’s nothing at all in the world
that you’ve not seen, heard, thought, or cognized.
Explain the teaching so that I may understand
the giving up of rebirth and old age here.”

§4“Observing mankind sunk in craving,”
replied the Buddha,
“tormented, mired in old age;
therefore, Piṅgiya, being diligent,
give up craving so as not to be reborn.”

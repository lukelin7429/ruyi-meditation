Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 2.9

What Morality?

Kiṃsīlasutta

Setting
No narrative frame is given — a freestanding question-and-answer poem, without a “So I have heard” opening
Speakers
An unnamed questioner and an unnamed respondent, traditionally reckoned as a student and the Buddha
Form
A single question in verse, answered across six unbroken verses
Length
~2 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — concrete and practical, though the list of qualities in verse 5 rewards careful reading

Why this discourse, the ninth

What Morality? asks the most basic question a serious student can ask — what conduct actually lays a right foundation? — and answers it not with abstraction but with the texture of an ordinary relationship to teacher and teaching: showing up at the right time, listening without obstinacy, giving up twelve small habitual faults, and finally naming stillness itself as the thing that makes any of this learning take root.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A question-and-answer poem asking what ethical conduct and practice “lays the foundations right” for reaching the highest goal, answered with a cluster of qualities centered on how a student treats teachers and the teaching itself — respect without envy, careful listening, giving up vanity and dishonesty, and grounding both speech and understanding in stillness.

The question

An unnamed questioner opens: “With what morality, what conduct, fostering what deeds, would a person lay the foundations right, and reach the highest goal?” The question is deliberately broad — not “what should I believe” but what conduct and habitual activity actually builds a stable foundation for the highest attainment. The reply that follows runs for six further verses without a break, itself building steadily from external etiquette toward internal stillness.

How to approach a teacher

The answer begins exactly where The Boat, the previous discourse in this chapter, left off: with the student’s posture toward teachers. “Honoring elders without jealousy, they’d know the time to visit their teachers. Treasuring the chance for a Dhamma talk, they’d listen carefully to the well-spoken words.” Approaching “at the right time,” “in placid manner, leaving obstinacy behind,” the student is then expected to actually do something with what is heard: “call to mind and put into practice the meaning, the teaching, self-control, and the spiritual life.” Listening, in other words, is only the entry point.

Loyalty to the teaching itself

Verse 4 turns from the teacher to the teaching: “delighting in the teaching, enjoying the teaching, standing on the teaching, evaluating the teaching, they’d never say anything that degraded the teaching, but would be guided by genuine words well-spoken.” The fourfold repetition of “the teaching” is not padding — it is the verse enacting the very absorption it describes, the same word returned to again and again the way a devoted student keeps returning to it.

What to give up

Verse 5 is the poem’s most concrete stretch, a list of twelve things to abandon in quick succession: “mirth, muttered prayer, weeping, ill will, deceit, fawning, greed, conceit, aggression, crudeness, stains, and indulgence.” Giving these up, the student would “wander free of vanity, steadfast.” It is a notably unglamorous list — not cosmic vices but the small, habitual behaviors (muttering, fawning, crude speech) that erode a serious student’s credibility and stability day to day.

Stillness as the essence

The poem’s closing movement names its own hierarchy of value directly: “Understanding is the essence of well-spoken words, stillness is the essence of learning and understanding. Wisdom and learning do not flourish in a hasty and negligent person.” Learning matters, understanding matters, but both are said to have a further essence — a settled stillness — without which they cannot actually take root. The poem’s last verse then folds speech, mind, and deed together: those “happy with the teaching proclaimed by the Noble One are supreme in speech, mind, and deed,” and “abiding in peace, gentleness, and stillness, they’ve realized the essence of learning and wisdom.”

Key terms

“lay the foundations right” — the opening question’s framing: not a request for doctrine but for the conduct and practice that establishes a stable base for reaching the highest goal.
the fourfold return to “the teaching” — verse 4’s repeated phrase (“delighting in the teaching, enjoying the teaching, standing on the teaching, evaluating the teaching”), a verbal enactment of sustained devotion to what is being described.
the twelve things given up — verse 5’s list of ordinary, habitual faults (mirth, muttering, weeping, ill will, deceit, fawning, greed, conceit, aggression, crudeness, stains, indulgence), notably concrete rather than abstract.
“stillness is the essence” — the poem’s closing claim that learning and understanding themselves have a further essence, without which wisdom “does not flourish in a hasty and negligent person.”

The text

The discourse in full — a single question and its unbroken seven-verse answer. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The question

Verse 1“With what morality, what conduct,
fostering what deeds,
would a person lay the foundations right,
and reach the highest goal?”

How to approach a teacher

Verse 2“Honoring elders without jealousy,
they’d know the time to visit their teachers.
Treasuring the chance for a Dhamma talk,
they’d listen carefully to the well-spoken words.

Verse 3At the right time, they’d enter the teacher’s presence
in placid manner, leaving obstinacy behind.
They’d call to mind and put into practice
the meaning, the teaching, self-control, and the spiritual life.

Loyalty to the teaching itself

Verse 4Delighting in the teaching, enjoying the teaching,
standing on the teaching, evaluating the teaching,
they’d never say anything that degraded the teaching,
but would be guided by genuine words well-spoken.

What to give up

Verse 5Giving up mirth, muttered prayer, weeping, ill will,
deceit, fawning, greed, conceit,
aggression, crudeness, stains, and indulgence,
they’d wander free of vanity, steadfast.

Stillness as the essence

Verse 6Understanding is the essence of well-spoken words,
stillness is the essence of learning and understanding.
Wisdom and learning do not flourish
in a hasty and negligent person.

Verse 7Those happy with the teaching proclaimed by the Noble One
are supreme in speech, mind, and deed.
Abiding in peace, gentleness, and stillness,
they’ve realized the essence of learning and wisdom.”

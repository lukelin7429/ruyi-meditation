Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 5.9

The Questions of Hemaka

Hemakamāṇavapucchā

Setting
Continuing directly from Snp 5.1–5.8 — the same gathering at the Pāsāṇaka Shrine near Rājagaha, the eighth student now stepping forward
Speakers
The Buddha and the student Hemaka, eighth of Bāvarī's sixteen students named in Snp 5.1
Form
Four verses, the shortest exchange in this set of nine, opening with a pointed complaint about earlier teachers
Length
~1 minute to read
Frame
Part of one continuous work: see Snp 5.1 for the full story of Bāvarī and his sixteen students
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — short and direct, with one of the more quotable single-sentence definitions of liberation in this chapter

Why this discourse, the eighth question

Hemaka, eighth-named among Bāvarī's sixteen students, opens with something unusual in this chapter: a direct complaint about the limits of every teacher he consulted before meeting the Buddha. Earlier teachers, he says, only ever explained “thus it was” or “so it shall be” — causal accounts, hearsay, speculation — and none of it brought him any real delight. What he wants instead is “the teaching that destroys craving.” The Buddha's answer is compact and precise: the removal of desire and lust for anything pleasant that is seen, heard, thought, or cognized is extinguishment, “the state that does not pass” — and those who understand this, mindful, are quenched in this very life.

Reading guide

The discourse in one sentence

Hemaka, the eighth of Bāvarī's sixteen students, complains that every teacher he consulted before the Buddha only ever offered causal accounts of “how things were,” never the actual removal of craving; the Buddha answers directly, defining extinguishment as the removal of desire and lust for anything pleasant seen, heard, thought, or cognized, achieved here and now rather than in some future state.

The frame: the eighth of sixteen questions

As told in full in Snp 5.1, Bāvarī, an aged brahmin teacher unable to travel himself, sent sixteen of his finest students — Ajita, Tissametteyya, Puṇṇaka, Mettagū, Dhotaka, Upasīva, Nanda, Hemaka, Todeyya, Kappa, Jatukaṇṇī, Bhadrāvudha, Udaya, Posāla, Mogharāja, and Piṅgiya — to question the newly awakened Buddha directly. Seven students have already spoken, in Snp 5.2 through 5.8; Hemaka, eighth in Bāvarī's list and marking the halfway point of the sixteen, now speaks — and does so with a noticeably more autobiographical opening than most of the students around him.

“I found no delight in that”: a complaint about earlier teachers

Hemaka begins not with a fresh question but with a verdict on his past experience: “those who have previously answered me, before Gotama's teaching, said ‘thus it was’ or ‘so it shall be.’” All of that, he says, was merely “the testament of hearsay,” and all of it “just fostered speculation” — and he found no delight in any of it. It is a rare moment of a student explicitly contrasting the Buddha with everyone else he has consulted, framing his question as coming from real prior disappointment rather than neutral curiosity.

“The teaching that destroys craving”

What Hemaka actually wants is specific: not another causal account of the world, but “the teaching that destroys craving,” so that, having understood it and living mindfully, he “may cross over clinging in the world.” The request itself is a small piece of doctrinal precision worth noticing: Hemaka is not asking for metaphysics — how things came to be, or how they will turn out — but for a practical teaching aimed squarely at craving's removal.

Extinguishment, defined in a single sentence

The Buddha's answer is among the more compact definitions of liberation anywhere in this chapter: “The removal of desire and lust, Hemaka, for pleasant things seen, heard, thought, or cognized here is extinguishment, the state that does not pass.” Four channels of experience are named exhaustively — seen, heard, thought, cognized — covering, between them, everything a person could possibly be drawn toward. Extinguishment is not located outside these channels of experience, but in the absence of desire and lust running through them.

Quenched in this very life

The closing verse makes explicit what several other discourses in this chapter also stress: those who have “fully understood this, mindful, are quenched in this very life,” not in some future existence. “Always at peace, they've crossed over clinging to the world.” Set against Hemaka's opening complaint about teachers who only spoke of “how it was” or “how it shall be,” the Buddha's answer pointedly locates the whole matter in the present tense throughout.

Key terms

Hemaka — eighth-named of Bāvarī's sixteen students in Snp 5.1, whose opening complaint about earlier teachers gives this discourse an unusually personal frame.
“thus it was” / “so it shall be” — Hemaka's description of what his previous teachers offered: causal or predictive accounts, dismissed here as “hearsay” and “speculation.”
seen, heard, thought, cognized — the four channels of experience named exhaustively in the Buddha's definition of extinguishment, covering everything a person could be drawn toward.
“quenched in this very life” — the discourse's closing claim that liberation is achieved here and now, not deferred to a future or predicted state.
context · the sixteen students — Hemaka is the eighth of sixteen brahmin students Bāvarī sends to question the Buddha (see Snp 5.1); each of Snp 5.2 through 5.17 belongs to one of them in turn.

The text

All four verses of Hemaka's exchange with the Buddha, given in full. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1084“Those who have previously answered me,”
said Venerable Hemaka,
“before Gotama’s teaching,
said ‘thus it was’ or ‘so it shall be’.
All that was just the testament of hearsay;
all that just fostered speculation:
I found no delight in that.

Verse 1085But you, sage, explain to me
the teaching that destroys craving.
Having understood it, one who lives mindfully
may cross over clinging in the world.”

Verse 1086“The removal of desire and lust, Hemaka,
for pleasant things
seen, heard, thought, or cognized here
is extinguishment, the state that does not pass.

Verse 1087Those who have fully understood this, mindful,
are quenched in this very life.
Always at peace,
they’ve crossed over clinging to the world.”

Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 4.14

Speedy

Tuvaṭakasutta

Setting
Not stated in the verse itself; no location or occasion is named
Speakers
An unnamed questioner, addressing the Buddha as “Kinsman of the Sun,” and the Buddha
Form
Continuous philosophical verse, minimal narrative frame — two questions, then an unusually long and concrete practical answer
Length
~5 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A version of much of the Aṭṭhakavagga survives in the Chinese Yizu Jing (義足經, T198), translated in the 3rd century CE, though exact correspondence to this individual sutta is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the most concrete and checklist-like discourse in this set, easier to follow than the more abstract dialogues around it

Why this discourse, the fourteenth

Every discourse examined so far in this set works mostly by argument or portrait; this one is different. Asked, after a first short answer, to say more — specifically to “speak of the practice… the monastic code and immersion in samādhi” — the Buddha responds with the longest sustained catalogue of concrete instruction in the whole vagga, covering diet, sleep, speech, folk divination, criticism, gossip, and buying and selling. The title, “Speedy” (tuvaṭaka, sometimes rendered “quick”), likely points to the compressed, rapid-fire density of this list rather than to any ease in following it — there is a great deal packed into twenty verses.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Extinguishment begins by cutting off the root idea “I am the thinker,” and from that single cut follows an entire, remarkably concrete way of living — restrained in diet, speech, sleep, and reaction to both criticism and praise, and free of the folk arts of fortune-telling many ascetics of the time practiced for a living.

Cutting off the thinker

The questioner, addressing the Buddha as “Kinsman of the Sun” and asking about seclusion and peace, wants to know how a mendicant becomes “extinguished,” grasping nothing in the world. The answer opens at the root: cutting off the idea “I am the thinker,” which the text names as the source of “all judgments that emerge from proliferation.” Whatever such a person comes to know, whether about themselves or the world, they wouldn’t be proud of that knowledge, “for that is not quenching” — knowledge itself, however extensive, is not the goal, and taking pride in it is precisely the kind of grasping the whole practice is meant to undo. Unmoved by comparing themselves better, worse, or equal to others, they would not keep justifying themselves even under repeated questioning.

Still water, no waves

The first half of the discourse closes with one of its most memorable images: “just as, in the middle of the ocean, no waves arise, it stays still; so too one unstirred is still.” A mendicant like this finds peace inside themselves rather than seeking it from another, and for one already at peace within, the discourse asks, “there’s no picking up, whence putting down?” — if nothing was ever grasped, there is nothing left that needs to be released. The questioner, satisfied but wanting more, asks the Buddha to go on and describe the actual practice: the monastic code, and the cultivation of concentration (samādhi).

An unusually concrete list

What follows reads less like poetry than a practical manual. Eyes not wanton, ears turned away from village gossip, no greed for flavors, no possessiveness; unmoved to lament even when struck by painful contact; no hoarding of food, drink, or clothing, and no anxiety about not getting them; meditative rather than restless, avoiding both remorse and negligence; little sleep, but wakeful rather than drowsy; giving up sloth, mirth, play, sex, and self-adornment entirely. Notably, the list also singles out a set of practices many ascetics of the era made a living from — casting spells, interpreting dreams and omens, practicing astrology, reading animal cries as portents, working as a doctor, or treating difficult pregnancies — and rules every one of them out for the Buddha’s own followers, treating divinatory and medical side-trades as incompatible with the mendicant life being described.

Neither shaken nor gloating

The closing stretch turns to speech and reputation directly: not shaken by criticism, not gloating when praised; giving up greed, stinginess, anger, and slander; no participation in buying and selling, no speaking ill of others, no lingering in the village or cajoling people out of desire for profit; no boasting, no suggestive speech, no impudence or argumentativeness; never lying, never being deliberately devious, and never looking down on anyone else for their livelihood, wisdom, or precepts. Even when provoked by rival ascetics arguing “all different things,” such a person would not react harshly, “for the virtuous do not retaliate.” The discourse closes by tying this entire practical portrait back to its root: understanding the teaching, a mendicant trains mindfully, knows quenching as peace, and follows it out of genuine respect for a teacher described as “the vanquisher, the unvanquished, seer of the truth as witness, not by hearsay.”

Key terms

tuvaṭaka — “speedy” or “quick” — the discourse’s title, likely describing the compressed, rapid-fire density of its instructions rather than any ease in following them.
“I am the thinker” — the root idea the discourse names as the source of all judgments arising from proliferation (papañca); cutting it off is described as the starting point of the whole practice.
“that is not quenching” — the discourse’s warning against taking pride in knowledge itself, however extensive — knowledge is not the goal, and pride in it is a form of grasping.
the divinatory arts — spell-casting, dream and omen interpretation, astrology, reading animal cries, and folk medicine — common side-trades among ascetics of the era, explicitly ruled out for the Buddha’s followers in this discourse.
“the vanquisher, the unvanquished” — the discourse’s closing epithet for the Buddha, praised as one who has seen the truth directly “as witness, not by hearsay.”

The text

The discourse in full, all twenty verses. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1“Great seer, I ask you, the Kinsman of the Sun,
about seclusion and the state of peace.
How, having seen, is a mendicant extinguished,
not grasping anything in this world?”

Verse 2“They would cut off the idea, ‘I am the thinker’,”
said the Buddha,
“which is the root of all judgments that emerge from proliferation.
Ever mindful, they would train to remove
any internal cravings.

Verse 3Regardless of what things they know,
whether internal or external,
they wouldn’t be proud because of that,
for that is not quenching, say the good.

Verse 4They wouldn’t let that make them conceited,
thinking themselves better or worse or alike.
When questioned in many ways,
they wouldn’t keep justifying themselves.

Verse 5A mendicant would find peace inside themselves,
and not seek peace from another.
For one at peace inside themselves,
there’s no picking up, whence putting down?

Verse 6Just as, in the middle of the ocean,
no waves arise, it stays still;
so too one unstirred is still—
a mendicant would make no pretension regarding anything.”

Verse 7“He whose eyes are open has explained
the truth he witnessed, where adversities are removed.
Please now speak of the practice, sir,
the monastic code and immersion in samādhi.”

Verse 8“With eyes not wanton,
they’d turn their ears from village gossip.
They wouldn’t be greedy for flavors,
nor possessive about anything in the world.

Verse 9Though struck by contacts,
a mendicant would not lament at all.
They wouldn’t pray for another life,
nor tremble in the face of dangers.

Verse 10When they receive food and drink,
edibles and clothes,
they wouldn’t store them up,
nor worry about not getting them.

Verse 11Meditative, not footloose,
they’d avoid remorse and not be negligent.
Then a mendicant would stay
in quiet places to sit and rest.

Verse 12They wouldn’t take much sleep,
but, being keen, would apply themselves to wakefulness.
They’d give up sloth, illusion, mirth, and play,
and sex and ornamentation.

Verse 13They wouldn’t cast Artharvaṇa spells, interpret dreams
or omens, or practice astrology.
My followers would not study ominous animal cries,
work as a doctor, or treat an impacted fetus.

Verse 14Not shaken by criticism,
a mendicant would not gloat when praised.
They’d cast aside greed and stinginess,
anger, and slander.

Verse 15They’d not stand for buying and selling;
a mendicant would not speak ill at all.
They wouldn’t linger in the village,
nor cajole people from desire for profit.

Verse 16A mendicant would be no boaster,
nor would they speak suggestively.
They wouldn’t train in impudence,
nor speak argumentatively.

Verse 17They wouldn’t be led into lying,
nor be deliberately devious.
And they’d never look down on another
because of livelihood, wisdom, or precepts and vows.

Verse 18Though provoked from hearing much talk
from ascetics saying all different things,
they wouldn’t react harshly,
for the virtuous do not retaliate.

Verse 19Having understood this teaching,
inquiring, a mendicant would always train mindfully.
Knowing quenching as peace,
they’d not be negligent in Gotama’s bidding.

Verse 20For he is the vanquisher, the unvanquished,
seer of the truth as witness, not by hearsay—
that’s why, being diligent, they would always train
respectfully in the Buddha’s teaching.”

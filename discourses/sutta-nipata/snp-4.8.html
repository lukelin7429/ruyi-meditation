Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 4.8

With Pasūra

Pasūrasutta

Verses
Eleven
Speaker
Traditionally the Buddha, addressing the wanderer Pasūra directly by name (“Sūra” in verse 8, “Pasūra” in verse 10) within the verses, though without dialogue tags or a narrative frame
Form
Continuous philosophical verse, addressed as direct speech to a named opponent, but without any narrated setting or reply from Pasūra himself
Length
~4 minutes to read
Northern parallel

Difficulty
★★★★☆ — the vagga's argument against dogmatism applied, concretely, to a single named debater

Why this discourse, the eighth of the Aṭṭhakavagga

The Pasūrasutta closes this reading’s selection from the Aṭṭhakavagga by making the whole chapter’s abstract argument suddenly, vividly personal. Where earlier discourses spoke of “some personage” or “a mendicant” in general terms, this one is addressed to a specific person — the wanderer Pasūra, named twice within the verses — and describes, in almost comic, deflating detail, exactly what it looks like when someone has made winning debates the whole point of having a philosophy. The discourse’s closing move is its sharpest: it doesn’t refute Pasūra’s doctrine. It observes that there is no one left standing on the other side of the argument for him to fight.

Reading guide

The discourse in one sentence

Someone who plunges into debate desiring praise, gets nervous when defending a claim to be uniquely right, and swings between gloating in victory and weeping in defeat, is playing a game that requires an opponent — and against someone who has genuinely given up grasping at any position, that game simply has no one left to play against.

Verse 1: “here alone is purity”

The discourse opens by quoting the exclusive claim its whole argument will dismantle: “here alone is purity,” each debater says, “denying that there is purification in other teachings.” The verse’s closing observation is precise and slightly wry: “each one is dogmatic about their own idiosyncratic interpretation.” The word “idiosyncratic” is doing real work here — it isn’t that these debaters have each discovered a genuinely distinct truth; it is that each has become attached to a personal, particular way of framing things and then insists that framing is the only correct one.

Verses 2–4: the machinery of a debate

What follows is close to a field description of competitive argument as a social activity, stripped of any pretense that truth is what’s actually at stake. Verse 2: “desiring debate, they plunge into an assembly, where each takes the other as a fool,” “desiring praise while claiming to be skilled.” Verse 3 names the emotional cost this exacts even before any result is known: “addicted to debating in the midst of the assembly, their need for praise makes them nervous” — and when “repudiated,” they “get embarrassed,” “upset at criticism,” and respond by finding “fault in others” rather than reconsidering their own position. Verse 4 gives the discourse’s most vivid, almost pitiable image: when a doctrine is judged “repudiated,” “the loser weeps and wails, moaning, ‘They beat me.’” The stakes of the whole enterprise, laid bare, turn out to be exactly this: not truth, but the difference between gloating and weeping.

Verses 5–7: excitement, dejection, and their common root

Verse 5 names the pattern directly: “when these disputes come up among ascetics, they get excited or dejected” — and states the discourse’s diagnosis in one line: “the only purpose is praise and profit.” Verse 6 completes the emotional picture by describing the winner’s side of the same coin: “they laugh and gloat because of it, having got what they wanted” — a mirror image of the weeping loser in verse 4, both reactions produced by the identical mechanism. Verse 7 names what actually produces both outcomes: “their pride is their downfall, yet they speak from conceit and arrogance,” concluding “those who are skilled say this is no way to purity.” The whole activity, win or lose, turns out to move a person further from, not closer to, whatever purity the original claim was supposedly about.

Verses 8–9: no one to fight

Verse 8 turns from general description to direct, almost taunting address, using a simile drawn from the world outside philosophy: “as a warrior, after feasting on royal food, goes roaring, looking for someone to fight — go off and find an opponent, Sūra, for here, as before, there is no-one to combat.” It is a striking image — a well-fed warrior who has worked himself into a battle-ready state before he has even located an enemy, restless energy with nowhere to land. Verse 9 completes the logical move that makes this taunt more than an insult: when someone disputes about their adopted view, insisting “this is the only truth,” the reply is “here you’ll have no adversary when a dispute has come up.” The claim is not that the Buddha’s position would win the debate. It is that there is, in the relevant sense, no position on offer to dispute with at all.

Verses 10–11: nothing grasped as highest

Verse 10 makes the same point from the other direction, describing “those who live far from the crowd, not countering views with view” — and asks Pasūra directly, by name, “who is there to argue with you... among those who grasp nothing here as the highest?” The phrase is the discourse’s key to everything preceding it: a dispute is a contest between positions, and a person holding no position “as the highest” has nothing on the table for a debater to attack, defend against, or defeat. Verse 11 closes with a final, pointed observation about Pasūra’s own method: “you come along speculating, thinking up theories in your mind” — and having “challenged someone who is cleansed,” the discourse predicts, “you’ll not be able to respond.” Not because the cleansed one has a superior counter-theory ready, but because the entire mode of engagement Pasūra has prepared for — theory meeting theory, position meeting position — finds nothing to lock onto.

The argument this vagga has been building toward

Read as the closing discourse of this selection, the Pasūrasutta demonstrates something the previous seven discourses argued for more abstractly: that non-attachment to views is not a philosophical position competing with other philosophical positions on the same field. Pasūra’s entire approach assumes debate is a zero-sum contest between competing claims, where skill, cleverness, and the crowd’s praise decide a winner. The discourse’s response isn’t to enter that contest with a better claim. It is to point out that the contest itself requires two parties standing on ground that can be fought over, and someone who has “shaken off all views,” to borrow Snp 4.3’s phrase, has simply stepped off that ground. This is why the earlier discourses spent so much care establishing that non-attachment doesn’t mean having no discernment, and isn’t itself a competing doctrine to be defended: if it were, Pasūra would have exactly what he came for. The freedom this whole vagga describes is not winning the argument. It is no longer needing the argument to be about winning at all.

Key terms

Pasūra — a wanderer (paribbājaka) known from the broader canon as a skilled, combative debater; addressed directly in this discourse as “Sūra” (verse 8) and “Pasūra” (verse 10).
“idiosyncratic interpretation” — verse 1’s description of each debater’s personal framing of their own doctrine, mistaken by its holder for the one correct account of reality.
“praise and profit” — verse 5’s blunt naming of what actually motivates competitive debate, beneath its surface appeal to truth.
the roaring warrior — verse 8’s image for Pasūra: battle-ready energy that has worked itself up before locating an actual opponent.
“no-one to combat” — the discourse’s central logical move: a dispute requires two positions in contact, and someone who grasps nothing as “the highest” has removed one side of the contest entirely.

The text

All eleven verses of the Pasūrasutta, complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1“Here alone is purity,” they say,
denying that there is purification in other teachings.
Speaking of the beauty
in that which they depend on,
each one is dogmatic about
their own idiosyncratic interpretation.

Verse 2Desiring debate, they plunge into an assembly,
where each takes the other as a fool.
Relying on others they state their contention,
desiring praise while claiming to be skilled.

Verse 3Addicted to debating in the midst of the assembly,
their need for praise makes them nervous.
But when they’re repudiated they get embarrassed;
upset at criticism, they find fault in others.

Verse 4If their doctrine is said to be weak,
and judges declare it repudiated,
the loser weeps and wails,
moaning, “They beat me.”

Verse 5When these disputes come up among ascetics,
they get excited or dejected.
Seeing this, refrain from contention,
for the only purpose is praise and profit.

Verse 6But if, having declared their doctrine,
they are praised there in the midst of the assembly,
they laugh and gloat because of it,
having got what they wanted.

Verse 7Their pride is their downfall,
yet they speak from conceit and arrogance.
Seeing this, one ought not dispute,
for those who are skilled say this is no way to purity.

Verse 8As a warrior, after feasting on royal food,
goes roaring, looking for someone to fight—
go off and find an opponent, Sūra,
for here, as before, there is no-one to combat.

Verse 9When someone disputes about a view they’ve adopted,
saying, “This is the only truth,”
say to them, “Here you’ll have no adversary
when a dispute has come up.”

Verse 10There are those who live far from the crowd,
not countering views with view.
Who is there to argue with you, Pasūra,
among those who grasp nothing here as the highest?

Verse 11And so you come along speculating,
thinking up theories in your mind.
Now that you’ve challenged
someone who is cleansed,
you’ll not be able to respond.

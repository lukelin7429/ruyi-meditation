Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 4.1

Sensual Pleasures

Kāmasutta

Verses
Six, opening the Aṭṭhakavagga (“Chapter of Eights”)
Speaker
Traditionally the Buddha; no interlocutor or setting is given in the verses themselves
Form
Continuous philosophical verse, minimal narrative frame — no dialogue, no named audience, no location
Length
~2 minutes to read
Northern parallel
The Aṭṭhakavagga as a whole has an early Chinese counterpart in the Yizu Jing (義足經, Taishō 198), translated in the third century CE; exact verse-by-verse correspondence for this specific discourse is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — short and vivid, but it opens a chapter that gets progressively harder

Why this discourse, the first of the Aṭṭhakavagga

Before the Aṭṭhakavagga takes up its most demanding subject — the danger of clinging to views and getting drawn into disputes about them — it opens with something more immediately recognizable: the mechanics of ordinary craving. In six spare verses, the Kāmasutta traces how wanting sensual pleasure sets up a person for exactly two outcomes, both of them precarious. Get what you want, and elation quietly primes a fall. Lose it, and the loss “inflicts them like an arrow strike.” Nothing here is abstract or difficult to picture; it is the placement of the whole vagga’s architecture in miniature, since the deeper chapters that follow will apply this exact same diagnosis — wanting, having, losing, suffering — not to sense objects but to opinions, positions, and the desire to be right.

Reading guide

The discourse in one sentence

Sensual craving is a trap with two identical doors — getting what you want breeds a fragile elation that only sets up the next loss, and not getting it (or losing it once had) wounds “like an arrow strike,” so the only stable ground is the mindfulness that steps around the whole mechanism, the way a careful walker steps around a snake’s head.

Verses 1–2: elation and its shadow

The sutta opens by describing satisfaction itself as a kind of setup. A person who wants sensual pleasure and gets it “definitely becomes elated, having got what they want” — that much is simply true, and the verse doesn’t pretend otherwise. But the very next line names the cost built into that elation: “when those pleasures fade, it inflicts them like an arrow strike.” The two verses are a single unit, cause and consequence. Nothing about the pleasure was fake; the problem is structural, not moral — anything that arises will also pass, and the more a person’s wellbeing is staked on having it, the sharper its departure will feel. This is a much narrower and more precise claim than “pleasure is bad.” It is closer to: pleasure that isn’t recognized as temporary quietly manufactures its own future pain.

Verse 3: the snake’s head

The sutta’s central image arrives early and does a lot of work: a mindful person avoids sensual pleasures “like side-stepping a snake’s head.” It is a carefully chosen comparison. Nobody steps around a snake’s head out of moral disapproval of snakes; they do it because they have clearly seen where the danger actually is and adjusted their path accordingly, calmly and without drama. That is the register this whole chapter will keep returning to: not renunciation as punishment or as a display of willpower, but as an ordinary, almost technical response to having accurately seen how something works. “Transcends attachment to the world” is the result of that clear seeing, not a separate, additional achievement.

Verses 4–5: an inventory, and what it costs

Verse 4 lists what people actually organize their lives around wanting — “fields, lands, and gold; cattle and horses; slaves and servants; women and relatives” — a catalogue that reads, notably, as an ancient audience’s version of property, status, and family, the same categories a modern list would still recognize under different names. Verse 5 then states the cost of lusting after this inventory in blunt, physical terms: “the weak overpower him and adversities crush him. Suffering follows him like water in a leaky boat.” The boat image matters. A leaky boat doesn’t sink all at once; it takes on water steadily, in the background, while its passenger may still be congratulating himself on being underway. That is what unexamined craving does to a life — not a single dramatic wreck, but a constant, manageable-looking seepage that eventually decides the outcome anyway.

Verse 6: crossing over

The closing verse supplies the discourse’s one clear instruction — “give them up and cross the flood” — and its closing image directly answers the leaky boat of verse 5: a boat that has been bailed out reaches the far shore. The metaphor doesn’t ask for a different vessel or a different route: the same boat, once the water is removed, does what it was always capable of doing. Nothing in this discourse asks its listener to distrust experience or the world in general — only to stop letting craving quietly flood the vessel that is meant to carry them across.

Why this opens the Aṭṭhakavagga

Read on its own, the Kāmasutta is a compact teaching on ordinary sensual craving, and nothing in it mentions philosophical disputes or dogmatic views at all. But its placement first in this chapter is exactly what lets the following seven discourses make their much harder argument: if wanting a sense object works this way — setting a person up, exhausting itself, needing the very letting-go it seems to threaten — then the same mechanics apply just as exactly to wanting an opinion to prevail. A cherished position that one has become attached to, that one wants others to validate, that one feels wounded when others reject: this is kāma operating in the register of ideas rather than the senses. The Aṭṭhakavagga will spend its next seven discourses insisting that clinging to a view is not fundamentally different from clinging to property or pleasure, and that the same “side-stepping,” the same bailing-out, applies to both.

Key terms

kāma — “sensual pleasure” or, more broadly, desire and wanting itself; the discourse’s title and the mechanism the whole sutta anatomizes.
Aṭṭhakavagga — “Chapter of Eights,” the traditional name for this section of the Sutta Nipāta, taken from the eight-verse length of several (though not all) of its sixteen discourses.
the snake’s head — this discourse’s governing image for mindful avoidance: not fear or aversion, but accurate perception of where danger actually lies, calmly acted on.
the leaky boat — the image for a life organized around unexamined craving: not a single dramatic collapse, but a steady, background accumulation of harm.
“cross the flood” — a recurring canonical image (it will return throughout this chapter) for the passage from a life driven by craving and views to one that is not; here it closes the sutta by answering its own opening image of elation and loss.

The text

All six verses of the Kāmasutta, complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1If a mortal desires sensual pleasure
and their desire succeeds,
they definitely become elated,
having got what they want.

Verse 2But for that personage in the throes of pleasure,
aroused by desire,
when those pleasures fade,
it inflicts them like an arrow strike.

Verse 3One who, being mindful,
avoids sensual pleasures
like side-stepping a snake’s head,
transcends attachment to the world.

Verse 4There are many objects of sensual desire:
fields, lands, and gold; cattle and horses;
slaves and servants; women and relatives.
When a man lusts over these,

Verse 5the weak overpower him
and adversities crush him.
Suffering follows him
like water in a leaky boat.

Verse 6That’s why a personage, ever mindful,
should avoid sensual pleasures.
Give them up and cross the flood,
as a bailed-out boat reaches the far shore.

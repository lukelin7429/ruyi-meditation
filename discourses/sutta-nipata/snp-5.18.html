Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 5.18

Homage to the Way to the Far Shore

Pārāyanānugīti

Setting
The Pāsāṇaka Shrine in Magadha, closing Bāvarī’s frame narrative (see Snp 5.1)
Speaker
An unnamed narrator’s voice, not the Buddha or any of the sixteen students directly
Form
Eight verses: an opening frame, a roll call of all sixteen names, and a closing explanation of the title “Pārāyana”
Length
~2–3 minutes to read
Position
Seventeenth of the Pārāyanavagga’s nineteen discourses; the collection’s own recap of itself
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — straightforward narration, mainly of interest for what it confirms

Why this discourse

After sixteen separate exchanges, each its own self-contained discourse, the Pārāyanavagga pauses to look back at itself. This short recapitulation is spoken by neither the Buddha nor any of the students but by a narrating voice standing outside the sixteen questions entirely — naming every questioner in order, confirming that all of them were satisfied, and explaining, in its own closing lines, exactly why this exposition of the teaching is called “The Way to the Far Shore.”

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

If a listener understands the meaning and the teaching of each of the sixteen questions and practices accordingly, they may go from the near shore to the far shore of old age and death — which is why this whole exposition of the teaching is named “The Way to the Far Shore” (Pārāyana).

A voice from outside the dialogue

Every one of the previous sixteen discourses was built from direct speech — a student’s question, the Buddha’s answer, occasionally a further exchange. This discourse breaks that pattern immediately: “This was said by the Buddha while staying in the land of the Magadhans at the Pāsāṇaka Shrine. When requested by the sixteen brahmin devotees, he answered their questions one by one.” The voice describing this is reporting on the Buddha and the students from outside their conversation, in the third person — almost certainly the voice of whoever compiled and transmitted this material, stepping forward briefly to explain what the reader has just been through.

All sixteen names, in order

The discourse’s middle section is, in effect, a table of contents rendered as verse — a complete roll call of all sixteen questioners in the exact sequence their discourses appear: “Ajita, Tissametteyya, Puṇṇaka and Mettagū, Dhotaka and Upasiva, Nanda and then Hemaka,” followed by “both Todeyya and Kappa, and Jatukaṇṇī the astute, Bhadrāvudha and Udaya, and the brahmin Posāla, Mogharāja the intelligent, and Piṅgiya the great seer.” For a text transmitted for centuries by memorization before being written down, this kind of mnemonic list was not incidental decoration; it was one of the mechanisms by which the material stayed intact and in order across generations of reciters.

A confirmed, satisfying answer

The narrator states plainly what each of the sixteen individual discourses only implied: “The Buddha answered their questions in accordance with truth. The sage satisfied the brahmins with his answers to their questions.” It adds a detail not given anywhere in the sixteen questions themselves — that those who were satisfied “led the spiritual life in his presence,” meaning at least some of Bāvarī’s students went forth and became the Buddha’s own students in turn, rather than simply returning to Bāvarī with a report and continuing as before.

Explaining its own name

The closing verses turn from narration to direct address, extending the same offer to whoever is reading or listening now: “if you practice in accordance with each of these questions as taught by the Buddha, you’ll go from the near shore to the far.” The very last lines supply the etymology directly, closing the loop on the chapter’s title: “this path is for going to the far shore; that’s why it’s called ‘The Way to the Far Shore.’” What began, in Snp 5.1, as an old brahmin’s wager on a rumor is here explicitly reframed as a path any reader can still walk.

Key terms

Pārāyana — “the way to,” or “means of going to,” the far shore; the name of the entire fifth vagga, explained directly in this discourse’s closing lines.
anugīti — “homage” or “recitation after”; the discourse’s title marks it as a recapitulation spoken after the main sixteen exchanges are complete.
the mnemonic roll call — the listing of all sixteen questioners’ names in sequence, a memorization device likely serving practical, not merely decorative, purposes in oral transmission.
near shore / far shore — the recurring image (apāra / pāra) for the ordinary condition of continued rebirth and the condition of liberation beyond it.

The text

The discourse in full — eight verses, the narrator’s complete recapitulation of the sixteen questions. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1This was said by the Buddha while staying in the land of the Magadhans at the Pāsāṇaka Shrine.
When requested by the sixteen brahmin devotees, he answered their questions one by one.
If you understand the meaning and the teaching of each of these questions, and practice in line with the teaching, you may go right to the far shore of old age and death.
These teachings are said to lead to the far shore, which is why the name of this exposition of the teaching is “The Way to the Far Shore”.

§2Ajita, Tissametteyya,
Puṇṇaka and Mettagū,
Dhotaka and Upasiva,
Nanda and then Hemaka,

§3both Todeyya and Kappa,
and Jatukaṇṇī the astute,
Bhadrāvudha and Udaya,
and the brahmin Posāla,
Mogharāja the intelligent,
and Piṅgiya the great seer:

§4they approached the Buddha,
the seer of consummate conduct.
Asking their subtle questions,
they came to the most excellent Buddha.

§5The Buddha answered their questions
in accordance with truth.
The sage satisfied the brahmins
with his answers to their questions.

§6Those who were satisfied by the Clear-eyed One,
the Buddha, Kinsman of the Sun,
led the spiritual life in his presence,
the one of such outstanding wisdom.

§7If you practice in accordance
with each of these questions
as taught by the Buddha,
you’ll go from the near shore to the far.

§8Developing the supreme path,
you’ll go from the near shore to the far.
This path is for going to the far shore;
that’s why it’s called “The Way to the Far Shore”.

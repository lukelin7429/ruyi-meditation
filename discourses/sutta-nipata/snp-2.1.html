Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 2.1

Gems

Ratana Sutta

Setting
No narrative frame survives in the verses themselves — they open directly with the first stanza; tradition connects the chant to a crisis of famine, disease, and hauntings in Vesālī
Speakers
An unnamed first-person voice throughout, addressing “all you beings” gathered on the ground and in the sky; no dialogue partner is named in the text
Form
Eighteen verses built on a fixed refrain — repeated praise of the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Saṅgha as three “gems,” almost every stanza closing “by this truth, may you be well”
Length
~3 minutes to read
Status
One of the most widely recited protective (paritta) chants in Theravada practice today, alongside the Mettā Sutta (Snp 1.8) and the Maṅgala Sutta (Snp 2.4)
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — short and formulaic, meant to be memorized and chanted rather than puzzled over

Why this discourse

Few texts in the entire Buddhist canon are recited more often, by more people, than this one. The Ratana Sutta names three things — the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Saṅgha — and calls each a ratana, a “gem” or “jewel,” then spends eighteen short verses explaining exactly why each one deserves the name. It is not a philosophical argument. It is closer to a proclamation, repeated with a refrain that turns almost hypnotic on the tongue: “by this truth, may you be well.” That refrain is why the sutta has the reputation it does — recited at housewarmings, illnesses, funerals, and New Year across the Theravada world, understood not as magic but as an act of truth-telling so complete, so aligned with what is actually real, that it clears the air around it.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Saṅgha each possess a quality “nothing equals” — and stating that fact out loud, verse after verse, in careful and specific terms, is itself presented as a source of protection and well-being for whoever gathers to listen.

An invitation to every being, in the sky and on the ground

The sutta opens mid-address, with no scene-setting at all: “Whatever beings have gathered here, on the ground or in the sky.” The first two verses are a summons and a charge — asking every being present, human or otherwise, to settle into a “happy heart” and listen carefully, and then asking them, in turn, to protect with diligence the humans who make offerings to them “day and night.” Before a single word is said about the Buddha, the Dhamma, or the Saṅgha, the sutta has already established its logic: a gathered assembly, human and non-human together, called to attention and asked to extend goodwill.

Three gems, unevenly divided

It would be tidy to imagine the eighteen verses splitting neatly into three blocks of six — Buddha, then Dhamma, then Saṅgha. They do not. Verse 3 praises the Buddha (“there’s no wealth in this life or the next... that equals the Realized One”); verses 4 and 5 turn to the Dhamma (the “ending, dispassion, the undying” that the Sakyan Sage attained, and the “immersion with immediate fruit”); then verses 6 through 12 — by far the longest stretch — stay with the Saṅgha, describing the community of accomplished disciples in real detail before the sutta swings back to the Buddha for verses 13 and 14, and closes its main sequence with one more Saṅgha verse at 15. The proportions are themselves a kind of statement: the sutta spends more time describing what a genuinely accomplished disciple is like than it spends on anything else.

The Saṅgha gem, described in the most detail

The Saṅgha verses are the most concrete in the whole sutta. Verse 6 names “the four pairs” and “eight individual persons” — the four stages of awakening (stream-enterer, once-returner, non-returner, and the fully awakened arhat), each counted twice, once for the person practicing toward that stage and once for the person who has fully attained it, giving eight in all. Verse 8 offers a striking image: such a person is like Indra’s pillar, rooted in the earth, unmoved by the four winds. Verses 10 and 11 read as one continuous thought split across two stanzas — the only place in the sutta where a verse (10) has no closing refrain at all, running straight into the next: on attaining “vision,” three things are given up (substantialist view, doubt, and attachment to precepts and observances — the three fetters cut at stream-entry), and the same person becomes “freed from the four places of loss” and incapable of six things the text doesn’t itemize. Verse 12 adds one more concrete claim: such a person, if they do something wrong by body, speech, or mind, is unable to conceal it — their own integrity makes hiding impossible.

The Buddha and Dhamma verses: superlative, then image

The Buddha and Dhamma verses work differently — less itemized, more image-driven. Verse 3 simply states that nothing in this world or any heaven equals the Realized One. Verses 4 and 5 name what the Dhamma actually is in the most compressed terms the sutta offers: “ending, dispassion, the undying, the sublime,” and an “immersion with immediate fruit” that has no equal. When the sutta returns to the Buddha at verses 13 and 14, it reaches for its most vivid image: a tall forest tree, “crowned with flowers in the first month of summer,” and compares the Buddha’s teaching of the Dhamma to that flowering — abundant, seasonal, visible from a distance, leading “to extinguishment, the ultimate benefit.”

The closing bows

The last three verses (16, 17, and 18) form a matched triad, each repeating the same opening two lines — “Whatever beings have gathered here, on the ground or in the sky” — that opened the whole sutta, and each closing with a direct bow: to the Buddha, then the Dhamma, then the Saṅgha, each followed by “May you be safe!” The sutta thereby closes exactly where it began, with the same gathered assembly of beings, but now having heard, in order, precisely why each of the three gems earns that bow.

A note on the traditional setting

Nothing in the verses themselves names a place, an audience, or an occasion — unusually, for a discourse of this length, there is no “so I have heard, at one time the Buddha was staying near…” frame at all. The strong association with Vesālī and a crisis of famine, illness, and hostile spirits comes from later tradition and commentary rather than from the sutta’s own words. What the text itself establishes, on its own terms, is simpler and in some ways more durable: an assembly of beings asked to listen, a case made in careful and specific language for why three things are worth taking refuge in, and a repeated act of truth-telling offered as protection.

Key terms

ratana — “gem” or “jewel,” the sutta’s title word, applied in turn to the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Saṅgha.
“by this truth, may you be well” — the refrain that closes almost every verse, the basis for the sutta’s use as a paritta, or protective recitation.
the four pairs, the eight individuals — the community of noble disciples at the four stages of awakening (stream-enterer, once-returner, non-returner, arhat), each stage counted for both the path and its fruit.
Indra’s pillar — the sutta’s image, in verse 8, for a person of realized understanding: rooted in earth, unshaken by the four winds.
paritta — the broader category of protective chant this sutta belongs to, recited for safety and well-being; the Ratana Sutta is among the most widely used examples in the Theravada world.

The text

All eighteen verses, complete, since each closes its own thought and the repeated refrain is part of the sutta’s design. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

An invitation to every being

Verse 1Whatever beings have gathered here,
on the ground or in the sky:
may beings all be of happy heart,
and listen carefully to what is said.

Verse 2So pay heed, all you beings,
have love for humankind,
who day and night bring offerings;
please protect them diligently.

The Buddha gem

Verse 3There’s no wealth in this life or the next,
no sublime gem in the heavens,
that equals the Realized One.
This sublime gem is in the Buddha:
by this truth, may you be well!

The Dhamma gem

Verse 4Ending, dispassion, the undying, the sublime,
attained by the Sakyan Sage immersed in samādhi;
there is nothing equal to that Dhamma.
This sublime gem is in the Dhamma:
by this truth, may you be well!

Verse 5The purity praised by the highest Buddha
is said to be the “immersion with immediate fruit”;
no equal to that immersion is found.
This sublime gem is in the Dhamma:
by this truth, may you be well!

The Saṅgha gem

Verse 6The eight individual persons praised by the good
are the four pairs of the Holy One’s disciples;
they are worthy of religious donations,
what’s given to them is very fruitful.
This sublime gem is in the Saṅgha:
by this truth, may you be well!

Verse 7Dedicated to Gotama’s dispensation,
strong-minded, free of sense desire,
they’ve attained the goal, plunged into freedom from death,
and enjoy the quenching they’ve freely gained.
This sublime gem is in the Saṅgha:
by this truth, may you be well!

Verse 8As Indra’s pillar rooted in earth
is not shaken by the four winds,
I say a true person is like this,
who sees the noble truths in experience.
This sublime gem is in the Saṅgha:
by this truth, may you be well!

Verse 9Those who fathom the noble truths
taught by the one of deep wisdom,
do not take an eighth life,
even if they are hugely negligent.
This sublime gem is in the Saṅgha:
by this truth, may you be well!

Verse 10When they attain to vision
they give up three things:
substantialist view, doubt, and any
attachment to precepts and observances.

Verse 11They’re freed from the four places of loss,
and cannot do six things.
This sublime gem is in the Saṅgha:
by this truth, may you be well!

Verse 12Even if they do a bad deed
by body, speech, or mind,
they are unable to conceal it;
this inability applies to one who has seen the truth, they say.
This sublime gem is in the Saṅgha:
by this truth, may you be well!

The Buddha gem, again

Verse 13Like a tall forest tree crowned with flowers
in the first month of summer;
that’s how he taught the superb Dhamma,
leading to extinguishment, the ultimate benefit.
This sublime gem is in the Buddha:
by this truth, may you be well!

Verse 14The superb, knower of the superb, giver of the superb, bringer of the superb;
taught the superb Dhamma supreme.
This sublime gem is in the Buddha:
by this truth, may you be well!

The Saṅgha gem, once more

Verse 15The old is ended, nothing new is produced.
their minds have no desire for future rebirth.
Withered are the seeds, there’s no desire for growth,
those attentive ones are extinguished like this lamp.
This sublime gem is in the Saṅgha:
by this truth, may you be well!

The closing bows

Verse 16Whatever beings have gathered here,
on the ground or in the sky:
the Realized One is honored by gods and humans!
We bow to the Buddha! May you be safe!

Verse 17Whatever beings have gathered here,
on the ground or in the sky:
the Realized One is honored by gods and humans!
We bow to the Dhamma! May you be safe!

Verse 18Whatever beings have gathered here,
on the ground or in the sky:
the Realized One is honored by gods and humans!
We bow to the Saṅgha! May you be safe!

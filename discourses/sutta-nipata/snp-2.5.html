Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 2.5

With Spiky

Sūcilomasutta

Setting
Near Gayā, on the cut-stone ledge in the haunt of the native spirit Sūciloma (“Spiky”)
Speakers
The native spirits Khara (“Shaggy”) and Sūciloma (“Spiky”), then Sūciloma and the Buddha
Form
A prose narrative — a hostile test, a threat, and calm composure — followed by a short verse dialogue on the origin of the mind’s afflictions
Length
~2 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a vivid narrative frame around a psychologically dense final answer

Why this discourse

Before this sutta becomes a teaching, it is a confrontation. A yakkha named Sūciloma — “Spiky,” presumably for a fearsome, bristling appearance — doubts that the seated ascetic before him is genuine, leans his body against him to test him, and threatens, when rebuffed, to drive him insane or hurl him across the Ganges. The Buddha’s reply is unhurried: he isn’t afraid, he simply finds the touch unpleasant, and he sees no one in any world who could actually carry out the threat — so Spiky may as well ask his real question. What follows is a compact, image-rich answer to one of the oldest psychological puzzles in the canon: where do greed, hatred, and the mind’s restless thoughts actually come from?

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Greed, hatred, discontent, desire, terror, and the mind’s restless wandering all arise “from here” — born of affection and originating in oneself, like the prop roots of a banyan sprouting from the tree itself — and understanding that origin is what allows a person to be free of them.

A fierce welcome

The sutta opens with two yakkhas, Khara (“Shaggy”) and Sūciloma (“Spiky”), passing near the seated Buddha. Shaggy remarks, simply, “that’s an ascetic” — and Spiky disagrees: “that’s no ascetic, he’s a faker! I’ll soon find out whether he’s an ascetic or a faker.” The test that follows is physical, not verbal: Spiky leans his body directly against the Buddha, who pulls away. Asked whether he is afraid, the Buddha gives an answer that reframes the entire exchange: “No, respectable sir, I’m not afraid. But your touch is nasty.” The distinction matters — not fear, but simple, honest distaste.

A threat, and a striking composure

Spiky escalates: answer my question, he says, or “I’ll drive you insane, or explode your heart, or grab you by the feet and throw you to the far shore of the Ganges!” The Buddha’s reply is calm to the point of dryness: he doesn’t see anyone “in this world with its gods, Māras, and Divinities, this population with its ascetics and brahmins, its gods and humans” who could actually do that to him — and, that settled, Spiky is welcome to ask whatever he wishes. The threat is answered not with a counter-threat or a display of power, but with an unbothered assessment of what is and isn’t actually possible.

The question: where do these things come from?

Spiky’s question, once it finally arrives, is precise and psychologically serious: “Where do greed and hate come from? From where spring discontent, desire, and terror? Where do the mind’s thoughts originate, like a crow let loose by boys?” That closing image is worth pausing on — a crow released by children, flying about erratically with nowhere safe to land, is an unusually exact picture of a mind full of thoughts that have no stable place to settle.

The answer: rooted in self, spreading like a creeper

The Buddha’s reply first repeats Spiky’s own images back to him almost verbatim — greed, hate, discontent, desire, terror, and the mind’s thoughts all come “from here” — before locating exactly where “here” is: “born of affection, originating in oneself, like prop roots sprouting from a banyan.” A banyan famously grows new roots downward from its own branches, each one eventually thickening into what looks like an independent trunk — a single tree that appears, from a distance, like an entire grove. The image suggests that these afflictions are not intrusions from outside but growths the self keeps generating from itself. The second image is sharper still: “the many kinds of attachment to sensual pleasures are like camel’s-foot creeper creeping through the woods” — an invasive vine, spreading unchecked, choking whatever it climbs.

Understanding the origin, crossing the flood

The sutta’s final verse turns the diagnosis into a path: “those who understand where they come from get rid of them — listen up, spirit! — they cross this flood so hard to cross, not crossed before, so as to not be reborn.” The “flood” is a recurring canonical image for the pull of craving and views that keeps beings cycling through existence; crossing it “not crossed before” suggests something genuinely final, not a temporary reprieve. Notably, the answer is addressed directly back to Spiky — “listen up, spirit!” — closing the discourse as a real reply to a real, if hostile, interlocutor, rather than an abstract teaching floated into the air.

Key terms

yakkha — a class of powerful, often fearsome nature spirits in the ancient Indian cosmos; Sūciloma and Khara both belong to this category.
“like a crow let loose by boys” — Spiky’s own image for the mind’s restless, unsettled thoughts.
the banyan’s prop roots — the Buddha’s image for how greed, hate, and related afflictions originate in oneself rather than arriving from outside.
the flood (ogha) — a standard canonical metaphor for the pull of craving, views, and ignorance that carries beings through repeated existence; crossing it is a synonym for liberation.

The text

Complete, including the prose frame with Khara and Sūciloma. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A fierce welcome

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Gayā on the cut-stone ledge in the haunt of Sūciloma the native spirit. Now at that time the native spirits Khara and Sūciloma were passing by not far from the Buddha. So Khara said to Sūciloma, “That’s an ascetic.” “That’s no ascetic, he’s a faker! I’ll soon find out whether he’s an ascetic or a faker.”

§2Then Sūciloma went up to the Buddha and leaned up against his body, but the Buddha pulled away. Then Sūciloma said to the Buddha, “Are you afraid, ascetic?” “No, respectable sir, I’m not afraid. But your touch is nasty.”

A threat, and a striking composure

§3“I will ask you a question, ascetic. If you don’t answer me, I’ll drive you insane, or explode your heart, or grab you by the feet and throw you to the far shore of the Ganges!”

§4“I don’t see anyone in this world with its gods, Māras, and Divinities, this population with its ascetics and brahmins, its gods and humans who could do that to me. But anyway, ask what you wish.” Then Sūciloma addressed the Buddha in verse:

The question

Verse 1“Where do greed and hate come from?
From where spring discontent, desire, and terror?
Where do the mind’s thoughts originate,
like a crow let loose by boys.”

The answer: rooted in self, spreading like a creeper

Verse 2“Greed and hate come from here;
from here spring discontent, desire, and terror;
here’s where the mind’s thoughts originate,
like a crow let loose by boys.

Verse 3Born of affection, originating in oneself,
like prop roots sprouting from a banyan;
the many kinds of attachment to sensual pleasures
are like camel’s foot creeper creeping through the woods.

Understanding the origin, crossing the flood

Verse 4Those who understand where they come from
get rid of them—listen up, spirit!
They cross this flood so hard to cross,
not crossed before, so as to not be reborn.”

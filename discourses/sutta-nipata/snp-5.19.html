Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 5.19

Preserving the Way to the Far Shore

Pārāyanānurakkhaṇa

Setting
Bāvarī’s hermitage on the Godāvarī, closing the frame narrative begun in Snp 5.1
Speakers
Piṅgiya, reciting for his teacher Bāvarī; an unnamed questioner (traditionally Bāvarī); the Buddha, addressing Piṅgiya from a distance
Form
Nineteen verses of devotional recollection, closing with the collection’s own colophon
Length
~5–6 minutes to read
Position
The 73rd and final discourse of the entire Sutta Nipāta
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — personal and devotional rather than doctrinal, with a few allusions worth unpacking

Why this discourse

This is the last discourse of the Sutta Nipāta — the 73rd of 73, closing not only the Pārāyanavagga but the entire collection. It does not close with a final doctrine or a summary teaching. It closes with an old man, too frail now to travel, explaining to his even older teacher how he still manages to stay close to someone he cannot physically reach: through faith, memory, and a mind that keeps returning, night after night, to what it once directly saw.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Piṅgiya, too old and feeble to travel to the Buddha in body, explains to Bāvarī that he never dwells apart from him even for an hour — seeing him in his mind’s eye day and night, bowing in whatever direction he travels — and is told, in return, that this quality of faith is itself enough to carry him beyond the domain of death.

Piṅgiya keeps his word

The title, “Preserving the Way to the Far Shore,” names exactly what this discourse shows happening: Piṅgiya, having returned to Bāvarī after his own question was answered (Snp 5.17), makes good on an implicit promise to pass on what he received. “I shall keep reciting the Way to the Far Shore,” he begins, “which was taught as it was seen by the immaculate one of vast intelligence” — and gives his reason for trusting it completely: “he is desireless, unentangled, a spiritual giant: why would he speak falsely?” What follows is an extended, unhurried act of praise, describing the Buddha as “all-seer, dispeller of darkness,” one who has “gone to world’s end, beyond all rebirths,” and “free of defilements.”

A bird finding a better forest

Piṅgiya offers one of the vagga’s more vivid images to describe leaving his earlier teachers behind: “like a bird that flees a little copse, to roost in a forest abounding in fruit, I’ve left the near-sighted behind, like a swan come to a great river.” He is candid about what his earlier teachers actually offered: claims about the past and future — “thus it was” or “so it shall be” — that amounted, in his own retrospective judgment, to “just the testament of hearsay” and “just fostered speculation.” Against that, he sets what the Buddha’s teaching offers instead: a Dhamma “apparent in the present life, immediately effective” — language drawn from the classic formula for reflecting on the qualities of the teaching itself, here applied by someone describing his own lived experience of it, not reciting the formula abstractly.

“Why would you dwell apart from him?”

The poem then shifts into a direct exchange. Someone — almost certainly Bāvarī himself, since Piṅgiya is repeatedly addressed as “brahmin” in a tone of concerned questioning — asks why Piṅgiya would “dwell apart from him even for an hour,” given everything he has just described. Piṅgiya’s answer is the discourse’s emotional core: “I never dwell apart from him, not even for an hour... being diligent, I see him in my mind’s eye day and night. I spend the night in homage to him, hence I think I dwell with him.” His faith, joy, intention, and mindfulness, he says, “never stray from Gotama’s teaching”; he bows “to whatever direction the one of vast wisdom heads.” Then, plainly: “I’m old and feeble, so my body cannot go there, but I always travel in my thoughts, for my mind, brahmin, is bound to him.”

Floundering in the mud, before this

One verse briefly opens onto Piṅgiya’s life before he found the Buddha: “lying floundering in the mud, I drifted from island to island. Then I saw the Buddha, the undefiled one who has crossed the flood.” It is a striking, self-exposing image from a man otherwise speaking in praise of someone else — a brief admission of how lost he had actually been, drifting without real direction between one inadequate teacher and the next, before whatever he found in the Buddha gave him somewhere to actually stand.

A verse sent across the distance

What follows next is one of this discourse’s most striking moments, and traditional commentary offers an explanation worth noting even if it cannot be verified from the verse alone: aware that Piṅgiya was too old and frail to travel, the Buddha is said to have sent him this verse of encouragement directly, by the power of his mind, across the distance separating them — comparing Piṅgiya’s faith to that of Vakkali, a monk elsewhere declared foremost among the Buddha’s followers for exactly this quality of devotion, and to two others, Bhadrāvudha and Gotama of Āḷavī, similarly remembered for their faith. (This second Gotama, sharing the Buddha’s own clan name — a common brahmin surname, not exclusive to him — is a different person entirely.) “So too you should commit to faith,” the verse tells Piṅgiya. “You will go, Piṅgiya, beyond the domain of death.” Vakkali’s own story (told at SN 22.87) is worth knowing here: he was a monk so devoted to gazing at the Buddha’s physical form that the Buddha eventually redirected him — “whoever sees the Dhamma sees me” — toward exactly the kind of inward, mind-held presence that Piṅgiya has already found for himself in these verses, without needing to be told.

“Remember me as one whose mind is made up”

The closing verses return to Piṅgiya’s own voice, and his confidence, rather than diminishing with his failing body, is described as growing: “my confidence grows as I hear the word of the sage.” He describes the Buddha once more — “the teacher who settles all questions for those who admit their doubts” — and ends the entire collection’s sequence of individual voices with a single, unhedged declaration: “for sure I will go there, I have no doubt of that. Remember me as one whose mind is made up.”

Closing the whole collection

The very last line of the source text is not part of the verse at all: a plain colophon, “the Anthology of Discourses is completed” (Suttanipāto samatto), marking the end not only of this one discourse, or even of the Pārāyanavagga, but of all seventy-three discourses of the Sutta Nipāta. It is a quiet way for a very old collection to end — not a grand summary or a final doctrinal pronouncement, but an elderly, failing-eyed student’s insistence that distance and frailty do not actually separate him from what he loves, followed by a single unadorned sentence noting that the text, at last, is finished. After sixty-nine discourses across four earlier chapters and eighteen more in this one, built from riddles, similes, arguments, and direct instruction, the collection chooses to close on faith and memory rather than argument — on a devoted, aging man’s insistence that what actually matters about presence was never really about distance in the first place.

Key terms

Pārāyanānurakkhaṇa — “preserving” or “guarding” the Way to the Far Shore; Piṅgiya’s act of keeping the teaching alive by reciting it faithfully for Bāvarī.
sandiṭṭhiko akāliko — “apparent in the present life, immediately effective”: language drawn from the classic formula for recollecting the qualities of the Dhamma, here spoken from lived experience rather than recited abstractly.
Vakkali — a monk elsewhere (SN 22.87) declared foremost in faith, once so devoted to gazing at the Buddha’s physical form that the Buddha redirected him toward seeing the Dhamma instead; invoked here as a point of comparison for Piṅgiya’s own devotion.
a mind-sent verse — commentarial tradition holds that, knowing Piṅgiya could not travel, the Buddha communicated his verse of encouragement to him directly across the distance; the verse itself does not explain its own delivery.
Suttanipāto samatto — “the Anthology of Discourses is completed”: the plain colophon closing not just this discourse but all seventy-three of the Sutta Nipāta.

The text

The discourse in full — all nineteen verses, given complete and without abbreviation, together with the closing colophon of the entire Sutta Nipāta. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§1“I shall keep reciting the Way to the Far Shore,”
said Venerable Piṅgiya,
“which was taught as it was seen
by the immaculate one of vast intelligence.
He is desireless, unentangled, a spiritual giant:
why would he speak falsely?

§2Come, let me extol
in sweet words of praise
the one who’s given up stains and delusions,
conceit and contempt.

§3The Buddha, all-seer, dispeller of darkness,
has gone to world’s end, beyond all rebirths;
he is free of defilements,
and has given up all suffering,
the rightly-named one, brahmin, is revered by me.

§4Like a bird that flees a little copse,
to roost in a forest abounding in fruit,
I’ve left the near-sighted behind,
like a swan come to a great river.

§5Those who explained to me previously,
before Gotama’s teaching,
said ‘thus it was’ or ‘so it shall be’.
All that was just the testament of hearsay;
all that just fostered speculation.

§6Alone, the dispeller of darkness
is brilliant, a beacon:
Gotama, vast in wisdom,
Gotama, vast in intelligence.

§7He is the one who taught me Dhamma,
apparent in the present life, immediately effective,
the untroubled, the end of craving,
to which there is no compare.”

§8“Why would you dwell apart from him
even for an hour, Piṅgiya?
From Gotama, vast in wisdom,
from Gotama, vast in intelligence?

§9He is the one who taught you Dhamma,
apparent in the present life, immediately effective,
the untroubled, the end of craving,
to which there is no compare.”

§10“I never dwell apart from him,
not even for an hour, brahmin.
From Gotama, vast in wisdom,
from Gotama, vast in intelligence.

§11He is the one who taught me Dhamma,
apparent in the present life, immediately effective,
the untroubled, the end of craving,
to which there is no compare.

§12Being diligent, I see him
in my mind’s eye day and night.
I spend the night in homage to him,
hence I think I dwell with him.

§13My faith and joy and intent and mindfulness
never stray from Gotama’s teaching.
I bow to whatever direction
the one of vast wisdom heads.

§14I’m old and feeble,
so my body cannot go there,
but I always travel in my thoughts,
for my mind, brahmin, is bound to him.

§15Lying floundering in the mud,
I drifted from island to island.
Then I saw the Buddha,
the undefiled one who has crossed the flood.”

§16“Just as Vakkali was committed to faith—
Bhadrāvudha and Gotama of Āḷavī too—
so too you should commit to faith.
You will go, Piṅgiya, beyond the domain of death.”

§17“My confidence grows
as I hear the word of the sage,
the Buddha with veil cast off,
so kind and eloquent.

§18Having directly known all about the gods,
he understands all top to bottom,
the teacher who settles all questions
for those who admit their doubts.

§19The unfaltering, the unshakable:
that to which there is no compare.
For sure I will go there, I have no doubt of that.
Remember me as one whose mind is made up.”

The manuscript’s own closing words. Following verse 19, the source text adds a single unadorned line, not itself part of the verse, marking the end not only of this discourse but of the entire Sutta Nipāta: “40”

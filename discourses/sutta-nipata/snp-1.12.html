Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 1.12

The Sage

Munisutta

Setting
No prose frame at all — the discourse opens and closes entirely in verse, with no narrative occasion given
Speakers
Traditionally the Buddha, addressed to no one named within the verses
Form
Fifteen verses of continuous portraiture, most closing on the refrain “that’s who the attentive know as a sage” — the closing discourse of Vagga 1
Length
~3 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No complete Chinese Āgama counterpart is known to survive for this discourse specifically, consistent with the Sutta Nipāta as a whole
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a dense sequence of similes describing an ideal that rewards slow, verse-by-verse reading

Why this discourse, the twelfth and last of its chapter

The Uragavagga has moved through an insult redefined, a wish for boundless love, two spirits testing a reputation before trusting it, a fierce yakkha converted by riddles, and a body taken apart to undercut its own vanity. It closes here, with a portrait of the figure all of that has been pointing toward — the muni, the sage: solitary, unshaken by praise or blame, a “leader of others, not by others led.” The discourse builds this portrait almost entirely through simile, comparing the sage in quick succession to a lion, the wind, water on a lotus leaf, and a peacock that, however beautiful, can never outfly a swan.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A sage is defined by solitude and non-attachment rather than social role — unshaken by praise or blame, free of speculation, restrained in every sense, and so thoroughly without partiality that a wandering mendicant, on this text’s own account, cannot be matched by even the most devoted householder.

Verses 1–3: the danger of attachment itself

The discourse opens with a striking claim about relationship and dwelling as such: “peril stems from intimacy, dust comes from an abode. Freedom from abode and intimacy: that is the sage’s vision.” The verses that follow use agricultural imagery to make the same point more concretely — a sage “having cut down what’s grown” would not replant it, nor nurture what is already growing; having “appraised the fields and measured the seeds,” they would not water them. The metaphor treats craving and rebirth as something actively cultivated, like a crop, and the sage as someone who has deliberately stopped tending the field rather than someone who merely lacks a harvest by accident.

Verses 4–6: freedom of a specific kind

Having “understood all the planes of rebirth” and desiring none of them, the sage is said to have “reached the far shore” without needing to swim — already there, rather than still crossing. Verse 5 piles up superlatives — “vanquisher of all, knower of all, so very intelligent, unsullied in the midst of all things” — before verse 6 grounds them in specific, checkable qualities: “strong in wisdom, with precepts and observances intact, serene, loving absorption, mindful, released from chains, kind, undefiled.” Here begins the discourse’s refrain, repeated across most of the remaining verses: “that’s who the attentive know as a sage.”

Verse 7: three images of imperviousness

This is the discourse’s most vivid verse, stacking three similes in a row for a mind unmoved by praise and blame: “like a lion not startled by sounds, like wind not caught in a net, like water not sticking to a lotus.” Each image works slightly differently — the lion is fearless, the wind cannot be physically trapped at all, water beads and rolls off a lotus leaf rather than soaking in — and together they describe not numbness but a specific kind of structural non-adherence, where criticism and flattery alike simply fail to attach. The verse closes with a line that has often been quoted on its own: “leader of others, not by others led.”

Verses 8–12: steadiness under provocation, in daily conduct, in restraint

The middle verses turn from grand imagery to ordinary tests. “Steady as a post in a bathing-place when others speak endlessly against them” describes composure under sustained verbal attack specifically, not just occasional insult. “Straight as a shuttle” — the weaving tool that must travel a perfectly true line to do its work — describes consistency of conduct. A telling detail follows in verse 11: when the sage “who lives on charity receives alms, from the top, the middle, or the leftovers,” they consider it beneath them to either praise or complain about what they are given — a small, concrete test of the same equanimity described more abstractly elsewhere. Verse 12 adds sexual restraint explicitly, “even when young,” to the portrait.

Verses 13–15: the sage against the householder

The discourse closes by drawing an explicit, somewhat pointed contrast. Verse 13 gathers the whole portrait into a final summary — “understanding the world, the seer of the ultimate goal… having crossed the flood and the ocean, has cut the ties, unattached and undefiled.” Verse 14 then states the contrast directly: “the householder providing for a wife” and “the selfless one true to their vows” are “not the same, far apart in lifestyle and conduct” — specifically because “the unrestrained householder kills other creatures, while the restrained sage always protects living creatures.” The closing verse supplies the discourse’s final image: “as the crested blue-necked peacock flying through the sky never approaches the speed of the swan, so the householder cannot compete with the mendicant, the sage meditating secluded in the woods.” It is worth noting that this is a comparison between ways of life within this particular text, not a verdict on individual worth — the peacock is still described as beautiful, only slower, and elsewhere in the canon householders are shown reaching deep attainment. Here, though, the chapter chooses to end on the specific ideal of the solitary sage, closing the Uragavagga on the same note of non-attachment it explored from its very first verses.

Key terms

muni — “sage,” the discourse’s subject and title; a figure defined by solitude, non-attachment, and equanimity rather than by social role or ritual status.
“peril stems from intimacy” — the discourse’s opening claim in verse 1, treating close attachment itself, not any specific misdeed, as a source of danger.
“that’s who the attentive know as a sage” — the refrain closing most of the discourse’s verses, each time following a fresh description of the sage’s qualities.
“leader of others, not by others led” — the closing line of verse 7, describing the sage’s independence from both praise and blame.

The text

All fifteen verses in full, exactly as they stand with no narrative frame at either end — the closing discourse of Vagga 1. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The danger of attachment itself

Verse 1Peril stems from intimacy,
dust comes from an abode.
Freedom from abode and intimacy:
that is the sage’s vision.

Verse 2Having cut down what’s grown, they wouldn’t replant,
nor would they nurture what’s growing.
That’s who they call a sage wandering alone,
the great seer has seen the state of peace.

Verse 3Having appraised the fields and measured the seeds,
they wouldn’t nurture them with moisture.
Truly that sage who sees the ending of rebirth
has left speculation behind and is beyond reckoning.

Freedom of a specific kind

Verse 4Understanding all the planes of rebirth,
not wanting a single one of them;
truly that sage freed of greed
need not swim, for they have reached the far shore.

Verse 5The vanquisher of all, knower of all, so very intelligent,
unsullied in the midst of all things,
has given up all, freed in the ending of craving:
that’s who the attentive know as a sage.

Verse 6Strong in wisdom, with precepts and observances intact,
serene, loving absorption, mindful,
released from chains, kind, undefiled:
that’s who the attentive know as a sage.

Three images of imperviousness

Verse 7The diligent sage wandering alone,
is unshaken by blame and praise—
like a lion not startled by sounds,
like wind not caught in a net,
like water not sticking to a lotus.
Leader of others, not by others led:
that’s who the attentive know as a sage.

Steadiness, conduct, restraint

Verse 8Steady as a post in a bathing-place
when others speak endlessly against them,
freed of greed, with senses stilled:
that’s who the attentive know as a sage.

Verse 9Steadfast, straight as a shuttle,
horrified by wicked deeds,
discerning the just and the unjust:
that’s who the attentive know as a sage.

Verse 10Restrained, they do no evil,
young or middle-aged, the sage is self-controlled.
Irreproachable, he does not insult anyone:
that’s who the attentive know as a sage.

Verse 11When one who lives on charity receives alms,
from the top, the middle, or the leftovers,
they think it unworthy to praise or put down:
that’s who the attentive know as a sage.

Verse 12The sage lives refraining from sex,
even when young is not tied down,
refraining from indulgence and negligence, freed:
that’s who the attentive know as a sage.

The sage against the householder

Verse 13Understanding the world, the seer of the ultimate goal,
the unaffected one, having crossed the flood and the ocean,
has cut the ties, unattached and undefiled:
that’s who the attentive know as a sage.

Verse 14The two are not the same, far apart in lifestyle and conduct—
the householder providing for a wife, and the selfless one true to their vows.
The unrestrained householder kills other creatures,
while the restrained sage always protects living creatures.

Verse 15As the crested blue-necked peacock flying through the sky
never approaches the speed of the swan,
so the householder cannot compete with the mendicant,
the sage meditating secluded in the woods.

Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 1.2

The Wealthy Rancher

Dhaniyasutta

Setting
On the bank of the river Mahī in the rainy season, as storm clouds gather
Speakers
Dhaniya the cattle-rancher, the Buddha, and, briefly, Māra the Wicked
Form
Continuous dialogue in verse — twelve matched, mirrored stanzas, each closing “so rain forth, heavens, if you wish,” followed by Dhaniya’s conversion and a short exchange with Māra
Length
~5 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — simple surface narrative, but each stanza works as a deliberate double meaning

Why this discourse, the second

Dhaniya the rancher has everything in order for the coming rains: rice boiled, milk drawn, hut roofed, wife faithful, children healthy, cattle penned and tethered. Twelve times he states his readiness and dares the sky to do its worst. Twelve times the Buddha answers with the identical closing line — “so rain forth, heavens, if you wish” — but each of his stanzas takes Dhaniya’s literal boast and answers it with something that sounds almost the same and means something else entirely: a hut “wide open” rather than roofed, a fire “quenched” rather than kindled, cattle he simply does not have. The rain does come, torrentially, and it is Dhaniya — not the Buddha — whose preparations turn out to matter less than he thought.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A wealthy rancher lists everything that makes him feel secure against the coming rains; the Buddha echoes each line with a spiritual counterpart that uses nearly identical words to say something like the opposite, and when the rain genuinely falls, it is the rancher who is moved to go for refuge.

A rancher’s boasts, mirrored line for line

The stanza pattern is exact and repeats twelve times: Dhaniya speaks, is tagged “said Dhaniya the rancher,” and closes with “so rain forth, heavens, if you wish” — then the Buddha answers in the identical form. Dhaniya opens with the domestic basics: rice boiled, milk drawn, hut roofed, sacred fire kindled. The Buddha’s reply reuses the same images with their sense inverted: he has boiled away anger and drawn out hard-heartedness; his hut is “wide open,” his fire “quenched.” A boiled pot and a kindled fire mean security to Dhaniya and unfinished business to the Buddha; an open hut and a doused fire mean exposure to Dhaniya and completion to the Buddha.

Six more mirrored pairs

The pattern continues through five more exchanges. Dhaniya’s cattle graze safely even in rain; the Buddha has already “bound a raft,” crossed the flood, and “dispelled” it, so he has “no need for a raft” at all — the crossing is already finished. Dhaniya’s wife is obedient and their marriage untroubled by scandal; the Buddha’s own mind is “obedient and freed, long nurtured and well-tamed.” Dhaniya is self-employed, with healthy, well-regarded children; the Buddha is “no-one’s lackey,” wandering on what he has earned, needing no wages at all. Dhaniya has heifers, sucklings, cows in calf, and a bull as “captain of the herd”; the Buddha, plainly, states four times over that he has none of these — no heifers, no sucklings, no cows in calf, no bull. And where Dhaniya’s stakes and halters are driven in, unshakable, well-woven, the Buddha says instead that he broke his bonds “like a bull,” snapped the vine “like an elephant,” and “will never lie in a womb again” — the discourse’s clearest statement, folded into Dhaniya’s own barnyard imagery, that the Buddha considers himself done with rebirth entirely.

The rain arrives

Immediately after the twelfth exchange, “a vast cloud rained forth, soaking the uplands and valleys.” The literal event both men had been discussing simply happens. Hearing it, Dhaniya does not double down on his preparations; instead he says it is “no small gain for us that we have seen the Buddha,” and, together with his wife, asks to go for refuge and “lead the spiritual life under the Holy One” — gone, in his own closing words, “beyond birth and death.” The rain that was going to test Dhaniya’s security instead exposes, to Dhaniya himself, which kind of security actually held.

Māra’s interruption

Where most of the discourse pits Dhaniya against the Buddha, the closing exchange introduces a third voice. Māra the Wicked steps in to argue the rancher’s original position back at the Buddha directly: “children bring you delight, cattle also bring you delight — for attachments are a man’s delight; without attachments there’s no delight.” The Buddha answers with the exact mirror-structure the whole discourse has used throughout: “your children bring you sorrow, your cattle also bring you sorrow — for attachments are a man’s sorrow; without attachments there are no sorrows.” The last word in the discourse belongs to this direct contradiction, delight against sorrow, closing a text that has run on doubled meanings from its very first line.

Key terms

the Mahī — the river on whose bank the entire exchange takes place, named at the very start of Dhaniya’s first boast.
“bound a raft…dispelled the flood” — the Buddha’s answer to Dhaniya’s remark about cattle surviving the rains: a crossing already completed, needing no further raft.
Māra the Wicked — personified death and temptation, who intervenes only in the discourse’s final exchange to argue that attachment is the source of delight.
attachments — the term at the center of the closing exchange with Māra: the same word describes what Dhaniya has built his life around and what the Buddha identifies as the actual source of sorrow.

The text

The full Dhaniyasutta, all seventeen verses, with the dialogue attributions preserved exactly as given. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1“I’ve boiled my rice and drawn my milk,” said Dhaniya the rancher,
“I stay with my family along the bank of the Mahī.
My hut is roofed, my sacred fire kindled:
so rain forth, heavens, if you wish.”

Verse 2“I boil not with anger and have drawn out hard-heartedness,” said the Buddha,
“I stay for one night along the bank of the Mahī.
My hut is wide open, my fire is quenched:
so rain forth, heavens, if you wish.”

Verse 3“No gadflies or mosquitoes are found,” said Dhaniya,
“cows graze on the lush meadow grass.
They get by even when the rain comes:
so rain forth, heavens, if you wish.”

Verse 4“I bound a raft and prepared it well,” said the Buddha,
“and with it I crossed over, went to the far shore, and dispelled the flood.
Now I have no need for a raft:
so rain forth, heavens, if you wish.”

Verse 5“My wife is obedient, not wanton,” said Dhaniya,
“long have we lived together happily.
I hear nothing bad about her:
so rain forth, heavens, if you wish.”

Verse 6“My mind is obedient and freed,” said the Buddha,
“long nurtured and well-tamed.
Nothing bad is found in me:
so rain forth, heavens, if you wish.”

Verse 7“I am self-employed,” said Dhaniya,
“and my healthy children likewise.
I hear nothing bad about them:
so rain forth, heavens, if you wish.”

Verse 8“I am no-one’s lackey,” said the Buddha,
“with what I have earned I wander the world.
I have no need for wages:
so rain forth, heavens, if you wish.”

Verse 9“I have heifers and sucklings,” said Dhaniya,
“cows in calf and breeding cows.
I’ve also got a bull, captain of the herd here:
so rain forth, heavens, if you wish.”

Verse 10“I have no heifers or sucklings,” said the Buddha,
“no cows in calf or breeding cows.
I haven’t got a bull, captain of the herd here:
so rain forth, heavens, if you wish.”

Verse 11“The stakes are driven in, unshakable,” said Dhaniya,
“The grass halters are new and well-woven,
not even the sucklings can break them:
so rain forth, heavens, if you wish.”

Verse 12“Like a bull I broke the bonds,” said the Buddha,
“like an elephant I snapped the vine.
I will never lie in a womb again:
so rain forth, heavens, if you wish.”

Verses 13–14Right then a vast cloud rained forth, soaking the uplands and valleys. Hearing the heavens rain down, Dhaniya said this: “It is no small gain for us that we have seen the Buddha. We come to you for refuge, Clear-eyed One. O great sage, please be our Teacher.

Verse 15My wife and I, obedient, shall lead the spiritual life under the Holy One. Gone beyond birth and death, we shall make an end of suffering.”

Verse 16“Children bring you delight!” said Māra the Wicked, “Cattle also bring you delight! For attachments are a man’s delight; without attachments there’s no delight.”

Verse 17“Your children bring you sorrow,” said the Buddha, “Your cattle also bring you sorrow. For attachments are a man’s sorrow; without attachments there are no sorrows.”

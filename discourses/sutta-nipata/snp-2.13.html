Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 2.13

The Right Way to Wander

Sammāparibbājanīyasutta

Setting
No narrative frame is given within the verses themselves; SuttaCentral’s summary identifies the questioner as a deity
Speakers
An unnamed questioner and the Buddha
Form
A single question, answered across fifteen verses that each close on the same refrain, followed by one verse of agreement
Length
~3 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — repetitive by design, with real density packed into each verse’s first three lines

Why this discourse, the thirteenth

The Right Way to Wander answers a single, practical question — how should someone who has left ordinary life actually conduct himself? — not with one summary but with fifteen distinct angles on it, each one closing on the same four words. By the time the refrain lands for the sixteenth time, in the questioner's own voice, it has stopped sounding like repetition and started sounding like a description finally complete.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A single question — how should a mendicant who has left home rightly wander the world? — is answered across fifteen verses, each closing on the same refrain, moving from freedom from superstition through freedom from social friction, craving, and speculation, to a final state of fully understood peace.

The question

The poem opens without any narrative frame: “I ask the sage abounding in wisdom — crossed-over, gone beyond, quenched, steadfast: when a mendicant has left home, casting aside sensuality, what’s the right way to wander the world?” SuttaCentral’s summary of this discourse identifies the questioner as a deity, though, as with several other poems in this stretch of the chapter, the verses themselves carry no prose setting naming who is asking or where. What is clear is the shape of the answer that follows: the Buddha takes up the phrase “they’d rightly wander the world” and repeats it, varied only by what precedes it, at the end of every single verse from here to the close — fifteen times in all, each one a fresh angle on the same completed life.

Freedom from superstition and desire

The answer begins, notably, not with meditation but with superstition: a mendicant who has “eradicated superstitions… about celestial portents, dreams, or bodily marks” is the first qualification named. Only after that does the poem turn to desire itself — “dispel desire for pleasures human or heavenly” — paired with rebirth transcended and truth comprehended.

Freedom from social friction

A long central stretch of the poem addresses how a rightly wandering mendicant moves through relationships with other people: giving up backbiting, anger, and stinginess (verse 4); leaving behind both the loved and the unloved, grasping at neither (verse 5); needing no core in what has been acquired, and no one to guide them (verse 6); free of hostility “in speech, mind, or deed” (verse 7); and, in one of the poem’s most concrete images, “not gloating that ‘they bow to me’” even while being reviled, “not besotted when getting food from others” (verse 8) — praise and blame, honor and neglect, all met the same way.

Freedom from craving and confusion

The poem’s middle verses turn inward: refraining from violence and abduction, rid of indecision and “free of thorns” (verse 9); hurting no one, understanding the teaching in line with the truth (verse 10); free of all underlying tendencies and unskillful roots, needing no hope because free of hope itself (verse 11); defilements ended, conceit given up (verse 12); joining “no faction among sects,” rid of greed, hate, and aversion (verse 13); and, in verse 14, “expert in knowledge of conditions’ cessation” — the poem’s most technical line, naming direct understanding of dependent origination’s unwinding as one mark of rightly wandering the world.

Freedom from speculation, and full peace

The final two verses of the answer step back from particular conduct to a completed overview: “over speculating on the future or past,” understanding purity, “freed from all the sense fields” (verse 15); and, closing the answer proper, “the state of peace is understood, the truth is comprehended… and with the ending of all attachments” (verse 16) — the refrain landing one final time on the discourse’s entire accumulated weight.

Agreement

The questioner’s single closing verse confirms rather than adds: “Clearly, Blessed One, it is just as you say. One who lives like this is a tamed mendicant, passed beyond all fetters and yokes: they’d rightly wander the world.” Even the confirmation keeps the refrain, closing a poem that has, by this point, made the phrase feel less like a repeated tag than like a bell struck fifteen times over.

Key terms

“they’d rightly wander the world” — the poem’s refrain, closing all fifteen verses of the Buddha’s answer and the questioner’s final verse of agreement, sixteen times in all.
superstitions about “celestial portents, dreams, or bodily marks” — the very first qualification named in verse 2, placing freedom from superstition ahead of freedom from desire in the poem’s order.
“not gloating that ‘they bow to me’” — verse 8’s image of even-handedness under both praise and reviling, and indifference to how well or poorly the almsround goes.
“expert in knowledge of conditions’ cessation” — verse 14’s most technical line, naming direct understanding of dependent origination’s unwinding.

The text

The discourse in full — a single question and its sixteen-verse answer, without narrative frame. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The question

Verse 1“I ask the sage abounding in wisdom—
crossed-over, gone beyond, quenched, steadfast:
when a mendicant has left home, casting aside sensuality,
what’s the right way to wander the world?”

Freedom from superstition and desire

Verse 2“When they’ve eradicated superstitions,” said the Buddha, “about celestial portents, dreams, or bodily marks;
with the stain of superstitions left behind,
they’d rightly wander the world.

Verse 3A mendicant ought dispel desire
for pleasures human or heavenly;
with rebirth transcended and truth comprehended,
they’d rightly wander the world.

Freedom from social friction

Verse 4Putting backbiting behind them,
a mendicant gives up anger and stinginess;
with favoring and opposing left behind,
they’d rightly wander the world.

Verse 5When the loved and the unloved are both left behind,
not grasping or dependent on anything;
freed from all things that fetter,
they’d rightly wander the world.

Verse 6Finding no core in attachments,
rid of desire for things they’ve acquired,
independent, needing no-one to guide them,
they’d rightly wander the world.

Verse 7Not hostile in speech, mind, or deed,
they’ve rightly understood the teaching.
Aspiring to the state of extinguishment,
they’d rightly wander the world.

Verse 8Not gloating that, ‘they bow to me’;
though reviled, they’d still stay in touch;
not besotted when getting food from others,
they’d rightly wander the world.

Freedom from craving and confusion

Verse 9When greed and craving to live again are cast off,
a mendicant refrains from violence and abduction;
rid of indecision, free of thorns,
they’d rightly wander the world.

Verse 10Knowing what is suitable for themselves,
a mendicant would hurt no-one in the world;
understanding the teaching in line with the truth,
they’d rightly wander the world.

Verse 11They have no underlying tendencies at all,
and are rid of unskillful roots;
free of hope, with no need for hope,
they’d rightly wander the world.

Verse 12Defilements ended, conceit given up,
beyond all manner of desire;
tamed, quenched, and steadfast,
they’d rightly wander the world.

Verse 13Faithful, learned, seer of the sure path,
an attentive one joins no faction among sects;
rid of greed, hate, and aversion,
they’d rightly wander the world.

Verse 14A purified victor with veil cast off,
among worldly things master, transcendent, stilled;
expert in knowledge of conditions’ cessation,
they’d rightly wander the world.

Freedom from speculation, and full peace

Verse 15They’re over speculating on the future or past,
and understand what it means to be pure;
freed from all the sense fields,
they’d rightly wander the world.

Verse 16The state of peace is understood, the truth is comprehended,
they’ve openly seen defilements cast off;
and with the ending of all attachments,
they’d rightly wander the world.”

Agreement

Verse 17“Clearly, Blessed One, it is just as you say.
One who lives like this is a tamed mendicant,
passed beyond all fetters and yokes:
they’d rightly wander the world.”

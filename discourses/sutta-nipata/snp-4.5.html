Eight on the Ultimate

Paramaṭṭhakasutta

Why this discourse, the fifth of the Aṭṭhakavagga

The four discourses before this one have circled the same problem from different angles — craving, entrapment, dogmatism, false purity. The Paramaṭṭhakasutta names the underlying move common to all of them: declaring one thing “the ultimate,” parama, the highest, final, unsurpassable truth. Once that declaration is made, the verse observes, everything else is automatically demoted to “lesser” — and the discourse’s entire argument is that this single move, not any particular doctrine’s content, is what manufactures dispute. It is, in a real sense, the philosophical center of the whole Aṭṭhakavagga.

Reading guide

The discourse in one sentence

Declaring any position — a view, a perception, a precept, a vow — to be “the highest in the world” automatically requires ranking everyone who disagrees as lesser, which is exactly what keeps a person locked into disputes; the only way out is not a better ultimate claim but no ultimate claim at all, not even about oneself.

Verse 1: the move that manufactures conflict

The discourse opens with a conditional that reads almost like a formal proof: “if, maintaining that theirs is the ‘ultimate’ view, a personage makes it out to be highest in the world; then they declare all others are ‘lesser’; that’s why they’re not beyond disputes.” Notice what the verse does and does not claim. It does not say the person’s view is false. It says that the specific act of ranking it as highest logically entails ranking every other view as lower — and it is that ranking, not the content of the view, that guarantees ongoing conflict. Two people can hold different views and remain at peace; two people can hold different views where one insists theirs is supreme, and dispute becomes close to inevitable.

Verses 2–3: the “knot” hiding inside self-interest

Verse 2 traces where this move usually comes from: someone sees “an advantage for themselves in what’s seen, heard, or thought; or in precepts or vows,” and having “adopted that one alone,” comes to “see all others as inferior.” The self-interest named here need not be crude or cynical — it can be as ordinary as the comfort of belonging to the group that has it right, or the psychological stability of having settled a hard question. Verse 3 names this precisely as a “knot,” the same word used elsewhere in the canon for the entanglements that bind a person to continued suffering: “those who are skilled say that, too, is a knot, relying on which people see others as lesser.” The remedy the verse offers is correspondingly comprehensive: a mendicant “ought not rely on what’s seen, heard, or thought, or on precepts and vows” — not select a better basis for ranking, but decline to rank at all.

Verse 4: not even “equal”

This is the discourse’s subtlest and most important move, and it is easy to read past. Having ruled out claiming superiority, the verse doesn’t stop at the obviously more modest claim of equality: such a person “would never represent themselves as ‘equal,’ nor conceive themselves ‘worse’ or ‘better’.” This matters because “we’re all equal, all paths are the same” sounds humble but is still a comparative claim, still a position taken up on the same battlefield of ranking that produced the dispute in the first place — it just declares a tie instead of a win. The verse is after something more thoroughgoing: stepping out of the entire activity of measuring oneself, or one’s view, against others at all.

Verses 5–6: no side among the factions

Verse 5 describes the resulting freedom in physical, almost tactile language: “what was picked up has been set down and is not grasped again; they form no dependency even on notions.” The phrase “even on notions” is important — this rules out not just crude dogmatism but the subtler dependency on having a settled worldview at all, of any kind. “They follow no side among the factions, and believe in no view at all” is the verse’s starkest line, and verse 6 supplies its practical, existential extension: such a person has “no wish for either end” — no craving for continued existence or its opposite, in this life or the next — and has “adopted no dogma at all after judging among the teachings.” The phrase “after judging among the teachings” matters: this isn’t someone who never examined the available views, but someone who examined them and, rather than adopting the least-bad one, declined the whole framework of adoption.

Verses 7–8: gone to the far shore, not returning

The closing verses restate the discourse’s claims with finality. Verse 7: “for them not even the tiniest idea is formulated here regarding what is seen, heard, or thought” — and, echoing Snp 4.4, “that brahmin does not grasp any view — how could anyone in this world judge them?” Verse 8 closes the discourse with an image that will recur across the vagga and the wider canon: having “gone to the far shore, one such does not return.” The final line is worth sitting with: “the brahmin has no need to be led by precept or vow.” This does not mean ethical conduct has been discarded — nothing in this discourse recommends misconduct — but that conduct which was once maintained defensively, as evidence for a claim about oneself, has become simply how a person who no longer needs to prove anything happens to live.

Not relativism, and not nihilism

It is worth being explicit about two ways this discourse is commonly misread, because both misreadings are almost the opposite of what the verses say. The first misreading takes “believe in no view at all” as a kind of relativism — the idea that no position is any better than any other, that truth doesn’t matter, that anything goes. But the discourse never says views are equally true or false; it says something narrower and more specific, that ranking oneself by a view — as superior, equal, or inferior — is the problem, regardless of the view’s actual accuracy. The second misreading takes the same language as a kind of nihilism, a denial that anything is real or knowable. But verse 6’s “after judging among the teachings” shows real discernment is presupposed, not abandoned; someone incapable of judgment couldn’t decline to adopt a dogma “after” examining the options. What the discourse is actually describing is a mind clear enough to see accurately and act appropriately without needing that clarity to be certified, publicly ranked, or defended as the highest truth in the world.

Key terms

parama — “ultimate,” “highest,” “supreme”: the discourse’s title-word and target, the specific act of declaring one position final and unsurpassable.
gantha — “knot,” a canonical term for an entanglement that binds a person to suffering; verse 3 applies it to the self-interest hidden inside ranking one’s own view as supreme.
“not even ‘equal’” — verse 4’s refusal to settle for a modest-sounding tie; declaring equality is still a comparative claim made from within the same framework of ranking.
“after judging among the teachings” — verse 6’s phrase establishing that the discourse presupposes real discernment; declining to adopt a dogma is not the same as never having examined the alternatives.
“gone to the far shore” — the discourse’s closing image, shared across the canon, for the crossing from a life organized around views and craving to one that is not.

The text

All eight verses of the Paramaṭṭhakasutta, complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1If, maintaining that theirs is the “ultimate” view,
a personage makes it out to be highest in the world;
then they declare all others are “lesser”;
that’s why they’re not beyond disputes.

Verse 2If they see an advantage for themselves
in what’s seen, heard, or thought;
or in precepts or vows,
in that case, having adopted that one alone,
they see all others as inferior.

Verse 3Those who are skilled say that, too, is a knot,
relying on which people see others as lesser.
That’s why a mendicant ought not rely
on what’s seen, heard, or thought,
or on precepts and vows.

Verse 4Nor would they form a view about the world
through a notion or through precepts and vows.
They would never represent themselves as “equal”,
nor conceive themselves “worse” or “better”.

Verse 5What was picked up has been set down
and is not grasped again;
they form no dependency even on notions.
They follow no side among the factions,
and believe in no view at all.

Verse 6One here who has no wish for either end—
for any state of existence in this life or the next—
has adopted no dogma at all
after judging among the teachings.

Verse 7For them not even the tiniest idea is formulated here
regarding what is seen, heard, or thought.
That brahmin does not grasp any view—
how could anyone in this world judge them?

Verse 8They don’t make things up or promote them,
and don’t subscribe to any of the doctrines.
The brahmin has no need to be led by precept or vow;
gone to the far shore, one such does not return.

Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 4.4

Eight on the Pure

Suddhaṭṭhakasutta

Why this discourse, the fourth of the Aṭṭhakavagga

Where the previous discourse examined how a view becomes a possession to be defended, this one turns to something even closer to the bone: the claim to be pure. Purity was, and is, one of the most emotionally loaded claims a religious or philosophical position can make — it promises not just that a view is correct, but that holding it makes the holder clean, safe, superior. The Suddhaṭṭhakasutta goes after every route by which that claim gets made — through vision, through what is seen, heard, or thought, through precepts and vows — and finds all of them, examined closely, still forms of grasping.

Reading guide

The discourse in one sentence

Whether purity is claimed through a particular vision, through what has been seen or heard or thought, or through precepts and vows, it is still a claim that requires an audience and a defense, so real purity looks less like winning that claim than like no longer needing to make it — unsullied “in the midst of good and evil,” letting go of what was picked up without creating anything new to defend.

Verses 1–2: the trouble with “vision grants purity”

The discourse opens by quoting, almost verbatim, a claim its speaker is about to dismantle: “I see someone pure, perfectly healthy; it is vision that grants a person purity.” This is presented as “this notion of the ultimate” — the belief that a particular way of seeing things, once attained, certifies the seer as clean. Verse 2 exposes the problem with locating purity in something “seen” or in a “notion”: if purity could really be handed over this way, “then one with attachments is purified by another” — purity would become something you could receive from someone else’s teaching or vision without doing anything yourself, which contradicts what purification actually requires. The verse’s closing line is quietly devastating: “their view betrays them as one who asserts thus.” The very act of claiming purity through a view exposes that the claimant is still operating from within a view — still asserting, still needing to be right about something.

Verses 3–4: unsullied without a formula, and the monkey’s branch

Verse 3 gives the discourse’s positive description, and it is worth reading slowly: the brahmin (here meaning an ideal, awakened person, not a member of a social class) “speaks not of purity from another in terms of what has been seen, heard, or thought; or by precepts or vows.” Three whole categories are ruled out as the basis for a claim to purity — not because seeing, hearing, thinking, or ethical discipline are bad, but because turning any of them into grounds for an assertion of one’s own purity re-creates the same trap as verse 1. What actually characterizes this person is instead a manner of engaging with experience: “unsullied in the midst of good and evil, letting go what was picked up, without creating anything new here.” Verse 4 makes the alternative vivid with one of the vagga’s most memorable images: an unawakened mind “grabs on and lets loose like a monkey grabbing and releasing a branch” — each new perception, each new impulse, becomes the next thing gripped, with no interval of stillness in between. “Having let go the last they lay hold of the next” describes this exact restlessness: not attachment to any one thing, but attachment to the act of grabbing itself.

Verses 5–6: shopping for teachers, and living openly

Verse 5 draws a sharp contrast between two figures who might look similar from a distance. One “having undertaken their own vows,” still “visits various teachers, being attached to perception” — restlessly seeking outside confirmation because their own understanding hasn’t settled. The other, “having comprehended the truth through the knowledges,” “does not visit various teachers, being of vast wisdom” — not from arrogance or a refusal to learn, but because the searching itself was a symptom of not yet having seen clearly. Verse 6 then makes an important, easily overlooked move: such a person is “remote from all things seen, heard, or thought,” yet visible, living “openly” — and the verse asks, “how could anyone in this world judge them?” This isn’t a claim to be beyond all scrutiny through secrecy; it is the opposite. Because there is no hidden position being defended, there is nothing concealed for a critic to expose.

Verses 7–8: neither passion nor its opposite

The closing verses complete the argument with a move that keeps this teaching from collapsing into its own opposite extreme. Verse 7 describes someone who doesn’t “make things up or promote them, or speak of the uttermost purity” — having “untied the tight knot of grasping,” they “long for nothing in the world,” not even for the prestige of being recognized as pure. Verse 8 then makes the discourse’s most precise philosophical statement: the brahmin, “knowing and seeing, adopts nothing,” being “neither in love with passion nor besotted by dispassion.” This closing line is essential to understanding the whole discourse correctly. The teaching is not simply trading attachment to pleasure for attachment to renunciation, or trading a positive claim to purity for a smug claim to have “seen through” purity. Both are still forms of adopting something as “the ultimate” — and the discourse’s closing words rule that move out explicitly: “there is nothing here they adopt as the ultimate.”

Why claims to purity are singled out

It is worth pausing on why this discourse treats purity as its own special case, distinct from the general critique of dogmatic views in the previous discourse. A claim to hold the correct opinion can be merely intellectual. A claim to be pure is a claim about one’s own person — it says, implicitly, that the holder has achieved something others have not, that they are safe from the corruption or confusion others remain caught in. That makes it an especially seductive and especially dangerous kind of view to become attached to, because defending it means defending one’s own worth, not just one’s argument. This is precisely why the discourse spends so much care ruling out every plausible source for that claim — vision, perception, precepts, vows — before landing on a description of purity that isn’t a possession or an achievement at all, just an absence of the grasping that would need such a possession in the first place.

Key terms

suddhi — “purity,” the discourse’s subject: a claim about one’s own spiritual cleanliness or attainment, examined here as another route by which grasping disguises itself.
diṭṭha, suta, muta — “the seen, the heard, the thought (or sensed),” a recurring formula in this vagga naming the raw material of experience that a person might mistakenly treat as grounds for certifying their own purity.
brahmin — used throughout this discourse (and the vagga generally) not as a social or caste designation but as an honorific for one who has fully seen through clinging; a reclaiming of the term on ethical rather than birth-based grounds.
the monkey and the branch — the discourse’s image for a mind that has let go of one grasped thing only to immediately seize the next, describing attachment to the act of grasping itself.
“neither passion nor dispassion” — the discourse’s closing formula, ruling out both ordinary craving and a subtler attachment to renunciation or having “seen through” things as the new ultimate.

The text

All eight verses of the Suddhaṭṭhakasutta, complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1“I see someone pure, perfectly healthy;
it is vision that grants a person purity.”
Recalling this notion of the ultimate,
they believe in the notion
that there is one who observes purity.

Verse 2If a person were granted purity through what is seen,
or if by a notion they could give up suffering,
then one with attachments is purified by another:
their view betrays them as one who asserts thus.

Verse 3The brahmin speaks not of purity from another
in terms of what has been seen, heard, or thought;
or by precepts or vows.
They are unsullied in the midst of good and evil,
letting go what was picked up,
without creating anything new here.

Verse 4Having let go the last they lay hold of the next;
following impulse, they don’t pass the chain.
They grab on and let loose like a monkey
grabbing and releasing a branch.

Verse 5Having undertaken their own vows, a personage
visits various teachers,
being attached to perception.
One who knows, having comprehended the truth
through the knowledges,
does not visit various teachers, being of vast wisdom.

Verse 6They are remote from all things
seen, heard, or thought.
Seeing them living openly,
how could anyone in this world judge them?

Verse 7They don’t make things up or promote them,
or speak of the uttermost purity.
After untying the tight knot of grasping
they long for nothing in the world.

Verse 8The brahmin has stepped over the perimeter;
knowing and seeing, they adopt nothing.
Neither in love with passion
nor besotted by dispassion,
there is nothing here they adopt as the ultimate.

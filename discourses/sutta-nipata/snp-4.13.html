Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 4.13

The Longer Discourse on Arrayed for Battle

Mahāviyūhasutta

Setting
Not stated in the verse itself; no location or occasion is named
Speakers
An unnamed questioner and the Buddha
Form
Continuous philosophical verse, minimal narrative frame — the vagga’s longest and most sustained treatment of its central theme
Length
~5 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A version of much of the Aṭṭhakavagga survives in the Chinese Yizu Jing (義足經, T198), translated in the 3rd century CE, though exact correspondence to this individual sutta is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★★★★ — the longest and most demanding discourse in this set, extending the previous sutta’s argument into new territory before turning to portraiture

Why this discourse, the thirteenth

This is the companion piece to Snp 4.12, twenty verses to the shorter discourse’s seventeen, and it earns the extra length by covering ground the shorter poem doesn’t reach: not just the logic of mutual accusation among rival philosophers, but the specific traps of ethical dogmatism (vow-keepers who tremble at their own lapses), ascetic self-mortification, and the seductive idea that seeing more, or seeing correctly, could itself be the whole of purity. It closes with some of the most quoted lines in the entire Aṭṭhakavagga — the sage who has “untied the knots” and, with “burden put down,” is released: “not formulating, not abstaining, not longing.”

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Debate itself, even when it produces praise rather than blame, is “a small thing, insufficient for peace”; the discourse examines several different routes people take toward claiming purity — through ethics, through austerity, through correct view — and shows each one still caught in the same underlying structure of comparison and defense, before describing the sage who has simply stepped outside that structure altogether.

The land of no dispute

The discourse opens by asking whether those who dispute that their own view is “the only truth” are always merely criticized for it, or whether some also win praise. The Buddha’s answer refuses to treat either outcome as a real prize: praise and blame are “two fruits of dispute,” and neither is “sufficient for peace.” The alternative he names has become one of the discourse’s most quoted phrases — not winning the argument, but “looking for sanctuary in the land of no dispute,” a place reached not by out-arguing one’s opponents but by no longer needing to enter the contest at all.

Ethics as the highest thing, and its cost

The discourse turns first to those who treat ethical precepts and vows as the highest measure of purity, undertaking a vow and sticking to it with the promise “let us train right here, then we will be pure.” The cost of this framing shows itself precisely when the vow is broken: such a person “trembles, having failed in their task,” praying and longing for the purity they believe they have now lost, compared to someone who has “lost their caravan while journeying far from home.” The discourse’s alternative is not laxity but a different relationship to the vow altogether: having given up precepts and vows as the measure of purity — along with the very categories of “blameworthy” and “blameless” used to score them — one lives detached, “fostering peace,” free of both the anxious keeping and the anxious breaking.

Austerity, and seeing as purity

A second route gets the same treatment: those who rely on self-mortification “in disgust of sin,” or on their own private experience of what is seen, heard, or thought, insisting that purification comes only “through heading upstream” against ordinary life — they too remain unfree of craving for future existence, still muttering prayers, still trembling over the ideas they’ve formed. A third route follows: some say “I know, I see, that’s how it is,” believing purity stems from having the correct view. The discourse’s reply is characteristically sharp — if someone has genuinely seen, what further use is the view itself? When a person sees, the text says, they simply see name and form, and gladly let them see much or little of it, for “this is no way to purity” regardless of how much is seen; accumulating correct perception is not what purification actually consists of.

Untying the knots

The closing portrait gathers every thread. The one who has understood “does not get involved with formulating and calculating,” is “not a follower of views, nor kinsman of notions,” and having understood the many different conventions people build their identities around, simply “looks on when others grasp” rather than joining in. “Having untied the knots here in the world, the sage takes no side among disputes” — peaceful among people who are not, equanimous, not grasping even as everyone around them does. Having given up old defilements without generating new ones, not swayed by preference and no proponent of dogma, that person is “released from views, not clinging to the world, nor reproaching themselves” — free, notably, of self-blame as much as of attachment. The final verse names the state directly: remote from everything seen, heard, or thought, “with burden put down, the sage is released: not formulating, not abstaining, not longing.”

Key terms

“the land of no dispute” — the discourse’s image for the alternative to winning or losing an argument — a state reached by no longer needing to enter the contest, not by defeating an opponent within it.
sīlabbata (precepts and vows) — ethical rules and observances treated by some as the highest and sole measure of purity; the discourse shows the anxiety this framing produces when a vow is broken, without recommending abandoning ethics altogether.
“heading upstream” — an image for self-mortification undertaken out of disgust with wrongdoing, presented here as another route to claimed purity that still leaves the practitioner unfree of craving for future lives.
“not a follower of views, nor kinsman of notions” — a description of the sage who has stopped identifying with any fixed position closely enough to defend or transmit it as a possession.
“untied the knots” — the discourse’s central image for liberation from the structure of dispute itself — not resolving every individual argument, but no longer being bound by the need to take a side in any of them.

The text

The discourse in full, all twenty verses. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1“Regarding those who maintain their own view,
disputing that, ‘This is the only truth’:
are all of them subject only to criticism,
or do some also win praise for that?”

Verse 2“That is a small thing, insufficient for peace,
these two fruits of dispute, I say.
Seeing this, one ought not dispute,
looking for sanctuary in the land of no dispute.

Verse 3One who knows does not get involved
with any of the many different conventions.
Why would the uninvolved get involved,
since they do not believe based on the seen or the heard?

Verse 4Those for whom ethics are the highest speak of purity through self-control;
having undertaken a vow, they stick to it:
‘Let us train right here, then we will be pure.’
Professing to be experts, they are led on to future lives.

Verse 5If they fall away from their precepts and vows,
they tremble, having failed in their task.
They pray and long for purity,
like one who has lost their caravan while journeying far from home.

Verse 6But having given up all precepts and vows,
and these deeds blameworthy or blameless;
not longing for ‘purity’ or ‘impurity’,
live detached, fostering peace.

Verse 7Relying on mortification in disgust of sin,
or else on what is seen, heard, or thought,
they moan that purification comes through heading upstream,
not rid of craving for life after life.

Verse 8For one who longs there are muttered prayers,
and trembling too over ideas they have formed.
But one here for whom there is no passing away or reappearing:
why would they tremble? For what would they pray?”

Verse 9“The very same teaching that some say is ‘ultimate’,
others say is inferior.
Which of these statements is true,
for all of them profess to be experts?”

Verse 10“They say their own teaching is perfect,
while the teaching of others is inferior.
So arguing, they dispute,
each saying their own conventions are the truth.

Verse 11If someone else’s disparagement makes you inferior,
no-one in any teaching would be distinguished.
For each of them says the other’s teaching is lacking,
while forcefully advocating their own.

Verse 12But if they honor their own teachings
just as they praise their own journeys,
then all doctrines would be equally valid,
and purity would be specific to them.

Verse 13After judging among the teachings, a brahmin has adopted nothing
that requires interpretation by another.
That’s why they’ve gotten over disputes,
for they see no other doctrine as best.

Verse 14Saying, ‘I know, I see, that’s how it is’,
some believe that purity stems from view.
But if they’ve really seen, what use is that view to them?
Overlooking what matters, they say purity comes from another.

Verse 15When a person sees, they see name and form,
and having seen, they will know just these things.
Gladly let them see much or little,
for those who are skilled say this is no way to purity.

Verse 16It’s not easy to educate someone who is dogmatic,
promoting a view they have formulated.
Speaking of the beauty in that on which they depend,
they talk of purity in accord with what they saw there.

Verse 17The brahmin does not get involved with formulating and calculating;
they’re not followers of views, nor kinsmen of notions.
Having understood the many different conventions,
they look on when others grasp.

Verse 18Having untied the knots here in the world,
the sage takes no side among disputes.
Peaceful among the peaceless, equanimous,
they don’t grasp when others grasp.

Verse 19Having given up former defilements and not making new ones,
not swayed by preference, nor a proponent of dogma,
that attentive one is released from views,
not clinging to the world, nor reproaching themselves.

Verse 20They are remote from all things
seen, heard, or thought.
With burden put down, the sage is released:
not formulating, not abstaining, not longing.”

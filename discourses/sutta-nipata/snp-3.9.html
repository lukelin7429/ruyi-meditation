Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 3.9

With Vāseṭṭha

Vāseṭṭhasutta

Setting
A forest near Icchānaṅgala, home at the time to several well-known and well-to-do brahmins, including Caṅkī, Tārukkha, Pokkharasāti, Jānussoṇi, and Todeyya
Speakers
The Buddha; the brahmin students Vāseṭṭha and Bhāradvāja, out walking together
Form
A short prose frame, then one long, sustained verse discourse — a taxonomy of species, an inventory of occupations, and a repeated refrain naming what actually makes a brahmin
Length
~11 minutes to read
Parallel
Preserved almost word for word in the Majjhima Nikāya as MN 98, also titled the Vāseṭṭhasutta
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — repetitive by design, and all the more forceful for it

Why this discourse

Two brahmin students, walking together, can’t agree on the single most consequential question their whole social world was built on: does birth make a brahmin, or does conduct? Rather than settle it themselves, they bring the dispute to the Buddha — and get an answer far more thorough than either expected. Working outward from farm animals and insects to snakes, fish, and birds, the Buddha builds a careful argument that the very idea of birth-determined caste, real enough among other species, simply doesn’t hold for human beings. What follows is one of the longest and most sustained ethical arguments in the entire Sutta Nipāta — and its closing catalog of what “a brahmin” actually means, verse after verse, reads as a close companion to the Dhammapada’s own closing chapter on the same subject, already on this site.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Species like plants, insects, and animals really are distinguished from one another by birth — but human beings are not; what actually makes someone “a brahmin” is entirely a matter of conduct, and the sutta spends dozens of verses spelling out exactly what that conduct looks like.

Two students, one dispute

Vāseṭṭha and Bhāradvāja, out walking, fall into an argument neither can resolve: Bhāradvāja holds the conventional view, that being a brahmin depends on unbroken, “irrefutable and impeccable” lineage back seven generations on both parents’ sides. Vāseṭṭha holds the opposite: that ethical conduct and diligence in one’s duties is what makes a brahmin. Since “neither was able to persuade the other,” and since both are, notably, already “authorized masters of the three Vedas” in their own right — this isn’t a dispute between the ignorant and the learned, but between two well-trained brahmins who simply disagree — they decide to bring the question to the ascetic Gotama, whose growing reputation has reached even their circle.

Species have castes; humans don’t

The Buddha’s answer begins from an unexpected direction: a survey of the natural world. Grasses and trees, insects, quadrupeds, snakes, fish, and birds — each, he says, really is “defined by birth,” because “species are indeed diverse.” Then comes the pivot the whole sutta turns on: “while the differences between these species are defined by birth, the differences between humans are not defined by birth.” What follows is a remarkably exhaustive catalog of human physical features — hair, head, ear, eye, mouth, nose, lips, eyebrow, shoulder, neck, belly, back, and on through the whole body — concluding that none of these mark out separate human “species” the way they do for animals. “The distinctions among humans are spoken of by convention,” not nature.

Occupation, not birth

Having dismantled birth as a biological category among humans, the Buddha turns to occupation, running through farmer, professional, trader, servant, bandit, warrior, sacrificer, and ruler — each defined strictly by what someone actually does for a living, with the refrain “not a brahmin” hammered home after every single one. Even priestly ritual work (“lives off priesthood”) doesn’t automatically make someone a brahmin in this account — a direct challenge to the assumption that ritual specialists inherit their status.

The refrain: “that’s who I declare a brahmin”

The sutta’s longest section is a sustained catalog of some thirty verses, each ending in the same refrain: “that’s who I declare a brahmin.” The qualities named are consistent with the ethical vision found throughout the early texts — freedom from attachment, patience under abuse, non-violence toward “creatures firm and frail,” truthful and inoffensive speech, freedom from theft, freedom from hope for future rebirth, and direct knowledge of “the passing away and rebirth of all beings.” Several verses here — almost word for word — also appear as standalone verses in the Dhammapada’s own closing chapter, “Brahmins” (Dhp 26), which draws on the same repertoire of brahmin-redefinition verses circulating in the early tradition.

Deeds make the world go on

The discourse closes its argument with a compressed statement of cause and effect: “deeds make the world go on, deeds make people go on; sentient beings are bound by deeds, like a moving chariot’s linchpin.” The phrase “seeing dependent origination, they’re expert in deeds and their results” explicitly ties this teaching on brahmin-hood back to the broader doctrine of kamma and paticcasamuppāda running through the whole canon. The final verses name the “supreme brahmin” as one accomplished in “the three knowledges,” at peace, with rebirth ended.

Two students go for refuge

The sutta ends almost tersely, in a formula abbreviated in the source text itself (“Excellent, worthy Gotama! Excellent! …”): Vāseṭṭha and Bhāradvāja both go for refuge as lay followers for life. Notably, the text never says which of the two students “won” their original argument — because by the end, the Buddha’s answer has reframed the dispute entirely rather than simply taking one side.

Key terms

jāti — birth or species — the category the Buddha argues genuinely distinguishes plant and animal species from one another, but not human beings from each other.
“that’s who I declare a brahmin” — the sutta’s extended refrain, repeated after dozens of verses describing ethical qualities rather than ancestry.
MN 98 — the Majjhima Nikāya’s parallel version of this same debate, textually very close to the Sutta Nipāta’s account.
Dhp 26 “Brahmins” — the Dhammapada’s closing chapter, already on this site, which shares much of this sutta’s repertoire of brahmin-redefinition verses.
paticcasamuppāda — dependent origination, invoked explicitly near the sutta’s close as the framework for understanding “deeds and their results.”

The text

The discourse in full: the students’ dispute, the taxonomy of species, the inventory of occupations, and the full catalog of verses on what makes a brahmin. This is one of the longest suttas in the collection, reproduced complete without abridgment. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Two students, one dispute

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in a forest near Icchānaṅgala. Now at that time several very well-known well-to-do brahmins were residing in Icchānaṅgala. They included the brahmins Caṅkī, Tārukkha, Pokkharasāti, Jānussoṇi, Todeyya, and others. Then as the students Vāseṭṭha and Bhāradvāja were going for a walk they began to discuss the question: “How do you become a brahmin?”

§2–3Bhāradvāja said this: “When you’re well born on both your mother’s and father’s side, stemming from a clean vessel, with irrefutable and impeccable genealogy back to the seventh paternal generation— then you’re a brahmin.” Vāseṭṭha said this: “When you’re ethical and accomplished in doing your duties— then you’re a brahmin.” But neither was able to persuade the other.

Bringing the question to the Buddha

§4So Vāseṭṭha said to Bhāradvāja, “Worthy Bhāradvāja, the ascetic Gotama—a Sakyan, gone forth from a Sakyan family—is staying in a forest near Icchānaṅgala. He has this good reputation: ‘That Blessed One is perfected, a fully awakened Buddha, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, holy, knower of the world, supreme guide for those fit for training, teacher of gods and humans, awakened, blessed.’ Come, let’s go to see him and ask him about this matter. As he answers, so we’ll remember it.” “Yes, worthy sir,” replied Bhāradvāja.

§5So they went to the Buddha and exchanged greetings with him. When the greetings and polite conversation were over, they sat down to one side, and Vāseṭṭha addressed the Buddha in verse:

§6–7“We’re both authorized masters
of the three Vedas.
I’m a student of Pokkharasāti,
and he of Tārukkha.
We’re fully qualified
in all the Vedic experts teach.
As philologists and grammarians,
we match our tutors in recitation.

§8–9We have a dispute
regarding genealogy.
For Bhāradvāja says that
one is a brahmin due to birth,
but I declare it’s because of one’s deeds.
Oh Clear-eyed One, know this as our debate.
Since neither of us was able
to convince the other,
we’ve come to ask you, sir,
renowned as the awakened one.

§10–11As people honor with cupped palms
the moon on the cusp of waxing,
bowing, they revere
Gotama in the world.
We ask this of Gotama,
the Eye arisen in the world:
is one a brahmin due to birth,
or else because of deeds?
We don’t know, please tell us,
so we can recognize a brahmin.”

Species have castes; humans don’t

§12–13“I shall explain to you,”
said the Buddha,
“accurately and in sequence,
the taxonomy of living creatures,
for species are indeed diverse.
Know the grass and trees,
though they lack self-awareness.
They’re defined by birth,
for species are indeed diverse.

§14–15Next there are bugs that crawl or fly,
and so on, to ants and termites.
They’re defined by birth,
for species are indeed diverse.
Know the quadrupeds, too,
both small and large.
They’re defined by birth,
for species are indeed diverse.

§16–17Know, too, the long-backed snakes,
crawling on their bellies.
They’re defined by birth,
for species are indeed diverse.
Next know the fish,
whose habitat is the water.
They’re defined by birth,
for species are indeed diverse.

§18–19Next know the birds,
winged chariots in flight.
They’re defined by birth,
for species are indeed diverse.
While the differences between these species
are defined by birth,
the differences between humans
are not defined by birth.

§20–21Not by hair nor by head,
not by ear nor by eye,
not by mouth nor by nose,
not by lips nor by eyebrow,
not by shoulder nor by neck,
not by belly nor by back,
not by buttocks nor by breast,
not by groin nor by genitals,

§22–23not by hands nor by feet,
not by fingers nor by nails,
not by knees nor by thighs,
not by color nor by voice:
none of these are defined by birth
as it is for other species.
In specific human bodies
you can’t find such distinctions.
The distinctions among humans
are spoken of by convention.

Occupation, not birth

§24–25Anyone among humans
who lives off keeping cattle:
know them, Vāseṭṭha,
as a farmer, not a brahmin.
Anyone among humans
who lives off various professions:
know them, Vāseṭṭha,
as a professional, not a brahmin.

§26–27Anyone among humans
who lives off trade:
know them, Vāseṭṭha,
as a trader, not a brahmin.
Anyone among humans
who lives off serving others:
know them, Vāseṭṭha,
as a servant, not a brahmin.

§28–29Anyone among humans
who lives off stealing:
know them, Vāseṭṭha,
as a bandit, not a brahmin.
Anyone among humans
who lives off archery:
know them, Vāseṭṭha,
as a warrior, not a brahmin.

§30–31Anyone among humans
who lives off priesthood:
know them, Vāseṭṭha,
as a sacrificer, not a brahmin.
Anyone among humans
who taxes village and nation,
know them, Vāseṭṭha,
as a ruler, not a brahmin.

The refrain: “that’s who I declare a brahmin”

§32–33I don’t call someone a brahmin
after the mother’s womb they’re born from.
If they still have attachments,
they’re just someone who says ‘worthy’.
Having nothing, taking nothing:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.
Having cut off all fetters
they have no anxiety;
they’ve slipped their chains and are detached:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

§34–35They’ve cut the strap and harness,
the halter and bridle too;
with cross-bar lifted, they’re awakened:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.
Abuse, killing, caging:
they endure these without anger.
Patience is their powerful army:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

§36–37Not irritable or pretentious,
dutiful in precepts and observances,
tamed, bearing their final body:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.
Like water from a lotus leaf,
like a mustard seed off a pin-point,
sensual pleasures slip off them:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

§38–39They understand for themselves
the end of suffering in this life;
with burden put down, detached:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.
Deep in wisdom, intelligent,
expert in the path and what is not the path;
arrived at the highest goal:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

§40–41Mixing with neither
householders nor the homeless;
a migrant with no bastion, few in wishes:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.
They’ve laid aside violence
against creatures firm and frail;
not killing or making others kill:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

§42–43Not fighting among those who fight,
quenched among those who have taken up arms,
not grasping among those who grasp:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.
They’ve discarded greed and hate,
along with conceit and contempt,
like a mustard seed off the point of a pin:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

§44–45The words they utter
are polished, informative, and true,
and don’t offend anyone:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.
They don’t steal anything in the world,
long or short,
fine or coarse, beautiful or ugly:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

§46–47They have no hope
for this world or the next;
with no need for hope, detached:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.
They have no clinging,
knowledge has freed them of indecision,
they’ve arrived at the objective, freedom from death:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

§48–49They’ve escaped clinging
to both good and bad deeds;
sorrowless, stainless, pure:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.
Pure as the spotless moon,
clear and undisturbed,
they’ve ended relish for rebirth:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

§50–51They’ve got past this grueling swamp
of delusion, transmigration.
Meditating in stillness, free of indecision,
they have crossed over to the far shore.
They’re quenched by not grasping:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.
They’ve given up sensual stimulations,
and have gone forth from lay life;
they’ve ended rebirth in the sensual realm:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

§52–53They’ve given up craving,
and have gone forth from lay life;
they’ve ended craving to be reborn:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.
They’ve thrown off the human yoke,
and slipped out of the heavenly yoke;
unyoked from all yokes:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

§54–55Giving up desire and discontent,
they’re cooled and free of attachments;
a hero, master of the whole world:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.
They know the passing away
and rebirth of all beings;
unattached, holy, awakened:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

§56–57Gods, centaurs, and humans
don’t know their destiny;
the perfected ones with defilements ended:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.
They have nothing before or after,
or even in between.
Having nothing, taking nothing:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

§58–59Captain of the herd, excellent hero,
great seer and victor;
unstirred, washed, awakened:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.
They who know their past lives,
see heaven and places of loss,
and have attained the ending of rebirth,
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

Deeds make the world go on

§60–61For name and clan are formulated
as mere convention in the world.
Produced by mutual agreement,
they’re formulated in each case.
For a long time this misconception
has prejudiced those who don’t understand.
Ignorant, they declare
that one is a brahmin by birth.

§62–63You’re not a brahmin by birth,
nor by birth a non-brahmin.
You’re a brahmin by your deeds,
and by deeds a non-brahmin.
You’re a farmer by your deeds,
by deeds you’re a professional;
you’re a trader by your deeds,
by deeds are you a servant;

§64–65you’re a bandit by your deeds,
by deeds you’re a warrior;
you’re a sacrificer by your deeds,
by deeds you’re a ruler.
In this way the astute regard deeds
in accord with truth.
Seeing dependent origination,
they’re expert in deeds and their results.

§66–67Deeds make the world go on,
deeds make people go on;
sentient beings are bound by deeds,
like a moving chariot’s linchpin.
By fervor and spiritual practice,
by restraint and by self-control:
that’s how to become a brahmin,
this is the supreme brahmin.

§68Accomplished in the three knowledges,
peaceful, with rebirth ended,
know them, Vāseṭṭha,
as the Divinity and Sakka to the wise.”

Two students go for refuge

§69When he had spoken, Vāseṭṭha and Bhāradvāja said to him, “Excellent, worthy Gotama! Excellent! … From this day forth, may the worthy Gotama remember us as lay followers who have gone for refuge for life.”

Eight on Malice

Why this discourse, the third of the Aṭṭhakavagga

This is the discourse where the Aṭṭhakavagga’s real subject comes into focus. Having spent two discourses on craving for sense objects and possessions, it now turns to a subtler and, in some ways, more stubborn form of the same clinging: attachment to one’s own opinions. The title names its target precisely — not disagreement itself, which is unavoidable, but duṭṭha, malice or corrupted intent, the specific ugliness that creeps into a dispute once a person has staked their sense of self on being the one who is right. The discourse’s answer is not a better argument. It is a description of a mind that has stopped needing to win at all.

Reading guide

The discourse in one sentence

A person who has built an identity around a fixed view cannot help but treat every dispute as personal, because losing the argument would mean losing themselves — while a person who has never planted that flag in the first place has, quite literally, nothing to defend and nothing anyone can dispute with.

Verse 1: two ways of speaking, and a sage's third option

The discourse opens by distinguishing “some” who “speak with malicious intent” from others who “speak set on truth” — a distinction worth pausing on, because it means the sutta is not simply attacking the idea of caring about truth. What it goes on to attack is something more specific: the reflex that turns a disagreement into a fight. “When disputes come up a sage does not get involved,” the verse says, and the reason given is unusual — “which is why they’ve no barrenness at all.” Barrenness (a dry, exhausted, argued-out state) is what disputing produces, win or lose. The sage’s non-involvement is presented not as timidity but as simply declining an activity that reliably wears a person down without producing anything of lasting value.

Verses 2–4: how a view becomes a possession

Verse 2 asks the discourse’s hardest question directly: “how can you transcend your own view when you’re led by preference, dogmatic in belief?” The word order matters — preference comes first, and the position follows from it, rather than the position being arrived at and then, incidentally, preferred. Verses 3 and 4 then draw the discourse’s central contrast. Someone who, unasked, volunteers “their own precepts and vows” to others is said to have “an ignoble nature” — not because their precepts are wrong, but because the act of announcing them unprompted reveals that identity is riding on the announcement. A mendicant who is genuinely “peaceful, quenched,” by contrast, “never boasts ‘thus am I’ of their precepts,” having “no pretensions about anything in the world.” The test the discourse proposes, in other words, isn’t what a person believes but whether they need anyone else to know it.

Verses 5–6: the trap inside “winning”

Verse 5 describes someone who “formulates and creates teachings” and “promotes them despite their defects” because they “see an advantage for themselves” in doing so — and the verse’s verdict is sharp: such a person “become[s] dependent on that, relying on unstable peace.” This is the discourse’s most important technical point, easy to miss on a first reading. A view that has been adopted for the psychological comfort it provides can feel like peace — the settled, confident feeling of having things figured out — but that peace is unstable precisely because it depends on the view continuing to be defended successfully. Verse 6 generalizes the pattern: “it’s not easy to get over dogmatic views adopted after judging among the teachings,” which is why people caught in this cycle simply “ditch[] one teaching and take[] up another” rather than stepping outside the whole mechanism of needing a position to stand on.

Verses 7–8: the one who has “shaken off all views”

The closing pair describes the alternative concretely. “The cleansed one has no formulated view at all in the world about the different realms” — not because they have failed to think about such questions, but because “having given up illusion and conceit,” there is no longer a “self” that a view could belong to and need protecting. The final verse states the discourse’s logical payoff directly: “for one who gets involved is embroiled in disputes about teachings — but how to dispute with the uninvolved? About what?” A dispute requires two positions in contact; someone who is not holding a position to defend has removed one side of the equation, not through evasion but because “picking up and putting down is not what they do — they have shaken off all views in this very life.”

Doesn’t “no views” just become another view?

The obvious objection is worth taking seriously, since it is the same objection every reader eventually raises: isn’t “you should hold no fixed views” itself a fixed view, and doesn’t that make the whole teaching self-defeating? The discourse’s own language points toward its answer. It never says correct perception, careful discernment, or ethical clarity should be abandoned — verse 1 explicitly allows for “speaking set on truth.” What it targets is a particular relationship to a position: adopting it as something to be defended, to win praise through, to derive identity from, to feel superior by holding. “Shaken off all views” describes releasing that grip, not refusing to think, form working judgments, or act with discernment. A person can walk carefully around a hazard, as Snp 4.1 put it, without needing anyone else to agree that the hazard is there. What the Aṭṭhakavagga is diagnosing is not thinking itself but the specific, corrosive way thinking curdles into identity — and it is precisely that curdling, not disagreement as such, that produces the malice named in this discourse’s title.

Key terms

duṭṭha — “malicious,” “corrupted”: the discourse’s title-word, naming the ugliness that enters a dispute once identity is at stake in it, not disagreement or truth-seeking as such.
diṭṭhi — “view” or “opinion,” especially in the sense of a fixed philosophical or doctrinal position adopted and defended; the discourse’s central object of analysis.
“thus am I” — the boast the peaceful mendicant of verse 4 never makes about their own precepts; identifying a practice or belief as evidence of who one is, rather than simply doing it.
“unstable peace” — verse 5’s description of the settled feeling a defended view can provide, named unstable because it depends on the view continuing to be successfully defended.
“shaken off all views” — the discourse’s closing description of the cleansed mind; not an absence of discernment, but the release of the grip that turns a position into something to defend.

The text

All eight verses of the Duṭṭhaṭṭhakasutta, complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1Some speak with malicious intent,
while others speak set on truth.
When disputes come up a sage does not get involved,
which is why they’ve no barrenness at all.

Verse 2How can you transcend your own view
when you’re led by preference, dogmatic in belief?
Inventing your own undertakings,
you’d speak according to your notion.

Verse 3Some personages, unasked, tell others
of their own precepts and vows.
They have an ignoble nature,
say those who are skilled,
since they speak on themselves of their own accord.

Verse 4A mendicant, peaceful, quenched,
never boasts “thus am I” of their precepts.
They have a noble nature, say those who are skilled,
who have no pretensions about anything in the world.

Verse 5For one who formulates and creates teachings,
and promotes them despite their defects,
if they see an advantage for themselves,
they become dependent on that,
relying on unstable peace.

Verse 6It’s not easy to get over dogmatic views
adopted after judging among the teachings.
That’s why, among all these dogmas, a person
ditches one teaching and takes up another.

Verse 7The cleansed one has no formulated view
at all in the world about the different realms.
Having given up illusion and conceit,
by what path would they go? They are not involved.

Verse 8For one who gets involved is embroiled in disputes about teachings—
but how to dispute with the uninvolved?
About what?
For picking up and putting down
is not what they do;
they have shaken off all views in this very life.

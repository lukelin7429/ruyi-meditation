Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 2.4

Blessings

Maṅgala Sutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery, late at night
Speakers
An unnamed, glorious deity who lights up the entire grove, and the Buddha
Form
A brief prose frame, then a verse dialogue — the deity’s one-stanza question, and the Buddha’s ten-stanza answer naming specific blessings in progressive order, closing with a summary stanza
Length
~3–4 minutes to read
Status
One of the single most famous and widely memorized texts in the Buddhist canon — traditionally reckoned as describing thirty-eight blessings, chanted throughout the Theravada world alongside the Ratana Sutta (Snp 2.1) and the Mettā Sutta (Snp 1.8)
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — plainly stated, though its progression rewards close attention

Why this discourse

A deity comes to the Buddha at night with a question that, on its face, sounds like it belongs to superstition: what is the “highest blessing”? In the culture this discourse comes from, maṅgala usually meant an auspicious sign or a lucky omen — the kind of thing people consulted before a journey or a marriage. The Buddha’s answer refuses that frame entirely. Instead of an omen, he gives thirteen stanzas of concrete, actionable conduct — who to associate with, how to treat your parents, what to avoid drinking, how to hold your mind when praised or blamed — arranged so that the list itself becomes a kind of path, moving from the most basic social choices toward a mind no longer shaken by anything at all. Few texts in the canon compress so much practical ethics into so short and memorable a space.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Asked to name the highest blessing, the Buddha answers not with a lucky sign but with a progressively arranged sequence of ethical, relational, and spiritual conduct — culminating not in prosperity but in a mind that worldly ups and downs can no longer shake.

A deity’s question, at night, in Jeta’s Grove

The frame is brief but specific: at Sāvatthī, in Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery, late at night, “a glorious deity, lighting up the entire Jeta’s Grove,” approaches the Buddha, bows, and stands to one side before addressing him in verse. The question itself notes that it is a shared human preoccupation, not a private curiosity: “Many gods and humans have thought about blessings, desiring well-being: declare the highest blessing.” The Buddha’s reply, uninterrupted, runs the rest of the discourse.

The first principle: choose your company

The very first blessing named sets the tone for everything that follows: “not to fraternize with fools, but to fraternize with the wise, and honoring those worthy of honor.” Before anything about ethics, family, or meditation, the sutta places the choice of who you associate with — an idea that echoes the warning against false friends in the previous discourse of this vagga (Snp 2.3). The blessings that follow can be read, in a sense, as an unpacking of what “the wise” actually value.

The blessings of ordinary life

The next several stanzas stay resolutely down to earth. “Living in a suitable region,” having “made merit in the past,” and being “rightly resolved in oneself” open the list; then education, a craft, discipline and training, and “well-spoken speech” — the practical equipment for a functioning life. From there the sutta turns to family and livelihood: “caring for mother and father, kindness to children and partners, and unstressful work”; then to active virtue: “giving and righteous conduct, kindness to relatives, blameless deeds”; and then to restraint: “desisting and abstaining from evil, avoiding drinking liquor, diligence in good qualities.” Nothing here requires renunciation. It is a vision of an ordinary life, well lived.

The blessings of Dhamma practice

Only after establishing this ordinary foundation does the sutta turn explicitly toward spiritual cultivation. “Respect and placidity, contentment and gratitude, and timely listening to the teaching” opens the shift; “patience, being easy to admonish, the sight of ascetics, and timely discussion of the teaching” follows — notably including the capacity to be corrected (“easy to admonish”) as a named blessing in its own right, a virtue that requires the very quality of honest companionship the sutta’s first line already pointed toward. The list then names the deepest attainments directly: “fervor and chastity, seeing the noble truths, and realization of extinguishment” — nibbāna itself, named without euphemism as one of the “highest blessings.”

The culmination: an unshaken mind

The sutta’s final substantive stanza is its most quoted: “though touched by worldly things, their mind does not tremble; sorrowless, stainless, secure — this is the highest blessing.” After twelve stanzas describing what to do — whom to befriend, how to treat parents, what to avoid, what to study — the sequence ends by describing a state of being: someone still fully in contact with the world’s praise and blame, gain and loss, pleasure and pain, whose composure nothing in that list can disturb. The closing stanza then folds every preceding blessing into a single claim: “having completed these things, undefeated everywhere; everywhere they go in safety: this is their highest blessing.”

Redefining the question itself

Read end to end, the sutta’s deepest move may be its redefinition of the very word the deity used. A maṅgala was, in the culture around this text, typically an outward sign — consulted, not cultivated. The Buddha’s answer relocates the entire category: every “blessing” he names is something a person does or becomes, arranged in a sequence that starts with a simple social choice and ends in an unshakeable mind. Nothing here is granted by chance or read off an omen; every item on the list is something within reach of ordinary effort, however far down the list it falls.

Key terms

maṅgala — “blessing,” but also, in wider usage, an auspicious sign or lucky omen; the sutta’s central term, redefined across its thirteen stanzas as a matter of conduct and character rather than fortune.
the thirty-eight blessings — the traditional count of individual blessings named across the Buddha’s ten stanzas of reply, from avoiding bad company through to the realization of extinguishment.
“easy to admonish” — named directly as a blessing in its own right: the capacity to accept correction, paired in the same stanza with patience.
“touched by worldly things, their mind does not tremble” — the sutta’s culminating description, an unshaken mind amid the ordinary vicissitudes of gain and loss, praise and blame.

The text

Complete: the prose frame, the deity’s question, and all ten stanzas of the Buddha’s answer with its closing summary. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A deity’s question, at night, in Jeta’s Grove

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Then, late at night, a glorious deity, lighting up the entire Jeta’s Grove, went up to the Buddha, bowed, and stood to one side. Standing to one side, that deity addressed the Buddha in verse:

Verse 1“Many gods and humans
have thought about blessings
desiring well-being:
declare the highest blessing.”

The first principle: choose your company

Verse 2“Not to fraternize with fools,
but to fraternize with the wise,
and honoring those worthy of honor:
this is the highest blessing.

The blessings of ordinary life

Verse 3Living in a suitable region,
having made merit in the past,
being rightly resolved in oneself,
this is the highest blessing.

Verse 4Education and a craft,
discipline and training,
and well-spoken speech:
this is the highest blessing.

Verse 5Caring for mother and father,
kindness to children and partners,
and unstressful work:
this is the highest blessing.

Verse 6Giving and righteous conduct,
kindness to relatives,
blameless deeds:
this is the highest blessing.

Verse 7Desisting and abstaining from evil,
avoiding drinking liquor,
diligence in good qualities:
this is the highest blessing.

The blessings of Dhamma practice

Verse 8Respect and placidity,
contentment and gratitude,
and timely listening to the teaching:
this is the highest blessing.

Verse 9Patience, being easy to admonish,
the sight of ascetics,
and timely discussion of the teaching:
this is the highest blessing.

Verse 10Fervor and chastity
seeing the noble truths,
and realization of extinguishment:
this is the highest blessing.

The culmination: an unshaken mind

Verse 11Though touched by worldly things,
their mind does not tremble;
sorrowless, stainless, secure,
this is the highest blessing.

Verse 12Having completed these things,
undefeated everywhere;
everywhere they go in safety:
this is their highest blessing.”

Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 2.12

Vaṅgīsa and His Mentor Nigrodhakappa

Nigrodhakappasutta

Setting
Near Āḷavī, at Āḷavī’s premier shrine, shortly after the death of the senior monk Nigrodhakappa
Speakers
The Buddha and Venerable Vaṅgīsa, mourning and questioning his own recently deceased mentor
Form
A brief prose frame, Vaṅgīsa’s twelve-verse plea for an answer, the Buddha’s four-line reply, and three closing verses of confirmation
Length
~4 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — emotionally direct, though its many honorific epithets for the Buddha reward slower reading

Why this discourse, the twelfth

Vaṅgīsa and His Mentor Nigrodhakappa is one of the most personal discourses in this chapter: a monk cannot rest with an unanswered question about whether his own teacher truly reached the end of the path, and spends twelve verses all but pleading with the Buddha to say so. The Buddha's answer, when it finally arrives, is four lines long — and it is enough.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The poet-monk Vaṅgīsa, grieving his teacher’s recent death, asks the Buddha in an extended, urgent verse plea whether that teacher attained final liberation, and the Buddha confirms in a few precise lines that he did — a discourse less about doctrine than about the raw, specific ache of not knowing what became of someone you loved.

A student’s private doubt

The discourse opens in prose, at Āḷavī’s premier shrine, not long after the senior monk Nigrodhakappa — Venerable Vaṅgīsa’s own mentor — had “become fully quenched,” the standard phrase for the death of someone who has reached final liberation. But quenched, precisely, is what Vaṅgīsa cannot be sure of. In private retreat the thought surfaces on its own: “Has my mentor become quenched or not?” He does not sit with the uncertainty; he goes directly to the Buddha, reports the very thought that arose, and then rises, arranges his robe, raises his cupped palms, and asks in verse — a shift in register the text marks explicitly, moving from a monk’s ordinary report into formal, urgent poetry.

Vaṅgīsa’s plea

What follows is one of the longest, most insistent requests for an answer anywhere in the early texts — twelve verses that circle the same question from every angle before ever letting the Buddha speak. Vaṅgīsa names his teacher precisely (“Nigrodhakappa was his name; it was given to that brahmin by you, Blessed One”), states the stakes in cosmic terms (“if no man were ever to disperse corruptions, like the wind dispersing the stormclouds, darkness would shroud the whole world”), and then, remarkably, coaches the Buddha on delivery: “swiftly send forth your sweet, sweet voice, like a goose stretching its neck, gently honking, lucid-flowing, with lovely tone.” The plea builds to open admission of need — “as a man baking in the summer sun would long for water, I long for your voice, so let the sound rain forth” — before finally asking outright: “Did he realize quenching, or did he still have a residue? Let us hear what kind of liberation he had!”

The Buddha’s answer

Against twelve verses of build-up, the answer is startlingly brief: “‘He cut off craving for name and form right here,’ said the Buddha, ‘the river of darkness that had long lain within him. He has entirely crossed over birth and death.’” No further elaboration is offered or apparently needed — four short lines settle what twelve verses of anxious poetry could not.

Confirmed

Vaṅgīsa’s closing three verses are relief rendered in real time: “Now that I have heard your words, seventh of seers, I am confident. My question, it seems, was not in vain, the brahmin did not deceive me.” He restates what has been confirmed — “he cut the net of death the deceiver, so extended and strong” — and closes on the plainest possible image of a hard passage completed: Nigrodhakappa “has indeed gone far beyond Death’s dominion so hard to pass.” The discourse ends not on doctrine but on a grieving student’s doubt actually, finally, put to rest.

Key terms

“fully quenched” — the standard description of a fully awakened person’s death, the very status Vaṅgīsa cannot be certain his teacher reached.
Nigrodhakappa / Kappāyana — Vaṅgīsa’s own teacher, a former brahmin, named by the Buddha himself (per verse 3) and referred to by both names across the poem.
“seventh of seers” — Vaṅgīsa’s epithet for the Buddha in verse 15, counting him as the seventh in a line of past Buddhas recognized by the tradition.
the goose and the sun-baked man — two of Vaṅgīsa’s similes coaxing the Buddha to answer (verses 9 and 12), asking for a “sweet, sweet voice” the way a man baking in summer sun longs for water.
“the river of darkness” — the Buddha’s own image, in his four-line answer, for the craving Nigrodhakappa is said to have cut off “right here.”

The text

The discourse in full — a prose opening, Vaṅgīsa’s extended plea, the Buddha’s brief answer, and Vaṅgīsa’s closing confirmation. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A student’s private doubt

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Āḷavī, at Āḷavī’s premier shrine. Now at that time it was not long after Venerable Vaṅgīsa’s mentor, the senior monk named Nigrodhakappa, had become fully quenched. Then as Vaṅgīsa was in private retreat this thought came to his mind: “Has my mentor become quenched or not?”

Then in the late afternoon, Venerable Vaṅgīsa came out of retreat and went to the Buddha. He bowed, sat down to one side, and said to him: “Just now, sir, as I was in private retreat this thought came to mind. ‘Has my mentor become quenched or not?’” Then Venerable Vaṅgīsa rose from his seat, arranged his robe over one shoulder, raised his cupped palms toward the Buddha, and addressed him in verse:

Vaṅgīsa’s plea

Verse 2“I ask the teacher unrivaled in wisdom,
who has cut off all doubts in this very life:
a monk has died at Āḷavī’s premier shrine,
who was well-known, famous, and quenched.

Verse 3Nigrodhakappa was his name;
it was given to that brahmin by you, Blessed One.
He wandered in your honor, yearning for freedom,
energetic, a resolute Seer of Truth.

Verse 4O Sakyan, All-seer,
we all wish to know about that disciple.
Our ears are eager to hear,
for you are the most excellent teacher.

Verse 5Cut off our doubt, declare this to us;
your wisdom is vast, tell us of his quenching!
All-seer, speak among us,
like the thousand-eyed Sakka in the midst of the gods!

Verse 6Whatever ties there are, or paths to delusion,
or things on the side of unknowing, or that are bases of doubt
vanish on reaching a Realized One,
for his eye is the best of all people’s.

Verse 7If no man were ever to disperse corruptions,
like the wind dispersing the stormclouds,
darkness would shroud the whole world;
not even brilliant men would shine.

Verse 8The attentive are bringers of light;
my hero, that is what I think of you.
We’ve come for your discernment and knowledge:
here in this assembly, declare to us about Kappāyana.

Verse 9Swiftly send forth your sweet, sweet voice,
like a goose stretching its neck, gently honking,
lucid-flowing, with lovely tone:
alert, we all listen to you.

Verse 10You have entirely abandoned birth and death;
restrained and pure, I urge you to speak the Dhamma!
For while ordinary people have no wish-granter,
Realized Ones have a comprehensibility-granter.

Verse 11Your answer is definitive, and we will adopt it,
for you have perfect understanding.
We raise our cupped palms one last time,
one of unrivaled wisdom, don’t deliberately confuse us.

Verse 12Knowing the noble teaching from top to bottom,
unrivaled hero, don’t deliberately confuse us.
As a man baking in the summer sun would long for water,
I long for your voice, so let the sound rain forth.

Verse 13Surely Kappāyana did not lead the spiritual life in vain?
Did he realize quenching,
or did he still have a residue?
Let us hear what kind of liberation he had!”

The Buddha’s answer

Verse 14“He cut off craving for name and form right here,” said the Buddha, “the river of darkness that had long lain within him. He has entirely crossed over birth and death.” So declared the Blessed One, the leader of the five.

Confirmed

Verse 15“Now that I have heard your words,
seventh of seers, I am confident.
My question, it seems, was not in vain,
the brahmin did not deceive me.

Verse 16As he said, so he did—
he was a disciple of the Buddha.
He cut the net of death the deceiver,
so extended and strong.

Verse 17Blessed One, Kappāyana saw
the starting point of grasping.
He has indeed gone far beyond
Death’s dominion so hard to pass.”

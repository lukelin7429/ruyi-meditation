Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 2.14

With Dhammika

Dhammikasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speakers
The Buddha and the lay follower Dhammika, accompanied by five hundred lay followers
Form
A prose opening, Dhammika’s nine-verse praise and question, and a full answer in two parts — instructions for mendicants, then for householders
Length
~6 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the longest and most systematic discourse in this run, closing Vagga 2

Why this discourse, the fourteenth

With Dhammika closes Vagga 2 by widening its lens all the way out: a layman arrives with five hundred companions and asks, in one breath, what makes a good disciple whether one has left home or not — and receives, in answer, the fullest statement of both a monastic code and a householder’s code, five precepts and observance day included, found anywhere in this chapter.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The layman Dhammika, with five hundred lay followers behind him, asks what makes a good disciple — both the one who has left home and the one who stays — and receives two complete, separate sets of instructions in return: a monastic code of conduct, and a householder’s code built on the five precepts and the observance-day practice, closing the whole of Vagga 2.

The setting: a lay follower’s question

The discourse opens in prose, near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove: the lay follower Dhammika, “together with five hundred lay followers,” approaches the Buddha and asks in verse “what does one do to become a good disciple, both one who has left the home, and the lay followers staying at home?” The question itself is unusual in scope — most discourses address one audience or the other, but Dhammika asks for both at once, and the size of his company (five hundred) signals this is not a private conversation.

Dhammika’s praise

Before the question is even finished, Dhammika spends nine verses building the case for why the Buddha is worth asking. He recalls that “the dragon king Erāvaṇa” and “King Kuvera Vessavaṇa” both once came to consult the Buddha and left consoled; he claims that debaters among “other religions… whether Ājīvakas or Jains” and senior brahmins alike “all fail to overtake you in wisdom, like a standing man next to a sprinter”; and he closes by noting that both “mendicants” and “layfolk” have gathered together to listen, “like gods listening to the fine words of Vāsava.”

Addressed first to the mendicants

The Buddha’s reply opens with a detail worth noticing: “Listen to me, mendicants, I will educate you in the cleansing teaching” — even though the question was asked by a layman surrounded by laypeople. The address matches what Dhammika himself had just described: both mendicants and layfolk had gathered to listen together, and the Buddha proceeds to give each group its own complete answer in turn, monastics first.

The monastic instructions

For seven verses the Buddha describes a mendicant’s ideal day: going for alms only at the right time, since “chains bind one who wanders at the wrong time”; dispelling desire for the sights, sounds, tastes, smells, and touches that “drive beings mad”; sitting in private reflection after the meal, curbing the mind “from outside things”; in conversation, bringing up “only the sublime teaching, not dividing or blaming,” since those who instead contend in debate are praised as having “little wisdom”; and using alms, lodging, and requisites only “after appraisal.” The section closes on one of the discourse’s most memorable images: such a mendicant remains “unsullied in the midst of these things, like a droplet on a lotus-leaf.”

The householder’s duty begins

The transition is marked explicitly: “Now I shall tell you the householder’s duty, doing which one becomes a good disciple. For one burdened with possessions does not get to realize the whole of the mendicant’s practice.” The line does real work — it neither collapses the two paths into one nor ranks the lay path as an afterthought, but names plainly why a householder needs a different, complete code of their own rather than a diluted version of the monastic one.

The five precepts

What follows, verse by verse, is the clearest statement of the five lay precepts found anywhere in this collection: not killing any creature, nor having them killed, nor permitting it (verse 20); not taking anything not given, in any form (verse 21); avoiding “the unchaste life… like a burning pit of coals,” and at minimum never transgressing “with another’s partner” (verse 22); never lying, whether in a council, one on one, or by proxy (verse 23); and abstaining entirely from liquor and drink, neither drinking nor encouraging others to (verse 24), since “drunken fools do bad things, and encourage other heedless folk” — a “field of demerit” the poem calls “the maddening, deluding frolic of fools.” Verse 26 then compresses all five into four crisp lines, a summary a lay follower could actually memorize.

The eight-factored sabbath

Verses 27–29 add a further, periodic practice on top of the five precepts: the uposatha, observed “on the fourteenth, fifteenth, and eighth of the fortnight, as well as on the fortnightly special displays.” On these days the five precepts are joined by three more restraints — no garlands or fragrance, sleeping “on a low bed, or a mat on the ground,” and no eating at night — making “the eight-factored sabbath.” The following morning, “a clever person, rejoicing with confident heart, would distribute food and drink to the mendicant Saṅgha as is fitting” — observance paired directly with generosity.

Parents and livelihood, and the close

The discourse’s final verse turns outward one last time, to two duties that sit outside the precept list entirely: “one should rightfully support one’s parents, and undertake a legitimate business.” The reward named for “a diligent layperson observing these duties” is rebirth “among the gods called Self-luminous” — and with that, the discourse, and Vagga 2 of the Sutta Nipāta, comes to its close.

Key terms

Dhammika — the lay follower who asks this discourse’s opening question, arriving with five hundred fellow lay followers and asking for guidance covering both monastics and laypeople at once.
“like a droplet on a lotus-leaf” — the image closing the monastic section, describing a mendicant who uses alms, lodging, and requisites without being stained by attachment to them.
the five precepts — not killing, not stealing, not engaging in sexual misconduct, not lying, and not taking liquor or drink, given here (verses 20–26) in their fullest verse form in this collection.
uposatha, the eight-factored sabbath — the periodic observance-day practice of verses 27–29, adding three further restraints to the five precepts on the 8th, 14th, and 15th of the fortnight.
the Self-luminous gods — the heavenly rebirth named in the closing verse for a diligent layperson who supports their parents, keeps a legitimate business, and observes these duties.

The text

The discourse in full — a prose opening, Dhammika’s nine-verse praise and question, and the Buddha’s full answer for both mendicants and householders. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The setting: a lay follower’s question

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Then the lay follower Dhammika, together with five hundred lay followers, went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and addressed him in verse:

Dhammika’s praise

Verse 2“I ask you, Gotama, whose wisdom is vast:
what does one do to become a good disciple,
both one who has left the home,
and the lay followers staying at home?

Verse 3For you understand the course and destiny
of the world with all its gods.
There is no equal to you who sees the subtle meaning,
for you are the Buddha most excellent, they say.

Verse 4Having experienced all knowledge,
you explain the teaching out of sympathy for beings.
All-seer, you have cast off the veil,
and immaculate, you shine on the whole world.

Verse 5The dragon king Erāvaṇa, hearing you called ‘Victor’,
came into your presence.
He consulted with you then, having heard your words,
left consoled, saying ‘Excellent!’

Verse 6And King Kuvera Vessavaṇa also
approached to ask about the teaching.
You also answered him, O attentive one,
and hearing you he too was consoled.

Verse 7Those monastics of other religions given to debate,
whether Ājīvakas or Jains,
all fail to overtake you in wisdom,
like a standing man next to a sprinter.

Verse 8Those brahmins given to debate,
some of whom are quite senior,
all end up beholden to you for the meaning,
and others too who think themselves debaters.

Verse 9So subtle and pleasant is the teaching
that is well proclaimed by you, Blessed One.
It’s all we long to hear. So when asked,
O Best of Buddhas, tell us!

Verse 10All these mendicants have gathered,
and the layfolk too are here to listen.
Let them hear the teaching the immaculate one discovered,
like gods listening to the fine words of Vāsava.”

Addressed first to the mendicants

Verse 11“Listen to me, mendicants, I will educate you
in the cleansing teaching; all bear it in mind.
An intelligent person, seeing the meaning,
would adopt the deportment proper to a renunciate.

The monastic instructions

Verse 12No way would a mendicant go out at the wrong time;
at the right time, they’d walk the village for alms.
For chains bind one who wanders at the wrong time,
which is why the Buddhas avoid it.

Verse 13Sights, sounds, tastes, smells, and touches,
which drive beings mad—
dispel desire for such things,
and enter for the morning meal at the right time.

Verse 14After receiving alms for the day,
on returning a mendicant would sit in private alone.
Inwardly reflective, they’d curb their mind
from outside things, keeping themselves collected.

Verse 15Should they converse with a disciple,
with anyone else, or with a mendicant,
they’d bring up only the sublime teaching,
not dividing or blaming.

Verse 16For some contend in debate,
but we praise not those of little wisdom.
In place after place they are bound in chains,
for they send their mind over there far away.

Verse 17Alms, a dwelling, a bed and seat,
and water for rinsing the dust from the cloak—
after hearing the teaching of the Holy One,
a disciple of outstanding wisdom would use these after appraisal.

Verse 18That’s why, when it comes to alms and lodgings,
and water for rinsing the dust from the cloak,
a mendicant is unsullied in the midst of these things,
like a droplet on a lotus-leaf.

The householder’s duty begins

Verse 19Now I shall tell you the householder’s duty,
doing which one becomes a good disciple.
For one burdened with possessions does not get to realize
the whole of the mendicant’s practice.

The five precepts

Verse 20They’d not kill any creature, nor have them killed,
nor grant permission for others to kill.
They’ve laid aside violence towards all creatures
frail or firm that there are in the world.

Verse 21Next, a disciple would avoid knowingly
taking anything not given at all,
they’d not get others to do it, nor grant them permission to steal;
they’d avoid all theft.

Verse 22A sensible person would avoid the unchaste life,
like a burning pit of coals.
But if unable to remain chaste,
they’d not transgress with another’s partner.

Verse 23In a council or assembly,
or one on one, they would not lie.
They’d not get others to lie, nor grant them permission to lie;
they’d avoid all untruths.

Verse 24A householder espousing this teaching
would not consume liquor or drink.
They’d not get others to drink, nor grant them permission to drink;
knowing that ends in intoxication.

Verse 25For drunken fools do bad things,
and encourage other heedless folk.
Shun this field of demerit,
the maddening, deluding frolic of fools.

Verse 26You shouldn’t kill living creatures, or steal,
or lie, or drink liquor.
Be chaste, refraining from sex,
and don’t eat at night, the wrong time.

The eight-factored sabbath

Verse 27Not wearing garlands or applying fragrance,
you should sleep on a low bed, or a mat on the ground.
This is the eight-factored sabbath, they say,
explained by the Buddha, who has gone to suffering’s end.

Verse 28Then having rightly undertaken the sabbath
complete in all its eight factors
on the fourteenth, fifteenth, and eighth of the fortnight,
as well as on the fortnightly special displays,

Verse 29at daybreak after the sabbath
a clever person, rejoicing with confident heart,
would distribute food and drink
to the mendicant Saṅgha as is fitting.

Parents and livelihood, and the close

Verse 30One should rightfully support one’s parents,
and undertake a legitimate business.
A diligent layperson observing these duties
ascends to the gods called Self-luminous.”

Old Age

Why this discourse, the sixth of the Aṭṭhakavagga

After five discourses of close argument against clinging to views, the Jarāsutta changes register. It doesn’t reason toward non-attachment; it simply looks steadily at something no one can argue their way out of — that this life is short, that everyone loses everyone, that even a name is eventually all that’s left of a person. Placed here, in the middle of the vagga’s hardest philosophical material, this discourse does something important: it grounds the whole abstract argument about views and disputes in the same plain fact every reader already knows from experience, whether or not they have ever thought about epistemology at all.

Reading guide

The discourse in one sentence

Life is short and separation from everyone and everything a person has ever loved is certain, so grief, lamentation, and stinginess — all of them forms of clinging to what was never going to stay — are simply not livable strategies, and the sage who has understood this moves through the world the way water moves off a lotus leaf, in contact with everything and holding on to nothing.

Verses 1–3: an inventory of what will be lost

The discourse opens as bluntly as any in the canon: “short, alas, is this life; you die before a hundred years. Even if you live a little longer, you still die of old age.” No argument is offered for this — none is needed. Verse 2 moves immediately to the practical consequence: “people grieve over belongings, yet there is no such thing as permanent possessions.” The grief is not dismissed as irrational; it is named as a response to a fact people already half-know and keep forgetting — “separation is a fact of life; when you see this, you wouldn’t stay living at home,” meaning the ordinary, settled arrangement of building a life around possessions doesn’t survive close inspection. Verse 3 broadens this from possessions to everything: “whatever a person thinks of as belonging to them, that too is given up when they die” — and the instruction that follows is disarmingly simple: an astute follower “would not be bent on ownership,” not because ownership is forbidden, but because bending one’s whole orientation around it is a losing arrangement from the start.

Verses 4–5: waking from a dream, and what a name leaves behind

The discourse’s central image compares death to waking up: “just as, upon awakening, a person does not see what they encountered in a dream; so too you do not see your loved ones when they are dead and gone.” It is a startling comparison, not because it minimizes loss but because of what it implies about the relationship between the living and the dead — a completeness to the disappearance that ordinary grief resists accepting. Verse 5 follows with one of the vagga’s most quietly devastating lines: “you used to see and hear those folk, and call them by their name. Yet the name is all that’s left to tell of a personage when they’re gone.” A name persists after everything else — the voice, the face, the presence — has gone; it is what remains of a whole living person, which is to say, remarkably little.

Verses 6–8: what the sages did with this knowledge

Verse 6 draws the practical conclusion the sages reached: “those who are greedy for belongings don’t give up sorrow, lamentation, and stinginess” — the three travel together, since holding tightly to what will inevitably be lost guarantees suffering when the loss arrives. “That’s why the sages, seers of sanctuary, left possessions behind and wandered” — not as an ascetic performance, but as a direct, practical response to having actually understood verses 1 through 5. Verses 7 and 8 then describe the resulting way of life: living “withdrawn,” frequenting “a secluded seat,” not showing oneself “in a home” — and, more importantly, becoming someone who is “independent everywhere,” who “doesn’t form likes or dislikes,” from whom “lamentation and stinginess slip off... like water from a leaf.”

Verses 9–10: the lotus leaf, and the return to “seen, heard, thought”

The discourse’s closing image is its most beautiful: “like a droplet slips from a lotus-leaf, like water from a lotus flower; the sage doesn’t cling to that which is seen or heard or thought.” A lotus leaf is not repelling water through effort or resistance — its surface simply doesn’t hold moisture, by its nature, so water beads and rolls away on contact. That is the discourse’s picture of non-clinging: not a strained, ongoing act of letting go, but a settled quality that experience simply doesn’t stick to. The final verse closes by explicitly rejoining the vagga’s central argument: “for the one who is cleansed does not conceive in terms of things seen, heard, or thought,” and “does not wish to be purified by another” — the same formula from Snp 4.4, now arrived at from an entirely different direction. Facing death honestly and facing the emptiness of fixed views turn out to be the same clarity, seen from two sides.

How mortality and non-attachment to views connect

It would be easy to read this discourse as a detour — a meditation on death dropped into the middle of an argument about philosophical disputes. But the connection is closer than it looks. Grasping a view and grasping a possession are structurally the same act: both involve treating something impermanent as though it were a stable ground to stand on, something that could finally settle the question of who one is and secure a person against loss. Old age and death are simply the most vivid, undeniable proof available that nothing plays that role reliably — not wealth, not family, not the body, and, by direct extension, not an opinion successfully defended in an argument. A person who has genuinely absorbed “the name is all that’s left” has very little motivation left to go on defending a position as though winning that argument could make them safe. This discourse doesn’t need to mention disputation once to be making exactly the same case as the discourses around it.

Key terms

jarā — “old age,” the discourse’s title and subject, one of the classic reminders of impermanence found throughout the canon.
“the name is all that’s left” — verse 5’s image for what survives a person’s death: a label persisting after voice, face, and presence are entirely gone.
the lotus leaf — the discourse’s closing image for non-clinging: not a strained, effortful letting-go, but a settled quality that experience simply doesn’t stick to.
“seers of sanctuary” — the discourse’s description of the sages who, having understood impermanence, left possessions behind and wandered as a direct, practical response.
“does not wish to be purified by another” — the discourse’s closing formula, echoing Snp 4.4 and joining this meditation on death explicitly to the vagga’s wider argument against clinging to views.

The text

All ten verses of the Jarāsutta, complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1Short, alas, is this life;
you die before a hundred years.
Even if you live a little longer,
you still die of old age.

Verse 2People grieve over belongings,
yet there is no such thing as permanent possessions.
Separation is a fact of life; when you see this,
you wouldn’t stay living at home.

Verse 3Whatever a person thinks of as belonging to them,
that too is given up when they die.
Knowing this, an astute follower of mine
would not be bent on ownership.

Verse 4Just as, upon awakening, a person does not see
what they encountered in a dream;
so too you do not see your loved ones
when they are dead and gone.

Verse 5You used to see and hear those folk,
and call them by their name.
Yet the name is all that’s left to tell
of a personage when they’re gone.

Verse 6Those who are greedy for belongings
don’t give up sorrow, lamentation, and stinginess.
That’s why the sages, seers of sanctuary,
left possessions behind and wandered.

Verse 7For a mendicant who lives withdrawn,
frequenting a secluded seat,
they say it’s fitting
to not show themselves in a home.

Verse 8The sage is independent everywhere,
they don’t form likes or dislikes.
Lamentation and stinginess
slip off them like water from a leaf.

Verse 9Like a droplet slips from a lotus-leaf,
like water from a lotus flower;
the sage doesn’t cling to that
which is seen or heard or thought.

Verse 10For the one who is cleansed does not conceive
in terms of things seen, heard, or thought.
They do not wish to be purified by another;
they are neither passionate
nor growing dispassioned.

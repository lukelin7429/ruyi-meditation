Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 3.3

Well-Spoken Words

Subhāsitasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta's Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika's monastery, addressed to the assembled mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha, who gives the teaching in brief prose and verse, and Venerable Vaṅgīsa, the Saṅgha's noted poet, who expands on it with the Buddha's express permission
Form
A short prose statement of four factors, restated by the Buddha in a single summary verse, then unpacked by Vaṅgīsa into four further verses
Length
~2 minutes to read
Northern parallel
The Sutta Nipāta as a whole has no direct counterpart among the four Chinese Āgamas; the core four-factor formula for well-spoken speech recurs in other parts of the Pali canon in closely related forms
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — the shortest and plainest of the six discourses treated here

Why this discourse, third of the Great Chapter

After two discourses of high biographical drama, the collection turns to something almost domestic: what makes speech good. The Buddha states it in one breath — true, principled, gentle, beneficial — and then, in a small and touching scene, invites his own poet-disciple Vaṅgīsa to stand up and say more. What Vaṅgīsa adds is not decoration; it ties the four factors to a single deeper claim, that truth itself is “undying speech,” and closes by naming the Buddha's own liberating words as speech at its highest possible pitch.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Speech with four factors — well phrased, principled, pleasant, and truthful — is well spoken and blameless; Venerable Vaṅgīsa, given leave to speak, expands this into four verses grounding good speech in truth as “an ancient teaching” and naming the Buddha's own words as its highest expression.

Four factors, stated plainly

The Buddha addresses the assembled mendicants directly: “speech that has four factors is well spoken, not poorly spoken. It's blameless and is not criticized by sensible people.” The four: “a mendicant speaks well, not poorly; their speech is principled, not unprincipled; they speak pleasantly, not unpleasantly; and they speak truthfully, not falsely.” Each factor is paired with its failure mode, in the same paired structure familiar from the Dhammapada's opening chapter — a flaw named alongside its cure.

The Buddha's own verse

He then restates the same four points in verse, in a fixed order that becomes the discourse's structuring principle: “Good people say well-spoken words are foremost; second, speech ought be principled, not unprincipled; third, speak pleasantly, not unpleasantly; and fourth, speak truthfully, not falsely.” The compression from prose to verse changes nothing about the content; it simply makes the four-part list easier to hold in mind and recite.

Vaṅgīsa steps forward

What happens next is a small, formal scene of its own: Vaṅgīsa rises, arranges his robe over one shoulder, raises his cupped palms toward the Buddha, and says, “I feel inspired to speak, Blessed One! I feel inspired to speak, Holy One!” — and only after the Buddha replies, “Then speak as you feel inspired,” does he begin. This same request-and-permission formula appears elsewhere in the canon attached to Vaṅgīsa specifically, the Saṅgha's recognized poet, whose verses are treated as worth pausing the discourse to include in full.

Vaṅgīsa's expansion

His four verses do not simply repeat the Buddha's list; each develops one strand of it. The first ties well-spoken words to harmlessness: “Speak only such words that do not hurt yourself nor harm others.” The second defines “pleasing” concretely, as words “gladly welcomed” that “bring nothing bad to others” — ruling out flattery or manipulation dressed up as kindness. The third makes the discourse's deepest claim: “Truth itself is undying speech: this is an ancient teaching” — the same phrase, “an ancient teaching,” used elsewhere in the canon for the principle that hatred ends only through love, marking both as truths that predate any single teacher. The fourth and final verse brings the whole discourse to its point: of all speech, the Buddha's own words, spoken “for finding the sanctuary, extinguishment, for making an end of suffering,” are “the best kind of speech” — the four ethical factors and the soteriological aim converging in a single claim.

Key terms

subhāsita — “well spoken”: the discourse's title and organizing concept, defined by four factors rather than by any single rule.
Vaṅgīsa — a monk formally recognized elsewhere in the canon as foremost among the Buddha's disciples in spontaneous verse composition; here he asks permission before adding his own expansion.
the four factors — well phrased (not poorly), principled (not unprincipled), pleasant (not unpleasant), and truthful (not false).
“an ancient teaching” — the phrase Vaṅgīsa uses for the claim that truth is “undying speech,” the same wording used in Dhammapada verse 5 for the claim that hatred ends only through love.

The text

The discourse in full: the narrative frame and the Buddha's prose statement given as paragraphs, the Buddha's summary verse and Vaṅgīsa's four expanding verses given with line breaks. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The four factors

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. There the Buddha addressed the mendicants, “Mendicants!” “Venerable sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

§2“Mendicants, speech that has four factors is well spoken, not poorly spoken. It’s blameless and is not criticized by sensible people. What four? It’s when a mendicant speaks well, not poorly; their speech is principled, not unprincipled; they speak pleasantly, not unpleasantly; and they speak truthfully, not falsely. Speech with these four factors is well spoken, not poorly spoken. It’s blameless and is not criticized by sensible people.”

That is what the Buddha said. Then the Holy One, the Teacher, went on to say:

§3“Good people say well-spoken words are foremost;
second, speech ought be principled, not unprincipled;
third, speak pleasantly, not unpleasantly;
and fourth, speak truthfully, not falsely.”

Vaṅgīsa asks to speak

§4Then Venerable Vaṅgīsa rose from his seat, arranged his robe over one shoulder, raised his cupped palms toward the Buddha, and said, “I feel inspired to speak, Blessed One! I feel inspired to speak, Holy One!” “Then speak as you feel inspired,” said the Buddha. Then Vaṅgīsa extolled the Buddha in his presence with fitting verses:

Vaṅgīsa's expansion

§5“Speak only such words
that do not hurt yourself
nor harm others;
such speech is truly well spoken.

§6Speak only pleasing words,
words gladly welcomed.
Pleasing words are those
that bring nothing bad to others.

§7Truth itself is undying speech:
this is an ancient teaching.
Good people say the teaching and meaning
are grounded in truth.

§8The words spoken by the Buddha
for finding the sanctuary, extinguishment,
for making an end of suffering,
this really is the best kind of speech.”

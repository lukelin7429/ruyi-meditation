Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 5.7

The Questions of Upasīva

Upasīvamāṇavapucchā

Setting
Continuing directly from Snp 5.1–5.6 — the same gathering at the Pāsāṇaka Shrine near Rājagaha, the sixth student now stepping forward
Speakers
The Buddha and the student Upasīva, sixth of Bāvarī's sixteen students named in Snp 5.1
Form
Eight verses, moving from a practical question about crossing the flood into one of the canon's most philosophically probing exchanges
Length
~2 minutes to read
Frame
Part of one continuous work: see Snp 5.1 for the full story of Bāvarī and his sixteen students
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — the closing exchange about a sage's fate after death is genuinely difficult, and deliberately resists a simple yes-or-no answer

Why this discourse, the sixth question

Upasīva, sixth-named among Bāvarī's sixteen students, asks how to cross the great flood “alone and independent,” without any external support to lean on. The Buddha's answer points him toward contemplating nothingness — but Upasīva's follow-up pushes the exchange into genuinely difficult territory: what happens to a sage who reaches that formless attainment and, in a sense, stays there? Does their consciousness simply cease? The Buddha's response is the discourse's most celebrated moment, and one of the most quoted images in the whole Sutta Nipāta: a flame blown out by a gust of wind “comes to an end and cannot be reckoned” — and to ask where it went is already to have asked the wrong kind of question.

Reading guide

The discourse in one sentence

Upasīva, the sixth of Bāvarī's sixteen students, asks how to cross the great flood without any external support, and then presses the Buddha on what becomes of a sage who has reached the formless attainment and grown cool there — receiving, in reply, the famous simile of a flame blown out by the wind, which cannot be tracked to anywhere it has “gone.”

The frame: the sixth of sixteen questions

As told in full in Snp 5.1, Bāvarī, an aged brahmin teacher unable to travel himself, sent sixteen of his finest students — Ajita, Tissametteyya, Puṇṇaka, Mettagū, Dhotaka, Upasīva, Nanda, Hemaka, Todeyya, Kappa, Jatukaṇṇī, Bhadrāvudha, Udaya, Posāla, Mogharāja, and Piṅgiya — to question the newly awakened Buddha directly. Five students have already spoken, in Snp 5.2 through 5.6; Upasīva, sixth in Bāvarī's list, now asks a question that will lead into some of the most philosophically demanding territory in the whole chapter.

Crossing the flood “alone and independent”

Upasīva's opening request is distinctive: he says he is not able to cross the great flood “alone and independent,” and asks for a support to depend on. The Buddha's answer directs him toward “mindfully contemplating nothingness,” depending on the perception “there is nothing” — a reference to the formless meditative attainment known as the base of nothingness — while giving up sensual pleasures and refraining from chatter, watching “day and night for the ending of craving.”

Remaining there without travelling on

Upasīva then asks something more specific: could someone free of desire for sensual pleasures, depending on nothingness, “freed in the ultimate liberation of perception,” simply remain there without “travelling on” — that is, without moving beyond that formless attainment into full awakening? The Buddha answers, almost surprisingly, that yes, such a person might remain there without travelling on. It is Upasīva's next question that turns the exchange toward its real difficulty.

The flame that cannot be tracked

Upasīva asks directly: if such a person remained there, even for countless years, and “grew cool right there,” would their consciousness “pass away”? This is a version of one of the oldest and hardest questions in the tradition — what happens, ultimately, to someone fully liberated? The Buddha's answer refuses the terms of the question rather than picking a side within it: “As a flame tossed by a gust of wind comes to an end and cannot be reckoned; so too, a sage freed from the set of mental phenomena comes to an end and cannot be reckoned.” A blown-out flame has not gone anywhere that can be pointed to — asking “where” it went is already a category error, and the same, the Buddha suggests, is true of a fully liberated person.

“Do they not exist? Or are they free from disease for eternity?”

Upasīva pushes once more, framing the two most obvious alternatives directly: has this person ceased to exist, or are they “free from disease for eternity”? The Buddha's final answer holds firm to the same refusal: “One who has disappeared cannot be defined. They have nothing by which others might describe them. When all things have been eradicated, eradicated, too, are all ways of speech.” The point is not evasiveness for its own sake, but a claim that ordinary categories of existence and non-existence — and the language built to describe them — simply do not apply to what has been “eradicated” in this way. The question is retired, not answered with either of its own two options.

Key terms

Upasīva — sixth-named of Bāvarī's sixteen students in Snp 5.1, whose exchange leads into one of the chapter's most philosophically demanding passages.
the base of nothingness — a formless meditative attainment, referenced here as “contemplating nothingness” and depending on the perception “there is nothing.”
the extinguished flame — the discourse's central simile: a flame blown out by wind “comes to an end and cannot be reckoned,” used to describe a liberated sage in the same terms.
“cannot be defined” — the Buddha's closing statement about one who has “disappeared”: ordinary categories of existence and non-existence, and the language built to describe them, do not apply.
context · the sixteen students — Upasīva is the sixth of sixteen brahmin students Bāvarī sends to question the Buddha (see Snp 5.1); each of Snp 5.2 through 5.17 belongs to one of them in turn.

The text

All eight verses of Upasīva's exchange with the Buddha, given in full. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1069“Alone and independent, O Sakyan,”
said Venerable Upasīva,
“I am not able to cross the great flood.
Tell me a support, All-seer,
depending on which I may cross this flood.”

Verse 1070“Mindfully contemplating nothingness,”
replied the Buddha,
“depending on the perception ‘there is nothing’, cross the flood.
Giving up sensual pleasures, refraining from chatter,
watch day and night for the ending of craving.”

Verse 1071“One who is free of desire for sensual pleasures,”
said Venerable Upasīva,
“depending on nothingness, all else left behind,
freed in the ultimate liberation of perception:
might they remain there without travelling on?”

Verse 1072“One free of desire for sensual pleasures,”
replied the Buddha,
“depending on nothingness, all else left behind,
freed in the ultimate liberation of perception:
they might remain there without travelling on.”

Verse 1073“If they were to remain there without travelling on,
even for myriad years, All-seer,
and, being freed, were to grow cool right there,
would the consciousness of that sort pass away?”

Verse 1074“As a flame tossed by a gust of wind,”
replied the Buddha,
“comes to an end and cannot be reckoned;
so too, a sage freed from the set of mental phenomena
comes to an end and cannot be reckoned.”

Verse 1075“One who has disappeared—do they not exist?
Or are they free from disease for eternity?
Please, sage, answer me clearly,
for truly you understand this matter.”

Verse 1076“One who has disappeared cannot be defined,”
replied the Buddha.
“They have nothing by which others might describe them.
When all things have been eradicated,
eradicated, too, are all ways of speech.”

Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 3.11

About Nālaka

Nālakasutta

Setting
Opens in the deva realms at the moment of Gotama’s birth in Lumbinī, then shifts, years later, to the time just after the Buddha’s awakening
Speakers
The seer Asita; the gods of the host of Thirty; the Sakyans; Asita’s nephew Nālaka; and, in the second half, the Buddha himself
Form
Entirely in verse, with no prose frame at all — an origin narrative (the “introductory verses”) followed by a dense teaching on the practice of a sage
Length
~10 minutes to read
Related account
The infancy narrative here is one of the earliest and most detailed accounts of Asita’s prophecy anywhere in the canon; compare the fuller genealogical treatment in DN 14, already on this site
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the narrative half is accessible; the teaching half is dense and demanding

Why this discourse

This sutta holds two of the most striking things in the Sutta Nipāta side by side: a tender, unusual account of an old seer weeping with joy and grief in the same breath at a baby’s birth, and then, years later, one of the canon’s most rigorous descriptions of what a committed, solitary spiritual practice actually demands. Asita sees, with certainty, that the infant Gotama will become a Buddha — and, with equal certainty, that he himself will die before it happens. His grief at his own mortality, set right beside his joy at someone else’s future awakening, is one of the most emotionally complex moments in the early texts. His parting instruction to his nephew Nālaka — wait, and go find this teacher when the time comes — sets up the second half of the sutta, delivered decades later, when that waiting finally pays off.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A sage’s practice — solitude, restraint in eating and speaking, equanimity toward praise and blame, and freedom from craving — is described here in exacting, almost austere detail, as the fulfillment of a prophecy made decades earlier at the future Buddha’s birth.

The seer Asita and the celebrating gods

The sutta opens not with the Buddha at all, but with Asita, an ascetic seer, noticing during his daily meditation that the gods of the host of Thirty are celebrating with unusual intensity — “waving streamers in exuberant exaltation” on a scale, he notes, greater even than their celebration after defeating the titans in their ancient war. Asked why, the gods explain plainly: “the being intent on awakening, a peerless gem, has been born in the human realm… in Lumbinī, a village in the Sakyan land.”

A prophecy and a paradox

Asita descends at once to see the child himself, and is struck by his radiance — “the boy shone like burning gold… dazzling with glory, of peerless beauty.” Examining him as an expert “master of marks and hymns” (the same lore of bodily marks that appears in Snp 3.7’s account of Sela), Asita declares him “the best of men” — and then, abruptly, weeps. The Sakyans, alarmed, ask if the boy is in danger. Asita’s answer is the emotional center of the whole narrative: there is no danger to the child at all; the child will “reach the highest awakening” and “roll forth the wheel of the teaching.” The tears are entirely for Asita himself — “I have not long left in this life, I shall die before then. I will never hear the teaching… that’s why I’m so upset and distraught — it’s a disaster for me!” It is a rare moment in the early texts where someone’s joy at a world-historical event and grief at their own limits are given equal, unresolved weight.

Instructing Nālaka: wait for the Buddha

Before leaving the royal compound, Asita seeks out his nephew Nālaka and, “out of sympathy,” gives him a single, patient instruction: when you hear that someone has “attained awakening and reveals the foremost teaching,” go to him and ask about his breakthrough, and take up the spiritual life under him. The text notes that Nālaka, having “a store of accumulated merit,” takes this instruction seriously enough to spend the intervening years “guarding his senses” in anticipation — a long wait rewarded only once he actually hears that the Buddha has “rolled forth the excellent wheel.”

Years later: Nālaka asks about sagacity

The sutta’s second half begins with a small editorial note in the source text itself — “the introductory verses are finished” — marking the shift from narrative to teaching. Nālaka, now grown, confirms that “Asita’s words have turned out to be true,” and asks the Buddha directly for an account of moneyya, sagacity or the practice of a sage, “the ultimate state,” for someone who has “entered the homeless life, seeking food on alms round.” The Buddha warns him plainly before beginning: this teaching is “difficult and challenging… brace yourself; stay strong.”

The training of a sage

What follows is a sustained, granular account of renunciant discipline: keeping the same attitude whether reviled or praised; resisting the seduction the text warns will come, “like tongues of fire in a forest”; treating all beings as oneself (“as am I, so are they”); eating with an “empty stomach,” limited food, and few wishes; alternating forest meditation with careful, unhurried almsround conduct; remaining equally unaffected whether one receives something or nothing. The famous instruction “practice as if you were licking a razor’s edge” captures the demanding, exacting tone of the whole passage — not asceticism for its own sake, but precision under real difficulty.

Streams and rivers: knowing when to speak

The teaching closes with one of the Sutta Nipāta’s most quoted similes: “the little creeks flow on resounding, while silent flow the great rivers… the fool is like a half-full pot; the wise like a brimfull lake.” This is immediately balanced, though, against a caution that solitude and silence aren’t ends in themselves: “when the Ascetic speaks much it is relevant and meaningful… knowing, he teaches the Dhamma.” The final verse defines sagacity precisely as knowing, and being self-controlled — not speaking much even while knowing.

Key terms

moneyya — sagacity, or the practice and state of a sage (muni) — the subject Nālaka asks the Buddha to teach, described here as “difficult and challenging.”
“licking a razor’s edge” — the sutta’s image for the exacting precision required of the sage’s training.
“little creeks… great rivers” — the closing simile contrasting noisy shallowness with silent fullness — “the fool is like a half-full pot; the wise like a brimfull lake.”
the marks of a great man — the same body of lore Asita, “master of marks and hymns,” uses to recognize the infant Gotama, also central to Snp 3.7’s account of Sela.

The text

The discourse in full: Asita’s prophecy at the Buddha’s birth, his instruction to his nephew Nālaka, and the Buddha’s later teaching to Nālaka on the practice of a sage, given complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The seer Asita and the celebrating gods

§1–2The seer Asita in his daily meditation
saw the bright-clad gods of the host of Thirty
and their lord Sakka joyfully celebrating,
waving streamers in exuberant exaltation.
Seeing the gods rejoicing, elated,
he paid respects and said this there:
“Why is the community of gods in such excellent spirits?
Why take up streamers and whirl them about?

§3–4Even in the war with the titans,
when gods were victorious and titans defeated,
there was no such excitement.
What marvel have the celestials seen that they so rejoice?
Shouting and singing and playing music,
they clap their hands and dance.
I ask you, dwellers on Mount Meru’s peak,
quickly dispel my doubt, good fellows!”

§5–6“The being intent on awakening, a peerless gem,
has been born in the human realm for the sake of welfare and happiness,
in Lumbinī, a village in the Sakyan land.
That’s why we’re so happy, in such excellent spirits.
He is supreme among all beings, the peak individual,
captain of men, supreme among all creatures.
He will roll forth the wheel in the grove of the seers,
roaring like a mighty lion, lord of beasts.”

A prophecy and a paradox

§7–8Hearing this, he swiftly descended
and right away approached Suddhodana’s home.
Seated there he said this to the Sakyans,
“Where is the boy? I too wish to see him!”
Then the Sakyans showed their son to the one named Asita—
the boy shone like burning gold
well-wrought in the forge;
dazzling with glory, of peerless beauty.

§9–10The boy beamed like crested flame,
pure as the moon, lord of stars traversing the sky,
blazing like the sun free of stormclouds after the rains;
seeing him, he was joyful, brimming with happiness.
The celestials held up a parasol in the sky,
many-ribbed and thousand-circled;
and golden-handled chowries waved—
but none could see who held the chowries or the parasols.

§11–12When the dreadlocked seer called “Dark Splendor”
had seen the boy like a gold ingot on a cream rug
with a white parasol held over his head,
he received him, elated and happy.
Having received the Sakyan bull,
the seeker, master of marks and hymns,
lifted up his voice with confident heart:
“He is supreme, the best of men!”

§13–14But then, remembering he would depart this world,
his spirits fell and his tears flowed.
Seeing the weeping seer, the Sakyans said,
“Surely there will be no threat to the boy?”
Seeing the crestfallen Sakyans, the seer said,
“I do not forsee harm befall the boy,
and there will be no threat to him,
not in the least; set your minds at ease.

§15–16This boy shall reach the highest awakening.
As one of perfectly purified vision, compassionate for the welfare of the many,
he shall roll forth the wheel of the teaching;
his spiritual path will become widespread.
But I have not long left in this life,
I shall die before then.
I will never hear the teaching of the one who bore the unequaled burden.
That’s why I’m so upset and distraught—it’s a disaster for me!”

Instructing Nālaka: wait for the Buddha

§17–18Having brought abundant happiness to the Sakyans,
the spiritual seeker left the royal compound.
He had a nephew; and out of sympathy
he encouraged him in the teaching of the one who bore the unequaled burden.
“When you hear the voice of another saying ‘Buddha’—
one who has attained awakening and who reveals the foremost teaching—
go there and ask about his breakthrough;
lead the spiritual life under that Blessed One.”

§19–20Now, that Nālaka had a store of accumulated merit;
so when instructed by one of such kindly intent,
with perfectly purified vision of the future,
he waited anticipating the Victor, guarding his senses.
When he heard of the Victor rolling forth the excellent wheel he went to him,
and seeing the boss seer, he became confident.
The time of Asita’s instruction had arrived;
so he asked the excellent sage about the highest sagacity.
The introductory verses are finished.

Years later: Nālaka asks about sagacity

§21–22“I now know that Asita’s words
have turned out to be true.
I ask you this, Gotama,
who has gone beyond all things:
For one who has entered the homeless life,
seeking food on alms round,
when questioned, O sage, please tell me
of sagacity, the ultimate state.”

§23“I shall school you in sagacity,”
said the Buddha,
“so difficult and challenging.
Come, I shall tell you all about it.
Brace yourself; stay strong!

The training of a sage

§24–25In the village, keep the same attitude
no matter if reviled or praised.
Guard against ill-tempered thoughts;
wander peaceful and humble.
Many different things come up,
like tongues of fire in a forest.
Women try to seduce a sage—
let them not seduce you!

§26–27Refraining from sex,
having left behind sensual pleasures high and low,
don’t be hostile or attached
to living creatures firm or frail.
‘As am I, so are they;
as are they, so am I’—
Treating others like oneself,
neither kill nor incite to kill.

§28–29Leaving behind desire and greed
for what ordinary people are attached to,
one whose eye is clear would set out to practice,
they’d cross over this abyss.
With empty stomach, taking limited food,
few in wishes, not greedy;
truly hungerless regarding all desires,
desireless, one is quenched.

§30–31Having wandered for alms,
they’d take themselves into the forest;
and nearing the foot of a tree,
the sage would take their seat.
That attentive one intent on absorption,
would delight within the forest.
They’d practice absorption at the foot of a tree,
filling themselves with bliss.

§32–33Then, at the end of the night,
they’d take themselves into a village.
They’d not welcome being called,
nor offerings brought from the village.
A sage who has come to a village
would not walk hastily among the families.
They’d not discuss their search for food,
nor would they speak suggestively.

§34–35‘I got something, that’s good.
I got nothing, that’s fine.’
Unaffected in both cases,
they return right to the tree.
Wandering with bowl in hand,
not dumb, but thought to be dumb,
they wouldn’t scorn a tiny gift,
nor look down upon the giver.

§36–37For the practice has many aspects,
as explained by the Ascetic.
They do not go to the far shore twice,
nor having gone once do they fall away.
When a mendicant has no creeping,
and has cut the stream of craving,
and given up all the various duties,
no fever is found in them.

§38–39I shall school you in sagacity.
Practice as if you were licking a razor’s edge.
With tongue pressed against the roof of your mouth,
be restrained regarding your stomach.
Don’t be sluggish in mind,
nor think overly much.
Be free of putrefaction and unattached,
committed to the spiritual life.

§40–41Train in a lonely seat,
attending closely to ascetics;
solitude is sagacity, they say.
If you welcome solitude,
you’ll light up the ten directions.
Having heard the words of the attentive,
the meditators who’ve given up sensual desires,
a follower of mine would develop
conscience and faith all the more.

Streams and rivers: knowing when to speak

§42–43Understand this by the way streams move
in clefts and crevices:
the little creeks flow on resounding,
while silent flow the great rivers.
What is lacking, resounds;
what is full is at peace.
The fool is like a half-full pot;
the wise like a brimfull lake.

§44–45When the Ascetic speaks much
it is relevant and meaningful:
knowing, he teaches the Dhamma;
knowing, he speaks much.
But one who, knowing, is self-controlled,
knowing, does not speak much;
that sage is worthy of sagacity,
that sage has achieved sagacity.”

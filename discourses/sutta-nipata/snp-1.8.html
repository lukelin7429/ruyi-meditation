Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 1.8

The Discourse on Love

Karaṇīyamettā Sutta

Setting
None given in the canonical text itself — unusually for the Sutta Nipāta, there is no narrative frame at all, no “so I have heard,” no named audience. Later commentary supplies a backstory of monks unsettled by forest spirits, but this is tradition, not text
Speakers
Traditionally the Buddha, addressed to no one named within the verses
Form
Ten verses of continuous, frameless poetry — the only discourse so far in this chapter with no prose at all, opening and closing entirely in verse
Length
~2 minutes to read; among the shortest discourses in the entire canon relative to its influence
Standing
One of the most widely memorized and daily-recited texts in all of Theravada Buddhism, chanted as a paritta (protective verse) in temples and homes across the tradition
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — simple, direct language carrying an unusually demanding practice instruction

Why this discourse, the eighth — and one of the most important in the whole canon

Ten verses, no story, no named audience, no historical occasion — and yet this is very likely the single most recited text in the Sutta Nipāta, quite possibly in the whole of Theravada Buddhism. It is chanted at ordinations, at funerals, in daily temple liturgy, and privately by countless practitioners as a stand-alone meditation instruction. Its central image, a mother protecting her only child with her own life, has become one of the most recognizable similes in Buddhist literature anywhere in the world. This reading guide accordingly takes it more slowly than most, verse by verse, because so much of what makes this text extraordinary is compressed into very few words.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Ground yourself first in a specific set of ethical and temperamental qualities, then radiate an unlimited wish for the safety and happiness of every living being — without exception, without boundary, and without ever setting it down — cherishing all beings the way a mother would protect her only child with her own life.

No story at all

It is worth pausing on something easy to miss: unlike almost every other discourse in this chapter, the Karaṇīyamettā Sutta has no narrative frame whatsoever. There is no “so I have heard,” no location, no questioner, no occasion. It simply begins mid-instruction and ends. The later commentarial tradition fills this gap with a well-known story — monks sent to meditate in a forest were so unsettled by hostile tree spirits that they fled back to the Buddha, who taught them this verse both as a protection to recite and as a practice to undertake, after which the spirits were themselves won over by the monks’ radiated goodwill. That story is worth knowing, but it belongs to tradition and commentary, not to the eighteen lines of canonical verse translated below. The text itself offers no setting at all — only the instruction.

Verses 1–3: character before technique

The discourse does not open with a meditation instruction. It opens with a character sketch. Before any wish is formed, the practitioner is asked to become “capable and upright, very upright, easy to speak to, gentle and humble” — then “content and unburdensome, unbusied, living lightly, alert, with senses calmed, courteous, not fawning on families.” This is a remarkably specific inventory: not just kindness in the abstract, but a whole temperament — simplicity of living, ease of manner, freedom from status-seeking, calm senses. Only after this list does the text add the negative counterpart, “let them not do the slightest thing that others who are wise would blame,” and only then arrive at the first statement of the wish itself: “May they be happy and safe! May all beings be happy!” The order is the argument: love that is not grounded in this kind of integrated character risks becoming sentimental rather than actually transformative.

Verses 4–5: a wish with no edges

Having stated the wish once, the text immediately widens it as far as language will stretch. The list of beings is deliberately exhaustive and deliberately redundant — “frail or firm, long or large, medium, small, tiny or round,” “seen or unseen, living far or near, those who have been born and those about to be born” — a catalogue built not to be efficient but to close off every possible exception a hearer might quietly reserve. Nothing is size-gated, distance-gated, or even existence-gated: beings not yet born are explicitly included. The refrain lands twice, once at the end of verse 3 and again at the end of verse 5, framing this catalogue on both sides: “may all beings be happy.”

Verse 6: the ethical floor beneath the wish

Before the discourse moves to its most famous image, it states the practice’s ethical minimum plainly: “Let none deceive another, nor look down on anyone anywhere. Though provoked or aggrieved, let them not wish pain on each other.” This verse is easy to read past, but it is doing real work — it explicitly anticipates the situation where love is hardest to sustain, when one has actually been wronged, and states the standard for exactly that case. The wish is not conditioned on how one has been treated.

Verse 7: the central image

“Even as a mother would protect with her life her child, her only child, so too for all creatures unfold a boundless heart.” This is the discourse’s hinge and its most quoted line by a wide margin. The comparison is carefully built: not a mother’s love in general, but a mother’s love for an only child, specifically under threat, specifically willing to risk her own life. It is the most intense, least divided form of protective care the text can name, and it is offered as the baseline — not the ceiling — for how one is asked to regard every living being without exception.

Verse 8: no direction left out

The image of verse 7 is immediately generalized in spatial terms: “above, below, all round, unconstricted, without enmity or foe.” This threefold spatial sweep — up, down, and all around — became the standard formula for describing the radiation of loving-kindness throughout the rest of the canon, appearing in meditation instructions across the four brahmavihāras (the “divine abodes”: love, compassion, appreciative joy, and equanimity). Nothing here is metaphorical decoration; it is a literal instruction for how the meditation is meant to be practiced — radiating outward until no direction and no being is left outside it.

Verse 9: in every posture, without lapse

“When standing, walking, sitting, or lying down while yet unweary, keep this ever in mind; for this, they say, is a divine meditation in this life.” The instruction is not confined to formal seated meditation. It is meant to continue through every physical posture a person occupies while awake, described here explicitly as a brahmavihāra — a “divine abode” or “divine meditation” — a term used elsewhere in the canon for the four boundless qualities of love, compassion, joy, and equanimity that a meditator is said to dwell in as if inhabiting the mental state of a divine being.

Verse 10: not sentiment alone, but a path

The discourse’s closing verse is easy to overlook precisely because it changes register so completely: “Avoiding harmful views, virtuous, accomplished in insight, with desire for sensual pleasures dispelled, they never return to a womb again.” This is the language of full liberation, of no further rebirth — not simply of a pleasant emotional state. The verse insists that this practice, brought to completion alongside right view and insight, is not merely a mood cultivated for its own sake but is continuous with the path to awakening itself. Love, on this text’s own account, is not a detour from liberation; sustained and paired with wisdom, it leads there.

A living practice today

Few texts in the canon have a more visible afterlife. The Karaṇīyamettā Sutta is chanted daily in innumerable temples and homes, recited at ordinations and funerals, used as a protective paritta alongside other blessing-texts, and taught as a self-contained meditation manual in its own right — recite it, or a version of its central wish, while systematically extending the feeling it describes outward in the very directions verse 8 names. Its companion piece elsewhere in the canon, AN 11.15, “The Benefits of Love”, lists eleven specific results said to follow from sustained practice of exactly this quality — sleeping and waking in comfort, freedom from nightmares, being dear to people and non-human beings alike, and a peaceful, undistracted mind, among others — giving concrete shape to what this discourse asks a practitioner to cultivate.

Key terms

mettā — usually rendered “love” or “loving-kindness”: an unconditional, non-romantic goodwill directed at the safety and happiness of every being, the subject of this entire discourse.
karaṇīya — “should be done,” the opening word that gives the discourse its Pali title, framing everything that follows as a practical undertaking rather than abstract doctrine.
brahmavihāra — “divine abode” or “divine meditation,” the term used in verse 9 for this practice; one of four boundless qualities (with compassion, appreciative joy, and equanimity) said to be radiated in all directions without limit.
the mother and her only child — the discourse’s central simile in verse 7, offered as the measure of intensity and completeness for how one is asked to regard all living beings.
paritta — a “protective” verse chanted for blessing and safety; this discourse is among the most widely used texts in that role across the Theravada world.

The text

All ten verses in full, with no omissions — the entire discourse, exactly as it stands in the canon with no narrative frame at either end. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Character before technique

Verse 1Those who are skilled in the meaning of scripture
should practice as follows to realize the state of peace.
Let them be capable and upright, very upright,
easy to speak to, gentle and humble;

Verse 2content and unburdensome,
unbusied, living lightly,
alert, with senses calmed,
courteous, not fawning on families.

Verse 3Let them not do the slightest thing
that others who are wise would blame.
May they be happy and safe!
May all beings be happy!

A wish with no edges

Verse 4Whatever living creatures there are
with not a one left out—
frail or firm, long or large,
medium, small, tiny or round,

Verse 5seen or unseen,
living far or near,
those who have been born and those about to be born—
may all beings be happy!

The ethical floor

Verse 6Let none deceive another,
nor look down on anyone anywhere.
Though provoked or aggrieved,
let them not wish pain on each other.

The central image

Verse 7Even as a mother would protect with her life
her child, her only child,
so too for all creatures
unfold a boundless heart.

No direction left out

Verse 8With love for the whole world,
unfold a boundless heart:
above, below, all round,
unconstricted, without enmity or foe.

In every posture

Verse 9When standing, walking, sitting,
or lying down while yet unweary,
keep this ever in mind;
for this, they say, is a divine meditation in this life.

Not sentiment alone

Verse 10Avoiding harmful views,
virtuous, accomplished in insight,
with desire for sensual pleasures dispelled,
they never return to a womb again.

Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 5.3

The Questions of Tissametteyya

Tissametteyyamāṇavapucchā

Setting
Continuing directly from Snp 5.1–5.2 — at the Pāsāṇaka Shrine near Rājagaha, the same gathering, the second student now stepping forward
Speakers
The Buddha and the student Tissametteyya, second of Bāvarī's sixteen students named in Snp 5.1
Form
Just three verses — the shortest of the sixteen student exchanges in this chapter
Length
under 1 minute to read
Frame
Part of one continuous work: see Snp 5.1 for the full story of Bāvarī and his sixteen students
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — brief, but the closing image of “escaping the seamstress” is a genuine riddle left for the reader

Why this discourse, the second question

Tissametteyya, second-named among Bāvarī's sixteen students in Snp 5.1, asks the shortest question of the whole sequence and receives one of the shortest answers: who in the world is truly content, who has no disturbances, and — in a striking closing phrase — who has “escaped the seamstress”? The Buddha's answer names someone who leads the spiritual life even in the midst of sensual pleasures, rid of craving and ever mindful, “quenched” after careful appraisal, and describes that person as “a great man” who has “known both ends” without becoming stuck between them. In barely three verses, the discourse packs in a full character sketch of a liberated person.

Reading guide

The discourse in one sentence

Tissametteyya, the second of Bāvarī's sixteen students, asks who in the world is truly content, undisturbed, and able to hold to a middle way without getting stuck; the Buddha answers by describing someone who lives the spiritual life even among sensual pleasures, free of craving and ever mindful, and calls that person “a great man” who has “escaped the seamstress.”

The frame: the second of sixteen questions

As told in full in Snp 5.1, the aged brahmin teacher Bāvarī sent sixteen of his finest students — Ajita, Tissametteyya, Puṇṇaka, Mettagū, Dhotaka, Upasīva, Nanda, Hemaka, Todeyya, Kappa, Jatukaṇṇī, Bhadrāvudha, Udaya, Posāla, Mogharāja, and Piṅgiya — to find the newly awakened Buddha and question him directly. Ajita went first, in Snp 5.2; Tissametteyya, named second in Bāvarī's list, follows immediately here, at the same gathering, with his own distinct question.

Who is content, and who has no disturbances

Tissametteyya's question is compact but layered: who here in the world is content? Who has no disturbances? What thoughtful person, having known both ends, is not stuck in the middle? Who is called a great man, and who has “escaped the seamstress”? Four questions folded into two verses, all pointing toward the same figure — someone who has resolved whatever tension the “two ends” represent (elsewhere in early Buddhist texts, this phrase is often read as the pull between indulgence and self-denial, or between any pair of opposed extremes) without becoming caught between them.

The spiritual life in the midst of sensual pleasures

The Buddha's answer is unexpected in its phrasing: such a person leads “the spiritual life among sensual pleasures,” not apart from them. The point is not that pleasures are absent from this person’s world, but that craving toward them is gone — “rid of craving, ever mindful,” a mendicant “who, after appraisal, is quenched.” That state, the Buddha says, is exactly what having “no disturbances” means.

A great man who has “escaped the seamstress”

The final verse answers the remaining two questions directly: that same person, having known both ends without being stuck in the middle, is the one the Buddha calls “a great man,” and is the one who has “escaped the seamstress.” The image of a seamstress — stitching things together, binding them — works as a figure for craving's habit of sewing a person back into repeated existence, one attachment stitched to the next. To escape the seamstress is to have nothing left that can be sewn.

Key terms

Tissametteyya — second-named of Bāvarī's sixteen students in Snp 5.1, asking the shortest of the sixteen questions in this chapter.
“known both ends” — a phrase describing someone who has resolved the tension between two extremes without becoming stuck between them, without the sutta specifying exactly which two extremes are meant.
“escaped the seamstress” — the discourse's closing image, read as craving's habit of stitching a person back into repeated existence, one attachment sewn to the next.
context · the sixteen students — Tissametteyya is the second of sixteen brahmin students Bāvarī sends to question the Buddha (see Snp 5.1); each of Snp 5.2 through 5.17 belongs to one of them in turn.

The text

All three verses of Tissametteyya's exchange with the Buddha, given in full — the shortest discourse in this chapter. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1040“Who here in the world is content?”
said Venerable Tissametteyya.
“Who has no disturbances?
What thoughtful one, having known both ends,
is not stuck in the middle?
Who do they say is a great man?
Who here has escaped the seamstress?”

Verse 1041“Leading the spiritual life among sensual pleasures,”
replied the Buddha,
“rid of craving, ever mindful;
a mendicant who, after appraisal, is quenched:
that’s who has no disturbances.

Verse 1042That thoughtful one, having known both ends,
is not stuck in the middle.
He is a great man, I declare,
he has escaped the seamstress here.”

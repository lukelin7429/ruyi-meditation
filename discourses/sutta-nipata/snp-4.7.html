Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 4.7

With Tissametteyya

Tissametteyyasutta

Why this discourse, the seventh of the Aṭṭhakavagga

After the sweeping meditation on mortality in Snp 4.6, the vagga returns to dialogue and to a single, specific, practical question: Venerable Tissametteyya asks about the drawbacks of sexual indulgence for someone who has committed to solitary practice. It is worth noticing what a small-scale, almost mundane question this is compared to the abstract arguments about views and ultimates surrounding it — and that is exactly the discourse’s point. The vagga’s larger theme, that clinging destabilizes a person from the inside, is here demonstrated at the scale of one ordinary human vulnerability, traced step by step from private indulgence to public unraveling.

Reading guide

The discourse in one sentence

Someone who has committed to a solitary path and then resorts to sexual indulgence doesn’t simply add one more habit to their life; they lose their bearings, their reputation erodes, they become defensive and prone to lying under criticism, and a sage who sees this danger clearly resolves in advance to wander alone rather than discover the pattern from the inside.

Verse 1: a direct, practical question

Venerable Tissametteyya asks the discourse’s only question, and it is refreshingly concrete after five discourses of dense philosophical argument: “when someone indulges in sex, tell us, good fellow: what trouble befalls them? After hearing your instruction, we shall train in seclusion.” The question presupposes an answer will be practically useful — not abstract doctrine, but something a person committed to solitary practice can actually use to recognize danger in themselves before it takes hold.

Verses 2–4: forgetting instructions, and a fall from status

The Buddha’s reply begins by naming the first casualty: “they forget their instructions and go the wrong way — that is something ignoble in them.” The teaching itself isn’t rejected or disputed; it simply stops being followed, which the verse treats as itself a form of loss, prior to any further consequence. Verse 3 supplies the discourse’s central image: someone who “formerly lived alone” and then “resorts to sex is like a chariot careening off-track” — not a controlled change of direction but a loss of steering, and “in the world they call them a low, ordinary person,” a reputational fall from whatever standing solitary practice had earned them. Verse 4 names the specific losses: “their former fame and reputation also fall away,” and offers the practical response — “seeing this, they’d train to give up sex” — framed, notably, as a response to clearly seeing consequences, not as an arbitrary rule imposed from outside.

Verses 5–6: brooding, embarrassment, and a slide into lying

What follows is a psychologically exact description of a mind under strain. Verse 5: “oppressed by thoughts, they brood like a wretch. When they hear what others are saying, that sort is embarrassed” — the person has become newly sensitive to others’ opinions in a way solitary confidence had previously protected them from. Verse 6 traces where that embarrassment leads: “then they lash out with verbal daggers when reproached by others. This is their great blind spot; they sink to lies.” The sequence matters — indulgence leads to brooding, brooding to embarrassment at criticism, embarrassment to lashing out defensively, and defensiveness finally to dishonesty. None of these steps was the original choice; they are what unfolds once a person’s footing has already been lost.

Verses 7–9: knowing the danger in advance

Verse 7 states the reversal plainly: someone “once... considered astute, committed to the solitary life,” who “then indulged in sex,” ends up “dragged along by desire like a dullard” — a sharp fall in status from the discourse’s own vocabulary, astute to dullard. Verse 8 draws the discourse’s practical conclusion: “knowing this danger in falling from a former state here, a sage would firmly resolve to wander alone, and would not resort to sex.” The resolve is described as coming before the danger is encountered, based on having clearly understood the pattern verses 2 through 7 just laid out — not a resolution formed in the heat of temptation, which the discourse has just shown to be unreliable, but one settled in advance. Verse 9 adds a characteristically careful qualification: even this seclusion, “highest” as it is for “the noble ones,” shouldn’t become its own occasion for a fresh conceit — “one who’d not think themselves ‘best’ due to that has truly drawn near to extinguishment.”

Verse 10: envy from the outside

The discourse closes by looking at the sage from the perspective of those still caught in the pattern just described: “people tied to sensual pleasures envy them: the isolated, wandering sage who has crossed the flood, unconcerned for sensual pleasures.” The envy is worth noting as the discourse’s final observation — it is not that indulgence looks unattractive from the outside, but that the freedom of someone who has stopped needing it is visibly, even enviably, more stable than anything indulgence itself provides.

How this fits the vagga’s larger argument

This discourse looks, on its surface, like straightforward monastic ethical advice rather than the vagga’s usual subject of clinging to views. But the underlying mechanism verse 5 and 6 trace — indulgence leading to brooding, brooding to embarrassment, embarrassment to lashing out, lashing out to lying — is exactly the same mechanism the discourses on dogmatism describe for a defended opinion. In both cases, something has been staked that shouldn’t have been: a reputation for solitary discipline in this case, a claim to correct doctrine in the case of Snp 4.3 through 4.5. In both cases, once that stake is threatened, defensiveness and dishonesty follow almost automatically, not because the person set out to be defensive or dishonest, but because protecting the stake becomes more urgent than telling the truth. The chariot careening off-track and the mind embroiled in dispute are, structurally, the same image.

Key terms

methuna — sexual intercourse or sensual indulgence, the discourse’s specific subject, addressed here in terms of its practical effect on someone committed to solitary practice rather than in abstract moral terms.
the careening chariot — verse 3’s image for a formerly solitary person who resorts to indulgence: not a controlled change of course but a loss of steering.
the sequence of verses 5–6 — brooding, embarrassment at criticism, lashing out, and finally lying: a step-by-step psychological account of how a lost footing compounds itself.
“would not think themselves ‘best’” — verse 9’s caution against turning even genuine seclusion into a fresh source of pride, closely paralleling the vagga’s wider critique of ranking oneself against others.
Tissametteyya — the questioner of this discourse, traditionally identified as one of the Buddha’s disciples; his question is direct and practical, unlike the philosophical challenges other figures pose elsewhere in this vagga.

The text

All ten verses of the Tissametteyyasutta, complete. Verses 2–10 form a single continuous reply from the Buddha. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1“When someone indulges in sex,”
said Venerable Tissametteyya,
“tell us, good fellow: what trouble befalls them?
After hearing your instruction,
we shall train in seclusion.”

Verse 2“When someone indulges in sex,”
replied the Buddha,
“they forget their instructions
and go the wrong way—
that is something ignoble in them.

Verse 3Someone who formerly lived alone
and then resorts to sex
is like a chariot careening off-track;
in the world they call them a low, ordinary person.

Verse 4Their former fame and reputation
also fall away.
Seeing this, they’d train
to give up sex.

Verse 5Oppressed by thoughts,
they brood like a wretch.
When they hear what others are saying,
that sort is embarrassed.

Verse 6Then they lash out with verbal daggers
when reproached by others.
This is their great blind spot;
they sink to lies.

Verse 7They once were considered astute,
committed to the solitary life.
But then they indulged in sex,
dragged along by desire like a dullard.

Verse 8Knowing this danger
in falling from a former state here,
a sage would firmly resolve to wander alone,
and would not resort to sex.

Verse 9They’d train themselves only in seclusion;
this, for the noble ones, is highest.
One who’d not think themselves ‘best’ due to that
has truly drawn near to extinguishment.

Verse 10People tied to sensual pleasures envy them:
the isolated, wandering sage
who has crossed the flood,
unconcerned for sensual pleasures.”

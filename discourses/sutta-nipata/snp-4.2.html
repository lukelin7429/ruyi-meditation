Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 4.2

Eight on the Cave

Guhaṭṭhakasutta

Why this discourse, the second of the Aṭṭhakavagga

The Guhaṭṭhakasutta opens with an image striking enough to give the discourse its name: beings “trapped in a cave, thickly overspread, sunk in delusion.” The cave in question is not a literal place — the early commentarial tradition reads it as the body, or more precisely as embodied existence itself, the physical basis from which craving, view, and identity all get generated. What makes this discourse distinctive is its blunt admission that liberation is not something one person can simply hand another: “the chains of desire, the bonds of life’s pleasures are hard to escape, for one cannot free another.” That single line reframes everything that follows — the counsel here isn’t comfort, it is a clear-eyed description of a difficulty each person has to work through for themselves.

Reading guide

The discourse in one sentence

Beings caught in the “cave” of embodied craving cannot be freed by anyone else and, gripped by regret only once suffering has already arrived, need to train now, while life is short, toward a specific kind of freedom: no longer clinging to the seen, the heard, or to any possession, in this life or hope for the next.

Verses 1–2: a trap no one else can spring

The opening verse pictures beings “trapped in a cave, thickly overspread, sunk in delusion” — “far from seclusion,” the verse says, meaning far from the inward clarity that comes from stepping back from constant sensory involvement, “for sensual pleasures in the world are not easy to give up.” Verse 2 then makes the discourse’s hardest claim explicit: “the chains of desire, the bonds of life’s pleasures are hard to escape, for one cannot free another.” This is not fatalism. It is a precise statement about the limits of what any teacher, however wise, or any doctrine, however true, can do from the outside. A description of the way out can be handed over; the actual letting-go of one’s own attachments cannot be performed by proxy. The same verse adds a second, quieter observation — that people caught in this bind keep “looking to the past or the future,” praying for pleasures already gone or pleasures not yet arrived, which is itself a description of a mind that is never quite present with what is actually here.

Verses 3–4: regret that comes too late, and the answer to it

Verse 3 describes a familiar psychological pattern: people “greedy, fixated, infatuated by sensual pleasures” who become “incorrigible, habitually immoral,” and only when “led to suffering” do they lament, asking “what will become of us when we pass away from here?” The question arrives exactly when it is least useful — after the pattern has already hardened into habit. Verse 4 answers this timing problem directly: “that’s why a personage should train in this life” — not later, not in some future circumstance more conducive to practice, but now, because “the attentive say this life is short.” The verse also offers a small, exact piece of ethical guidance embedded in the middle of the philosophical material: “should you know that anything in the world is wrong, don’t act wrongly on account of that” — recognizing a fault in the world is not an excuse to match it.

Verses 5–6: fish in a drying stream

The discourse’s second major image follows: “see them flounder over belongings, like fish in puddles of a dried-up stream.” It is a merciless but accurate picture — beings who have organized their lives around possessions discover, too late, that the water that made those possessions meaningful is already receding, and what is left is frantic movement in a shrinking space. Verse 5 widens the lens further, describing “the world’s population floundering, given to craving for future lives,” with “base men” who “wail in the jaws of death, not rid of craving for life after life” — extending the same diagnosis from a single lifetime’s possessions to the deeper craving for continued existence itself. The response the verses offer is not despair but a redirection: “seeing this, live unselfishly, forming no attachment to future lives.”

Verses 7–8: understanding contact and perception

The closing pair introduces vocabulary that will recur across the whole vagga: contact (phassa) and perception (saññā), the points where raw sense experience first gets processed into something a mind can want or resist. Verse 7 describes someone “rid of desire for both ends” — not swinging between craving existence and craving its opposite — who, “having completely understood contact,” does nothing they would blame themselves for and does “not cling to the seen and the heard.” Verse 8 completes the picture: having “completely understood perception and having crossed the flood,” the sage doesn’t cling to possessions, has the “dart” (the arrow-strike image from the previous discourse) already plucked out, and “does not hope for this world or the next.” The discourse ends, in other words, not with a rejection of experience but with a description of experience fully seen through — contact and perception understood so thoroughly that they no longer manufacture craving on their own.

The cave as a way of naming attachment to identity

It is worth being precise about what “the cave” is doing as an image, because it is easy to flatten into a generic metaphor for ignorance. A cave is enclosed, dark, and — crucially — a place a person can settle into as if it were home, mistaking its confines for the whole world. Read this way, the cave names something close to a felt sense of self built up out of unexamined craving and views: not merely “delusion” in the abstract, but the specific, comfortable enclosure of taking one’s accumulated wants, opinions, and possessions to be who one actually is. That is exactly the theme the rest of the vagga will pursue, moving from craving for objects and future lives here toward craving to be right, to have one’s view vindicated, to be the one whose position wins the argument. The cave doesn’t need walls of stone to be a trap; unexamined identification with an opinion works the same way.

Key terms

guhā — “cave”: the discourse’s central image, traditionally read as embodied existence itself, the enclosure a person can mistake for the whole of reality.
phassa — “contact,” the meeting point of a sense faculty, a sense object, and consciousness; verse 7 names its full understanding as what stops clinging to “the seen and the heard” at the root.
saññā — “perception,” the mental act of recognizing and labeling experience; verse 8 pairs its full understanding with “crossing the flood.”
“one cannot free another” — the discourse’s most direct statement of the limits of external help: teaching can point the way, but the letting-go itself cannot be performed by proxy.
fish in a drying stream — the discourse’s image for beings who have staked their sense of security on possessions that are already, imperceptibly, running out.

The text

All eight verses of the Guhaṭṭhakasutta, complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1Trapped in a cave, thickly overspread,
sunk in delusion they stay.
That sort is far from seclusion,
for sensual pleasures in the world
are not easy to give up.

Verse 2The chains of desire, the bonds of life’s pleasures
are hard to escape, for one cannot free another.
Looking to the past or the future,
they pray for these pleasures or former ones.

Verse 3Greedy, fixated, infatuated by sensual pleasures,
they are incorrigible, habitually immoral.
When led to suffering they lament,
“What will become of us
when we pass away from here?”

Verse 4That’s why a personage should train in this life:
should you know that anything in the world is wrong,
don’t act wrongly on account of that;
for the attentive say this life is short.

Verse 5I see the world’s population floundering,
given to craving for future lives.
Base men wail in the jaws of death,
not rid of craving for life after life.

Verse 6See them flounder over belongings,
like fish in puddles of a dried-up stream.
Seeing this, live unselfishly,
forming no attachment to future lives.

Verse 7Rid of desire for both ends,
having completely understood contact, free of greed,
doing nothing for which they’d blame themselves,
the attentive don’t cling to the seen and the heard.

Verse 8Having completely understood perception
and having crossed the flood,
the sage, not clinging to possessions,
with dart plucked out, living diligently,
does not hope for this world or the next.

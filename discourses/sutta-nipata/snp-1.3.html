Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 1.3

The Horned Rhino

Khaggavisāṇasutta

Setting
None given — like Snp 1.1, the verses are recited with no narrative frame at all
Speakers
None named. Tradition associates these verses with paccekabuddhas, solitary awakened figures who arrive at insight without a teacher, though the text itself names no speaker
Form
Continuous verse — forty-one verses built on the refrain “live alone like a horned rhino,” with one two-verse exception (11–12) commending wise companionship first
Length
~11 minutes to read
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — long and cumulative, with imagery ranging from immediately clear (a lion, an elephant) to locally specific (the Shady Orchid Tree)

Why this discourse, the third

This is the single most quoted discourse to come out of the Sutta Nipāta’s opening chapter, and the source of an image that has entered English on its own terms: the rhinoceros horn, a solitary spike rather than a paired antler, standing for a kind of aloneness that isn’t loneliness so much as unentangled clarity. Forty-one verses build the case from nearly every angle available — the danger in affection, the hazard of shallow friendship, a literal catalogue of physical hardship to be endured, and, in a genuine surprise partway through, two verses that reverse the entire argument: if you actually find a wise and steady companion, the text says, travel with them joyfully. Solitude, this discourse insists, is the second-best option — chosen only because good companionship is so rare.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Forty-one verses argue, from many different angles — the risk in attachment, the burden of relationships, the test of physical hardship, the scarcity of real friendship — that a serious practitioner who cannot find a wise companion should walk the path alone, “like a horned rhino,” single-pointed rather than paired.

A refrain without an introduction

Like the Uragasutta that opens the vagga, this discourse names no speaker, no place, and no listener. Its unity comes entirely from the refrain that closes almost every verse — “live alone like a horned rhino” — built on the image of the rhinoceros’s single horn, as against the paired horns or antlers of most other animals. The refrain varies only twice: once into “wander alone like a tusker in the wilds” (v.12), and once, in the discourse’s sole real exception, into an instruction to travel with someone (v.11).

Why solitude: the risk built into closeness

The opening verses state the danger plainly. Verse 1 begins from non-violence — “laid down violence toward all creatures, not harming even a single one” — and moves immediately to relationships: “don’t wish for a child, let alone a companion.” Verse 2 names the mechanism directly: “those with close relationships have affection, following which this pain arises.” Verse 3 calls intimacy a place where “you miss out on the goal”; verse 4 compares concern for partners and children to a spreading, entangling bamboo. None of this argues that relationships are false or worthless — only that they reliably produce exactly the kind of pain the practice is meant to end.

The one exception: verses 11 and 12

After ten verses building toward solitude, the discourse pauses to say something unexpected: “if you find an alert companion, an attentive friend to live happily together, then, overcoming all adversities, wander with them, joyful and mindful” (v.11). Only if no such companion can be found does verse 12 return to the refrain, and even then with an image of retreat rather than simple preference — “like a king who flees his conquered realm, wander alone like a tusker in the wilds.” Solitude, on this reading, is not the discourse’s ideal so much as its fallback: verse 13 makes the ranking explicit, “we praise the blessing of a friend, it’s good to be with friends your equal or better” — “but failing to find them,” the refrain returns.

False friendship and true

Several verses return to distinguish real companionship from its counterfeit. Verse 14 compares two bracelets sharing one arm, made of the same shining gold, that nonetheless “clash up against each other” — a warning that even superficially matched people in close proximity generate friction. Verse 23 counsels avoiding “a wicked companion, blind to the good, habitually immoral,” while verse 24 commends the opposite: time spent with “the learned who have memorized the teachings, an eloquent and uplifting friend.” The discourse’s final verse (41) names the problem most bluntly: “they befriend you and serve you for their own sake; these days it’s hard to find friends lacking ulterior motive.”

A catalogue of hardship

Verse 18 lists, without elaboration, exactly what solitary practice is expected to withstand: “heat and cold, hunger and thirst, wind and sun, flies and snakes.” It is one of the discourse’s most concrete moments — a reminder that the solitude being recommended is not a metaphor but an actual, physically demanding way of living, undertaken without the shelter a household or a community would normally provide.

Not cold, even alone: verse 39

Near the close, the discourse specifies what the solitary practitioner is meant to cultivate, and it is not indifference: “in time, cultivate freedom through love, compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity. Not upset by anything in the world, live alone like a horned rhino.” Traditionally grouped as the four divine qualities, these are inherently relational states of mind, oriented toward other beings — their presence here, inside a discourse about walking away from company, is a deliberate qualification: the aloneness recommended is not coldness toward others, only freedom from entanglement with them.

Key terms

khaggavisāṇa — “rhinoceros horn”: a single horn, unlike the paired horns or antlers of most animals, used throughout as the image for undivided, unpaired solitude.
paccekabuddha — “solitary buddha”: a figure who reaches awakening without a teacher and does not found a wider teaching community; tradition associates this discourse’s verses with such figures, though the text does not name a speaker.
the five mental obstacles — named in verse 32 as given up together with “all corruptions” and “affection and hate,” on the way to independence.
love, compassion, rejoicing, equanimity — the four qualities named in verse 39 as what the solitary practitioner cultivates, traditionally grouped as the four divine abodes (brahmavihārā) — inherently relational states, even within a discourse about walking alone.

The text

All forty-one verses of the Khaggavisāṇasutta, given in full and in order, including the two-verse exception on companionship (11–12) exactly as it appears in sequence. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1When you’ve laid down violence toward all creatures,
not harming even a single one,
don’t wish for a child, let alone a companion:
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 2Those with close relationships have affection,
following which this pain arises.
Seeing this danger born of affection,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 3When feelings for friends and loved ones
are tied up in selfish love, you miss out on the goal.
Seeing this peril in intimacy,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 4As a spreading bamboo gets entangled,
so does concern for partners and children.
Like a bamboo shoot unobstructed,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 5As a wild deer loose in the forest
grazes wherever it wants,
a smart person looking for freedom would
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 6When among friends, whether staying in place
or going on a journey, you’re always on call.
Looking for the uncoveted freedom,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 7Among friends you have fun and games,
and for children you are full of fondness.
Though loathe to depart from those you hold dear,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 8At ease in any quarter, unresisting,
content with whatever comes your way;
prevailing over adversities, dauntless,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 9Even some renunciates are hard to please,
as are some layfolk dwelling at home.
Don’t worry about others’ children,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 10Having shed the marks of the home life,
like the fallen leaves of the Shady Orchid Tree;
having cut the bonds of the home life, a hero would
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 11If you find an alert companion,
an attentive friend to live happily together,
then, overcoming all adversities,
wander with them, joyful and mindful.

Verse 12If you find no alert companion,
an attentive friend to live happily together,
then, like a king who flees his conquered realm,
wander alone like a tusker in the wilds.

Verse 13Clearly we praise the blessing of a friend,
it’s good to be with friends your equal or better.
But failing to find them, eating blamelessly,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 14Though made of shining gold, well-finished by a smith,
when two bracelets share the same arm
they clash up against each other. Seeing this,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 15Thinking, “So too, if I had a partner,
there’d be flattery or curses.”
Seeing this peril in the future,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 16Sensual pleasures are diverse, sweet, delightful,
appearing in disguise they disturb the mind.
Seeing danger in sensual stimulations,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 17This is a calamity, a boil, a disaster,
an illness, a dart, and a danger for me.
Seeing this peril in sensuality,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 18Heat and cold, hunger and thirst,
wind and sun, flies and snakes:
having put up with all these things,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 19As a full-grown elephant, lotus-fond, magnificent,
forsaking the herd,
stays as it pleases in the forest,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 20It’s impossible for one who delights in company
to experience even temporary freedom.
Heeding the speech of the Kinsman of the Sun,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 21Thinking, “I have left twisted views behind,
reached the sure way, and gained the path;
I’ve given rise to knowledge, and need no-one to guide me”,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 22No greed, no guile, no thirst, no slur,
dross and delusion is smelted off;
free of hoping for anything in the world,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 23Avoid a wicked companion,
blind to the good, habitually immoral.
Don’t befriend the heedless and the hankering, but
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 24Spend time with the learned who have memorized the teachings,
an eloquent and uplifting friend.
When you understand the meanings and have dispelled doubt,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 25When you realize that worldly fun and games
and pleasure are inadequate, unconcerned with them,
as one unadorned, a speaker of truth,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 26Children, partner, father, mother,
wealth and grain and relatives:
having given up sensual pleasures to this extent,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 27“This is a chain. Here there’s hardly any happiness,
little gratification, and it’s full of drawbacks.
It’s a hook.” Knowing this, a thoughtful person would
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 28Having burst apart the fetters,
like a fish that tears the net and swims free,
or a fire not returning to ground it has burned,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 29Eyes downcast, not footloose,
senses guarded, mind protected,
not festering or burning with desire,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 30Having shed the marks of the home life,
like the fallen leaves of the Orchid Tree,
and gone forth in the ocher robe,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 31Not wanton, nor rousing greed for tastes,
providing for no other, wandering indiscriminately for alms,
not attached to this family or that,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 32When you’ve given up five mental obstacles,
and cast aside all corruptions,
and cut off affection and hate, being independent,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 33When you’ve put pleasure and pain behind you,
and former happiness and sadness,
and gained equanimity serene and pure,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 34With energy roused to reach the ultimate goal,
not sluggish in mind or lazy,
vigorous, strong and powerful,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 35Not neglecting retreat and absorption,
always living in line with the teaching,
comprehending the danger in rebirths,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 36One whose aim is the ending of craving—
diligent, clever, learned, mindful, resolute—
who has appraised the teaching and is assured, destined for awakening, should
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 37Like a lion not startled by sounds,
like wind not caught in a net,
like water not sticking to a lotus,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 38Like the fierce-fanged lion, king of beasts,
who wanders as conqueror and victor,
you should frequent remote lodgings, and
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 39In time, cultivate freedom through
love, compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity.
Not upset by anything in the world,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 40Having given up greed, hate, and delusion,
having burst apart the fetters,
unafraid at the end of life,
live alone like a horned rhino.

Verse 41They befriend you and serve you for their own sake;
these days it’s hard to find friends lacking ulterior motive.
Impure folk cleverly profit themselves—
live alone like a horned rhino.

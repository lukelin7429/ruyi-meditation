Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 5.4

The Questions of Puṇṇaka

Puṇṇakamāṇavapucchā

Setting
Continuing directly from Snp 5.1–5.3 — the same gathering at the Pāsāṇaka Shrine near Rājagaha, the third student now stepping forward
Speakers
The Buddha and the student Puṇṇaka, third of Bāvarī's sixteen students named in Snp 5.1
Form
Six verses of paired question-and-answer, escalating across three exchanges toward a single point
Length
~2 minutes to read
Frame
Part of one continuous work: see Snp 5.1 for the full story of Bāvarī and his sixteen students
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a clear, escalating argument, among the more accessible exchanges in this chapter

Why this discourse, the third question

Puṇṇaka, third-named among Bāvarī's sixteen students, asks a question with real stakes for a group of brahmins raised on ritual practice: why do sages and brahmins across the world perform so many different sacrifices to the gods? The Buddha's first answer is blunt — all such sacrifice is “bound to old age,” performed by people still longing for a particular kind of rebirth. Puṇṇaka pushes further, asking directly whether diligent sacrifice actually gets anyone past rebirth and old age, and the Buddha says no: hoping, invoking, praying, and worshiping for a good result is itself the very thing that keeps a person bound. Only in the third and final exchange does the Buddha describe what actually does cross over — not another ritual, but the simple absence of hope itself.

Reading guide

The discourse in one sentence

Puṇṇaka, the third of Bāvarī's sixteen students, asks why sages and brahmins perform so many different sacrifices to the gods, and whether doing so diligently actually crosses over rebirth and old age; the Buddha answers, across three escalating exchanges, that sacrifice performed in hope of a good result cannot do this — only someone who has appraised the world and needs nothing more from it has truly crossed over.

The frame: the third of sixteen questions

As Snp 5.1 tells in full, the aged brahmin teacher Bāvarī sent sixteen of his finest students — Ajita, Tissametteyya, Puṇṇaka, Mettagū, Dhotaka, Upasīva, Nanda, Hemaka, Todeyya, Kappa, Jatukaṇṇī, Bhadrāvudha, Udaya, Posāla, Mogharāja, and Piṅgiya — to question the newly awakened Buddha directly. Ajita and Tissametteyya have already asked their questions in Snp 5.2 and 5.3; Puṇṇaka, third in Bāvarī's list, now steps forward with a question that goes to the heart of the very ritual tradition all sixteen students were trained in.

Why sacrifice at all?

Puṇṇaka's opening question is broad and almost sociological: on what grounds have “seers and men, aristocrats and brahmins” performed so many different kinds of sacrifice to the gods? It is a question about religious motive itself, asked by someone raised inside that very tradition. The Buddha's first answer cuts straight to the underlying cause: whatever sacrifices have been performed, by anyone, were “bound to old age, longing for this place” — that is, offered by people hoping for continued existence in some favorable form, still caught in the wish to keep going.

Does diligent sacrifice actually work?

Puṇṇaka does not let the point go easily. He asks the follow-up question directly: have those who are diligent in the technique of sacrifice actually crossed over rebirth and old age? The Buddha's answer is unambiguous: “hoping, invoking, praying, and worshiping” for the sake of profit, “devoted to sacrifice, besotted by rebirth” — such people have not crossed over rebirth and old age. The verb choices matter: hoping, invoking, praying are all activities oriented toward a future outcome, and it is precisely that orientation, not the ritual technique itself, that the Buddha identifies as the problem.

Who, then, has actually crossed over?

Puṇṇaka's final question follows naturally: if sacrifice does not get anyone across, then who, among gods and humans, has actually crossed over rebirth and old age? The Buddha's answer turns from ritual entirely to appraisal and freedom from need: someone who has “appraised the world high and low,” finding nothing in it that disturbs them, “peaceful, unclouded, untroubled, with no need for hope” — that person has crossed over. The phrase “no need for hope” directly answers Puṇṇaka's opening question: sacrifice was always an expression of hope, and it is precisely hope's absence, not any technique, that marks the one who is actually free.

Key terms

Puṇṇaka — third-named of Bāvarī's sixteen students in Snp 5.1, whose question challenges the value of ritual sacrifice directly.
“bound to old age” — the Buddha's diagnosis of all sacrifice performed by seers, brahmins, and aristocrats: an expression of longing for continued, favorable existence.
“no need for hope” — the discourse's closing description of someone who has actually crossed rebirth and old age, directly answering Puṇṇaka's opening question about why people sacrifice at all.
context · the sixteen students — Puṇṇaka is the third of sixteen brahmin students Bāvarī sends to question the Buddha (see Snp 5.1); each of Snp 5.2 through 5.17 belongs to one of them in turn.

The text

All six verses of Puṇṇaka's exchange with the Buddha, given in full. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1043“To the imperturbable, the seer of the root,”
said Venerable Puṇṇaka,
“I have come seeking with a question.
On what grounds have seers and men,
aristocrats and brahmins here in the world
performed so many different sacrifices to the gods?
I ask you, Blessed One; please tell me this.”

Verse 1044“Whatever seers and men,”
replied the Buddha,
“aristocrats and brahmins here in the world
have performed so many different sacrifices to the gods:
all performed sacrifices bound to old age,
longing for this place.”

Verse 1045“As to those seers and men,”
said Venerable Puṇṇaka,
“and aristocrats and brahmins here in the world
who have performed so many different sacrifices to the gods:
being diligent in the technique of sacrifice,
have they crossed over rebirth and old age, good fellow?
I ask you, Blessed One; please tell me this.”

Verse 1046“Hoping, invoking, praying, and worshiping,”
replied the Buddha,
“they pray for pleasure derived from profit.
Devoted to sacrifice, besotted by rebirth,
they’ve not crossed over rebirth and old age, I declare.”

Verse 1047“If those devoted to sacrifice,”
said Venerable Puṇṇaka,
“have not, by sacrificing, crossed over rebirth and old age,
who then in the world of gods and humans
has crossed over rebirth and old age, good fellow?
I ask you, Blessed One; please tell me this.”

Verse 1048“Having appraised the world high and low,”
replied the Buddha,
“there is nothing in the world that disturbs them.
Peaceful, unclouded, untroubled, with no need for hope—
they’ve crossed over rebirth and old age, I declare.”

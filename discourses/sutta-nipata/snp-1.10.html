Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 1.10

With Āḷavaka

Āḷavakasutta

Setting
Near Āḷavī, in the very haunt of the native spirit Āḷavaka — the Buddha stays overnight on the fierce yakkha’s own territory
Speakers
The Buddha and the native spirit (yakkha) Āḷavaka, who threatens him before questioning him
Form
A prose frame of escalating threats, giving way to twelve riddle-like verses of questions and answers
Length
~5 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No complete Chinese Āgama counterpart is known to survive for this discourse specifically, consistent with the Sutta Nipāta as a whole
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a vivid, almost folkloric opening scene followed by compact, quotable verse

Why this discourse, the tenth

Āḷavaka does not politely request an audience. He orders the Buddha out of his own haunt, then orders him back in, four times over, before threatening to drive him insane, explode his heart, or hurl him across the Ganges if he fails to answer a question. The Buddha’s reply is remarkable for its total absence of anxiety: he simply notes that no one in the entire universe, human or divine, could actually do that to him — and invites the question anyway. What follows is one of the Sutta Nipāta’s most quotable exchanges, a set of terse riddles about wealth, happiness, taste, and grief, answered in language plain enough to convert a hostile spirit on the spot.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A fierce native spirit threatens the Buddha, then poses a set of terse riddles about wealth, happiness, taste, crossing the flood, and how a person departs this life without grief; the Buddha’s plain, memorable answers — faith, truth, wisdom, and generosity chief among them — convert Āḷavaka into a devoted follower by the discourse’s end.

Get out, get in: a test of composure

The scene is almost comic in its repetition. The Buddha, staying overnight in Āḷavaka’s own territory, is ordered out of the dwelling, then back in, then out again, then in again — four full rounds — and each time responds with the same unruffled “All right, respectable sir” and simply complies. Only on the fourth “get out” does he decline, plainly: “No, respectable sir, I won’t get out. Do what you must.” The refusal is not defiance for its own sake; it reads as the Buddha declining to be provoked into an endless, meaningless contest, while still leaving Āḷavaka free to act as he wishes.

A threat, and an answer without fear

Āḷavaka escalates immediately to explicit violence: answer a question correctly, or be driven insane, have one’s heart exploded, or be seized by the feet and thrown across the Ganges. The Buddha’s reply is worth reading closely for what it does not do — it does not bargain, does not appeal to protection, does not try to talk Āḷavaka down. It simply states a fact: “I don’t see anyone in this world with its gods, Māras, and Divinities… who could do that to me.” This is not bravado so much as a description of a mind with nothing left for such a threat to grip. Having said it, he immediately invites the question anyway — the confrontation dissolves into an actual conversation.

Verses 1–4: wealth, happiness, taste, and the flood

Āḷavaka’s first four questions are compact and universal — what is a person’s best wealth, what practice brings happiness, what is the sweetest taste, how does one cross the flood. The Buddha’s answers redefine each ordinary-sounding term: “faith here is a person’s best wealth,” not gold or property; “the teaching brings happiness when practiced well”; “truth is the sweetest taste of all,” outranking any literal flavor; and the flood, the deluge, suffering, and impurity are each crossed or overcome by a specific quality in turn — faith, diligence, energy, and wisdom. Four everyday words, each quietly redefined around an inner quality rather than an external good.

Verses 5–8: wisdom, wealth, reputation, friends, and grief

Āḷavaka’s second round of questions turns to practical life — how to gain wisdom, earn wealth, build a good reputation, keep friends, and, in its most striking line, how the departed do not grieve “when passing from this world to the next.” The Buddha’s answer treats these as connected rather than separate achievements: wisdom through eagerness to learn and faith in the teaching; wealth through responsibility and hard work; reputation through truthfulness; friendship maintained by giving. The answer to grief closes the sequence with a named set of four qualities — “truth, principle, steadfastness, and generosity” — said to let a faithful, discerning householder pass away without regret.

An open invitation to verify

Before Āḷavaka responds, the Buddha adds something unusual for a teacher confident in his answer: “Go ahead, ask others as well, there are many ascetics and brahmins. See whether anything better is found than truth, principle, generosity, and patience.” Rather than asking for uncritical acceptance, the invitation is explicitly to comparison-shop the claim against every other teacher available — a striking gesture of confidence that does not require the listener’s unquestioning trust.

A conversion, and a vow to spread the word

Āḷavaka’s closing verses answer that invitation directly: “Why now would I question the many ascetics and brahmins? Today I understand what’s good for the next life.” He reflects that the Buddha’s presence at Āḷavī was, in fact, “truly for my benefit,” and pledges, like the thousand spirits at the end of Snp 1.9, to travel “village to village, town to town,” carrying word of the teaching. A spirit who opened the encounter with threats of violence closes it planning a speaking tour on the Buddha’s behalf.

Key terms

Āḷavaka — a fierce native spirit (yakkha) whose haunt near the town of Āḷavī the Buddha stays in overnight; his name gives the discourse its title.
“faith is a person’s best wealth” — the first and most quoted of the Buddha’s riddle-answers, redefining an ordinary word around an inner quality.
the flood (ogha) — the same canonical image for the defilements that appears in Snp 1.9, here crossed specifically “by faith.”
truth, principle, steadfastness, generosity — the four qualities the Buddha names as letting a householder pass away without grief.

The text

The discourse in full: the confrontation, the threat, and all twelve verses of question and answer. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Get out, get in

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Āḷavī in the haunt of the native spirit Āḷavaka. Then the native spirit Āḷavaka went up to the Buddha, and said to him: “Get out, ascetic!” Saying, “All right, respectable sir,” the Buddha went out. “Get in, ascetic!” Saying, “All right, respectable sir,” the Buddha went in.

§2For a second time… And for a third time the native spirit Āḷavaka said to the Buddha, “Get out, ascetic!” Saying, “All right, respectable sir,” the Buddha went out. “Get in, ascetic!” Saying, “All right, respectable sir,” the Buddha went in.

§3And for a fourth time the native spirit Āḷavaka said to the Buddha, “Get out, ascetic!” “No, respectable sir, I won’t get out. Do what you must.”

A threat, and an answer without fear

§4“I will ask you a question, ascetic. If you don’t answer me, I’ll drive you insane, or explode your heart, or grab you by the feet and throw you to the far shore of the Ganges!”

§5“I don’t see anyone in this world with its gods, Māras, and Divinities, this population with its ascetics and brahmins, its gods and humans who could do that to me. But anyway, ask what you wish.” Then the native spirit Āḷavaka addressed the Buddha in verse:

Wealth, happiness, taste, and the flood

Verse 1“What’s a person’s best wealth?
What brings happiness when practiced well?
What’s the sweetest taste of all?
The one they say has the best life: how do they live?”

Verse 2“Faith here is a person’s best wealth.
the teaching brings happiness when practiced well.
Truth is the sweetest taste of all.
The one they say has the best life lives by wisdom.”

Verse 3“How do you cross the flood?
How do you cross the deluge?
How do you get over suffering?
How do you get purified?”

Verse 4“By faith you cross the flood,
and by diligence the deluge.
By energy you get past suffering,
and you’re purified by wisdom.”

Wisdom, wealth, reputation, friends, and grief

Verse 5“How do you get wisdom?
How do you earn wealth?
How do you get a good reputation?
How do you hold on to friends?
How do the departed not grieve
when passing from this world to the next?”

Verse 6“One who is diligent and discerning
gains wisdom by wanting to learn,
having faith in the perfected ones,
and the teaching for becoming extinguished.

Verse 7Being responsible, acting appropriately,
and working hard you earn wealth.
Truthfulness wins you a good reputation.
You hold on to friends by giving.

Verse 8A faithful householder of discernment
who possesses these four qualities
does not grieve after passing away:
truth, principle, steadfastness, and generosity.

An open invitation to verify

Verse 9Go ahead, ask others as well,
there are many ascetics and brahmins.
See whether anything better is found
than truth, principle, generosity, and patience.”

A conversion, and a vow to spread the word

Verse 10“Why now would I question
the many ascetics and brahmins?
Today I understand
what’s good for the next life.

Verse 11It was truly for my benefit
that the Buddha came to stay at Āḷavī.
Today I understand
where a gift is very fruitful.

Verse 12I myself will journey
village to village, town to town,
paying homage to the Buddha,
and the clear rightness of the teaching!”

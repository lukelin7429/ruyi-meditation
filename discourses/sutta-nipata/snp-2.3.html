Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 2.3

Conscientious Companionship

Hirisutta

Setting
No narrative frame; the discourse consists entirely of unattributed gnomic verse
Speakers
An unnamed teaching voice, addressing the listener directly (“know they’re not on your side”)
Form
Five short verses contrasting false friendship (words without follow-through) with true friendship (steady, undoubting, joy-producing)
Length
<1 minute to read
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — brief and aphoristic, though its final image repays a second look

Why this discourse

Five verses, no story, no dialogue partner — just a compact meditation on a distinction everyone has run into and few articulate this precisely: the difference between someone who says “I’m on your side” and someone who actually is. The sutta’s title turns on hiri, conscience or moral shame, and the argument is that a friend worth the name is exactly the person willing to risk your displeasure by naming your faults, rather than the person who only ever tells you what you want to hear.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A friend who flouts conscience and only ever speaks pleasantly, without ever following words with deeds, is no true friend — a true friend is one you can rest on without suspicion, “like a child on the breast.”

What a title word carries

Hiri means conscience, or moral shame — the inner sense that keeps a person from doing wrong even unobserved. Naming the sutta after it is a quiet piece of framing: the “companionship” being described is not simply pleasant company, but a relationship in which conscience is actively present — a friend who has enough regard for you, and enough honesty, to risk the friendship itself by telling you something you don’t want to hear.

The false friend, twice described

The opening two verses both describe the same failure from slightly different angles. Verse 1: someone who “flouts conscience, loathing it,” while still insisting “I’m on your side” — but “not following up in deeds.” The verdict is blunt: “know they’re not on your side.” Verse 2 restates the same pattern in social terms — people who “say nice things to their friends without following it up” — and adds a note of confidence: “the wise will recognize one who talks without doing.” The repetition is deliberate. This is presented not as a subtle failing that requires great insight to detect, but as a pattern anyone paying attention will eventually see through.

The true friend, and a striking image

Verse 3 turns to the positive case, and gives the sutta its most memorable line. A true friend does not “relentlessly suspect betrayal, looking for fault” — there is no low simmering distrust to manage. Instead: “one on whom you rest, like a child on the breast, is a true friend, not split from you by others.” The comparison is deliberately unguarded — a nursing child has no strategy, no wariness, no calculation about whether the trust is warranted. That total, unselfconscious reliance is offered as the measure of real friendship, along with a second criterion: such a bond cannot be pulled apart by outside interference (“not split from you by others”).

The reward of showing up

Verse 4 shifts from describing the friend to describing the effect of being one: someone “whose reward is the fruit of bearing the burden of service” — that is, someone who actually does the effortful, sometimes unglamorous work friendship requires — “develops a happy state, producing joy and attracting praise.” The claim is practical rather than purely moral: genuine follow-through is not a sacrifice with no return: it generates its own happiness.

From companionship to seclusion

The final verse makes an unexpected turn. Having spent four verses on human companionship, the sutta closes with an image of solitude: “one who has drunk the nectar of seclusion and the nectar of peace, free of stress, free of evil, drinks the joyous nectar of Dhamma.” Nothing in the text explains the connection explicitly, but the placement suggests one: the same honesty and reliability that make for real friendship — no performance, no fear of exposure, no need to manage what others think — are also the qualities that make solitary practice possible. A person capable of the steady, undoubting trust described in verse 3 is, perhaps, also the person capable of being equally at ease alone.

Key terms

hiri — conscience, or moral shame; the sutta’s title word, framing genuine friendship as a relationship where conscience is actively present.
“like a child on the breast” — the sutta’s central image for true friendship: total, unselfconscious reliance, free of calculation or suspicion.
amata (“nectar”) — the word rendered “nectar” in the closing verse also carries the sense of “the deathless”; the same term used elsewhere in the canon for the goal of practice itself.

The text

All five verses, complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The false friend

Verse 1Flouting conscience, loathing it,
saying “I’m on your side”,
but not following up in deeds—
know they’re not on your side.

Verse 2Some say nice things to their friends
without following it up.
The wise will recognize
one who talks without doing.

The true friend

Verse 3No true friend relentlessly
suspects betrayal, looking for fault.
One on whom you rest, like a child on the breast,
is a true friend, not split from you by others.

The reward of showing up

Verse 4One whose reward is the fruit
of bearing the burden of service
develops a happy state,
producing joy and attracting praise.

From companionship to seclusion

Verse 5One who has drunk the nectar of seclusion
and the nectar of peace,
free of stress, free of evil,
drinks the joyous nectar of Dhamma.

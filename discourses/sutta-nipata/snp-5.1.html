Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 5.1

Introductory Verses

Vatthugāthā

Setting
The brahmin Bāvarī’s hermitage on the Godāvarī River, then a long journey north ending at the Pāsāṇaka Shrine near Rājagaha, where the Buddha is teaching
Speakers
Narrated throughout in the third person; direct speech from Bāvarī, a begging brahmin, a goddess, and finally the Buddha and the student Ajita
Form
Fifty-six narrative verses — the only sustained storytelling in the whole Sutta Nipāta, framing everything that follows in this chapter
Length
~11 minutes to read
Structure
Opens the Pārāyanavagga (“The Way to the Far Shore”), the fifth and final vagga of the Sutta Nipāta — one continuous work told across Snp 5.1–5.19
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a folk-tale narrative, easy to follow, though the riddle at its center (“heads and head-splitting”) is deliberately cryptic until answered

Why this discourse, the frame for everything that follows

Nothing else in the Sutta Nipāta reads quite like this. Where the earlier vaggas mostly gather independent verses and short dialogues, the Pārāyanavagga is a single continuous story stretched across nineteen discourses: an old brahmin named Bāvarī, too frail to travel, is humiliated by a curse he cannot verify, learns there is now a Buddha in the world, and sends sixteen of his most brilliant students north to find him — each carrying his own question. This opening discourse, the Vatthugāthā or “Introductory Verses,” tells that whole backstory in full: Bāvarī’s curse, his rescue by a goddess, the sixteen students named one by one, their long march across the map of ancient India, and their arrival at the Buddha’s feet. Every discourse from here through Snp 5.9 (and on to 5.17) is one of those sixteen students, in turn, putting his question to the Buddha — so this page is essential reading even for someone who only wants to read a single student’s exchange later in the chapter.

Reading guide

The discourse in one sentence

An aged brahmin teacher named Bāvarī, cursed by a beggar he could not pay, is consoled by a goddess who tells him of the newly awakened Buddha; overjoyed, he sends sixteen of his finest brahmin students on a long northward journey to question the Buddha directly, and this discourse follows them all the way to their arrival — setting up every exchange that follows in the Pārāyanavagga.

A curse over five hundred coins

Bāvarī is introduced as a brahmin expert in the sacred hymns who has settled by the Godāvarī River, living simply and teaching a large following. After completing a great sacrifice, he is approached by a second brahmin — footsore, thirsty, “with grotty teeth and dusty head” — who asks him for five hundred coins. Bāvarī, having already given away everything he had to give, says he cannot. The visitor’s response is a real curse, delivered after performing a ritual: if Bāvarī does not pay, “on the seventh day, let your head explode in seven!” It is a small, almost petty humiliation — a debt dispute, not a grand spiritual crisis — and that is part of the discourse’s craft: it takes Bāvarī’s very ordinary distress completely seriously, tracking his loss of appetite, his emaciation, his inability to find any peace of mind.

A goddess, and the reveal of “heads and head-splitting”

A local goddess, moved by Bāvarī’s distress, tells him the truth: the beggar “understands nothing about the head” and only wanted his money. But when Bāvarī asks her, then, what does she know about “heads and head-splitting,” she admits she doesn’t know either — only the Victors have that vision. She tells him a Buddha has newly arisen in the world, a Sakyan from Kapilavatthu, “gone beyond all things,” and that near Sāvatthī he can be found. The riddle of “heads and head-splitting” is left deliberately unresolved here — it becomes the test question Bāvarī sends with his students, and its answer only arrives near the very end of this discourse.

Sixteen students, sent to verify a stranger’s claim

Too old to travel the distance himself, Bāvarī addresses his assembled students directly and tells them to go find this Buddha. Anticipating that they will need proof he really is fully awakened, Bāvarī recites the traditional thirty-two marks of a great man, and gives them a specific test: ask him, silently, in the mind alone, about Bāvarī’s own birth, clan, bodily marks, and teaching — and about heads and head-splitting. If he is truly a Buddha of unhindered vision, he will answer the unspoken questions aloud, unprompted. The discourse then names all sixteen students in full: Ajita, Tissametteyya, Puṇṇaka, Mettagū, Dhotaka, Upasīva, Nanda, Hemaka, Todeyya, Kappa, Jatukaṇṇī, Bhadrāvudha, Udaya, Posāla, Mogharāja, and Piṅgiya — the very roster whose individual questions, in this same order, structure the rest of the Pārāyanavagga.

A journey across the map of ancient India

The students’ route is given almost like an itinerary: from Muḷaka through Māhissatī, Ujjenī, Gonaddha, Vedisa, Vanasa, Kosambī, Sāketa, Sāvatthī itself, Setavyā, Kapilavatthu, Kusinārā, Pāvā, Bhoga City, Vesālī, and the Magadhan capital, finally reaching the Pāsāṇaka Shrine. Scholars have long used this passage as evidence for a real trade route of the period, running roughly along the line later scholars call the “southern road,” connecting the Deccan to the Buddha’s own heartland in the middle Ganges valley. The discourse marks their eagerness with three similes at once — thirsty for water, a merchant for profit, someone heat-struck for shade — before they climb the mountain to the shrine.

The test, answered before it is asked

Ajita is the first to see the Buddha, and the description leans on familiar comparisons — the sun, the full moon — before he silently poses, in his mind alone, the question about Bāvarī’s birth, clan, marks, and teaching. The Buddha answers aloud, in full and correctly, without having been asked out loud at all, astonishing the assembled crowd, who wonder whether a god must be involved. Only then does the deeper riddle get raised on Bāvarī’s behalf: what are “heads and head-splitting”? The Buddha’s answer reframes the whole image away from anything literal: “know ignorance as the head, and knowledge as the head-splitter” — when joined with faith, mindfulness, immersion, enthusiasm, and energy. The curse that opened the discourse turns out to have been, in a sense, exactly right: ignorance really is a kind of head waiting to be split open, just not by any ritual a beggar could perform.

Permission granted, and the frame set in motion

The lead student prostrates himself in gratitude, and the Buddha grants the whole group “the opportunity to clear up all doubt” — permission to ask whatever they came to ask. The discourse ends there, naming Ajita as the one who puts “the first question right there,” which is exactly where Snp 5.2 begins. Everything from this point through Snp 5.9, and on through the rest of the Pārāyanavagga, is that permission being exercised, one student and one question at a time.

Key terms

Vatthugāthā — “introductory verses” or “verses of the story,” the traditional name for this discourse’s narrative frame, distinguishing it from the question-and-answer verses (pucchā) that follow in Snp 5.2 onward.
Bāvarī — the elderly brahmin teacher whose curse, rescue, and dispatching of his sixteen students structures the entire Pārāyanavagga, though he himself never appears again in person after this discourse.
“heads and head-splitting” — Bāvarī’s test riddle, answered only at this discourse’s close: ignorance is the head, and knowledge — joined with faith, mindfulness, immersion, enthusiasm, and energy — is what splits it.
the thirty-two marks — the traditional bodily marks of a “great man,” recited by Bāvarī as proof his students should look for, said to indicate one of two possible destinies: a world-ruling monarch, or a fully awakened Buddha.
the sixteen students — Ajita, Tissametteyya, Puṇṇaka, Mettagū, Dhotaka, Upasīva, Nanda, Hemaka, Todeyya, Kappa, Jatukaṇṇī, Bhadrāvudha, Udaya, Posāla, Mogharāja, and Piṅgiya, named here in the exact order their individual questions appear across the rest of the Pārāyanavagga.

The text

The full narrative prologue, given as flowing prose paragraphs rather than line-by-line verse, since it is telling a continuous story; each paragraph is marked with the traditional verse numbers it covers (976–1031). Reproduced in full. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

§976–979From the fair city of the Kosalans to the southern region came a brahmin expert in hymns, aspiring to nothingness. He settled in a region under the shared dominion of Assaka and Muḷaka, where he lived on the bank of the Godāvarī River, getting by on gleanings and fruit. He was supported by a prosperous village nearby. With the revenue earned from there he performed a great sacrifice. When he had completed the great sacrifice, he returned to his hermitage once more. Upon his return, another brahmin arrived.

§980–985Foot-sore and thirsty, with grotty teeth and dusty head, he approached the other and asked for five hundred coins. When Bāvari saw him, he invited him to sit down, asked of his happiness and well-being, and said the following. “Whatever I had available to give, I have already distributed. Believe me, brahmin, I don’t have five hundred coins.” “If, good fellow, you do not give me what I ask, then on the seventh day, let your head explode in seven!” After performing a ritual, that charlatan uttered his dreadful curse. When he heard these words, Bāvari became distressed. Not eating, he grew emaciated, stricken by the dart of sorrow. And in such a state of mind, he could not enjoy absorption.

§986–996Seeing him anxious and distraught, a goddess wishing to help, approached Bāvari and said the following. “That charlatan understands nothing about the head, he only wants money. When it comes to heads or head-splitting, he has no knowledge at all.” “Then you, Madam, must know—please answer my question. Let me hear what you say about heads and head-splitting.” “I too do not know that, I have no knowledge in that matter. When it comes to heads or head-splitting, it is the Victors who have vision.” “So who then, in all this vast territory, does know about heads and head-splitting? Please tell me, goddess.” “From the city of Kapilavatthu the World Leader has gone forth. He is a scion of King Okkāka, a Sakyan, and a beacon. For he, brahmin, is the Awakened One! He has gone beyond all things; he has attained to all knowledge and power; his eye sees clearly in all things, he has attained the end of all deeds; he is freed with the ending of attachments. That Buddha, the Blessed One in the world, the Clear-eyed One, teaches Dhamma. Go to him and ask—he will answer you.” When he heard the word “Buddha”, Bāvari was elated. His sorrow faded, and he was filled to brimming with joy. Uplifted, elated, and inspired, Bāvari questioned that goddess: “But in what village or town, or in what land is the protector of the world, where we may go and pay respects to the Awakened One, best of men?” “Near Sāvatthī, home of the Kosalans, is the Victor abounding in wisdom, vast in intelligence. That Sakyan is indefatigable, free of defilements, that captain of men understands head-splitting.”

§997–1005Therefore he addressed his acolytes, brahmins who had mastered the hymns: “Come, students, I shall speak. Listen to what I say. Today has arisen in the world one whose appearance in the world is hard to find again—he is renowned as the Awakened One. Quickly go to Sāvatthī and see the best of men.” “Brahmin, how then are we to know the Buddha when we see him? We don’t know, please tell us, so we can recognize him.” “The marks of a great man have been handed down in our hymns. Thirty-two have been described, complete and in order. One upon whose body is found these marks of a great man has two possible destinies, there is no third. If he stays at home, having conquered this land without rod or sword, he shall govern by principle. But if he goes forth from the lay life to homelessness, he becomes an Awakened One, a perfected one, with veil cast off, supreme. Ask him about my birth, clan, and marks, my hymns and acolytes; and further, about heads and head-splitting—but do so only in your mind! If he is the Buddha of unhindered vision, he will answer with his voice the questions in your mind.”

§1006–1009Sixteen brahmin acolytes heard what Bāvari said: Ajita, Tissametteyya, Puṇṇaka and Mettagū, Dhotaka and Upasīva, Nanda and then Hemaka, both Todeyya and Kappa, and Jatukaṇṇī the astute, Bhadrāvudha and Udaya, and the brahmin Posāla, Mogharāja the intelligent, and Piṅgiya the great seer. Each of them had their own following, they were renowned the whole world over. Those attentive ones, meditators who love absorption, were redolent with the potential of their past deeds.

§1010–1014Having bowed to Bāvari, and circled him to his right, they set out for the north, with their dreadlocks and hides. First to Patiṭṭhāna of Muḷaka, then on to the citadel of Māhissatī; to Ujjenī and Gonaddhā, and Vedisa, and Vanasa. Then to Kosambī and Sāketa, and the capital city of Sāvatthī; on they went to Setavyā and Kapilavatthu, and the homestead at Kusinārā. To Pāvā they went, and Bhoga City, and on to Vesālī and the Magadhan city. Finally they reached the Pāsāṇaka Shrine, fair and delightful. Like a thirsty person to cool water, like a merchant to great profit, like a heat-struck person to shade, they quickly climbed the mountain.

§1015–1024At that time the Buddha at the fore of the mendicant Saṅgha, was teaching the mendicants the Dhamma, like a lion roaring in the jungle. Ajita saw the Buddha, like the sun shining with a hundred rays, like the moon on the fifteenth day when it has come into its fullness. Then he saw his body, complete in all features. Thrilled, he stood to one side and asked this question in his mind. “Speak about the brahmin’s birth; of his clan; and his own marks; what hymns is he proficient in; and how many he teaches.” “His age is a hundred and twenty. By clan he is a Bāvari. There are three marks on his body. He is a master of the three Vedas, the teachings on the marks, the testaments, the vocabularies, and the rituals. He teaches recitation to five hundred, and has reached proficiency in his own teaching.” “O supreme man, cutter of craving, please reveal in detail Bāvari’s marks—let us doubt no longer!” “He can cover his face with his tongue; there is a tuft of hair between his eyebrows; his private parts are sheathed in a foreskin: know them as this, student.” Hearing the answers without having heard any questions, all the people, inspired, with cupped palms, wondered: “Who is it that asked a question with their mind? Was it a god or divinity? Or Indra, Sujā’s husband? To whom does the Buddha reply?”

§1025–1031“Bāvari asks about heads and head-splitting. May the Buddha please answer, and so, O seer, dispel our doubt.” “Know ignorance as the head, and knowledge as the head-splitter, when joined with faith, mindfulness, and immersion, and enthusiasm and energy.” At that, the student, full of inspiration, arranged his antelope-skin cloak over one shoulder, and fell with his head to the Buddha’s feet. “Good fellow, the brahmin Bāvari together with his acolytes, elated and happy, bows to your feet, O Clear-eyed One!” “May the brahmin Bāvari be happy, together with his acolytes. And may you, too, be happy! May you live long, student. To Bāvari and you all I grant the opportunity to clear up all doubt. Please ask whatever you want.” Granted the opportunity by the Buddha, they sat down with cupped palms. Ajita asked the Realized One the first question right there. The introductory verses are finished.

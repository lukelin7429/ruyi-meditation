Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 3.12

Contemplating Pairs

Dvayatānupassanāsutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother in the Eastern Monastery, on the sabbath — the full moon of the fifteenth day
Speakers
The Buddha, addressing the entire Saṅgha of mendicants seated in silence around him
Form
A long, systematic teaching — sixteen different ways of “contemplating a pair,” each stated first in prose as a question-and-answer, then restated in verse
Length
~18 minutes to read
Scale
One of the longest suttas in the entire Sutta Nipāta, and the discourse that closes the Mahāvagga
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — the structure is highly repetitive, but the content tracks most of the links of dependent origination in close detail

Why this discourse

This sutta closes the Mahāvagga with its most demanding and most systematic teaching — sixteen separate ways of “contemplating a pair,” each one approaching the single underlying reality of suffering and its ending from a different angle: attachment, ignorance, choices, consciousness, contact, feeling, craving, grasping, and on through territory that closely tracks the twelve links of dependent origination already covered on this site in SN 12.1. What makes the sutta remarkable isn’t just its length — among the longest in the whole collection — but its ending: while the Buddha is still speaking, sixty mendicants in the audience are freed from all defilements on the spot, a result the sutta itself simply states and moves past, without any fanfare at all.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Suffering and its cessation can be truly understood as a single “pair” approached from at least sixteen different angles — through attachment, ignorance, choices, consciousness, contact, feeling, craving, grasping, activity, sustenance, perturbation, dependence, form, and two closing contemplations on how the world’s ordinary sense of “true” and “happy” is inverted by right understanding — and contemplating any one of these pairs rightly leads to full awakening or, at minimum, non-return.

A silent assembly, and a question about pairs

The setting is unusually formal: the sabbath, the full moon, the entire Saṃgha seated in the open in total silence. The Buddha opens not with a story but with a hypothetical: if someone asked you why it’s worth listening to teachings that are “noble, emancipating, and lead to awakening,” what would you say? His own answer is the sutta’s organizing principle: “only so as to truly know the pairs of teachings.” The first pair given is the most basic possible statement of the four noble truths compressed into two: “this is suffering, this is the origin of suffering” as one contemplation, “this is the cessation of suffering, this is the practice that leads to cessation” as the second. Meditating rightly on any pair, the Buddha states, and this refrain repeats after all sixteen contemplations, “they can expect one of two results: enlightenment in this very life, or if there’s residue left behind, non-return.”

Working through the chain of conditions

What follows tracks strikingly close to the twelve links of dependent origination familiar from SN 12.1, already on this site, though presented here as a series of independent “pairs” rather than one continuous chain: suffering originates from attachment (upadhi); from ignorance (avijjā); from choices (saṃkhārā); from consciousness (viññāṇa); from contact (phassa); from feeling (vedanā); from craving (taṇhā); from grasping (upādāna); from instigating activity; and from sustenance. Each pair follows the same pattern precisely: a prose statement (“all the suffering that originates is caused by X” paired with “when X has faded away… there is no origination of suffering”), then the Buddha restating the same point in verse.

Beyond the causal chain

The final several pairs move past the strict causal sequence into broader territory. Perturbation (iñjita) — a kind of restless agitation — gets its own pair, followed by a contemplation of dependence itself: “for the dependent there is agitation… for the independent there’s no agitation.” Then comes a contemplation contrasting the formless states with states of form, and cessation with both: “formless states are more peaceful than states of form… cessation is more peaceful than formless states” — an explicit ranking of increasingly refined levels of meditative attainment against final release.

What the world calls true is false, what it calls happiness is suffering

The sutta’s final two contemplations are its most philosophically startling. The first: “what this world… focuses on as true, the noble ones have clearly seen with right wisdom to be actually false” — and the reverse holds too. The verses explain the mechanism: the world, “habituated to name and form,” mistakes what is not-self for self, and mistakes the changeable for the fixed — “for whatever you imagine it is, it turns out to be something else,” a line that echoes Snp 3.8’s teaching on loss almost verbatim. The second closing contemplation goes further still: what the world calls happiness — the pleasure of sights, sounds, tastes, smells, touches, and ideas — the noble ones call suffering, precisely because it depends on conditions that must eventually cease; and what the world calls suffering — the cessation of that very stimulation — the noble ones call happiness. The text is unusually candid about how counterintuitive this is: “this teaching is hard to understand, it confuses the ignorant… this insight by those who see contradicts the whole world.”

Sixty mendicants freed

The sutta ends almost abruptly, without ceremony: “while this discourse was being spoken, the minds of sixty mendicants were freed from defilements by not grasping.” No names are given, no further reaction is recorded. After sixteen sustained contemplations and dozens of verses, the sutta simply reports the result and stops — letting the scale of the outcome, sixty people reaching the end of the path during a single sitting, speak for itself.

Key terms

dvayatānupassanā — “contemplation of a pair” or duality — the sutta’s title and its organizing method, approaching suffering and its cessation from sixteen different angles.
upadhi — attachment or substrate of rebirth — the first of the causal factors examined after the opening pair on suffering itself.
iñjita — perturbation or agitation — a subtler condition examined among the sutta’s later contemplations, closely tied to dependence.
the refrain — “they can expect one of two results: enlightenment in this very life, or if there’s residue left behind, non-return” — repeated after every one of the sixteen contemplations.
SN 12.1 — the Saṃyutta Nikāya’s foundational statement of dependent origination, already on this site, tracking closely with much of this sutta’s causal sequence.

The text

The discourse in full: all sixteen contemplations, each given in both its prose statement and its verse restatement, plus the closing frame and the report of sixty mendicants’ liberation. This is one of the longest suttas in the collection, reproduced complete without abridgment. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The first pair: suffering and its cause

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother in the Eastern Monastery. Now, at that time it was the sabbath—the full moon on the fifteenth day—and the Buddha was sitting in the open surrounded by the Saṅgha of monks. Then the Buddha looked around the Saṅgha of mendicants, who were so very silent. He addressed them:

§2–4“Suppose, mendicants, they questioned you thus: ‘There are skillful teachings that are noble, emancipating, and lead to awakening. What is the real reason for listening to such teachings?’ You should answer: ‘Only so as to truly know the pairs of teachings.’ And what pairs do they speak of? ‘This is suffering; this is the origin of suffering’: this is the first contemplation. ‘This is the cessation of suffering; this is the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates rightly contemplating a pair of teachings in this way—diligent, keen, and resolute—they can expect one of two results: enlightenment in this very life, or if there’s residue left behind, non-return.” That is what the Buddha said. Then the Holy One, the Teacher, went on to say:

§5–6“There are those who don’t understand suffering
and suffering’s cause,
and where all suffering
ceases no residue left behind.
And they do not know the path
that leads to the stilling of suffering.
They lack the heart’s release,
as well as the release by wisdom.
Unable to make an end,
they continue to be reborn and grow old.

§7–8But there are those who understand suffering
and suffering’s cause,
and where all suffering
ceases with no residue left behind.
And they understand the path
that leads to the stilling of suffering.
They’re endowed with the heart’s release,
as well as the release by wisdom.
Able to make an end,
they don’t continue to be reborn and grow old.”

Attachment, ignorance, and choices

§9“Suppose, mendicants, they questioned you thus: ‘Could there be another way to contemplate the pairs?’ You should say, ‘There could.’ And how could there be? ‘All the suffering that originates is caused by attachment’: this is one contemplation. ‘When attachment has faded away and ceased with no residue left behind, there is no origination of suffering’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates in this way they can expect enlightenment or non-return.” Then the Teacher went on to say:

§10“Attachment is the source of suffering
in all its countless forms in the world.
When an ignorant person builds up attachments,
that dullard returns to suffering again and again.
So let one who understands not build up attachments,
contemplating the inception of suffering and rebirth.”

§11“Suppose, mendicants, they questioned you thus: ‘Could there be another way to contemplate the pairs?’ You should say, ‘There could.’ And how could there be? ‘All the suffering that originates is caused by ignorance’: this is one contemplation. ‘When ignorance has faded away and ceased with no residue left behind, there is no origination of suffering’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates in this way they can expect enlightenment or non-return.” Then the Teacher went on to say:

§12–13“Those who journey again and again,
transmigrating through birth and death;
they go from this state to another,
that destination due only to ignorance.
For ignorance is the great delusion
because of which we have long transmigrated.
Those beings who have knowledge
do not proceed to a future life.”

§14“‘Could there be another way?’ … And how could there be? ‘All the suffering that originates is caused by choices’: this is one contemplation. ‘When choices has faded away and ceased with no residue left behind, there is no origination of suffering’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates in this way they can expect enlightenment or non-return.” Then the Teacher went on to say:

§15–17“All the suffering that originates
is caused by choices.
With the cessation of choices,
there is no origination of suffering.
Knowing this danger,
that suffering is caused by choices;
through the stilling of all choices,
and the stopping of perceptions,
this is the way suffering ends
when one has known this as it is.
Rightly seeing, knowledge masters,
the astute, understanding rightly,
having thrown off Māra’s yoke,
do not proceed to a future life.”

Consciousness, contact, and feeling

§18“‘Could there be another way?’ … And how could there be? ‘All the suffering that originates is caused by consciousness’: this is one contemplation. ‘When consciousness has faded away and ceased with no residue left behind, there is no origination of suffering’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates in this way they can expect enlightenment or non-return.” Then the Teacher went on to say:

§19–20“All the suffering that originates
is caused by consciousness.
With the cessation of consciousness,
there is no origination of suffering.
Knowing this danger,
that suffering is caused by consciousness,
with the stilling of consciousness a mendicant
is hungerless, quenched.”

§21“‘Could there be another way?’ … And how could there be? ‘All the suffering that originates is caused by contact’: this is one contemplation. ‘When contact has faded away and ceased with no residue left behind, there is no origination of suffering’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates in this way they can expect enlightenment or non-return.” Then the Teacher went on to say:

§22–23“Those mired in contact,
swept down the stream of lives,
practicing the wrong way,
are far from the ending of fetters.
But those who completely understand contact,
who, understanding, delight in peace;
by comprehending contact
they are hungerless, quenched.”

§24“‘Could there be another way?’ … And how could there be? ‘All the suffering that originates is caused by feeling’: this is one contemplation. ‘When feeling has faded away and ceased with no residue left behind, there is no origination of suffering’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates in this way they can expect enlightenment or non-return.” Then the Teacher went on to say:

§25–26“Whether it’s pleasant or painful,
as well as what’s neutral,
whatever there is that is felt
internally and externally:
having known, ‘this is suffering,
deceptive, breaking apart’,
seeing feelings vanish with every contact,
that’s how they understand them.
With the ending of feelings, a mendicant
is hungerless, quenched.”

Craving, grasping, and instigating activity

§27“‘Could there be another way?’ … And how could there be? ‘All the suffering that originates is caused by craving’: this is one contemplation. ‘When craving has faded away and ceased with no residue left behind, there is no origination of suffering’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates in this way they can expect enlightenment or non-return.” Then the Teacher went on to say:

§28–29“Craving is a person’s partner
as they transmigrate on this long journey.
They go from this state to another,
but don’t escape transmigration.
Knowing this danger,
that craving is the cause of suffering—
rid of craving, free of grasping,
a mendicant would wander mindful.”

§30“‘Could there be another way?’ … And how could there be? ‘All the suffering that originates is caused by grasping’: this is one contemplation. ‘When grasping has faded away and ceased with no residue left behind, there is no origination of suffering’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates in this way they can expect enlightenment or non-return.” Then the Teacher went on to say:

§31–32“Grasping is the cause of continued existence;
one who exists falls into suffering.
Death comes to those who are born—
this is the origination of suffering.
That’s why with the end of grasping,
the astute, understanding rightly,
having directly known the end of rebirth,
do not proceed to a future life.”

§33“‘Could there be another way?’ … And how could there be? ‘All the suffering that originates is caused by instigating activity’: this is one contemplation. ‘When instigation has faded away and ceased with no residue left behind, there is no origination of suffering’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates in this way they can expect enlightenment or non-return.” Then the Teacher went on to say:

§34–36“All the suffering that originates
is caused by instigating activity.
With the cessation of instigation,
there is no origination of suffering.
Knowing this danger,
that suffering is caused by instigating activity,
having given up all instigation,
one is freed with respects to instigation.
For the mendicant with peaceful mind,
who has cut off craving for continued existence,
transmigration through births is finished;
there’ll be no more future lives for them.”

Sustenance and perturbation

§37“‘Could there be another way?’ … And how could there be? ‘All the suffering that originates is caused by sustenance’: this is one contemplation. ‘When sustenance has faded away and ceased with no residue left behind, there is no origination of suffering’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates in this way they can expect enlightenment or non-return.” Then the Teacher went on to say:

§38–40“All the suffering that originates
is caused by sustenance.
With the cessation of sustenance,
there is no origination of suffering.
Knowing this danger,
that suffering is caused by sustenance,
completely understanding all sustenance,
one is independent of all sustenance.
Having rightly understood the state of health,
through the ending of defilements,
using after reflection, firm in principle,
a knowledge master cannot be reckoned.”

§41“‘Could there be another way?’ … And how could there be? ‘All the suffering that originates is caused by perturbation’: this is one contemplation. ‘When perturbation has faded away and ceased with no residue left behind, there is no origination of suffering’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates in this way they can expect enlightenment or non-return.” Then the Teacher went on to say:

§42–43“All the suffering that originates
is caused by perturbation.
With the cessation of perturbation,
there is no origination of suffering.
Knowing this danger,
that suffering is caused by perturbation,
that’s why, having relinquished perturbation,
and stopped making karmic choices,
imperturbable, free of grasping,
a mendicant would wander mindful.”

Dependence, form, and cessation

§44“‘Could there be another way?’ … And how could there be? ‘For the dependent there is agitation’: this is the first contemplation. ‘For the independent there’s no agitation’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates in this way they can expect enlightenment or non-return.” Then the Teacher went on to say:

§45–46“For the independent there’s no agitation.
The dependent, grasping,
goes from this state to another,
without escaping transmigration.
Knowing this danger,
the great fear in dependencies,
independent, free of grasping,
a mendicant would wander mindful.”

§47“‘Could there be another way?’ … And how could there be? ‘Formless states are more peaceful than states of form’: this is the first contemplation. ‘Cessation is more peaceful than formless states’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates in this way they can expect enlightenment or non-return.” Then the Teacher went on to say:

§48–49“There are beings in the realm of luminous form,
and others stuck in the formless.
Not understanding cessation,
they return in future lives.
But the people who completely understand form,
not stuck in the formless,
released in cessation—
they are conquerors of death.”

What the world calls true is false

§50“‘Could there be another way?’ … And how could there be? ‘What this world—with its gods, Māras, and Divinities, this population with its ascetics and brahmins, its gods and humans—focuses on as true, the noble ones have clearly seen with right wisdom to be actually false’: this is the first contemplation. ‘What this world focuses on as false, the noble ones have clearly seen with right wisdom to be actually true’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates in this way they can expect enlightenment or non-return.” Then the Teacher went on to say:

§51–52“See how the world with its gods
imagines not-self to be self;
habituated to name and form,
imagining this is truth.
For whatever you imagine it is,
it turns out to be something else.
And that is what is false in it,
for the ephemeral is deceptive by nature.

§53Extinguishment has an undeceptive nature,
the noble ones know it as truth.
Having comprehended the truth,
they are hungerless, quenched.”

What the world calls happiness is suffering

§54“Suppose, mendicants, they questioned you thus: ‘Could there be another way to contemplate the pairs?’ You should say, ‘There could.’ And how could there be? ‘What this world—with its gods, Māras, and Divinities, this population with its ascetics and brahmins, its gods and humans—focuses on as happiness, the noble ones have clearly seen with right wisdom to be actually suffering’: this is the first contemplation. ‘What this world focuses on as suffering, the noble ones have clearly seen with right wisdom to be actually happiness’: this is the second contemplation. When a mendicant meditates rightly contemplating a pair of teachings in this way—diligent, keen, and resolute—they can expect one of two results: enlightenment in this very life, or if there’s residue left behind, non-return.” That is what the Buddha said. Then the Holy One, the Teacher, went on to say:

§55–56“Sights, sounds, tastes, smells,
touches, and ideas, the lot of them—
they’re likable, desirable, and pleasurable
as long as you can say that they exist.
For all the world with its gods,
this is what they deem happiness.
And where they cease
is deemed as suffering for them.

§57–58The noble ones have seen as happiness
the ceasing of substantial reality.
This insight by those who see
contradicts the whole world.
What others say is happiness
the noble ones say is suffering.
What others say is suffering
the noble ones know as happiness.

§59–60See, this teaching is hard to understand,
it confuses the ignorant.
There is darkness for the shrouded;
blackness for those who don’t see.
But the good are open;
like light for those who see.
Though close, they do not understand,
those fools inexpert in the teaching.

§61–62They’re mired in desire for continued existence,
flowing along the stream of lives,
mired in Māra’s dominion:
this teaching isn’t easy for them to understand.
Who, apart from the noble ones,
is qualified to understand this state?
Having rightly understood this state,
the undefiled are fully extinguished.”

Sixty mendicants freed

§63That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said. And while this discourse was being spoken, the minds of sixty mendicants were freed from defilements by not grasping.

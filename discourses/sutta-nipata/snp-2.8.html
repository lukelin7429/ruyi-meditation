Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 2.8

The Boat

Nāvāsutta

Setting
No narrative frame is given — a freestanding set of verses on choosing and honoring a teacher, without a “So I have heard” opening
Speaker
Not identified within the text itself; traditionally reckoned among the words of the Buddha
Form
Eight verses built around a single sustained simile of crossing a river by boat
Length
~2 minutes to read
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — short, direct, and built on one memorable image

Why this discourse, the eighth

The Boat opens Vagga 2’s closing run of teachings with a single, exacting image: a teacher deserves honor the way a skilled ferryman deserves it — not for standing on the bank pointing, but for actually being equipped to make the crossing and bring others across with him. Honor paid to someone who cannot make that crossing themselves, the poem warns, is honor wasted on a person already being swept downstream.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Honor a teacher the way you would honor a skilled ferryman who gets you safely across a raging river — because a “teacher” who cannot make that crossing themselves, however much honor is paid them, cannot bring you across either.

Honor owed to a genuine guide

The poem opens with a direct instruction: “If you would learn the teaching from someone, honor them as the gods honor Indra” — that is, as the chief of the gods, the highest standard of reverence the tradition has available. The logic given is practical, not merely pious: a teacher who is honored in this way “will have confidence in you, and being learned, they reveal the teaching.” Honor is not decoration here; it is what opens a teacher up to actually teach. The student who then heeds well, stays attentive, and practices in line with what is taught “grows intelligent, discerning, and subtle” simply “through diligently sticking close to such a person.”

The petty fool who cannot help

The poem then turns to the failure case. Associating instead with “a petty fool who falls short of the goal, jealous,” leaves a person “unable to discern the teaching in this life,” so that they “proceed to death still plagued by doubts.” The image that follows is the poem’s central device: such a person is “like a man who has plunged into a river, a rushing torrent in spate” — swept away downstream themselves, with no capacity whatsoever to help anyone else across. “Just so,” the verse continues, one who cannot discern the teaching, has never studied its meaning under someone learned, and remains plagued by doubt, cannot possibly help others to contemplate it either. The honor owed to a real teacher, in other words, is wasted — even dangerous — when paid to someone who is drowning just as badly as anyone else.

The skilled ferryman

Against the drowning fool, the poem sets its answering image: “one who has embarked on a strong boat, equipped with rudder and oar, would bring many others across there with skill, care, and intelligence.” This is the discourse’s title image, and its point is exact — a real teacher is not simply someone standing on the far bank shouting encouragement, but someone equipped, practiced, and already capable of making the crossing themselves. “So too one who understands — a knowledge master, evolved, learned, and unflappable — can help others to contemplate, so long as they are prepared to listen carefully.” That final clause matters: even a genuinely capable guide cannot ferry a student who will not get in the boat.

Spend time with a true person

The poem closes on its practical conclusion: “That’s why you should spend time with a true person, intelligent and learned. Having understood the meaning, putting it into practice, one who understands the teaching would find happiness.” Understanding by itself is not the finish line — it is understanding put into practice that the poem names as the actual source of happiness, closing a discourse that began with a question of who deserves honor by insisting that honor, understanding, and practice all have to travel together.

Key terms

Indra (Sakka) — the king of the gods, invoked in the opening verse as the standard of honor owed to a teacher who has genuinely helped a student understand the teaching.
the river-crossing simile — the poem’s governing image: an unqualified teacher is like someone being swept away in a river torrent, unable to help anyone else across; a genuine teacher is like a skilled boatman with a strong boat, rudder, and oar.
“a true person” — the poem’s closing term (elsewhere transliterated sappurisa) for the intelligent, learned figure worth spending time with, distinguished throughout from the “petty fool” who falls short of the goal.
“unflappable” — one of the qualities of the “knowledge master” in verse 7, describing the settled steadiness that lets a genuine teacher actually complete the crossing rather than merely point toward it.

The text

The discourse in full — eight verses with no narrative frame, built around a single sustained image of crossing a river by boat. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Honor owed to a genuine guide

Verse 1If you would learn the teaching from someone,
honor them as the gods honor Indra.
Then they will have confidence in you,
and being learned, they reveal the teaching.

Verse 2Heeding well, an attentive one,
practicing in line with that teaching
grows intelligent, discerning, and subtle
through diligently sticking close to such a person.

The petty fool who cannot help

Verse 3But associating with a petty fool
who falls short of the goal, jealous,
then unable to discern the teaching in this life,
one proceeds to death still plagued by doubts.

Verse 4It’s like a man who has plunged into a river,
a rushing torrent in spate.
As they are swept away downstream,
how could they help others across?

Verse 5Just so, one unable to discern the teaching,
who hasn’t studied the meaning under the learned,
not knowing it oneself, still plagued by doubts,
how could they help others to contemplate?

The skilled ferryman

Verse 6But one who has embarked on a strong boat
equipped with rudder and oar,
would bring many others across there
with skill, care, and intelligence.

Verse 7So too one who understands—a knowledge master,
evolved, learned, and unflappable—
can help others to contemplate,
so long as they are prepared to listen carefully.

Spend time with a true person

Verse 8That’s why you should spend time with a true person,
intelligent and learned.
Having understood the meaning, putting it into practice,
one who understands the teaching would find happiness.

What image does the poem use for someone unfit to be relied on as a teacher?

Question 2 of 10
How does the opening verse say one should honor a teacher who can genuinely reveal the teaching?
Correct: B. This honor is described as practical, not merely pious: it is what gives the teacher confidence to actually teach.
Question 3 of 10
What two qualities does the poem attribute to the "petty fool" one should not rely on?
Correct: C. Verse 3 names both qualities directly.
Question 4 of 10
What happens to someone who relies on such a person rather than a genuinely learned teacher, according to verse 3?
Correct: D. This grim outcome sets up the river-crossing simile that follows in verses 4–5.
Question 5 of 10
What equipment does the "strong boat" have in the poem's answering image (verse 6)?
Correct: A. The equipped boat stands for a teacher who is actually capable of making the crossing, not merely encouraging from the bank.
Question 6 of 10
According to verse 7, what condition is placed even on a genuinely capable teacher's ability to help?
Correct: D. Even a real ferryman cannot carry a student who will not get in the boat.
Question 7 of 10
What final outcome does verse 8 promise to "one who understands the teaching"?
Correct: C. The poem insists understanding alone is not the finish line; practice is what completes it.
Question 8 of 10
Does this discourse open with a narrative frame like "So I have heard, at one time the Buddha was staying..."?
Correct: B. Unlike several of the discourses around it in this chapter, The Boat has no prose frame at all.
Question 9 of 10
What does verse 2 say happens to an attentive student who practices in line with a genuine teacher's guidance?

Who is invoked in verse 1 as the standard for how a helpful teacher should be honored?

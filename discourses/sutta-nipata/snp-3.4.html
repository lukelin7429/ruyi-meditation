Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 3.4

With Bhāradvāja of Sundarikā on the Sacrificial Cake

Sundarikabhāradvājasutta

Setting
The Kosalan countryside, on the bank of the Sundarikā river, at the site of a brahmin's fire sacrifice
Speakers
The Buddha and the brahmin Bhāradvāja of Sundarikā, a fire-worshipper searching for someone worthy to receive his oblation's leftovers
Form
A narrative frame leading into an extended verse dialogue and, at its center, a long catalogue of the qualities that make one truly “worthy of the sacrificial cake”
Length
~10 minutes to read
Northern parallel
The Sutta Nipāta as a whole has no direct counterpart among the four Chinese Āgamas; its argument that worth is a matter of conduct rather than birth recurs across many parts of the wider canon
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — uses specific brahmanical vocabulary (the Sāvitrī verse, the sacred fire Jātaveda) that repays a little background

Why this discourse, fourth of the Great Chapter

A brahmin mid-sacrifice looks up, sees a shaven ascetic, and almost turns away in disgust before curiosity gets the better of him. What follows is one of the Sutta Nipāta's clearest statements of a principle that recurs throughout this collection: birth tells you nothing about worth. The Buddha refuses to be drawn into a contest of pedigree, answers a question about who deserves an offering with an extended, cumulative portrait of a genuinely worthy person, and then — in a small but pointed detail — declines to eat the very food he has just been offered, on principle.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A brahmin performing a fire sacrifice, looking for someone worthy of its leftovers, is told by the Buddha not to ask about birth but about conduct — and receives, in reply, an extended catalogue of the qualities that make a person genuinely worthy of an offering, culminating in an unusual refusal: the Buddha will not eat food given as payment for a teaching just spoken.

A brahmin looking for someone to feed

Bhāradvāja of Sundarikā is performing the fire sacrifice on the riverbank that gives him his name, and rises to survey the four directions, wondering who might eat the leftovers of the oblation. He sees the Buddha meditating at the root of a tree, robe pulled over his head, and approaches with the leftovers in one hand and a pitcher in the other.

“This man is shaven!”

When the Buddha, hearing footsteps, uncovers his head, Bhāradvāja's first reaction is recoil: “This man is shaven, he is shaven!” — and he wants to turn back. What stops him is a moment of self-correction: “Even some brahmins are shaven. Why don't I go to him and ask about his birth?” He approaches and puts the question directly: “Sir, what are you by birth?”

Not asking the right question

The Buddha's answer refuses the frame of the question entirely: “I am no brahmin, nor am I a ruler, nor peasant nor anything else” — declining all four traditional classes at once. “Fully understanding the clan of ordinary people, I wander in the world owning nothing, reflective.” He is unburdened, he says, “by the sons of Manu” — the mythic progenitor whose name anchors brahmanical claims about lineage — and so it is simply “inappropriate to ask me about clan.”

The Sāvitrī challenge

Bhāradvāja pushes back on etiquette grounds: brahmins customarily ask each other this question when they meet. The Buddha's reply turns the etiquette back on him: if he insists on being recognized as a brahmin by that standard, “I shall question you on that Sāvitrī Prayer, with its three lines and twenty-four syllables” — the central Vedic verse a genuine brahmin scholar would be expected to know cold. Rather than take up that test, Bhāradvāja pivots, impressed, to a different and more useful question: on what grounds do sacrifices actually profit the one who gives them?

Conduct, not birth

The Buddha's answer supplies the discourse's central line: “Don't ask about birth, ask about conduct; for the fire of Jātaveda is born from sticks” — sacred fire can be kindled from any wood at all, regardless of species, just as “a steadfast sage, even though from a low class family, is a thoroughbred checked by conscience.” The point is not that birth is irrelevant to everything; it is that birth is irrelevant to this specific question — who deserves an offering.

The catalogue of the worthy

What follows is one of the Sutta Nipāta's long cumulative portraits, verse after verse ending on the same refrain, “the Realized One is worthy of the sacrificial cake” (or, in the earlier verses, that a brahmin seeking merit “should bestow a timely oblation as sacrifice” on such a person). The qualities named build a composite picture rather than a checklist: freed of greed, senses stilled “like the moon released from the eclipse”; wandering the world unobstructed, calling nothing one's own; knowing the end of rebirth and death, “quenched and cool as a lake”; free of deceit and conceit, with anger cast aside; having “given up the mind's home” and possessing nothing at all; having crossed the flood and understood the teaching “with ultimate view”; free of hope, having “fully understood suffering with its field and ground”; and, in the discourse's final lines, one who “harbors no delusions within at all” and has “insight into all things,” bearing his “final body” and attained to “the supreme awakening.”

Food enchanted by a verse

Overjoyed, Bhāradvāja tries to offer the very sacrificial cake on the spot: “I see divinity in person! Accept my offering, Blessed One: please eat my sacrificial cake.” The refusal that follows is a small but pointed moment: “Food enchanted by a verse isn't fit for me to eat. That's not the principle of those who see, brahmin. The Buddhas cast aside things enchanted with verses.” The objection is specific — not to food itself, but to food offered as direct payment in exchange for a teaching just given, treated here as against the very principle a Buddha lives by. He tells Bhāradvāja instead to serve such food to “the consummate one, the great seer, with defilements ended and remorse stilled — for he is the field for the seeker of merit.”

Who then should eat it?

Asked directly who, then, should receive a religious donation, the Buddha gives a second, shorter portrait: one rid of aggression, unclouded mind, liberated from sensual pleasures, dullness cast aside, “expert in birth and death, a sage, blessed with sagacity.” When such a person comes to the sacrifice, Bhāradvāja is told plainly: “get rid of your scowl! Honor them with cupped palms, and venerate them with food and drink.”

Ordination on the spot

The discourse closes with the formula used throughout the canon for a listener whose confusion has genuinely lifted — “as if he were righting the overturned, or revealing the hidden…” — and Bhāradvāja goes for refuge and asks for ordination directly. He receives it, and, the text notes simply, “soon after, he became one of the perfected” — an arahant, the same swift trajectory from skeptical brahmin to awakened disciple that recurs at the close of Snp 3.6.

Key terms

Jātaveda — an epithet for the sacred fire (“knower of all born things”), invoked in the discourse's central image: fire kindled from any wood, worth arising from any birth.
the Sāvitrī Prayer — the central Vedic verse (also known as the gāyatrī), three lines and twenty-four syllables, which the Buddha offers to test Bhāradvāja on if birth-based etiquette is to be insisted upon.
“the sons of Manu” — the mythic progenitor figure associated with brahmanical lineage, invoked by the Buddha to explain why a question about his own clan is beside the point.
“food enchanted by a verse” — a donation offered as direct payment for a teaching just given; the Buddha refuses to eat it on principle, redirecting it to a genuinely worthy recipient instead.
dakkhiṇeyya — “worthy of offerings,” a field of merit; the discourse's central question, answered twice, in two overlapping but distinct catalogues of qualities.

The text

The discourse in full: the narrative frame given as paragraphs, the verse dialogue and the long catalogue of the worthy given with line breaks, verse by verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A brahmin looking for someone to feed

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the Kosalan lands on the bank of the Sundarikā river. Now at that time the brahmin Bhāradvāja of Sundarikā was serving the sacred flame and performing the fire sacrifice on the bank of the Sundarikā. Then he rose from his seat and surveyed the four quarters, wondering, “Now who might eat the leftovers of this oblation?” He saw the Buddha meditating at the root of a certain tree with his robe pulled over his head. Taking the leftovers of the oblation in his left hand and a pitcher in the right he approached the Buddha.

“This man is shaven!”

§2When he heard Bhāradvāja of Sundarikā’s footsteps the Buddha uncovered his head. Bhāradvāja of Sundarikā thought, “This man is shaven, he is shaven!” And he wanted to turn back. But he thought, “Even some brahmins are shaven. Why don’t I go to him and ask about his birth?” Then the brahmin Bhāradvāja of Sundarikā went up to the Buddha, and said to him, “Sir, what are you by birth?”

§3Then the Buddha addressed Bhāradvāja of Sundarikā in verse:

Not asking the right question

§4“I am no brahmin, nor am I a ruler,
nor peasant nor anything else.
Fully understanding the clan of ordinary people,
I wander in the world owning nothing, reflective.

§5Clad in my cloak, I wander without home,
my hair shorn, quenched.
Since I’m unburdened by the sons of Manu,
it’s inappropriate to ask me about clan.”

The Sāvitrī challenge

§6“Actually sir, when brahmins meet they politely
ask each other whether they are brahmins.”

§7“Well, if you say that you’re a brahmin,
and that I am not,
I shall question you on that Sāvitrī Prayer,
with its three lines and twenty-four syllables.”

§8“On what grounds have seers and men,
aristocrats and brahmins here in the world
performed so many different sacrifices to the gods?”

§9“During a sacrifice, should a past-master, a knowledge master,
receive an oblation, it profits the donor, I say.”

§10“Then clearly my oblation will be profitable,”
said the brahmin,
“since I have met such a knowledge master.
It’s because I’d never met anyone like you
that others ate the sacrificial cake.”

§11“So then, brahmin, since you have approached me
as a seeker of the good, ask.
Perhaps you may find here someone intelligent,
peaceful, unclouded, untroubled, with no need for hope.”

§12“Worthy Gotama, I like to sacrifice
and wish to perform a sacrifice. Please advise me,
for I do not understand
where an oblation is profitable; tell me this.”

Conduct, not birth

§13“Well then, brahmin, listen up, I will teach you the Dhamma.

§14Don’t ask about birth, ask about conduct;
for the fire of Jātaveda is born from sticks.
A steadfast sage, even though from a low class family,
is a thoroughbred checked by conscience.

The catalogue of the worthy

§15Tamed by truth, fulfilled by taming,
a complete knowledge master who has completed the spiritual journey—
that is where a brahmin seeking merit
should bestow a timely oblation as sacrifice.

§16Those who have left sensuality behind, wandering homeless,
self-controlled, straight as a shuttle—
that is where a brahmin seeking merit
should bestow a timely oblation as sacrifice.

§17Those freed of greed, with senses stilled,
like the moon released from the eclipse—
that is where a brahmin seeking merit
should bestow a timely oblation as sacrifice.

§18They wander the world unobstructed,
always mindful, calling nothing their own—
that is where a brahmin seeking merit
should bestow a timely oblation as sacrifice.

§19Having left sensuality behind, wandering triumphant,
knowing the end of rebirth and death,
quenched and cool as a lake:
the Realized One is worthy of the sacrificial cake.

§20Good among the good, far from the bad,
the Realized One has infinite wisdom.
Unsullied in this life and the next:
the Realized One is worthy of the sacrificial cake.

§21In whom dwells no deceit or conceit,
rid of greed, unselfish, with no need for hope,
with anger cast aside, quenched,
a brahmin rid of sorrow’s stain:
the Realized One is worthy of the sacrificial cake.

§22He has given up the mind’s home,
and has no possessions at all.
Not grasping to this life or the next:
the Realized One is worthy of the sacrificial cake.

§23Serene, he has crossed the flood,
and has understood the teaching with ultimate view.
With defilements ended, bearing his final body:
the Realized One is worthy of the sacrificial cake.

§24In whom desire to be reborn, and caustic speech
are cleared and ended, and are no more;
that knowledge master, everywhere free:
the Realized One is worthy of the sacrificial cake.

§25He has slipped his chains, he’s chained no more,
among those caught in conceit he is free of conceit;
he has fully understood suffering with its field and ground:
the Realized One is worthy of the sacrificial cake.

§26Not relying on hope, seeing seclusion,
well past the views proclaimed by others.
In him there are no supporting conditions at all:
the Realized One is worthy of the sacrificial cake.

§27He has comprehended all things, high and low,
cleared them and ended them, so they are no more.
Peaceful, freed in the ending of grasping:
the Realized One is worthy of the sacrificial cake.

§28He sees the ending of rebirth’s fetter,
and has cast aside all manner of desire.
Pure, stainless, immaculate, flawless:
the Realized One is worthy of the sacrificial cake.

§29Not seeing himself in terms of a self,
he is stilled, sincere, and steadfast.
Imperturbable, kind, wishless:
the Realized One is worthy of the sacrificial cake.

§30He harbors no delusions within at all,
he has insight into all things.
He bears his final body,
attained to the state of grace, the supreme awakening.
That’s how the purity of a spirit is defined:
the Realized One is worthy of the sacrificial cake.”

Food enchanted by a verse

§31“Let my oblation be a true offering,
since I have found such a knowledge master!
I see divinity in person! Accept my offering, Blessed One:
please eat my sacrificial cake.”

§32“Food enchanted by a verse isn’t fit for me to eat.
That’s not the principle of those who see, brahmin.
The Buddhas cast aside things enchanted with verses.
Since there is such a principle, brahmin, that’s how they live.

§33Serve with other food and drink
the consummate one, the great seer,
with defilements ended and remorse stilled.
For he is the field for the seeker of merit.”

Who then should eat it?

§34“Please, Blessed One, help me understand:
now that I have found your teaching,
when I look for someone during a sacrifice,
who should eat the religious donation of one like me?”

§35“One who is rid of aggression,
whose mind is unclouded,
who is liberated from sensual pleasures,
and who has cast aside dullness.

§36One who has erased perimeters and limits,
expert in birth and death,
a sage, blessed with sagacity.
When such a person comes to the sacrifice,

§37get rid of your scowl!
Honor them with cupped palms,
and venerate them with food and drink,
and in this way your religious donation will succeed.”

§38“The Buddha is worthy of the sacrificial cake,
he is the supreme field of merit,
recipient of gifts from the whole world;
what’s given to the worthy one is very fruitful.”

Ordination on the spot

§39Then the brahmin Bhāradvāja of Sundarikā said to the Buddha, “Excellent, worthy Gotama! Excellent! As if he were righting the overturned, or revealing the hidden, or pointing out the path to the lost, or lighting a lamp in the dark so people with clear eyes can see what’s there, worthy Gotama has made the teaching clear in many ways. I go for refuge to the worthy Gotama, to the teaching, and to the mendicant Saṅgha. May I receive the going forth, the ordination in the worthy Gotama’s presence?” And the brahmin Bhāradvāja of Sundarikā received the going forth, the ordination in the Buddha’s presence. And soon after, he became one of the perfected.

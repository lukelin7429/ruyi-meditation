Sutta Nipāta · Discourse 4.10

Before the Breakup

Purābhedasutta

Setting
Not stated in the verse itself; no location or occasion is named
Speakers
An unnamed questioner and the Buddha
Form
Continuous philosophical verse, minimal narrative frame — a single question answered in one uninterrupted reply
Length
~3 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A version of much of the Aṭṭhakavagga survives in the Chinese Yizu Jing (義足經, T198), translated in the 3rd century CE, though exact correspondence to this individual sutta is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — a long, cumulative list of qualities rather than an argument, but demanding in how much it compresses into each line

Why this discourse, the tenth

The title comes from a single striking phrase buried in the second verse: “rid of craving before the breakup” — before the body comes apart, before death. The question that opens the discourse asks, in effect, what a person would have to be like right now, while still alive, to be genuinely at peace regardless of what is coming. Unlike Snp 4.9, which builds its case through back-and-forth argument, this discourse is almost entirely descriptive: verse after verse simply lists what such a person is not swayed by, not owning, not grieving, not asserting. Read together, the qualities build into one of the fullest character portraits the Aṭṭhakavagga offers of what non-attachment to views actually looks like when it is lived out, rather than merely argued for.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A person genuinely at peace “before the breakup” of the body is described not by any doctrine they hold but by an accumulating list of attachments they no longer have — to a beginning, a self-image, a possession, an opinion of others, or even to continued existence itself.

A single question

The discourse opens with an unnamed questioner asking, directly, what someone must see and how they must behave to be called “at peace,” addressing the Buddha and requesting an account of “the ultimate person.” There is no dialogue back and forth after this — the entire rest of the sutta is the Buddha’s single, sustained reply, verse building on verse without interruption. This shape suits the content: rather than working through an argument that could be challenged and refined, the discourse simply lays out a portrait, item by item, the way one might describe a person by naming everything they are free of.

Not dependent on a beginning or an end

The reply opens by describing someone “not dependent on the first beginning, unfathomable in the middle,” who is “not governed by anything” — language that immediately signals this isn’t a portrait of good behavior alone but of a mind that has stopped anchoring itself to a fixed starting point or a settled position at all. From there the list turns concrete: unangry, unafraid, not boastful or regretful, thoughtful in counsel rather than restless, controlled in speech; rid of attachment to the future and free of grief for the past; a “seer of seclusion in the midst of contacts” who is not led astray among views even while still meeting the world through the senses.

Ordinary virtues, unusually completed

A long central stretch reads almost like an ethical checklist — free of deceit, not envious or stingy, not rude, not given to slander, not swept up in pleasure or arrogance, gentle and articulate. But the discourse keeps pushing past ordinary virtue into something more specific: not merely honest but “not hostile due to craving,” not merely content but free of desire for profit and unbothered by its absence, not merely calm but equanimous in a way that leaves “no craving… to continue existence or to end it” — ruling out not just attachment to living but also, notably, any wish for annihilation as its opposite extreme.

Nothing to pick up, nothing to put down

Several verses describe this person through what they simply lack: no children or livestock, no fields or lands, “no picking up or putting down” — and, strikingly, indifference to how others categorize them, whether as ordinary person, ascetic, or brahmin, “which is why they’re unperturbed by words.” The discourse closes by drawing the whole portrait together: freed of greed, not stingy, never speaking of themselves as superior, inferior, or equal to anyone, having nothing in the world to call their own, and so having nothing left to grieve over — “that’s who is said to be at peace.”

Key terms

purābheda — literally “before the breaking up” — the discourse’s title, referring to the dissolution of the body at death, and by extension to the question of what peace looks like while one is still alive to have it.
“not governed by anything” — part of the opening description of the peaceful person, signaling detachment from any fixed starting point or settled position, not merely good conduct.
“a seer of seclusion in the midst of contacts” — a description of someone who remains inwardly withdrawn even while still meeting the world through the six senses — seclusion as a quality of mind, not a change of location.
“no picking up or putting down” — a recurring phrase across the Aṭṭhakavagga for a mind that has stopped the whole cycle of grasping and releasing, since it never took hold of anything to begin with.
“equal, special, or less than” — the three forms of self-comparison the discourse repeatedly names as what a genuinely peaceful person has simply stopped doing, echoed almost word for word in Snp 4.9 and Snp 4.15.

The text

The discourse in full, all fourteen verses: a single question, and the Buddha’s unbroken reply. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 1“Seeing how, behaving how,
is one said to be at peace?
When asked, Gotama, please tell me
about the ultimate person.”

Verse 2“Rid of craving before the breakup,”
said the Buddha,
“not dependent on the first beginning,
unfathomable in the middle,
they are not governed by anything.

Verse 3Unangry, unafraid,
not boastful or regretful,
thoughtful in counsel, not restless—
truly that sage is controlled in speech.

Verse 4Rid of attachment to the future,
they don’t grieve for the past.
A seer of seclusion in the midst of contacts
is not led astray among views.

Verse 5Withdrawn, free of deceit,
they’re not envious or stingy,
nor rude or disgusting,
or given to slander.

Verse 6Not swept up in pleasures,
or given to arrogance,
they’re gentle and articulate,
neither hungering nor growing dispassionate.

Verse 7Not training for desire for profit,
nor getting annoyed at lack of profit;
they are not hostile due to craving,
for they are not greedy for flavors.

Verse 8Equanimous, ever mindful,
they never conceive themselves in the world
as equal, special, or less than;
they have no pretensions.

Verse 9They have no dependencies,
understanding the teaching, they are independent.
No craving is found in them
to continue existence or to end it.

Verse 10I declare them to be at peace,
unconcerned for sensual pleasures.
No ties are found in them,
they have crossed over clinging.

Verse 11They have no children or livestock,
nor possess fields or lands.
No picking up or putting down
is found in them.

Verse 12That by which one might describe
an ordinary person or ascetics and brahmins
has no importance to them,
which is why they’re unperturbed by words.

Verse 13Freed of greed, not stingy,
a sage doesn’t speak of themselves as being
among superiors, inferiors, or equals.
One not prone to creation does not return to creation.

Verse 14They who have nothing in the world of their own
do not grieve for that which is not,
or drift among the teachings;
that’s who is said to be at peace.”

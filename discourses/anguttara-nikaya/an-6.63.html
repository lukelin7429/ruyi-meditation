Aṅguttara Nikāya · Discourse 6.63

Penetrative

Nibbedhikasutta

Setting
No location given; addressed directly to the assembled mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha alone, in a long, uninterrupted systematic exposition
Form
One analytical method — source, variety, result, cessation, and the path to cessation — applied in turn to six major topics
Length
~16 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Ekottarika-āgama (T125), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — dense and systematic, repeating the same five-part analysis six times over; rewards a slow, structured reading rather than a quick one

Why this discourse

This is not a story or a dialogue — it is a method, demonstrated six times in a row. The Buddha takes six major topics that between them cover most of what a person’s inner life is made of — sensual pleasure, feeling, perception, defilement, deed, and suffering — and puts every single one through the same precise analytical grid: what is it, where does it come from, how does it vary, what does it produce, how does it end, and what practice actually ends it? The discourse rewards patience. Read straight through, its most quoted passage — a compact, technical definition of kamma as intention itself — turns out to be one movement in a much larger, carefully symmetrical piece of analysis.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A single method — tracing any phenomenon back to its source, mapping its variety, naming its result, identifying its cessation, and specifying the practice that leads to that cessation — is applied in turn to sensual pleasures, feelings, perceptions, defilements, deeds, and suffering, showing that the same noble eightfold path uproots all six.

“Penetrative”: a method announced up front

The Buddha opens by naming what he is about to do: teach “a penetrative exposition of the teaching” (nibbedhika-pariyāya), then lists the six things that “should be known” together with their “source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation”: sensual pleasures, feelings, perceptions, defilements, deeds, and suffering. Only after stating the whole scheme in outline does he go back through it topic by topic, using an identical rhetorical device throughout — stating a claim, asking “why did I say it?”, and then supplying the analysis.

Six topics, one recurring lens

The six analyses share a structure closely enough that reading them side by side reveals as much as reading any single one in isolation:

TopicSourceWhat stands out
Sensual pleasuresContactRedefined: the world’s attractive sights, sounds, and so on are merely “kinds of sensual stimulation” — the actual sensual pleasure is a person’s own “greedy intention” toward them
FeelingsContactThree basic kinds (pleasant, painful, neutral), each further split into feelings “of the flesh” and “not of the flesh”
PerceptionsContactSix kinds, one per sense door; their “result” is communication — putting what was perceived into words for others
DefilementsIgnoranceOnly three named — sensuality, desire to be reborn, and ignorance itself — and uniquely traced to ignorance rather than contact
Deeds (kamma)ContactDefined directly as intention; its result is described as threefold by timing — in this life, on rebirth, or at some later time
SufferingCravingRestated in the familiar terms of the first noble truth; its “result” is either confusion or a search for a way out

Every single one of the six closes with an identical line: the practice leading to its cessation is “simply this noble eightfold path.” The repetition is not padding — it is the discourse’s real point. Six very different-looking aspects of experience, sensual craving, raw feeling, the labeling of experience, deep-seated defilement, willed action, and suffering itself, all terminate in exactly the same practice. There are not six paths to six different problems; there is one path that resolves all six, because the six are, at bottom, deeply interlinked.

Sensual pleasure is not “out there”

The analysis of sensual pleasures makes a sharp distinction worth pausing on. The five kinds of sensual stimulation — attractive sights, sounds, smells, tastes, and touches — are explicitly said not to be sensual pleasures themselves: “in the training of the Noble One they’re called ‘kinds of sensual stimulation.’” The actual sensual pleasure is “a person’s greedy intention” toward those things. The verse that closes this section states the point with unusual directness: “the world’s pretty things aren’t sensual pleasures… the world’s pretty things stay just as they are, but the attentive remove desire for them.” The object in the world is morally neutral; the craving a mind directs toward it is where the actual problem, and the actual work, lies.

What contact, and what ignorance, produce

Four of the six topics — sensual pleasures, feelings, perceptions, and deeds — are traced back to the same single source: contact (phassa), the meeting of a sense faculty, its object, and consciousness. Defilements break this pattern, tracing instead to ignorance, and suffering traces to craving. Read together, this sketches a rough map of how the standard chain of dependent origination (contact → feeling → craving → grasping) is being cut into cross-sections here from several different angles at once, rather than narrated as a single forward-moving sequence the way it usually is elsewhere.

The kamma section, closely read

The analysis of deeds contains the discourse’s most quoted line: “It is intention that I call deeds. For after making a choice one acts by way of body, speech, and mind.” This single sentence locates the entire moral weight of an action in the intention (cetanā) behind it, rather than in the bare physical or verbal act considered on its own — the same intentional act might be expressed through the body, through speech, or purely in the mind, and in every case it is the intention, not the channel, that constitutes the deed. The section goes on to describe the “diversity” of deeds as leading to five different destinations — hell, the animal realm, the ghost realm, the human world, or the world of the gods — and its “result” as unfolding on three possible timescales: in this very life, upon rebirth in the next life, or at some later time still. Between the definition of kamma as intention and this threefold account of when a deed can ripen, this short passage is one of the most frequently cited in the entire canon for understanding how the tradition technically defines moral action.

Suffering, closing the circle

The final topic, suffering, restates the first noble truth almost verbatim — rebirth, old age, illness, death, sorrow, and not getting what one wishes for, summarized as “the five grasping aggregates.” Its source is craving; its “result” is described with unusual psychological precision as either “confusion” (someone overwhelmed simply wails and beats their breast) or a “search” (someone overwhelmed instead goes looking outward, “who knows one or two phrases to stop this suffering?”). Ending on suffering, after five other topics that each, in their own way, feed into it, gives the whole discourse a cumulative shape: sensual craving, feeling, perception, defilement, and kamma are not separate problems alongside suffering — they are its ingredients.

Key terms

nibbedhika-pariyāya — “penetrative exposition”: the discourse’s own name for its method, an analysis sharp and thorough enough to cut through to the root of a phenomenon.
phassa — contact; the meeting of sense faculty, object, and consciousness, named as the source of sensual pleasures, feelings, perceptions, and deeds alike.
āsava — defilement or effluent; here limited to three — sensuality, desire to be reborn, and ignorance — and uniquely traced back to ignorance rather than contact.
cetanā — intention; defined here as identical with kamma itself: “it is intention that I call deeds.”
sāmisa / nirāmisa — “of the flesh” and “not of the flesh”: the discourse’s subdivision of each of the three feelings, distinguishing worldly, sense-based feeling from feeling arising in a more renunciate or meditative context.

The text

The discourse in full: the method announced, then applied in turn to sensual pleasures, feelings, perceptions, defilements, deeds, and suffering. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The method announced

§1–7“Mendicants, I will teach you a penetrative exposition of the teaching. Listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this: “Mendicants, what is the penetrative exposition of the teaching? Sensual pleasures should be known. And their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation should be known. Feelings should be known. And their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation should be known. Perceptions should be known. And their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation should be known. Defilements should be known. And their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation should be known. Deeds should be known. And their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation should be known. Suffering should be known. And its source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to its cessation should be known.

Sensual pleasures

§8‘Sensual pleasures should be known. And their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation should be known.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? There are these five kinds of sensual stimulation. Sights known by the eye, which are likable, desirable, agreeable, pleasant, sensual, and arousing. Sounds known by the ear… smells known by the nose… tastes known by the tongue… touches known by the body, which are likable, desirable, agreeable, pleasant, sensual, and arousing. However, these are not sensual pleasures. In the training of the Noble One they’re called ‘kinds of sensual stimulation’. Greedy intention is a person’s sensual pleasure. The world’s pretty things aren’t sensual pleasures. Greedy intention is a person’s sensual pleasure. The world’s pretty things stay just as they are, but the attentive remove desire for them.

§9–14And what is the source of sensual pleasures? Contact is their source. And what is the diversity of sensual pleasures? The sensual desire for sights, sounds, smells, tastes, and touches are all different. This is called the diversity of sensual pleasures. And what is the result of sensual pleasures? When one who desires sensual pleasures generates a corresponding incarnation, partaking of either good or bad deeds—this is called the result of sensual pleasures. And what is the cessation of sensual pleasures? When contact ceases, sensual pleasures cease. The practice that leads to the cessation of sensual pleasures is simply this noble eightfold path, that is: right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion. When a noble disciple understands sensual pleasures in this way—and understands their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation—they understand that this penetrative spiritual life is the cessation of sensual pleasures. ‘Sensual pleasures should be known. And their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation should be known.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

Feelings

§15‘Feelings should be known. And their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation should be known.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? There are these three feelings: pleasant, painful, and neutral.

§16–20And what is the source of feelings? Contact is their source. And what is the diversity of feelings? There are pleasant feelings of the flesh, pleasant feelings not of the flesh, painful feelings of the flesh, painful feelings not of the flesh, neutral feelings of the flesh, and neutral feelings not of the flesh. This is called the diversity of feelings. And what is the result of feelings? When one who feels generates a corresponding incarnation, partaking of either good or bad deeds—this is called the result of feelings. And what is the cessation of feelings? When contact ceases, feelings cease. The practice that leads to the cessation of feelings is simply this noble eightfold path, that is: right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion. When a noble disciple understands feelings in this way … they understand that this penetrative spiritual life is the cessation of feelings. ‘Feelings should be known. And their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation should be known.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

Perceptions

§21‘Perceptions should be known. And their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation should be known.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? There are these six perceptions: perceptions of sights, sounds, smells, tastes, touches, and ideas.

§22–26And what is the source of perceptions? Contact is their source. And what is the diversity of perceptions? The perceptions of sights, sounds, smells, tastes, touches, and ideas are all different. This is called the diversity of perceptions. And what is the result of perceptions? Communication is the result of perception, I say. You communicate something in whatever manner you perceive it, saying ‘That’s what I perceived.’ This is called the result of perceptions. And what is the cessation of perception? When contact ceases, perception ceases. The practice that leads to the cessation of perceptions is simply this noble eightfold path, that is: right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion. When a noble disciple understands perception in this way … they understand that this penetrative spiritual life is the cessation of perception. ‘Perceptions should be known. And their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation should be known.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

Defilements

§27‘Defilements should be known. And their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation should be known.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? There are these three defilements: the defilements of sensuality, desire to be reborn, and ignorance.

§28–32And what is the source of defilements? Ignorance is the source of defilements. And what is the disparity of defilements? There are defilements that lead to rebirth in hell, the animal realm, the ghost realm, the human world, and the world of the gods. This is called the disparity of defilements. And what is the result of defilements? When one who is ignorant generates a corresponding incarnation, partaking of either good or bad deeds—this is called the result of defilements. And what is the cessation of defilements? When ignorance ceases, defilements cease. The practice that leads to the cessation of defilements is simply this noble eightfold path, that is: right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion. When a noble disciple understands defilements in this way … they understand that this penetrative spiritual life is the cessation of defilements. ‘Defilements should be known. And their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation should be known.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

Deeds

§33‘Deeds should be known. And their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation should be known.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? It is intention that I call deeds. For after making a choice one acts by way of body, speech, and mind.

§34–38And what is the source of deeds? Contact is their source. And what is the disparity of deeds? There are deeds that lead to rebirth in hell, the animal realm, the ghost realm, the human world, and the world of the gods. This is called the disparity of deeds. And what is the result of deeds? The result of deeds is threefold, I say: in this very life, on rebirth in the next life, or at some later time. This is called the result of deeds. And what is the cessation of deeds? When contact ceases, deeds cease. The practice that leads to the cessation of deeds is simply this noble eightfold path, that is: right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion. When a noble disciple understands deeds in this way … they understand that this penetrative spiritual life is the cessation of deeds. ‘Deeds should be known. And their source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to their cessation should be known.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

Suffering

§39‘Suffering should be known. And its source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to its cessation should be known.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? Rebirth is suffering; old age is suffering; illness is suffering; death is suffering; sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress are suffering; not getting what you wish for is suffering. In brief, the five grasping aggregates are suffering.

§40–44And what is the source of suffering? Craving is the source of suffering. And what is the diversity of suffering? There is suffering that is severe, mild, slow to fade, and quick to fade. This is called the diversity of suffering. And what is the result of suffering? It’s when someone who is overcome and overwhelmed by suffering sorrows and wails and laments, beating their breast and falling into confusion. Or else, overcome by that suffering, they begin an external search, wondering: ‘Who knows one or two phrases to stop this suffering?’ The result of suffering is either confusion or a search, I say. This is called the result of suffering. And what is the cessation of suffering? When craving ceases, suffering ceases. The practice that leads to the cessation of suffering is simply this noble eightfold path, that is: right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion. When a noble disciple understands suffering in this way … they understand that this penetrative spiritual life is the cessation of suffering. ‘Suffering should be known. And its source, diversity, result, cessation, and the practice that leads to its cessation should be known.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

§45This is the penetrative exposition of the teaching.”

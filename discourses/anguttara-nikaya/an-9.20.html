Aṅguttara Nikāya · Discourse 9.20

About Velāma

Velāmasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, at Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speakers
The Buddha and the householder Anāthapiṇḍika
Form
A dialogue on ordinary giving that opens into the Buddha’s own past-life story, then a long, deliberately escalating chain of comparisons
Length
~9 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Ekottarika-āgama (T125), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the escalating structure is easy to follow, but keeping every rung of the ladder straight takes attention

Why this discourse

Anāthapiṇḍika, the wealthy patron whose generosity built much of the material foundation for the Buddha’s community, believes that no gift could possibly be more fruitful than what his household is already giving. The Buddha doesn’t simply agree or correct him — he tells a story about an almost unimaginably lavish gift from his own distant past, and then, with careful deliberateness, shows rung by rung that even that legendary offering falls short of things that cost far less: feeding one person of genuine insight, taking refuge with a confident heart, keeping five simple precepts, a few moments of loving-kindness, and finally, a single instant of clearly seeing impermanence. Few discourses make the relative weight of generosity, ethics, and insight so vivid, or so deliberately humbling.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Recounting his own past life as the brahmin Velāma, who once gave a fantastically lavish material gift, the Buddha builds a long, deliberately escalating ladder of comparisons to show that generosity toward increasingly awakened recipients, then supporting the monastic community as a whole, then going for refuge, then ethical conduct, then loving-kindness, and finally a moment of insight into impermanence, are each more fruitful than the last — ending with the strongest possible claim: that direct insight outweighs even the most extraordinary generosity imaginable.

An ordinary conversation, at first

Anāthapiṇḍika, the householder whose donations of land and buildings made much of the Buddha’s settled monastic life possible, is asked a simple, almost domestic question: does his household still give gifts? He answers modestly — only coarse gruel, he says, “with false black pepper.” The Buddha’s reply reframes the whole question of what makes a gift valuable, independent of how expensive it is: a gift given carelessly, thoughtlessly, not with one’s own hand, without any thought for its consequences, brings a correspondingly weak result, however costly the item given; a gift given carefully, thoughtfully, and with real consideration brings a strong result, even if the item itself is modest. Only after establishing that the manner of giving matters as much as its material scale does the Buddha turn to Velāma.

An almost unimaginable gift

The Buddha then reveals that in a distant past life he was himself the brahmin Velāma, and that he once gave a legendary gift: 84,000 gold cups filled with silver, 84,000 silver cups filled with gold, 84,000 bronze cups filled with gold coins, 84,000 elephants and 84,000 chariots decked in fine hides and gold ornaments, 84,000 milk cows, 84,000 jewel-adorned attendants, 84,000 couches spread with fine covers, and 8,400,000,000 lengths of the finest cloth — “who can say how much food, drink, snacks, meals, refreshments, and beverages? It seemed like an overflowing river.” The scale is deliberately, almost comically vast; nothing in the ordinary economy of giving could plausibly exceed it. And then, immediately, the Buddha adds the detail that undercuts the whole story: at that time, there was no one who had reached any stage of awakening to receive it, and so no one able to fully purify the gift by receiving it well.

The ladder, rung by rung

What follows is one continuous chain, each step declared more fruitful than the one before, moving from ordinary material giving toward what the discourse treats as most valuable of all:

Each rung multiplies the value of the one before by a factor of roughly a hundred, and the chain runs for eleven full steps before it even reaches ethics, refuge, and loving-kindness — and then adds one further, twelfth step beyond all of that.

What the escalation is actually arguing

It would be easy to read this only as a claim that recipients matter — that a gift to a highly attained person outweighs the same gift to an ordinary one. That is part of the point, but not the discourse’s real destination. Notice where the ladder ends up: after the recipients run out (there being nothing higher than the Buddha and the full Saṅgha to give to), the comparison pivots entirely away from giving as such. Building a shelter, going for refuge, keeping precepts, and cultivating loving-kindness are not gifts to any recipient at all — they are things the giver does for their own mind and conduct. And the discourse’s final and strongest claim — that a bare instant of perceiving impermanence outweighs everything listed before it, including a period of loving-kindness “as long as it takes to pull a cow’s udder” — completes that pivot. The most fruitful thing a person can do, in this account, isn’t an act of generosity at all, however sincere or well-directed; it is a moment of direct insight.

Not a devaluation of giving

This is not, however, an argument that generosity doesn’t matter, or that Anāthapiṇḍika’s modest gifts of gruel are worthless. The entire ladder is built by comparison, and every rung, including the very first, is still described as fruitful — feeding even an ordinary recipient is not condemned, only shown to be exceeded by something else. The discourse’s rhetorical strategy is escalation, not rejection: it takes the single most extravagant act of material generosity its audience could imagine, grants it its full weight, and then insists that ethical conduct, refuge, loving-kindness, and insight are worth still more. For a wealthy donor like Anāthapiṇḍika, already inclined to think of merit primarily in terms of what he can afford to give away, that is a pointed and useful redirection — not away from generosity, but toward the completion generosity is meant to serve.

Key terms

Anāthapiṇḍika — the wealthy Sāvatthī householder whose donation of Jeta’s Grove provided the Buddha’s community with its most famous monastery; a recurring figure across the early texts as a model lay donor.
Velāma — the brahmin the Buddha identifies as one of his own past lives, remembered for an almost unimaginably lavish act of material generosity.
dakkhiṇā — a religious offering or gift; the discourse concerns what makes such an offering more or less “purified,” both by the manner of giving and by the spiritual attainment of the recipient.
paccekabuddha — an “Independent Buddha,” who awakens without a teacher but, unlike a fully awakened Buddha, does not establish a wider teaching for others; placed here between the arahants and the Buddha himself.
anicca-saññā — the perception of impermanence; a single moment of it, even “for as long as a finger-snap,” is named as the discourse’s single most fruitful thing to cultivate.

The text

The discourse in full: the conversation on ordinary giving, the story of Velāma’s legendary gift, and the complete escalating chain of comparisons running from feeding a stream-enterer through to a single moment’s perception of impermanence. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

An ordinary question

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Then the householder Anāthapiṇḍika went up to the Buddha, bowed, and sat down to one side. The Buddha said to him,

Careless giving, careful giving

§2“Householder, I wonder whether your family gives gifts?” “It does, sir. But only coarse gruel with false black pepper.” “Householder, someone might give a gift that’s either coarse or fine. But they give it carelessly, thoughtlessly, not with their own hand. They give the dregs, and they give without consideration for consequences. Then wherever the result of any such gift manifests, their mind doesn’t incline toward enjoyment of nice food, clothes, vehicles, or the five refined kinds of sensual stimulation. And their children, wives, bondservants, servants, and workers don’t want to listen to them. They don’t actively listen or try to understand. Why is that? Because that is the result of deeds done carelessly.

§3Someone might give a gift that’s either coarse or fine. And they give it carefully, thoughtfully, with their own hand. They don’t give the dregs, and they give with consideration for consequences. Then wherever the result of any such gift manifests, their mind inclines toward enjoyment of nice food, clothes, vehicles, or the five refined kinds of sensual stimulation. And their children, wives, bondservants, servants, and workers want to listen. They actively listen and try to understand. Why is that? Because that is the result of deeds done carefully.

The gift of Velāma

§4Once upon a time, householder, there was a brahmin named Velāma. He gave the following gift, a great offering. 84,000 gold cups filled with silver. 84,000 silver cups filled with gold. 84,000 bronze cups filled with gold coins. 84,000 elephants with gold adornments and banners, covered with snow gold netting. 84,000 chariots upholstered with the hide of lions, tigers, and leopards, and cream rugs, with gold adornments and banners, covered with snow gold netting. 84,000 milk cows with muslin halter and bronze pails. 84,000 maidens bedecked with jeweled earrings. 84,000 couches spread with woolen covers—shag-piled, pure white, or embroidered with flowers—and spread with a fine deer hide, with canopies above and red pillows at both ends. 8,400,000,000 delicate cloths of linen, cotton, silk, and wool. And who can say how much food, drink, snacks, meals, refreshments, and beverages? It seemed like an overflowing river.

No one to receive it

§5Householder, you might think: ‘Surely the brahmin Velāma must have been someone else at that time?’ But you should not see it like this. I myself was the brahmin Velāma at that time. I gave that gift, a great offering. But at that event there was no-one worthy of a religious donation, and no-one to purify the religious donation.

The ladder begins

§6–7It would be more fruitful to feed one individual accomplished in view than that great offering of Velāma.

It would be more fruitful to feed one once-returner than a hundred of those accomplished in view.

The ladder continues

§8–9It would be more fruitful to feed one non-returner than a hundred once-returners. It would be more fruitful to feed one perfected one than a hundred non-returners. It would be more fruitful to feed one Independent Buddha than a hundred perfected ones. It would be more fruitful to feed one Realized One, a perfected one, a fully awakened Buddha than a hundred Independent Buddhas. It would be more fruitful to feed the mendicant Saṅgha headed by the Buddha than to feed one Realized One, a perfected one, a fully awakened Buddha. It would be more fruitful to build a dwelling especially for the Saṅgha of the four quarters than to feed the mendicant Saṅgha headed by the Buddha. It would be more fruitful to go for refuge to the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha with a confident heart than to build a dwelling for the Saṅgha of the four quarters. It would be more fruitful to undertake the training rules—not to kill living creatures, steal, commit sexual misconduct, lie, or consume intoxicants of beer, wine, and liquor—than to go for refuge to the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha with a confident heart. It would be more fruitful to develop a heart of love—even just as long as it takes to pull a cow’s udder—than to undertake the training rules.

It would be more fruitful to develop the perception of impermanence—even for as long as a finger-snap—than to do all of these things, including developing a heart of love for as long as it takes to pull a cow’s udder.”

Aṅguttara Nikāya · Discourse 3.65

With the Kālāmas of Kesamutta

Kesamuttisutta — widely known as the Kālāma Sutta

Setting
Kesamutta, a town of the Kālāma people in the Kosalan territories, while the Buddha was wandering with a large Saṅgha
Speakers
The Buddha and the assembled Kālāmas of Kesamutta
Form
A community's question about conflicting teachers, answered with a list of unreliable criteria, a positive test, a worked example, and a closing “four assurances” passage
Length
~9 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Ekottarika-āgama (T125), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — plainly stated and famous, though its popular reputation often gets its actual argument wrong

Why this discourse

This is probably the single most quoted discourse in the entire Aṅguttara Nikāya — excerpted on posters, invoked in interfaith dialogue, and cited as an early charter for free inquiry and rational skepticism. That popularity is well earned, but it also invites a shortcut: the sutta is often boiled down to “believe nothing, question everything,” as if the Buddha were simply endorsing whatever each person happens to personally prefer. Read in full, the discourse gives a much more specific and demanding test than that — one grounded in observing the actual, practical, ethical consequences of a teaching, not in individual taste. Understanding that difference is most of the value of reading it directly rather than only in excerpt.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Rather than trusting a claim because of tradition, logic, or a teacher’s apparent authority, test it against its observed effect — does undertaking it lead, in practice, to harm and suffering, or to welfare and happiness — and let that verdict, not the source, decide whether to accept or reject it.

A confused community

The Kālāmas of Kesamutta bring the Buddha a genuinely practical problem, not an abstract philosophical one. Many different wandering ascetics and brahmins pass through their town; each one “explains and promotes only their own doctrine, while they attack, badmouth, disparage, and smear the doctrines of others.” Faced with this stream of confident, mutually contradicting teachers, the Kālāmas are left, in their own words, “doubting and uncertain” about which of them speaks truth. The Buddha’s first response is a small but important reassurance: doubt in the face of a genuinely uncertain matter is not a failure of faith — “it is enough, Kālāmas, for you to be doubting and uncertain.”

Ten things not to rely on

The Buddha then lists ten grounds people commonly use to settle such disputes, and tells the Kālāmas not to rely on any of them by themselves: oral tradition, a teacher’s lineage, hearsay or report, the authority of a scriptural collection, pure logic, pure inference, reasoned trains of thought, agreement with a view after mulling it over, a speaker’s seeming competence or charisma, or the simple thought “this ascetic is our own teacher.” This is a genuinely broad list — it rules out appeals to scripture and tradition as much as it rules out appeals to raw logic or to personal loyalty. None of these are dismissed as worthless in every context; the point is narrower and sharper: none of them, alone, settles which doctrine is actually true or actually good to live by.

The actual test: observed consequences

In place of all ten, the Buddha gives a single, concrete criterion: “when you know for yourselves: ‘these things are unskillful, blameworthy, criticized by sensible people, and when you undertake them, they lead to harm and suffering’, then you should give them up” — and its positive mirror, for whatever leads to welfare and happiness. This is not a demand for private intuition or unfalsifiable feeling; it specifies four separate marks to check — unskillful, blameworthy, criticized by sensible people, and, decisively, actually leading to harm when undertaken — and it is this last, observable mark that does the real work. The Buddha then walks the Kālāmas through a worked example rather than leaving the principle abstract: he asks whether greed, hate, and delusion arise for a person’s welfare or their harm, gets their agreement that each is harmful, has them confirm that a greedy, hateful, or deluded person is liable to kill, steal, lie, or push others toward the same, and only then draws the conclusion together with them, step by step, that such qualities are unskillful, blameworthy, criticized, and harmful. The same walkthrough is then repeated for contentment, love, and understanding, with the opposite result at every step.

Not “anything goes”

It is worth being direct about a common misreading. The sutta is often summarized as though the Buddha told the Kālāmas simply to trust their own judgment, full stop — a proto-modern endorsement of individual relativism, “believe whatever feels right to you.” That is not, in fact, what the text does. The criterion offered is specific and outward-facing: does a quality or teaching lead, in observable practice, to harm and suffering, or to welfare and happiness, when it is actually undertaken? Greed, hate, and delusion fail this test not because the Kālāmas merely feel that they are bad, but because the Buddha walks them through the concrete behavior — killing, stealing, lying — that these states actually produce. The test is empirical and ethical, aimed at real-world consequences that in principle anyone applying it carefully should be able to converge on, not a license for each person to reach a different, equally valid answer.

The four assurances

Having established the test, the Buddha describes what a “noble disciple” who has actually given up ill will looks like: someone who meditates spreading a boundless heart of love, compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity in all directions, to the whole world. Such a person, the Buddha says, has won four consolations in this very life — and this closing passage is the discourse’s most quietly radical move. Each consolation is stated as a conditional, deliberately keeping open exactly the metaphysical questions the Kālāmas might otherwise get stuck debating: if there is another world and kamma bears fruit, this person is set for a good rebirth; if there is no other world and no fruit of kamma, they are still, right now, free of enmity and living happily; if bad things do befall the bad, they have no bad intentions to worry about; and if bad things don’t befall the bad, they can still see themselves as pure either way. The ethical life recommended here does not wait on first settling questions about karma or an afterlife that cannot be settled by ordinary observation — it pays off, the Buddha argues, under either possible answer.

Key terms

Kālāma — the clan or people of the town of Kesamutta, in Kosalan territory, whose question to the Buddha gives the discourse its popular name.
the ten unreliable grounds — oral tradition, lineage, hearsay, scriptural authority, logic, inference, reasoned thought, agreement after deliberation, a speaker’s apparent competence, and teacher-loyalty; none, alone, settles a doctrine’s truth.
the actual test — whether a quality, when undertaken, is unskillful, blameworthy, criticized by sensible people, and leads to harm and suffering — or the opposite.
the four immeasurables — love (mettā), compassion (karuṇā), rejoicing (muditā), and equanimity (upekkhā), spread without limit in all directions by the noble disciple described near the close.
the four assurances — a set of conditional consolations showing that blameless conduct pays off whether or not kamma, rebirth, and the fruit of good and bad deeds ultimately turn out to be real.

The text

The discourse in full: the Kālāmas' question, the ten unreliable grounds, the worked example testing greed, hate, and delusion against their opposites, and the closing four assurances. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A confused community

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was wandering in the land of the Kosalans together with a large Saṅgha of mendicants when he arrived at a town of the Kālāmas named Kesamutta. The Kālāmas of Kesamutta heard: “It seems the ascetic Gotama — a Sakyan, gone forth from a Sakyan family — has arrived at Kesamutta. He has this good reputation: ‘That Blessed One is perfected, a fully awakened Buddha…’ It’s good to see such perfected ones.” Then the Kālāmas went up to the Buddha. Before sitting down to one side, some bowed, some exchanged greetings and polite conversation, some held up their cupped palms toward the Buddha, some announced their name and clan, while some kept silent. Seated to one side the Kālāmas said to the Buddha:

§3“There are, sir, some ascetics and brahmins who come to Kesamutta. They explain and promote only their own doctrine, while they attack, badmouth, disparage, and smear the doctrines of others. Then some other ascetics and brahmins come to Kesamutta. They too explain and promote only their own doctrine, while they attack, badmouth, disparage, and smear the doctrines of others. So, sir, we’re doubting and uncertain: ‘I wonder who of these respected ascetics and brahmins speaks the truth, and who speaks falsehood?’” “It is enough, Kālāmas, for you to be doubting and uncertain. Doubt has come up in you about an uncertain matter.

Ten things not to rely on

§4Please, Kālāmas, don’t go by oral transmission, don’t go by lineage, don’t go by testament, don’t go by canonical authority, don’t rely on logic, don’t rely on inference, don’t go by reasoned train of thought, don’t go by the acceptance of a view after deliberation, don’t go by the appearance of competence, and don’t think ‘The ascetic is our respected teacher.’ But when you know for yourselves: ‘These things are unskillful, blameworthy, criticized by sensible people, and when you undertake them, they lead to harm and suffering’, then you should give them up.

The test applied: greed, hate, delusion

§5–8What do you think, Kālāmas? Does greed come up in a person for their welfare or harm?” “Harm, sir.” “A greedy individual, overcome by greed, kills living creatures, steals, commits adultery, lies, and encourages others to do the same. Is that for their lasting harm and suffering?” “Yes, sir.”

§9–12“What do you think, Kālāmas? Does hate come up in a person for their welfare or harm?” “Harm, sir.” “A hateful individual, overcome by hate, kills living creatures, steals, commits adultery, lies, and encourages others to do the same. Is that for their lasting harm and suffering?” “Yes, sir.”

§13–16“What do you think, Kālāmas? Does delusion come up in a person for their welfare or harm?” “Harm, sir.” “A deluded individual, overcome by delusion, kills living creatures, steals, commits adultery, lies, and encourages others to do the same. Is that for their lasting harm and suffering?” “Yes, sir.”

§17–25“What do you think, Kālāmas, are these things skillful or unskillful?” “Unskillful, sir.” “Blameworthy or blameless?” “Blameworthy, sir.” “Criticized or praised by sensible people?” “Criticized by sensible people, sir.” “When you undertake them, do they lead to harm and suffering, or not? Or how do you see this?” “When you undertake them, they lead to harm and suffering. That’s how we see it.” “So, Kālāmas, when I said: ‘Please, don’t go by oral transmission… and don’t think “The ascetic is our respected teacher.” But when you know for yourselves: “These things are unskillful, blameworthy, criticized by sensible people, and when you undertake them, they lead to harm and suffering”, then you should give them up’, that’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

The test applied: contentment, love, understanding

§26–30Please, Kālāmas, don’t go by oral transmission… and don’t think ‘The ascetic is our respected teacher.’ But when you know for yourselves: ‘These things are skillful, blameless, praised by sensible people, and when you undertake them, they lead to welfare and happiness’, then you should acquire them and keep them. What do you think, Kālāmas? Does contentment come up in a person for their welfare or harm?” “Welfare, sir.” “An individual who is content, not overcome by greed, doesn’t kill living creatures, steal, commit adultery, lie, or encourage others to do the same. Is that for their lasting welfare and happiness?” “Yes, sir.”

§31–32“What do you think, Kālāmas? Does love come up in a person for their welfare or harm?… Does understanding come up in a person for their welfare or harm?… Is that for their lasting welfare and happiness?” “Yes, sir.”

§33–41“What do you think, Kālāmas, are these things skillful or unskillful?” “Skillful, sir.” “Blameworthy or blameless?” “Blameless, sir.” “Criticized or praised by sensible people?” “Praised by sensible people, sir.” “When you undertake them, do they lead to welfare and happiness, or not? Or how do you see this?” “When you undertake them, they lead to welfare and happiness. That’s how we see it.” “So, Kālāmas, when I said: ‘Please, don’t go by oral transmission… and don’t think “The ascetic is our respected teacher.” But when you know for yourselves: “These things are skillful, blameless, praised by sensible people, and when you undertake them, they lead to welfare and happiness”, then you should acquire them and keep them’, that’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

The four immeasurables and four assurances

§42Then that noble disciple is rid of desire, rid of ill will, unconfused, aware, and mindful. They meditate spreading a heart full of love to one direction, and to the second, and to the third, and to the fourth. In the same way above, below, across, everywhere, all around, they spread a heart full of love to the whole world — abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will. They meditate spreading a heart full of compassion… a heart full of rejoicing… a heart full of equanimity to one direction, and to the second, and to the third, and to the fourth. In the same way above, below, across, everywhere, all around, they spread a heart full of equanimity to the whole world — abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will.

§43–46When that noble disciple has a mind that’s free of enmity and ill will, uncorrupted and purified, they’ve won four consolations in this very life. ‘If it turns out there is another world, and good and bad deeds have a result, then — when the body breaks up, after death — I’ll be reborn in a good place, a heavenly realm.’ This is the first consolation they’ve won. ‘If it turns out there is no other world, and good and bad deeds don’t have a result, then in this very life I’ll keep myself free of enmity and ill will, untroubled and happy.’ This is the second consolation they’ve won. ‘If it turns out that bad things happen to people who do bad things, then since I have no bad intentions, and since I’m not doing anything bad, how can suffering touch me?’ This is the third consolation they’ve won. ‘If it turns out that bad things don’t happen to people who do bad things, then I still see myself pure on both sides.’ This is the fourth consolation they’ve won.

§47–48When that noble disciple has a mind that’s free of enmity and ill will, uncorrupted and purified, they’ve won these four consolations in this very life.” “That’s so true, Blessed One! That’s so true, Holy One! When that noble disciple has a mind that’s free of enmity and ill will, uncorrupted and purified, they’ve won these four consolations in this very life…

§53Excellent, sir! Excellent!… We go for refuge to the Buddha, to the teaching, and to the mendicant Saṅgha. From this day forth, may the Buddha remember us as lay followers who have gone for refuge for life.”

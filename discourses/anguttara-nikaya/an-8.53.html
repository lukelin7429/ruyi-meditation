Aṅguttara Nikāya · Discourse 8.53

Brief Advice to Gotamī

Gotamīsutta

Setting
Near Vesālī, at the Great Wood, in the hall with the peaked roof
Speakers
The Buddha and Mahāpajāpati Gotamī, his foster mother and the first ordained bhikkhunī
Form
A request for a brief teaching, answered with two mirrored eightfold lists — one diagnostic of what is not the Dhamma, one of what is
Length
~4 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Ekottarika-āgama (T125), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — short and direct, though applying the eight abstract criteria to a real, specific teaching takes some reflection

Why this discourse

Gotamī asks for something almost every serious student eventually wants: a teaching compact enough to actually carry into solitary practice, rather than one so long it has to be studied rather than lived. What she receives is more valuable than a summary of doctrine — it is a test. Give the Buddha eight qualities any teaching either has or lacks, and you can tell, on your own, without deferring to any external authority, whether that teaching is genuinely his. Few passages in the early texts do so much epistemic work in so little space.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Asked for a teaching she can carry into solitary practice, the Buddha gives Mahāpajāpati Gotamī not a summary of doctrine but a test: any set of teachings that leads toward dispassion, unyoking, shedding, few wishes, contentment, seclusion, energy, and being easy to support can be recognized with confidence as the Buddha’s actual Dhamma and training — and any set of teachings that leads the other way categorically cannot, regardless of who claims to be transmitting it.

Gotamī’s request

Mahāpajāpati Gotamī was the Buddha’s maternal aunt, who raised him after his mother’s early death, and who later became the first woman ordained as a mendicant, going on to lead the newly founded community of bhikkhunīs. Her request here is simple and practical, the kind any experienced practitioner eventually makes: “May the Buddha please teach me Dhamma in brief. When I’ve heard it, I’ll live alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute.” She is not asking for an introduction to the teaching; she is asking for something dense enough to sustain an entire retreat of solitary reflection, and precise enough not to need the teacher present to interpret it.

Two mirrored lists, one on each side

The Buddha’s answer takes the form of two exactly parallel eightfold lists, one describing what a set of teachings looks like when it is not the genuine Dhamma, and one describing the same eight qualities inverted, describing what it looks like when it is:

passion, not dispassiondispassion, not passion

The Buddha states each result “categorically” (a term the tradition takes to mean without exception or qualification): whichever direction a body of teaching leads on all eight counts, that settles the matter — it either is, or is not, “the teaching, the training, and the Teacher’s instructions.”

Why this is a test, not just a description

What makes this passage unusually useful is what it does not require. It doesn’t require verifying a lineage, cross-checking a chain of transmission, or trusting a particular teacher’s reputation. It gives Gotamī, and by extension anyone else, a way of checking a teaching’s authenticity by its actual effects and orientation — does living by it, in practice, move a person toward less craving or more; toward more independence or more entanglement; toward a lighter footprint on others or a heavier one? Applied honestly, the test doesn’t flatter any particular institution; a teaching that carries the right vocabulary and the right chain of custody but leads, in practice, toward accumulation, crowding, and increased desire fails the test regardless of its pedigree, while unfamiliar teaching that genuinely produces dispassion, contentment, and seclusion passes it.

A pair of qualities worth pausing on

Two of the eight repay special attention because they cut against common intuitions about what makes a spiritual path successful. “Dispersal, not accumulation” runs directly counter to any teaching, religious or otherwise, that measures its own success by growth — more followers, more property, more institutional weight. And “being unburdensome, not burdensome” asks a concrete, almost logistical question: does supporting this practitioner or this practice place a manageable load on other people, or an unreasonable one? Both criteria push toward a teaching that shrinks a person’s footprint in the world rather than expanding it — the opposite of how success is usually measured almost everywhere else.

A teaching that fits its own criteria

There is also something fitting about the form of the answer itself. Gotamī asked for something brief enough to actually use, and the reply is exactly that — two short, symmetrical lists, with no narrative framing, no similes, nothing to memorize beyond the eight paired terms. The teaching about how to recognize genuine teaching is itself an example of what it recommends: compact, unburdensome, and easy to carry into solitude.

Key terms

Mahāpajāpati Gotamī — the Buddha’s maternal aunt and foster mother, and the first woman to be ordained as a mendicant; later the leader of the bhikkhunī community.
ekaṃsena — “categorically”: the term marking each of the eight criteria as decisive without exception, not merely as one factor among others to weigh.
the eight criteria — dispassion, unyoking, shedding, few wishes, contentment, seclusion, energy, and being easy to support — the test applied to any teaching claiming to be the Buddha’s.
Dhammavinaya — “the teaching and the training”: the standard compound term for the Buddha’s doctrine and discipline together, used here as what the eight criteria ultimately identify.

The text

The discourse in full: Gotamī’s request for a brief teaching, and the Buddha’s two mirrored eightfold lists in response. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Gotamī’s request

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Vesālī, at the Great Wood, in the hall with the peaked roof. Then Mahāpajāpati Gotamī went up to the Buddha, bowed, stood to one side, and said to him: “Sir, may the Buddha please teach me Dhamma in brief. When I’ve heard it, I’ll live alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute.”

What is not the teaching

§2“Gotamī, you might know that certain things lead to passion, not dispassion; to yoking, not to unyoking; to accumulation, not dispersal; to more desires, not fewer; to lack of contentment, not contentment; to crowding, not seclusion; to laziness, not energy; to being burdensome, not being unburdensome. Categorically, you should remember these things as not the teaching, not the training, and not the Teacher’s instructions.

What is the teaching

§3You might know that certain things lead to dispassion, not passion; to unyoking, not to yoking; to dispersal, not accumulation; to fewer desires, not more; to contentment, not lack of contentment; to seclusion, not crowding; to energy, not laziness; to being unburdensome, not being burdensome. Categorically, you should remember these things as the teaching, the training, and the Teacher’s instructions.”

Aṅguttara Nikāya · Discourse 6.16

Nakula’s Father

Nakulapitusutta

Setting
In the land of the Bhaggas, at Crocodile’s Bellow, in the deer park at Bhesakaḷā’s Wood, at the sickbed of a lay householder
Speakers
Chiefly the housewife Nakula’s mother, addressing her gravely ill husband Nakula’s father; the Buddha speaks in the closing exchange
Form
Six anticipated worries a dying man might carry, each named and then answered in turn, followed by the Buddha’s direct praise of the wife who gave that answer
Length
~12 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Ekottarika-āgama (T125), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a plainspoken domestic scene; the six reassurances follow one repeated pattern, easy to follow start to finish

Why this discourse

Most teachings in this collection come from the Buddha directly. This one doesn’t — its real teacher is a laywoman, sitting at her dying husband’s bedside, working through every fear he might be carrying toward death and answering each one so precisely that his illness “died down on the spot.” The Buddha’s own words arrive only afterward, and only to confirm, in detail, that everything she said about her own standing on the path was exactly true. It is one of the clearest pictures in the early texts of what a mature, decades-long lay partnership in the Dhamma could actually look like.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Sitting beside her gravely ill husband, the housewife Nakula’s mother names and dismantles, one at a time, every worry he might carry toward death — for their household, their marriage, and her own place on the spiritual path — and the Buddha later confirms in detail that every claim she made about herself was true.

A householder’s couple, long known to the Buddha

Nakula’s father and Nakula’s mother appear elsewhere in the canon as an elderly householder couple with an unusually close, long-standing relationship to the Buddha, who is said to have addressed them once as being like his own parents in a past life. Here that backstory is left implicit; the scene opens simply with the householder “sick, suffering, gravely ill,” and his wife stepping in to speak to him directly — not with vague comfort, but with a structured list of exactly the anxieties a dying man in his position might be turning over in his mind.

Six worries, named and answered

Nakula’s mother opens by naming the underlying problem: “don’t pass away with concerns — such concern is suffering, and it’s criticized by the Buddha.” She then anticipates six specific concerns her husband might hold, states each one in his own possible words (“householder, you might think…”), and answers each in turn:

The anticipated worryHer answer
She won’t be able to provide for the children and householdShe is skilled at spinning cotton and carding wool, and can provide for them herself
She will remarry after his deathBoth of them have remained chaste at home for the past sixteen years
She won’t want to keep visiting the Buddha and the SaṅghaShe will want to see them even more once he is gone
She won’t fulfill ethical conductShe is a lay disciple who fulfills the ethics expected of her, and anyone who doubts it may go and ask the Buddha directly
She lacks inner serenity of heartShe has that serenity, and again, anyone who doubts it may ask the Buddha
She has not found a firm footing in the teaching, gone beyond doubt, or gained independent confidence in itShe has found exactly that footing, gone beyond doubt, and is independent of others in the Teacher’s instructions

The pattern moves deliberately from the practical (household finances, remarriage) to the devotional (continued visits to the Saṅgha) to the specifically spiritual (her own ethical conduct, inner calm, and confirmed confidence in the teaching) — as if addressing, in order, every register in which a dying husband might reasonably worry about the wife he is leaving behind.

An illness that recedes with the mind

The sutta then reports something understated but striking: “as Nakula’s mother was giving this advice to Nakula’s father, his illness died down on the spot,” and he recovered. The text does not dwell on this or generalize it into a theory about mind curing body; it simply records the outcome as fact. Still, the implicit suggestion is hard to miss — a body in the grip of anxious, unresolved concern can be genuinely eased once that concern is directly, honestly addressed and let go of, without any medical intervention at all.

The Buddha’s confirmation

Once recovered, Nakula’s father makes his way to the Buddha, leaning on a staff, and the Buddha responds not by teaching something new but by confirming, point for point, everything his wife had claimed about herself: she does fulfill ethical conduct; she does have internal serenity of heart; she has found a firm footing in the teaching, gone beyond doubt, and stands independent of others in the Teacher’s instructions. He calls the householder “fortunate, so very fortunate” to have such a wife — a rare moment in the canon where the Buddha’s teaching consists almost entirely of validating, in detail, a lay person’s own account of another lay person’s spiritual attainment.

A note on a more famous companion teaching

Readers familiar with early Buddhist texts may know a related, and more widely quoted, teaching to this same householder — recorded separately in the Saṃyutta Nikāya — in which the Buddha and then Sāriputta teach Nakula’s father that “though the body is sick, the mind need not be sick along with it,” unpacking this through the five aggregates and the difference between clinging to them as self and not doing so. That is a distinct discourse from the one on this page. AN 6.16 stands on its own: a scene of lay partnership and the recognizable, practical relief of having one’s deepest household and spiritual fears named and answered by someone who knows you well.

Key terms

gahapati / gahapatānī — householder and housewife; the standard terms for the lay couple at the center of this discourse, Nakula’s father and Nakula’s mother.
odātavasana — “white-robed”: the standard description of a lay follower, as opposed to the ochre or saffron robes of a renunciate; used here of Nakula’s mother throughout.
brahmacariya — the chaste or spiritual life; invoked here in its narrower sense when the couple recall having “remained chaste at home” for sixteen years while still living as householders.
a firm footing, beyond doubt — the reading guide’s gloss for the discourse’s description of confirmed, independent confidence in the teaching — qualities the tradition elsewhere associates with a stream-enterer, someone who no longer needs to rely on another’s word for their basic orientation to the path.
Nakulapitā / Nakulamātā — “Nakula’s father” and “Nakula’s mother”: the couple are known throughout the canon only by this parental naming convention, never by names of their own.

The text

The discourse in full: the scene of Nakula’s father’s illness, his wife’s six-part reassurance, his recovery, and the Buddha’s confirming praise. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A sick householder

§1At one time the Buddha was staying in the land of the Bhaggas at Crocodile’s Bellow, in the deer park at Bhesakaḷā’s Wood. Now at that time the householder Nakula’s father was sick, suffering, gravely ill. Then the housewife Nakula’s mother said to him:

Six worries, named and answered

§2“Householder, don’t pass away with concerns. Such concern is suffering, and it’s criticized by the Buddha. Householder, you might think: ‘When I’ve gone, the housewife Nakula’s mother won’t be able to provide for the children and keep up the household carpets.’ But you should not see it like this. I’m skilled at spinning cotton and carding wool. I’m able to provide for the children and keep up the household carpets. So householder, don’t pass away with concerns…

§3Householder, you might think: ‘When I’ve gone, the housewife Nakula’s mother will take another husband.’ But you should not see it like this. Both you and I know that we have remained chaste while at home for the past sixteen years. So householder, don’t pass away with concerns…

§4Householder, you might think: ‘When I’ve gone, the housewife Nakula’s mother won’t want to see the Buddha and his Saṅgha of mendicants.’ But you should not see it like this. When you’ve gone, I’ll want to see the Buddha and his mendicant Saṅgha even more. So householder, don’t pass away with concerns…

§5Householder, you might think: ‘The housewife Nakula’s mother won’t fulfill ethics.’ But you should not see it like this. I am one of those white-robed disciples of the Buddha who fulfills their ethics. For whoever doubts this, that Blessed One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha is staying in the land of the Bhaggas at Crocodile’s Bellow, in the deer park at Bhesakaḷā’s Wood. Let them approach him and ask. So householder, don’t pass away with concerns…

§6Householder, you might think: ‘The housewife Nakula’s mother doesn’t have internal serenity of heart.’ But you should not see it like this. I am one of those white-robed disciples of the Buddha who has internal serenity of heart. For whoever doubts this, that Blessed One is staying in the land of the Bhaggas at Crocodile’s Bellow, in the deer park at Bhesakaḷā’s Wood. Let them approach him and ask. So householder, don’t pass away with concerns…

§7Householder, you might think: ‘The housewife Nakula’s mother has not found a footing, a firm footing, and solace in this teaching and training. She has not gone beyond doubt, got rid of indecision, and gained assurance. And she’s not independent of others in the Teacher’s instructions.’ But you should not see it like this. I am one of those white-robed disciples of the Buddha who has found a footing, a firm footing, and solace in this teaching and training. I have gone beyond doubt, got rid of indecision, and gained assurance. And I am independent of others in the Teacher’s instructions. For whoever doubts this, that Blessed One is staying in the land of the Bhaggas at Crocodile’s Bellow, in the deer park at Bhesakaḷā’s Wood. Let them approach him and ask. So householder, don’t pass away with concerns. Such concern is suffering, and it’s criticized by the Buddha.”

Recovery, and the Buddha’s praise

§8And then, as Nakula’s mother was giving this advice to Nakula’s father, his illness died down on the spot. And that’s how Nakula’s father recovered from that illness. Soon after recovering, leaning on a staff he went to the Buddha, bowed, and sat down to one side. The Buddha said to him:

§9“You’re fortunate, householder, so very fortunate, to have the housewife Nakula’s mother advise and instruct you out of kindness and sympathy. She is one of those white-robed disciples of the Buddha who fulfills their ethics. She is one of those white-robed disciples of the Buddha who has internal serenity of heart. She is one of those white-robed disciples of the Buddha who has found a footing, a firm footing, and solace in this teaching and training. She has gone beyond doubt, got rid of indecision, and gained assurance. And she is independent of others in the Teacher’s instructions. You’re fortunate, householder, so very fortunate, to have the housewife Nakula’s mother advise and instruct you out of kindness and sympathy.”

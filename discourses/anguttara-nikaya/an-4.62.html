Aṅguttara Nikāya · Discourse 4.62

Debtlessness

Ānaṇyasutta

Setting
Addressed directly to the householder Anāthapiṇḍika, the Buddha’s great lay donor
Speakers
The Buddha, speaking to Anāthapiṇḍika
Form
A fourfold prose analysis of lay happiness, closed with two summary verses
Length
~3 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Ekottarika-āgama (T125), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — clear and direct, though it asks readers to hold two things at once: candid enjoyment of legitimate prosperity, and its firm subordination to ethical soundness

Why this discourse

Early Buddhist texts are sometimes assumed to treat wealth and worldly enjoyment with suspicion at best. This short discourse, addressed to the tradition’s single most important lay patron, complicates that picture directly. The Buddha does not tell Anāthapiṇḍika that his prosperity is a problem or a distraction — he names four distinct kinds of happiness a layperson can legitimately enjoy from wealth, validates all four, and then ranks them, putting ethical soundness of conduct so far above the rest that the other three combined are said not to be worth even a sixteenth of it. It is a rare passage that affirms ordinary economic life on its own terms while still being completely unambiguous about what ultimately matters most.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A layperson who enjoys sensual pleasures through legitimate means can rightly take pleasure in four things — owning wealth honestly earned, using it well, owing no debts, and having blameless conduct — but the first three together are not worth a sixteenth part of the fourth.

Addressed to the great donor

The householder Anāthapiṇḍika needs little introduction within the tradition: he is remembered as the Buddha’s most generous lay patron, the donor of Jeta’s Grove, and a figure the texts return to again and again as a model of lay devotion. That this teaching is addressed to him specifically matters. It is not a warning issued to someone the Buddha suspects of greed, nor an ascetic’s caution to the wealthy in general. It reads instead as an honest accounting, offered to a person already known for both his prosperity and his generosity, of what wealth is actually good for and how far that good extends.

Four kinds of happiness

The Buddha lays out the four plainly, each defined the same way: a state that arises when a person reflects on their situation and, in doing so, “is filled with pleasure and happiness.”

HappinessWhat it is
OwnershipOwning legitimate wealth earned by one’s own effort and initiative, “built up with his own hands, gathered by the sweat of the brow”
Using wealthActually enjoying that wealth, and using it to make merit — through generosity and good deeds
DebtlessnessOwing no debt, large or small, to anyone
BlamelessnessHaving conduct free of blame in body, speech, and mind

Two things are worth noticing about this list before its ranking. First, none of the four is treated as illegitimate or suspect on its own — the discourse validates the ordinary satisfactions of a working life without qualification, so long as the wealth behind them was legitimately earned. Second, the first three are all, in one way or another, about money: having it, spending it well, and not owing it. Only the fourth steps outside the financial frame entirely.

A firm hierarchy

Having validated all four, the Buddha closes with an unambiguous ranking. “The other kind,” the closing verses say — meaning the three financial happinesses combined — “is not worth a sixteenth part of the happiness of blamelessness.” This is not a small qualification; it is a hierarchy stated in the strongest available terms. Financial security, however honestly gained and well used, is real and worth acknowledging — but it is not remotely comparable in weight to the simple fact of conducting oneself, in body, speech, and mind, without fault.

The distinction matters because it draws a sharp line between two kinds of goods that can otherwise get blurred together: goods that depend on circumstance (how much one owns, how one came to own it, whether debts remain) and a good that depends only on conduct, available in principle to a poor person exactly as much as to a wealthy one. Blamelessness, unlike the other three, cannot be taken away by misfortune, theft, or economic downturn — a quietly radical claim to make to the wealthiest lay patron in the community.

A householder-affirming teaching

What makes this discourse distinctive is its tone. Nothing here tells Anāthapiṇḍika to divest, to feel guilty about his prosperity, or to regard his enjoyment of legitimate wealth as an obstacle. The first three happinesses are treated as genuinely worthwhile, part of a full and reasonable lay life. The teaching’s force comes entirely from where it places the emphasis afterward — establishing, gently but unmistakably, that all of this is secondary to whether a person’s conduct itself can withstand scrutiny.

Key terms

Anāthapiṇḍika — the wealthy householder and foremost lay donor to whom this teaching is addressed, remembered as the donor of Jeta’s Grove.
atthisukha — “the happiness of ownership”: pleasure taken in legitimately earned wealth, simply by reflecting that one has it.
bhogasukha — “the happiness of using wealth”: pleasure taken in enjoying that wealth and making merit with it.
ānaṇyasukha — “the happiness of debtlessness”: owing no debt, large or small, to anyone — the discourse’s title term.
anavajjasukha — “the happiness of blamelessness”: blameless conduct in body, speech, and mind — ranked far above the other three.

The text

The discourse in full: the four kinds of happiness, each defined, and the closing verses that rank them. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The setting

§1Then the householder Anāthapiṇḍika went up to the Buddha, bowed, and sat down to one side. The Buddha said to him:

Four kinds of happiness

§2“Householder, these four kinds of happiness can be earned by a layperson who enjoys sensual pleasures, depending on time and occasion. What four? The happiness of ownership, using wealth, debtlessness, and blamelessness.

§3And what is the happiness of ownership? It’s when a gentleman owns legitimate wealth that he has earned by his own efforts and initiative, built up with his own hands, gathered by the sweat of the brow. When he reflects on this, he’s filled with pleasure and happiness. This is called ‘the happiness of ownership’.

§4And what is the happiness of using wealth? It’s when a gentleman uses his legitimate wealth, and makes merit with it. When he reflects on this, he’s filled with pleasure and happiness. This is called ‘the happiness of using wealth’.

§5And what is the happiness of debtlessness? It’s when a gentleman owes no debt, large or small, to anyone. When he reflects on this, he’s filled with pleasure and happiness. This is called ‘the happiness of debtlessness’.

§6And what is the happiness of blamelessness? It’s when a noble disciple has blameless conduct by way of body, speech, and mind. When he reflects on this, he’s filled with pleasure and happiness. This is called ‘the happiness of blamelessness’. These four kinds of happiness can be earned by a layperson who enjoys sensual pleasures, depending on time and occasion.

The verses: a firm ranking

§7–8Knowing the happiness of debtlessness, and the extra happiness of possession, a mortal enjoying the happiness of using wealth, then discerns clearly with wisdom. Discerning clearly, a clever person knows both kinds of happiness: the other kind is not worth a sixteenth part of the happiness of blamelessness.”

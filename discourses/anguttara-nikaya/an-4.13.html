Why this discourse

Some of the most important teachings in the early texts are also the shortest — compact formulas meant to be memorized, recollected, and applied on the spot, rather than unpacked at length in the moment they’re taught. This is one of them. In a few lines, the Buddha sets out the four right efforts, a standard piece of the thirty-seven qualities said throughout the canon to contribute to awakening. What makes the formula worth pausing over is its shape: it insists that ethical and mental cultivation is not simply a matter of avoiding what’s bad, but an active, fourfold discipline that also has to build and sustain what’s good.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Ethical and mental cultivation is fourfold and active — preventing unskillful qualities from arising, abandoning those that have already arisen, generating skillful qualities not yet present, and maintaining and developing to fullness the skillful qualities already there.

Four efforts, one formula

The discourse gives no narrative setting at all — no questioner, no location, no backstory — just the Buddha addressing the mendicants directly with a formula meant to be held in the mind as a single unit. He names four right efforts (sammappadhāna), each introduced with the same repeated language of active engagement: a mendicant “generates enthusiasm, tries, makes an effort, exerts the mind, and strives” toward each of four distinct goals:

The effortIts target
Restraintpreventing unskillful qualities that have not yet arisen from arising at all
Abandoninggiving up unskillful qualities that have already arisen
Developmentgiving rise to skillful qualities that have not yet arisen
Maintenancekeeping skillful qualities that have already arisen from being lost, and bringing them to increase, maturity, and fulfillment

Why four, and why in this order

The fourfold structure is doing real conceptual work, not simply padding out a list to a round number. It divides the moral life along two independent axes at once: unskillful versus skillful, and not-yet-arisen versus already-arisen. That gives every possible situation a matching form of effort — there is no state of mind, wholesome or unwholesome, present or merely possible, that falls outside these four categories. Just as importantly, the formula refuses to treat ethical practice as purely defensive. The first two efforts are about restraint and cleanup — keeping bad qualities from arising, and clearing out the ones already there. But the second two are unmistakably about active construction — not simply staying pure, but positively building up qualities like generosity, patience, or concentration, and then pushing them further, toward full maturity, rather than letting them plateau or quietly erode.

A formula meant to be used, not just admired

Unlike some Aṅguttara discourses, which unfold a single insight in one particular scene, this one is built for repetition and application: the same four right efforts appear, in the same wording, throughout the canon, listed as one of the seven sets that together make up the thirty-seven qualities said to contribute to awakening, alongside the four kinds of mindfulness meditation, the five faculties, the seven factors of awakening, and the noble eightfold path. Its brevity here is the point — a discourse this short is meant to function less as a one-time teaching and more as a checklist to carry into ordinary moments of practice: when a harmful impulse is rising, which of the four applies right now?

The closing verses

The prose formula is followed by four short verses that restate the same idea in more martial, image-laden language, describing the person who strives rightly as having “crushed Māra’s sovereignty” and gone “beyond the peril of birth and death.” Māra, the personification of temptation, distraction, and death throughout the canon, stands here for exactly the unskillful qualities the four efforts are meant to prevent, abandon, or crowd out. The verses end on a strikingly plain, almost understated note for such dramatic imagery: the one who has done this work is simply “contented and unstirred” and “happy” — a reminder that the goal of this active, fourfold discipline is not grim struggle for its own sake but a settled ease on the other side of it.

Key terms

sammappadhāna — “right effort,” the four-part formula that gives this discourse its title; one of the seven sets making up the thirty-seven qualities contributing to awakening.
akusala / kusala — unskillful and skillful qualities of mind, the two poles each of the four efforts is directed toward preventing, abandoning, generating, or maintaining.
the fourfold matrix — unskillful-not-yet-arisen, unskillful-arisen, skillful-not-yet-arisen, and skillful-arisen: the four categories that between them cover every possible state of mind.
Māra — the personification of temptation, distraction, and death in the early texts; in the closing verses, defeating Māra stands for the successful exercise of the four right efforts.
Namuci — an epithet for Māra used in the closing verses, meaning roughly “the one who does not let go,” here said to be vanquished along with his “legions.”

The text

The discourse in full: the four right efforts stated as a formula, followed by the closing verses. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The four right efforts

§1“Mendicants, there are these four right efforts. What four? A mendicant generates enthusiasm, tries, makes an effort, exerts the mind, and strives so that bad, unskillful qualities don’t arise. They generate enthusiasm, try, make an effort, exert the mind, and strive so that bad, unskillful qualities that have arisen are given up. They generate enthusiasm, try, make an effort, exert the mind, and strive so that skillful qualities arise. They generate enthusiasm, try, make an effort, exert the mind, and strive so that skillful qualities that have arisen remain, are not lost, but increase, mature, and are fulfilled by development. These are the four right efforts.

The closing verses

§2By rightly striving, they’ve crushed Māra’s sovereignty; unattached, they’ve gone beyond the peril of birth and death. Contented and unstirred, they’ve vanquished Māra with his legions; now they’ve gone beyond all Namuci’s forces, they’re happy.”

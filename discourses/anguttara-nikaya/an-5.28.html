Aṅguttara Nikāya · Discourse 5.28

With Five Factors

Samādhaṅgasutta

Setting
No location given; the Buddha addresses the mendicants directly
Speakers
The Buddha, teaching a group of mendicants
Form
A five-part analysis of deep meditative absorption, each factor illustrated with a vivid simile, followed by three further similes on its result
Length
~13 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Ekottarika-āgama (T125), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — long and technical, dense with precise meditative vocabulary, though its similes make the content vivid rather than abstract

Why this discourse

Descriptions of deep meditative absorption in the early texts are often compressed into a handful of technical terms — rapture, bliss, equanimity — that can be hard to feel from the outside. This discourse does something unusual: it takes each of those terms and gives it a full-body simile, asking what it would actually feel like for rapture, or bliss, or a settled, luminous mind to spread through every part of a person sitting still. Bath powder kneaded until no part is dry; a lake fed entirely from below, with no external inlet; a lotus growing fully submerged and soaked through with cool water from root to tip; someone wrapped head to foot in white cloth. Few passages in the canon make jhāna feel this physically concrete.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

“Noble right immersion with its vital conditions and its factors” is developed through five things that, one after another, are made to saturate the whole body without exception — the rapture and bliss of the first two absorptions, the bliss of the third, the pure bright mind of the fourth, and finally a distinct fifth factor, a “reviewing sign,” through which the mind turns to examine itself with the same steadiness it has just achieved.

Four factors are the four absorptions

The first four of the five factors will be recognizable to anyone familiar with the standard description of the four jhānas, but this discourse's contribution is to isolate, for each one, exactly what pervades the body and to give that pervasion its own concrete image.

FactorWhat pervades the bodySimile
First (1st jhāna)Rapture and bliss born of seclusion, alongside placing and sustaining the mindA bath attendant kneading bath powder with water until it is soaked through, with no moisture oozing out
Second (2nd jhāna)Rapture and bliss born of immersion, with internal clarity and unification of mindA deep lake fed only by its own spring, with no inlet from any direction, yet filled and pervaded throughout by its own cool water
Third (3rd jhāna)Bliss free of rapture, experienced with equanimity and mindfulnessBlue, pink, or white lotuses growing fully submerged, drenched with cool water from tip to root
Fourth (4th jhāna)A pure, bright mind, beyond pleasure and painSomeone sitting wrapped from head to foot in white cloth, with no part left uncovered

Each simile makes the same basic point in a different register: whatever the meditative factor is, it is not partial or patchy. It is not a pleasant feeling localized in the chest, or a calm confined to the surface of the mind — it is described as reaching every part of the body, the way water reaches every part of a well-soaked ball of powder, or the way cloth wrapped correctly leaves no gap.

The fifth factor: a reviewing sign

The fifth factor breaks the pattern of the first four, and is easy to read past too quickly. Rather than another meditative attainment layered on top of the fourth jhāna, it is described as “the meditation that is a basis for reviewing” — something “properly grasped, focused on, borne in mind, and penetrated with wisdom.” Its own simile is notably different from the first four: not an image of saturation, but of one person observing another from a related but different vantage point — “someone standing might view someone sitting, or someone sitting might view someone lying down.” The suggestion seems to be a kind of internal self-observation, made possible only once the first four factors have settled the mind: having become fully steady and unified, the mind can now turn and examine its own state clearly, the way one person watching another can see them plainly precisely because the observer is not tangled up in what is being observed.

What this concentration is said to make possible

Having laid out the five factors, the discourse pivots to what this fully developed immersion enables: a mendicant can “extend the mind to realize by insight each and every thing that can be realized by insight,” described as being fully “within range.” Three further similes drive the point home, each varying the same image of readiness — a water jar filled right to the brim, ready to spill the instant it is tipped; a walled lotus pond, full to the brim, ready to release its water the instant a wall is opened; a chariot already harnessed to well-trained horses, with the driver's hands already on the reins, ready to go wherever and whenever the driver wishes. None of these images describe effort still to come. They describe a state of complete readiness, where the only thing left is to direct it.

The list of things this readiness makes available is the traditional set of higher attainments: psychic power in its various forms, clairaudience able to hear both human and heavenly sounds, direct knowledge of other people's states of mind (with greed or without, with hate or without, constricted or expansive, and so on through a careful list of paired opposites), recollection of one's own past lives in detail, clairvoyant knowledge of how beings pass away and are reborn according to their deeds, and finally the “undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom” that marks full awakening. The discourse does not claim that developing the five factors guarantees any of these will actually be exercised — only that, once this concentration is established, they are genuinely available, “within range,” should the practitioner wish to realize them.

Why the similes matter

It would be possible to read this discourse purely as a technical inventory — four jhānas plus a reviewing capacity, followed by a standard list of higher knowledges. What keeps the text memorable, and what likely accounts for its continuing citation in meditation instruction, is the insistence on total, gapless pervasion at every stage. The powder with no dry spot, the lake with no unfed corner, the lotus soaked from root to tip, the cloth with no bare patch, the jar full to overflowing: together they describe concentration not as a mental event happening somewhere behind the eyes, but as a state that is meant to be felt, completely and without remainder, through the whole body sitting there.

Key terms

ariya sammāsamādhi — “noble right immersion”: the discourse’s subject, concentration developed together with its supporting conditions and its five factors.
pīti, sukha — rapture and bliss: the pervading qualities of the first two absorptions, distinguished from each other in how they arise (from seclusion, then from immersion).
upekkhā — equanimity: the settled, undistracted quality that characterizes the third and especially the fourth absorption, alongside mindfulness.
paccavekkhaṇā-nimitta — the “reviewing sign,” the discourse’s fifth factor: a capacity for the settled mind to turn and examine its own state, illustrated by one person observing another.
abhiññā — the higher knowledges said to come within range once the five factors are developed: psychic power, clairaudience, mind-reading, past-life recollection, clairvoyance, and the ending of defilements.

The text

The discourse in full: the five factors of noble right immersion, each with its simile, followed by the three similes of readiness and the list of higher knowledges this concentration makes available. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The Buddha announces the teaching

§1“Mendicants, I will teach you how to develop noble right immersion with five factors. Listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

The first factor

§2“And how do you develop noble right immersion with five factors? Firstly, a mendicant, quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unskillful qualities, enters and remains in the first absorption. It has the rapture and bliss born of seclusion, while placing the mind and keeping it connected.

They drench, steep, fill, and spread their body with rapture and bliss born of seclusion. There’s no part of the body that’s not spread with rapture and bliss born of seclusion. It’s like when a deft bathroom attendant or their apprentice pours bath powder into a bronze dish, sprinkling it little by little with water. They knead it until the ball of bath powder is soaked and saturated with moisture, spread through inside and out; yet no moisture oozes out. In the same way, a mendicant drenches, steeps, fills, and spreads their body with rapture and bliss born of seclusion. There’s no part of the body that’s not spread with rapture and bliss born of seclusion. This is the first way to develop noble right immersion with five factors.

The second factor

§3Furthermore, as the placing of the mind and keeping it connected are stilled, a mendicant enters and remains in the second absorption. It has the rapture and bliss born of immersion, with internal clarity and mind at one, without placing the mind and keeping it connected.

They drench, steep, fill, and spread their body with rapture and bliss born of immersion. There’s no part of the body that’s not spread with rapture and bliss born of immersion. It’s like a deep lake fed by spring water. There’s no inlet to the east, west, north, or south, and the heavens would not properly bestow showers from time to time. But the stream of cool water welling up in the lake drenches, steeps, fills, and spreads throughout the lake. There’s no part of the lake that’s not spread through with cool water. In the same way, a mendicant drenches, steeps, fills, and spreads their body with rapture and bliss born of immersion. There’s no part of the body that’s not spread with rapture and bliss born of immersion. This is the second way to develop noble right immersion with five factors.

The third factor

§4Furthermore, with the fading away of rapture, a mendicant enters and remains in the third absorption. They meditate with equanimity, mindful and aware, personally experiencing the bliss of which the noble ones declare, ‘Equanimous and mindful, one meditates in bliss.’

They drench, steep, fill, and spread their body with bliss free of rapture. There’s no part of the body that’s not spread with bliss free of rapture. It’s like a pool with blue water lilies, or pink or white lotuses. Some of them sprout and grow in the water without rising above it, thriving underwater. From the tip to the root they’re drenched, steeped, filled, and soaked with cool water. There’s no part of them that’s not spread through with cool water. In the same way, a mendicant drenches, steeps, fills, and spreads their body with bliss free of rapture. There’s no part of the body that’s not spread with bliss free of rapture. This is the third way to develop noble right immersion with five factors.

The fourth factor

§5Furthermore, with the giving up of pleasure and pain and the disappearance of former happiness and sadness, a mendicant enters and remains in the fourth absorption. It is without pleasure or pain, with pure equanimity and mindfulness.

They sit spreading their body through with pure bright mind. There’s no part of the body that’s not spread with pure bright mind. It’s like someone sitting wrapped from head to foot with white cloth. There’s no part of the body that’s not spread over with white cloth. In the same way, they sit spreading their body through with pure bright mind. There’s no part of the body that’s not spread with pure bright mind. This is the fourth way to develop noble right immersion with five factors.

The fifth factor

§6Furthermore, the meditation that is a basis for reviewing is properly grasped, focused on, borne in mind, and penetrated with wisdom by a mendicant. It’s like when someone views someone else. Someone standing might view someone sitting, or someone sitting might view someone lying down. In the same way, the meditation that is a basis for reviewing is properly grasped, focused on, borne in mind, and penetrated with wisdom by a mendicant. This is the fifth way to develop noble right immersion with five factors.

What this readiness makes possible

§7–10When the noble right immersion with five factors is developed and cultivated in this way, a mendicant extends the mind to realize by insight each and every thing that can be realized by insight; and they are capable of realizing those things, since each and every one is within range.

Suppose a water jar was placed on a stand, full to the brim so a crow could drink from it. If a strong man was to tip it any which way, would water pour out?” “Yes, sir.” “In the same way, when noble right immersion with five factors is developed and cultivated in this way, a mendicant extends the mind to realize by insight each and every thing that can be realized by insight; and they are capable of realizing those things, since each and every one is within range.

Suppose there was a square, walled lotus pond on level ground, full to the brim so a crow could drink from it. If a strong man was to open the wall on any side, would water pour out?” “Yes, sir.” “In the same way, when noble right immersion with five factors is developed and cultivated in this way, a mendicant extends the mind to realize by insight each and every thing that can be realized by insight; and they are capable of realizing those things, since each and every one is within range.

Suppose a chariot stood harnessed to thoroughbreds at a level crossroads, with a goad ready. A deft horse trainer, a master charioteer, might mount the chariot, taking the reins in his right hand and goad in the left. He’d drive out and back wherever he wishes, whenever he wishes. In the same way, when noble right immersion with five factors is developed and cultivated in this way, a mendicant extends the mind to realize by insight each and every thing that can be realized by insight; and they are capable of realizing those things, since each and every one is within range.

The higher knowledges within range

§11–16If you wish: ‘May I wield the many kinds of psychic power: multiplying myself and becoming one again … controlling the body as far as the realm of divinity.’ You’re capable of realizing it, since each and every one is within range.

If you wish: ‘With clairaudience that is purified and superhuman, may I hear both kinds of sounds, human and heavenly, whether near or far.’ You’re capable of realizing it, since each and every one is within range.

If you wish: ‘May I understand the minds of other beings and individuals, having encompassed them with my mind. May I understand mind with greed as “mind with greed”, and mind without greed as “mind without greed”; mind with hate as “mind with hate”, and mind without hate as “mind without hate”; mind with delusion as “mind with delusion”, and mind without delusion as “mind without delusion”; constricted mind as “constricted mind”, and scattered mind as “scattered mind”; expansive mind as “expansive mind”, and unexpansive mind as “unexpansive mind”; mind that is not supreme as “mind that is not supreme”, and mind that is supreme as “mind that is supreme”; mind immersed in samādhi as “mind immersed in samādhi”, and mind not immersed in samādhi as “mind not immersed in samādhi”; freed mind as “freed mind”, and unfreed mind as “unfreed mind”.’ You’re capable of realizing it, since each and every one is within range.

If you wish: ‘May I recollect many kinds of past lives, with features and details.’ You’re capable of realizing it, since each and every one is within range.

If you wish: ‘With clairvoyance that is purified and superhuman, may I see sentient beings passing away and being reborn according to their deeds.’ You’re capable of realizing it, since each and every one is within range.

If you wish: ‘May I realize the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom in this very life, and live having realized it with my own insight due to the ending of defilements.’ You’re capable of realizing it, since each and every one is within range.”

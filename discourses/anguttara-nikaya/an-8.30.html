Aṅguttara Nikāya · Discourse 8.30

Anuruddha and the Great Thoughts

Anuruddhamahāvitakkasutta

Setting
The land of the Bhaggas (Bhesakaḷā’s Wood) and, in the same episode, the land of the Cetīs (Park of the East-Raftered Hall), joined by the Buddha’s psychic power
Speakers
The Buddha and the elder monk Anuruddha; the Buddha then addresses the wider community of mendicants
Form
A solitary meditator’s reflection, confirmed and completed in person by the Buddha, then restated and explained clause by clause
Length
~12 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Ekottarika-āgama (T125), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the list itself is simple to memorize, but the closing analysis of “non-proliferation” repays careful attention

Why this discourse

This is the source of one of the most quoted checklists in the early texts — the “eight thoughts of a great person” — and it earns that status by showing exactly how such a list is supposed to arise: not handed down as doctrine to be memorized, but worked out, alone, by a serious meditator testing his own practice against experience. Anuruddha gets seven of the eight thoughts right entirely on his own. The discourse is built around the one he is missing, and around why that particular gap matters more than it might first appear.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Meditating alone, the elder monk Anuruddha arrives at seven qualities that support the training — wanting little, contentment, seclusion, energy, mindfulness, immersion, and wisdom — and the Buddha, appearing before him in person, confirms all seven and adds an eighth he had missed: a settled preference for the ending of proliferation over the mind’s habit of spinning elaborations around experience.

A monk thinking alone in the forest

Anuruddha was one of the Buddha’s close relatives who went forth into homelessness, and by the time of this discourse he is already an accomplished, independent meditator — living apart from the Buddha, in a different region entirely, working things out for himself in private retreat. That detail matters: what follows isn’t a teacher instructing a beginner, but the Buddha checking, confirming, and completing the fruit of somebody else’s own reflection. Anuruddha’s seven thoughts amount to a diagnostic self-portrait of the kind of practitioner the training is actually meant for: someone who wants little, who is content with what is at hand, who prefers seclusion to company, who keeps up energetic effort, who stays mindful, whose mind is composed in immersion, and who is genuinely wise.

The Buddha arrives, and adds an eighth

The Buddha does not simply happen to visit; the text says he directly knows Anuruddha’s train of thought and travels to him “as easily as a strong person would extend or contract their arm” — a stock formula elsewhere used for feats of psychic power, marking the meeting as significant rather than incidental. He praises the seven thoughts by name, then supplies the one Anuruddha could not complete on his own: “This teaching is for those who don’t enjoy proliferating and don’t like to proliferate, not for those who enjoy proliferating and like to proliferate.” Proliferation (papañca) names the mind’s tendency to elaborate raw experience into commentary, comparison, and conceptual spin — the running inner narration that turns a bare sense impression into “me,” “mine,” and endless further thought. It is a subtle addition precisely because the other seven qualities can all, in principle, be practiced by a mind that is still quietly proliferating around its own progress — congratulating itself on being content, narrating its own seclusion, elaborating a self-image out of its own mindfulness. The eighth thought closes that loophole.

Why the eight thoughts lead somewhere

Having stated all eight, the Buddha explains what they make possible: someone established in them gains the four absorptions (jhāna) “when you want, without trouble or difficulty.” The four are given in their standard, formulaic description — rapture and bliss born of seclusion in the first; internal clarity as thought and evaluation drop away in the second; equanimous, mindful bliss in the third; and, in the fourth, the letting go of both pleasure and pain into pure equanimity and mindfulness. The eight thoughts, in other words, are not merely ethical advice; they describe the psychological ground out of which stable meditative absorption grows with comparatively little strain.

A wardrobe compared to a rag robe

The Buddha then turns to the ascetic’s bare requisites — the coarse rag-robe, scraps of almsfood, a spot at the root of a tree, a mat of grass, and fermented urine as medicine — and matches each, in turn, to its luxurious lay counterpart: a wardrobe of fine colored cloth, boiled rice served with rich sauces, a sealed and plastered bungalow, a canopied couch with cushions, and proper medicines of ghee and honey. The comparison isn’t a claim that the monk’s robe literally becomes soft or the almsfood literally becomes rich. It is a claim about perception: once someone is genuinely established in the eight thoughts, ordinary requisites stop registering as deprivation and start functioning “for enjoyment, relief, and comfort” — contentment changes what counts as enough.

Returning to explain, clause by clause

The Buddha then travels back to his original location and repeats the entire list to the assembled mendicants, this time subjecting each of the eight thoughts to the same recurring analytical device used throughout the early texts: state the line, ask “why did I say it?”, then answer. The explanation of the first thought is notably compressed and covers all eight at once — a mendicant with few wishes, he says, doesn’t wish to be recognized for having few wishes, and the same holds for each of the other seven qualities in turn. The point generalizes: genuine practice of any of these eight is not compatible with wanting credit for practicing it. The remaining explanations are more specific — contentment is defined concretely as accepting whatever robes, food, lodging, or medicine happen to be available; seclusion is shown to require actively sending visitors away, even sympathetic and well-meaning ones, rather than simply avoiding company by circumstance; energy, mindfulness, and wisdom are each given brief working definitions; and the eighth, non-proliferation, is defined as a mind that “leaps forth, gains confidence, settles down, and becomes decided” specifically regarding the cessation of proliferation — not merely the absence of distracting thought, but a settled orientation toward its ending.

Anuruddha’s own conclusion

The discourse closes by reporting that Anuruddha, following this instruction, stayed on for the rains retreat exactly where he was and, living alone and diligent, soon attained full awakening. His own closing verses frame the encounter from the inside: the Teacher, aware of his thoughts, came to him “in a mind-made body” and taught him more than he had thought of himself — a teacher, the verse notes pointedly, “who loves non-proliferation” teaching non-proliferation. The discourse is, among other things, a small, self-contained model of how far diligent solitary practice can go on its own, and of the specific kind of help a teacher can still supply once it does.

Key terms

mahāpurisavitakka — “thoughts of a great person”: the technical name, used within the sutta itself, for this set of eight qualities.
papañca — “proliferation”: the mind’s tendency to spin raw experience out into elaboration, self-referential commentary, and conceptual complication; its cessation is the subject of the eighth thought.
Anuruddha — a monk from the Buddha’s own extended clan, later declared foremost among the Buddha’s disciples in the “divine eye” (clairvoyant vision); here still working toward full awakening.
“as easily as a strong person…” — a stock formula elsewhere describing feats of psychic power (iddhi), used here for the Buddha’s instantaneous travel between the two locations.
paṃsukūla — the “rag-robe” pieced together from discarded cloth, the traditional mark of the most austere standard of monastic dress; compared here to a householder’s wardrobe of fine colored garments.

The text

The discourse in full: Anuruddha’s private reflection, the Buddha’s visit and completion of the list, the four absorptions and five contentment similes, the Buddha’s return to explain each of the eight thoughts to the assembly, and Anuruddha’s own verses on attaining awakening. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Anuruddha’s reflection

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the land of the Bhaggas at Crocodile’s Bellow, in the deer park at Bhesakaḷā’s Wood. And at that time Venerable Anuruddha was staying in the land of the Cetīs in the Park of the East-Raftered Hall. Then as Anuruddha was in private retreat this thought came to his mind: “This teaching is for those of few wishes, not those of many wishes. It’s for the contented, not those who lack contentment. It’s for the secluded, not those who enjoy company. It’s for the energetic, not the lazy. It’s for the mindful, not the unmindful. It’s for those with immersion, not those without immersion. It’s for the wise, not the witless.”

The Buddha appears

§2Then the Buddha knew Anuruddha’s train of thought. As easily as a strong person would extend or contract their arm, he vanished from the deer park at Bhesakaḷā’s Wood in the land of the Bhaggas and reappeared in front of Anuruddha in the Park of the East-Raftered Hall in the land of the Cetīs, and sat on the seat spread out. Anuruddha bowed to the Buddha and sat down to one side. The Buddha said to him:

The eighth thought

§3“Good, good, Anuruddha! It’s good that you reflect on these thoughts of a great man: ‘This teaching is for those of few wishes, not those of many wishes. It’s for the contented, not those who lack contentment. It’s for the secluded, not those who enjoy company. It’s for the energetic, not the lazy. It’s for the mindful, not the unmindful. It’s for those with immersion, not those without immersion. It’s for the wise, not the witless.’ Well then, Anuruddha, you should also reflect on the following eighth thought of a great man: ‘This teaching is for those who don’t enjoy proliferating and don’t like to proliferate, not for those who enjoy proliferating and like to proliferate.’

The four absorptions made easy

§4–7First you’ll reflect on these eight thoughts of a great man. Then as far as you want, quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unskillful qualities, you’ll enter and remain in the first absorption, which has the rapture and bliss born of seclusion, while placing the mind and keeping it connected.

You’ll enter and remain in the second absorption, which has the rapture and bliss born of immersion, with internal clarity and mind at one, without placing the mind and keeping it connected.

You’ll enter and remain in the third absorption, where you’ll meditate with equanimity, mindful and aware, personally experiencing the bliss of which the noble ones declare, ‘Equanimous and mindful, one meditates in bliss.’

With the giving up of pleasure and pain and the disappearance of former happiness and sadness, you’ll enter and remain in the fourth absorption, without pleasure or pain, with pure equanimity and mindfulness.

Contentment with little

§8–12First you’ll reflect on these eight thoughts of a great man, and you’ll get the four absorptions—blissful meditations in this life that belong to the higher mind—when you want, without trouble or difficulty. Then as you live contented, your rag robe will seem to you like a chest full of garments of different colors seems to a householder or householder’s child. It will be for your enjoyment, relief, and comfort, and for alighting upon extinguishment.

As you live contented, your scraps of almsfood will seem to you like boiled fine rice with the dark grains picked out, served with many soups and sauces seems to a householder or householder’s child. It will be for your enjoyment, relief, and comfort, and for alighting upon extinguishment.

As you live contented, your lodging at the root of a tree will seem to you like a bungalow, plastered inside and out, draft-free, with door fastened and window shuttered seems to a householder or householder’s child. It will be for your enjoyment, relief, and comfort, and for alighting upon extinguishment.

As you live contented, your grass mat for lodging will seem to you like a couch spread with woolen covers—shag-piled, pure white, or embroidered with flowers—and spread with a fine deer hide, with a canopy above and red pillows at both ends seems to a householder or householder’s child. It will be for your enjoyment, relief, and comfort, and for alighting upon extinguishment.

As you live contented your rancid urine as medicine will seem to you like various medicines—ghee, butter, oil, honey, and molasses—seem to a householder or householder’s child. It will be for your enjoyment, relief, and comfort, and for alighting upon extinguishment. Well then, Anuruddha, for the next rainy season residence you should stay right here in the land of the Cetīs in the Park of the East-Raftered Hall.” “Yes, sir,” Anuruddha replied.

The Buddha returns and addresses the mendicants

§13After advising Anuruddha like this, the Buddha—as easily as a strong person would extend or contract their arm, vanished from the Park of the East-Raftered Hall in the land of the Cetīs and reappeared in the deer park at Bhesakaḷā’s Wood in the land of the Bhaggas at Crocodile’s Bellow. He sat on the seat spread out and addressed the mendicants: “Mendicants, I will teach you the eight thoughts of a great man. Listen … And what are the eight thoughts of a great man? This teaching is for those of few wishes, not those of many wishes. It’s for the contented, not those who lack contentment. It’s for the secluded, not those who enjoy company. It’s for the energetic, not the lazy. It’s for the mindful, not the unmindful. It’s for those with immersion, not those without immersion. It’s for the wise, not the witless. It’s for those who don’t enjoy proliferating and don’t like to proliferate, not for those who enjoy proliferating and like to proliferate.

Why the Buddha said each of these

§14‘This teaching is for those of few wishes, not those of many wishes.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? A mendicant with few wishes doesn’t wish: ‘May they know me as having few wishes!’ When contented, they don’t wish: ‘May they know me as contented!’ When secluded, they don’t wish: ‘May they know me as secluded!’ When energetic, they don’t wish: ‘May they know me as energetic!’ When mindful, they don’t wish: ‘May they know me as mindful!’ When immersed, they don’t wish: ‘May they know me as immersed!’ When wise, they don’t wish: ‘May they know me as wise!’ When not enjoying proliferation, they don’t wish: ‘May they know me as one who doesn’t enjoy proliferating!’ ‘This teaching is for those of few wishes, not those of many wishes.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

§15‘This teaching is for the contented, not those who lack contentment.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? It’s for a mendicant who’s content with any kind of robes, almsfood, lodgings, and medicines and supplies for the sick. ‘This teaching is for the contented, not those who lack contentment.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

§16‘This teaching is for the secluded, not those who enjoy company.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? It’s for a mendicant who lives secluded. But monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen, rulers and their chief ministers, monastics of other religions and their disciples go to visit them. With a mind slanting, sloping, and inclining to seclusion, withdrawn, and loving renunciation, that mendicant invariably gives a talk concerned only with sending them away. ‘This teaching is for the secluded, not those who enjoy company.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

§17‘This teaching is for the energetic, not the lazy.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? It’s for a mendicant who lives with energy roused up for giving up unskillful qualities and embracing skillful qualities. They’re strong, staunchly vigorous, not slacking off when it comes to developing skillful qualities. ‘This teaching is for the energetic, not the lazy.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

§18‘This teaching is for the mindful, not the unmindful.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? It’s for a mendicant who’s mindful. They have utmost mindfulness and alertness, and can remember and recall what was said and done long ago. ‘This teaching is for the mindful, not the unmindful.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

§19‘This teaching is for those with immersion, not those without immersion.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? It’s for a mendicant who, quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unskillful qualities, enters and remains in the first absorption … second absorption … third absorption … fourth absorption. ‘This teaching is for those with immersion, not those without immersion.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

§20‘This teaching is for the wise, not the witless.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? It’s for a mendicant who’s wise. They have the wisdom of arising and passing away which is noble, penetrative, and leads to the complete ending of suffering. ‘This teaching is for the wise, not the witless.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

§21‘This teaching is for those who don’t enjoy proliferating and don’t like to proliferate, not for those who enjoy proliferating and like to proliferate.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? It’s for a mendicant whose mind leaps forth, gains confidence, settles down, and becomes decided regarding the cessation of proliferation. ‘This teaching is for those who don’t enjoy proliferating and don’t like to proliferate, not for those who enjoy proliferating and like to proliferate.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.”

Anuruddha’s attainment

§22Then Anuruddha stayed the next rainy season residence right there in the land of the Cetīs in the Park of the East-Raftered Hall. And Anuruddha, living alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute, soon realized the supreme culmination of the spiritual path in this very life. He lived having achieved with his own insight the goal for which gentlemen rightly go forth from the lay life to homelessness. He understood: “Rebirth is ended; the spiritual journey has been completed; what had to be done has been done; there is nothing further for this place.” And Venerable Anuruddha became one of the perfected. And on the occasion of attaining perfection he recited these verses:

Anuruddha’s verses

§23–25“Knowing my thoughts, the supreme Teacher in the world came to me in a mind-made body, using his psychic power. He taught me more than I had thought of. The Buddha who loves non-proliferation taught me non-proliferation. Understanding that teaching, I happily did his bidding. I’ve attained the three knowledges, and have fulfilled the Buddha’s instructions.”

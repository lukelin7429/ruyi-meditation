Aṅguttara Nikāya · Discourse 3.100

A Lump of Salt

Loṇaphalasutta

Setting
No location given; addressed directly to the assembled mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha alone
Form
A rejected thesis stated up front, then defended through the simile of salt in water and two further paired similes
Length
~5 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Ekottarika-āgama (T125), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a short, vivid simile with a clear practical payoff

Why this discourse

If kamma worked like a fixed exchange rate — this deed, exactly this result, no matter who commits it — then character would be beside the point, and anyone’s single youthful mistake would permanently condemn them regardless of everything they did afterward. This short discourse rejects exactly that picture. Using one of the most memorable similes in the early texts — a pinch of salt dropped into a cup versus dropped into a river — the Buddha argues that the very same deed can land with completely different weight depending on the character of the person who did it, which is also the discourse’s quiet argument for why spiritual practice and character development are worth undertaking at all.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A person's developed character — not the size of the deed alone — determines whether a given bad deed is experienced as a crushing result or as something barely felt at all, just as the same pinch of salt makes a small cup of water undrinkable but leaves the Ganges essentially unchanged.

The view rejected, stated as a dilemma

The Buddha opens with two contrasting hypotheticals and immediately shows why only one of them makes the spiritual life possible at all. Suppose, he says, that “no matter how this person performs a deed, they experience it the same way” — that is, suppose a deed's result is always fixed in exact, unmodifiable proportion to the deed itself, regardless of who performed it. If that were true, he says bluntly, “the spiritual life could not be lived, and there'd be no chance of making a complete end of suffering” — because no amount of ethical or meditative development could ever change how a person experiences the consequences of what they've done. But suppose instead that a person experiences a deed's result “as it should be experienced” — meaning the felt weight of a result depends on more than the deed alone. In that case, the spiritual life can be lived, and full liberation from suffering remains possible. The Buddha states plainly which of these two pictures is correct, then spends the rest of the discourse showing why.

Two people, the same trivial bad deed

He sets up a direct comparison: one individual commits a trivial bad deed and it lands them in hell; another commits the very same trivial bad deed and experiences its result fully in this very life, so completely that “not even a little remains, let alone a lot.” The difference between them is not the deed — it is the person. The first is someone who “hasn't developed their physical endurance, ethics, mind, or wisdom,” who is “small-minded and mean-spirited, living in suffering” — someone, in short, whose character gives a minor bad deed room to dominate their whole trajectory. The second has developed exactly those same four qualities, is not small-minded but big-hearted, and specifically meditates on the immeasurables — the four qualities of unlimited love, compassion, appreciative joy, and equanimity described elsewhere as the boundless heart. For this second person, the very same deed simply doesn't have the same room to operate.

The simile: salt in a cup, salt in a river

The central image follows directly: drop a lump of salt into a small mug of water, and the whole mug turns salty and undrinkable, because there is so little water to dilute it. Drop that same lump of salt into the Ganges river, and the river's taste is essentially unaffected, because the river is such a vast mass of water. The deed — the lump of salt — is identical in both cases. What differs entirely is the capacity of what receives it. A person of poor character and an undeveloped, “limited” mind is like the mug: a small deed floods the whole vessel. A person of well-developed character and a “measureless” mind, cultivated through sustained practice, is like the river: the same deed is absorbed into something far larger than itself and barely registers.

Two more similes, same underlying point

The Buddha then adds two social illustrations that make the same point from a different angle, this time about how consequences are enforced rather than merely felt. A poor person, thrown in jail for stealing even a modest sum, faces the full weight of that debt; a wealthy person who owes exactly the same amount typically isn't thrown in jail at all — their resources absorb the situation differently. Likewise, a sheep dealer or butcher can execute, jail, fine, or otherwise punish an ordinary thief who steals from them, but not a powerful ruler or minister who does the very same thing; against such a person, all they can do is politely ask for their sheep back or its price. In both cases the deed is identical; what changes the outcome is the standing, resources, and capacity of the person who did it — exactly the point the salt simile makes about inner character rather than social status.

What this does and doesn't overturn

This discourse directly complicates any reading of kamma as a fixed, forensic ledger in which a specific deed always yields one specific, unmodifiable result no matter who commits it. It does not claim that bad deeds carry no consequence, or that a well-developed person can act however they like without any effect — the deed still has its result; what changes is the felt weight of that result against the backdrop of an entire character. Read alongside the strict determinist view rejected in AN 3.61, and alongside the practical, this-life-verifiable ethics of AN 3.65, this sutta supplies the psychological mechanism that makes those other two discourses cohere: character genuinely can be developed, and developing it genuinely changes how one's own past and present actions are experienced — not by erasing kamma, but by enlarging the vessel that has to hold it.

Key terms

loṇaphala — “lump of salt,” the discourse’s central image: identical in both the mug and the river, yet producing entirely different effects.
appamāṇacetaso — “measureless in mind,” the description of the well-developed person, likened to the vast Ganges rather than a small mug of water.
the four immeasurables — love, compassion, appreciative joy, and equanimity; the practice specifically credited with developing the “big-hearted,” measureless mind described here.
kāyabhāvanā, sīla, citta, paññā — physical endurance/development of the body, ethical conduct, the mind, and wisdom: the four areas of development whose presence or absence marks the difference between the two individuals in the sutta.
the dilemma at the opening — if deeds always yielded identical, unmodifiable results regardless of the person, the spiritual life could not be lived at all; this sutta exists to explain why that isn’t the case.

The text

The discourse in full: the opening dilemma, the two contrasting individuals, the salt-in-water simile, and the two further similes of debt and punishment. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The dilemma stated

§1“Mendicants, suppose you say: ‘No matter how this person performs a deed, they experience it the same way.’ This being so, the spiritual life could not be lived, and there’d be no chance of making a complete end of suffering. Suppose you say: ‘No matter how this person performs a deed, they experience the result as it should be experienced.’ This being so, the spiritual life can be lived, and there is a chance of making a complete end of suffering. Take the case of the individual who does a trivial bad deed, but it lands them in hell. Meanwhile, another individual does the same trivial bad deed, but experiences it in this life, so that not even a little remains, let alone a lot.

Two kinds of person

§2What kind of individual does a trivial bad deed, but it lands them in hell? An individual who hasn’t developed their physical endurance, ethics, mind, or wisdom. They’re small-minded and mean-spirited, living in suffering. That kind of individual does a trivial bad deed, but it lands them in hell.

§3What kind of individual does the same trivial bad deed, but experiences it in this life, so that not even a little remains, let alone a lot? An individual who has developed their physical endurance, ethics, mind, and wisdom. They’re not small-minded, but are big-hearted, meditating on the immeasurables. That kind of individual does the same trivial bad deed, but experiences it in this life, so that not even a little remains, let alone a lot.

The simile of the salt

§4Suppose a person was to drop a lump of salt into a mug of water. What do you think, mendicants? Would that mug of water become salty and undrinkable?” “Yes, sir. Why is that? Because there is only a little water in the bowl.” “Suppose a person was to drop a lump of salt into the Ganges river. What do you think, mendicants? Would the Ganges river become salty and undrinkable?” “No, sir. Why is that? Because the Ganges river is a vast mass of water.”

§5“This is how it is in the case of an individual who does a trivial bad deed, but it lands them in hell. Meanwhile, another individual does the same trivial bad deed, but experiences it in this life, so that not even a little remains, let alone a lot.…

The simile of the debtor

§8–10Take the case of someone who is thrown in jail for stealing half a dollar, a dollar, or a hundred dollars. While someone else isn’t thrown in jail for stealing half a dollar, a dollar, or a hundred dollars. What sort of person is thrown in jail for stealing half a dollar, a dollar, or a hundred dollars? Someone who is poor, with few possessions and little wealth. That kind of person is thrown in jail for stealing half a dollar, a dollar, or a hundred dollars. What sort of person isn’t thrown in jail for stealing half a dollar, a dollar, or a hundred dollars? Someone who is rich, affluent, and wealthy. That kind of person isn’t thrown in jail for stealing half a dollar, a dollar, or a hundred dollars. This is how it is in the case of an individual who does a trivial bad deed, but they go to hell. Meanwhile, another individual does the same trivial bad deed, but experiences it in this life, so that not even a little remains, let alone a lot.…

The simile of the sheep dealer

§13–15It’s like a sheep dealer or butcher. They can execute, jail, fine, or otherwise punish one person who steals from them, but not another. What sort of person can they punish? Someone who is poor, with few possessions and little wealth. That’s the kind of person they can punish. What sort of person can’t they punish? A ruler or their chief minister who is rich, affluent, and wealthy. That’s the kind of person they can’t punish. In fact, all they can do is raise their cupped palms and ask: ‘Please, good fellow, give me my sheep or pay me for it.’ This is how it is in the case of an individual who does a trivial bad deed, but it lands them in hell. Meanwhile, another individual does the same trivial bad deed, but experiences it in this life, so that not even a little remains, let alone a lot.…

The dilemma restated

§18Mendicants, suppose you say: ‘No matter how this person performs a deed, they experience it the same way.’ This being so, the spiritual life could not be lived, and there’d be no chance of making a complete end of suffering. Suppose you say: ‘No matter how this person performs a deed, they experience the result as it should be experienced.’ This being so, the spiritual life can be lived, and there is a chance of making a complete end of suffering.”

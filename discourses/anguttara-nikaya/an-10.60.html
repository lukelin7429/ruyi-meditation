Aṅguttara Nikāya · Discourse 10.60

With Girimānanda

Girimānandasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speakers
The Buddha and Venerable Ānanda; Venerable Girimānanda, gravely ill, receives the teaching secondhand
Form
A request for a hospital visit, answered instead with ten contemplative “perceptions” meant to be learned and recited aloud
Length
~15 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Ekottarika-āgama (T125), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — each perception is individually simple, but the full list of bodily afflictions and the sixteen-stage breathing section reward patience with detail

Why this discourse

A monk is dying, or fears he might be, and the Buddha does not go to him. Instead he teaches Ānanda ten short reflections and sends him to recite them at the bedside — on the premise that hearing them, understood and turned over in the mind, might itself relieve the illness. It is one of the few places in the canon where a teaching is applied less like an explanation and more like a medicine, and it is still used exactly that way: chanted today in some Theravada communities as a protective recitation for the seriously ill. Whatever one makes of the mechanism, the content is a remarkably complete miniature course in contemplative practice — impermanence, non-self, the body, its afflictions, letting go, dispassion, cessation, world-weariness, and the breath — compressed into a single bedside teaching.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Rather than visit the gravely ill Venerable Girimānanda in person, the Buddha teaches Ānanda ten “perceptions” — compact contemplative themes covering the body, the mind, and the nature of conditioned existence — to recite to him, and upon hearing them recited, Girimānanda’s illness subsides.

A visit that doesn’t happen

Ānanda brings the Buddha a simple, urgent request: a fellow monk is sick, suffering, gravely ill — please go to him out of sympathy. It is the kind of request that expects a straightforward answer, a visit. Instead the Buddha redirects entirely: if Ānanda recites ten specific perceptions to Girimānanda, his illness may die down on the spot. The Buddha does not go himself; he transmits a teaching through an intermediary, on the stated premise that the content itself, properly heard, can have a therapeutic effect. This is unusual enough that it’s worth pausing on: nothing here suggests magical intervention or a blessing conferred by the Buddha’s presence. What matters is Girimānanda hearing and reflecting on these ten specific contemplations.

Ten perceptions, one at a time

Each perception follows the same question-and-answer shape — “And what is the perception of …?” — and each names a concrete object of reflection:

PerceptionWhat it contemplates
Impermanencethe five aggregates — form, feeling, perception, choices, consciousness — are impermanent
Non-selfthe six sense fields and their objects, inside and out, are not-self
Uglinessa detailed, unsentimental anatomical survey of the body’s parts, skin to marrow
Drawbacksthe body as beset by a long, explicit catalog of diseases and afflictions
Giving upnot tolerating sensual, malicious, or cruel thoughts as they arise — eliminating them outright
Fading away (dispassion)the peace found in the stilling of all conditioned activity and the ending of craving
Cessationthe same stilling and ending of craving, framed as cessation itself
Dissatisfaction with the whole worldgiving up any grasping, fixation, or underlying tendency toward any state in the world
Impermanence of all conditionsa felt sense of horror, repulsion, and disgust toward all conditioned things
Mindfulness of breathingthe sixteen-stage ānāpānasati formula, given here in full

The body, unsentimentally observed

Two of the ten — ugliness and drawbacks — ask the meditator to look squarely at the body without flinching. The perception of ugliness lists its parts in the traditional order: hair, nails, teeth, skin, flesh, sinews, bones, organs, fluids, all the way down — not as morbid dwelling but as a corrective to the mind’s habitual, unexamined attraction to the body’s surface. The perception of drawbacks goes further, cataloging disease after disease the body is prone to, from the mundane (cough, fever, itch) to the serious (leprosy, epilepsy, tuberculosis), plus afflictions from weather, neglect, overexertion, and the residue of past deeds. Read outside its context this could sound bleak; read as medicine offered to someone gravely ill, it reframes sickness itself as expected, ordinary, and workable — not a personal catastrophe but the body doing what bodies do.

From giving up to cessation

The middle perceptions turn from the body to the mind’s activity. Giving up asks the meditator to catch a sensual, malicious, or cruel thought the moment it arises and simply refuse to entertain it — abandoning it outright rather than negotiating with it. Fading away and cessation both point toward the same experience of peace, described in near-identical language, “the stilling of all activities, the letting go of all attachments, the ending of craving” — the first naming that peace as dispassion, the second as cessation, two angles on one destination. Dissatisfaction with the whole world extends this outward: not grasping at any state, anywhere, as something to hold onto. Together these four perceptions move the mind from active resistance (giving up) to settled non-attachment (fading away, cessation, world-weariness).

Breath as medicine

The tenth perception, mindfulness of breathing, is by far the longest, and it is given in its complete, canonical sixteen-step form — the same formula found in its own dedicated discourse elsewhere in the canon (MN 118). The sixteen steps fall into four natural groups of four: attending to the breath’s length and its effect on the body; attending to rapture, bliss, and the mental process it stirs; attending to the mind itself, gladdening it, steadying it, freeing it; and finally observing impermanence, fading away, cessation, and letting go directly within the breath. Placed last in this list of ten, after all the others, it reads almost as a culmination — a single, sustained practice that can carry forward everything the earlier nine perceptions have pointed toward.

A teaching as a healing rite

The sutta ends almost abruptly: Ānanda learns the ten perceptions, recites them to Girimānanda, and “that’s how he recovered from that illness.” No further explanation is offered for the mechanism, and the text does not dwell on it. What it does establish, clearly, is a precedent still very much alive in Theravada practice: this sutta (known in Pali liturgical use simply as the Girimānanda Sutta) continues to be chanted today, particularly in Sri Lanka, as a protective recitation for people who are seriously unwell — not because the words are believed to work as incantation independent of meaning, but because reflecting on impermanence, the body, and letting go is itself understood to loosen the mind’s grip on fear, aversion, and clinging, which can be as much a part of serious illness as the illness itself.

Key terms

saññā — “perception”: not a passive sensory registering but an active, trainable way of seeing an object, which is why all ten items here are called “perceptions” rather than doctrines or facts to memorize.
asubha — “ugliness” or unattractiveness, the third perception’s anatomical survey of the body’s parts, used across the canon as a counterweight to habitual bodily attachment.
ānāpānasati — “mindfulness of breathing,” the tenth and longest perception, given here in its full sixteen-stage form across four tetrads of body, feeling, mind, and dhamma.
virāga — “fading away” or dispassion, the sixth perception, described in language nearly identical to the seventh perception, cessation (nirodha).
paritta — “protection”: the broader genre of protective recitation this sutta belongs to in later Theravada practice, chanted for the sick and afflicted.

The text

The discourse in full, including all ten perceptions given complete — the ugliness and drawbacks lists in full, and the sixteen-stage breathing formula in its entirety, since compressing any of these would misrepresent what Ānanda actually recited. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A monk gravely ill

§1–3At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Now at that time Venerable Girimānanda was sick, suffering, gravely ill. Then Venerable Ānanda went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and said to him: “Sir, Venerable Girimānanda is sick, suffering, gravely ill. Sir, please go to Venerable Girimānanda out of sympathy.” “Ānanda, if you were to recite to the mendicant Girimānanda these ten perceptions, it’s possible that after hearing them his illness will die down on the spot. What ten? The perceptions of impermanence, not-self, ugliness, drawbacks, giving up, fading away, cessation, dissatisfaction with the whole world, impermanence of all conditions, and mindfulness of breathing.

Impermanence and not-self

§4And what is the perception of impermanence? It’s when a mendicant has gone to a wilderness, or to the root of a tree, or to an empty hut, and reflects like this: ‘Form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness are impermanent.’ And so they meditate observing impermanence in the five grasping aggregates. This is called the perception of impermanence.

§5And what is the perception of not-self? It’s when a mendicant has gone to a wilderness, or to the root of a tree, or to an empty hut, and reflects like this: ‘The eye and sights, ear and sounds, nose and smells, tongue and tastes, body and touches, and mind and ideas are not-self.’ And so they meditate observing not-self in the six interior and exterior sense fields. This is called the perception of not-self.

Ugliness and drawbacks

§6And what is the perception of ugliness? It’s when a mendicant examines their own body up from the soles of the feet and down from the tips of the hairs, wrapped in skin and full of many kinds of filth. ‘In this body there is head hair, body hair, nails, teeth, skin, flesh, sinews, bones, bone marrow, kidneys, heart, liver, diaphragm, spleen, lungs, intestines, mesentery, undigested food, feces, bile, phlegm, pus, blood, sweat, fat, tears, grease, saliva, snot, synovial fluid, urine.’ And so they meditate observing ugliness in this body. This is called the perception of ugliness.

§7And what is the perception of drawbacks? It’s when a mendicant has gone to a wilderness, or to the root of a tree, or to an empty hut, and reflects like this: ‘This body has much suffering and many drawbacks. For this body is beset with many kinds of affliction, such as the following. Diseases of the eye, inner ear, nose, tongue, body, head, outer ear, mouth, teeth, and lips. Cough, asthma, catarrh, inflammation, fever, stomach ache, fainting, dysentery, gastric pain, cholera, leprosy, boils, eczema, tuberculosis, epilepsy, herpes, itch, scabs, smallpox, scabies, hemorrhage, diabetes, piles, pimples, and ulcers. Afflictions stemming from disorders of bile, phlegm, wind, or their conjunction. Afflictions caused by change in weather, by not taking care of yourself, by overexertion, or as the result of past deeds. Cold, heat, hunger, thirst, defecation, and urination.’ And so they meditate observing drawbacks in this body. This is called the perception of drawbacks.

Giving up, fading away, cessation

§8And what is the perception of giving up? It’s when a mendicant doesn’t tolerate a sensual, malicious, or cruel thought that has arisen, and they don’t tolerate any bad, unskillful qualities that have arisen, but give them up, get rid of them, eliminate them, and obliterate them. This is called the perception of giving up.

§9And what is the perception of fading away? It’s when a mendicant has gone to a wilderness, or to the root of a tree, or to an empty hut, and reflects like this: ‘This is peaceful; this is sublime—that is, the stilling of all activities, the letting go of all attachments, the ending of craving, fading away, extinguishment.’ This is called the perception of fading away.

§10And what is the perception of cessation? It’s when a mendicant has gone to a wilderness, or to the root of a tree, or to an empty hut, and reflects like this: ‘This is peaceful; this is sublime—that is, the stilling of all activities, the letting go of all attachments, the ending of craving, cessation, extinguishment.’ This is called the perception of cessation.

Dissatisfaction with the whole world, and impermanence of all conditions

§11And what is the perception of dissatisfaction with the whole world? It’s when a mendicant lives giving up and not grasping on to the attraction and grasping to the world, the mental fixation, insistence, and underlying tendencies. This is called the perception of dissatisfaction with the whole world.

§12And what is the perception of the impermanence of all conditions? It’s when a mendicant is horrified, repelled, and disgusted with all conditions. This is called the perception of the impermanence of all conditions.

Mindfulness of breathing

§13.1–3And what is mindfulness of breathing? It’s when a mendicant has gone to a wilderness, or to the root of a tree, or to an empty hut, sits down cross-legged, sets their body straight, and brings mindfulness to the present. Just mindful, they breathe in. Mindful, they breathe out.

§13.4–7Breathing in heavily they know: ‘I’m breathing in heavily.’ Breathing out heavily they know: ‘I’m breathing out heavily.’ When breathing in lightly they know: ‘I’m breathing in lightly.’ Breathing out lightly they know: ‘I’m breathing out lightly.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe in experiencing the whole body.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe out experiencing the whole body.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe in stilling the physical process.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe out stilling the physical process.’

§13.8–11They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe in experiencing rapture.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe out experiencing rapture.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe in experiencing bliss.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe out experiencing bliss.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe in experiencing the mental process.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe out experiencing the mental process.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe in stilling the mental process.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe out stilling the mental process.’

§13.12–15They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe in experiencing the mind.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe out experiencing the mind.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe in gladdening the mind.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe out gladdening the mind.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe in immersing the mind in samādhi.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe out immersing the mind in samādhi.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe in freeing the mind.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe out freeing the mind.’

§13.16–20They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe in observing impermanence.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe out observing impermanence.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe in observing fading away.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe out observing fading away.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe in observing cessation.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe out observing cessation.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe in observing letting go.’ They practice like this: ‘I’ll breathe out observing letting go.’ This is called mindfulness of breathing.

Recited, and the illness subsides

§14–15If you were to recite to the mendicant Girimānanda these ten perceptions, it’s possible that after hearing them his illness will die down on the spot.” Then Ānanda, having learned these ten perceptions from the Buddha himself, went to Girimānanda and recited them. Then after Girimānanda heard these ten perceptions his illness died down on the spot. And that’s how he recovered from that illness.

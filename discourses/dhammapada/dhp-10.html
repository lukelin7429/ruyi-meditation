Dhammapada · Chapter 10

The Rod

Daṇḍavagga

Attribution
One chapter of a compiled verse anthology traditionally attributed to the Buddha; there is no narrative setting, speaker, or occasion given for these verses
Verses
129–145 (17 verses)

Length
~3 minutes to read

A broad counterpart exists in the Chinese Faju Jing (法句經, T210) and related Dharmapada collections, though exact verse-by-verse correspondence is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — largely direct, though the list of consequences in verses 137–140 is a little denser than the rest

Why this chapter

Daṇḍavagga takes its title from a word, daṇḍa, that means both a literal rod or stick and, by extension, coercive force in general — and the chapter uses both senses. It opens with what may be the single most quoted verse in the entire Dhammapada, a plain statement of universal empathy toward violence and death, then follows the theme of “the rod” outward: into harsh speech, into a cowherd's literal stick, into the specific consequences that fall on someone who attacks the innocent, and finally into a closing contrast between crude physical austerity and the quieter, harder discipline of an undisturbed mind.

Reading guide

All tremble, all fear: the golden rule twice over

The chapter opens with two verses so similar they function as one repeated statement: “All tremble at the rod, all fear death. Treating others like oneself, neither kill nor incite to kill” (v.129), followed immediately by “All tremble at the rod, all love life. Treating others like oneself, neither kill nor incite to kill” (v.130). The only change between the two is “fear death” against “love life” — the same fact stated once negatively and once positively. The reasoning offered is not a rule imposed from outside but a simple appeal to symmetry: since you know from the inside what it is to fear death and love life, and every other creature shares exactly that, harming them requires ignoring something you already know to be true of yourself. Verses 131–132 extend the same logic one step further, into consequence: “Creatures love happiness, so if you harm them with a stick in search of your own happiness, after death you won't find happiness” (v.131), against its positive mirror, “if you don't harm them... after death you will find happiness” (v.132). Seeking your own happiness at the cost of another's is presented as self-defeating, not just wrong.

Words too can be a rod

Verse 133 extends “the rod” from the physical into speech: “Don't speak harshly, they may speak harshly back. For aggressive speech is painful, and the rod may spring back on you.” The verse's closing image — the rod “springing back” — treats a harsh word as functionally the same kind of act as a blow, subject to the same possibility of return. Verse 134 answers with a simile of restraint: “If you still yourself like a broken gong, you reach extinguishment and know no conflict.” A broken gong, struck, produces no ringing note — it simply absorbs the blow without sounding. The image asks for a mind that does the same with provocation.

Old age and death as the greater cowherd

Verse 135 is a quiet pivot in the chapter's use of its own title image: “As a cowherd drives the cows to pasture with the rod, so too old age and death drive life from living beings.” Here “the rod” is no longer an instrument of violence between people at all — it becomes a simile for time itself, driving every living being forward the way a herder drives cattle, indifferent to consent. It is a small but pointed shift: having spent four verses on the rod as chosen human violence, the chapter shows the same image describing a force no one chooses and no one can refuse.

What harming the innocent brings

Verse 136 returns to human wrongdoing directly: “The fool does not understand the evil that they do. But because of those deeds, that simpleton is tormented as if burnt by fire.” Verses 137 through 140 then lay out, in list form, what specifically follows from attacking “the peaceful and the innocent”: such a person “swiftly falls to one of ten bad states” — “harsh pain; loss; the breakup of the body; serious illness; mental distress; hazards from rulers; vicious slander; loss of kin; destruction of wealth; or else their home is consumed by fire,” concluding starkly that “when their body breaks up, that witless person is reborn in hell.” The list is unusually concrete and specific for this collection — social, physical, and material harm named side by side — and its underlying logic matches the previous chapter's image of dust thrown into the wind: harm aimed at someone who has done nothing to deserve it does not simply dissipate but circles back on the one who caused it.

Not the rod of asceticism, but the discipline of the mind

The chapter's closing movement turns unexpectedly toward religious practice itself. Verses 141–142 draw a sharp line between outward austerity and inward change: “Not nudity, nor matted hair, nor mud, nor fasting, nor lying on bare ground, nor wearing dust and dirt, or squatting on the heels, will cleanse a mortal not free of doubt” (v.141). Every item on this list was a recognized ascetic practice in the world these verses come from, and the verse rejects every one of them as sufficient on its own. Verse 142 then supplies the actual criterion: “Dressed up they may be, but if they live well — peaceful, tamed, committed to the spiritual path, having laid aside violence towards all creatures — they are a brahmin, an ascetic, a mendicant.” Even someone in fine clothes, the verse insists, counts as a genuine renunciant if their conduct is right; no costume, comfortable or uncomfortable, substitutes for it.

The whip that a fine horse doesn't need

The chapter closes with two verses on conscience as the truer form of discipline. “Can a person constrained by conscience be found in the world? Who shies away from blame, like a fine horse from the whip?” (v.143) asks the question directly, and verse 144 answers by turning the simile into instruction: “Like a fine horse under the whip, be keen and full of urgency. With faith, ethics, and energy, immersion, and investigation of principles, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, mindful, give up this vast suffering.” The image is deliberately gentler than the chapter's opening violence — a well-trained horse barely needs the whip at all, responding to the slightest cue — the same shift from crude coercion to responsive inner discipline that verses 141–142 already made. The final verse (145) closes the chapter by returning, almost word for word, to an image from Chapter 6: “While irrigators guide water, fletchers shape arrows, and carpenters carve timber — those true to their vows tame themselves.” The near-identical return of that craft simile, seventeen verses and four chapters after it first appeared (Ch.6, v.80), quietly closes the loop between the astute person taming themselves and the disciplined mind this chapter has been describing all along.

Key terms

daṇḍa — “the rod,” the chapter's title term, used across the chapter for literal violence (v.129–132), harsh speech (v.133), a cowherd's tool and, by extension, time itself (v.135), and coercive punishment generally.
“treating others like oneself” — (v.129–130) the chapter's opening ethical principle, grounded in the shared fact of fearing death and loving life rather than an external command.
the ten bad states — (v.137–140) the specific list of consequences said to follow from attacking a peaceful, innocent person.
the rejected austerities — (v.141) nudity, matted hair, mud, fasting, lying on bare ground, and similar practices, named and set aside as insufficient for inner purification on their own.
the closing echo — (v.145) an almost word-for-word repetition of the craft simile that closed Chapter 6 (v.80), linking self-taming across both chapters.

Dhammapada · Chapter 20

The Path

Maggavagga

Verses
273–289 (seventeen verses)
Attribution
A compiled verse anthology with no narrative frame, traditionally attributed to the Buddha
Form
Opens with doctrinal summary (the eightfold path, the four truths), moves through direct exhortation, then similes on cutting through entanglement and facing death without illusion
Length
~3 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart exists in the Chinese Faju Jing (法句經, T210) and related Dharmapada collections, though exact verse-by-verse correspondence is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — doctrinally dense, presuming familiarity with the eightfold path, four truths, and three marks of existence

Why this chapter, twentieth

Maggavagga is one of the doctrinal centers of the entire Dhammapada. It opens by declaring the eightfold path the best of paths and the four truths the best of truths (273), states flatly that this is the only path to purified vision (274), and delivers what may be the single most quoted verse in the whole collection: the practitioner, not the Buddha, must do the actual work; “the Realized Ones just show the way” (276). A few verses later comes the threefold refrain that underlies the entire teaching — all conditions are impermanent, all conditions are suffering, all things are not-self — each ending on the identical line: “this is the path to purity” (277–279). Few chapters carry this much doctrinal weight in so few verses.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

There is exactly one path to purified vision — the eightfold path, matched to the four truths it makes sense of — and while the Buddha can point it out completely, only the practitioner's own sustained effort actually walks it, an effort the chapter grounds in seeing, with wisdom, that all conditions are impermanent and suffering, and that all things whatsoever are not-self.

The best of paths, truths, states, and humans (273)

The chapter opens with a compact, four-part superlative: “of paths, the eightfold is the best; of truths, the four statements; dispassion is the best of things, and the Clear-eyed One is the best of humans.” In one breath, the verse names the path (the noble eightfold path: right view, thought, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, and immersion — laid out factor by factor in SN 45.8), the truths it serves (the four noble truths, first proclaimed in full in SN 56.11), the goal those truths point toward (dispassion, freedom from craving), and the person who taught all of it (the Buddha, “the Clear-eyed One”).

"This is the path, there is no other" (274–276)

Three verses build toward the chapter's most quoted line. Verse 274 states the claim without qualification: “this is the path, there is no other for the purification of vision... it is the way to baffle Māra.” Verse 275 adds a striking image for what following it accomplishes — the path was explained “for extracting the thorn with wisdom,” ending suffering the way a skilled hand draws out an embedded splinter. Then verse 276: “you yourselves must do the work, the Realized Ones just show the way. Meditators practicing absorption are released from Māra's bonds.” This verse is often circulated in a shorter, more compact popular phrasing — along the lines of insisting that Buddhas only point out the path, while the actual walking is left entirely to the individual — and whichever wording reaches a given reader, the claim is the same: teaching, however complete, is not a substitute for practice.

The threefold refrain: impermanent, suffering, not-self (277–279)

Three verses in a row state the chapter's doctrinal foundation, each ending on an identical line:

VerseWhat is seenScope, in Sujato's wording
277impermanent“all conditions”
278suffering“all conditions”
279not-self“all things”

The wording shift in the third line is not incidental. “All conditions” (sabbe saṅkhārā) restricts impermanence and suffering to what is conditioned — things that arise dependent on causes. “All things” (sabbe dhammā) is broader, and not-self is stated to hold even of the unconditioned. Each verse ends the identical way: “when this is seen with wisdom, one grows disillusioned with suffering: this is the path to purity.” A closely related discourse in this site's Saṃyutta Nikāya series, SN 22.59, works through the not-self teaching in full prose detail; this chapter compresses the same insight into three lines.

Diligence, purity of action, and the two paths of wisdom (280–282)

A sharper, more personal register follows. Verse 280 criticizes someone “young and strong, but given to sloth” — their mind “depressed in sunken thought,” unable to discern the path at all. Verse 281 names the three doors of purified action needed to “win the path known to seers”: guarded speech, restrained mind, and no unskillful bodily deed. Verse 282 turns to meditation directly: “from meditation springs wisdom, without meditation, wisdom ends” — naming two paths, of progress and of decline, and urging the reader to conduct themselves so that wisdom grows rather than fades.

Cutting the jungle and the vine (283–284)

Two vivid similes follow. “Cut down the jungle, not just a tree; from the jungle springs fear” (283) — a warning against addressing only the visible symptom of craving while leaving its deeper tangle of roots intact. Verse 284 narrows to a specific entanglement: “so long as the vine, no matter how small, that ties a man to women is not cut, his mind remains trapped, like a calf suckling its mother” — a simile for how even a slight, seemingly minor attachment can hold a mind as completely as full dependency.

Facing death without illusion (285–289)

The chapter's final movement turns to mortality directly. Verse 285 urges cutting out “affection for oneself, like plucking an autumn lotus,” fostering only “the path to peace, the extinguishment the Holy One taught.” Verse 286 pictures a fool making seasonal plans — “here I will stay for the rains; here for winter, here the summer” — “not realizing the danger” that death may intervene first. Verse 287 delivers the same warning as a flood: “as a mighty flood sweeps away a sleeping village, death steals away a man who dotes on children and cattle, his mind caught up in them.” Verse 288 states plainly that “children provide you no shelter, nor does father, nor relatives” once “you're seized by the terminator.” The chapter's closing verse draws the whole argument to its point: knowing this, astute and ethically restrained, a person “would quickly clear the path that leads to extinguishment” (289) — returning, in its final line, to the same path the chapter opened by naming as the best of all paths.

Key terms

ariya aṭṭhaṅgika magga — the noble eightfold path: right view, thought, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, and immersion, defined factor by factor in SN 45.8, named here simply as “the eightfold” and “the best of paths” (273).
the four truths — suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path to its cessation, first proclaimed together in SN 56.11 and named here as “the best of truths.”
Māra — personified death and temptation; verse 274 calls the path “the way to baffle Māra,” and verse 276 says meditators are “released from Māra's bonds.”
saṅkhārā / dhammā — “conditions” versus “things”: the deliberate wording shift between verses 277–278 (impermanence, suffering) and verse 279 (not-self), where the scope of the claim widens from the conditioned to everything whatsoever.
tathāgata — “Realized One,” an epithet of the Buddha; verse 276 insists that even the Realized Ones can only show the path, not walk it on another's behalf.

The text

All seventeen verses, given complete and in order. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The best of paths, truths, states, and humans

Verse 273Of paths, the eightfold is the best;
of truths, the four statements;
dispassion is the best of things,
and the Clear-eyed One is the best of humans.

"This is the path, there is no other"

Verse 274This is the path, there is no other
for the purification of vision.
You all must practice this,
it is the way to baffle Māra.

Verse 275When you all are practicing this,
you’ll make an end of suffering.
I have explained the path to you
for extracting the thorn with wisdom.

Verse 276You yourselves must do the work,
the Realized Ones just show the way.
Meditators practicing absorption
are released from Māra’s bonds.

The threefold refrain

Verse 277All conditions are impermanent—
when this is seen with wisdom,
one grows disillusioned with suffering:
this is the path to purity.

Verse 278All conditions are suffering—
when this is seen with wisdom,
one grows disillusioned with suffering:
this is the path to purity.

Verse 279All things are not-self—
when this is seen with wisdom,
one grows disillusioned with suffering:
this is the path to purity.

Diligence, purity of action, and the two paths of wisdom

Verse 280They don’t get going when it’s time to start;
they’re young and strong, but given to sloth.
Their mind depressed in sunken thought,
lazy and slothful, they can’t discern the path.

Verse 281Guarded in speech, restrained in mind,
doing no unskillful bodily deed.
Purify these three ways of performing deeds,
and win the path known to seers.

Verse 282From meditation springs wisdom,
without meditation, wisdom ends.
Knowing these two paths—
of progress and decline—
you should conduct yourself
so that wisdom grows.

Cutting the jungle and the vine

Verse 283Cut down the jungle, not just a tree;
from the jungle springs fear.
Having cut down jungle and snarl,
be free of jungles, mendicants!

Verse 284So long as the vine, no matter how small,
that ties a man to women is not cut,
his mind remains trapped,
like a calf suckling its mother.

Facing death without illusion

Verse 285Cut out affection for oneself,
like plucking an autumn lotus.
Foster only the path to peace,
the extinguishment the Holy One taught.

Verse 286“Here I will stay for the rains;
here for winter, here the summer”;
thus the fool thinks,
not realizing the danger.

Verse 287As a mighty flood sweeps away a sleeping village,
death steals away a man
who dotes on children and cattle,
his mind caught up in them.

Verse 288Children provide you no shelter,
nor does father, nor relatives.
When you’re seized by the terminator,
there’s no shelter in family.

Verse 289Knowing the reason for this,
astute, and ethically restrained,
one would quickly clear the path
that leads to extinguishment.

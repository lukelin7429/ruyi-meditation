Dhammapada · Chapter 22

Hell

Nirayavagga

Why this chapter, twenty-second

The Nirayavagga is the Dhammapada’s shortest sustained warning — fourteen verses on what happens when speech, robe, and conduct come apart from one another. It opens with a liar and closes with a plain contrast between wrong view and right view, and in between it reaches for some of the most physically vivid imagery anywhere in the collection: a red-hot iron ball swallowed rather than eaten, a frontier city guarded on every side, a piece of kusa grass that cuts the careless hand that grasps it. None of this is aimed at outsiders. Every verse here is addressed to people who are already, in one way or another, claiming a spiritual life — and the chapter’s whole force lies in insisting that the claim and the conduct have to match.

Reading guide

The chapter in one sentence

A liar, a false claimant to spiritual attainment, and an unrestrained wearer of the robe are all said to head toward a bad rebirth just as surely as an outright wrongdoer — because in this chapter’s logic, the danger was never the robe or the words, but what a person actually does.

Two liars, one destination (306)

The chapter opens without preamble: “A liar goes to hell, as does one who denies what they did. Both are equal in the hereafter, those men of base deeds.” It is a deliberately symmetrical opening — the person who invents a falsehood and the person who disowns a truth are treated as equally “base,” because both are, at bottom, refusing to let their words track reality.

The robe that doesn’t save you (307–308)

From there the chapter turns immediately to a harder case: people who have taken on the outward form of the spiritual life without its substance. “Many who wear a scrap of ocher cloth are unrestrained and wicked. Being wicked, they are reborn in hell due to their bad deeds.” The ocher robe, the mark of a mendicant, offers no protection at all if the person wearing it is unrestrained. Verse 308 pushes the point to its most extreme image: “It’d be better for the immoral and unrestrained to eat an iron ball, scorching, like a burning flame, than to eat the nation’s alms.” The alms of the faithful are a gift given in trust; to consume them while lacking the ethical discipline the robe implies is described as worse than swallowing fire. The image recurs almost verbatim later in the Dhammapada, in the mendicants’ chapter (verse 371), which suggests it was a genuinely memorable teaching tool rather than a one-off flourish.

A heedless man (309–310)

The chapter’s one extended narrative example concerns adultery: “Four things befall a heedless man who sleeps with another’s wife: wickedness, poor sleep, ill-repute, and rebirth in hell.” Verse 310 spells out the disproportion directly — a frightened couple snatching “a moment’s pleasure” against consequences that include both a ruler’s heavy punishment in this life and a bad rebirth in the next. The verse is blunt about the trade: momentary and small against lasting and severe.

Grasping the spiritual life the wrong way (311–313)

Verse 311 offers the chapter’s central image: “When kusa grass is wrongly grasped it only cuts the hand. So too, the ascetic life, when wrongly taken, drags you to hell.” Kusa grass is sharp-edged; held correctly it is harmless, held carelessly it slices the hand that holds it. The renunciant life works the same way — taken up loosely, half-heartedly, or as mere performance, it becomes a source of harm rather than protection. Verses 312 and 313 make the same point without the metaphor: “Any lax act, any corrupt observance, or suspicious spiritual life, is not very fruitful,” and the life “gone forth when laxly led just stirs up dust all the more.”

What plagues you later (314)

A brief, almost proverbial verse sits in the chapter’s middle: “A bad deed is better left undone, for it will plague you later on. A good deed is better done, one that does not plague you.” It is the chapter’s only verse without any reference to hell or a bad rebirth at all — a plain statement about which of one’s own actions will come back to trouble the mind, regardless of cosmology.

Guarding the frontier city (315)

Verse 315 shifts to a martial image: “As a frontier city is guarded inside and out, so you should ward yourselves — don’t let the moment pass you by. For if you miss your moment you’ll grieve when sent to hell.” A city on a contested border cannot afford a single unguarded gate; the verse asks for the same total vigilance turned toward one’s own conduct, with the same sense of an exposed, high-stakes position.

Wrong view and right view (316–319)

The chapter closes with four verses built on an identical frame, each contrasting a distortion with reality: shame misplaced on what isn’t shameful and absent where it belongs (316), danger imagined where there is none while real danger goes unseen (317), fault imagined where there is none while real fault goes unseen (318), and finally the corrective — “knowing a fault as a fault and the faultless as faultless” (319). The first three all end “beings who uphold wrong view go to a bad place”; the fourth flips the refrain to its positive form, “beings who uphold right view go to a good place.” The whole chapter, in other words, closes by naming its actual subject: not hell as a place, but wrong view as the mechanism that leads there, and the possibility of simply seeing correctly as its remedy.

Key terms

niraya — “hell,” “a place of loss”: not a permanent destination in Buddhist cosmology but one of several realms of rebirth, entered and eventually left according to the fruiting of past deeds.
kusa grass — a sharp-bladed grass used in the chapter’s central simile (311): grasped correctly it is harmless, grasped carelessly it cuts, just as the ascetic life wrongly undertaken becomes a source of harm.
the iron ball — the image in verse 308 (echoed again at verse 371, in the mendicants’ chapter): swallowing a scorching iron ball is said to be preferable to consuming alms one has not earned through genuine ethical restraint.
the wrong-view refrain — the closing four verses (316–319), each built on the same frame of misplaced shame, imagined danger, or imagined fault, ending in the phrase “go to a bad place” — reversed to “a good place” only once right view is named.

The text

All fourteen verses of the Nirayavagga, complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 306A liar goes to hell,
as does one who denies what they did.
Both are equal in the hereafter,
those men of base deeds.

Verse 307Many who wear a scrap of ocher cloth
are unrestrained and wicked.
Being wicked, they are reborn in hell
due to their bad deeds.

Verse 308It’d be better for the immoral and unrestrained
to eat an iron ball,
scorching, like a burning flame,
than to eat the nation’s alms.

Verse 309Four things befall a heedless man
who sleeps with another’s wife:
wickedness, poor sleep,
ill-repute, and rebirth in hell.

Verse 310He accrues wickedness and is reborn in a bad place,
all so a frightened couple may snatch a moment’s pleasure,
for which rulers impose a heavy punishment.
That’s why a man should not sleep with another’s wife.

Verse 311When kusa grass is wrongly grasped
it only cuts the hand.
So too, the ascetic life, when wrongly taken,
drags you to hell.

Verse 312Any lax act,
any corrupt observance,
or suspicious spiritual life,
is not very fruitful.

Verse 313If one is to do what should be done,
one should staunchly strive.
For the life gone forth when laxly led
just stirs up dust all the more.

Verse 314A bad deed is better left undone,
for it will plague you later on.
A good deed is better done,
one that does not plague you.

Verse 315As a frontier city
is guarded inside and out,
so you should ward yourselves —
don’t let the moment pass you by.
For if you miss your moment
you’ll grieve when sent to hell.

Verse 316Unashamed of what is shameful,
ashamed of what is not shameful;
beings who uphold wrong view
go to a bad place.

Verse 317Seeing danger where there is none,
and blind to the actual danger,
beings who uphold wrong view
go to a bad place.

Verse 318Seeing fault where there is none,
and blind to the actual fault,
beings who uphold wrong view
go to a bad place.

Verse 319Knowing a fault as a fault
and the faultless as faultless,
beings who uphold right view
go to a good place.

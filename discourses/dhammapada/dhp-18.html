Dhammapada · Chapter 18

Stains

Malavagga

Verses
235–255 (twenty-one verses)
Attribution
A compiled verse anthology with no narrative frame, traditionally attributed to the Buddha
Form
An urgent opening memento mori, a catalog of stains specific to each area of life, and closing similes on fault-finding and the ascetic's proper path
Length
~4 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart exists in the Chinese Faju Jing (法句經, T210) and related Dharmapada collections, though exact verse-by-verse correspondence is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — mixes plain simile with terser, riddling lines that reward a second reading

Why this chapter, eighteenth

Malavagga begins with as urgent a warning as the Dhammapada offers anywhere: “today you’re like a withered leaf, Yama’s men await you... yet you have no supplies for the road.” From that opening jolt, the chapter settles into its real subject — not death in general, but the specific, avoidable stains that corrupt each area of a life: non-recitation for scripture, neglect for a house, laziness for beauty, misconduct for a wife, stinginess for a giver. Then, having built a careful catalog, the text drops its most important claim almost in passing: worse than any of these named stains, worse than all of them together, is ignorance — “the worst stain of all.”

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Every domain of life has its own characteristic corruption — scripture unrehearsed, a house unrepaired, a body left lazy, a guard grown negligent — but the chapter's real argument is that all of these are minor beside the one stain most people never think to name: ignorance itself, harder to give up than any of the others.

An urgent warning (235–238)

The chapter opens with two near-identical, escalating warnings. “Today you’re like a withered leaf, Yama’s men await you. You stand at the departure gates, yet you have no supplies for the road” (235); “you’ve journeyed the stages of life, and now you set out to meet Yama... yet you have no supplies for the road” (237). Yama is death personified, and his “men” are the traditional messengers — old age, sickness, and death itself — that announce his approach. Each warning is followed by the identical exhortation: “make an island of yourself! Swiftly strive, learn to be wise! Purged of stains, flawless...” (236, 238) — with the reward stated twice, first as reaching “the heavenly realm of the noble ones,” then, more finally, as never again returning “to rebirth and old age.”

Purging stains, gradually and by neglect (239–240)

Two brief similes follow. A smart person purges their own stains “gradually, bit by bit, moment by moment, like a smith smelting silver” — refinement as patient, incremental work, not a single dramatic event (239). Verse 240 turns the same logic toward warning: “it is the rust born on the iron that eats away the place it arose” — and just so, a person’s own deeds are what lead “the overly-ascetic” to a bad place. The stain, in other words, is not an external contaminant; it is generated by, and then consumes, the very thing that produced it.

A catalog of stains (241–243)

The chapter's centerpiece is a tight, almost proverbial list, matching a specific failing to a specific domain of life:

DomainIts characteristic stain
hymns (scripture)not rehearsing them
housesneglect
beautylaziness
a guardnegligence
a womanmisconduct
a giverstinginess

Bad qualities generally, the text adds, are a stain “in this world and the next” (242). Then comes the turn that gives the catalog its point: “but a worse stain than these is ignorance, the worst stain of all. Having given up that stain, be without stains, mendicants!” (243). Every stain named before this is domain-specific — a hymn forgotten, a house untended — but ignorance corrupts every domain at once, which is exactly why the verse calls it the worst.

Two ways of living (244–245)

A sharp contrast follows: life is easy for the shameless, who live “with all the rude courage of a crow” — pushy, rude, corrupt (244); life is hard for the conscientious, who seek purity, cling to nothing, and live discerning and clean of livelihood (245). The chapter does not pretend that the harder path is the more comfortable one.

Serious misconduct, and the almsfood test (246–250)

Verses 246 through 248 name grave misconduct plainly — killing, lying, stealing, adultery, and drinking — and warn that in doing these things, a person “digs up the root of their own self”; the closing line is direct address: “know this, my man: they are unrestrained and wicked” and should not let greed and unrighteousness “inflict pain on you for long.” Verses 249 and 250 then turn to a subtler, easily overlooked failing: resentment over what other people receive as alms. People give “according to their faith, according to their confidence,” and a mendicant who gets upset about others’ food and drink “will not, by day or by night, become immersed in samādhi” — while one who has “cut that out, dug it up at the root” will.

No fire like greed (251)

A single, tightly balanced verse states four comparisons at once: “there is no fire like greed, no crime like hate, no net like delusion, no river like craving.” Each image is chosen precisely — fire consumes indiscriminately, a net entangles without the victim quite realizing it, a river carries a person along whether or not they intend to go.

Seeing others' faults, not one's own (252–253)

“It’s easy to see the faults of others, hard to see one’s own” — other people’s faults are “tossed high like hay,” visible to everyone, while one’s own are hidden, “as a cheat hides a bad hand” (252). The next verse warns where this habit leads: looking for others’ flaws, always finding fault, only grows one’s own defilements, leaving a person further, not closer, to ending them (253).

No track in the sky (254–255)

The chapter closes with two nearly identical verses built on the same striking image: “in the atmosphere there is no track, there’s no true ascetic outside here.” The first completes the thought by contrasting ordinary people, who “enjoy proliferation” — the mind’s endless elaboration and complication of experience — with the Realized Ones, who are free of it (254). The second completes it instead with impermanence: “no conditions last forever, the Awakened Ones are not shaken” (255). Just as birds leave no lasting track in open sky, the text implies, there is no shortcut to genuine renunciation found anywhere outside the path actually walked.

Key terms

mala — “stain,” the word that gives the chapter its name, applied across verses 241–243 to a different domain of life each time.
Yama — death personified; his “men” in verse 235 are the traditional messengers — old age, sickness, and death — that herald his approach.
papañca — “proliferation,” the mind’s tendency to endlessly elaborate and complicate experience, named in verse 254 as what ordinary people enjoy and the Realized Ones are free of.
avijjā — “ignorance,” named in verse 243 as the worst stain of all, harder to give up than any of the domain-specific stains listed before it.

The text

All twenty-one verses, given complete and in order. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

An urgent warning

Verse 235Today you’re like a withered leaf,
Yama’s men await you.
You stand at the departure gates,
yet you have no supplies for the road.

Verse 236Make an island of yourself!
Swiftly strive, learn to be wise!
Purged of stains, flawless,
you’ll go to the heavenly realm of the noble ones.

Verse 237You’ve journeyed the stages of life,
and now you set out to meet Yama.
Along the way there’s nowhere to stay,
yet you have no supplies for the road.

Verse 238Make an island of yourself!
Swiftly strive, learn to be wise!
Purged of stains, flawless,
you’ll not come again to rebirth and old age.

Purging stains, gradually and by neglect

Verse 239A smart person would purge
their own stains gradually,
bit by bit, moment by moment,
like a smith smelting silver.

Verse 240It is the rust born on the iron
that eats away the place it arose.
And so it is their own deeds
that lead the overly-ascetic to a bad place.

A catalog of stains

Verse 241Not rehearsing is the stain of hymns.
The stain of houses is neglect.
Laziness is the stain of beauty.
A guard’s stain is negligence.

Verse 242Misconduct is a woman’s stain.
A giver’s stain is stinginess.
Bad qualities are a stain
in this world and the next.

Verse 243But a worse stain than these
is ignorance, the worst stain of all.
Having given up that stain,
be without stains, mendicants!

Two ways of living

Verse 244Life is easy for the shameless.
With all the rude courage of a crow,
they live pushy,
rude, and corrupt.

Verse 245Life is hard for the conscientious,
always seeking purity,
neither clinging nor rude,
pure of livelihood and discerning.

Serious misconduct, and the almsfood test

Verses 246–248Take anyone in this world
who kills living creatures,
speaks falsely, steals,
commits adultery,
and indulges in drinking
beer and wine.
Right here they dig up
the root of their own self.
Know this, my man:
they are unrestrained and wicked.
Don’t let that greed and unrighteousness
inflict pain on you for long.

Verses 249–250The people give according to their faith,
according to their confidence.
If you get upset over that,
over others’ food and drink,
you’ll not, by day or by night,
become immersed in samādhi.
Those who have cut that out,
dug it up at the root, eradicated it,
they will, by day or by night,
become immersed in samādhi.

No fire like greed

Verse 251There is no fire like greed,
no crime like hate,
no net like delusion,
no river like craving.

Seeing others' faults, not one's own

Verse 252It’s easy to see the faults of others,
hard to see one’s own.
For the faults of others
are tossed high like hay,
while one’s own are hidden,
as a cheat hides a bad hand.

Verse 253When you look for the flaws of others,
always finding fault,
your defilements only grow,
you’re far from ending defilements.

No track in the sky

Verse 254In the atmosphere there is no track,
there’s no true ascetic outside here.
People enjoy proliferation,
the Realized Ones are free of proliferation.

Verse 255In the atmosphere there is no track,
there’s no true ascetic outside here.
No conditions last forever,
the Awakened Ones are not shaken.

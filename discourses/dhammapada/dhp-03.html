Dhammapada · Chapter 3

The Mind

Cittavagga

Verses
33–43
Speaker
Traditionally the Buddha, though as a compiled verse anthology individual verses may not all share a single narrative occasion
Form
Eleven verses of vivid, often paired imagery on the mind’s flightiness, the difficulty of taming it, and the great benefit of doing so
Length
~3 minutes to read

Why this chapter, the third

After mind and intention open the collection and diligence supplies the discipline to sustain attention, the third chapter turns to the object that attention must actually work on: the mind itself, described here as quivering, hard to guard, hard to curb — and yet, once tamed, more valuable than any relative could ever be. The chapter’s images are unusually vivid even by Dhammapada standards: a fish flopping on dry land, a citadel under siege, a body discarded like a worthless log.

Reading guide

The chapter in one sentence

Eleven verses characterize the untrained mind as quivering, hard to hold, and dangerously mobile, then argue — through similes of fletchers, fish, citadels, and family relations — that the work of taming it is both difficult and worth more than almost anything else a person could accomplish.

Verse 33: the fletcher and the arrow

The chapter opens by naming the problem directly: “the mind quivers and shakes, hard to guard, hard to curb.” Its solution comes as a craft simile: “the discerning straighten it out, like a fletcher straightens an arrow.” An arrow shaft is not naturally straight; it becomes straight through a deliberate, skilled, repeated correction — exactly the kind of ongoing labor the verse implies the mind requires, not a one-time fix.

Verses 34–37: flopping, flighty, and hidden

Verse 34 gives the chapter’s most visceral image: “like a fish pulled from the sea and cast upon the shore, this mind flounders about, trying to throw off Māra’s dominion” — the mind out of its element, thrashing against a control it cannot escape by force alone. Verses 35 and 36 pair nearly identical structures: the mind is “hard to hold back, flighty, alighting where it will” (35) and “so hard to see, so subtle, alighting where it will” (36), yet in both cases the conclusion is the same — taming or guarding it “leads to bliss.” Verse 37 adds a further description: the mind “travels far, wandering alone; incorporeal, it lies hidden in the heart” — and those who restrain it “are freed from Māra’s bonds.”

Verses 38–39: wavering versus undamaged

Verse 38 describes the cost of instability: those “of unsteady mind, who don’t understand the true teaching, and whose confidence wavers” never “perfect their wisdom.” Verse 39 answers with the opposite condition: a mind “not festering,” a heart “undamaged,” someone who has “given up right and wrong” (in the sense of no longer being pulled by reactive judgment) and remains alert — such a person “has nothing to fear.”

Verses 40–41: the body as pot, the mind as citadel

Verse 40 gives instructions rather than description: “knowing this body breaks like a pot, and fortifying the mind like a citadel, attack Māra with the sword of wisdom, guard your conquest, and never settle.” It is one of the few verses in this chapter framed as active instruction rather than observation, and it explicitly links the body’s fragility to the urgency of the mind’s defense. Verse 41 makes the body’s fragility even starker: “all too soon this body will lie upon the earth, bereft of consciousness, tossed aside like a worthless log” — a blunt reminder of what is actually at stake in whether the mind, which will outlast this particular arrangement of matter, has been well or poorly trained.

Verses 42–43: worse than an enemy, better than a mother

The chapter’s closing pair is its most quoted. Verse 42: “a wrongly directed mind would do you more harm than a hater to the hated, or an enemy to their foe.” Verse 43 answers: “a rightly directed mind would do you more good than your mother or father or any other relative.” The comparison is deliberately extreme in both directions — no external enemy, however hostile, and no relative, however loving, can affect a person as deeply as their own mind, well or poorly directed, inevitably will.

Key terms

citta — mind or heart-mind, the chapter’s subject, treated throughout as something quivering, mobile, and difficult to hold still.
the fletcher’s arrow — verse 33’s image for the discerning person’s ongoing, skilled correction of the mind, implying repeated labor rather than a single fix.
Māra — personified death and temptation, whose “dominion” and “bonds” the untamed mind struggles against in verses 34 and 37.
the citadel — verse 40’s image for the fortified mind, set against the body’s fragility, “a pot” that inevitably breaks.
“more good than your mother or father” — the closing comparison of verse 43, one of the most quoted lines in this chapter, stressing the mind’s outsized influence on one’s own welfare.

The text

All eleven verses of the Cittavagga. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 33The mind quivers and shakes, hard to guard, hard to curb. The discerning straighten it out, like a fletcher straightens an arrow.

Verse 34Like a fish pulled from the sea and cast upon the shore, this mind flounders about, trying to throw off Māra’s dominion.

Verse 35Hard to hold back, flighty, alighting where it will; it’s good to tame the mind; a tamed mind leads to bliss.

Verse 36So hard to see, so subtle, alighting where it will; the discerning protect the mind, a guarded mind leads to bliss.

Verse 37The mind travels far, wandering alone; incorporeal, it lies hidden in the heart. Those who will restrain the mind are freed from Māra’s bonds.

Verse 38Those of unsteady mind, who don’t understand the true teaching, and whose confidence wavers, do not perfect their wisdom.

Verse 39One whose mind is not festering, whose heart is undamaged, who’s given up right and wrong, alert, has nothing to fear.

Verse 40Knowing this body breaks like a pot, and fortifying the mind like a citadel, attack Māra with the sword of wisdom, guard your conquest, and never settle.

Verse 41All too soon this body will lie upon the earth, bereft of consciousness, tossed aside like a worthless log.

Verses 42–43A wrongly directed mind would do you more harm than a hater to the hated, or an enemy to their foe.

A rightly directed mind would do you more good than your mother or father or any other relative.

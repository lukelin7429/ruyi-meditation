Dhammapada · Chapter 4

Flowers

Pupphavagga

Verses
44–59
Speaker

Form
Sixteen verses built around flower, bee, and fragrance imagery, ranging from meditative instruction to plain ethical advice
Length
~4 minutes to read

Why this chapter, the fourth

After three chapters on intention, diligence, and the mind, the Pupphavagga steps back into imagery drawn from an ordinary garden — flowers, bees, fragrance carried on the wind — and finds in each a lesson about impermanence, restraint, or virtue. Its two most celebrated verses both concern a bee: taking nectar from a flower “without harming its color or fragrance,” the way a sage should move through a village, taking only what is offered and leaving no damage behind.

Reading guide

The chapter in one sentence

Sixteen verses use flowers, bees, and fragrance to make a series of related points about impermanence, restraint, and virtue — culminating in the image of a lotus blossoming, fragrant and unstained, out of a heap of roadside trash.

Verses 44–46: who arranges the truth like a flower?

The chapter opens with a riddling question and its answer, phrased identically apart from one word: “Who bestirs this earth, and the Yama realm with its gods? Who sets out the well-taught word of truth, as an expert a flower?” (44). The answer: “A trainee bestirs this earth… a trainee sets out the well-taught word of truth, as an expert a flower” (45) — a sekha, someone still training on the path, likened to a skilled florist arranging blossoms with care. Verse 46 turns from arranging flowers to the body itself: “knowing this body’s like foam, realizing it’s all just a mirage, and cutting off Māra’s blossoming, vanish from the King of Death” — the same floral language now applied ironically to what must be cut off rather than arranged.

Verses 47–48: death steals the gatherer

A pair on the danger of absorption in pleasure: “as a mighty flood sweeps off a sleeping village, death steals away a man even as he gathers flowers, his mind caught up in them” (47); “the terminator gains control of the man who has not had his fill of pleasures, even as he gathers flowers, his mind caught up in them” (48). The repetition of “gathering flowers, mind caught up in them” across both verses stresses that death does not wait for satisfaction to arrive — it interrupts mid-gesture, in the middle of an unfinished pleasure.

Verse 49: the bee and the village

The chapter’s most famous single verse: “a bee takes the nectar and moves on, doing no damage to the flower’s beauty and fragrance; and that’s how a sage should walk in the village.” The simile sets a standard for how a wandering mendicant should receive alms — taking only what is needed and freely given, leaving the household undiminished, exactly as a bee leaves a flower undamaged after feeding.

Verse 50: watch yourself, not others

A brief, plainly stated verse without floral imagery, placed at the chapter’s center: “don’t find fault with others, with what they’ve done or left undone. You should only watch yourself, what you’ve done or left undone.” Its position between the bee simile and the following verses on speech and action suggests it functions as a hinge — the discipline that lets a sage move through the world like the bee of verse 49, undamaging and undamaged, is fundamentally a matter of self-scrutiny rather than judgment of others.

Verses 51–53: color, fragrance, and acting on words

Verses 51 and 52 pair a flower that is “colorful but lacks fragrance” with eloquent speech that is “fruitless… for one who does not act on it,” against a flower “both colorful and fragrant” matched to speech “fruitful… for one who acts on it.” The point is blunt: eloquence without follow-through is decorative but empty. Verse 53 shifts to a different floral image — “just as one would create many garlands from a heap of flowers” — applied to a human life: “when a person has come to be born, they should do many skillful things,” treating a lifetime as raw material meant to be worked into something, not merely accumulated.

Verses 54–57: the fragrance that travels against the wind

Verse 54 states the chapter’s second most quoted claim: “the fragrance of flowers doesn’t spread upwind, nor sandalwood, pinwheel, or jasmine; but the fragrance of the good spreads upwind; a true person’s virtue spreads in every direction.” Ordinary scent obeys the wind; virtue does not obey ordinary constraints at all, reaching wherever a person’s reputation for goodness reaches. Verses 55 and 56 rank virtue’s fragrance above sandalwood, pinwheel, lotus, and jasmine, “supreme” among fragrances and reaching “the highest gods.” Verse 57 closes this run with a practical claim: for those “accomplished in ethics, meditating diligently, freed through the highest knowledge, Māra cannot find their path” — the fragrance of virtue is not just admired, it is protective.

Verses 58–59: a lotus from a heap of trash

The closing pair supplies the chapter’s final image, and arguably its most hopeful: “from a heap of trash discarded on the highway, a lotus might blossom, fragrant and delightful” (58); “so too, among those thought of as trash, a disciple of the perfect Buddha outshines with their wisdom the blind ordinary folk” (59). The lotus, rooted in mud yet unstained by it, is among the most recognizable images in Buddhist art precisely because of a verse like this one: origin does not determine outcome, and the most unpromising circumstance can still produce something fragrant and clear.

Key terms

sekha — a “trainee,” someone who has entered the path but not yet completed it; verse 45’s answer to who can skillfully set out the teaching, “as an expert a flower.”
the bee and the village — verse 49’s simile for how a mendicant should receive alms: taking only what is needed, leaving no damage behind, exactly as a bee leaves a flower undiminished.
fragrance against the wind — verse 54’s image for virtue’s reach: ordinary scent travels only downwind, but a good reputation is said to spread “in every direction.”
the lotus from trash — verses 58–59’s closing image, applied to how a disciple’s wisdom can arise regardless of unpromising origin or circumstance.
Māra’s “blossoming” — verse 46’s ironic use of floral language for what must be “cut off” rather than cultivated, contrasting the chapter’s otherwise positive flower imagery.

The text

All sixteen verses of the Pupphavagga. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verses 44–45Who bestirs this earth, and the Yama realm with its gods? Who sets out the well-taught word of truth, as an expert a flower?

A trainee bestirs this earth, and the Yama realm with its gods. A trainee sets out the well-taught word of truth, as an expert a flower.

Verse 46Knowing this body’s like foam, realizing it’s all just a mirage, and cutting off Māra’s blossoming, vanish from the King of Death.

Verses 47–48As a mighty flood sweeps off a sleeping village, death steals away a man even as he gathers flowers, his mind caught up in them.

The terminator gains control of the man who has not had his fill of pleasures, even as he gathers flowers, his mind caught up in them.

Verse 49A bee takes the nectar and moves on, doing no damage to the flower’s beauty and fragrance; and that’s how a sage should walk in the village.

Verse 50Don’t find fault with others, with what they’ve done or left undone. You should only watch yourself, what you’ve done or left undone.

Verses 51–52Just like a glorious flower that’s colorful but lacks fragrance; eloquent speech is fruitless for one who does not act on it.

Just like a glorious flower that’s both colorful and fragrant, eloquent speech is fruitful for one who acts on it.

Verse 53Just as one would create many garlands from a heap of flowers, when a person has come to be born, they should do many skillful things.

Verse 54The fragrance of flowers doesn’t spread upwind, nor sandalwood, pinwheel, or jasmine; but the fragrance of the good spreads upwind; a true person’s virtue spreads in every direction.

Verse 55Among all the fragrances—sandalwood or pinwheel or lotus or jasmine—the fragrance of virtue is supreme.

Verse 56Faint is the fragrance of sandal or pinwheel; but the fragrance of the virtuous floats to the highest gods.

Verse 57For those accomplished in ethics, meditating diligently, freed through the highest knowledge, Māra cannot find their path.

Verses 58–59From a heap of trash discarded on the highway, a lotus might blossom, fragrant and delightful.

So too, among those thought of as trash, a disciple of the perfect Buddha outshines with their wisdom the blind ordinary folk.

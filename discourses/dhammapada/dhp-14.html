Dhammapada · Chapter 14

The Buddhas

Buddhavagga

Verses
179–196 (18 verses)

Length
~5 minutes to read

Why this chapter, fourteenth

The Buddhavagga steps back from ethical instruction to sing, briefly, in praise of the awakened — how rare a Buddha’s arising is, how untraceable the awakened mind becomes, and, at its center, the single most quoted line in the entire Dhammapada: “not doing any evil, cultivating the good, purifying one’s own mind — this is the instruction of the Buddhas.” It closes by naming the supreme refuge available to anyone who understands what that instruction points to.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A Buddha’s victory leaves no track to follow because craving, the very thing that leaves tracks, has been abandoned entirely; the whole of that teaching compresses into one sentence — not doing evil, cultivating good, purifying the mind — and the supreme refuge available to anyone is going, with genuine understanding, to the Buddha, the teaching, and the community that lives it.

A victory with no track

The chapter opens with two linked verses describing the Buddha’s awakening as something almost geometrically strange: “he whose victory may not be undone, a victory unrivaled in all the world; by what track would you trace that Buddha, who leaves no track in his infinite range?” (179), and, naming the reason directly, “of craving, the weaver, the clinger, he has none: so where can he be traced?” (180). Craving is the thing that leaves footprints — it attaches to results, reaches for more experience, weaves a self out of what it grasps — so a mind with none of it simply doesn’t leave the kind of trail an ordinary mind leaves. Verse 181 adds that such minds, “intent on absorption,” who “love the peace of renunciation,” are “envied by even the gods” — a small but pointed claim that whatever the gods have, the peace of a purified mind outranks it.

Four things that are hard

Verse 182 lists four rare and difficult attainments in a single breath: “it’s hard to gain a human birth; the life of mortals is hard; it’s hard to hear the true teaching; the arising of Buddhas is hard.” Each clause narrows the odds further — being born human at all, surviving that fragile life, encountering a genuine teaching within it, and having that teaching exist in the world in the first place because a Buddha arose to give it. The verse doesn’t argue for despair at these odds; if anything, later verses (193–194) treat the rarity itself as cause for celebration once the conditions do align.

The instruction of the Buddhas

Verse 183 is the chapter’s center of gravity and among the most widely quoted lines in the whole canon: “not to do any evil; to embrace the good; to purify one’s mind: this is the instruction of the Buddhas.” Its appeal is exactly its compression — three clauses that could serve as a complete ethical and contemplative program on their own, without a single technical term. Verses 184–185 extend the same summary with more specific monastic content: “patient acceptance is the ultimate fervor,” “extinguishment is the ultimate,” “no true renunciate injures another,” and a short list — not speaking ill, restraint, moderation in eating, remote lodgings, commitment to the higher mind — each closing with the identical refrain, “this is the instruction of the Buddhas.” This same passage, almost word for word, is recited elsewhere in the canon as the verse-code given by the Buddha Vipassī to his own gathered Saṅgha — a detail suggesting the tradition regarded this compact formula as timeless, repeated by every Buddha rather than unique to any one of them.

What doesn’t satisfy, and what does

Verses 186–187 turn briefly to sensual pleasure: “even if it were raining money, you’d not be sated in sensual pleasures,” since “an astute person understands that sensual pleasures offer little gratification and much suffering” — and so a genuine disciple “finds no delight even in celestial pleasures,” delighting instead “in the ending of craving.” Verses 188–192 then take up refuge directly, first describing a common but limited response to fear: “so many go for refuge to mountains and forest groves, to shrines in tended parks; those people are driven by fear” (188), and naming its limit plainly: “such refuge is no sanctuary, it is no supreme refuge… you’re not released from all suffering” (189). The contrast comes immediately: one who instead goes “for refuge to the Buddha, to his teaching and to the Saṅgha” and “sees the four noble truths with right understanding” (190) — suffering, its origin, its transcendence, and the eightfold path to its stilling (191) — finds a refuge that is “a sanctuary,” “the supreme refuge,” by which “you’re released from all suffering” (192). The difference isn’t the sincerity of the fear driving either search, but whether what’s found actually addresses the fear’s root.

Rare, and worth celebrating

The closing verses circle back to rarity, but now as celebration rather than warning. “It’s hard to find a thoroughbred man: they’re not born just anywhere. A family where that attentive one is born prospers in happiness” (193), and then, in one of the collection’s most joyful passages: “happy, the arising of Buddhas! Happy, the teaching of Dhamma! Happy is the harmony of the Saṅgha, and the striving of the harmonious is happy” (194) — praising, in sequence, exactly the triple gem named as the supreme refuge two verses earlier. The chapter’s final image (195–196) turns to what happens when a person venerates one of the worthy — a Buddha or a disciple who has “transcended proliferation,” “left behind grief and lamentation,” and become “quenched, fearing nothing from any quarter” — and the answer is deliberately unquantifiable: “the merit of one venerating such as these cannot be calculated by anyone, saying it is just this much.”

Key terms

apadaṁ — “no track,” the chapter’s opening image (179–180) for a mind free of the craving that would otherwise leave a traceable path.
ovāda-pātimokkha — the traditional name for the passage in verses 183–185, “the instruction of the Buddhas,” widely quoted as a one-line summary of the entire teaching.
tisaraṇa — “the three refuges” (190), going to the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Saṅgha, contrasted here with the limited refuge of mountains and forest shrines.
cattāri ariyasaccāni — “the four noble truths” (191): suffering, its origin, its transcendence, and the eightfold path leading to its stilling.
papañca — “proliferation,” named in verse 195 as something the worthy have transcended: the mind’s tendency to elaborate experience into endless conceptual spinning.

The text

All eighteen verses of the Buddhavagga, given complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A victory with no track

Verse 179He whose victory may not be undone, a victory unrivaled in all the world; by what track would you trace that Buddha, who leaves no track in his infinite range?

Verse 180Of craving, the weaver, the clinger, he has none: so where can he be traced? By what track would you trace that Buddha, who leaves no track in his infinite range?

Verse 181The attentive intent on absorption, who love the peace of renunciation, the Buddhas, ever mindful, are envied by even the gods.

Four things that are hard

Verse 182It’s hard to gain a human birth; the life of mortals is hard; it’s hard to hear the true teaching; the arising of Buddhas is hard.

The instruction of the Buddhas

Verse 183Not to do any evil; to embrace the good; to purify one’s mind: this is the instruction of the Buddhas.

Verse 184Patient acceptance is the ultimate fervor. Extinguishment is the ultimate, say the Buddhas. No true renunciate injures another, nor does an ascetic hurt another.

Verse 185Not speaking ill nor doing harm; restraint in the monastic code; moderation in eating; staying in remote lodgings; commitment to the higher mind — this is the instruction of the Buddhas.

What doesn’t satisfy, and what does

Verse 186Even if it were raining money, you’d not be sated in sensual pleasures. An astute person understands that sensual pleasures offer little gratification and much suffering.

Verse 187Thus they find no delight even in celestial pleasures. A disciple of the fully awakened Buddha delights in the ending of craving.

The supreme refuge

Verse 188So many go for refuge to mountains and forest groves, to shrines in tended parks; those people are driven by fear.

Verse 189But such refuge is no sanctuary, it is no supreme refuge. By going to that refuge, you’re not released from all suffering.

Verse 190One gone for refuge to the Buddha, to his teaching and to the Saṅgha, sees the four noble truths with right understanding:

Verse 191suffering, suffering’s origin, suffering’s transcendence, and the noble eightfold path that leads to the stilling of suffering.

Verse 192Such refuge is a sanctuary, it is the supreme refuge. By going to that refuge, you’re released from all suffering.

Rare, and worth celebrating

Verse 193It’s hard to find a thoroughbred man: they’re not born just anywhere. A family where that attentive one is born prospers in happiness.

Verse 194Happy, the arising of Buddhas! Happy, the teaching of Dhamma! Happy is the harmony of the Saṅgha, and the striving of the harmonious is happy.

Verses 195–196When a person venerates the worthy — the Buddha or his disciple, who have transcended proliferation, and have left behind grief and lamentation, quenched, fearing nothing from any quarter — the merit of one venerating such as these, cannot be calculated by anyone, saying it is just this much.

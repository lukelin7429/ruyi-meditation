Dhammapada · Chapter 26

Brahmins

Brāhmaṇavagga

Verses
383–423 (41 verses — the longest chapter in the Dhammapada)
Attribution
A compiled verse anthology, traditionally attributed to the Buddha; verses gathered here by theme rather than narrative setting
Form
A sustained refrain, redefining “brahmin” verse after verse by inner quality rather than birth, closing the entire collection
Length
~7 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart exists in the Chinese Faju Jing (法句經, T210) and related Dharmapada collections, though exact verse-by-verse correspondence is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the refrain itself is simple, but its cumulative, deliberate redefinition of an ancient social category rewards close reading

Why this chapter, twenty-sixth, and last

Every book has to end somewhere, and the Dhammapada ends here: forty-one verses, the longest chapter in the collection by a wide margin, almost all of them built on a single repeated line — “that’s who I declare a brahmin.” Brāhmaṇa was, by birth, one of the most privileged social categories in the world this text came from, inherited rather than earned. This chapter takes that word and, verse after verse, empties it of birth entirely, refilling it instead with cut fetters, ended craving, and a mind that has reached the far shore. It is not a new argument for the Dhammapada to make — the same claim appears elsewhere across the early texts — but there is something exactly right about it closing the book. A collection that opened, chapters ago, on the mind as the forerunner of all things ends by asking, one more time, what actually deserves to be called noble. Its answer, unchanged across forty-one repetitions, is conduct, not birth.

Reading guide

The chapter in one sentence

Verse after verse, the chapter takes the word “brahmin” — a birth-caste, in the ordinary sense of the time — and redefines it entirely around ethical and spiritual accomplishment: cut fetters, ended craving, ended hatred, a mind that has reached the far shore, so that by the chapter’s end the word has nothing left to do with ancestry at all.

Cut the stream, know the uncreated (383–384)

The chapter opens with direct address and imperative: “Cut the stream, striving! Cast aside sensual pleasures, brahmin. Knowing the ending of conditions, know the uncreated, brahmin” (383). Verse 384 follows immediately: a brahmin who has “gone beyond dualistic phenomena” thereby “consciously make[s] an end of all fetters.” The chapter’s method is announced at once — “brahmin” is being addressed as a title to be earned through this striving, not assumed as already possessed.

No crossing over, no crossing back (385–386)

Two verses use the language of a far shore reached and left behind: “one for whom there is no crossing over or crossing back, or crossing over and back; stress-free, detached — that’s who I declare a brahmin” (385), followed immediately by “absorbed, rid of hopes, their task completed, without defilements, arrived at the highest goal” (386). Here the refrain that will carry the rest of the chapter appears for the first time in full: “that’s who I declare a brahmin.”

Sun, moon, aristocrat, brahmin, Buddha (387)

One of the chapter’s most visually striking verses stands alone: “The sun blazes by day, the moon radiates at night, the aristocrat shines in armor, and the brahmin shines in absorption. But all day and all night, the Buddha blazes with glory.” Four familiar forms of shining are named and then, in the final line, all of them are exceeded — a rare moment where the chapter looks past its own redefined brahmin to name something greater still.

What the words actually mean (388)

Verse 388 pauses to do something close to etymology: “A brahmin’s so-called since they’ve banished evil, an ascetic’s so-called since they live a serene life. One who has renounced all stains is said to be a ‘renunciant.’” Three titles — brahmin, ascetic, renunciant — are each traced back to a plain description of conduct, as if the chapter wants to show its method isn’t a stretch: the words themselves, taken seriously, already point to conduct rather than birth.

A brahmin should not be struck (389)

A single warning-verse breaks the pattern: “One should never strike a brahmin, nor should a brahmin retaliate. Woe to the one who hurts a brahmin, and woe for the one who retaliates.” Read against the rest of the chapter, this is not a special legal protection for a caste but a statement about how a person who has actually reached this state relates to violence — and a warning to anyone who would harm such a person.

Nothing better than a mind held back (390–392)

Verse 390: “Nothing is better for a brahmin than to hold their mind back from attachment. From wherever a cruel wish recoils, right there suffering subsides.” Verse 391 turns to the threefold discipline familiar from elsewhere in the Dhammapada — doing no wrong “by body, speech or mind” — and verse 392 asks that whoever teaches this Dhamma be honored “as a brahmin honors the sacred flame,” borrowing the very ritual vocabulary the chapter is otherwise redefining, and turning it toward reverence for the teaching itself.

The thesis: not by matted hair or family (393)

This is the chapter’s central claim, stated as plainly as anywhere in the whole Dhammapada: “Not by matted hair or family, or birth is one a brahmin. Those who are truthful and principled: they are pure, they are brahmins.” Everything before this verse builds toward it, and everything after elaborates it. Verse 394 drives the same point home with an image of mockery: “Why the matted hair, you simpleton, and why the skin of deer? The tangle is inside you, yet you polish up your outsides” — the ascetic’s traditional external markers dismissed as irrelevant if the underlying mind remains tangled.

Robes of rags, meditating alone (395–396)

Verse 395 offers a physical portrait — “robes of rags, lean, their limbs showing veins, meditating alone in the forest” — that’s who is declared a brahmin. Verse 396 returns explicitly to the question of birth: “I don’t call someone a brahmin after the mother’s womb they’re born from. If they still have attachments, they’re just someone who says ‘worthy.’ Having nothing, taking nothing: that’s who I declare a brahmin” — a claim to the honorific title without the corresponding inner freedom is dismissed as empty self-description.

Fetters cut, chains slipped (397–398)

Two verses continue with images of bondage undone: “having cut off all fetters they have no anxiety... they’ve slipped their chains and are detached” (397), and then a specifically equestrian image: “they’ve cut the strap and harness, the halter and bridle too, with cross-bar lifted, they’re awakened” (398) — the full tack of a working animal, removed piece by piece.

Patience as an army (399–400)

Verse 399 names a striking measure of the chapter’s ideal: “Abuse, killing, caging: they endure these without anger. Patience is their powerful army” — the most severe provocations imaginable met without retaliation, patience itself described in military terms as a strength rather than passivity. Verse 400 adds “not irritable or pretentious, dutiful in precepts and observances, tamed, bearing their final body.”

Slipping off like water, like a mustard seed (401)

A single vivid image: “Like water from a lotus leaf, like a mustard seed off a pin-point, sensual pleasures slip off them” — not gripped and then released with effort, but simply incapable of adhering in the first place, the way water beads on a waxed leaf and a mustard seed cannot balance on a needle’s point.

The highest goal, wisdom, the path (402–404)

Verses 402–403 describe direct understanding — “the end of suffering in this life,” a burden put down, deep wisdom, expertise in “the path and what is not the path” — and verse 404 turns to social position: “mixing with neither householders nor the homeless; a migrant with no bastion, few in wishes” — belonging fully to neither the lay world nor even the settled monastic one, a figure defined by not needing a fixed place at all.

No violence, no fighting, no grasping (405–406)

Two verses concern nonviolence directly: laying “aside violence against creatures firm and frail; not killing or making others kill” (405), and remaining “not fighting among those who fight, quenched among those who have taken up arms, not grasping among those who grasp” (406) — a description of standing apart from conflict even while remaining present within a contentious world.

Greed, hate, conceit discarded (407)

“They’ve discarded greed and hate, along with conceit and contempt, like a mustard seed off the point of a pin” — the same mustard-seed image from verse 401 returns, now applied to the three root defilements directly.

True words, nothing stolen (408–409)

Verse 408 turns to speech: words that are “polished, informative, and true,” that “don’t offend anyone.” Verse 409 turns to honesty in action: someone who “doesn’t steal anything in the world, long or short, fine or coarse, beautiful or ugly” — an unusually exhaustive list, covering every possible category of object, to close off any imagined exception.

No hope, no clinging (410–411)

“They have no hope in this world or the next; with no need for hope, detached” (410), and “they have no clinging, knowledge has freed them of indecision, they’ve arrived at the objective, freedom from death” (411) — hope itself, ordinarily treated as virtuous, here named as one more attachment to release.

Beyond both merit and wickedness (412–413)

Verse 412 makes one of the chapter’s more startling claims: “They’ve escaped clinging to both good and bad deeds; sorrowless, stainless, pure” — not merely avoiding wickedness but having gone beyond attachment to merit itself, since even clinging to one’s own good deeds is, in this analysis, still clinging. Verse 413 follows with an image of unclouded purity: “pure as the spotless moon, clear and undisturbed, they’ve ended relish for rebirth.”

Crossing the grueling swamp (414)

A single, image-rich verse: “They’ve got past this grueling swamp of delusion, transmigration. Meditating in stillness, free of indecision, they have crossed over to the far shore. They’re quenched by not grasping” — the swamp of continued wandering through rebirth, and the far shore reached by stillness rather than struggle.

Sensual stimulation and craving, both given up (415–416)

Two nearly parallel verses close out the chapter’s middle section: one who has “given up sensual stimulations, and gone forth from lay life,” ending “rebirth in the sensual realm” (415), and one who has “given up craving, and gone forth from lay life,” ending “craving to be reborn” (416) — the same structure applied first to sensual pleasure specifically and then to craving in its broadest sense.

Every yoke thrown off (417–418)

Verse 417 names two yokes specifically — the human and the heavenly — “unyoked from all yokes” being the state declared a brahmin; even a fortunate rebirth among the gods is, in this analysis, still a yoke to be thrown off. Verse 418 adds: “giving up desire and discontent, they’re cooled and free of attachments; a hero, master of the whole world.”

Knowing the passing and rebirth of all beings (419–420)

Two verses describe a specific, exalted knowledge: understanding “the passing away and rebirth of all beings” while remaining “unattached, holy, awakened” (419), and a destiny that “gods, centaurs, and humans don’t know” — belonging only to “the perfected ones with defilements ended” (420). Even the celestial beings of Indian cosmology, the verse insists, cannot perceive where such a person has gone.

Nothing before, after, or between (421)

“They have nothing before or after, or even in between. Having nothing, taking nothing” — a return to the language of verse 396, now stated across the entirety of time rather than a single life.

Captain of the herd, great seer (422)

“Captain of the herd, excellent hero, great seer and victor; unstirred, washed, awakened” — martial and leadership imagery gathered into a single verse, an unusually forceful, almost triumphant register just before the chapter’s close.

The closing verse, and the collection's closing words (423)

The Dhammapada’s final verse gathers several threads at once: “One who knows their past lives, sees heaven and places of loss, and has attained the end of rebirth; a sage of perfect insight at the summit of spiritual perfection: that’s who I declare a brahmin.” It is, fittingly, the fullest and most complete of the forty-one variations — past, present, and future, insight and its summit, all named together in the very last lines of a text that has spent an entire chapter, and in some sense an entire book, asking what actually deserves to be honored. In the source manuscript, this verse is followed directly by a brief closing line that is not itself part of the poetry: “The Sayings of the Dhamma are complete.” It is a plain, unadorned way for a text with no narrator, no frame, and no ending scene of its own to simply stop.

Key terms

brāhmaṇa — ordinarily a hereditary priestly caste; the word this entire chapter takes and, through forty-one repetitions, redefines around cut fetters and ended craving rather than birth.
“that’s who I declare a brahmin” — the chapter’s governing refrain, appearing (in slightly varying phrasing) after most of its forty-one verses.
verse 393 — the chapter’s clearest single statement of its thesis: “Not by matted hair or family, or birth is one a brahmin. Those who are truthful and principled: they are pure, they are brahmins.”
“escaped clinging to both good and bad deeds” — verse 412’s claim that the ideal here goes beyond avoiding wickedness, releasing attachment even to one’s own merit.
the human yoke and the heavenly yoke — named together in verse 417: even a fortunate rebirth among the gods is treated as one more yoke to be thrown off, not a final destination.

The text

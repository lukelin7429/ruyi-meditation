Dhammapada · Chapter 17

Anger

Kodhavagga

Verses
221–234 (fourteen verses)
Attribution
A compiled verse anthology with no narrative frame, traditionally attributed to the Buddha
Form
Direct ethical exhortation built on two similes — the runaway chariot and the coin of pure gold — closing with a triad on restraint of body, speech, and mind
Length
~3 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart exists in the Chinese Faju Jing (法句經, T210) and related Dharmapada collections, though exact verse-by-verse correspondence is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — plain ethical exhortation, though verses 227–228 reward a second look

Why this chapter, seventeenth

Kodhavagga opens with a command — give up anger — and spends the rest of its fourteen verses explaining exactly what that takes, offering along the way two of the Dhammapada’s most durable images. The first is a chariot lurching out of control: the one who reins it in before it crashes, the text says, is the only person who deserves to be called a charioteer, since anyone can hold the reins when the horses are calm. The second is a coin of pure, unadulterated gold, held up as the closest thing this chapter offers to a portrait of a person beyond reproach — immediately followed by the sober admission that such praise is vanishingly rare, since “no-one in the world escapes blame.”

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Anger is not managed by suppressing it after the fact but by the same disciplined restraint that governs an unruly chariot — and the chapter measures that restraint not by whether a person ever draws criticism (no one does escape it) but by the quality of conduct they maintain regardless.

Giving up anger: the charioteer (221–222)

The opening verse states the goal without any softening: give up anger, get rid of conceit, escape every fetter — since suffering cannot touch one who clings to nothing, not even to “name and form” (221). Verse 222 supplies the chapter’s most famous image: “when anger surges like a lurching chariot, keep it in check. That’s what I call a charioteer; others just hold the reins.” The point is precise — anyone can hold reins when the road is smooth and the horses calm; the real test, and the real skill, is checking a chariot that has already begun to lurch out of control.

Three remedies, and the reward (223–224)

Verse 223 pairs four unwholesome states with their specific antidotes: defeat anger with kindness, villainy with virtue, stinginess with giving, and lies with truth — a structure recalling the paired contrasts found elsewhere across the Dhammapada. Verse 224 narrows this to three practical means — speaking the truth, not getting angry, and giving something when asked, even if only a little — and makes an unusually concrete promise: by these three means alone, “you may enter the presence of the gods.”

Restraint and its result (225–226)

Two brief verses widen the frame from anger specifically to a settled, harmless way of life. Harmless sages, restrained in body, are said to go to “the state that does not pass, where there is no sorrow” (225) — a description of the deathless, nibbāna. Verse 226 adds the discipline behind that state: constant wakefulness, practice by night and day, and a mind focused only on extinguishment, until defilements themselves come to an end.

On blame, praise, and a coin of pure gold (227–230)

This section opens with a direct address to someone named Atula, and a piece of plain, almost consoling realism: “it’s always been this way... it’s not just today” — people are blamed for silence, blamed for talking too much, and blamed even when they speak exactly right, “no-one in the world escapes blame” (227). Verse 228 generalizes the point into a small, quietly startling claim: there never was, never will be, and is not now, anyone who is wholly praised or wholly blamed. Only after establishing that no one is immune from criticism does the chapter offer its closest thing to an ideal: a sage of impeccable conduct, endowed with ethics and wisdom, watched day after day by discerning people, is compared to a pendant of pure, unadulterated Black Plum River gold — gold so refined that no one is in a position to find fault with it — praised, the text adds, even by the gods and by the Divinity itself (229–230).

The threefold restraint (231–234)

The chapter closes with a tight, formulaic triad, applying an identical structure to each of the three doors of action in turn: guard against ill-tempered deeds and be restrained in body (231); guard against ill-tempered words and be restrained in speech (232); guard against ill-tempered thoughts and be restrained in mind (233). The final verse draws the three together into a single description: someone attentive, restrained in body, restrained also in speech, restrained too in thought — “restrained in every way” (234). What began as a command to give up anger ends as a complete, three-part discipline covering everything a person does, says, and thinks.

Key terms

kodha — “anger,” the word that gives the chapter its name and the target of its opening command.
the charioteer — the image in verse 222: not the one who merely holds calm reins, but the one who checks a chariot already lurching out of control.
Jambūnada gold — the “Black Plum River gold” of verse 230, a byword in Indian literature for gold of the highest, most unadulterated purity.
Atula — the person directly addressed in verse 227; the commentarial tradition identifies him as a lay follower, though the bare verse gives no further narrative detail.
the threefold restraint — guarding body, speech, and mind against ill-tempered deeds, words, and thoughts in turn (231–234).

The text

All fourteen verses, given complete and in order. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Giving up anger: the charioteer

Verse 221Give up anger, get rid of conceit,
and escape every fetter.
Sufferings don’t befall one who has nothing,
not clinging to name and form.

Verse 222When anger surges like a lurching chariot,
keep it in check.
That’s what I call a charioteer;
others just hold the reins.

Three remedies, and the reward

Verse 223Defeat anger with kindness,
villainy with virtue,
stinginess with giving,
and lies with truth.

Verse 224Speak the truth, do not be angry,
and give when asked, if only a little.
By these three means,
you may enter the presence of the gods.

Restraint and its result

Verse 225Those harmless sages,
always restrained in body,
go to the state that does not pass,
where there is no sorrow.

Verse 226Always wakeful,
practicing night and day,
focused only on extinguishment,
their defilements come to an end.

On blame, praise, and a coin of pure gold

Verse 227It’s always been this way, Atula,
it’s not just today.
They blame you when you’re silent,
they blame you when you speak a lot,
and even when you speak just right:
no-one in the world escapes blame.

Verse 228There never was, nor will be,
nor is there today,
someone who is wholly praised
or wholly blamed.

Verses 229–230If, after watching them day in day out,
discerning people praise
that sage of impeccable conduct,
endowed with ethics and wisdom;
like a pendant of Black Plum River gold,
who is worthy to criticize them?
Even the gods praise them,
and by the Divinity, too, they’re praised.

The threefold restraint

Verse 231Guard against ill-tempered deeds,
be restrained in body.
Giving up bad bodily conduct,
conduct yourself well in body.

Verse 232Guard against ill-tempered words,
be restrained in speech.
Giving up bad verbal conduct,
conduct yourself well in speech.

Verse 233Guard against ill-tempered thoughts,
be restrained in mind.
Giving up bad mental conduct,
conduct yourself well in mind.

Verse 234An attentive one is restrained in body
restrained also in speech,
in thought, too, they are restrained:
they are restrained in every way.

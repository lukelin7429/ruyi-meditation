Dhammapada · Chapter 6

The Astute

Paṇḍitavagga

Attribution
One chapter of a compiled verse anthology traditionally attributed to the Buddha; there is no narrative setting, speaker, or occasion given for these verses
Verses
76–89 (14 verses)
Form
Aphoristic verse addressed directly to the reader, built on similes drawn from ordinary crafts and from nature
Length
~3 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart exists in the Chinese Faju Jing (法句經, T210) and related Dharmapada collections, though exact verse-by-verse correspondence is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — direct and image-based, though a few similes reward a second look

Why this chapter

Paṇḍitavagga is the deliberate mirror of the chapter before it. Where Bālavagga describes the fool who resents correction and cannot tell a good friend from a bad one, this chapter opens with almost the same scene turned inside out: someone points out your faults, and instead of resentment, you treat them “as a guide to a hidden treasure.” From that single reversal, the chapter builds outward — through friendship, self-discipline, imperturbability, and finally the rare few who actually complete the crossing to what the verses call “the far shore” — into a portrait of the astute person that sets up the even more concentrated portrait of the arahant in the chapter that follows.

Reading guide

A critic worth seeking out

The chapter's opening image is one of the most striking in the whole Dhammapada: “Regard one who sees your faults as a guide to a hidden treasure. Stay close to one so wise and astute who corrects you when you need it. Sticking close to such an impartial person, things get better, not worse” (v.76). This is not comfortable advice, and it is not meant to be — it asks the reader to actively want out a critic, the way a prospector wants out a vein of ore. The next two verses widen the frame from the individual critic to friendship in general: “Advise and instruct; curb wickedness” (v.77), and the plain, almost proverbial couplet, “Don’t mix with bad friends, nor with the worst of men. Mix with spiritual friends, and with the best of men” (v.78). Read together, these three verses treat good company not as a pleasant accompaniment to practice but as one of its direct causes.

Tamed like water, arrows, and timber

Verse 79 turns inward, describing the astute person's ease and confidence as a direct result of “joy in the teaching.” Then comes one of the chapter's best-known images, verse 80: “Irrigators guide water, fletchers straighten arrows, carpenters carve timber, the astute tame themselves.” Each of the three crafts named works on raw, uncooperative material — water that wants to run wherever gravity takes it, a shaft that wants to warp, a log that is still just a log — and turns it into something useful through patient, specific effort. Self-taming is presented the same way: not a mysterious grace but a craft, learnable and practiced. Notably, this exact verse returns almost unchanged as the closing line of Chapter 10, applied there to a very different context — worth watching for when you reach it.

Unmoved by praise and blame

Three verses in a row build a single, cumulative image of stability. “As the wind cannot stir a solid mass of rock, so too blame and praise do not affect the wise” (v.81). “Like a deep lake, clear and unclouded, so clear are the astute when they hear the teachings” (v.82). And then, extending the same equanimity from hearing teachings to ordinary experience: “Though touched by sadness or happiness, the astute appear neither depressed nor elated” (v.83). The progression is worth noticing — rock, then lake, then a description of the person themselves — moving from an external simile of sheer unmovability to an internal one of clarity, and finally to the astute person's actual bearing under both loss and gain.

Refusing shortcuts

Verse 84 breaks from the run of similes to state something closer to a rule: “Never wish for success by unjust means, for your own sake or that of another, desiring children, wealth, or nation; rather, be virtuous, wise, and just.” It is a deliberately comprehensive list of motives — self-interest, interest in others, offspring, riches, political power — each one ruled out as a justification for acting unjustly. Nothing, the verse insists, is important enough to buy with wrongdoing.

The far shore, and the few who reach it

Verses 85 and 86 form a matched pair on one of the collection's recurring images: “Few are those among humans who cross to the far shore. The rest just run around on the near shore” (v.85), followed immediately by, “When the teaching is well explained, those who practice accordingly will cross over Death’s dominion so hard to pass” (v.86). The crossing is not automatic and not guaranteed by hearing the teaching alone — it depends on practicing “accordingly.” The image of a near shore crowded with activity and a far shore reached by only a few gives the whole chapter, up to this point mostly about personal qualities, a much larger horizon.

Renunciation and the finished path

The chapter's final movement (v.87–89) turns explicitly toward renunciation: “Rid of dark qualities, an astute person should develop the bright. Leaving home behind for the seclusion so hard to enjoy, try to find satisfaction there, having left behind sensual pleasures. Owning nothing, an astute person would cleanse themselves of mental corruptions.” The closing verse (v.89) then describes the result in language that anticipates the very next chapter almost word for word: minds “rightly developed in the awakening factors,” free of grasping, “with defilements ended, brilliant, they are quenched in this world.” Paṇḍitavagga, in other words, does not just describe a wise person — by its last verse, it has quietly described someone who has arrived.

Key terms

paṇḍita — “astute” or “wise,” the chapter's title term and its recurring subject; distinguished throughout from the bāla, or fool, of the previous chapter.
the three crafts — irrigating, fletching, and carpentry (v.80), each a simile for the deliberate, learnable work of self-taming; the same simile returns to close Chapter 10.
“the far shore” — a standard image (v.85–86) for liberation, contrasted with the crowded “near shore” of ordinary, unreflective activity.
awakening factors — the qualities named in the closing verse (v.89) as rightly developed in one who is “quenched”; a set of seven qualities (among them mindfulness and equanimity) treated at length elsewhere in the canon.
“quenched” — a standard rendering for the extinguishing of defilement at the root of the word nibbāna, used here (v.89) as the chapter's final word on where this path leads.

The text

All fourteen verses of Paṇḍitavagga, complete. Short, closely related verses are grouped under a single verse marker where they form one continuous thought. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A critic worth seeking out

Verse 76Regard one who sees your faults as a guide to a hidden treasure. Stay close to one so wise and astute who corrects you when you need it. Sticking close to such an impartial person, things get better, not worse.

Verse 77Advise and instruct; curb wickedness: for you shall be loved by the good, and disliked by the bad.

Verse 78Don’t mix with bad friends, nor with the worst of men. Mix with spiritual friends, and with the best of men.

Tamed like water, arrows, and timber

Verse 79Through joy in the teaching you sleep at ease, with clear and confident heart. An astute person always delights in the teaching proclaimed by the Noble One.

Verse 80Irrigators guide water, fletchers straighten arrows, carpenters carve timber, the astute tame themselves.

Unmoved by praise and blame

Verse 81As the wind cannot stir a solid mass of rock, so too blame and praise do not affect the wise.

Verse 82Like a deep lake, clear and unclouded, so clear are the astute when they hear the teachings.

Verse 83True persons give up everything, they don’t cajole for the things they desire. Though touched by sadness or happiness, the astute appear neither depressed nor elated.

Refusing shortcuts

Verse 84Never wish for success by unjust means, for your own sake or that of another, desiring children, wealth, or nation; rather, be virtuous, wise, and just.

The far shore, and the few who reach it

Verses 85–86Few are those among humans who cross to the far shore. The rest just run around on the near shore.

When the teaching is well explained, those who practice accordingly will cross over Death’s dominion so hard to pass.

Renunciation and the finished path

Verses 87–88Rid of dark qualities, an astute person should develop the bright. Leaving home behind for the seclusion so hard to enjoy, try to find satisfaction there, having left behind sensual pleasures. Owning nothing, an astute person would cleanse themselves of mental corruptions.

Verse 89Those whose minds are rightly developed in the awakening factors; who, letting go of attachments, delight in not grasping: with defilements ended, brilliant, they are quenched in this world.

Dhammapada · Chapter 15

Happiness

Sukhavagga

Verses
197–208 (12 verses)
Attribution
A compiled verse anthology, traditionally attributed to the Buddha, gathered here rather than delivered as a single narrated occasion
Form
Twelve verses distinguishing true, deeper happiness from its ordinary substitutes, closing on good company and the value of association with the wise
Length
~3–4 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart exists in the Chinese Faju Jing (法句經, T210) and related Dharmapada collections, though exact verse-by-verse correspondence is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — among the most accessible and quotable chapters in the collection

Why this chapter, fifteenth

The Sukhavagga asks what happiness actually is, once the obvious candidates — victory, wealth, sensual satisfaction — are set aside. Its answer arrives as a series of refrains: living with love among the hostile, health among the ailing, contentment among the greedy, and finally naming health, contentment, trust, and extinguishment as the four things that outrank every ordinary substitute for happiness people usually reach for instead.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

True happiness isn’t the absence of a hostile, sick, or grasping world around you, but the absence of hatred, affliction, and anxious craving within you regardless of what surrounds you — and the deepest happiness of all, extinguishment, is available only once victory and defeat, and the possessions people usually chase, are set down entirely.

Living well among the difficult

The chapter opens with three verses sharing an identical structure, each naming a difficult surrounding condition and a way of living that doesn’t depend on that condition changing: “let us live so very happily, loving among the hostile. Among hostile humans, let us live with love” (197); “let us live so very happily, healthy among the ailing. Among ailing humans let us live healthily” (198); “let us live so very happily, content among the greedy. Among greedy humans, let us live content” (199). The pattern is deliberate: happiness, in each case, is not a report on the environment but a description of one’s own inner state within it — love that doesn’t require being loved back, health of mind amid others’ affliction, contentment untouched by the greed around it. Verse 200 completes the set with a different but related point: “let us live so very happily, we who have nothing. We shall feed on rapture, like the gods of streaming radiance” — happiness that has nothing to do with possessions at all, sustained instead by an inner rapture the verse compares to the food of a class of luminous deities.

Beyond victory and defeat

Verse 201 turns from inner qualities to a specific worldly pursuit that looks like it should produce happiness but doesn’t: “victory breeds enmity; the defeated sleep badly. The peaceful sleep at ease, having left victory and defeat behind.” Winning creates a loser who resents you; losing creates its own misery; only stepping outside that entire contest produces rest. Verse 202 then names, in parallel clauses, the worst version of several categories at once: “there is no fire like greed, no crime like hate, no suffering like the aggregates, no bliss beyond peace” — greed as the most consuming fire, hate as the worst offense, the five aggregates of body and mind themselves as the deepest suffering, and peace as a bliss nothing surpasses. Verse 203 adds two more superlatives before its conclusion: “hunger is the worst illness, conditions are the worst suffering. When one has known this as it is, extinguishment is the ultimate happiness.”

The best possessions

Verse 204 is the chapter’s most frequently quoted line, a compact ranking of what actually matters: “health is the ultimate blessing; contentment, the ultimate wealth; trust is the ultimate family; extinguishment, the ultimate happiness.” Each clause quietly redefines a category people usually chase through external means — wealth as something to accumulate, family as something to be born into, blessing as something conferred from outside — as instead an internal condition anyone can cultivate. Verse 205 extends the same idea with a taste-based metaphor: “having drunk the nectar of seclusion and the nectar of peace — free of stress, free of evil, drink the joyous nectar of truth,” treating solitude and peace not as deprivations but as genuinely delicious.

The company you keep

The chapter’s closing verses turn, somewhat unexpectedly, to companionship. “It’s good to see the noble ones, staying with them is always good. Were you not to see fools, you’d always be happy” (206) is followed by the chapter’s sharpest image: “for one who consorts with fools grieves long. Painful is living with fools, like being stuck with your enemy. Happy is living with an attentive one, like meeting with your kin” (207) — a fool’s company compared directly to an enemy’s, a wise person’s company to one’s own family. The chapter’s final verse draws the practical conclusion: “therefore: an attentive one, wise and learned, a behemoth of virtue, true to their vows, noble: follow a true and intelligent person such as this, as the moon tracks the path of the stars” (208) — the moon here not overtaking the stars but steadily, reliably keeping to their path, an image of patient, consistent association with good company rather than a single dramatic encounter.

Key terms

sukha — “happiness” or “ease,” the chapter’s namesake, redefined throughout as an inner condition rather than a report on external circumstances.
ābhassara — “of streaming radiance,” the class of luminous gods invoked in verse 200 as feeding on rapture, the image for a happiness that needs no possessions.
khandha — “the aggregates,” named in verse 202 as themselves the deepest suffering: the five components (form, feeling, perception, formations, consciousness) that make up a clung-to sense of self.
nibbāna / extinguishment — named twice in this chapter (203, 204) as “the ultimate happiness,” the deepest of the superlatives the chapter offers.
the moon tracking the stars — the chapter’s closing image (208) for steady, patient association with wise company, rather than a single dramatic encounter.

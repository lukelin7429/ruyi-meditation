Dhammapada · Chapter 24

Craving

Taṇhāvagga

Verses
334–359 (26 verses)
Attribution
A compiled verse anthology, traditionally attributed to the Buddha; verses gathered here by theme rather than narrative setting
Form
The longest chapter so far — a sustained meditation on craving as a creeper to be uprooted, not merely trimmed
Length
~5 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart exists in the Chinese Faju Jing (法句經, T210) and related Dharmapada collections, though exact verse-by-verse correspondence is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the imagery is vivid but the underlying analysis of craving repays slow, repeated reading

Why this chapter, twenty-fourth

Craving, taṇhā, is named in the second noble truth as the origin of suffering, and this chapter is the Dhammapada’s longest sustained treatment of exactly that claim. It is built almost entirely around a single botanical image — craving as a creeper, a vine, a weed that grows back the moment its root is left in the ground — and it refuses, verse after verse, to let the reader settle for managing craving’s symptoms. Trimming the visible growth is explicitly not enough; the whole chapter insists on the root.

Reading guide

The chapter in one sentence

Craving is pictured throughout as a creeper that spreads and chokes if left unchecked, and the chapter’s repeated demand is not to trim its visible growth but to dig up the root entirely — the only way, it insists, that suffering actually stops recurring.

A creeper, a langur, a flood of sorrow (334–336)

The chapter opens with its governing image: “When a man lives heedlessly, craving grows in them like a camel’s foot creeper. They jump from one thing to the next, like a langur greedy for fruit in a forest grove.” Craving is not pictured as a single fixed desire but as restless movement — jumping, as the verse says, the way a monkey leaps between branches chasing fruit. Verse 335 names the cost: “Whoever is beaten by this wretched craving... their sorrow grows, like grass in the rain.” Verse 336 supplies the reversal, one of the chapter’s gentlest images: for whoever prevails over craving instead, “their sorrows fall from them, like a drop from a lotus-leaf” — effortlessly, cleanly, leaving no residue.

Digging up the root (337–338)

Verse 337 is addressed directly to an audience — “I say this to you, good people, all those who have gathered here” — with a direct instruction: “dig up the root of craving, as you’d dig up grass in search of roots.” Verse 338 explains why partial measures fail: “A tree grows back even when cut down, so long as its roots are strong and undamaged; suffering springs up again and again, so long as the tendency to craving is not pulled out.” This is the chapter’s central logic, stated as plainly as it ever will be: cutting the visible trunk changes nothing if the root system underneath is untouched.

Thirty-six streams (339–341)

One of the Dhammapada’s most distinctive images follows: “A person of low views in whom the thirty-six streams that flow to pleasure are mighty, is swept away by lustful thoughts.” The number is traditionally explained as a classification of craving across the senses, both internal and external, multiplying out to thirty-six distinct channels — but the verse doesn’t require knowing the exact taxonomy to land: craving is not one stream but many, converging from every direction on anyone without the wisdom to hold them back. Verse 340 makes the remedy explicit: “Seeing this weed that has been born, cut the root with wisdom” — wisdom, not willpower or avoidance alone, is what actually severs the root.

Bound like a trapped rabbit (342–347)

A run of verses depicts craving as literal captivity: “People governed by thirst, crawl about like a trapped rabbit. Bound and fettered, for a long time they return to pain time and again” (342–343, repeated nearly word for word, underscoring the point by repetition). Verse 344 adds a paradox — someone who sets out for the ascetic life and then “runs right back to the jungle they left behind,” free but running to bondage. Verses 345–346 make an important distinction about what actually restrains a person: “the attentive say that shackle is not strong that’s made of iron, wood, or knots,” but obsession with jewelry, partners, and children — that is “a strong shackle... hard to escape.” Verse 347 closes the sequence with the image of a spider: “Besotted by lust they fall into the stream, like a spider caught in the web she wove” — craving as a trap of one’s own construction.

Letting go of all three times (348)

A single compact verse widens the frame beyond craving’s objects to time itself: “Let go of the past, let go of the future, let go of the present, having gone beyond rebirth. With your heart freed in every respect, you’ll not come again to rebirth and old age.” It is one of the most quoted verses in the whole Dhammapada precisely because of this scope — not one craving released, but the entire structure of clinging to time.

Absorption, wisdom, and one who has arrived (349–352)

Verses 349–350 contrast a mind “churned by thoughts, very lustful, focusing on beauty” against “one who loves to calm their thoughts, developing perception of ugliness” — a reference to a specific meditative practice of contemplating the body’s decay to counter obsessive desire. Verses 351–352 then describe the person who has actually finished this work: “rid of craving, free of blemish, having struck down the arrows flying to future lives, this bag of bones is their last” — someone who will not be reborn again.

“I am the vanquisher of all” (353)

Verse 353 shifts abruptly into the first person, in a declaration widely recognized elsewhere in the canon as the Buddha’s own words spoken just after his awakening, on the road to Varanasi: “I am the vanquisher of all, the knower of all, unsullied in the midst of all things. I’ve given up all, freed in the ending of craving. Since I know for myself, whose follower should I be?” The same declaration, in the same circumstances, appears in the account of the Buddha’s encounter with the wanderer Upaka shortly after his enlightenment — its presence here roots the entire chapter’s teaching on craving in the Buddha’s own claimed experience of ending it.

The gift that surpasses all gifts (354)

Verse 354 pivots from personal declaration to a more general claim, in four parallel lines: the gift of the teaching surpasses all other gifts, its taste surpasses all other tastes, its joy surpasses all other joys, and “the ending of craving surmounts all suffering” — the final line functioning as the summary of the entire chapter compressed into a single clause.

Weeds are the bane of crops (355–359)

The chapter closes with a five-verse refrain returning to agricultural imagery: greed, hate, delusion, and desire are each, in turn, called the bane of “these folk,” as weeds are the bane of crops, and each verse concludes that a gift given to someone free of that particular fault is therefore “so very fruitful.” Verse 355 stands slightly apart, warning that riches ruin the simpleton who craves wealth, harming “themselves and others” — a final, concrete instance of the same uncontrolled growth the whole chapter has been describing.

Key terms

taṇhā — “craving,” “thirst”: named as the origin of suffering in the second noble truth, and pictured throughout this chapter as a creeper or vine that regrows unless its root is pulled out entirely.
the thirty-six streams — a traditional classification of craving flowing through the senses, named in verse 339 as converging on anyone without the wisdom to hold them back.
“a shackle... made of iron, wood, or knots” — the contrast drawn in verses 345–346 between literal restraints, judged weak, and attachment to possessions and relationships, judged the truly strong shackle.
“I am the vanquisher of all” — verse 353’s first-person declaration, traditionally identified with the Buddha’s own words spoken just after his awakening, in his roadside encounter with the wanderer Upaka.

The text

All twenty-six verses of the Taṇhāvagga, complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 334When a man lives heedlessly,
craving grows in them like a camel’s foot creeper.
They jump from one thing to the next, like a langur
greedy for fruit in a forest grove.

Verse 335Whoever is beaten by this wretched craving,
this attachment to the world,
their sorrow grows,
like grass in the rain.

Verse 336But whoever prevails over this wretched craving,
so hard to get over in the world,
their sorrows fall from them,
like a drop from a lotus-leaf.

Verse 337I say this to you, good people,
all those who have gathered here:
dig up the root of craving,
as you’d dig up grass in search of roots.
Don’t let Māra break you again and again,
like a stream breaking a reed.

Verse 338A tree grows back even when cut down,
so long as its roots are strong and undamaged;
suffering springs up again and again,
so long as the tendency to craving is not pulled out.

Verse 339A person of low views
in whom the thirty-six streams
that flow to pleasure are mighty,
is swept away by lustful thoughts.

Verse 340The streams flow everywhere;
a weed springs up and remains.
Seeing this weed that has been born,
cut the root with wisdom.

Verse 341A personage’s joys
flow from senses and cravings.
Seekers of happiness, bent on pleasure,
continue to be reborn and grow old.

Verses 342–343People governed by thirst,
crawl about like a trapped rabbit.
Bound and fettered, for a long time
they return to pain time and again.

People governed by thirst,
crawl about like a trapped rabbit.
That’s why one who longs for dispassion
should dispel thirst.

Verse 344Unsnarled, they set out for the jungle,
then they run right back to the jungle they left behind.
Just look at this individual!
Freed, they run to bondage.

Verses 345–346The attentive say that shackle is not strong
that’s made of iron, wood, or knots.
But obsession with jeweled earrings,
concern for your partners and children:

this, say the attentive, is a strong shackle
dragging the indulgent down, hard to escape.
Having cut this one too they go forth,
unconcerned, having given up sensual pleasures.

Verse 347Besotted by lust they fall into the stream,
like a spider caught in the web she wove.
The attentive proceed, having cut this one too,
unconcerned, having given up all suffering.

Verse 348Let go of the past, let go of the future,
let go of the present, having gone beyond rebirth.
With your heart freed in every respect,
you’ll not come again to rebirth and old age.

Verses 349–350For a personage churned by thoughts,
very lustful, focusing on beauty,
their craving grows and grows,
tying them with a stout bond.

But one who loves to calm their thoughts,
developing perception of ugliness, ever mindful,
will surely eliminate that craving,
cutting off the bonds of Māra.

Verse 351One who is confident, unafraid,
rid of craving, free of blemish,
having struck down the arrows flying to future lives,
this bag of bones is their last.

Verse 352Rid of craving, free of grasping,
expert in the definition of terms,
knowing the correct
structure and sequence of syllables,
they are said to be one who bears their final body,
one of great wisdom, a great person.

Verse 353I am the vanquisher of all, the knower of all,
unsullied in the midst of all things.
I’ve given up all, freed in the ending of craving.
Since I know for myself, whose follower should I be?

Verse 354The gift of the teaching surmounts all other gifts;
the taste of the teaching surmounts all other tastes;
the joy of the teaching surmounts all other joys;
the ending of craving surmounts all suffering.

Verse 355Riches ruin a simpleton,
but not a seeker of the far shore.
From craving for wealth, a simpleton
ruins themselves and others.

Verses 356–359Weeds are the bane of crops,
but greed is these folk’s bane.
That’s why a gift to one rid of greed
is so very fruitful.

Weeds are the bane of crops,
but hate is these folk’s bane.
That’s why a gift to one rid of hate
is so very fruitful.

Weeds are the bane of crops,
but delusion is these folk’s bane.
That’s why a gift to one rid of delusion
is so very fruitful.

Weeds are the bane of crops,
but desire is these folk’s bane.
That’s why a gift to one rid of desire
is so very fruitful.

Why this chapter, the first

The Dhammapada opens with its title in miniature: yamaka means “pair,” and every one of this chapter’s ten units states a flaw and then, in an answering verse built from nearly identical language, states its cure. The very first pair is the most famous lines in the whole collection — mind precedes all things, and a corrupt mind brings suffering the way a cart-wheel follows the ox that pulls it, while a peaceful mind brings happiness the way a shadow never leaves its owner. Everything that follows, across all 423 verses, elaborates variations on this one architecture: name the failing, then name its answer.

Reading guide

The chapter in one sentence

Ten paired verses, each stating a flawed mental state and then its purified opposite in matching language, opening with the Dhammapada’s single most quoted claim: that mind precedes and shapes everything a person says and does.

Verses 1–2: mind precedes all things

The chapter’s opening pair states the collection’s governing premise. Verse 1: “Intention shapes experiences; intention is first, they’re made by intention. If with corrupt intent you speak or act, suffering follows you, like a wheel, the ox’s foot.” Verse 2 repeats the first two lines nearly verbatim, then reverses the outcome: “If with pure intent you speak or act, happiness follows you like a shadow that never leaves.” The two similes are carefully chosen and asymmetrical — a wheel dragged by a labouring ox is heavy and grinding, while a shadow is weightless and simply present. Suffering is depicted as a burden that must be pulled; happiness, as something that quietly accompanies. Nothing else in the chapter, or arguably the whole Dhammapada, is quoted more often than these two verses.

Verses 3–6: the grudge, and how it ends

Verses 3 and 4 share an identical opening line — “They abused me, they hit me! They beat me, they robbed me!” — the interior monologue of someone nursing an injury. For those who keep rehearsing it, verse 3 says, “hatred is never laid to rest”; for those who let it go, verse 4 says, it is. Verse 5 then states the principle behind the pair directly, without the paired structure, in the chapter’s second most celebrated line: “For never is hatred laid to rest by hate, it’s laid to rest by love: this is an ancient teaching.” Verse 6 adds a practical note: most people don’t realize they are actively creating conflict this way, but those who do understand should restrain themselves, since that restraint is what actually settles disputes.

Verses 7–8: what Māra can and cannot strike down

A pair built on a single image: someone who dwells on the attractive, whose senses are unguarded, who eats without moderation and lacks energy, is struck down by Māra “like the wind, a feeble tree.” Someone who instead contemplates the unattractive aspects of experience, restrains the senses, eats moderately, and is faithful and energetic, cannot be struck down — “like the wind, a rocky mountain.” The contrast is architectural: a tree bends and falls to the same wind a mountain simply ignores.

Verses 9–10: who may wear the robe

A pointed pair about religious appearance versus substance. One who is not free of moral stains has no business wearing the ochre robe of a renunciant, “bereft of self-control and of truth”; one who has purged those stains, “steady in ethics, possessed of self-control and of truth,” is truly worthy of it. The robe itself does nothing — it is only ever a marker of what should already be true of the person wearing it.

Verses 11–14: essentials, and what seeps in

Verses 11 and 12 pair confusion about what matters — “thinking the inessential is essential, seeing the essential as inessential” — against clear discernment of the same, calling wrong thoughts and right thoughts, respectively, each person’s “habitat.” Verses 13 and 14 then supply the chapter’s best-known architectural simile: rain seeps into a poorly roofed house exactly as lust seeps into an undeveloped mind, while a well-roofed house, like a well-developed mind, keeps it out entirely. The image works because it locates the vulnerability in preparation, not willpower in the moment of the storm.

Verses 15–18: grief and delight, here and hereafter

Two back-to-back pairs describe an evildoer who grieves both now and later, “seeing their own corrupt deeds,” against one who does good and rejoices in both places, “seeing their own pure deeds” (15–16); then the same structure intensified into torment and delight, with the evildoer’s torment growing worse still “when gone to a bad place” (17–18). The repetition across four verses is deliberate: the chapter is building toward the claim that ethical consequence isn’t only external circumstance but a felt, present-tense experience of one’s own conduct.

Verses 19–20: reciting without living it

The closing pair turns from ethics to practice itself. Someone who recites a great deal of scripture but doesn’t act on it is “like a cowherd who counts the cattle of others” — tending something that isn’t theirs to keep, missing the actual blessings of the ascetic life. Someone who recites little but lives in line with the teaching, who has given up greed, hate, and delusion and grasps at neither this life nor the next, does share those blessings. It is a fitting close to the chapter that opened the whole collection with intention: recitation, like intention, only matters insofar as it shapes what a person actually does.

Key terms

yamaka — “pair”: the chapter’s name, describing its structure of ten matched units, each stating a flaw and then its cure.
mano-pubbaṅgamā dhammā — the opening phrase of verse 1, “mind precedes all mental states” (rendered here as “intention shapes experiences”), the single most quoted line in the Dhammapada.
Māra — the personification of death and temptation, invoked in verses 7–8 as a force that overcomes the undisciplined the way wind fells a weak tree, but cannot move the disciplined, who stand like a mountain.
the ochre robe — the renunciant’s garment, discussed in verses 9–10 as worthless without the ethical substance it is meant to signal.
“this is an ancient teaching” — the closing phrase of verse 5, marking the claim that hatred ends only through love as a principle older than any single teacher, not a novel doctrine.

The text

All twenty verses of the Yamakavagga, in their ten traditional pairs. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verses 1–2Intention shapes experiences; intention is first, they’re made by intention. If with corrupt intent you speak or act, suffering follows you, like a wheel, the ox’s foot.

Intention shapes experiences; intention is first, they’re made by intention. If with pure intent you speak or act, happiness follows you like a shadow that never leaves.

Verses 3–4“They abused me, they hit me! They beat me, they robbed me!” For those who bear such a grudge, hatred is never laid to rest.

“They abused me, they hit me! They beat me, they robbed me!” For those who bear no such grudge, hatred is laid to rest.

Verses 5–6For never is hatred laid to rest by hate, it’s laid to rest by love: this is an ancient teaching.

When others do not understand, let us, who do understand this, restrain ourselves in this regard; for that is how conflicts are laid to rest.

Verses 7–8Those who contemplate the beautiful, their faculties unrestrained, immoderate in eating, lazy, lacking energy: Māra strikes them down like the wind, a feeble tree.

Those who contemplate the ugly, their faculties well-restrained, eating in moderation, faithful and energetic: Māra cannot strike them down, like the wind, a rocky mountain.

Verses 9–10One who, not free of stains themselves, would wear the robe stained in ocher, bereft of self-control and of truth: they are not worthy of the ocher robe.

One who’s purged all their stains, steady in ethics, possessed of self-control and of truth, they are truly worthy of the ocher robe.

Verses 11–12Thinking the inessential is essential, seeing the essential as inessential; they don’t realize the essential, for wrong thoughts are their habitat.

Having known the essential as essential, and the inessential as inessential; they realize the essential, for right thoughts are their habitat.

Verses 13–14Just as rain seeps into a poorly roofed house, lust seeps into an undeveloped mind.

Just as rain doesn’t seep into a well roofed house, lust doesn’t seep into a well developed mind.

Verses 15–16Here they grieve, hereafter they grieve, an evildoer grieves in both places. They grieve and fret, seeing their own corrupt deeds.

Here they rejoice, hereafter they rejoice, one who does good rejoices in both places. They rejoice and celebrate, seeing their own pure deeds.

Verses 17–18Here they’re tormented, hereafter they’re tormented, an evildoer is tormented in both places. They’re tormented thinking of bad things they’ve done; when gone to a bad place, they’re tormented all the more.

Here they delight, hereafter they delight, one who does good delights in both places. They delight thinking of good things they’ve done; when gone to a good place, they delight all the more.

Verses 19–20Much though they may recite scripture, if a negligent person does not apply them, then, like a cowherd who counts the cattle of others, they miss out on the blessings of the ascetic life.

Little though they may recite scripture, if they live in line with the teaching, having given up greed, hate, and delusion, with deep understanding and heart well freed, not grasping to this life or the next, they share in the blessings of the ascetic life.

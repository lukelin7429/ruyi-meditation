Dhammapada · Chapter 19

The Just

Dhammaṭṭhavagga

Verses
256–272 (seventeen verses)
Attribution
A compiled verse anthology with no narrative frame, traditionally attributed to the Buddha
Form
Almost entirely paired contrast — each verse rejecting a superficial marker of a spiritual status in favor of a substantive one
Length
~3 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart exists in the Chinese Faju Jing (法句經, T210) and related Dharmapada collections, though exact verse-by-verse correspondence is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — each verse rests on a contrast that rewards close attention

Why this chapter, nineteenth

Dhammaṭṭhavagga has one of the most consistent structures anywhere in the Dhammapada: verse after verse, it names a respected status — being just, being astute, being a senior, being holy, being an ascetic, a mendicant, a sage, a noble one — and then, in the very same breath, denies that its usual, superficial marker actually earns it. Grey hair does not make an elder. A shaved head does not make an ascetic. Reciting scripture does not make someone who has “memorized the teaching.” Each denial is immediately followed by what the chapter insists really does qualify: not the appearance, but the underlying quality of conduct, wisdom, or restraint.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

None of the titles this chapter examines — just, astute, senior, holy, ascetic, mendicant, sage, noble — is earned by its usual outward sign; each is earned only by a specific, substantive quality of conduct or understanding that the chapter names explicitly, one status at a time.

What makes one just (256–257)

The chapter opens by denying that hasty judgment makes anyone just: “an astute person evaluates both what is pertinent and what is irrelevant” (256). Verse 257 restates the positive case: a wise one who judges others without haste, justly and impartially, acting as “that guardian of the law,” is the one truly said to be just.

Astute, and one who has "memorized the teaching" (258–259)

Two related denials follow. Talking a lot does not make one an astute scholar; rather, “one who is secure, free of enmity and fear, is said to be astute” (258). And reciting a lot does not make one “a person who has memorized the teaching” — someone who directly sees the teaching after hearing only a little, and can therefore never forget it, is the one who has truly memorized it (259). Both verses point at the same gap: between quantity of speech or recitation, and quality of genuine understanding.

A true senior (260–261)

“You don’t become a senior by getting some grey hairs; for one ripe only in age is said to have aged in vain” (260) — one of the chapter’s bluntest lines. What actually qualifies someone as a senior, verse 261 answers, is being truthful and principled, harmless, restrained and self-controlled, attentive, and purged of stains.

What makes one holy (262–263)

Neither fine speech nor a beautiful appearance makes a person holy, “if they’re jealous, stingy, and devious” (262); only someone who has cut out, dug up at the root, and eradicated exactly those qualities is truly holy (263).

A true ascetic (264–265)

“A liar and breaker of vows is no ascetic just because they shave their head. How on earth can one be an ascetic who’s full of desire and greed?” (264) — a genuinely pointed question. The answer: one who stops all wicked deeds, great and small, precisely because of stopping wicked deeds, is called an ascetic (265).

A true mendicant, and a true sage (266–269)

Simply begging from others does not make a mendicant — “one who has undertaken domestic duties has not yet become a mendicant” (266); what qualifies instead is living a spiritual life, having banished both merit and evil, wandering having appraised the world (267). Nor does merely acting sagelike make a sage while “still confused and ignorant” (268) — the astute one who “holds the scales,” weighing both sides of the world and taking only the best while shunning the bad, is a sage precisely because of that weighing (269).

A true noble one, and the bliss no external practice alone secures (270–272)

“You don’t become a noble one by harming living beings. One harmless towards all living beings is said to be a noble one” (270) — the shortest and starkest of the chapter’s pairs. The closing verses generalize the whole pattern into a single first-person statement: not by precepts and observances, not by much learning, not by meditative immersion, and not even by living in seclusion does the speaker experience “the bliss of renunciation not frequented by ordinary people” (271) — “a mendicant cannot rest confident without attaining the end of defilements” (272). Even the most respectable external practices, the chapter insists to the very end, are not by themselves the thing that matters.

Key terms

dhammaṭṭha — “established in the law,” “just”: the word that gives the chapter its name, defined in verse 257 as judging others without haste, justly and impartially.
samaṇa — “ascetic”: verse 265 defines it not by appearance but by having stopped all wicked deeds, great and small.
bhikkhu — “mendicant”: verse 267 defines it by a spiritual life that has banished both merit and evil, not by the act of begging.
muni — “sage”: verse 269 defines it by weighing both sides of the world and taking only the best.
ariya — “noble one”: verse 270 defines it by harmlessness toward all living beings.

The text

All seventeen verses, given complete and in order. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

What makes one just

Verse 256You don’t become just
by passing hasty judgment.
An astute person evaluates both
what is pertinent and what is irrelevant.

Verse 257A wise one judges others without haste,
justly and impartially;
that guardian of the law
is said to be just.

Astute, and one who has "memorized the teaching"

Verse 258You’re not an astute scholar
just because you speak a lot.
One who is secure, free of enmity and fear,
is said to be astute.

Verse 259You’re not one who has memorized the teaching
just because you recite a lot.
Someone who directly sees the teaching
after hearing only a little
is truly one who has memorized the teaching,
for they can never forget it.

A true senior

Verse 260You don’t become a senior
by getting some grey hairs;
for one ripe only in age,
is said to have aged in vain.

Verse 261One who is truthful and principled,
harmless, restrained, and self-controlled,
attentive, purged of stains,
is said to be a senior.

What makes one holy

Verse 262Not by mere enunciation,
or a beautiful complexion
does a person become holy,
if they’re jealous, stingy, and devious.

Verse 263But if they’ve cut that out,
dug it up at the root, eradicated it,
that wise one, purged of vice,
is said to be holy.

A true ascetic

Verse 264A liar and breaker of vows is no ascetic
just because they shave their head.
How on earth can one be an ascetic
who’s full of desire and greed?

Verse 265One who stops all wicked deeds,
great and small,
because of stopping wicked deeds
is said to be an ascetic.

A true mendicant, and a true sage

Verse 266You don’t become a mendicant
just by begging from others.
One who has undertaken domestic duties
has not yet become a mendicant.

Verse 267But one living a spiritual life,
who has banished both merit and evil,
who wanders having appraised the world,
is said to be a mendicant.

Verse 268You don’t become a sage by being sagelike,
while still confused and ignorant.
The astute one who holds the scales,
taking only the best,

Verse 269and shunning the bad—
that is a sage, and that is how one becomes a sage.
One who sagely weighs both in the world,
is thereby said to be a sage.

A true noble one, and the bliss no external practice alone secures

Verse 270You don’t become a noble one
by harming living beings.
One harmless towards all living beings
is said to be a noble one.

Verses 271–272Not by precepts and observances,
nor by much learning,
nor by meditative immersion,
nor by living in seclusion,
do I experience the bliss of renunciation
not frequented by ordinary people.
A mendicant cannot rest confident
without attaining the end of defilements.

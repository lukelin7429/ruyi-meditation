Dhammapada · Chapter 16

The Beloved

Piyavagga

Verses
209–220 (twelve verses)
Attribution
A compiled verse anthology with no narrative frame, traditionally attributed to the Buddha
Form
An opening warning, then a five-fold refrain stripping away one object of attachment at a time, closing with two positive images
Length
~2 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart exists in the Chinese Faju Jing (法句經, T210) and related Dharmapada collections, though exact verse-by-verse correspondence is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — direct, emotionally plain, no technical vocabulary

Why this chapter, sixteenth

Piyavagga contains what may be the most quietly devastating argument in the whole Dhammapada, and it makes that argument almost entirely by repetition. Five times in a row — verses 212 through 216 — the text states the identical formula, changing only a single word: sorrow and fear spring from this, and one free of this has no sorrow, let alone fear. What changes is the object: what is dear, then attachment, then relishing, then desire, and finally craving itself, the very root the Buddha names elsewhere as the origin of suffering. It is not a subtle argument. It is closer to a hammer, struck five times in the same place.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Sorrow and fear are not free-floating misfortunes that simply happen to a person — this chapter argues that they track, with almost mechanical precision, exactly what a mind holds dear; strip away the holding, and there is nothing left for grief or fear to attach to.

An opening scene of misdirected devotion (209–211)

The chapter opens without preamble, describing someone who applies their energy where it does not belong, neglects what actually needs doing, forgets their own real goal, and clings instead to whatever happens to be dear to them — while resenting others who remain genuinely devoted to their aim (209). The advice that follows is blunt: do not get too close to what you like or dislike, “for not seeing the liked is suffering, and so is seeing the disliked” (210) — both directions of feeling, in other words, carry their own cost. Verse 211 pushes the point further still, to its logical extreme: do not hold anything dear at all, since losing what is loved is painful, and “no ties are found” in one who holds nothing loved or loathed.

The refrain: five words, one formula (212–216)

What follows is the chapter’s structural core, and its best-known passage: the same sentence, restated five times, with a single word substituted each time.

VerseWhat sorrow and fear spring from
212what we hold dear
213attachment
214relishing
215desire
216craving

Read in sequence, the five terms move from the most concrete and relatable case — a person or thing we happen to hold dear — toward increasingly fundamental psychological ground, ending on craving (taṇhā), the very term the second noble truth names as the origin of suffering elsewhere in the canon. Verse 212 alone is probably quoted more often than the rest of the chapter combined, but the repetition is the point: the same insight is being pressed from five separate angles so that no reader can dismiss it as applying only to romantic loss, or only to material things, or only to some other person's particular attachment.

Two images of what is actually worth having (217–220)

Having spent eight verses on what to release, the chapter turns, in its final third, to what remains. Verse 217 describes what genuinely earns a person’s affection from others: not clinging, but conduct — virtue, vision, firm principle, truthfulness, and simply doing what ought to be done. Verse 218 introduces a vivid image: one eager to realize the ineffable, whose mind is no longer bound to sensual pleasure, is said to be “heading upstream” — since the ordinary current of desire runs toward ever more attachment, moving against it takes real and sustained effort. The chapter’s closing image, spanning verses 219 and 220, is its gentlest: a man who returns home after a long absence abroad is joyfully welcomed by family and friends; just so, the text says, a person’s own good deeds welcome them, the way a loving family welcomes home one they love, when they pass from this life to the next. After eight verses on the cost of holding things dear, the chapter ends by pointing to the one kind of belonging — one's own good conduct — that turns out not to betray you.

Key terms

piya — “dear” or “beloved,” the word that gives the chapter its name and the first term substituted into the five-fold refrain.
taṇhā — “craving,” the final and most fundamental term in the refrain sequence (216), and the cause of suffering named in the second noble truth.
paṭisotagāmī — “heading upstream,” the image in verse 218 for a mind moving against the ordinary current of sensual desire.
the refrain — the identical sentence restated across verses 212–216, substituting dear, attachment, relishing, desire, and craving in turn.

The text

All twelve verses, given complete and in order. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

An opening warning

Verse 209Applying yourself where you ought not,
neglecting what you should be doing,
forgetting your goal, you cling to what you hold dear,
jealous of those devoted to their heart’s goal.

Verse 210Don’t ever get too close
to those you like or dislike.
For not seeing the liked is suffering,
and so is seeing the disliked.

Verse 211Therefore don’t hold anything dear,
for it’s bad to lose those you love.
No ties are found in they who
hold nothing loved or loathed.

The refrain

Verse 212Sorrow springs from what we hold dear,
fear springs from what we hold dear;
one free from holding anything dear
has no sorrow, let alone fear.

Verse 213Sorrow springs from attachment,
fear springs from attachment;
one free from attachment
has no sorrow, let alone fear.

Verse 214Sorrow springs from relishing,
fear springs from relishing;
one free from relishing
has no sorrow, let alone fear.

Verse 215Sorrow springs from desire,
fear springs from desire;
one free from desire
has no sorrow, let alone fear.

Verse 216Sorrow springs from craving,
fear springs from craving;
one free from craving
has no sorrow, let alone fear.

Closing images

Verse 217One accomplished in virtue and vision,
firmly principled, and truthful,
doing oneself what ought be done:
that’s who the people love.

Verse 218One eager to realize the ineffable
would be filled with awareness.
Their mind not bound to pleasures of sense,
they’re said to be heading upstream.

Verse 219When a man returns safely
after a long time spent abroad,
family, friends, and loved ones
celebrate his return.

Verse 220Just so, when one who has done good
goes from this world to the next,
their good deeds receive them there,
as family welcomes home one they love.

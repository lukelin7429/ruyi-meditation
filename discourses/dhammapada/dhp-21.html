Dhammapada · Chapter 21

Miscellaneous

Pakiṇṇakavagga

Verses
290–305 (sixteen verses)
Attribution
A compiled verse anthology with no narrative frame, traditionally attributed to the Buddha
Form
A loosely gathered collection without a single unifying theme (hence "Miscellaneous"), including a sixfold recollection refrain and closing praise of solitude
Length
~3 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart exists in the Chinese Faju Jing (法句經, T210) and related Dharmapada collections, though exact verse-by-verse correspondence is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — accessible aphorisms, though verses 294–295 need unpacking

Why this chapter, twenty-first

Pakiṇṇakavagga is exactly what its name promises: a gathering of verses that do not share one single thread the way, say, Kodhavagga shares anger or Dhammaṭṭhavagga shares paired contrast. Instead the chapter moves from a spare calculation about trading a lesser happiness for a greater one, through six near-identical verses on what keeps the Buddha's disciples “always waking up refreshed,” to a pair of genuinely startling verses about a brahmin who has “slain mother and father” and walks on untroubled — verses the tradition reads allegorically rather than literally — and closes on some of the collection's most quoted lines about the value of being alone.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Without one unifying thread, this chapter still returns repeatedly to a single, quietly practical idea: what looks like giving something up — a lesser happiness, company, comfort, even the two extremist views and craving itself in the allegorical brahmin verses — is in each case actually the trade that secures something considerably better.

Trading a lesser happiness for a greater one (290)

The chapter opens with a spare, almost mathematical statement: “if by giving up material happiness one sees abundant happiness, the attentive would give up material happiness, seeing the abundant happiness.” It is less a command than an observation about what a clear-eyed calculation actually recommends.

Whose happiness comes at others' expense (291)

A pointed warning follows: someone “seeking their own happiness by imposing suffering on others” becomes “intimate with enmity,” and precisely because of that intimacy, “they're not freed from enmity.” Happiness built on another's suffering, the verse implies, is not really a happiness secured at all.

What should and shouldn't be done (292–293)

A tight contrast follows the same shape found across many Dhammapada chapters: those who “dump what should be done, and do what should not be done” — the insolent and negligent — find their defilements only growing (292); those who have properly undertaken “constant mindfulness of the body” instead cultivate only what should be done, and being “mindful and aware,” their defilements come to an end (293).

The brahmin who has slain mother and father (294–295)

These two verses are the chapter's strangest and most demanding: a brahmin who has “slain mother and father, and two aristocratic kings,” wiped out “the kingdom with its tax collector” (294) or, in the twin verse, slain “a tiger as the fifth” (295), “walks on untroubled.” Read literally, this would be a shocking endorsement of violence — and the traditional commentary is explicit that it is not meant literally at all. The figures are read allegorically: the “mother” killed is craving, which gives rise to further existence the way a mother gives birth; the “father” is conceit; the two kings represent the views of eternalism and annihilationism; the “kingdom with its tax collector” stands for the sense fields together with the delight that clings to them; and the tiger named as a fifth victim represents one of the remaining obstacles to liberation. On this reading, the brahmin's untroubled walk describes not a murderer but an arahant who has cut through every one of these inner obstacles — the verses' violent surface language deliberately unsettling a listener into asking what is really being described.

The disciples of Gotama: a sixfold recollection (296–301)

Six verses in a row share an identical frame — “the disciples of Gotama always wake up refreshed, who day and night constantly…” — completed a different way each time: recollect the Buddha (296), the teaching (297), the Saṅgha (298), are mindful of the body (299), delight in harmlessness (300), and delight in meditation (301). Six distinct objects of recollection, the same claimed result each time: a mind that wakes up genuinely refreshed rather than merely rested.

Going forth is hard (302)

A rare moment of plain-spoken difficulty: “going forth is hard, it's hard to be happy; life at home is hard too, and painful, it's painful to stay when you've nothing in common. A traveler is a prey to pain, so don't be a traveler, don't be prey to pain.” Neither the household life nor the wandering life is presented as automatically easy — the verse offers no shortcut, only an honest accounting.

Two who are seen, and two who are not (303–304)

One who is “faithful, accomplished in ethics, blessed with fame and wealth, is honored in whatever place they frequent” (303). Verse 304 supplies the chapter's most visual image: “the good shine from afar, like the Himalayan peaks, but the wicked are not seen, like arrows scattered in the night” — virtue as something visible from a great distance, its absence as something that vanishes without a trace.

Alone (305)

The chapter's closing verse is among the most quoted in the entire Dhammapada: “sitting alone, sleeping alone, tirelessly wandering alone; one who tames themselves alone would delight within a forest.” After sixteen verses that touch trade-offs, enmity, mindfulness, an allegorical killing, communal recollection, and the shared difficulty of any way of life, the chapter ends by praising the solitary work no one else can do on a person's behalf.

Key terms

pakiṇṇaka — “scattered” or “miscellaneous,” the word that gives the chapter its name and describes its loosely gathered structure.
the brahmin verses (294–295) — read allegorically by the traditional commentary: mother as craving, father as conceit, the two kings as eternalism and annihilationism, the kingdom and its tax collector as the sense fields and the delight that clings to them.
the sixfold recollection — the Buddha, the teaching, the Saṅgha, mindfulness of the body, harmlessness, and meditation, each completing the identical refrain across verses 296–301.
the solitary refrain (305) — sitting, sleeping, and wandering alone, closing the chapter on the value of the work a practitioner does entirely on their own.

The text

All sixteen verses, given complete and in order. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Trading a lesser happiness for a greater one

Verse 290If by giving up material happiness
one sees abundant happiness,
the attentive would give up material happiness,
seeing the abundant happiness.

Whose happiness comes at others' expense

Verse 291Seeking their own happiness
by imposing suffering on others,
intimate with enmity,
they’re not freed from enmity.

What should and shouldn't be done

Verse 292They dump what should be done,
and do what should not be done.
For the insolent and the negligent,
their defilements only grow.

Verse 293Those that have properly undertaken
constant mindfulness of the body,
don’t cultivate what should not be done,
but always do what should be done.
Mindful and aware,
their defilements come to an end.

The brahmin who has slain mother and father

Verse 294Having slain mother and father,
and two aristocratic kings,
and having wiped out the kingdom with its tax collector,
the brahmin walks on untroubled.

Verse 295Having slain mother and father,
and two prosperous kings,
and a tiger as the fifth,
the brahmin walks on untroubled.

The disciples of Gotama: a sixfold recollection

Verse 296The disciples of Gotama
always wake up refreshed,
who day and night
constantly recollect the Buddha.

Verse 297The disciples of Gotama
always wake up refreshed,
who day and night
constantly recollect the teaching.

Verse 298The disciples of Gotama
always wake up refreshed,
who day and night
constantly recollect the Saṅgha.

Verse 299The disciples of Gotama
always wake up refreshed,
who day and night
are constantly mindful of the body.

Verse 300The disciples of Gotama
always wake up refreshed,
whose minds day and night
delight in harmlessness.

Verse 301The disciples of Gotama
always wake up refreshed,
whose minds day and night
delight in meditation.

Going forth is hard

Verse 302Going forth is hard, it’s hard to be happy;
life at home is hard too, and painful,
it’s painful to stay when you’ve nothing in common.
A traveler is a prey to pain,
so don’t be a traveler,
don’t be prey to pain.

Two who are seen, and two who are not

Verse 303One who is faithful, accomplished in ethics,
blessed with fame and wealth,
is honored in whatever place
they frequent.

Verse 304The good shine from afar,
like the Himalayan peaks,
but the wicked are not seen,
like arrows scattered in the night.

Alone

Verse 305Sitting alone, sleeping alone,
tirelessly wandering alone;
one who tames themselves alone
would delight within a forest.

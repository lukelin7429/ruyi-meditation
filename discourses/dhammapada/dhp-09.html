Dhammapada · Chapter 9

Wickedness

Pāpavagga

Attribution
One chapter of a compiled verse anthology traditionally attributed to the Buddha; there is no narrative setting, speaker, or occasion given for these verses
Verses
116–128 (13 verses)
Form
Direct ethical exhortation built on paired verses and homely images — a pot filling drop by drop, dust thrown into the wind, poison carried in an unwounded hand
Length
~2 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart exists in the Chinese Faju Jing (法句經, T210) and related Dharmapada collections, though exact verse-by-verse correspondence is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — plainly stated, with vivid, easily remembered imagery

Why this chapter

Pāpavagga shifts the previous chapter's arithmetic of quantity and quality onto the ground of ethics itself: if one good day can outweigh a hundred empty years, what is the actual weight of a single choice made today? This chapter's answer is urgency. Four times it states the same idea twice, once in each direction — do the good deed again, avoid the bad one again — and its two central images, a pot filling drop by drop and dust thrown into the wind, both make the same point from different angles: nothing you do simply vanishes, and nothing you postpone stays small forever.

Reading guide

Hurry toward good, guard against evil

The chapter opens with a warning about timing rather than intention: “Rush to do good, shield your mind from evil; for when you're slow to do good, your thoughts delight in wickedness” (v.116). The claim is psychological as much as moral — hesitation is not neutral, because a mind not actively occupied with good tends to drift toward its opposite. Verses 117 and 118 then form the chapter's first explicit pair, stating the same structure twice with opposite content: “If you do something bad, don't do it again and again, don't set your heart on it, for piling up evil is suffering” (v.117), followed immediately by “If you do something good, do it again and again, set your heart on it, for piling up goodness is joyful” (v.118). The identical sentence structure, repeated word for word except for its outcome, makes the two verses function almost as a single statement viewed from two directions.

Ripening, not immediate

Verses 119 and 120 introduce a theme of delay: “Even the wicked see good things, so long as their wickedness has not ripened. But as soon as that wickedness ripens, then the wicked see wicked things” (v.119) — and its mirror, “Even the good see wicked things, so long as their goodness has not ripened. But as soon as that goodness ripens, then the good see good things” (v.120). Both verses insist that present experience is not a reliable guide to the direction one's life is actually heading; a run of apparent good fortune can precede a bad outcome still ripening, just as a run of apparent hardship can precede a good one. This idea — that consequences ripen on their own schedule rather than arriving at once — is often illustrated elsewhere with the image of a deed that tastes sweet at first and turns bitter only once it has fully matured; these two verses give the same idea in more abstract terms.

Filled drop by drop

The chapter's best-known image follows immediately, again as an exact paired repetition: “Think not lightly of evil, that it won't come back to you. The pot is filled with water falling drop by drop; the fool is filled with wickedness piled up bit by bit” (v.121), and “Think not lightly of goodness, that it won't come back to you. The pot is filled with water falling drop by drop; the attentive one is filled with goodness piled up bit by bit” (v.122). The two verses share every line except the final word — “fool” against “attentive one,” wickedness against goodness — a structure that makes the point almost by its shape alone: whichever direction a person is filling their own pot, it happens the same way, one drop at a time, whether or not any single drop feels significant.

Avoiding the road, not just the destination

Verses 123 and 124 turn from patience to prevention. “Avoid wickedness, as a merchant with rich cargo and small escort would avoid a dangerous road, or one who loves life would avoid drinking poison” (v.123) treats wickedness as a hazard to be routed around entirely, not merely resisted once encountered. Verse 124 then makes a sharper, almost technical point: “You can carry poison in your hand if it has no wound, for poison does not infect without a wound; nothing bad happens unless you do bad.” The image implies that vulnerability to wrongdoing is not passive exposure but something that requires an opening — an existing “wound” of intention or action — for harm to take hold.

What backfires, and what doesn't

Verse 125 gives the chapter's sharpest image of consequence: “Whoever wrongs a man who has done no wrong, a pure man who has not a blemish, the evil backfires on the fool, like fine dust thrown upwind.” Thrown against the wind, dust cannot travel forward — it simply returns to the thrower's own face. Verse 126 then widens the lens to rebirth in general, naming four destinies at once: “Some are born in a womb; evildoers go to hell; the virtuous go to heaven; the undefiled are fully extinguished.” The four are not presented as parallel options so much as an ascending list, ending, as so many chapters in this collection do, on the possibility of stepping outside the cycle altogether.

No place to hide

The chapter's closing pair (v.127–128) shares an identical opening in both verses and diverges only in its final line: “Not in midair, nor mid-ocean, nor hiding in a mountain cleft; you'll find no place on the planet to escape your wicked deeds” (v.127), and “Not in midair, nor mid-ocean, nor hiding in a mountain cleft; you'll find no place on the planet where you won't be vanquished by death” (v.128). Deeds and death, named side by side in nearly identical language, are placed on the same footing: both are, in the end, unavoidable, wherever one might try to hide from them.

Key terms

pāpa — “wickedness” or “evil,” the chapter's title term, set throughout against its opposite, puñña (goodness or merit).
the four paired verses — 117/118, 119/120, 121/122, and 127/128, each stating the same structure twice with opposite or near-identical content — more explicit paired repetition than any other chapter covered so far.
“ripening” — (v.119–120) the idea that the consequences of both good and bad deeds mature on their own timetable, so present appearances are not a reliable guide to what is actually approaching.
“filled drop by drop” — (v.121–122) the pot-filling parable warning against dismissing any single small deed, good or bad, as insignificant.
“dust thrown upwind” — (v.125) the image of harm done to an innocent person returning to its source, much as dust thrown against the wind blows back on the thrower.

Dhammapada · Chapter 5

The Fool

Bālavagga

Verses
60–75
Speaker
Traditionally the Buddha, though as a compiled verse anthology individual verses may not all share a single narrative occasion
Form
Sixteen verses examining folly from many angles — self-deception, attachment, corrosive companionship, and the slow ripening of bad deeds
Length
~4 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart exists in the Chinese Faju Jing (法句經, T210) and related Dharmapada collections, though exact verse-by-verse correspondence is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — direct and occasionally unsparing, but built from clear, memorable similes

Why this chapter, the fifth

Having examined mind, diligence, and imagery drawn from the garden, the Dhammapada turns to a plainer subject: folly itself, examined with a precision that can feel almost clinical. This chapter’s definition of a fool has little to do with intelligence and everything to do with self-awareness — “the fool who thinks they’re a fool is wise at least to that extent. But the true fool is said to be one who imagines that they are wise.” Its most memorable verse makes the same point through taste: a fool can live beside a wise person for an entire lifetime and still not perceive the truth, “like a spoon the taste of the soup.”

Reading guide

The chapter in one sentence

Sixteen verses define folly not as a lack of intelligence but as a lack of self-awareness and self-restraint, tracing its costs through long nights of wandering, corrosive companionship, self-deceiving frets over ownership, and deeds whose bitter fruit ripens slowly but surely.

Verse 60: the long night

The chapter opens with a triad of comparisons building toward its main claim: “long is the night for the wakeful; long is the league for the weary; long transmigrate the fools who don’t understand the true teaching.” Each image describes subjective time stretched by discomfort — a sleepless night, an exhausting journey — before applying the same stretching to the entire span of saṃsāra itself for those who lack understanding.

Verse 61: no fellowship with fools

A piece of blunt practical counsel: “if while wandering you find no partner equal or better than yourself, then firmly resolve to wander alone — there’s no fellowship with fools.” The verse doesn’t counsel isolation as an ideal; it treats solitude as the better of two options when the alternative is companionship that actively erodes one’s own progress.

Verse 62: sons are mine, wealth is mine

A verse built on a fool’s interior monologue: “‘sons are mine, wealth is mine’ — thus the fool frets. For even your self is not your own, let alone your sons or wealth.” The claim is not that possessions are illusory in some abstract sense, but that the fretting itself rests on an ownership no one actually has — not even, the verse pointedly adds, over one’s own self.

Verse 63: the fool who knows, the fool who doesn’t

One of the chapter’s sharpest and most quoted lines: “the fool who thinks they’re a fool is wise at least to that extent. But the true fool is said to be one who imagines that they are wise.” This single verse effectively defines the chapter’s entire subject: folly here is fundamentally a failure of self-recognition, and admitting one’s own foolishness is itself the beginning of its correction.

Verses 64–65: the spoon and the tongue

The chapter’s most famous pair, contrasting two people in the exact same circumstance — proximity to a wise teacher — with opposite results. “Though a fool attends to the wise even for the rest of their life, they still don’t understand the teaching, like a spoon the taste of the soup” (64); “if a clever person attends to the wise even just for an hour or so, they swiftly understand the teaching, like a tongue the taste of the soup” (65). A spoon sits in the soup indefinitely without ever tasting it; a tongue registers flavor almost instantly. The point is not that duration doesn’t matter at all, but that mere exposure, without the receptivity that makes exposure count, accomplishes nothing.

Verses 66–69: bitter fruit, however sweet it first appears

Verse 66 states that fools “behave like their own worst enemies, doing wicked deeds that ripen as bitter fruit.” Verses 67 and 68 pair a deed that “plagues you later on” against one that “doesn’t plague you later on,” the latter gladdening and cheering “as its effect stays with you.” Verse 69 explains the fool’s characteristic error in timing: “the fool imagines that evil is sweet, so long as it has not yet ripened. But as soon as that evil ripens, they fall into suffering” — folly here is specifically a failure to reason about consequences that haven’t yet arrived.

Verses 70–71: a sixteenth part, and smoldering ash

Verse 70 makes an arresting comparison: a fool practicing an extreme, prolonged austerity — eating “month after month… food from a grass-blade’s tip” — is still “not worth a sixteenth part of one who has appraised the teaching,” a pointed rejection of severe self-mortification as any kind of shortcut to wisdom. Verse 71 returns to the timing theme of verse 69 from a different angle: a wicked deed “does not curdle quickly like milk,” but instead smolders, following the fool “like a fire smothered over with ash” — still burning, invisibly, long after it appears to have gone out.

Verses 72–75: fame, status, and the different path

The closing verses turn to a subtler form of folly: the pursuit of status within a religious community. Verse 72 warns that “whatever fame a fool may get, it only gives rise to harm,” ruining their good features and, memorably, blowing “their head into bits.” Verse 73 lists what such a fool actually wants — esteem, status among mendicants, authority over monasteries, honor among families — and verse 74 dramatizes the internal monologue behind it: “let both layfolk and renunciants think the work was done by me alone… let them fall under my sway alone,” growing greed and pride. The chapter’s final verse supplies the needed contrast: “the means to profit and the path to quenching are two quite different things,” and a genuine disciple, understanding this, “would never delight in honors, but rather would foster seclusion” — ending the chapter on folly by naming, precisely, what its opposite looks like.

Key terms

bāla — “fool,” the chapter’s subject, defined throughout not by low intelligence but by a failure of self-recognition and an inability to reason about future consequences.
the spoon and the tongue — verses 64–65’s central simile: mere proximity to wisdom, without receptivity, accomplishes nothing, however long it is sustained.
“the fool who thinks they’re a fool” — verse 63’s definition of folly as fundamentally self-ignorance; recognizing one’s own foolishness is already a form of wisdom.
“smothered over with ash” — verse 71’s image for a wicked deed’s delayed but inevitable consequence, smoldering invisibly rather than curdling quickly.
“the means to profit and the path to quenching” — verse 75’s closing distinction between seeking status and seeking awakening, ending the chapter with the deliberate contrast to everything folly has been shown to be.

The text

All sixteen verses of the Bālavagga. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 60Long is the night for the wakeful; long is the league for the weary; long transmigrate the fools who don’t understand the true teaching.

Verse 61If while wandering you find no partner equal or better than yourself, then firmly resolve to wander alone—there’s no fellowship with fools.

Verse 62“Sons are mine, wealth is mine”—thus the fool frets. For even your self is not your own, let alone your sons or wealth.

Verse 63The fool who thinks they’re a fool is wise at least to that extent. But the true fool is said to be one who imagines that they are wise.

Verses 64–65Though a fool attends to the wise even for the rest of their life, they still don’t understand the teaching, like a spoon the taste of the soup.

If a clever person attends to the wise even just for an hour or so, they swiftly understand the teaching, like a tongue the taste of the soup.

Verse 66Fools and simpletons behave like their own worst enemies, doing wicked deeds that ripen as bitter fruit.

Verses 67–68It’s not good to do a deed that plagues you later on, for which you weep and wail, as its effect stays with you.

It is good to do a deed that doesn’t plague you later on, that gladdens and cheers, as its effect stays with you.

Verse 69The fool imagines that evil is sweet, so long as it has not yet ripened. But as soon as that evil ripens, they fall into suffering.

Verse 70Month after month a fool may eat food from a grass-blade’s tip; but they’ll never be worth a sixteenth part of one who has appraised the teaching.

Verse 71For a wicked deed that has been done does not curdle quickly like milk. Smoldering, it follows the fool, like a fire smothered over with ash.

Verse 72Whatever fame a fool may get, it only gives rise to harm. Whatever good features they have it ruins, and blows their head into bits.

Verse 73They’d seek the esteem that they lack, and status among the mendicants; authority over monasteries, and honor among other families.

Verse 74“Let both layfolk and renunciants think the work was done by me alone. In anything at all that’s to be done, let them fall under my sway alone.” So thinks the fool, their greed and pride only growing.

Verse 75For the means to profit and the path to quenching are two quite different things. A mendicant disciple of the Buddha, understanding what this really means, would never delight in honors, but rather would foster seclusion.

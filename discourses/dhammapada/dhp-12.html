Dhammapada · Chapter 12

The Self

Attavagga

Verses
157–166 (10 verses)
Attribution
A compiled verse anthology, traditionally attributed to the Buddha, gathered here rather than delivered as a single narrated occasion
Form
Ten aphoristic verses on self-guarding, self-discipline, and self-responsibility as the foundation of practice
Length
~3 minutes to read
Northern parallel

Difficulty

Why this chapter, twelfth

The Attavagga makes an argument that’s easy to state and hard to actually live by: nobody else can practice for you, protect you, corrupt you, or purify you. Ten short verses build a case for the self as both the whole problem and the whole solution — “the self is the lord of the self, for who else would be one’s lord?” — without ever collapsing into either self-indulgence or self-hatred.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

If you actually considered yourself dear, you would guard yourself well — because the self is its own only possible lord and its own only possible refuge, evil is disturbingly easy to do and harmful to no one but yourself, good is genuinely hard to do and beneficial to no one but yourself, and purity or impurity are, in the end, entirely personal matters that nobody else can settle on your behalf.

If you loved yourself

The chapter opens on a conditional that is really an accusation in disguise: “If you knew your self as beloved, you’d look after it so well” (157) — implying, of course, that most people don’t actually look after themselves this well, despite claiming to love themselves above all else. What real self-love would look like, the verse continues, is staying “alert” through at least one of the night’s three watches, an old formula for sustained attention rather than sleep. Verses 158–159 sharpen the point into a familiar but uncomfortable test: before instructing anyone else, “the astute would avoid being corrupted by first grounding themselves in what is suitable” (158), and “if one were to treat oneself as one instructs another, the well-tamed indeed would tame: for the self, it seems, is hard to tame” (159). It is far easier to hand out good advice than to take it.

Your own only lord

Verse 160 states what may be the chapter’s single best-known line: “One is indeed the lord of oneself, for who else would be one’s lord? By means of a well-tamed self, one gains a lord that’s rare indeed.” No teacher, deity, or institution can occupy that position on your behalf — not because outside help is worthless, but because the actual work of taming happens nowhere except inside the one mind doing it. The two verses that follow describe what happens without that work: “the evil that is done by oneself, born and produced in oneself, grinds down a simpleton, as diamond grinds a lesser gem” (161), and “one choked by immorality, as a sal tree by a creeper, does to themselves what a foe only wishes” (162) — two of the chapter’s more startling images, since a creeper and a diamond are not obviously violent things, yet both are shown quietly, patiently destroying whatever they’ve attached to.

Easy evil, hard good

Verse 163 is another of the collection’s most quoted lines, precise about where the real difficulty in ethical life actually sits: “It’s easy to do bad things harmful to oneself, but good things that are helpful are the hardest things to do.” The asymmetry is the point — not that good and evil are equally likely, requiring only a coin flip, but that the entire architecture of habit and impulse tilts naturally toward the easier, more harmful path, which is exactly why sustained attention (157) and self-taming (159–160) are needed at all. Verse 164 adds a specific failure mode: “on account of wicked views — scorning the guidance of the perfected ones, the noble ones living righteously — the idiot begets their own self’s demise, like the bamboo bearing fruit,” an image drawn from certain bamboo species that die once they fruit — self-destruction built into the very act that looks, from outside, like flourishing.

Nobody else can purify you

Verse 165 draws the chapter’s logic to its sharpest point: “For it is by oneself that evil’s done, one is corrupted by oneself. It’s by oneself that evil’s not done, one is purified by oneself. Purity and impurity are personal matters, no one can purify another.” This is not a claim that other people or teachings are useless — the chapter has already praised seeking guidance (158) — but a claim about where the actual transaction of purification takes place, which is nowhere but within the person doing it. The closing verse (166) makes the practical instruction explicit and, notably, permits a kind of self-regard that might elsewhere sound selfish: “Never neglect what is good for yourself for the sake of another, however great. Knowing well what is good for yourself, be intent upon your heart’s goal.” Read alongside the rest of the chapter, this isn’t license for indifference to others — it’s a warning against abandoning the one project only you can actually complete.

Key terms

atta — “self,” the chapter’s namesake and its central figure, both the one doing the taming and the one being tamed.
attā hi attano nātho — “the self is the lord of the self” (160), among the most frequently quoted lines in the whole Dhammapada.
dutaman — “hard to tame,” describing the self in verse 159; the same root (dama) that gives the well-tamed mind of verse 160 its name.
the bamboo that fruits — the chapter’s image (164) for self-destruction disguised as flourishing, drawn from bamboo species that die after fruiting.
“no one can purify another” — the chapter’s closing principle (165), locating the entire transaction of purification within the individual rather than in any outside agent.

The text

All ten verses of the Attavagga, given complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 158The astute would avoid being corrupted by first grounding themselves in what is suitable, and then instructing others.

Verse 159If one were to treat oneself as one instructs another, the well-tamed indeed would tame: for the self, it seems, is hard to tame.

Verse 161For the evil that is done by oneself, born and produced in oneself, grinds down a simpleton, as diamond grinds a lesser gem.

Verse 162One choked by immorality, as a sal tree by a creeper, does to themselves what a foe only wishes.

Easy evil, hard good

Verse 163It’s easy to do bad things harmful to oneself, but good things that are helpful are the hardest things to do.

Verse 164On account of wicked views — scorning the guidance of the perfected ones, the noble ones living righteously — the idiot begets their own self’s demise, like the bamboo bearing fruit.

Nobody else can purify you

Verse 165For it is by oneself that evil’s done, one is corrupted by oneself. It’s by oneself that evil’s not done, one is purified by oneself. Purity and impurity are personal matters, no one can purify another.

Verse 166Never neglect what is good for yourself for the sake of another, however great. Knowing well what is good for yourself, be intent upon your heart’s goal.

Dhammapada · Chapter 23

Elephants

Nāgavagga

Why this chapter, twenty-third

The Nāgavagga takes its name from nāga, an elephant — specifically the great tusked battle-elephant of ancient Indian warfare, an animal trained for years before it could be trusted to hold steady under a rain of arrows. That image opens the chapter, and everything else follows from it: taming compared to taming, the wild compared to the trained, and finally the solitary wanderer compared to the bad company that undoes a good life. It is one of the most concrete, least abstract chapters in the whole Dhammapada — a chapter you can picture, verse by verse, almost without translation.

Reading guide

The chapter in one sentence

Praise for taming runs through the whole chapter — a tamed elephant is useful, a tamed mule or horse is fine, but a person who has tamed themselves is called the best of all, because no vehicle, however well trained, can carry anyone to the untamed realm of freedom the way a disciplined mind can.

Enduring abuse like a battle elephant (320–321)

The chapter opens in the first person: “Like an elephant struck with arrows in battle, I shall endure abuse, for so many folk are badly behaved.” A war elephant does not flinch at every arrow; it has been trained to hold its ground under fire. The verse asks for the same composure in the face of criticism and hostility, on the grounds that unprincipled behavior from others is simply common, not a personal emergency requiring retaliation. Verse 321 develops the comparison: a well-tamed animal is led to the crowd, a tamed elephant is the one a king mounts, but “the tamed person who endures abuse is the best of human beings” — ranking human self-mastery above even the most impressively trained animal.

Self-tamed is best of all (322–323)

Verse 322 makes the ranking explicit: those who have tamed themselves are said to be better than “fine tamed mules, thoroughbreds from Sindh, or giant tuskers” — naming, in order, some of the most prized animals of the ancient world. Verse 323 explains why: no mount, however excellent, will carry a rider “to the untrodden place” — nibbāna, the destination no animal or vehicle can reach. Only a person “whose self is well tamed” can go there, and only by having tamed themselves in turn: “tamed by the tamed.”

The tusker who misses the forest (324)

A single vivid portrait follows: “The tusker named Dhanapāla is musky in rut, hard to control. Bound, he eats not a morsel, for he misses the elephant forest.” Even a named, presumably famous elephant, once captured and bound, refuses food out of longing for the wild. It is a startlingly sympathetic image of captivity and loss placed right in the middle of a chapter otherwise devoted to praising taming — a reminder that the taming being praised elsewhere is meant to be chosen and self-directed, not simply imposed by force.

The dullard who overeats (325)

Verse 325 turns from the noble tusker to an unflattering comparison: a person made drowsy by overeating, “fond of sleep, rolling round the bed like a great hog stuffed with grain,” is said to return “to the womb again and again” — caught in repeated rebirth by simple heedlessness and self-indulgence.

Guiding the mind like an elephant driver (326–327)

Two verses return to the taming image, now applied directly to the practitioner’s own mind: “In the past my mind wandered how it wished, where it liked, as it pleased. Now I’ll carefully guide it, as a trainer with a hook guides a rutting elephant.” Verse 327 pushes the same image toward urgency: “Pull yourself out of this pit, like an elephant sunk in a bog” — picturing the untrained mind not merely as wayward but as actually stuck, requiring deliberate effort to free.

Company or solitude (328–330)

The chapter then turns to companionship. If you find “an alert companion, an attentive friend to live happily together,” the advice is to travel with them, “joyful and mindful,” overcoming adversity together (328). But absent such a friend, the counsel changes completely: “like a king who flees his conquered realm,” one should “wander alone like a tusker in the wilds” (329) — and verse 330 states the principle behind it plainly: “It’s better to wander alone, there’s no fellowship with fools. Wander alone and do no wrong, at ease like a tusker in the wilds.” Solitude, in other words, is not the ideal in itself; it is simply preferable to bad company, exactly as a solitary wild tusker is preferable to a badly matched herd.

A closing list of blessings (331–333)

The chapter ends on a different register entirely — three verses built as a simple list, each line naming something “a blessing”: a friend in need, contentment with whatever one has, good deeds at the end of life, giving up all suffering (331); serving one’s parents, serving ascetics and brahmins (332); keeping precepts into old age, being grounded in faith, gaining wisdom, avoiding wrong (333). After fourteen verses of elephants, taming, and solitude, the chapter closes quietly, almost like a benediction, on the ordinary blessings of a stable, well-grounded life.

Key terms

nāga — “elephant,” specifically the trained battle-elephant that gives the chapter its name and its opening simile for enduring abuse without retaliating.
the untrodden place — nibbāna, described in verse 323 as a destination no tamed animal, however excellent, can carry a rider to; only a self-tamed person can go there.
Dhanapāla — the named tusker of verse 324, who refuses to eat once captured, longing for the forest — a rare moment of sympathy for the untamed within a chapter that otherwise praises taming.
“no fellowship with fools” — the principle behind verses 329–330: solitude is not prized for its own sake but preferred over bad company, exactly as a lone tusker is better off than one badly matched.

The text

All fourteen verses of the Nāgavagga, complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 320Like an elephant struck
with arrows in battle,
I shall endure abuse,
for so many folk are badly behaved.

Verse 321The well-tamed beast is the one led to the crowd;
the tamed elephant’s the one the king mounts;
the tamed person who endures abuse
is the best of human beings.

Verse 322Those who have tamed themselves are better
than fine tamed mules,
thoroughbreds from Sindh,
or giant tuskers.

Verse 323For not on those mounts
would you go to the untrodden place,
whereas, with the help of one whose self is well tamed,
you go there, tamed by the tamed.

Verse 324The tusker named Dhanapāla
is musky in rut, hard to control.
Bound, he eats not a morsel,
for he misses the elephant forest.

Verse 325One who gets drowsy from overeating,
fond of sleep, rolling round the bed
like a great hog stuffed with grain:
that dullard returns to the womb again and again.

Verse 326In the past my mind wandered
how it wished, where it liked, as it pleased.
Now I’ll carefully guide it,
as a trainer with a hook guides a rutting elephant.

Verse 327Delight in diligence!
Take good care of your mind!
Pull yourself out of this pit,
like an elephant sunk in a bog.

Verse 328If you find an alert companion,
an attentive friend to live happily together,
then, overcoming all adversities,
wander with them, joyful and mindful.

Verse 329If you find no alert companion,
no attentive friend to live happily together,
then, like a king who flees his conquered realm,
wander alone like a tusker in the wilds.

Verse 330It’s better to wander alone,
there’s no fellowship with fools.
Wander alone and do no wrong,
at ease like a tusker in the wilds.

Verse 331A friend in need is a blessing;
it’s a blessing to be content with whatever;
good deeds are a blessing at the end of life,
and giving up all suffering is a blessing.

Verse 332In this world it’s a blessing to serve
one’s mother and one’s father.
And it’s a blessing also to serve
ascetics and brahmins.

Verse 333It’s a blessing to keep precepts until you grow old;
a blessing to be grounded in faith;
the getting of wisdom’s a blessing;
and it’s a blessing to avoid doing wrong.

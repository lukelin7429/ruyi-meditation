Dhammapada · Chapter 8

The Thousands

Sahassavagga

Attribution
One chapter of a compiled verse anthology traditionally attributed to the Buddha; there is no narrative setting, speaker, or occasion given for these verses
Verses
100–115 (16 verses)
Form
A chapter built almost entirely on one rhetorical device — quality outweighing quantity — repeated across a long series of parallel comparisons
Length
~3 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart exists in the Chinese Faju Jing (法句經, T210) and related Dharmapada collections, though exact verse-by-verse correspondence is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — clear, formulaic, and among the most quotable chapters in the whole collection

Why this chapter

Sahassavagga takes its name from its opening word — sahassa, “thousand” — and then builds an entire chapter on a single comparative device: a small amount of something valuable set against a vastly larger amount of something worthless. The device starts out measuring words (a thousand meaningless verses against one meaningful one), shifts to measuring military conquest, then ritual observance, and finally settles into its longest and best-known run: six verses in a row weighing a single day lived well against a hundred years lived without it. Few chapters in the Dhammapada are easier to quote from memory, precisely because the pattern itself does most of the work.

Reading guide

One word worth more than a thousand

The chapter opens with three near-identical verses, each substituting one word for another: “Better than a thousand meaningless sayings is a single meaningful saying, hearing which brings you peace” (v.100); “Better than a thousand meaningless verses is a single meaningful verse, hearing which brings you peace” (v.101); and “Better than reciting a hundred meaningless verses is a single saying of Dhamma, hearing which brings you peace” (v.102). The repetition itself is the argument — by restating the same claim three times with only the unit of measure changing (sayings, verses, recitations), the chapter insists that volume of speech and value of speech are entirely separate questions.

Conquering yourself is the harder victory

Verses 103–105 shift the comparison from words to conquest: “The supreme conqueror is not he who conquers a million men in battle, but he who conquers a single man: himself” (v.103). The chapter then doubles down on the claim's strength: “It is surely better to conquer oneself than all those other folk. When a person has tamed themselves, always living restrained, no god nor centaur, nor Māra nor divinity, can undo the victory of such a personage” (v.104–105). Unlike a military victory, which can always be reversed by a stronger force, self-conquest is described as permanent — not even Māra, the tradition's personification of temptation and death, can take it back.

An hour's honor over a century of ritual

The next four verses turn to religious observance, and here the chapter becomes pointed rather than merely poetic. “Rather than a thousandfold sacrifice, every month for a full century, it's better to honor for a single hour one who has developed themselves. That offering is better than the hundred year sacrifice” (v.106) — and verse 107 repeats the same comparison, substituting a hundred years of “serving the sacred flame” for the sacrifice. Verse 108 generalizes the point beyond any single ritual: “Whatever sacrifice or offering in the world a seeker of merit may make for a year, none of it is worth a quarter of bowing to the sincere.” Verse 109 then names what such bowing produces: “For one in the habit of bowing, always honoring the elders, four blessings grow: lifespan, beauty, happiness, and strength.” Read together, these four verses value a moment of genuine respect toward a developed person over even a lifetime of costly ritual.

One good day over a hundred empty years

The chapter's longest and most famous run is its closing sequence, six verses built on a single refrain: “Better to live a single day” doing or seeing one thing well, “than to live a hundred years” without it. In order, the six qualities are: living ethically and absorbed in meditation (v.110); living wisely and absorbed in meditation (v.111); living energetically (v.112); seeing the rise and fall of things — that is, seeing impermanence directly (v.113); seeing “the state free of death” (v.114); and seeing “the supreme teaching” (v.115). The six are not interchangeable restatements; they build in a rough progression from basic ethical and meditative conduct, through energy and insight into impermanence, toward the deathless itself and the teaching that points to it. A hundred years is, in ordinary human terms, close to the longest possible lifespan — which makes the chapter's point as strong as it can be made: even the fullest possible span of ordinary life is outweighed by a single day genuinely lived with insight.

The chapter's arithmetic, zoomed in

Taken as a whole, Sahassavagga narrows its unit of comparison as it goes: a thousand words, then a million soldiers, then a hundred years of sacrifice, then a hundred years of ordinary life — each time set against a single instance of something genuine. The effect is cumulative rather than repetitive: the chapter keeps making the same basic claim at a larger and larger scale, until, by the final verse, it has compared one day of insight to nearly the largest quantity a human life can offer.

Key terms

sahassa — “thousand,” the chapter's title word and the unit of comparison in its opening verses.
the self-conquest verses — (v.103–105) contrasting military victory over others with the harder, and permanent, victory of self-taming.
the four blessings — (v.109) lifespan, beauty, happiness, and strength, said to grow for one who habitually bows to and honors elders.
“rise and fall” — (v.113) a standard phrase for the direct contemplation of impermanence in all conditioned things.
the sixfold refrain — (v.110–115) “Better to live a single day” with one named quality “than to live a hundred years” without it, repeated across ethics, wisdom, energy, and three degrees of insight.

The text

All sixteen verses of Sahassavagga, complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 100Better than a thousand meaningless sayings is a single meaningful saying, hearing which brings you peace.

Verse 102Better than reciting a hundred meaningless verses is a single saying of Dhamma, hearing which brings you peace.

Verse 103The supreme conqueror is not he who conquers a million men in battle, but he who conquers a single man: himself.

Verses 104–105It is surely better to conquer oneself than all those other folk. When a person has tamed themselves, always living restrained, no god nor centaur, nor Māra nor divinity, can undo the victory of such a personage.

An hour's honor over a century of ritual

Verse 106Rather than a thousandfold sacrifice, every month for a full century, it’s better to honor for a single hour one who has developed themselves. That offering is better than the hundred year sacrifice.

Verse 107Rather than serve the sacred flame in the forest for a hundred years, it’s better to honor for a single hour a personage who has developed themselves. That offering is better than the hundred year sacrifice.

Verse 108Whatever sacrifice or offering in the world a seeker of merit may make for a year, none of it is worth a quarter of bowing to the sincere.

Verse 109For one in the habit of bowing, always honoring the elders, four blessings grow: lifespan, beauty, happiness, and strength.

One good day over a hundred empty years

Verse 110Better to live a single day ethical and absorbed in meditation than to live a hundred years unethical and lacking immersion.

Verse 111Better to live a single day wise and absorbed in meditation than to live a hundred years witless and lacking immersion.

Verse 112Better to live a single day energetic and strong, than to live a hundred years lazy and lacking energy.

Verse 113Better to live a single day seeing rise and fall than to live a hundred years blind to rise and fall.

Verse 114Better to live a single day seeing the state free of death than to live a hundred years blind to the state free of death.

Verse 115Better to live a single day seeing the supreme teaching than to live a hundred years blind to the supreme teaching.

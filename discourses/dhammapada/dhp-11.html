Dhammapada · Chapter 11

Old Age

Jarāvagga

Verses
146–156 (11 verses)
Attribution
A compiled verse anthology, traditionally attributed to the Buddha, gathered here rather than delivered as a single narrated occasion
Form
Short, image-driven verses on bodily decay, closing with two verses traditionally linked to the night of the Buddha’s awakening
Length
~3 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart exists in the Chinese Faju Jing (法句經, T210) and related Dharmapada collections, though exact verse-by-verse correspondence is not asserted here
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the imagery is plain and physical, but the closing verses reward some background on the story they allude to

Why this chapter, eleventh

The Jarāvagga opens with a question that still lands: “What is joy, what is laughter, when the flames are ever burning? Swathed in darkness, would you not seek a light?” Everything that follows is commentary on that one jolt — eleven verses that look straight at the body’s aging, sickness, and decay without flinching, and then, in its two most famous lines, turn from decay to the one thing the chapter insists doesn’t decay at all.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The body that seems so worth defending — worth laughing over, worth decorating, worth being proud of — is a burning house already collapsing into bones and blood and disease, and the only thing in this chapter that doesn’t age is the truth the good hold onto and the mind that has finally stopped building itself a new house to collapse in.

A question, then a body

The chapter’s opening verse (146) is a rhetorical jolt rather than a riddle: “What is joy, what is laughter, when the flames are ever burning? Swathed in darkness, would you not seek a light?” The “flames” here are not a distant hellfire but the ordinary burning of old age, sickness, and death that every living body is already undergoing, all the time, whether or not anyone notices. Verses 147 through 150 answer the implicit “what is this body, really” with a string of deliberately unflattering images: a “fancy puppet, a body built of sores, diseased, obsessed over, in which nothing lasts at all” (147); “decrepit and frail, a nest of disease” whose “foul carcass falls apart, for life ends in death” (148); bones “tossed away like dried gourds in the autumn” (149); and, in the chapter’s most compressed image, a “city built of bones, plastered with flesh and blood,” inside which “old age and death are stashed away, along with conceit and contempt” (150). That last phrase is worth sitting with: the body doesn’t just contain decay, it contains the pride and contempt that decay is meant to dissolve, and mostly doesn’t.

What doesn’t age

Verse 151 turns the chapter on its hinge. “Fancy chariots of kings wear out, and even this body gets old. But the truth of the good never gets old — so the good proclaim to the good.” The comparison to a royal chariot is deliberately grand: even the most lavishly maintained object owned by the most powerful person available still wears down to nothing, and the body is no exception. What survives that wearing-down, the verse says, is saddhamma, the true teaching or the goodness good people hold and pass to one another — not a possession that ages with use but something that, being passed on, doesn’t. Verse 152 then adds a sharper edge: a person of little learning simply “ages like an ox — their flesh grows, but not their wisdom.” Aging by itself guarantees nothing; the years accumulate on the body whether or not any understanding accumulates alongside them.

The house-builder

The chapter’s two most celebrated verses (153–154) are traditionally recited as the Buddha’s own exultant cry on the night of his awakening, after having searched, across countless lives, for whatever it was that kept building him a new body to grow old and die in again: “Transmigrating through countless rebirths, I’ve journeyed without reward, searching for the house-builder; painful is birth again and again.” Then the answer, and the triumph: “I’ve seen you, house-builder! You won’t build a house again! Your rafters are all broken, your roof-peak demolished. The mind, set on demolition, has reached the end of cravings.” The “house” is the repeatedly constructed body and identity of one more rebirth; the “house-builder” is craving (taṇhā), the force that keeps assembling rafters and roof-peaks out of the raw material of wanting; and its being “seen” is nothing less than awakening itself. Everything the chapter has just spent eight verses describing — the puppet-body, the nest of disease, the bones plastered with flesh — is precisely the house that stops getting rebuilt once the house-builder is found.

Wasted youth

The chapter closes on a quieter, sadder register. Two nearly identical verses (155–156) describe those who, “when young,… spurned the spiritual path and failed to earn any wealth” — missing both worldly and spiritual investment in their youth, and left with nothing to draw on in old age. They “brood like old cranes in a pond bereft of fish” (155) and “lie like spent arrows, bemoaning over things past” (156). After the triumphant note of 153–154, these closing images function as a warning rather than a contradiction: the house-builder can be found and defeated, but only by those who use the time they have, while they still have it, rather than discovering too late — fishless pond, spent arrow — that the time for either kind of wealth has passed.

Key terms

jarā — “old age,” the chapter’s namesake and one leg of the universal triad of old age, sickness, and death that frames nearly every verse here.
gahakāraka — “house-builder,” the chapter’s central image (153–154) for craving, the force that repeatedly constructs a new body and identity out of wanting.
saddhamma — “the truth of the good,” named in verse 151 as the one thing that, unlike the body or a royal chariot, does not wear out with age.
taṇhākkhaya — “the end of cravings,” the state the mind is said to reach in verse 154 once the house-builder has been seen and every rafter broken.
the puppet and the city of bones — the chapter’s two harshest images for the body (147, 150), used not to induce despair but to loosen an ordinary, unexamined attachment to appearance.

The text

All eleven verses of the Jarāvagga, given complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Why laugh, when the world is burning?

Verse 146What is joy, what is laughter, when the flames are ever burning? Swathed in darkness, would you not seek a light?

The fragile body

Verse 147See this fancy puppet, a body built of sores, diseased, obsessed over, in which nothing lasts at all.

Verse 148This body is decrepit and frail, a nest of disease. This foul carcass falls apart, for life ends in death.

Verse 149These dove-grey bones are tossed away like dried gourds in the autumn — what joy is there in such a sight?

Verse 150In this city built of bones, plastered with flesh and blood, old age and death are stashed away, along with conceit and contempt.

What doesn’t age

Verse 151Fancy chariots of kings wear out, and even this body gets old. But the truth of the good never gets old — so the good proclaim to the good.

Verse 152A person of little learning ages like an ox — their flesh grows, but not their wisdom.

The house-builder

Verse 153Transmigrating through countless rebirths, I’ve journeyed without reward, searching for the house-builder; painful is birth again and again.

Verse 154I’ve seen you, house-builder! You won’t build a house again! Your rafters are all broken, your roof-peak demolished. The mind, set on demolition, has reached the end of cravings.

Wasted youth

Verse 155When young they spurned the spiritual path and failed to earn any wealth. Now they brood like old cranes in a pond bereft of fish.

Verse 156When young they spurned the spiritual path and failed to earn any wealth. Now they lie like spent arrows, bemoaning over things past.

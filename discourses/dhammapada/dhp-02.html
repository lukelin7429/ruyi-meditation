Dhammapada · Chapter 2

Diligence

Appamādavagga

Why this chapter, the second

Having opened with mind and intention, the Dhammapada turns immediately to the quality that keeps intention on track over time: appamāda, heedfulness or diligence, sustained attentiveness that does not lapse. The chapter’s claim is stark — diligence is nothing less than “the state free of death,” while negligence “is the state of death” — and it was reportedly the Buddha’s own final instruction to his community, condensed to two words: appamādena sampādetha, “strive on with diligence.” Twelve verses spell out why heedfulness carries such weight.

Reading guide

The chapter in one sentence

Twelve verses argue, from a dozen different angles, that heedful, sustained attention (appamāda) is what separates the deathless from the dead, the awakened from the merely sleeping, and the person who actually reaches the goal from the one who only talks about it.

Verse 21: the state free of death

The chapter opens with its thesis stated as bluntly as possible: “Heedfulness is the state free of death; heedlessness is the state of death. The heedful do not die, while the heedless are like the dead.” This is not a claim about literal immortality — heedful people still die physically — but about which quality of mind actually engages with reality versus merely drifting through it unconscious of consequence. To be heedless, on this view, is already to be as good as dead; nothing is being lived, only endured.

Verses 22–25: what the astute build

Verse 22 distinguishes those who grasp this distinction — “the astute rejoice in heedfulness” — from those who don’t. Verse 23 links regular meditation and staunch vigor to reaching extinguishment, “the supreme sanctuary from the yoke.” Verse 24 promises that a hard-working, mindful, restrained, diligent person’s reputation “only grows.” Verse 25 supplies the chapter’s most memorable image: “by hard work and diligence, by restraint and by self-control, a smart person would build an island that the floods cannot overflow” — diligence as infrastructure built in advance of the flood, not a response improvised once the water has already risen.

Verses 26–29: fools below, sages on the mountain

Verse 26 contrasts fools and simpletons, who “devote themselves to negligence,” with the wise, who “protect diligence as their best treasure.” Verse 27 counsels directly against negligence and “erotic intimacy,” promising that diligent meditation yields “abundant happiness.” Verse 28 offers a striking visual: the astute person who dispels negligence through diligence ascends “the palace of wisdom” and, now sorrowless, looks down at “this generation of sorrow” the way someone on a mountaintop looks down at fools below — not with contempt, the verse implies, but with the simple clarity of altitude. Verse 29 adds a second image for the same gap: “heedful among the heedless, wide awake while others sleep” — a true sage leaves the rest behind “like a swift horse passing a feeble.”

Verse 30: a king among gods, by diligence alone

The chapter’s one mythological aside: “Maghavā became chief of the gods by means of diligence.” Maghavā is an epithet of Sakka, ruler of the Tāvatiṃsa heaven, and the verse’s point isn’t really about celestial politics — it’s that even the highest position imaginable, in the traditional cosmology, was won by diligence rather than birth or luck. “People praise diligence,” the verse concludes, “while negligence is always deplored.”

Verses 31–32: a mendicant on fire

The closing pair, both beginning identically — “a mendicant who loves diligence, seeing fear in negligence” — supply the chapter’s final images. Verse 31: such a mendicant “advances like fire, burning up fetters big and small.” Verse 32: such a mendicant “can’t decline, and has drawn near to extinguishment.” The repetition itself performs a kind of diligence — the same warning restated, refusing to let the point lapse from attention, which is exactly what the whole chapter has been arguing diligence requires.

Key terms

appamāda — heedfulness, diligence, sustained non-negligent attention; the chapter’s subject and, according to tradition, the Buddha’s own final instruction to his community.
pamāda — negligence or heedlessness, appamāda’s opposite, repeatedly equated in this chapter with a kind of living death.
the island that floods cannot overflow — verse 25’s image for diligence as preparation built before hardship arrives, not improvised once it has.
Maghavā — an epithet of Sakka, ruler of the Tāvatiṃsa heaven of the thirty-three gods, cited in verse 30 as having earned that position through diligence.
nibbāna (rendered “extinguishment”) — the goal repeatedly linked to diligence in this chapter, described in verse 23 as “the supreme sanctuary from the yoke.”

The text

All twelve verses of the Appamādavagga. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Verse 21Heedfulness is the state free of death; heedlessness is the state of death. The heedful do not die, while the heedless are like the dead.

Verse 22Understanding this distinction when it comes to heedfulness, the astute rejoice in heedfulness, happy in the noble ones’ domain.

Verse 23They who regularly meditate, always staunchly vigorous; the attentive realize extinguishment, the supreme sanctuary from the yoke.

Verse 24For the hard-working and mindful, pure of deed and attentive, restrained, living righteously, and diligent, their reputation only grows.

Verse 25By hard work and diligence, by restraint and by self-control, a smart person would build an island that the floods cannot overflow.

Verse 26Fools and simpletons devote themselves to negligence. But the wise protect diligence as their best treasure.

Verse 27Don’t devote yourself to negligence, or delight in erotic intimacy. For if you’re diligent and meditate, you’ll attain abundant happiness.

Verse 28When the astute dispel negligence by means of diligence, ascending the palace of wisdom, sorrowless, they behold this generation of sorrow, as an attentive one on a mountain top beholds the fools below.

Verse 29Heedful among the heedless, wide awake while others sleep—a true sage leaves them behind, like a swift horse passing a feeble.

Verse 30Maghavā became chief of the gods by means of diligence. People praise diligence, while negligence is always deplored.

Verse 31A mendicant who loves diligence, seeing fear in negligence—advances like fire, burning up fetters big and small.

Verse 32A mendicant who loves diligence, seeing fear in negligence—such a one can’t decline, and has drawn near to extinguishment.

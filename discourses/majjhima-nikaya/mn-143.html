Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 143

Advice to Anāthapiṇḍika

Anāthapiṇḍikovādasutta

Setting
Anāthapiṇḍika’s own home in Sāvatthī, at his deathbed
Speakers
Venerable Sāriputta (as the main teacher), Venerable Ānanda, the householder Anāthapiṇḍika, and finally the Buddha
Form
A bedside visit that turns into an extended, systematic meditation instruction on non-grasping, followed by a narrative epilogue
Length
~15 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A Chinese parallel survives in the Saṃyukta-āgama (SĀ 1032), translated and studied by Bhikkhu Anālayo
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — the repeating structure is simple to follow, but the scope of what is being released is unusually deep for a teaching addressed to a layperson

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-forty-third

With this sutta the Majjhima Nikāya turns its final page: MN 143 opens the Saḷāyatanavagga, “The Chapter on the Six Sense Fields,” the fifteenth and last of the collection’s fifteen chapters, running from here to MN 152. It is a fitting discourse to begin that closing stretch, because it takes the six sense fields — the chapter’s organizing theme — and pushes the teaching on them further than almost anywhere else in the canon, given not to a monk in training but to a layman on his deathbed. Anāthapiṇḍika, the merchant who purchased and donated the very grove the Buddha spent so much of his life teaching in, receives here one of the most demanding instructions in the entire Majjhima — and weeps, he says, because he has never heard anything like it before.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Dying, Anāthapiṇḍika is taught by Sāriputta to train himself not to grasp at anything at all — not the six senses, not the six kinds of consciousness or contact or feeling they produce, not the elements or the aggregates, not even the four formless meditative attainments, and not this world or any world beyond it — and weeps on hearing it, because a teaching this thoroughgoing, he says, is ordinarily reserved for those who have gone forth, not for a householder like him.

A message sent twice

Anāthapiṇḍika — whose name means “feeder of the destitute” and who purchased Jeta’s Grove at Sāvatthī for the Buddha at legendary expense, becoming the Buddha’s most celebrated lay donor — is gravely ill. He sends a messenger with two nearly identical requests: one to the Buddha, simply reporting his condition and bowing at a distance; and one to Sāriputta, adding a request that he visit in person. Sāriputta, silently, agrees. The next morning he goes to Anāthapiṇḍika’s home with Ānanda as his companion, and asks the standard question any visitor to the sick would ask: is the pain fading, or growing?

The pain, described without euphemism

Anāthapiṇḍika’s answer is unflinching, built from a set of similes that recur elsewhere in the canon for the most severe kinds of physical suffering: winds piercing his head like a sharp point drilled in by a strong man; a leather strap tightened around his skull; his belly sliced as if by a butcher’s knife; his whole body burning as if held over a pit of coals by two strong men. He is, in his own words, “not keeping well” and “not getting by.” The sutta does not soften this. It is against this exact backdrop — a man in the last, most acute stage of a fatal illness — that Sāriputta begins to teach.

Not grasping, field by field

What follows is a single instruction, repeated across an unusually complete map of experience. “I shall not grasp the eye, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on the eye” — and the same, in turn, for the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind; then for their objects (sights, sounds, smells, tastes, touches, ideas); then for the six kinds of consciousness that arise from each sense meeting its object; then for the six kinds of contact; then for the six kinds of feeling born of that contact. Sāriputta then moves outward again, through the six elements (earth, water, fire, air, space, and consciousness), the five aggregates (form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness), and the four formless meditative attainments (infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness, and neither-perception-nor-non-perception). Finally he releases the widest possible frames: “I shall not grasp this world” and “I shall not grasp the other world,” and then anything at all “seen, heard, thought, known, attained, sought, and explored by my mind.” Nothing, at any scale, is left as a foothold for identity or ownership.

The refrain. Each item receives the identical formula: “I shall not grasp [x], and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on [x]. That’s how you should train.” The content changes; the structure of release does not.

Tears, and a striking admission

Anāthapiṇḍika bursts into tears. Ānanda, alarmed, asks if he is “failing” — a euphemism for a mind giving way under the strain of illness. Anāthapiṇḍika says no: he has followed the Buddha and his monastics for a long time, and has simply never before heard teaching of this depth. Sāriputta's reply is candid about why: “such Dhamma talk does not strike when teaching white-clothed laypeople. Rather, it strikes when teaching those gone forth” — that is, this level of instruction was not typically offered to householders, who were usually given teachings on generosity, ethical conduct, and heaven rather than the full analysis given to renunciants. Anāthapiṇḍika’s response reframes the moment as more than personal: “let such Dhamma talk strike when teaching white-clothed laypeople as well… there will be those who understand the teaching.” He is not just receiving the teaching; he is arguing, from his deathbed, that it should be given more widely.

Death, rebirth, and a return in radiant form

Sāriputta and Ānanda leave; Anāthapiṇḍika dies not long after, reborn among the joyful gods. That same night, as a “glorious godling,” he returns to Jeta’s Grove — the property he once purchased for the Buddha — lighting it up with his radiance, and speaks a verse of praise: for ethical conduct and livelihood, not birth or wealth, are what truly purify a person, and Sāriputta above all is “full of wisdom, ethics, and peace.” He circles the Buddha respectfully and vanishes. The next morning, hearing the account, Ānanda deduces correctly that the godling must have been Anāthapiṇḍika himself, devoted as he always was to Sāriputta — and the Buddha confirms it.

Why teach a dying man this way

The discourse is remarkable less for any single doctrinal point than for what it implies about audience and timing. Sāriputta does not simplify the teaching for a layperson in his final hours; if anything, he gives Anāthapiṇḍika the fullest, most systematic version of non-attachment found anywhere in the Majjhima Nikāya, holding nothing back because there is, at the point of death, nothing left to protect him from. The tears are not distress at hearing something frightening, but the recognition — explicit in his own words — that such a complete teaching had simply never been offered to him before, not because he was incapable of it, but because convention had reserved it for monastics.

Key terms

Anāthapiṇḍika — “feeder of the destitute,” an honorific name for the merchant Sudatta, who purchased Jeta’s Grove at Sāvatthī and became the Buddha’s foremost lay patron.
na upādiyissāmi — “I shall not grasp”: the recurring formula of this discourse, applied in turn to the senses, their objects, the resulting consciousness, contact, and feeling, the elements, the aggregates, the formless attainments, and finally to any world at all.
the four formless attainments — the dimensions of infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness, and neither-perception-nor-non-perception; the most refined states of meditative absorption, here explicitly included among what must not be grasped.
“white-clothed laypeople” — the standard description of householders as opposed to the ochre-robed monastic Saṅgha; Sāriputta’s comment that such teaching “does not strike” when given to laypeople reflects a documented convention, which Anāthapiṇḍika immediately challenges.
the joyful gods — the heavenly realm (Tusita, in later tradition) into which Anāthapiṇḍika is reborn, from which he returns the same night to praise the teaching and the teacher.

The text

The discourse in full: Anāthapiṇḍika’s illness and the messages sent, Sāriputta’s visit and his complete instruction on non-grasping, and the narrative of Anāthapiṇḍika’s death, rebirth, and return. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A message sent twice

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery.

Now at that time the householder Anāthapiṇḍika was sick, suffering, gravely ill. Then he addressed a man, “Please, my man, go to the Buddha, and in my name bow with your head to his feet. Say to him: ‘Sir, the householder Anāthapiṇḍika is sick, suffering, gravely ill. He bows with his head to your feet.’ Then go to Venerable Sāriputta, and in my name bow with your head to his feet. Say to him: ‘Sir, the householder Anāthapiṇḍika is sick, suffering, gravely ill. He bows with his head to your feet.’ And then say: ‘Sir, please visit him at his home out of sympathy.’” “Yes, sir,” that man replied. He did as Anāthapiṇḍika asked… Sāriputta consented with silence.

The pain, described without euphemism

§3–4Then Venerable Sāriputta robed up in the morning and, taking his bowl and robe, went with Venerable Ānanda as his second monk to Anāthapiṇḍika’s home. He sat down on the seat spread out, and said to Anāthapiṇḍika, “I hope you’re keeping well, householder; I hope you’re all right. And I hope the pain is fading, not growing, that its fading is evident, not its growing.”

“I’m not keeping well, Honorable Sāriputta, I’m not getting by. The pain is terrible and growing, not fading, its growing, not its fading, is evident. The winds piercing my head are so severe, it feels like a strong man drilling into my head with a sharp point. The pain in my head is so severe, it feels like a strong man tightening a strong leather strap around my head. The winds slicing my belly are so severe, like a deft butcher or their apprentice were slicing open a cow’s belly with a sharp meat cleaver. The burning in my body is so severe, it feels like two strong men grabbing a weaker man by the arms to burn and scorch him on a pit of glowing coals. That’s how severe the burning is in my body. I’m not keeping well, Honorable Sāriputta, I’m not getting by. The pain is terrible and growing, not fading, its growing, not its fading, is evident.”

Not grasping, field by field

§5–9“That’s why, householder, you should train like this: ‘I shall not grasp the eye, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on the eye.’ That’s how you should train. You should train like this: ‘I shall not grasp the ear, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on the ear.’… ‘I shall not grasp the nose, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on the nose.’… ‘I shall not grasp the tongue, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on the tongue.’… ‘I shall not grasp the body, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on the body.’… ‘I shall not grasp the mind, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on the mind.’ That’s how you should train.

You should train like this: ‘I shall not grasp sight, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on sight.’… ‘I shall not grasp sound… smell… taste… touch… idea, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on idea.’ That’s how you should train.

You should train like this: ‘I shall not grasp eye consciousness, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on eye consciousness.’… ‘I shall not grasp ear consciousness… nose consciousness… tongue consciousness… body consciousness… mind consciousness, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on mind consciousness.’ That’s how you should train.

You should train like this: ‘I shall not grasp eye contact… ear contact… nose contact… tongue contact… body contact… mind contact, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on mind contact.’ That’s how you should train.

You should train like this: ‘I shall not grasp feeling born of eye contact… feeling born of ear contact… feeling born of nose contact… feeling born of tongue contact… feeling born of body contact… feeling born of mind contact, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on the feeling born of mind contact.’ That’s how you should train.

Elements, aggregates, and beyond every world

§10–14You should train like this: ‘I shall not grasp the earth element… water element… fire element… air element… space element… consciousness element, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on the consciousness element.’ That’s how you should train.

You should train like this: ‘I shall not grasp form… feeling… perception… choices… consciousness, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on consciousness.’ That’s how you should train.

You should train like this: ‘I shall not grasp the dimension of infinite space… the dimension of infinite consciousness… the dimension of nothingness… the dimension of neither perception nor non-perception, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on the dimension of neither perception nor non-perception.’ That’s how you should train.

You should train like this: ‘I shall not grasp this world, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on this world.’ That’s how you should train. You should train like this: ‘I shall not grasp the other world, and there shall be no consciousness of mine dependent on the other world.’ That’s how you should train. You should train like this: ‘I shall not grasp whatever is seen, heard, thought, known, attained, sought, and explored by my mind, and my consciousness will not have that as support.’ That’s how you should train.”

Tears, and a striking admission

§15When he said this, Anāthapiṇḍika cried and burst out in tears. Venerable Ānanda said to him, “Are you failing, householder? Are you fading, householder?” “No, Honorable Ānanda. But for a long time I have paid homage to the Buddha and the esteemed mendicants. Yet I have never before heard such a Dhamma talk.” “Householder, such Dhamma talk does not strike when teaching white-clothed laypeople. Rather, it strikes when teaching those gone forth.” “Well then, Honorable Sāriputta, let such Dhamma talk strike when teaching white-clothed laypeople as well! There are gentlemen with little dust in their eyes. They’re in decline because they haven’t heard the teaching. There will be those who understand the teaching!”

Death, rebirth, and a return in radiant form

§16–18And when the venerables Sāriputta and Ānanda had given the householder Anāthapiṇḍika this advice they rose from their seat and left. Not long after they had left, Anāthapiṇḍika passed away and was reborn in the host of joyful gods.

Then, late at night, the glorious godling Anāthapiṇḍika, lighting up the entire Jeta’s Grove, went up to the Buddha, bowed, stood to one side, and addressed the Buddha in verse:

“This is indeed that Jeta’s Grove,
frequented by the Saṅgha of seers,
where the King of Dhamma stayed:
it brings me joy!

Deeds, knowledge, and principle;
ethical conduct, an excellent livelihood;
by these are mortals purified,
not by clan or wealth.

That’s why an astute person,
seeing what’s good for themselves,
would examine the teaching rationally,
that’s how they become purified in that.

Sāriputta is full of wisdom,
ethics, and peace.
Even a mendicant who has crossed over
might at best equal him.”

This is what the godling Anāthapiṇḍika said, and the teacher approved. Then the godling Anāthapiṇḍika, knowing that the teacher approved, bowed and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on his right, before vanishing right there.

Ānanda's deduction, confirmed

§19–20Then, when the night had passed, the Buddha told the mendicants all that had happened.

When he had spoken, Venerable Ānanda said to the Buddha: “Sir, that godling must surely have been Anāthapiṇḍika. For the householder Anāthapiṇḍika was devoted to Venerable Sāriputta.” “Good, good, Ānanda. You’ve reached the logical conclusion, as far as logic goes. For that was indeed the godling Anāthapiṇḍika.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Ānanda approved what the Buddha said.

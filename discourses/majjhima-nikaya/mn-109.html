Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 109

The Longer Discourse on the Full-Moon Night

Mahāpuṇṇamasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in the Eastern Monastery, on the full-moon sabbath night
Speakers
The Buddha and an inquiring mendicant, before the assembled Saṅgha
Form
A catechism on the five aggregates, ending in a misframed question corrected and many minds freed.
Length
~15 minutes to read
Northern parallel
Closely parallel to SN 22.82; a counterpart is preserved in the Saṁyukta Āgama (SĀ 58)
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — the core analysis of the five aggregates and not-self.

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-ninth

On a bright full-moon night, with the whole community gathered in the open, one monk rises and asks the Buddha to walk him through the heart of the teaching — the five aggregates that we take to be a self. Question by question, the Buddha lays bare what they are, where they come from, how the sense of “I” is built on them, and how it can be dismantled. And when a listener stumbles into the oldest objection — if there is no self, who reaps the deeds? — the Buddha turns him gently back to looking, and sixty minds come free.

This is one of the canon’s most complete short treatments of the five grasping aggregates (pañcupādānakkhandhā): form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness — the whole of what a person is, and the whole of what a person mistakes for “me and mine.” The setting is deliberately serene: the sabbath, the full moon, the silent Saṅgha. Into that stillness the questions come one after another, each building on the last, like a teacher being examined by an eager student — and the Buddha answering with the precision that makes this discourse a small map of insight practice.

The questions move from analysis to liberation: what are the aggregates, what roots them, what grasping is, how identity-view (sakkāyadiṭṭhi) arises and ceases, their gratification, drawback, and escape, and finally how to see them so that “I-making, mine-making, and the underlying tendency to conceit” fall away entirely. The discourse’s most famous moment is the wrong question it then heads off — and the way the Buddha answers not the confused thought but the confusion itself, redirecting every mind present back to the one thing that frees: seeing each aggregate, truly, as “not mine, not I, not my self.”

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The five grasping aggregates — form, feeling, perception, choices, consciousness — are rooted in desire, are clung to through desire-and-greed, and are mistaken for a self in twenty ways; but one who sees each of them, without exception, as “not mine, not I, not my self” dismantles all I-making and conceit, grows disenchanted, and is freed.

The aggregates and their root

The monk begins with the basics, and the Buddha confirms them: the five grasping aggregates are form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness. Their root? — “they are rooted in desire” (chanda). It is wanting that gathers the aggregates into a clung-to bundle and keeps the process of becoming turning. Then a subtle point about grasping itself: is the grasping the same as the aggregates, or something separate? Neither, the Buddha says:

Grasping is the desire-and-greed. “The desire and greed for the five grasping aggregates is the grasping there.” The clinging is neither identical to the aggregates nor a thing apart from them — it is the craving directed at them. So liberation is not destroying the aggregates but removing the desire-and-greed that grips them.

And this desire can vary — even projecting a future self: “May I be of such form, such feeling… in time to come!” Craving not only clings to what is; it reaches forward to fashion what will be.

What each aggregate is, and what conditions it

Two crisp answers map the field. The scope of each aggregate is total: “any kind of form at all — past, future, or present; internal or external; gross or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near” — the whole category, with nothing left over to be a hidden self. And each aggregate has its condition:

  • Form is conditioned by the four principal elements (earth, water, fire, air).
  • Feeling, perception, and choices are conditioned by contact.
  • Consciousness is conditioned by name-and-form.

Each is a conditioned, dependent process — arising from causes, not a self-standing substance. This is the ground that makes the not-self insight possible: what depends on conditions cannot be the unconditioned owner one takes it for.

How the sense of self is built — and unbuilt

The pivot of the discourse is identity-view (sakkāyadiṭṭhi) — how the conviction “I am” is constructed on the aggregates. The untrained person, the Buddha says, regards each aggregate in one of four ways: as self; as something the self possesses; as contained in the self; or as containing the self. Four stances times five aggregates gives the famous twenty modes of self-view — the complete grammar of every way a person can plant “me” in experience. Identity-view ceases when the trained noble disciple stops doing exactly this. The self is not an entity to be found and abolished; it is a habit of viewing, to be seen through.

Gratification, drawback, escape

For each aggregate the Buddha gives the threefold analysis that runs throughout his teaching: its gratification (the pleasure and happiness that arise from it), its drawback (that it is impermanent, suffering, and perishable), and its escape (removing and giving up desire-and-greed for it). This is the balanced seeing that neither denies that the aggregates can please nor forgets that they cannot satisfy — and that locates freedom not in the aggregates’ disappearance but in the fading of craving for them.

Seeing so that “I-making” ends

The monk asks the practical heart of it: how to know and see so that there is no “I-making, mine-making, or underlying tendency to conceit” (ahaṅkāra, mamaṅkāra, mānānusaya) regarding this very body and all external things? The answer is the great refrain of insight:

“This is not mine; I am not this; this is not my self.” One sees every kind of each aggregate — past, future, present, near, far, all of it — with right understanding in exactly this way. This is not mine dissolves mine-making and craving; I am not this dissolves the conceit “I am”; this is not my self dissolves identity-view.

The wrong question, and the redirection

Then the discourse’s most instructive moment. A monk in the assembly thinks: “If form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness are not-self, then what self do the deeds done by not-self affect?” It is the perennial objection: no self, so who is reborn, who reaps karma? The Buddha, knowing the thought, names it for what it is — a “futile person, their mind dominated by craving,” trying to leap past the teaching with a question that smuggles a self back in (“the self,” “my deeds”). He does not answer the speculation. Instead he returns the whole assembly to direct investigation:

The catechism of not-self. Is form permanent or impermanent? — Impermanent. Then is it suffering or happiness? — Suffering. Then is what is impermanent, suffering, and perishable fit to be regarded as “mine, I, my self”? — No. (And so for all five.)

The lesson is profound: the question “who receives the deeds?” cannot be answered on its own terms because it presupposes the very self the inquiry dissolves. The Buddha’s reply is not a counter-theory but a turning of attention — stop theorising about a self and look at what is actually here. Seeing the five as not-self, the disciple grows disenchanted; disenchantment fades desire; with the fading of desire comes release: “Rebirth is ended.” The discourse closes with its quiet proof — “while this was being spoken, the minds of sixty mendicants were freed from defilements by not grasping.”

Key terms

pañcupādānakkhandhā — the five grasping aggregates: form, feeling, perception, choices, consciousness. The whole of what a person is, and the whole of what is mistaken for “me and mine.”
rooted in desire (chanda) — wanting is what gathers the aggregates into a clung-to bundle and keeps becoming turning. The desire can even project a future self.
grasping = desire-and-greed — the clinging is neither the aggregates themselves nor a thing apart; it is the craving directed at them. So freedom removes the craving, not the aggregates.
conditions of the aggregates — form from the four elements; feeling, perception, choices from contact; consciousness from name-and-form. Each is a dependent process, not a self-standing substance.
sakkāyadiṭṭhi & the twenty modes — identity-view: regarding each aggregate as self, as the self’s possession, as in the self, or as containing the self — four stances times five aggregates. The complete grammar of self-making.
“not mine, not I, not my self” — the threefold refrain that dissolves, in order, mine-making and craving, the conceit “I am,” and identity-view.
“who receives the deeds?” — the misframed objection that smuggles a self back in. The Buddha answers not the speculation but the confusion, turning attention from theory to direct seeing.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats each aggregate in turn. The chain of questions, the twenty modes of self-view, the threefold refrain, the misframed question, and the closing catechism are given closely. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A night of questions

§1–7So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in the Eastern Monastery. On the full-moon sabbath night, sitting in the open among the Saṅgha, a mendicant rose and asked leave to question him. “Are the five grasping aggregates form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness?” “They are.” “What is their root?” “They are rooted in desire.” “Is that grasping the same as the aggregates, or another thing?” “Neither: the desire and greed for the five grasping aggregates is the grasping there.” “Can there be variety in that desire and greed?” “There can — as when one thinks, ‘In the future, may I be of such form, such feeling…’”

Scope and condition

§8–9“What is the scope of the term ‘aggregate’?” “Any kind of form at all — past, future, present; internal or external; gross or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near — this is the aggregate of form; and so for the rest.” “What is the cause of each aggregate?” “The four principal elements are the cause of form; contact is the cause of feeling, perception, and choices; name-and-form is the cause of consciousness.”

How identity-view arises and ceases

§10–11“How does identity-view arise?” “An untrained ordinary person regards form as self, or self as having form, or form in self, or self in form — and likewise for feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness. That is how identity-view arises.” “And how does it not arise?” “A trained noble disciple does not regard any aggregate in any of those ways. That is how identity-view does not arise.”

Gratification, drawback, escape; and the end of I-making

§12–13“What is the gratification, drawback, and escape of each aggregate?” “The pleasure and happiness that arise from it: its gratification. That it is impermanent, suffering, perishable: its drawback. Removing desire and greed for it: its escape.” “How does one know and see so that there is no I-making, mine-making, or underlying conceit regarding this conscious body and all external signs?” “One sees every kind of each aggregate — all of it — with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’”

The wrong question

§14Then a monk thought: “So the aggregates are not-self — then what self do deeds done by not-self affect?” Knowing his thought, the Buddha said: “It is possible that some futile person here, unknowing, their mind dominated by craving, thinks to overstep the teacher’s instructions with such a question. Mendicants, you have been trained by me to question in all these cases.”

The catechism, and sixty freed

§15–18“Is form permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “Is what is impermanent suffering or happiness?” “Suffering.” “Is what is impermanent, suffering, and perishable fit to be regarded as ‘mine, I, my self’?” “No, sir.” — and so for all five. “Therefore see all of it: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’ Seeing thus, a noble disciple grows disillusioned, desire fades, and they are freed: ‘Rebirth is ended.’” That is what the Buddha said. And while this discourse was being spoken, the minds of sixty mendicants were freed from defilements by not grasping.

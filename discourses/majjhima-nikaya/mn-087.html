Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 87

Born from the Beloved

Piyajātikasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove; and the royal compound of King Pasenadi
Speakers
The Buddha, a grieving householder, King Pasenadi, Queen Mallikā, and the brahmin messenger Nāḷijaṅgha
Form
A hard truth refused and then proven — a saying about love and grief that a mourner cannot bear, brought home to a king by his wise queen.
Length
~10 minutes to read
Northern parallel
EĀ 13.3 (Ekottarika-āgama)
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — simple to read, but the truth it states is one of the hardest to accept.

Why this discourse, eighty-seventh

A father’s only child has died, and he is unmade by it — he cannot work, cannot eat, and wanders to the cremation ground crying, “Where are you, my only child?” He comes to the Buddha for comfort, and hears instead the one thing he cannot bear: “Our loved ones are a source of sorrow.” He leaves angry. The discourse follows that saying as it travels — rejected by the grieving, denied by gamblers, doubted by a king — until a wise queen makes it impossible to deny.

The Buddha’s words are gentle but unflinching: “That is so true — for our loved ones are a source of sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress.” To a man drowning in exactly that sorrow, it sounds like cruelty. “Who could ever think such a thing!” he protests; “our loved ones are a source of joy” — and he walks out. He finds some gamblers who tell him what he wants to hear (“yes, loved ones are joy and happiness”), and goes away comforted by the half-truth.

But the saying reaches the palace. King Pasenadi repeats it to Queen Mallikā as if it were obviously absurd; she replies simply, “If the Buddha said it, then that is how it is.” The king mocks her unquestioning faith — and so she sets out to show him, not tell him. After confirming the Buddha’s words through a messenger, she leads the king, question by question, through everyone and everything he loves, until he sees the truth in his own heart. It is one of the canon’s finest portraits of a teacher who replaces blind agreement with real seeing.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Our loved ones are a source not only of joy but of sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress — for grief is the shadow that love casts, and the more we hold dear, the more we have to lose; the grieving and the comfortable both flee this truth, but the one who sees it clearly — through their own loves — has taken the first step toward a freedom that mere attachment can never reach.

The saying no one wants to hear

“From the beloved is born sorrow” — piyato jāyatī soko — is the discourse’s single, hard truth, and the whole drama is about how people receive it. The grieving father cannot. He came for the comfort that says “your love was beautiful, your loss is a wound in something good”; instead he hears the connection drawn out plainly: the very love that made the child precious is the source of the pain now tearing him apart. To a heart in fresh grief this feels like an attack on love itself, and he rejects it indignantly. The gamblers he turns to give him the other half — “loved ones are joy and happiness” — and he goes away soothed. But notice what has happened: he has chosen the comfortable half of the truth over the whole of it. The Buddha never denied that love brings joy; he completed the picture by naming the sorrow bound up inside the joy. The worldly view sees only one face of love; the Buddha sees both, and will not pretend otherwise even to a mourner.

The examples: where love goes when it loses

When the brahmin messenger asks the Buddha to confirm the saying, the Buddha does not soften it; he illustrates it with case after case from the very city they stand in. A woman whose mother died “went mad and lost her mind,” roaming the streets crying, “Has anyone seen my mother?” The same for one who lost a father, a brother, a sister, a son, a daughter, a husband, a wife. And a darker case still: a man whose wife was to be taken from him by her family killed her and then himself, “so that we shall be together after death.” These are not abstractions; they are love’s wreckage — madness, despair, murder-suicide — the extremity of what grief and possessive attachment can do. The Buddha is not exaggerating love’s danger; he is reporting it. The same bond that, intact, is our sweetest happiness becomes, when threatened or broken, the engine of our deepest derangement. The point is sober: this is simply what attachment does.

Mallikā’s method: from faith to sight

The discourse’s second half is a small masterpiece of teaching, and its hero is Queen Mallikā. When she first agrees with the Buddha’s saying — “If the Buddha said it, then that is how it is” — the king dismisses her as a pupil who parrots her teacher. Her faith, to him, is empty agreement. So Mallikā does something wiser than argue: she turns the king’s own heart into the proof. “Do you love Princess Vajirī, your daughter? If she were to die, would sorrow arise in you?” “My life would fall apart — how could it not?” “This is what the Buddha meant.” She walks him through them all — his daughter, his queen Vāsabhā, his son the general Viḍūḍabha, Mallikā herself, even the realms of Kāsi and Kosala that sustain his comforts — and at each, the king confirms in his own words that to lose what he loves would be to lose himself. The truth he had laughed at as absurd, he now finds written in every attachment he owns. Mallikā has converted her own faith into his direct seeing — the move from “the Buddha said so” to “I see it is so.”

Why this is not a counsel against love

It would be easy to misread the discourse as telling us not to love — but that is not its teaching. The Buddha does not say love is bad; he says it is costly, and that the cost is grief. He is naming a law, not issuing a prohibition: wherever there is cherishing, there is exposure to loss; the size of the love is the size of the wound it can become. This clear seeing matters for two reasons. First, it is honest: those who insist “love is only joy” are setting themselves up to be shattered, as the grieving father was, by a sorrow they refused to see coming. Second, it points beyond itself. If grief is born of clinging attachment (piya), then there is a freedom on the far side of clinging — not a cold heart, but a love that has let go of grasping, the boundless goodwill of one who wishes all beings well without needing to possess them. The Dhammapada draws the conclusion the sutta implies: “From the dear comes grief, from the dear comes fear; for one freed from the dear there is no grief — so whence fear?”

The king’s homage

Pasenadi’s response is the discourse’s quiet climax. He does not grieve or recoil; he is filled with wonder: “How far the Buddha sees with penetrating wisdom!” He calls for water to rinse his hands, then rises, bows toward the Buddha, and three times pays homage: “Homage to the blessed one, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha!” The man who began by mocking his wife’s faith ends by paying the deepest reverence — not because he was argued into it, but because he was led to see for himself a truth about his own heart. And Mallikā, who could have simply insisted, instead gave him eyes. It is a portrait of how the hardest truths are actually transmitted: not by demanding agreement, but by helping another person find the truth already living, unseen, in their own experience.

Key terms

piyato jāyatī soko — “from the beloved is born sorrow.” The discourse’s hard truth: grief is the shadow love casts; the more we cherish, the more we have to lose.
piya — “the dear, the beloved”: a loved person or thing. Its joy and its sorrow are inseparable — the Buddha completes the worldly half-truth by naming both.
the half-truth — “loved ones are a source of joy.” True, but only half. The grieving father and the gamblers cling to it; those who see only love’s joy are set up to be shattered by its grief.
Mallikā’s method — turning faith into sight: rather than insisting the Buddha is right, she leads the king through his own loves until he sees the truth in his own heart.
not a counsel against love — the Buddha names a law, not a prohibition: love is costly, and the cost is grief. The teaching points beyond clinging to a goodwill that does not need to possess.
Pasenadi’s homage — the man who mocked his wife’s faith ends bowing three times, “Homage to the fully awakened Buddha!” — led not by argument but by seeing the truth in himself.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The grieving father, the Buddha’s examples, and Mallikā’s questioning of the king are given in full; the repeated lists of bereaved relatives are condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The grieving father

§1–4So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī. A certain householder’s dear and beloved only child had died; after the death he could not work or eat, and went to the cremation ground wailing, “Where are you, my only child?” He came to the Buddha, who said, “Your faculties are unsteady, householder; there is a deterioration in your faculties.” He explained his grief, and the Buddha said, “That is so true, householder! For our loved ones are a source of sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress.” “Who could ever think such a thing! Our loved ones are a source of joy and happiness!” And rejecting the Buddha’s words, he left. Nearby some gamblers told him what he wished to hear — “yes, loved ones are joy” — and he went away satisfied.

The saying reaches the palace

§5–6The talk reached the royal compound. King Pasenadi said to Queen Mallikā, “Your ascetic Gotama said: ‘Our loved ones are a source of sorrow.’” “If the Buddha said it, great king, then that is how it is.” “Whatever he says, you agree, like a pupil parroting her tutor! Get out!” So Mallikā sent the brahmin Nāḷijaṅgha to ask the Buddha whether he had truly said it.

The Buddha’s examples

§7–22“That is right, brahmin — our loved ones are a source of sorrow. Here in Sāvatthī a woman whose mother died went mad, roaming the streets crying, ‘Has anyone seen my mother?’” The Buddha gives the same for one who lost a father, brother, sister, son, daughter, husband, or wife — each driven out of their mind by grief. And a man whose wife was to be taken from him by her family killed her and then himself, “so that we shall be together after death.” “That too, brahmin, is how our loved ones are a source of sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress.”

Mallikā leads the king to see

§23–28The brahmin reported this to Mallikā, who went to the king. “Great king, do you love Princess Vajirī?” “Indeed I do.” “If she were to die, would sorrow arise in you?” “If she were to die, my life would fall apart — how could sorrow not arise?” “This is what the Buddha meant.” She asked the same of Queen Vāsabhā, of his son General Viḍūḍabha, of herself, and of the realms of Kāsi and Kosala that supply his every comfort — and to each the king answered that to lose them would be to lose himself.

The king’s homage

§29“It is incredible, Mallikā, how far the Buddha sees with penetrating wisdom! Come, rinse my hands.” Then King Pasenadi rose, arranged his robe over one shoulder, raised his cupped palms toward the Buddha, and three times said: “Homage to the blessed one, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha! Homage to the blessed one, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha! Homage to the blessed one, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha!”

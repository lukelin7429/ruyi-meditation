Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 145

Advice to Puṇṇa

Puṇṇovādasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, shortly before Puṇṇa departs for the frontier country of Sunāparanta
Speakers
The Buddha and the mendicant Puṇṇa
Form
A request for brief teaching, answered with a formula on non-clinging to the six senses, then tested through six escalating questions about violence and death
Length
~12 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No specific Āgama match is confidently established in the available commentary, though teachings on non-clinging to the six sense fields recur throughout the parallel Saṃyukta-āgama material on the six sense bases
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a short, vivid narrative; its central formula is simple, though its final image rewards careful reading

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-forty-fifth

A monk asks for a teaching brief enough to carry into solitude, then reveals where he intends to carry it: a frontier so violent the Buddha warns him about it directly. What follows is one of the most quietly dramatic exchanges in the entire Majjhima Nikāya — six escalating questions about being abused, beaten, and finally killed — and Puṇṇa’s answers, each one calmer than the last, turn the interrogation into a demonstration of exactly what the Buddha’s brief teaching, properly absorbed, can do to a mind.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A brief instruction on not clinging to whatever is pleasant in the six senses becomes, in this sutta, the foundation for something much harder to teach directly: the capacity to meet escalating violence, up to and including one’s own death, without a flicker of resentment.

A monk’s request

Venerable Puṇṇa comes out of retreat one afternoon and asks the Buddha for something specific: a teaching “in brief,” so that having heard it he can “live alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute.” This is a recognizable type-scene in the canon — a mendicant who wants a compact teaching precisely because he intends to go off and put it into practice on his own, without further access to the teacher. The Buddha obliges with a formula about the six senses: sights, sounds, smells, tastes, touches, and ideas are all, by nature, “likable, desirable, agreeable, pleasant, sensual, and arousing.” If a mendicant approves of them, welcomes them, and keeps clinging to them, this gives rise to relishing — and relishing, the Buddha says plainly, is the origin of suffering. Turn the same formula around, and it becomes the path out: don’t approve, welcome, or cling, and relishing ceases; when relishing ceases, suffering ceases. It is a compressed restatement of the second and third noble truths, mapped directly onto ordinary sense experience rather than left as abstract doctrine.

Where Puṇṇa intends to go

Having received this teaching, Puṇṇa reveals his plan: he means to go live in a country called Sunāparanta. The Buddha’s response is not encouragement but a warning, stated flatly: “The people of Sunāparanta are wild and rough, Puṇṇa.” Sunāparanta was a real frontier region on India’s northwestern seaboard, well outside the Ganges heartland where the Buddha usually taught — the kind of place later Buddhist geography associates with trade, distance from settled Dhamma culture, and a reputation for violence. Rather than forbidding the trip or softening the warning, the Buddha turns it into a test.

Six escalating questions

What follows is built from one repeated question, each time with the stakes raised: if the people of Sunāparanta do this to you, what will you think of them?

What they might doPuṇṇa’s answer
Abuse and insult himThey’re gracious, since they don’t hit me with their fists
Hit him with their fistsThey’re gracious, since they don’t throw stones at me
Throw stones at himThey’re gracious, since they don’t beat me with a club
Beat him with a clubThey’re gracious, since they don’t stab me with a knife
Stab him with a knifeThey’re gracious, since they don’t take my life
Take his lifeHe has found, without seeking it, what some disciples go looking for a weapon to obtain

At every stage Puṇṇa reframes the lesser violence as evidence of the people’s restraint, rather than as evidence against them — a discipline of attention, not merely a stoic pose. The final answer is the most striking, and the strangest to a modern ear. It alludes to something the tradition records elsewhere: mendicants who, overwhelmed by disgust with the body during meditation on its foulness, sought their own deaths. Puṇṇa’s point is not that he wants to die. It is that the freedom those disciples were desperately seeking through a weapon — release from clinging to body and life — is exactly what he would find, without needing to look for it, if his life were taken from him by someone else’s violence. Nothing that could happen to him, in other words, is capable of touching what he has actually attained.

Approval, and what follows

The Buddha’s response is immediate and warm: “Good, good, Puṇṇa! Having such self-control and peacefulness, you will be quite capable of living in Sunāparanta.” He sends Puṇṇa off with nothing more than that blessing. The epilogue is compressed into a few lines but covers an enormous amount of ground: within his very first rainy-season retreat there, Puṇṇa establishes roughly five hundred male and five hundred female lay followers, and in that same season realizes the three knowledges himself — recollection of past lives, knowledge of beings’ rebirth according to their deeds, and knowledge of the ending of defilements, the traditional mark of full awakening. Some time later, he dies. When mendicants ask the Buddha where Puṇṇa was reborn, the answer refuses the premise of the question: Puṇṇa practiced in line with the teaching, never troubled the Buddha with anxious follow-up questions, and has become “fully quenched” — parinibbāna, the ending of the round of rebirth altogether. There is nowhere he was reborn, because there was no further rebirth to have.

Key terms

nandi — “relishing”: the approving, clinging response to pleasant sense experience that the Buddha identifies here as the direct origin of suffering.
Sunāparanta — a frontier region on India’s northwestern coast, described by the Buddha himself as inhabited by people who are “wild and rough,” and by tradition as one of the most dangerous places a mendicant could choose to live.
the six escalating tests — insult, fists, stones, a club, a knife, and finally death itself; the same reframing move repeated at each level of violence.
tevijjā — the “three knowledges”: recollection of past lives, seeing how beings are reborn according to their deeds, and knowledge of the ending of defilements — realized by Puṇṇa within his first rains retreat in Sunāparanta.
parinibbuto — “fully quenched”: the Buddha’s description of Puṇṇa after his death, meaning final Nibbāna and the end of rebirth, not rebirth into some new destination.

The text

The discourse in full: the Buddha’s brief teaching on the six senses, Puṇṇa’s announcement of his destination, the complete escalating dialogue, and the epilogue recounting his mission and death. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A monk’s request

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Then in the late afternoon, Venerable Puṇṇa came out of retreat and went to the Buddha. He bowed, sat down to one side, and said to the Buddha, “Sir, may the Buddha please teach me Dhamma in brief. When I’ve heard it, I’ll live alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute.” “Well then, Puṇṇa, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, sir,” replied Puṇṇa. The Buddha said this:

The brief teaching

§3–4“Puṇṇa, there are sights known by the eye, which are likable, desirable, agreeable, pleasant, sensual, and arousing. If a mendicant approves, welcomes, and keeps clinging to them, this gives rise to relishing. Relishing is the origin of suffering, I say. There are sounds known by the ear … smells known by the nose … tastes known by the tongue … touches known by the body … ideas known by the mind, which are likable, desirable, agreeable, pleasant, sensual, and arousing. If a mendicant approves, welcomes, and keeps clinging to them, this gives rise to relishing. Relishing is the origin of suffering, I say.

There are sights known by the eye, which are likable, desirable, agreeable, pleasant, sensual, and arousing. If a mendicant doesn’t approve, welcome, and keep clinging to them, relishing ceases. When relishing ceases, suffering ceases, I say. There are sounds known by the ear … smells known by the nose … tastes known by the tongue … touches known by the body … ideas known by the mind, which are likable, desirable, agreeable, pleasant, sensual, and arousing. If a mendicant doesn’t approve, welcome, and keep clinging to them, relishing ceases. When relishing ceases, suffering ceases, I say.

Sunāparanta, and the first question

§5Puṇṇa, now that I’ve given you this brief advice, what country will you live in?” “Sir, there’s a country named Sunāparanta. I shall live there.” “The people of Sunāparanta are wild and rough, Puṇṇa. If they abuse and insult you, what will you think of them?” “If they abuse and insult me, I will think: ‘These people of Sunāparanta are gracious, truly gracious, since they don’t hit me with their fists.’ That’s what I’ll think, Blessed One. That’s what I’ll think, Holy One.”

Five more questions

§5, cont.“But if they do hit you with their fists, what will you think of them then?” “If they hit me with their fists, I’ll think: ‘These people of Sunāparanta are gracious, truly gracious, since they don’t throw stones at me.’ That’s what I’ll think, Blessed One. That’s what I’ll think, Holy One.”

“But if they do throw stones at you, what will you think of them then?” “If they throw stones at me, I’ll think: ‘These people of Sunāparanta are gracious, truly gracious, since they don’t beat me with a club.’ That’s what I’ll think, Blessed One. That’s what I’ll think, Holy One.”

“But if they do beat you with a club, what will you think of them then?” “If they beat me with a club, I’ll think: ‘These people of Sunāparanta are gracious, truly gracious, since they don’t stab me with a knife.’ That’s what I’ll think, Blessed One. That’s what I’ll think, Holy One.”

“But if they do stab you with a knife, what will you think of them then?” “If they stab me with a knife, I’ll think: ‘These people of Sunāparanta are gracious, truly gracious, since they don’t take my life with a sharp knife.’ That’s what I’ll think, Blessed One. That’s what I’ll think, Holy One.”

“But if they do take your life with a sharp knife, what will you think of them then?” “If they take my life with a sharp knife, I’ll think: ‘There are disciples of the Buddha who looked for a suicide weapon because they were horrified, repelled, and disgusted with the body and with life. And I have found this without looking!’ That’s what I’ll think, Blessed One. That’s what I’ll think, Holy One.”

Approval, and the epilogue

§6–8“Good, good Puṇṇa! Having such self-control and peacefulness, you will be quite capable of living in Sunāparanta. Now, Puṇṇa, go at your convenience.”

And then Puṇṇa welcomed and agreed with the Buddha’s words. He rose from his seat, bowed, and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on his right. Then he set his lodgings in order and, taking his bowl and robe, set out for Sunāparanta. Traveling stage by stage, he arrived at Sunāparanta, and stayed there. Within that rainy season he confirmed around five hundred male and five hundred female lay followers. And within that same rainy season he realized the three knowledges. Some time later he became fully extinguished.

Then several mendicants went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and said to him, “Sir, the gentleman named Puṇṇa, who was advised in brief by the Buddha, has passed away. Where has he been reborn in his next life?” “Mendicants, Puṇṇa was astute. He practiced in line with the teaching, and did not trouble me about the teachings. Puṇṇa has become fully quenched.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said.

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 59

The Many Kinds of Feeling

Bahuvedanīyasutta

Setting
Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speakers
The Buddha and Ānanda; framed by a dispute between Venerable Udāyī and the chamberlain Pañcakaṅga
Form
A dispute resolved into a teaching — the Buddha grants two rival counts of feeling, then unfolds an ascending scale of happiness up to the cessation of feeling itself.
Length
~10 minutes to read
Northern parallel
SĀ 485 (Saṃyukta-āgama 485); a Pāli doublet at SN 36.19, with Sanskrit fragments
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the structure is clear, but the closing redefinition of “happiness” — that the cessation of feeling is the highest happiness — is deep and easily missed.

Why this discourse, fifty-ninth

Two of the Buddha’s followers fall into a stubborn argument: did the Buddha teach two kinds of feeling or three? Each is sure, neither will yield. When the matter reaches the Buddha, he does something disarming — he tells them both are right. From this small lesson in not clinging to one’s own framing he opens into something vast: an ascending scale of happiness that rises past every pleasure we know, to a summit that turns our very idea of happiness inside out.

The first half is about how the teaching is presented. The Buddha has classified feeling in many ways — as two, three, five, six, eighteen, thirty-six, even a hundred and eight — each scheme valid for its purpose. The error is not in counting differently but in insisting that one’s own count is the only true one. Those who refuse to grant what others have “well spoken” will “fight, quarrel, and dispute”; those who concede it will live “blending like milk and water, regarding each other with kindly eyes.” It is a quiet charter against doctrinal quarreling.

The second half climbs. Starting from the pleasure of the five senses, the Buddha names a finer happiness, and a finer one beyond that, through the four absorptions and the four formless attainments — and then past even those, to the cessation of perception and feeling. And here is the turn that has occupied readers ever since: he calls that cessation a happiness, even though no feeling is felt there at all. When this puzzles outsiders, the answer is given plainly: “When the Buddha describes what is included in happiness, he is not just referring to pleasant feeling.” The highest happiness is not a sublime sensation but the peace where even sensation has been laid down.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Buddha taught feeling in many valid schemes (two, three, five… a hundred and eight), so disputing over the count is needless; and happiness itself rises in a graded scale from sensual pleasure through the absorptions and formless attainments to the cessation of perception and feeling — for the Buddha calls “happiness” not only pleasant feeling, but peace wherever it is found, even the peace beyond all feeling.

The dispute: two feelings or three?

The chamberlain Pañcakaṅga asks Venerable Udāyī how many feelings the Buddha taught. Udāyī says three: pleasant, painful, and neutral. Pañcakaṅga objects: the Buddha taught only two — pleasant and painful — because he called neutral feeling “a peaceful and subtle kind of pleasure,” folding it into the pleasant. Three times each repeats his position, and “neither was able to persuade the other.” It is the perfect picture of a doctrinal stalemate: two people, each holding a true statement, each unable to see that the other’s is also true.

“Both are right”: the many modes of teaching

When Ānanda reports the argument, the Buddha’s verdict is generous to both: “The explanation by Udāyī, which Pañcakaṅga didn’t agree with, was quite correct. But the explanation by Pañcakaṅga, which Udāyī didn’t agree with, was also quite correct.” He has taught feeling, he says, “in one explanation as two; in another as three, or five, six, eighteen, thirty-six, or a hundred and eight.” These are not contradictions but different modes of presentation — ways of cutting the same reality for different purposes. The two-fold scheme stresses the basic poles of experience; the three-fold adds the neutral; the larger numbers analyze feeling by sense-door, by past/present/future, and so on. Each is “well spoken” in its place.

Harmony, not quarrel

The Buddha draws the practical lesson sharply. Where the teaching is given in many valid ways, “you can expect that those who don’t concede, approve, or agree with what has been well spoken will fight, quarrel, and dispute, continually wounding each other with barbed words.” But “those who do concede… will live in harmony, appreciating each other, without quarreling, blending like milk and water, and regarding each other with kindly eyes.” The image of milk and water — two things poured together until they cannot be told apart — is the canon’s emblem of a community at peace. The root of so much religious quarreling, the Buddha implies, is simply the refusal to allow that another’s true framing can stand alongside one’s own.

The ascending scale of happiness

The discourse then turns from feeling to happiness (sukha), and builds a ladder. At each rung the Buddha says: some would call this the highest happiness, “but I don’t grant them that — because there is another pleasure finer than that.”

  1. Sensual pleasure — the happiness arising from the five strands of sense-stimulation.
  2. The first absorption — rapture and bliss born of seclusion.
  3. The second, third, and fourth absorptions — each stiller and finer.
  4. Infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness — the formless attainments.
  5. Neither perception nor non-perception — the subtlest of states.
  6. The cessation of perception and feeling — finer than all the rest.

The structure makes a quiet but radical claim: the pleasures the world chases — and even the sublime bliss of deep meditation — are real, but none is the summit. Each is surpassed. Happiness deepens precisely as the coarser, more agitated forms of pleasure are let go.

The summit: happiness beyond feeling

The ladder ends somewhere unexpected. The highest happiness is the cessation of perception and feeling — a state in which the stream of experience has entirely stopped. And the Buddha includes it in “happiness.” He anticipates the obvious objection: outsiders may say, “The ascetic Gotama speaks of the cessation of perception and feeling, yet includes it in happiness — what’s up with that?” If happiness is a pleasant feeling, then a state with no feeling cannot be happy. The objection is exactly right — and that is the point.

What “happiness” really means

The answer reframes everything: “When the Buddha describes what is included in happiness, he is not just referring to pleasant feeling. The Realized One describes pleasure as included in happiness wherever it is found, and in whatever context.” Here sukha, “happiness,” is not the same as sukha-vedanā, “pleasant feeling.” Pleasant feeling is one kind of happiness — the agitated, conditioned kind, which arises and passes and must be felt. But there is a deeper happiness that is not a feeling at all: the peace of cessation, where the very restlessness of feeling has been stilled. As Venerable Sāriputta puts it elsewhere, it is precisely because nothing is felt there that it is the highest happiness. This is the discourse’s great gift: it loosens our grip on the assumption that to be happy is to feel something pleasant, and points beyond it — toward the unconditioned peace that the whole path is for.

Key terms

vedanā — feeling: the affective tone of every experience. Classed most simply as three (pleasant, painful, neutral) or two (pleasant, painful), but also as five, six, eighteen, thirty-six, or a hundred and eight.
pariyāya — a mode or method of presentation. The Buddha taught feeling “in one pariyāya” as two, “in another” as three — different valid framings of the same reality, not contradictions.
sukha — happiness, pleasure, well-being. The discourse’s key word: sukha is broader than pleasant feeling. The Buddha includes in it “pleasure wherever it is found” — even the cessation of feeling.
sukha-vedanā — pleasant feeling. One kind of happiness — the conditioned, felt kind — but not the whole of it, and not the highest.
saññāvedayitanirodha — the cessation of perception and feeling: the deepest attainment, in which the stream of experience stops. Here named the finest happiness of all — a peace beyond what is felt.
khīrodakībhūta — “blended like milk and water”: the image of a community living in true harmony, conceding what others have well spoken, “regarding each other with kindly eyes.”

The text

The full discourse. The ascending scale of happiness is given with the first absorption and the highest states in full; the intervening attainments are abbreviated with “…” as in the source. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Two feelings or three?

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Then the chamberlain Pañcakaṅga went up to Venerable Udāyī, bowed, sat down, and said, “Sir, how many feelings has the Buddha spoken of?” “Chamberlain, the Buddha has spoken of three feelings: pleasant, painful, and neutral.” “Sir, the Buddha hasn’t spoken of three feelings. He’s spoken of two: pleasant and painful. The Buddha said that neutral feeling is included as a peaceful and subtle kind of pleasure.”

§2–3For a second and a third time, Udāyī said, “The Buddha hasn’t spoken of two feelings, he’s spoken of three.” And for a second and a third time, Pañcakaṅga said, “The Buddha hasn’t spoken of three feelings, he’s spoken of two.” But neither was able to persuade the other.

“Both are right”

§4–5Venerable Ānanda heard this discussion, and went and told the Buddha all that had been said. The Buddha said, “Ānanda, the explanation by the mendicant Udāyī, which Pañcakaṅga didn’t agree with, was quite correct. But the explanation by Pañcakaṅga, which Udāyī didn’t agree with, was also quite correct. In one explanation I’ve spoken of two feelings. In another explanation I’ve spoken of three feelings, or five, six, eighteen, thirty-six, or a hundred and eight feelings. I’ve explained the teaching in all these different ways. This being so, you can expect that those who don’t concede, approve, or agree with what has been well spoken will fight, quarrel, and dispute, continually wounding each other with barbed words. But you can expect that those who do concede, approve, or agree with what has been well spoken will live in harmony, appreciating each other, without quarreling, blending like milk and water, and regarding each other with kindly eyes.”

The ascending scale of happiness

§6“There are these five kinds of sensual stimulation. What five? Sights known by the eye… sounds… smells… tastes… touches known by the body, which are likable, desirable, agreeable, pleasant, sensual, and arousing. These are the five kinds of sensual stimulation. The pleasure and happiness that arise from these five kinds of sensual stimulation is called sensual pleasure.

§7There are those who might say that this is the highest pleasure and happiness that sentient beings experience. But I don’t grant them that. Why is that? Because there is another pleasure that is finer than that. And what is that pleasure? It’s when a mendicant, quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unskillful qualities, enters and remains in the first absorption, which has the rapture and bliss born of seclusion, while placing the mind and keeping it connected. This is a pleasure that is finer than that.

The same pattern repeats, each state “finer than” the last: the second absorption (rapture and bliss born of immersion) … the third (equanimous and mindful, meditating in bliss) … the fourth (pure equanimity, beyond pleasure and pain) … the dimension of infinite space … of infinite consciousness … of nothingness … of neither perception nor non-perception. Each time: “some would say this is the highest happiness, but I don’t grant them that, because there is another finer than that.”

§15There are those who would say that this is the highest pleasure and happiness that sentient beings experience. But I don’t grant them that. Why is that? Because there is another pleasure that is finer than that. And what is that pleasure? It’s when a mendicant, going totally beyond the dimension of neither perception nor non-perception, enters and remains in the cessation of perception and feeling. This is a pleasure that is finer than that.

Happiness beyond feeling

§16It’s possible that wanderers of other religions might say, ‘The ascetic Gotama spoke of the cessation of perception and feeling, and he includes it in happiness. What’s up with that?’ When wanderers of other religions say this, you should say to them, ‘Reverends, when the Buddha describes what’s included in happiness, he’s not just referring to pleasant feeling. The Realized One describes pleasure as included in happiness wherever it is found, and in whatever context.’”

That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Ānanda approved what the Buddha said.

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 84

At Madhurā

Madhurasutta

Setting
Near Madhurā, in the Nut Grass Grove — after the Buddha’s final extinguishment
Speakers
Venerable Mahākaccāna and King Avantiputta of Madhurā
Form
A reasoned refutation — the great elder dismantles the brahmin claim of caste supremacy with five arguments drawn from the king’s own experience.
Length
~10 minutes to read
Northern parallel
SĀ 548 (Saṁyukta-āgama 548)
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — clear and argumentative; a model of how the Dhamma reasons against social prejudice.

Why this discourse, eighty-fourth

Are some people, by birth, simply better than others? The brahmins of ancient India taught exactly that — that their caste alone was pure, noble, and divinely descended, and all others beneath them. A king brings this claim to Venerable Mahākaccāna, and the great elder answers it not with scripture or assertion but with five plain questions about the world the king already knows — and by the end, the doctrine of inborn superiority lies in pieces.

The speaker is not the Buddha but Mahākaccāna, the disciple the Buddha praised as foremost in drawing out the full meaning of a brief saying — and the setting is notable: the discourse takes place after the Buddha’s final extinguishment, one of the rare suttas to do so. The questioner is King Avantiputta of Madhurā, who relays the brahmins’ sweeping claim: “Only brahmins are the best class; others are inferior. Only brahmins are pure. Only brahmins are the true-born sons of Brahmā, born from his mouth.”

Mahākaccāna’s reply is calm and devastating: “That is just a saying in the world” — mere noise, unbacked by anything real. And he proves it not by counter-assertion but by leading the king, step by step, through the four domains that actually shape a human life — wealth and social power, the results of bad deeds, the results of good deeds, the law, and the religious life — and showing that in every one of them, the four castes stand exactly equal. What divides people is never their birth-class, but only what they do.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The brahmin claim that one caste is inherently the best, purest, and divinely descended is “just a saying in the world” with nothing behind it — for in wealth and social power, in the results of bad deeds and of good deeds, before the law, and in the religious life, the four classes are exactly equal, and what truly distinguishes a person is not their birth but their conduct.

The claim, and the method of answering it

The brahmin doctrine the king reports is sweeping and theological: brahmins are the “fair” caste and others “dark”; brahmins alone are pure; brahmins are “Brahmā’s true-born sons, born from his mouth” — an allusion to the old creation hymn in which the priestly class issues from the creator’s mouth, the warriors from his arms, and so on. It is a claim of inborn, metaphysical superiority. Mahākaccāna does not meet it with a rival theology. He calls it what it is — ghoso, “a mere saying, hearsay” — and then does something more powerful than asserting the opposite: he asks the king to look. Five times he poses a concrete question about ordinary life, and five times the king, reasoning from his own experience, is forced to conclude: “these four classes are equal; I can see no difference between them.” The truth is established not by authority but by the king’s own honest observation.

The five proofs of equality

Each argument takes a domain in which superiority might be claimed, and shows the castes faring identically:

DomainThe testResult
1. Wealth & powerIf any caste prospers, do members of all four castes serve and defer to him?Yes — precedence follows wealth, not birth
2. Bad deedsIf a member of any caste does the ten unwholesome deeds, is he reborn in hell?Yes — equally, all four
3. Good deedsIf a member of any caste does the ten wholesome deeds, is he reborn in heaven?Yes — equally, all four
4. The lawIf a member of any caste turns bandit, how do you punish him?The same — “he is just reckoned a bandit”
5. The religious lifeIf a member of any caste goes forth as an ethical ascetic, how do you treat him?The same — with reverence; “he is just reckoned an ascetic”

The five span the whole range of what could matter: social standing, the moral order of rebirth (in both its grim and its bright directions), the civil law, and the spiritual realm. In not one of them does birth-caste make a particle of difference. Wherever you look — how others treat you, where your deeds lead you, how the king’s court judges you, how a holy life is honoured — the four classes are level.

The dissolving of caste into conduct

The fourth and fifth arguments carry a sharper point, visible in the king’s own words. The criminal, whatever his birth, “has lost his former status as an aristocrat (or brahmin, or whatever) and is just reckoned a bandit.” The renunciant, whatever his birth, “has lost his former status… and is just reckoned an ascetic.” In other words, what a person actually is — how they are treated, named, and judged — is determined by their conduct and their role, not by the caste they were born into. Birth-class is not some indelible essence; it is a label that real action overwrites. Become a criminal and you are a criminal; become a renunciant and you are a renunciant. The caste recedes to irrelevance behind the deed. This is the Buddhist position in miniature: a person is defined by their kamma — what they choose and do — not by the accident of their birth.

Why this mattered — and still does

It is hard to overstate how radical this was. In a society organised top to bottom around the conviction that worth was inherited with the blood, the Dhamma quietly insisted that the only meaningful hierarchy is moral, not hereditary — that a menial of good conduct is genuinely superior to a brahmin of bad conduct, and that any of the four castes can fall to hell or rise to heaven or reach awakening on exactly equal terms. The argument is also a model of method: against a prejudice dressed in religious authority, Mahākaccāna offers neither outrage nor counter-dogma, but a patient appeal to observable fact, letting the prejudiced person dismantle the prejudice himself. The brahmins’ grand claim is not denounced; it is simply shown to have nothing behind it — “just a saying in the world.”

“Go for refuge not to me”

Convinced, King Avantiputta declares he goes for refuge “to the worthy Kaccāna.” And here the elder shows the humility that marks a true disciple: “Do not go for refuge to me, great king. Go for refuge to that same Blessed One to whom I have gone for refuge.” “And where is he?” “The Buddha has already attained final extinguishment.” The detail quietly dates the discourse to after the Buddha’s passing — and the king’s response is moving: “If I heard the Buddha was a hundred leagues away, I would go a hundred leagues to see him. But since he is fully quenched, I go for refuge to that quenched Buddha, to the teaching, and to the Saṅgha.” Even a Buddha no longer in the world remains the true refuge; the teacher points past himself to the Teacher, and the Teacher’s Dhamma stands whether he is present or not. A discourse that began by levelling caste ends by levelling something else: the distance between a living Buddha and a Buddha gone — for the refuge is in the awakening, not the body.

Key terms

vaṇṇa — “class, caste” (literally “colour”). The four: aristocrats (khattiya), brahmins, peasants/merchants (vessa), and menials (sudda). The discourse shows all four equal in every domain that matters.
ghoso — “a mere saying, hearsay, noise.” Mahākaccāna’s verdict on the brahmin claim of supremacy: a sound circulating in the world, with nothing real behind it.
“born from Brahmā’s mouth” — the brahmanical creation myth in which the priestly class issues from the creator’s mouth. The claim of divine, inborn superiority that the discourse dismantles.
“lost his former status” — the king’s own phrase: a criminal is “just reckoned a bandit,” a renunciant “just reckoned an ascetic.” Caste is a label that real conduct overwrites.
kamma over birth — the discourse’s core: a person is defined by what they choose and do, not by the accident of their birth. A menial of good conduct surpasses a brahmin of bad conduct.
Mahākaccāna — the disciple foremost in drawing out the full meaning of the Buddha’s brief sayings. Here he teaches in his own right, after the Buddha’s passing — and points the king’s refuge past himself to the Buddha.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The claim, the five arguments (given once in full pattern and then condensed across the castes), and the closing on refuge are given complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The brahmins’ claim

§1–4So I have heard. At one time Venerable Mahākaccāna was staying near Madhurā, in the Nut Grass Grove. King Avantiputta of Madhurā, hearing of his reputation, came to see him and said: “Worthy Kaccāna, the brahmins say: ‘Only brahmins are the best class; others are inferior. Only brahmins are the fair class; others are dark. Only brahmins are pure, not others. Only brahmins are Brahmā’s true-born sons, born from his mouth, created by him, his heirs.’ What do you say to this?”

First proof: wealth and precedence

§5“Great king, that is just a saying in the world. Here is one way to see it. Suppose an aristocrat prospers in wealth — would there be aristocrats, brahmins, peasants, and menials alike who rise before him, retire after him, and serve him politely?” “There would.” “And the same if a brahmin prospered, or a peasant, or a menial?” “The same.” “Then are the four classes equal, or not?” “Certainly they are equal, worthy Kaccāna. I can see no difference.”

Second and third proofs: the results of deeds

§6–7“Here is another way. Take an aristocrat who kills, steals, lies, is covetous and of wrong view — is he reborn in a bad place?” “He is — I think so, and have heard it from the perfected ones.” “And a brahmin, peasant, or menial who does the same?” “The same.” “Then are the classes equal?” “Equal, worthy Kaccāna.” And conversely: a member of any of the four classes who refrains from such deeds, contented, kind-hearted, of right view, is reborn in a good place, a heaven — equally. “Then are the classes equal?” “Equal, worthy Kaccāna.”

Fourth proof: the law

§8“Take an aristocrat who turns bandit — breaking into houses, robbing, committing adultery — and your men bring him before you. What would you do?” “Have him executed, fined, or banished as the crime deserves. For he has lost his former status as an aristocrat, and is just reckoned a bandit.” “And a brahmin, peasant, or menial who turns bandit?” “The same — he has lost his former status, and is just reckoned a bandit.” “Then are the classes equal?” “Equal, worthy Kaccāna.”

Fifth proof: the religious life

§9“Take an aristocrat who goes forth as an ascetic — ethical, celibate, of good character. How would you treat him?” “I would bow to him, rise for him, offer him a seat and requisites, and arrange his lawful protection. For he has lost his former status as an aristocrat, and is just reckoned an ascetic.” “And a brahmin, peasant, or menial who goes forth likewise?” “The same — he is just reckoned an ascetic.” “Then are the classes equal?” “Equal, worthy Kaccāna. And so it is clear that the brahmins’ claim is just a saying in the world.”

The refuge

§10–11“Excellent, worthy Kaccāna! I go for refuge to the worthy Kaccāna, to the teaching, and to the Saṅgha.” “Do not go for refuge to me, great king. Go for refuge to that same Blessed One to whom I have gone for refuge.” “And where is he now?” “The Buddha has already become fully quenched.” “If I heard he was a hundred leagues away, I would go a hundred leagues to see him. But since he is fully quenched, I go for refuge to that quenched Buddha, to the teaching, and to the Saṅgha. From this day forth, may the worthy Kaccāna remember me as a lay follower gone for refuge for life.”

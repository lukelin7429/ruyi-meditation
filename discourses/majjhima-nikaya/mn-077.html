Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 77

The Longer Discourse with Sakuludāyī

Mahāsakuludāyisutta

Setting
Near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove; the Buddha visits the wanderers’ park at the peacocks’ feeding ground
Speakers
The Buddha and the wanderer Sakuludāyī (“Udāyī”)
Form
A question about devotion that opens into a panorama — why disciples revere the Buddha, answered first by ruling out austerity, then by laying the entire map of the practice before us.
Length
~22 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 207 (Madhyama-āgama 207)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — long but not difficult; the second half is an inventory of the whole path, best skimmed for its sweep.

Why this discourse, seventy-seventh

Why are the Buddha’s disciples so devoted to him — sitting in such silence that not a cough is heard while he teaches hundreds? The wanderer Sakuludāyī thinks he knows: it must be the Buddha’s austere simplicity. The Buddha gently corrects him, and in correcting him opens out the real answer into a vast survey of everything his teaching offers — one of the most complete maps of the path in the canon.

The discourse opens with a vivid contrast. At the debating hall, wanderers have been comparing the great teachers of the day and asking which of them is truly honoured by his own followers. The verdict on the heterodox masters — Pūraṇa Kassapa, Gosāla, the Jain Nātaputta, and the rest — is withering: their disciples interrupt them, shout them down, refute them, and walk away. Even mid-teaching, a follower will cry, “Don’t ask him — he doesn’t know — ask me!” The Buddha’s assembly is the opposite: a crowd “poised expectantly, like people gathered to watch pure honey being pressed at the crossroads,” and even those who fail and disrobe blame only themselves and keep praising the teaching.

So the Buddha asks Sakuludāyī directly: what qualities do you see that earn this devotion? Sakuludāyī names five — the Buddha eats little, is content with any robe, any food, any lodging, and loves seclusion. The Buddha’s reply is surprising and important: those are not the reasons. Many of his own disciples out-do him in every one of those austerities, yet they revere him, not each other. Devotion is not won by being the most extreme ascetic in the room. The true grounds are five quite different things — and the fifth unfolds into the entire architecture of practice.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Disciples revere the Buddha not for out-austering them — many fast more, dress poorer, and dwell rougher than he does — but for five deeper things: his perfect virtue, his honest knowledge that claims only what it sees, his unmatched wisdom, his satisfying answers to their real suffering through the four noble truths, and above all a complete, verifiable path of practice under which great numbers of his disciples reach the consummation of insight.

A community that holds together

The opening tableau is doing real work. The heterodox communities are pictured as fractious and disloyal: teachers heckled by their own pupils, doctrines refuted to their faces, followers defecting with taunts. The Buddha’s assembly is marked by an attentive, trusting silence — the “honey at the crossroads” image catches a crowd leaning in, hungry for what is offered. Most telling is what happens at the edges: even a disciple who fails, quarrels, and disrobes “speaks only praise of the teacher, the teaching, and the Saṅgha,” blaming himself alone — “we were unlucky, we had too little merit” — and stays on as a lay follower keeping the precepts. A teaching is partly judged by how its failures leave it.

Why it is not austerity

Sakuludāyī’s guess — that the Buddha is revered for eating little, wearing and accepting anything, and loving seclusion — is the natural assumption of his world, where holiness was measured in deprivation. The Buddha’s refutation is disarmingly concrete. He sometimes eats a full bowl or more; some of his disciples eat a tiny fraction of that. He sometimes wears fine robes given by householders; some of his disciples wear only rags from charnel grounds. He sometimes accepts invitations to fine rice, stays in snug plastered bungalows, and lives surrounded by crowds of every kind; some disciples eat only gleaned alms, dwell eight months at the foot of a tree, and vanish into the deep forest. “If it were austerity that won their loyalty,” he reasons, “those stricter disciples would not be loyal to me.” The charisma of extremity is not what binds them. It is a quiet but radical point: the worth of a teacher is not the severity of his lifestyle.

The five true qualities

What does bind them is five things of substance:

QualityWhat it means
1. Higher virtueHe possesses “the entire spectrum of ethical conduct to the highest degree.”
2. Honest knowledge & visionHe “claims to know only what he knows, to see only what he sees”; he teaches from direct knowledge, with reason and a demonstrable basis — never bluffing.
3. Higher wisdomSo complete that disciples never interrupt to correct him — they come expecting instruction from him, not to give it.
4. The four noble truthsWhen disciples ask how suffering, its origin, cessation, and the path apply to the suffering they are “swamped and mired” in, he gives answers that satisfy.
5. A complete, working pathHe has laid out a full course of practice under which “many disciples attain perfection and the consummation of insight.”

The first four are about the teacher’s own character and competence: he is good, he is honest about the limits of his knowledge, he is wise, and he speaks to people’s actual pain rather than to abstractions. Authority here is earned, not asserted — a striking contrast to the omniscience-claimers and self-promoters of the surrounding discourses.

The fifth quality: the whole map of practice

The fifth quality is the longest passage, and it is essentially a complete syllabus of the Buddhist path — the Buddha’s answer to “what, concretely, do you actually teach?” It runs through, in order:

  • The thirty-seven aids to awakening — the four kinds of mindfulness, the four right efforts, the four bases of psychic power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven awakening factors, and the noble eightfold path.
  • The contemplative attainments — the eight liberations, the eight dimensions of mastery, and the ten universal meditations (kasiṇa).
  • The four absorptions — each with its famous simile: the kneaded bath-powder ball (first), the spring-fed lake (second), lotuses steeped in cool water (third), and a body wrapped head to foot in white cloth (fourth).
  • Insight and the higher knowledges — seeing body and consciousness clearly (the threaded beryl gem), the mind-made body (reed from its sheath, sword from scabbard, snake from slough), psychic powers (the potter’s clay), the divine ear (the horn-blower), reading others’ minds (the mirror), recollecting past lives (journeys between villages), the divine eye (two houses with a watcher between), and finally the ending of the defilements (a clear mountain lake where every pebble and fish is visible).

The point of the parade is not that every disciple masters all of it, but that the path is concrete, graded, and fruitful: “many of my disciples meditate on that, having attained perfection and the consummation of insight.” What earns devotion, in the end, is not the teacher’s mystique but that his method demonstrably works — it produces, in great numbers, people who have actually arrived.

What earns a teacher loyalty

Read as a whole, the discourse is a quiet manifesto on spiritual authority. Set against the surrounding suttas — the false omniscient of MN 76, the self-glorifying Ājīvakas, the teachers heckled by their own students — it answers the question of what a trustworthy teacher actually looks like. Not the most austere, not the loudest in his claims, not the one who demands deference: but one whose conduct is clean, whose knowledge is honest about its own edges, whose wisdom is deep, who meets people in their real suffering, and who offers a path that carries them, verifiably, to freedom. Sakuludāyī, for his part, is “satisfied” — though, unlike many of the Buddha’s visitors, he does not yet go for refuge; that step waits for another day.

Key terms

adhisīla / adhipaññā — “higher virtue” and “higher wisdom,” the first and third of the true qualities: the Buddha’s complete ethical conduct and his unsurpassed understanding.
honest knowledge & vision — he “claims to know only when he knows,” teaching from direct experience, with reason and a demonstrable basis. The antidote to the bluffing omniscience-claimers of the age.
bodhipakkhiyā dhammā — the thirty-seven “aids to awakening” (mindfulnesses, efforts, bases of power, faculties, powers, awakening factors, eightfold path), the backbone of the fifth quality’s map.
the four absorptions — the jhānas, each given a vivid simile: bath-powder kneaded with moisture, a lake fed from its own depths, lotuses soaked in cool water, a body wrapped in white cloth.
abhiññā-vosāna-pāramippatta — “attained perfection and the consummation of insight.” The recurring refrain: many disciples actually reach each attainment. A path proven by its results.
the honey at the crossroads — the image of the Buddha’s assembly: a crowd poised in eager silence, as people gather to watch pure honey being pressed — the mark of a community that trusts its teacher.

The text

The full discourse, substantially abridged — this is among the longest in the collection. The frame, the contrast of communities, the rejection of the five austerities, and the five true qualities are given in full; the encyclopedic map of practice under the fifth quality is summarized rather than reproduced in its entirety. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Which teacher is truly honoured?

§1–6So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove. Finding it too early for alms, he went to visit the wanderer Sakuludāyī, who hushed his noisy assembly to welcome him. Sakuludāyī reported a debate-hall discussion: of all the famous teachers gathered at Rājagaha for the rains — Pūraṇa Kassapa, Gosāla, Ajita, Pakudha, Sañjaya, the Jain Nātaputta — which is honoured by his disciples? The heterodox teachers, it was agreed, are not: their pupils heckle and refute them — “Don’t ask him, he doesn’t know; ask me!” — and abandon them. But the ascetic Gotama, teaching hundreds, is heard in such silence that not a cough sounds; his crowd waits “poised, like people watching pure honey pressed at the crossroads.” Even those who disrobe blame only themselves and keep praising the teaching.

Not the five austerities

§7–9“But Udāyī, what qualities do you see in me that make my disciples so loyal?” “Five, sir: you eat little, and are content with any robe, any almsfood, and any lodging; and you are secluded, and praise seclusion.” “Suppose they were loyal because I eat little — yet some of my disciples live on a cupful of food, far less than I, who sometimes eat a full bowl or more. Suppose it were contentment with robes — yet some wear only rags from charnel grounds, while I sometimes wear fine robes. So too with food, with lodging — some dwell eight months at the foot of a tree, while I stay in plastered bungalows — and with seclusion, for some vanish into the deep forest, while I live crowded by monks, nuns, laypeople, and kings. If austerity won their loyalty, those stricter disciples would not be loyal to me. So it is not for these five.”

The five true qualities

§10–14“There are five others. First, my disciples esteem me for the higher ethics: ‘He possesses the whole spectrum of ethical conduct to the highest degree.’ Second, for my honest knowledge and vision: ‘He claims to know only what he knows, to see only what he sees; he teaches from direct knowledge, with reason and a demonstrable basis.’ Third, for my higher wisdom: so complete that they never break in to correct me, for they come expecting instruction from me, not to give it. Fourth, when they come swamped and mired in suffering and ask how the four noble truths apply, I give them satisfying answers.”

The fifth: the whole path

§15–37“And fifth, I have explained to my disciples a practice by which they develop the whole path — and many reach its consummation.” The Buddha then lays out the entire map: the four kinds of mindfulness, the four right efforts, the four bases of psychic power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven awakening factors, and the noble eightfold path; the eight liberations, the eight dimensions of mastery, and the ten universal meditations; the four absorptions — each with its simile of the kneaded bath-powder, the spring-fed lake, the steeped lotuses, the body wrapped in white cloth; and the higher knowledges — insight into body and consciousness (the threaded gem), the mind-made body (reed, sword, snake), psychic powers (the potter’s clay), the divine ear (the horn-blower), the reading of minds (the mirror), the recollection of past lives (journeys between villages), the divine eye (two houses and a watcher), and the ending of the defilements (a clear mountain lake where every fish and pebble shows). After each, the refrain: “and many of my disciples meditate on that, having attained perfection and the consummation of insight.”

§37–38“This is the fifth quality because of which my disciples honour, respect, revere, and venerate me, and remain loyal to me.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the wanderer Sakuludāyī approved what the Buddha said.

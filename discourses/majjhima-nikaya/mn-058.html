Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 58

With Prince Abhaya

Abhayarājakumārasutta

Setting
Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground; the teaching is given at Prince Abhaya’s home
Speakers
The Buddha and Prince Abhaya, a son of King Bimbisāra, sent by the Jain leader to refute him
Form
A trap dissolved into a teaching — the Buddha answers a planned dilemma about speech, then lays out the six kinds of speech and what governs when he speaks at all.
Length
~10 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No full Āgama parallel; the analysis of speech is echoed in later treatises (e.g. T 1509, T 1521)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the story is easy to follow and immediately practical; the six-fold grid of speech repays a careful read.

Why this discourse, fifty-eighth

Prince Abhaya is sent to spring a clever trap. The Jain leader has designed a two-horned question about whether the Buddha ever says things people don’t want to hear — a dilemma meant to leave him “like a man with an iron spike stuck in his throat, unable to spit it out or swallow it down.” What Abhaya gets instead is the canon’s clearest and most useful teaching on wise speech.

The trap turns on a false choice. Either the Buddha says unwelcome things — in which case how is he different from an ordinary person? — or he doesn’t, in which case why did he make a harsh prediction about his cousin Devadatta? The Buddha’s reply slips between the horns with three words: “This matter is not categorical.” The real question was never whether speech is pleasant or unpleasant. It is whether speech is true and beneficial — and whether the moment is right to say it.

Then comes the image that anchors the whole teaching. A baby is sitting in Abhaya’s lap, and the Buddha asks what he would do if the child got a stick or stone caught in its mouth. Abhaya answers without hesitation: he would hook it out, even if it drew blood — “because I have sympathy for the boy.” That is exactly why the Buddha will speak a hard truth: not to wound, but out of compassion, the way a loving parent will cause a moment’s pain to save a child from choking. Pleasant or unpleasant is not the point; true, beneficial, timely, and kind — that is the point.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Buddha speaks only what is both true and beneficial — whether it pleases or displeases — and only at the right time; he never speaks the false or pointless, nor the merely-true-but-harmful, however welcome; and the root of it all is compassion, like a parent who hooks a stone from a child’s throat even though it hurts.

The dilemma: a trap with two horns

The Jain leader (the same Niganṭha Nātaputta of MN 56) coaches Prince Abhaya in a classic debating trap: a question that looks unanswerable whichever way it is taken. Ask the Buddha, “May the Realized One say things others dislike?” If he says yes, retort: then how are you different from an ordinary person, who also says unwelcome things? If he says no, retort: then why did you predict that Devadatta would go to hell “for an eon, irredeemable” — words that surely upset him? Either answer, the Jain promises, will stick in the Buddha’s throat. The trap assumes that “pleasant” and “unpleasant” are the only two categories that matter.

The meal, and the trap sprung

There is a touch of comedy in the staging. Abhaya, having accepted the mission, looks up at the sun and decides it is too late in the day — he will spring the trap tomorrow, on home ground, after feeding the Buddha a fine meal. So he invites the Buddha and three monks to his house. Only when the meal is done and the bowl washed does he sit down low and ask his loaded question. The careful preparation makes the gentleness of what follows all the more striking: the Buddha, who surely knows what is afoot, simply accepts the meal and answers plainly.

“Not categorical”: the trap dissolves

To “May the Realized One say things others dislike?” the Buddha replies, “This matter is not categorical, prince” — it cannot be settled with a flat yes or no. Abhaya, who had memorized only the two scripted follow-ups, immediately blurts, “Then the Jains have lost in this, sir!” — and, asked why, spills the entire plan. The dilemma has collapsed not because the Buddha dodged it but because its hidden assumption was false. “Liked” versus “disliked” was never the axis on which the Buddha’s speech turns. Once a third and fourth factor enter — truth and benefit — the neat two-horned question has no force.

The baby and the stick

Before giving the analysis, the Buddha grounds it in an image drawn from the scene itself. A baby boy sits in Abhaya’s lap. “If your boy got a stick or stone in his mouth, what would you do?” Abhaya: “I’d take it out; and if that failed, I’d hold his head and hook it out with a finger, even if it drew blood — because I have sympathy for the boy.” This is the key to the whole discourse. A loving parent will cause a child pain — even draw blood — when it is necessary to save him, and only then. So too the Buddha will say what stings, but only when it is true, beneficial, and timely, and always from care. The criterion is never the parent’s comfort or the child’s momentary pleasure; it is the child’s good.

The six kinds of speech

The Buddha then sorts all speech by three questions — is it true? is it beneficial? is it liked? — and states what he does with each:

The speech is…The Realized One…
Untrue, false, pointless — and dislikeddoes not speak it
True and correct, but harmful — and dislikeddoes not speak it
True, correct, and beneficial — and dislikedknows the right time to speak it
Untrue, false, pointless — but likeddoes not speak it
True and correct, but harmful — even if likeddoes not speak it
True, correct, and beneficial — and likedknows the right time to speak it

Read down the right-hand column and the pattern leaps out: of six possibilities, the Buddha speaks only two — and they are exactly the two that are true and beneficial, whether liked or disliked. Everything false is left unsaid. And, strikingly, even what is true is left unsaid when it is harmful — truth alone is never a license to speak. “Liked” and “disliked” do not decide the matter at all; they only affect the timing.

Truth, benefit, and timing

So the Buddha’s speech rests on three pillars. Truth is the first filter: nothing false is ever spoken, however pleasing. Benefit is the second: of true things, only what conduces to welfare is spoken — the gratuitous true remark that merely wounds is withheld. And timing (kālaññū, “knowing the time”) governs the rest: true and beneficial speech, whether welcome or not, is given when the moment is right for it to land. The thread running through all three is named explicitly: “Because the Realized One has sympathy for sentient beings.” Compassion is not a seventh factor added on; it is the reason the other three are honored at all.

The answer on the spot

Abhaya, won over, asks a further question: when learned visitors arrive with a planned challenge, does the Buddha rehearse his answers, or do they come to him in the moment? The Buddha replies with a question of his own. Abhaya is a skilled charioteer; when someone asks him the name of a chariot-part, does he plan the answer or does it “just appear on the spot”? “On the spot, sir — all the parts are well-known to me.” Just so, the Buddha says: “Because the Realized One has clearly comprehended the principle of the teachings, the answer just strikes him on the spot.” Mastery, whether of chariots or of the Dhamma, means the right response arises of itself, without rehearsal. Abhaya goes for refuge as a lay follower for life.

Key terms

Abhaya-rājakumāra — Prince Abhaya, a son of King Bimbisāra of Magadha. A follower of the Jains, sent to refute the Buddha, who becomes a lay disciple by the end.
na ekaṃsena — “not categorical”; not to be answered one-sidedly. The Buddha’s reply that slips between the two horns of the dilemma: the question needs analysis, not a flat yes or no.
bhūta · attha-saṃhita — true / connected with benefit. The two essential filters of right speech: the Buddha speaks only what is both true and beneficial — never the false, never the merely-true-but-harmful.
piya / appiya — liked / disliked. The very axis the Jain trap relied on — shown to be secondary. Whether speech is welcome affects only its timing, not whether it is spoken.
kālaññū — knowing the time. The Buddha “knows the right time to speak” true and beneficial words, whether liked or disliked — the factor that governs the when.
anukampā — sympathy, compassion. The root motive of all the Buddha’s speech — the reason he will say even what stings, like a parent hooking a stone from a child’s throat.

The text

The full discourse. Where the source marks Abhaya’s retelling of the plan with empty segments, the narrative is given as a brief note. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The trap is set

§1–3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground. Then Prince Abhaya went to the Jain ascetic of the Ñātika clan, bowed, and sat down. The Jain Ñātika said to him, “Come, prince, refute the ascetic Gotama’s doctrine. Then you will get a good reputation: ‘Prince Abhaya refuted the doctrine of the ascetic Gotama, so mighty and powerful!’” “But sir, how am I to do this?” “Go to the ascetic Gotama and say: ‘Sir, may the Realized One utter speech that is disliked by others?’ If he answers, ‘He may, prince,’ say: ‘Then what is the difference between you and an ordinary person? For an ordinary person too may utter speech disliked by others.’ But if he answers, ‘He may not, prince,’ say: ‘Then why did you declare of Devadatta that he is going to hell for an eon, irredeemable? Devadatta was angry and upset with what you said.’ When you put this dilemma to him, the Buddha won’t be able to either spit it out or swallow it down. He’ll be like a man with an iron spike stuck in his throat.”

The meal

§4–5“Yes, sir,” replied Abhaya, and went to the Buddha. But looking up at the sun, he thought, “It’s too late to refute the Buddha’s doctrine today. I shall refute his doctrine in my own home tomorrow.” So he said, “Sir, would the Buddha, together with three other monks, please accept tomorrow’s meal from me?” The Buddha consented with silence. When the night had passed, the Buddha went to Abhaya’s home and sat down. Abhaya served and satisfied him with his own hands with delicious foods. When the Buddha had eaten and washed his hand and bowl, Abhaya took a low seat and sat to one side.

“Not categorical”

§6And he said, “Sir, may the Realized One utter speech that is disliked by others?” “This matter is not categorical, prince.” “Then the Jains have lost in this, sir.” “But prince, why do you say that the Jains have lost in this?” Then Abhaya told the Buddha all that had happened.

The baby and the stick

§7Now at that time a little baby boy was sitting in Prince Abhaya’s lap. Then the Buddha said to Abhaya, “What do you think, prince? If—because of your negligence or his nursemaid’s negligence—your boy were to put a stick or stone in his mouth, what would you do to him?” “I’d try to take it out, sir. If that didn’t work, I’d cradle his head with my left hand and take it out using a hooked finger of my right hand, even if it drew blood. Why is that? Because I have sympathy for the boy, sir.”

The six kinds of speech

§8“In the same way, prince, the Realized One does not utter speech that he knows to be untrue, false, and pointless, and which is disliked by others. The Realized One does not utter speech that he knows to be true and correct, but which is harmful and disliked by others. The Realized One knows the right time to speak so as to explain what he knows to be true, correct, and beneficial, but which is disliked by others. The Realized One does not utter speech that he knows to be untrue, false, and pointless, but which is liked by others. The Realized One does not utter speech that he knows to be true and correct, but which is harmful, even if it is liked by others. The Realized One knows the right time to speak so as to explain what he knows to be true, correct, and beneficial, and which is liked by others. Why is that? Because the Realized One has sympathy for sentient beings.”

The answer on the spot

§9–10“Sir, there are clever aristocrats, brahmins, householders, or ascetics who come to see you with a question already planned. Do you think beforehand that if they ask you like this, you’ll answer like that, or does the answer just strike you on the spot?” “Well then, prince, I’ll ask you about this in return. Are you skilled in the various parts of a chariot?” “I am, sir.” “When they come to you and ask, ‘What’s the name of this chariot part?’ do you think beforehand how you’ll answer, or does the answer appear to you on the spot?” “Sir, I’m well-known as a charioteer skilled in a chariot’s parts. All the major and minor parts are well-known to me. The answer just appears to me on the spot.”

§11–12“In the same way, when clever aristocrats, brahmins, householders, or ascetics come to see me with a question already planned, the answer just strikes me on the spot. Why is that? Because the Realized One has clearly comprehended the principle of the teachings, so that the answer just strikes him on the spot.” When he had spoken, Prince Abhaya said, “Excellent, sir! Excellent! … From this day forth, may the Buddha remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.”

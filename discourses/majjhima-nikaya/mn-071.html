Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 71

To Vacchagotta on the Three Knowledges

Tevijjavacchagottasutta

Setting
Near Vesālī, in the Great Wood, at the hall with the peaked roof; the Buddha visits the wanderer Vacchagotta at the Single Lotus Monastery
Speakers
The Buddha and the wanderer Vacchagotta, here curious and respectful
Form
A friendly visit and a correction — the Buddha sets right a rumour about his powers, then answers two pairs of pointed questions about who reaches heaven and who reaches the end of suffering.
Length
~7 minutes to read
Northern parallel
SĀ 964 (Saṁyukta-āgama 964)
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — short and accessible; the one nuance to grasp is the difference between “constant omniscience” and the three knowledges.

Why this discourse, seventy-first

A rumour was going round that the ascetic Gotama claimed to know and see everything, at every moment, even while asleep. The wanderer Vacchagotta, too courteous to assume the worst, comes straight to the source: “Is that really what you say?” The Buddha’s answer is a quiet but important correction — one that defines what kind of knowledge actually matters on the path.

The scene is warm and unguarded. Finding it too early for his alms-round in Vesālī, the Buddha decides on his own to drop in on Vacchagotta at the wanderers’ park. Vacchagotta is delighted — “Welcome, sir! It’s been a long time” — and offers him a seat. This is the first of three discourses tracing Vacchagotta’s slow turning toward the Buddha (MN 71, 72, 73); here he is simply an interested seeker, testing a report before he believes it.

What he has heard is the claim of total, continuous omniscience — a claim that other teachers of the day, including the Jain leader Mahāvīra and the Ājīvaka founder Gosāla, were said to make. The Buddha flatly disowns it: those who report it “misrepresent me with what is false and untrue.” What he does claim is the three knowledges (tevijjā) — and the difference is the whole point. His knowledge is not a magical all-seeing switched permanently on; it is specific, available “whenever I want,” and aimed at one thing: the understanding that liberates.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Buddha does not claim constant, total omniscience — that report is false; what he claims is the three knowledges, a knowing that is available at will and serves liberation; and on that authority he tells Vacchagotta that heaven is open to many, but the end of suffering only to those who cut the inner fetter of lay life, and never to a path that denies moral cause and effect.

The rumour, and why it is wrong

The claim Vacchagotta has heard is precise: “Knowledge and vision are constantly and continually present to me, while walking, standing, sleeping, and waking.” This is the image of an all-seeing eye that never closes — and the Buddha rejects it as a misrepresentation. The correction is not modesty; it is accuracy. Elsewhere the Buddha denies that anyone knows all things simultaneously, while affirming that he can know whatever he turns his attention to. The popular craving for a teacher who is omniscient in the crudest sense — a mind permanently saturated with all facts — is exactly what he declines to encourage. (Later tradition would soften this and ascribe full omniscience to him; the early text is more careful.)

What “the three knowledges” means

In the brahmanical world, a man of “three knowledges” (tevijja) was a master of the three Vedas. The Buddha quietly repurposes the phrase: his three knowledges are the three realisations of the night of awakening.

KnowledgeWhat is known
Recollection of past livesHis own manifold former existences, “with features and details,” across many eons of the world’s contracting and expanding.
The divine eyeSeeing beings pass away and be reborn “according to their deeds” — the workings of kamma made visible.
The ending of defilementsThe undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom, realised in this very life — liberation itself.

Two features matter. First, each begins “whenever I want” — the knowledge is a capacity exercised at will, not a flood that never stops. Second, the three build toward freedom: the first two reveal the vast machinery of rebirth and the law of action that drives it, and the third is the exit from that machinery. This is knowledge that is, as the commentary puts it, “specific and functional” — it serves the one purpose of liberation, and the Buddha measures the worth of knowing by exactly that.

The fetter of lay life

Reassured, Vacchagotta turns practical. Can a layperson, “without giving up the fetter of lay life,” make an end of suffering? “No, Vaccha.” Can a layperson, without giving it up, still go to heaven? “Not just a hundred… but many more than that.” The key term is the “fetter of lay life” (gihisaṃyojana) — and it is inner, not outer. It is not the house or the family as such, but the attachment to them, the mind still bound to household concerns. A vast number of laypeople, living virtuously, reach the heavens; but the final freedom — the end of all suffering — asks for the cutting of that inner bond, which the householder’s life rarely allows. The point is not that robes are required, but that the heart must be unhooked.

The verdict on the Ājīvakas

Then a sharper exchange. Can an Ājīvaka ascetic make an end of suffering? “No.” Can one even reach heaven? Here the Buddha invokes the first knowledge: looking back ninety-one eons, he can find only a single Ājīvaka who reached heaven — “and he taught the efficacy of deeds and action.” That is, the one exception succeeded precisely by holding the opposite of his school’s doctrine. The Ājīvakas were notorious for denying moral causality — teaching that beings are purified or defiled by fate, not by their own deeds. Vacchagotta draws the conclusion himself: “That sectarian fold is empty even of the chance to go to heaven,” and the Buddha agrees. The judgement is exact and limited: it falls on a doctrine that denies that actions have moral consequences — not on non-Buddhist paths in general. A teaching that cuts the nerve of ethics cannot lead even to a good rebirth, let alone to freedom.

Why the two halves belong together

The discourse looks like two unrelated conversations — one about the Buddha’s knowledge, one about who reaches heaven — but they are one argument. The Buddha first establishes what he knows and how: by the divine eye he has directly seen beings reborn according to their deeds. Only then does he pronounce on destinies, and his pronouncements rest on that seen evidence, not on dogma. When he says the Ājīvaka fold is barren of even a heavenly rebirth, he is not condemning rivals; he is reporting what ninety-one eons of recollection have shown him. The conversation that began by refusing an inflated claim ends by exercising the real one — modest in scope, but grounded, and turned always toward the question that matters: what leads beings to suffering, and what leads them out.

Key terms

tevijjā — “the three knowledges.” A brahmin’s term for mastery of the three Vedas, here repurposed for the Buddha’s three realisations: past lives, the divine eye, and the ending of defilements.
sabbaññū-sabbadassāvī — “all-knowing and all-seeing.” The claim of constant, total omniscience that the Buddha disowns as a misrepresentation; what he affirms instead is knowledge available “whenever I want.”
pubbenivāsānussati — recollection of past lives, the first knowledge; remembering one’s own former existences in detail across many eons.
dibbacakkhu — the “divine eye,” the second knowledge; seeing beings pass away and take rebirth “according to their deeds” — kamma made visible.
gihisaṃyojana — the “fetter of lay life”: the inner attachment to household existence, not the mere outward fact of it. It must be cut for the end of suffering, though not for heaven.
Ājīvaka — a rival ascetic order, founded by Gosāla, that denied moral causality, holding beings to be purified by fate rather than by their deeds. The discourse judges this doctrine barren even of heaven.

The text

The full discourse. The visit, the correction about the Buddha’s knowledge, the three knowledges, and the questions about destinies are given complete; only the long recital of past-life details is lightly trimmed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The Buddha calls on Vacchagotta

§1–4So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Vesālī, at the Great Wood, in the hall with the peaked roof. Now at that time the wanderer Vacchagotta was residing in the Single Lotus Monastery of the wanderers. Then the Buddha robed up in the morning and, taking his bowl and robe, entered Vesālī for alms. It occurred to him, “It’s too early to wander for alms in Vesālī. Why don’t I visit the wanderer Vacchagotta?” So he did. Seeing the Buddha coming in the distance, Vacchagotta said, “Let the Blessed One come, sir! Welcome! It’s been a long time since you took the opportunity to come here. Please, sir, sit down; a seat is ready.” The Buddha sat, and Vacchagotta took a low seat to one side.

The rumour of omniscience

§5Then Vacchagotta said: “Sir, I have heard this: ‘The ascetic Gotama claims to be all-knowing and all-seeing, to know and see everything without exception: “Knowledge and vision are constantly and continually present to me, while walking, standing, sleeping, and waking.”’ I trust that those who say this repeat what you have said, and do not misrepresent you? Is their explanation in line with the teaching?” “Vaccha, those who say this do not repeat what I have said. They misrepresent me with what is false and untrue.”

How to report it rightly: the three knowledges

§6–7“So how should we answer so as to repeat what you have said, with no legitimate grounds for criticism?” “Answering ‘The ascetic Gotama has the three knowledges’ — that would repeat what I have said. For whenever I want, I recollect my many kinds of past lives — one, two, five, ten, a hundred, a thousand, a hundred thousand rebirths; many eons of the world contracting and expanding — ‘There I was named this, my clan was that, such was my food, such my pleasure and pain, and that was how my life ended; passing away from there, I was reborn here.’ So I recollect my many past lives, with features and details.

§8–10And whenever I want, with clairvoyance that is purified and superhuman, I see sentient beings passing away and being reborn — inferior and superior, beautiful and ugly, in a good place or a bad place — and I understand how they fare according to their deeds. And I have realised the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom in this very life, having realised it with my own insight due to the ending of defilements. Answering ‘The ascetic Gotama has the three knowledges’ would be to repeat what I have said, in line with my teaching, with no legitimate grounds for criticism.”

Who reaches heaven, who reaches the end of suffering

§11–12Then Vacchagotta said: “Worthy Gotama, are there any laypeople who, without giving up the fetter of lay life, make an end of suffering when the body breaks up?” “No, Vaccha.” “But are there any laypeople who, without giving up the fetter of lay life, go to heaven when the body breaks up?” “There’s not just one hundred laypeople, Vaccha, or two or three or four or five hundred, but many more than that who, without giving up the fetter of lay life, go to heaven when the body breaks up.”

The Ājīvakas

§13–15“Worthy Gotama, are there any Ājīvaka ascetics who make an end of suffering when the body breaks up?” “No, Vaccha.” “But are there any Ājīvaka ascetics who go to heaven?” “Vaccha, when I recollect the past ninety-one eons, I can’t find any Ājīvaka ascetics who have gone to heaven, except one — and he taught the efficacy of deeds and action.” “In that case, worthy Gotama, that sectarian fold is empty even of the chance to go to heaven.” “Yes, Vaccha, that sectarian fold is empty even of the chance to go to heaven.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the wanderer Vacchagotta approved what the Buddha said.

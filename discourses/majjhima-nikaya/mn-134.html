Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 134

Lomasakaṅgiya and One Fine Night

Lomasakaṅgiyabhaddekarattasutta

Setting
The Banyan Tree Monastery at Kapilavatthu, then a journey to Jeta’s Grove near Sāvatthī
Speakers
Venerable Lomasakaṅgiya, the godling Candana, and the Buddha
Form
The origin story of the “one fine night” verse, traced back to a teaching given to the gods of the Thirty-Three
Length
~8 minutes to read
Northern parallel
Chinese parallels place the original teaching at Rājagaha rather than in the heaven of the Thirty-Three, and avoid this discourse’s narrative wrinkle about what the godling actually remembered
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the shortest and most narratively charming of the four “one fine night” discourses

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-thirty-fourth

The fourth and final discourse built around the “one fine night” verse closes the set by tracing it back to its source — and the source turns out to be considerably more exotic than a human teaching. Here, for the first time in the sequence, a deity actually does remember the verse, and explains where he heard it: not from any human mendicant, but from the Buddha himself, teaching an assembly of gods in their own heaven. What follows is a small, satisfying story about a teaching’s reach — passed from the Buddha to gods, from a god to a mendicant, and finally, in a very human act of diligence, carried on foot across the country to be confirmed at the source.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A godling named Candana reveals that he first heard the “one fine night” verse when the Buddha taught it directly to the gods of the Thirty-Three in their own heaven — and passes it on to a human mendicant, who then travels the length of the country to have the complete teaching confirmed by the Buddha himself.

A godling who actually remembers

The scene opens exactly as it did in MN 133: a “glorious godling,” lighting up the entire monastery, appears late at night to a mendicant — this time Lomasakaṅgiya, staying at Kapilavatthu — and asks whether he remembers “the summary recital and the analysis of the one who has one fine night.” Lomasakaṅgiya doesn’t. But when he asks the godling in return, the answer this time is different: “I do.”

Taught to the gods of the Thirty-Three

Asked how he came to know it, the godling — named Candana, “Sandalwood” — explains: “This one time, the Buddha was staying among the gods of the Thirty-Three at the root of the Orchid Tree, on the stone spread with a cream rug. There he taught the summary recital and the analysis of the one who has one fine night to the gods of the Thirty-Three.” He then recites the verse in full, urges Lomasakaṅgiya to “learn it, memorize it, and remember it—it is beneficial and relevant to the fundamentals of the spiritual life,” and vanishes. The commentary notes an interesting divergence here: the Chinese parallels to this discourse locate the original teaching at Rājagaha, among humans, rather than in a heavenly realm — a case where different transmissions of the same story genuinely disagree about where, and to whom, the Buddha first spoke these words.

A journey to confirm it

Lomasakaṅgiya doesn’t simply take the verse and go. He “set his lodgings in order” at Kapilavatthu and traveled all the way to Sāvatthī and Jeta’s Grove — a real journey across the Sakyan and Kosalan countryside — specifically to ask the Buddha for the complete teaching. It is the same instinct Mahākaccāna urged on the mendicants in MN 133: go to the source, don’t settle for a secondhand account, however reliable the messenger seemed.

“That godling pays attention”

When Lomasakaṅgiya arrives and asks for the teaching, the Buddha first asks whether he knows the god who gave it to him. He doesn’t. The Buddha identifies him: “That godling was named Candana. Candana pays attention, applies the mind, concentrates wholeheartedly, and actively listens to the teaching.” It’s a small but genuine character reference — and the commentary flags a subtle wrinkle in it: Candana is praised here for his skill in learning, and yet, by his own account just given, he had only remembered the verses of the original teaching, not its full analysis. Some ancient versions of this story sidestep the tension entirely by having Candana say from the start that he only heard the verses — but in this telling, the compliment and the god’s own admission sit slightly, humanly, at odds.

Full circle

The Buddha then gives Lomasakaṅgiya the complete summary recital and analysis — the identical verse and the identical explanation given in MN 131, working through what it means to run to the past, anticipate the future, and falter or not falter amid the present, in terms of the five aggregates. The set of four discourses closes exactly where it began, but by a considerably longer and stranger road: a teaching first given to gods, carried down by a god to a human, and finally verified, on foot, at its living source.

Four discourses, four kinds of transmission

Read together, the “one fine night” discourses trace out nearly every way a teaching can travel: given directly by the Buddha (MN 131); taught independently by a senior disciple and confirmed exactly (MN 132, Ānanda); withheld by the Buddha and reconstructed by a different disciple through a genuinely complementary lens, then verified (MN 133, Mahākaccāna); and carried across realms of existence entirely, from a heavenly assembly through a deity to a human seeker who insists on confirming it in person (MN 134). The verse itself never changes. What changes, across all four, is the path by which it reaches the person who needs it — and in every case, the path ends the same way: back at the source.

Key terms

Candana — “Sandalwood,” the godling who heard the Buddha teach the “one fine night” verse to the gods of the Thirty-Three, and passed it on to Lomasakaṅgiya.
the gods of the Thirty-Three (Tāvatiṃsa) — the heaven ruled by Sakka, where the Buddha is said, in this account, to have first taught the verse, at the root of the Orchid (Pāricchattaka) Tree.
“pays attention, applies the mind…” — the Buddha’s character reference for Candana’s listening skill, standing in some tension with Candana’s own admission that he remembered only the verses, not the full analysis.
the journey to Sāvatthī — Lomasakaṅgiya’s decision to travel from Kapilavatthu to confirm the teaching in person, echoing Mahākaccāna’s advice in MN 133 to always go to the primary source.

The text

The discourse in full: the godling’s visit and his account of the heavenly teaching, Lomasakaṅgiya’s journey, the Buddha’s identification of Candana, and the complete verse and analysis. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A godling who actually remembers

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove. Now at that time Venerable Lomasakaṅgiya was staying in the Sakyan country at Kapilavatthu, in the Banyan Tree Monastery. Then, late at night, the glorious godling Candana, lighting up the entire monastery, went up to Lomasakaṅgiya and said: “Mendicant, do you remember the summary recital and the analysis of the one who has one fine night?” “No, reverend, I do not. Do you?” “I also do not. But do you remember just the verses on the one who has one fine night?” “I do not. Do you?” “I do.”

Taught to the gods of the Thirty-Three

§2–4“How do you remember the verses on the one who has one fine night?” “This one time, the Buddha was staying among the gods of the Thirty-Three at the root of the Orchid Tree, on the stone spread with a cream rug. There he taught the summary recital and the analysis of the one who has one fine night to the gods of the Thirty-Three: ‘Don’t run back to the past, don’t anticipate the future. What’s past is left behind, the future has not arrived; and any present phenomenon you clearly discern in every case. The unfaltering, the unshakable: having known that, foster it. Today’s the day to keenly work — who knows, tomorrow may bring death! For there is no bargain to be struck with Death and his mighty horde. One who keenly meditates like this, tireless all night and day: that’s who has one fine night — so declares the peaceful sage.’

That’s how I remember the verses of the one who has one fine night. Learn the summary recital and the analysis of the one who has one fine night, mendicant, memorize it, and remember it. It is beneficial and relevant to the fundamentals of the spiritual life.” That’s what the godling Candana said, before vanishing right there.

A journey to confirm it

§5Then Lomasakaṅgiya set his lodgings in order and, taking his bowl and robe, set out for Sāvatthī. Eventually he came to Sāvatthī and Jeta’s Grove. He went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and told him what had happened, adding: “Sir, please teach me the summary recital and the analysis of the one who has one fine night.”

“That godling pays attention”

§6“But mendicant, do you know that god?” “I do not, sir.” “That godling was named Candana. Candana pays attention, applies the mind, concentrates wholeheartedly, and actively listens to the teaching. Well then, mendicant, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, sir,” Lomasakaṅgiya replied.

Full circle

§7–14The Buddha said this: “Don’t run back to the past, don’t anticipate the future. What’s past is left behind, the future has not arrived; and any present phenomenon you clearly discern in every case. The unfaltering, the unshakable: having known that, foster it. Today’s the day to keenly work — who knows, tomorrow may bring death! For there is no bargain to be struck with Death and his mighty horde. One who keenly meditates like this, tireless all night and day: that’s who has one fine night — so declares the peaceful sage.

And how do you run back to the past? You muster delight there, thinking: ‘I had such form in the past… such feeling… perception… choices… consciousness in the past.’ That’s how you run back to the past. And how do you not run back to the past? You don’t muster delight there in the same way.

And how do you anticipate the future? You muster delight there, thinking: ‘May I have such form… feeling… perception… choices… consciousness in the future.’ And how do you not anticipate the future? You don’t muster delight there in the same way.

And how do you falter amid presently arisen phenomena? It’s when an unlearned ordinary person, untrained in the teaching of the noble ones, regards form as self, self as having form, form in self, or self in form — and feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness in the same fourfold way. And how do you not falter? It’s when a learned noble disciple, trained in that teaching, doesn’t regard any of the five aggregates in any of those four ways.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Lomasakaṅgiya approved what the Buddha said.

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 81

With Ghaṭīkāra

Ghaṭīkārasutta

Setting
Wandering in the land of the Kosalans; the Buddha stops at the site of a long-vanished town
Speakers
The Buddha and Venerable Ānanda; within the story, the past Buddha Kassapa, the potter Ghaṭīkāra, the student Jotipāla, and King Kikī
Form
A story of a former age — the Buddha recalls the life of the past Buddha Kassapa, the model lay disciple, and his own earlier self.
Length
~14 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 63 (Madhyama-āgama 63)
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a warm narrative; the teaching is carried by character, not analysis.

Why this discourse, eighty-first

The Buddha steps off the road, looks at an empty patch of ground, and smiles — for here, ages ago, stood a town where a previous Buddha lived. The story he then tells is one of the most beloved in the canon: a humble potter whose faith and goodness outshone a king’s, a scornful young brahmin dragged to the teaching against his will, and a quiet revelation at the end about who that young brahmin really was.

“Realized Ones do not smile for no reason,” Ānanda observes, and asks the cause. The Buddha explains that this spot was once the market town of Vebhaliṅga, home to the Buddha Kassapa — the awakened one of a former age. Moved, Ānanda folds his robe and invites the Buddha to sit, “so this land will have been occupied by two perfected Buddhas.” And from that seat the Buddha tells the tale.

Its hero is Ghaṭīkāra the potter, Kassapa Buddha’s chief lay supporter — a man of no wealth or rank who is, nonetheless, a portrait of the perfect lay life: a non-returner, content, harmless, devoted, and caring for his blind old parents. Its second figure is his friend Jotipāla, a proud young brahmin who first sneers at “that shaveling, that fake ascetic” — until Ghaṭīkāra, refusing to give up on him, hauls him bodily to the Buddha. The story turns on the worth of such a friend; and it closes with the Buddha revealing that the scornful Jotipāla, won over and ordained, was none other than himself in a past life.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A poor potter’s faith, contentment, harmlessness, and care for his parents make him a model lay disciple and a non-returner — dearer to a Buddha than a wealthy king’s patronage; and a true friend who will not give up on you is worth so much that the very Buddha telling the story owes his first turning toward the Dhamma, lifetimes ago, to one.

The friend who would not give up

The heart of the story is Ghaṭīkāra’s refusal to let his friend miss the Buddha. Again and again he urges Jotipāla, “Come, let’s see the Blessed One Kassapa — I deem it holy to see him”; again and again Jotipāla brushes him off — “What’s the use of seeing that shaveling, that fake ascetic?” Ghaṭīkāra escalates: he proposes a trip to the river to bathe (to get him near the monastery), then grabs Jotipāla by the belt, and finally — the shocking gesture — takes hold of the freshly-washed hair of a high-born brahmin’s head. For a low-caste potter to seize a brahmin’s purified head was a grave breach; and it is exactly that breach that breaks through. Jotipāla thinks, “This is no ordinary matter,” and relents. The lesson is pointed: a friend’s love sometimes shows not in politeness but in the refusal to stand by while you wreck your own good. Ghaṭīkāra would rather offend his friend than lose him to scorn.

Two ways of responding

One teaching from Kassapa Buddha, and both men are changed — but differently. Jotipāla, the convert, “goes forth” at once, leaving the home life for the robe. Ghaṭīkāra cannot: “Don’t you know I provide for my blind old parents?” Here is a gentle but profound point. The one who has just arrived ordains; the one whose faith is older and deeper stays a layman — not from lesser devotion, but because his duty to his helpless parents is itself part of his practice. The discourse never treats Ghaṭīkāra’s lay life as second-best; on the contrary, it is he, the stay-at-home potter, who becomes the story’s spiritual centre. Renunciation is not the only road; a life of faithful, harmless duty is honoured here as a path of its own.

The portrait of the ideal layperson

When King Kikī, stung that Kassapa Buddha won’t spend the rains with him, asks “Do you have a better supporter than me?”, the Buddha’s answer is a full portrait of Ghaṭīkāra — the canon’s clearest sketch of the perfected lay disciple:

Having ended the five lower fetters, Ghaṭīkāra is a non-returner — bound for a refined heaven and final extinguishment there, never to return to this world. A potter who will not even dig his own clay lest he harm the life in the soil, who lives by trust and supports his parents, has reached the third stage of awakening without leaving home. The Buddha is, in effect, telling a king that the holiest person he knows is a craftsman too poor and too principled to own a coin.

The king and the potter

The contrast with King Kikī is the discourse’s quiet argument about devotion. The king offers lavish meals, royal carriages, an invitation to a season’s grand hospitality — and when refused, “becomes sad and upset.” Ghaṭīkāra offers grass off his own roof and rice from his own pot — and is simply, deeply happy. Three vignettes make the point: twice Kassapa Buddha helps himself to food from Ghaṭīkāra’s home in his absence, and the potter, far from feeling used, is overjoyed — “The Buddha trusts me so much!” Once the monks strip the grass roof off his workshop to patch the Buddha’s leaking hut, and again Ghaṭīkāra’s only response is delight — and the roofless workshop, the text says, stayed dry through three months of rains. The difference between the two patrons is not the size of the gift but the state of the heart: the king’s giving still wants something back (and grieves when refused), while the potter’s wants nothing and so cannot be disappointed. When King Kikī later sends Ghaṭīkāra five hundred cartloads of rice, the potter declines: “The king has much to do; I have enough. Let it be for the king himself.” Contentment needs no cartloads.

“I myself was Jotipāla”

The story ends with a turn that recolours everything. “Ānanda, you might think Jotipāla was someone else. But you should not see it so: I myself was the student Jotipāla at that time.” The proud young brahmin who jeered at “that fake ascetic,” who had to be dragged by the hair to the Dhamma — that was the Bodhisatta, the being who would become the Buddha Gotama. Two things land at once. First, a startling humility: the Buddha owns a past self who was contemptuous and resistant, and credits his first turning toward awakening not to his own wisdom but to a humble friend’s stubborn love. Second, the long arc of the path: awakening is not the work of a single life but of countless turnings, and even a Buddha-to-be once needed someone to grab him by the hair. The debt is never forgotten — the whole discourse is, in a sense, the Buddha honouring the potter who once would not let him walk past a Buddha.

Key terms

Kassapa Buddha — the fully awakened Buddha of the age before Gotama. The story is set in his lifetime; the discourse is part of the canon’s memory of a lineage of Buddhas.
kalyāṇamitta — a “good friend” in the spiritual sense. Ghaṭīkāra is its living image: one who will risk giving offence rather than let a friend miss the path.
anāgāmī — a “non-returner,” one who has ended the five lower fetters and will not be reborn in this world again. Ghaṭīkāra reaches this third stage of awakening as a layman.
aveccappasāda — “experiential” or verified confidence in the Buddha, teaching, and Saṅgha — the unshakable faith of a noble disciple, grounded in insight, not mere belief.
harmless livelihood — Ghaṭīkāra will not dig the earth, lest he harm the beings in the soil, taking only clay that has crumbled loose. Compassion reaching even into how one earns a living.
Jotipāla = the Bodhisatta — the scornful young brahmin of the story was the Buddha himself in a former life. A reminder that even a Buddha-to-be was once resistant, and needed a friend.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The smile, the dragging of Jotipāla, the portrait of Ghaṭīkāra, and the closing revelation are given in full; the threefold repetition of the urging, and the three vignettes of trust, are condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The smile, and the place of two Buddhas

§1–5So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was wandering in the land of the Kosalans. He left the road and, at a certain spot, smiled. Ānanda thought, “Realized Ones do not smile for no reason,” and asked the cause. “Once, Ānanda, there was a market town here named Vebhaliṅga, and the Buddha Kassapa — perfected, fully awakened — lived supported by it; here was his monastery.” Ānanda folded his robe and said, “Then may the Blessed One sit here, so this land will have been occupied by two fully awakened Buddhas.” And the Buddha sat.

Ghaṭīkāra drags his friend to the Buddha

§6–9“Kassapa Buddha’s chief supporter was a potter named Ghaṭīkāra, whose dear friend was the brahmin student Jotipāla. Again and again Ghaṭīkāra urged him, ‘Come, let’s see the Blessed One Kassapa — I deem it holy to see him.’ And again and again Jotipāla refused: ‘Enough — what’s the use of seeing that shaveling, that fake ascetic?’ So Ghaṭīkāra proposed a trip to the river to bathe; and there, near the monastery, urged him again. Refused again, he grabbed Jotipāla by the belt; refused again, he grabbed the hair of his freshly-washed head. Then Jotipāla thought, ‘How amazing — that this lowly potter should dare seize my washed head! This is no ordinary matter.’ ‘You’d milk it even this far?’ ‘I would — that is how holy I deem it to see the Blessed One.’ ‘Then release me; we shall go.’”

One teaching, two responses

§10–12“Kassapa Buddha taught them both. Afterward Jotipāla said, ‘You have heard this teaching — why don’t you go forth?’ ‘Don’t you know I provide for my blind old parents?’ ‘Then I shall go forth.’ And Jotipāla received the going forth and ordination in Kassapa Buddha’s presence.”

A better supporter than a king

§14–18“Later, King Kikī of Kāsi served Kassapa Buddha a great meal and begged him to spend the rains at Varanasi. Three times the Buddha declined — ‘I have already accepted an invitation for the rains’ — and the king grew sad. ‘Sir, have you a better supporter than me?’ ‘Great king, in the town of Vebhaliṅga there is a potter, Ghaṭīkāra — he is my chief supporter. When you thought I had refused you, you became sad; but Ghaṭīkāra does not get upset. He has gone for refuge and keeps the precepts; he has experiential confidence in the Three Jewels and the ethics loved by the noble ones; he is free of doubt about the four truths; he is celibate, eats once a day, and has set aside gold and money. He will not dig the earth with his own hands, but takes only clay crumbled from the bank or dug up by mice; and he leaves his pots on trust — take what you wish, leave what you can. He provides for his blind old parents. And having ended the five lower fetters, he is a non-returner.’”

The Buddha tells three stories of Ghaṭīkāra’s joy at being trusted. Twice Kassapa Buddha, finding Ghaṭīkāra out, took food from his pot at the parents’ invitation; and Ghaṭīkāra, hearing of it, was overjoyed — “The Buddha trusts me so much!” Once the monks stripped the grass roof from his workshop to mend the Buddha’s leaking hut; again Ghaṭīkāra rejoiced — and the roofless workshop stayed dry through the whole three months of rains. “That, great king, is what Ghaṭīkāra is like.”

Contentment, and the revelation

§22–23“Then King Kikī sent Ghaṭīkāra five hundred cartloads of rice. But Ghaṭīkāra declined: ‘The king has much to do; I have enough. Let it be for the king himself.’” Then the Buddha said: “Ānanda, you might think the student Jotipāla was someone else at that time. But you should not see it so. I myself was the student Jotipāla at that time.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Ānanda approved what the Buddha said.

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 61

Advice to Rāhula at Ambalaṭṭhika

Ambalaṭṭhikārāhulovādasutta

Setting
Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove; the Buddha visits Rāhula at Ambalaṭṭhika
Speakers
The Buddha and his son, the young monk Rāhula
Form
A father’s practical instruction — two vivid object-lessons (a water-pot, a mirror) teaching truthfulness and the habit of examining every deed before, during, and after.
Length
~10 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 14 (Madhyama-āgama 14, “Rāhula”); also T 211, T 212, a Vinaya version (T 1442), and Tibetan and Sanskrit versions
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — one of the most accessible and practical discourses, taught to a child, with images anyone can use.

Why this discourse, sixty-first

Here the Buddha teaches his own son. Rāhula was ordained as a boy, and this discourse — with its simple, concrete props of a water-pot and a mirror — reads like a father patiently planting in a child the two roots of an ethical life: an unbreakable commitment to truthfulness, and the habit of looking honestly at one’s own conduct.

The first lesson is about lying. Leaving a little water in a pot, then throwing it out, then turning the pot upside down, then showing it empty, the Buddha says: that is how the “ascetic’s nature” dwindles, is discarded, is overturned, and is left hollow in one who feels no shame at telling a deliberate lie. He drives it home with the image of a war-elephant that, in battle, risks every part of itself but guards its trunk — until the day it risks even the trunk, and then “there is nothing it would not do.” So with a person who has lost all shame about lying: “there is no bad deed they would not do.” Hence the famous resolve: “I will not tell a lie, even for a joke.”

The second lesson is the mirror. Just as a mirror is for examining your reflection, the Buddha says, so every deed of body, speech, and mind should be done only after repeated examination — before you act (“will this harm anyone?”), while you act (“is this harming anyone?”), and after you act (“did this harm anyone?”). It is the whole of ethical mindfulness reduced to a single, repeatable practice that a child — or anyone — can carry for life.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Deliberate lying empties out the whole spiritual life — so never lie, even in jest; and like checking your face in a mirror, examine every deed of body, speech, and mind before you act, while you act, and after you act, by the single question: does this harm myself, others, or both?

A father teaching his son

Rāhula was the Buddha’s only child, born just before his father left home, and ordained as a novice while still a boy. Three discourses in the canon are addressed to him as he grows: this one on truthfulness and self-examination (MN 61), the later one on the elements and meditation (MN 62), and the final one under which he attains awakening (MN 147). MN 61 has the unmistakable texture of a lesson for a young mind — it does not argue or analyze; it shows. The Buddha picks up the very water-pot Rāhula has just used to wash his feet, and a mirror, and teaches with things the boy can see and touch. It is the Buddha as patient, exacting parent.

The water-pot: how lying empties the spiritual life

The Buddha begins with the water left in the foot-washing pot, and runs four quick images:

  • A little water left in the pot — “that is how little of the ascetic’s nature remains” in one unashamed to lie deliberately.
  • The water thrown away — “that is how it is discarded.”
  • The pot turned upside down — “that is how it is turned upside down.”
  • The pot empty and hollow — “that is how vacant and hollow it is.”

The “ascetic’s nature” (sāmañña) is the inner reality of being a genuine contemplative — the very thing the robes are supposed to signify. The point is stark: shameless lying does not just blemish that reality; it pours it out entirely. A person can keep every outward form and still be a hollow, upturned vessel within.

The elephant’s trunk: no lie, even for a joke

Then the war-elephant. A trained royal elephant in battle will use its feet, head, ears, tusks, and tail — but it instinctively protects its trunk, and its rider knows that as long as it does, “it has not yet given its life.” But when at last it risks even the trunk, the rider thinks: “Now there is nothing this elephant would not do.” The trunk is the last thing held back, the line not yet crossed. Truthfulness, the Buddha implies, is that line for a human being. “When someone is not ashamed to tell a deliberate lie, there is no bad deed they would not do.” Lying is not one fault among many; it is the breach through which every other fault can follow, because it dissolves the very honesty that would restrain them. Hence the resolve Rāhula is given to train in: “I will not tell a lie, even for a joke.” Not even in jest — because the habit, once allowed any opening, spreads.

The mirror: action examined

The second teaching opens with a question fit for a child: “What is a mirror for?” — “For checking your reflection, sir.” “In the same way,” the Buddha says, “deeds of body, speech, and mind should be done only after repeated checking.” The Pali word, paccavekkhaṇa, means reviewing, looking back at, reflecting on — and the mirror makes it concrete. You would not go out with a dirty face if you could simply look and see; so too you need not act blindly when you can pause and examine the deed in the mirror of reflection. Conscience is given a tool: not vague good intentions, but a definite habit of looking.

The threefold reflection: before, during, after

The method is then laid out with great precision, for each of the three doors — body, speech, and mind — at three moments:

WhenThe reflection & what to do
Before acting“Will this deed harm me, others, or both?” If yes — do not do it. If no — go ahead.
While acting“Is this deed harming me, others, or both?” If yes — stop. If no — continue.
After acting“Did this deed harm me, others, or both?” If yes — confess it and restrain yourself in future. If no — rejoice, and keep training.

This is one of the most usable ethical instructions in all the discourses: foresight before, monitoring during, and honest review after. It assumes we will sometimes get it wrong even after checking — and so it builds in the remedy: not despair, but disclosure and resolve.

The one criterion

Notice that all nine reflections turn on a single question: does this deed lead to “hurting myself, hurting others, or hurting both” — is it “unskillful, with suffering as its outcome,” or “skillful, with happiness as its outcome”? This is the Buddha’s consistent moral measure: not obedience to a rule for its own sake, but the actual effect of the deed on the welfare of beings, oneself included. Ethics here is empirical and compassionate — you test an action by what it does, and the test is harm.

What to do afterward

The after-the-fact step deserves attention. For an unwholesome deed of body or speech — something outward, that touched others — the instruction is to “confess, reveal, and clarify it to the Teacher or a sensible spiritual companion,” and then restrain oneself. Wrongdoing is brought into the open and not allowed to fester in secrecy. But for an unwholesome deed of mind — a private thought of ill will or cruelty — there is no one to confess to; instead one should be “horrified, repelled, and disgusted” by it, and restrain oneself. And for any wholesome deed, of any door, the instruction is simply to “live in rapture and joy, training day and night in skillful qualities.” The path is not grim self-policing; its keynote, even here, is the gladness of a clear conscience.

Key terms

Rāhula — the Buddha’s son, ordained as a novice while still a boy. The recipient of three graded discourses (MN 61, 62, 147), of which this is the first.
sampajāna-musāvāda — deliberate, conscious lying. The one fault the discourse singles out as fatal to the spiritual life: “there is no bad deed they would not do.”
sāmañña — the “ascetic’s nature”: the inner reality of being a genuine contemplative. Shameless lying empties it out, “vacant and hollow” like an upturned pot.
paccavekkhaṇa — reviewing, reflecting, checking. The mirror is its image: examine each deed of body, speech, and mind as you would check your face in a mirror.
attabyābādha / parabyābādha — hurting oneself / hurting others. The single criterion of every reflection: does the deed harm myself, others, or both — is it skillful or unskillful?
kāya- / vacī- / mano-kamma — deeds of body, speech, and mind. The three doors of action, each to be examined at three moments: before, during, and after.

The text

The full discourse. The threefold reflection is given in full for deeds of the body; the parallel passages for speech and mind are abbreviated, as in the source, with their distinctive endings preserved. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The water-pot

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground. Now at that time Venerable Rāhula was staying at Ambalaṭṭhika. Then in the late afternoon, the Buddha came out of retreat and went to see him. Rāhula spread out a seat and placed water for washing the feet. The Buddha sat and washed his feet, and Rāhula bowed and sat down to one side.

§3–6Then the Buddha, leaving a little water in the pot, said, “Rāhula, do you see this little bit of water left in the pot?” “Yes, sir.” “That’s how little of the ascetic’s nature is left in those who are not ashamed to tell a deliberate lie.” Then, discarding the water, he said, “Do you see this water that was discarded? That’s how the ascetic’s nature is discarded…” Then, turning the pot upside down, “That’s how the ascetic’s nature is turned upside down…” Then, turning it right side up, “Do you see how this pot is vacant and hollow? That’s how vacant and hollow the ascetic’s nature is in those who are not ashamed to tell a deliberate lie.”

The war-elephant’s trunk

§7“Suppose there was a royal bull elephant, pedigree and battle-hardened. In battle it uses its fore-feet and hind-feet, its fore-quarters and hind-quarters, its head, ears, tusks, and tail — but it still protects its trunk. So its rider thinks: ‘This royal elephant still protects its trunk. It has not yet given its life.’ But when that elephant uses its trunk as well, its rider thinks: ‘It has given its life. Now there is nothing this elephant would not do.’ In the same way, when someone is not ashamed to tell a deliberate lie, there is no bad deed they would not do, I say. So you should train like this: ‘I will not tell a lie, even for a joke.’

The mirror, and the threefold reflection

§8What do you think, Rāhula? What is the purpose of a mirror?” “It’s for checking your reflection, sir.” “In the same way, deeds of body, speech, and mind should be done only after repeated checking.

§9When you want to act with the body, you should check on that same deed: ‘Does this act with the body that I want to do lead to hurting myself, hurting others, or hurting both? Is it unskillful, with suffering as its outcome and result?’ If, while checking, you know it is — then to the best of your ability you should not do such a deed. But if you know it does not lead to hurting, and is skillful, with happiness as its result — then you should do it.

§10While you are acting with the body, you should check on that same act: ‘Does this act that I am doing lead to hurting myself, others, or both?’ If you know it does — you should desist. But if you know it does not — you should continue.

§11After you have acted with the body, you should check on that same act: ‘Did this act that I have done lead to hurting myself, others, or both? Was it unskillful, with suffering as its result?’ If you know it did — then you should confess, reveal, and clarify it to the Teacher or a sensible spiritual companion, and having revealed it, restrain yourself in future. But if you know it did not — that it was skillful, with happiness as its result — then you should live in rapture and joy because of this, training day and night in skillful qualities.

The same threefold reflection is then applied to deeds of speech, and to deeds of mind. For unwholesome deeds of body or speech done in the past, one should “confess, reveal, and clarify” them to a teacher or wise companion. But for an unwholesome deed of mind, where there is no one to confess to, one should instead be “horrified, repelled, and disgusted” by it, and restrain oneself in future. For any wholesome deed, of any door, one should “live in rapture and joy, training day and night in skillful qualities.”

§18“All the ascetics and brahmins of the past, future, and present who purify their physical, verbal, and mental actions do so after repeatedly checking. So, Rāhula, you should train yourself like this: ‘I will purify my physical, verbal, and mental actions after repeatedly checking.’” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Rāhula approved what the Buddha said.

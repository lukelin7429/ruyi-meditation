Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 108

With Moggallāna the Guardian

Gopakamoggallānasutta

Setting
Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove — soon after the Buddha’s final extinguishment
Speakers
Venerable Ānanda, the brahmin Moggallāna the Guardian, and the minister Vassakāra
Form
A statesman’s questions on succession and authority, answered after the Teacher is gone.
Length
~16 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No full counterpart is known in the Chinese Āgamas
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — how the community holds together without a successor.

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-eighth

The Buddha has died. To a statesman’s eye, the obvious question is: who is in charge now? Did the Teacher name a successor? Did the community elect one? To both, Ānanda answers plainly: no. Yet the Saṅgha is not in disarray. How can a community without a head, without an appointed refuge, still cohere? Ānanda’s answer — the teaching is our refuge — is one of the most consequential statements in early Buddhism.

The scene is dated with unusual precision: “not long after the Buddha had become fully quenched,” with King Ajātasattu fortifying Rājagaha against a rival. Ānanda, on alms-round, falls into conversation with a brahmin overseer, and then with Vassakāra, a powerful minister of Magadha — a political man, used to chains of command, probing how this leaderless order works. His questions are pointed and recur in sharper form each time: is there anyone — appointed by the Buddha, or elected by the Saṅgha — who is now your refuge, whom you all turn to?

Ānanda’s replies redraw the very idea of authority. There is no single successor, because no one equals the Buddha — he was the path-finder, and the disciples are path-followers. There is no appointed refuge, because the Dhamma and Vinaya are the refuge: harmony is kept not by a person’s command but by the recited code that “makes us act.” And honour is given not by office but by ten inspiring qualities — wherever they are found, there the community reveres. The discourse closes with Ānanda drawing, for Vassakāra, the line between the meditation the Buddha praised and the kind he did not.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

After the Buddha’s death there is no appointed successor — for no one equals the path-finder — yet the community holds together because the teaching and training are its refuge, harmony is kept by the recited code rather than any person’s command, and honour is given not by office but to whoever embodies ten inspiring qualities.

The question of succession

Moggallāna the Guardian opens with the natural question: is there a single monk “who has all the same qualities in every way as the Buddha”? Ānanda’s answer draws a fundamental distinction:

Path-finder and path-followers. “The Blessed One gave rise to the unarisen path, gave birth to the unborn path, explained the unexplained path. He is the knower, the discoverer, the expert on the path. And now the disciples live following the path; they acquire it later.”

No one equals the Buddha, because his role was unique and unrepeatable: he found the path that no one had taught him. His disciples can walk that path to its end — even to full awakening — but they walk a road already charted, not one they discovered. This is why there can be no “second Buddha” as a successor: the founding act cannot be inherited. (It is the same point as the road-to-Rājagaha in MN 107, seen from the other side.)

Vassakāra’s pressure, and “the teaching is our refuge”

The minister Vassakāra arrives and presses harder, with a statesman’s instinct for the structure of power. Did the Buddha appoint a monk, saying “this one will be your refuge when I am gone”? — No. Did the Saṅgha elect one, appointed by senior monks? — No. Then, lacking a refuge, what is the source of your harmony? Ānanda’s correction is decisive:

“We do not lack a refuge. The teaching is our refuge.” The Buddha laid down the training rules and the monastic code; on the sabbath, all who live in one district gather and recite it; if anyone recalls an offense, “we make them act in line with the teaching. It is not the persons who make us act — it is the teaching that makes us act.”

This is the constitutional heart of the Saṅgha. Authority does not reside in any individual but in the Dhamma-Vinaya, the teaching-and-discipline, enacted communally through the fortnightly recitation. No one rules; the code governs, and the community administers it together. It is government by shared law rather than by a person — the very arrangement that lets the order survive its founder, where the Jains’ could not (MN 104).

Honour without office: the ten inspiring qualities

Vassakāra, still thinking in terms of hierarchy, asks: is there then no monk you honour and rely on? Ānanda says there is — but reframes what honour rests on. The community reveres anyone in whom ten inspiring qualities (pasādanīya dhammā) are found:

  1. Ethical conduct, restrained in the code.
  2. Deep learning, “remembering and keeping” the teachings.
  3. Contentment with the four requisites.
  4. Attaining the four absorptions at will.
  5. The many kinds of psychic power.
  6. Clairaudience.
  7. Knowing the minds of others.
  8. Recollection of past lives.
  9. The divine eye, seeing beings reborn by their deeds.
  10. The ending of the defilements — liberation here and now.

The list runs from the foundational (virtue, learning, contentment) through deepening meditation to the crowning liberation. The point is that reverence is earned by what a person has become, not by any title conferred. There is no rank to be appointed to; there is only the embodiment of these qualities, which any practitioner may come to possess. (Vassakāra’s general puts it simply: if they did not honour these, whom would they honour?) Honour, like authority, is decentralised — located in qualities, not positions.

The meditation the Buddha praised — and did not

A final exchange. Vassakāra, recalling a visit to the Buddha, says he praised “all kinds of meditation.” Ānanda corrects this firmly — an important clarification against a lazy reverence for any inward absorption:

Meditation NOT praisedMeditation praised
A mind “overcome and mired” in the five hindrances — sensual desire, ill will, dullness, restlessness, doubt — that “hides them within” and broods, not knowing the escape.The four absorptions: secluded from sensuality and unskillful states, entering and abiding in the rapture, bliss, and pure equanimity of deepening composure.

Not all sitting is good meditation. To concentrate while “hiding sensual desire within” — ruminating in a mind still ruled by the hindrances — is exactly what the Buddha did not praise. What he praised is the absorption that has first let the hindrances go. Vassakāra concedes the distinction gracefully and departs. And Moggallāna, his original question still hanging, gets Ānanda’s quiet final word: it is precisely because no one equals the path-finder that the disciples now, together, simply follow the path he found.

Key terms

path-finder & path-followers — the Buddha “gave rise to the unarisen path” that no one had taught him; disciples walk that charted road. The founding act cannot be inherited — so there is no successor-Buddha.
“the teaching is our refuge” — authority resides not in any person but in the Dhamma-Vinaya, enacted through the fortnightly recitation of the code. “It is the teaching that makes us act,” not the persons.
Dhamma-Vinaya — the teaching-and-discipline that governs the community in place of a ruler. Government by shared law, which lets the order outlast its founder (cf. MN 104).
pasādanīya dhammā — the ten “inspiring qualities,” from ethics and learning through the absorptions and higher knowledges to the ending of defilements. Honour is given to whoever embodies them, not by any title.
honour without office — reverence is earned by what a person has become, not by a position conferred. There is no rank to be appointed to — only the qualities any practitioner may come to possess.
the meditation the Buddha did not praise — concentration in a mind still “mired in” the five hindrances, brooding while hiding them within. Not all sitting is good meditation.
the meditation he praised — the four absorptions, entered only after the hindrances have first been let go: composure that has cleared the mind, not merely stilled a troubled one.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats Vassakāra’s questions and lists the ten qualities. The succession question, “the teaching is our refuge,” the ten inspiring qualities, and the two kinds of meditation are given closely. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

No equal to the Buddha

§1–5So I have heard. Soon after the Buddha’s full extinguishment, Venerable Ānanda was staying near Rājagaha in the Bamboo Grove, while King Ajātasattu was fortifying the city. On alms-round, Ānanda visited the brahmin Moggallāna the Guardian, who asked: “Is there a single mendicant with all the same qualities in every way as the worthy Gotama?” “No, brahmin. For the Blessed One gave rise to the unarisen path, gave birth to the unborn path, explained the unexplained path. He is the knower, discoverer, and expert on the path. The disciples now live following the path; they acquire it later.”

Vassakāra presses on succession

§6–9The minister Vassakāra arrived and asked: “Is there a single mendicant the Buddha appointed, saying ‘this one will be your refuge when I am gone’?” “No.” “Or one elected by the Saṅgha and appointed by senior monks?” “No.” “Then lacking a refuge, what is the reason for your harmony?” “We do not lack a refuge, brahmin. The teaching is our refuge.”

The teaching is our refuge

§10“How so?” “The Blessed One laid down the training rules and recited the monastic code. On the sabbath, all of us who live in one district gather as one and invite a reciter to recite the code. If anyone recalls an offense, we make them act in line with the teaching and the instructions. It is not the good fellows who make us act — it is the teaching that makes us act.”

The ten inspiring qualities

§11–23“Is there a single mendicant you honour, revere, and rely on?” “There is, brahmin.” “But you said none was appointed — how is this?” “There are ten inspiring qualities taught by the Buddha. We honour anyone in whom they are found: they are ethical, restrained in the code; very learned, keeping what they learn; content with the requisites; gaining the four absorptions at will; wielding psychic powers; with clairaudience; knowing others’ minds; recollecting past lives; seeing with the divine eye beings reborn by their deeds; and realising the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom, the ending of defilements. We honour and rely on anyone in whom these are found.” Vassakāra asked his general, “Do they honour the worthy?” “Indeed — if not these, whom would they honour?”

The two kinds of meditation

§25–28Vassakāra said: “Once the worthy Gotama, at Vesālī, praised all kinds of meditation.” “No, brahmin — he did not praise all meditation, nor dispraise all. He did not praise meditation where one’s heart is overcome and mired in the five hindrances — sensual desire, ill will, dullness, restlessness, doubt — hiding them within and brooding. But he praised the meditation of one who, secluded from sensuality and unskillful states, enters the first, second, third, and fourth absorptions.” “So he criticised the meditation deserving criticism and praised that deserving praise.” And Vassakāra, with much to do, departed.

Ānanda’s closing word

§29Then Moggallāna the Guardian said: “You still haven’t answered my question.” “But brahmin, did I not say: there is no single mendicant equal in every way to the Buddha? For the Blessed One was the discoverer of the unarisen path; the disciples now live following the path, and acquire it later.”

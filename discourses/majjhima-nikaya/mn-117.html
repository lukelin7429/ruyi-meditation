Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 117

The Great Forty

Mahācattārīsakasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, addressed to the mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha alone; the mendicants only reply “yes, sir”
Form
A systematic analysis of the eightfold path expanded to ten factors, each split into a mundane and a transcendent form, closing in an argument for its unassailability
Length
~15 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A counterpart survives in the Chinese Madhyama Āgama (MĀ 189) and in Tibetan; the mundane/transcendent split appears to be a Pali-tradition elaboration
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — one repeating pattern applied five times, then extended and defended

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-seventeenth

Ask a meditator what “right concentration” means and you will likely hear about breath, or jhāna, or a still mind. This discourse insists that’s not the whole story: samādhi only counts as “noble right immersion” when it arises supported by seven other factors working in concert. Remove them, and what you have is still concentration — but not the kind that leads anywhere.

From there the Buddha builds outward: view, purpose, speech, action, and livelihood each split into a mundane form (good, but still tied to rebirth) and a transcendent form (the same virtue, but functioning as an actual factor of the path to freedom); the familiar eight-factored path turns out to have two further factors waiting at its end; and the whole edifice is capped with a blunt piece of rhetoric — not even history’s most committed skeptics, the Buddha says, dared attack this teaching. Along with MN 141, this is one of the two great treatments of the eightfold path in the Majjhima Nikāya, and the one that shows how its parts depend on each other.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

“Noble right immersion” is not a solitary meditative skill but the fruit of seven supporting path factors, each of which the Buddha splits into a mundane form (that ripens as merit and rebirth) and a transcendent form (that functions as an actual factor of the path); together these ten factors produce and depend on one another, generating a teaching — “the Great Forty” — that the Buddha claims cannot honestly be refuted.

Right immersion needs support

The Buddha opens by promising to teach “noble right immersion with its vital conditions and its prerequisites” — and then immediately defines it not as a meditative technique but as the unification of mind that arises when the other seven path factors (right view, purpose, speech, action, livelihood, effort, and mindfulness) are all in place. It is possible to get absorbed and still, the commentarial tradition notes, without these factors — but such concentration leads only to a pleasant rebirth, never to liberation. Only immersion built on the whole path counts as “right.”

The pattern: view comes first

The discourse then works through five factors — view, purpose, speech, action, livelihood — using the same procedure each time, which is worth learning once in full:

  1. Right view comes first. You first need to understand wrong X as wrong and right X as right — that recognition is your right view regarding this factor.
  2. Wrong X is defined.
  3. Right X is defined twice over — once in a “mundane” form (good, wholesome, but still sāsava, “accompanied by defilements,” ripening only in a good rebirth) and once in a “transcendent” form (the same quality functioning as an actual factor of the noble path in someone developing it toward liberation).
  4. Effort and mindfulness join in. The effort to abandon wrong X and take up right X is right effort; doing so mindfully is right mindfulness. So three factors — view, effort, mindfulness — “run and circle around” every other factor of the path, supporting it from outside.

Applied to the five factors, the pattern yields:

FactorWrongRight (mundane)
Viewdenying that deeds have results, an afterlife, or awakened teachersaffirming that giving, deeds, the afterlife, and realized teachers are real
Purposesensuality, malice, crueltyrenunciation, good will, harmlessness
Speechlying, backbiting, harsh speech, nonsenserefraining from all four
Actionkilling, stealing, sexual misconductrefraining from all three
Livelihoodfawning, flattery, hinting, belittling, chasing profit with profitgiving up wrong livelihood for a right one

The transcendent form of each — deliberately abstract, defined as “the desisting, abstaining, abstinence… in one intent on the noble, intent on the undefiled” — is what makes the same ethical act into a piece of the path to freedom rather than just good karma. Notably, right speech and right action here are defined purely as restraint, not as active virtues — a technical point noted in the commentary, since restraint alone still applies even when a person is silent or absorbed in deep meditation.

From eight factors to ten

Having built the first five factors up from the ground, the Buddha extends the chain of production all the way to the end of the path: “Right view produces right purpose. Right purpose produces right speech…” through action, livelihood, effort, and mindfulness, until “right mindfulness produces right immersion. Right immersion produces right knowledge. Right knowledge produces right freedom.” Two factors beyond the familiar eightfold path appear at the very end — right knowledge (the wisdom that has finished ending the defilements) and right freedom (liberation itself) — giving a ten-factored path found only in the perfected one. “The trainee has eight factors,” the Buddha says, “while the perfected one has ten.” The remaining two, unlike the first eight, are not split into mundane and transcendent versions: they belong solely to the arahant.

The Great Forty

The discourse then restates the whole structure once more, but generalized to all ten factors: for one who has right view, wrong view is worn away, and so are the many unskillful qualities that wrong view produces — while skillful qualities flourish. The same is said for purpose, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, immersion, knowledge, and freedom. Counting outward from here gives the discourse its name: ten factors, each promoting a further quality, makes twenty on the skillful side, and twenty in exact mirror-image on the unskillful side — the “Great Forty” (Mahācattārīsaka), a single exposition, the Buddha says, that “cannot be rolled back by any ascetic or brahmin or god or Māra or divinity or by anyone in the world.”

An argument no honest critic can make

The closing passage is unusually combative for the Majjhima Nikāya. Anyone who tries to reject this teaching, the Buddha says, deserves criticism on ten legitimate grounds: to criticize right view is, in effect, to praise and honor those ascetics and brahmins who hold wrong view — and the same follows for each of the other nine factors. No one, framed this way, actually wants to defend wrong view, wrong action, or wrong livelihood outright. As proof, the Buddha points to the most radical skeptics of his day — “loud and bold advocates of the doctrines of no-cause, inaction, and nihilism” — who nonetheless never dared to criticize the Great Forty by name, “for fear of being blamed, provoked, and faulted.” Even those who denied karma altogether, in other words, could not bring themselves to publicly defend cruelty, theft, and lying as such.

Key terms

upanisa & parikkhāra — “vital condition” (a necessary, strongly conducive support) and “prerequisite” (the equipment a thing requires): the two words the Buddha uses for how the other seven path factors relate to right immersion.
sāsava vs. lokuttara — “accompanied by defilements” (the mundane form of a virtue, which ripens as merit and a good rebirth) versus “transcendent” (the same virtue functioning as a genuine factor of the path beyond rebirth). Later Abhidhamma calls transcendent immersion lokuttara jhāna.
“intent on the noble, intent on the undefiled” — the phrase marking the transcendent definitions: the mind of someone still developing the path toward, but not yet at, freedom.
right knowledge & right freedom (sammā-ñāṇa, sammā-vimutti) — the ninth and tenth factors, found only in the perfected one, produced when right immersion is complete. The trainee has eight factors; the arahant has ten.
nijjiṇṇa — “worn away,” the term used for how wrong qualities are eroded by their right counterparts; a term the commentary notes is otherwise associated with Jain vocabulary.
the Great Forty — ten factors, each promoting a further quality: twenty on the skillful side, twenty in mirror-image on the unskillful side. The discourse’s namesake structure.

The text

The discourse in full for the “right view” cycle, which establishes the pattern; the remaining four cycles (purpose, speech, action, livelihood) abridged to their core definitions since they repeat the same structure exactly. The extension to ten factors, the count of Forty, and the closing argument are given complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Right immersion needs support

§1–3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, and addressed the mendicants. “I will teach you noble right immersion with its vital conditions and its prerequisites.” And what is that? “They are: right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, and right mindfulness. Unification of mind with these seven factors as prerequisites is what is called noble right immersion ‘with its vital conditions’ and also ‘with its prerequisites.’”

Right view

§4–9“In this context, right view comes first. And how does right view come first? When you understand wrong view as wrong view and right view as right view, that’s your right view. And what is wrong view? ‘There’s no meaning in giving, sacrifice, or offerings. There’s no fruit or result of good and bad deeds. There’s no afterlife. There’s no such thing as mother and father, or beings that are reborn spontaneously. And there’s no ascetic or brahmin who is rightly comported and rightly practiced, and who describes the afterlife after realizing it with their own insight.’ This is wrong view.

And what is right view? Right view is twofold, I say. There is right view that is accompanied by defilements, partakes of good deeds, and ripens in attachments — the affirmation of everything wrong view denies. And there is right view that is noble, undefiled, transcendent, a factor of the path: it’s the wisdom — the faculty of wisdom, the power of wisdom, the awakening factor of investigation of principles, and right view as a factor of the path — in one intent on the noble, intent on the undefiled, who possesses the noble path and develops the noble path.

They make an effort to give up wrong view and embrace right view: that’s their right effort. Mindfully they give up wrong view and take up right view: that’s their right mindfulness. So these three things keep running and circling around right view, namely: right view, right effort, and right mindfulness.”

Right purpose, speech, action, and livelihood

§10–33The same fourfold pattern is repeated for the remaining factors. Wrong purpose is purposes of sensuality, malice, and cruelty; right purpose (mundane) is purposes of renunciation, good will, and harmlessness, and (transcendent) is “the thinking… in one intent on the noble.” Wrong speech is speech that is false, backbiting, harsh, or nonsensical; right speech (mundane) is refraining from all four, and (transcendent) is “the desisting, abstaining, abstinence, and refraining from the four kinds of bad verbal conduct in one intent on the noble.” Wrong action is killing living creatures, stealing, and sexual misconduct; right action (mundane) is refraining from these three, and (transcendent) is the same restraint “in one intent on the noble.” Wrong livelihood is fawning, flattery, hinting, belittling, and using material things to chase after other material things; right livelihood (mundane) is giving up wrong livelihood and earning a living rightly, and (transcendent) is “the desisting, abstaining, abstinence, and refraining from wrong livelihood in one intent on the noble.” For each, right effort is the effort to abandon the wrong and embrace the right, and right mindfulness is doing so mindfully — so view, effort, and mindfulness “run and circle around” each factor in turn.

From eight factors to ten

§34“In this context, right view comes first. And how does right view come first? Right view produces right purpose. Right purpose produces right speech. Right speech produces right action. Right action produces right livelihood. Right livelihood produces right effort. Right effort produces right mindfulness. Right mindfulness produces right immersion. Right immersion produces right knowledge. Right knowledge produces right freedom. So the trainee has eight factors, while the perfected one has ten factors. And here too, the eradication of many bad, unskillful qualities is fully developed due to right knowledge.”

The Great Forty

§35–36“For one of right view, wrong view is worn away, and the many bad, unskillful qualities that arise because of wrong view are worn away, and because of right view, many skillful qualities are fully developed. For one of right purpose, wrong purpose is worn away… for one of right speech… right action… right livelihood… right effort… right mindfulness… right immersion… right knowledge… for one of right freedom, wrong freedom is worn away, and the many bad, unskillful qualities that arise because of wrong freedom are worn away, and because of right freedom, many skillful qualities are fully developed. So there are twenty on the side of the skillful, and twenty on the side of the unskillful. This exposition of the teaching on the Great Forty has been rolled forth. And it cannot be rolled back by any ascetic or brahmin or god or Māra or divinity or by anyone in the world.”

An argument no one dared make

§37–38“If any ascetic or brahmin imagines they can criticize and reject the exposition of the teaching on the Great Forty, they deserve rebuttal and criticism on ten legitimate grounds in this very life. If such a gentleman criticizes right view, they praise and honor the ascetics and brahmins who have wrong view.” And so for each of the other nine factors in turn, down to right freedom. “Even those who were loud and bold advocates of the doctrines of no-cause, inaction, and nihilism, didn’t imagine that the Great Forty should be criticized or rejected. Why is that? For fear of being blamed, provoked, and faulted.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said.

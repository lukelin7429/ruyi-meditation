Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 68

At Naḷakapāna

Naḷakapānasutta

Setting
The land of the Kosalans, near Naḷakapāna, in a grove of flame-of-the-forest trees
Speakers
The Buddha and Venerable Anuruddha, speaking for a company of well-known gentlemen who had gone forth
Form
A check-in and a clarification — the Buddha confirms his disciples’ contentment, then explains the one reason he ever declares the rebirth-destinies of those who have died.
Length
~12 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 77 (Madhyama-āgama 77)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — accessible; the four stages of awakening and the pastoral purpose behind the Buddha’s “rebirth declarations” are the heart of it.

Why this discourse, sixty-eighth

When a spiritual teacher announces that a deceased follower attained awakening — “so-and-so, having died, is now a non-returner; this other, a stream-enterer” — is he advertising the success of his school? The Buddha takes up exactly this delicate question, and gives a clear, generous answer about the one and only reason he ever declares the rebirth-destinies of those who have passed away.

The setting is warm. A company of well-known young men of good family — Anuruddha, Bhaddiya, Kimbila, Bhagu, Ānanda, and others — have gone forth. The Buddha asks the assembled monks whether these gentlemen are content in the holy life; they keep silent. So he asks the men themselves, and Anuruddha answers for all: “Indeed, sir, we are satisfied.” The Buddha praises them — young, in their prime, able to enjoy every pleasure, yet they went forth not from debt or fear or to make a living, but from the genuine wish to “make an end to this entire mass of suffering.”

It is to such people that the rebirth-declarations are addressed. The Buddha makes plain what they are not for: “not for the sake of fawning or flattering people, nor for the benefit of possessions, honour, or popularity, nor thinking, ‘So let people know about me!’” They are for the faithful who, hearing that someone they knew and respected reached a stage of the path, recollect that person’s virtues and “apply their minds to the same end.” A real example of someone who made it — not an abstract doctrine — draws the living onward. The teaching is at once a map of the four stages of awakening and a quiet reflection on how inspiration actually works.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Buddha declares the attainments of deceased disciples not to flatter or for gain, honour, or fame, but so that faithful people, hearing that someone they knew and respected reached a stage of awakening, recollect that person’s virtues and “apply their minds to the same end” — and so live at ease, drawn onward by a worthy example.

The contented disciples

The framing matters. The gentlemen named — Anuruddha and his companions — are not nobodies; they are prominent young men who had every reason to stay in the world. The Buddha’s praise turns on why they left it: not to dodge a king’s summons or a creditor, not from fear, not to make an easier living, but because they saw they were “swamped by rebirth, old age, and death… mired in suffering,” and hoped to make an end of it. Their contentment is the mark of a renunciation that was true at the root. The Buddha is, in effect, holding them up as living examples of the very thing the rest of the discourse is about — people whose sincerity makes them worth emulating.

What a renunciant should achieve

Before the main teaching, the Buddha names what such a one’s practice should yield: “the rapture and bliss that are secluded from sensual pleasures and unskillful qualities, or something even more peaceful than that” — that is, the bliss of the absorptions and what lies beyond them. The test is concrete: in one who attains this, the mind is “not occupied by desire, ill will, dullness and drowsiness, restlessness and remorse, doubt, discontent, and sloth.” A contented renunciant is not merely one who has not gone back; it is one whose mind has found a happiness deep enough that the hindrances no longer have room to settle. Going forth is only the beginning; the work is to reach the peace that makes the going-forth fruitful.

The Buddha who has nothing left to manage

A subtle exchange follows. The Buddha asks whether the gentlemen think of him as one who still has defilements — such that “after appraisal, he uses some things, endures some things, avoids some things, and dispels some things.” (This four-fold phrase describes the reflective use of life’s necessities — using robes and food wisely, enduring hardships, avoiding dangers, dispelling unwholesome thoughts.) They answer rightly: no, they think of him as one who has destroyed the defilements, “cut off at the root, made like a palm stump.” The Buddha confirms it. The point is fine but important: an awakened one still uses requisites carefully and still avoids dangers — the outward conduct looks the same — but for the Buddha this care flows from wisdom, not from any remaining defilement that must be held in check. He manages his life thoughtfully; he has nothing left within to manage.

The question: why declare rebirths?

Now the heart of the discourse. The Buddha asks: “What advantage does the Realized One see in declaring the rebirth of disciples who have passed away — ‘this one is reborn here, that one there’?” The question is pointed, because such declarations could easily be misread — as a teacher tallying his successes, trading on the dead to impress the living. The Buddha rules every such motive out: “not for the sake of fawning or flattering people, nor for the benefit of possessions, honour, or popularity, nor thinking, ‘So let people know about me!’” He has no use for the reputational currency that a lesser teacher’s “my disciple became enlightened” would buy.

The four stages of awakening

The declarations name where a deceased disciple stood on the path — the four stages of awakening, defined by the fetters cut:

StageWhat has been ended
Stream-entererthe first three fetters; “not liable to be reborn in the underworld, assured, destined for awakening.”
Once-returnerthe three fetters, with greed, hate, and delusion weakened; returns to this world once more, then ends suffering.
Non-returnerthe five lower fetters; reborn spontaneously in a refined realm, extinguished there, “not liable to return from that world.”
Arahantall defilements; fully “enlightened.”

The Buddha applies this to all four assemblies — monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen alike — a quiet but significant affirmation that the noble attainments are open to laypeople too, not the monastics’ preserve.

An example for the living

The mechanism of inspiration is described with care. A monk hears that a certain fellow-monk has died and been declared, say, a non-returner. Crucially, he “has either seen for himself, or heard from someone else, that that venerable had such ethics, such qualities, such wisdom, such meditation, or such freedom.” The declaration is not a bare label; it lands on a real, remembered person whose virtues the hearer knew. And so “recollecting that monk’s faith, ethics, learning, generosity, and wisdom, he applies his mind to that end. That’s how a monk lives at ease.” The same is said of the nun, the layman, and the laywoman, each inspired by one of their own. The point is psychologically shrewd: we are moved less by ideals in the abstract than by the proof that someone like us, whom we actually knew, walked this path to its fruit. The dead become a ladder for the living.

Not for fame, but to inspire

The discourse closes by repeating the disavowal and the purpose together, so neither is forgotten: not for flattery, gain, honour, popularity, or self-promotion, but because “there are gentlemen of faith who are full of joy and gladness; when they hear that, they apply their minds to that end, for their lasting welfare and happiness.” In an age — ancient or modern — thick with teachers eager to broadcast their followers’ breakthroughs as proof of their own greatness, the discourse draws a clean line. To name another’s attainment can be an act of vanity or an act of generosity, and the difference lies entirely in whether it is done to magnify oneself or to encourage someone else. The Buddha’s declarations are gifts to the faithful, not advertisements for the Teacher.

Key terms

abhirata — satisfied, content, delighting (in the spiritual life). The contentment of Anuruddha and his companions — the mark of a renunciation true at the root.
the four stages — stream-enterer (three fetters gone), once-returner (+ greed, hate, delusion weakened), non-returner (five lower fetters gone), and arahant (all defilements gone). The path mapped by what each has ended.
“use, endure, avoid, dispel” — the four-fold reflective use of life’s necessities (from MN 2): using requisites wisely, enduring hardship, avoiding danger, dispelling unwholesome thoughts. The Buddha does all this — from wisdom, not from any defilement left to manage.
lābha-sakkāra-siloka — gain, honour, and fame. Precisely not the reason the Buddha declares disciples’ attainments. He has no use for reputational currency.
tad-atthāya cittaṃ upasaṃharati — “applies the mind to that end.” The mechanism of inspiration: recollecting a real person’s virtues and attainment, one inclines toward the same goal.
phāsu-vihāra — living at ease, dwelling in comfort. The fruit, for the faithful, of being inspired by a worthy example: a heart settled and encouraged on the path.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The opening, the palm-stump exchange, and the statement of purpose are given in full; the application to the four assemblies and four stages is given once and then condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The contented disciples

§1–5So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the land of the Kosalans near Naḷakapāna in the grove of flame-of-the-forest trees. Now several very well-known gentlemen had gone forth out of faith — Anuruddha, Bhaddiya, Kimbila, Bhagu, Koṇḍañña, Revata, Ānanda, and others. The Buddha, seated among the Saṅgha, asked the mendicants, “I trust those gentlemen are satisfied with the spiritual life?” They kept silent — a second and a third time. So the Buddha asked Anuruddha directly: “Anuruddha and friends, I hope you’re satisfied with the spiritual life?” “Indeed, sir, we are satisfied.” “Good, good! It is appropriate for gentlemen like you, blessed with youth, in the prime of life, who could have enjoyed sensual pleasures, to be content. And you did not go forth to escape a king’s summons, or out of debt or fear, or to make a living, but thinking: ‘I’m swamped by suffering… Hopefully I can make an end to this entire mass of suffering.’” “Yes, sir.”

What a renunciant should achieve

§6“When a gentleman has gone forth like this, what should he do? Take someone who doesn’t achieve the rapture and bliss secluded from sensual pleasures and unskillful qualities, or something more peaceful than that. Their mind is still occupied by desire, ill will, dullness and drowsiness, restlessness and remorse, doubt, discontent, and sloth. But take someone who does achieve that rapture and bliss: their mind is not occupied by those things.”

The palm stump

§7“Is this what you think of me — ‘The Realized One has not given up the defilements; that’s why, after appraisal, he uses some things, endures some things, avoids some things, and dispels some things’?” “No, sir. We think of you this way: ‘The Realized One has given up the defilements; that’s why, after appraisal, he uses, endures, avoids, and dispels.’” “Good, good! The Realized One has given up the defilements — cut them off at the root, made them like a palm stump, so they are unable to arise in the future. Just as a palm tree with its crown cut off cannot grow again, so the Realized One has given up the defilements. That’s why, after appraisal, he uses, endures, avoids, and dispels.”

Why declare rebirths?

§8–9“What advantage does the Realized One see in declaring the rebirth of disciples who have passed away — ‘this one is reborn here, that one there’?” “Our teachings are rooted in the Buddha. May the Buddha himself clarify the meaning.” “The Realized One does not declare such things for the sake of fawning or flattering people, nor for the benefit of possessions, honour, or popularity, nor thinking, ‘So let people know about me!’ Rather, there are gentlemen of faith who are full of sublime joy and gladness. When they hear that, they apply their minds to that end. That is for their lasting welfare and happiness.”

An example for the living

§10–13“Take a monk who hears: ‘The monk so-and-so has passed away; the Buddha has declared that he was enlightened.’ And he has seen for himself, or heard, that that venerable had such ethics, such qualities, such wisdom, such meditation, or such freedom. Recollecting that monk’s faith, ethics, learning, generosity, and wisdom, he applies his mind to that end. That’s how a monk lives at ease. Or he hears that so-and-so, ‘with the ending of three fetters, is a stream-enterer, not liable to be reborn in the underworld, assured, destined for awakening’ — and recollecting that monk’s qualities, he applies his mind to that end, and lives at ease.”

The Buddha applies the same pattern across all four assemblies and all the stages. A monk, a nun, a layman, or a laywoman hears that one of their own has died and been declared by the Buddha to be an arahant (for monastics), a non-returner (with the five lower fetters gone, “extinguished there, not liable to return”), a once-returner (the three fetters gone and greed, hate, and delusion weakened, returning to this world once more), or a stream-enterer (the three fetters gone, “assured, destined for awakening”). In each case, recollecting that person’s faith, ethics, learning, generosity, and wisdom, they “apply the mind to that end” — and so “live at ease.” The noble attainments are named for laypeople as well as monastics.

Not for fame, but to inspire

§24“So it is not for the sake of fawning or flattering people, nor for the benefit of possessions, honour, or popularity, nor thinking, ‘So let people know about me!’ that the Realized One declares the rebirth of disciples who have passed away. Rather, there are gentlemen of faith who are full of joy and gladness. When they hear that, they apply their minds to that end. That is for their lasting welfare and happiness.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Anuruddha approved what the Buddha said.

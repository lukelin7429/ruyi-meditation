Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 75

With Māgaṇḍiya

Māgaṇḍiyasutta

Setting
In the land of the Kurus, near the town of Kammāsadhamma, on a grass mat in a brahmin’s fire chamber
Speakers
The Buddha and the wanderer Māgaṇḍiya, a committed sensualist who first reviles the Buddha as “anti-life”
Form
A confrontation over sensual pleasure, carried by a chain of vivid similes — the leper at the coals, the god in the pleasure garden, and the man blind from birth.
Length
~16 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 153 (Madhyama-āgama 153)
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — rich and demanding; the similes are simple but the diagnosis of pleasure as distorted perception is profound.

Why this discourse, seventy-fifth

Seeing the Buddha’s simple grass mat, the wanderer Māgaṇḍiya sneers that its owner is a “destroyer of life” — for a man who teaches restraint of the senses must be the enemy of everything that makes living sweet. The Buddha’s reply is not a defence but a diagnosis, and it overturns the charge entirely: it is not the ascetic who is sick, but the sensualist who cannot feel how sick he is.

Māgaṇḍiya is a hedonist on principle — elsewhere in the canon he tries to give the Buddha his beautiful daughter, baffled that anyone would refuse. To him, the goal of life is the enjoyment of sights, sounds, tastes, and touches; a teaching of sense-restraint looks like a war on joy itself, so he brands the Buddha bhūnahan, a “killer of growth,” an enemy of life. The accusation rests on a simple assumption: that sensual pleasure is happiness, and to renounce it is to renounce happiness.

The Buddha dismantles that assumption with three of the most memorable images in the canon. He grants that he teaches the restraint of the senses — but asks what such restraint is for. Drawing first on his own life as a pleasure-soaked prince who walked away not from lack but from finding something better, he then turns to the leper who burns himself for relief, and finally to the man blind from birth who clutches a filthy rag, certain it is a clean white cloth. By the end, the man who came to revile the Buddha as life’s enemy is begging to be ordained.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Sensual pleasure is not happiness but a distorted perception of pain — like a leper’s relief in scorching his sores — so the Buddha teaches restraint of the senses not to kill joy but to cure it; and the “health” that is the ultimate happiness is not the body’s wellness, which Māgaṇḍiya mistakes it for, but Nibbāna, which can be known only when the eye of the Dhamma opens.

The insult, and the misunderstanding inside it

Māgaṇḍiya calls the Buddha bhūnahan — an old and ugly word, “slayer of life in the womb,” an enemy of all that grows and flourishes. His reasoning, when the Buddha draws it out, is that the Buddha has “tamed, guarded, and restrained” the senses and teaches others to do the same — and to a man for whom sense-pleasure is the whole of the good, that is the murder of life. The misunderstanding is total but instructive: Māgaṇḍiya cannot imagine a happiness that is not sensual, so restraint can only mean deprivation. (When pressed for his evidence, he can only say the idea “came to me while sleeping” — a sly pun, since the word for “sleeping” also means “scripture.” His conviction has no better ground than a dream.)

Renunciation from fullness, not lack

The Buddha’s first move is autobiographical, and it disarms the charge of sour grapes. He was no stranger to pleasure: as a young prince he had three palaces for the three seasons, and passed the four rainy months “without coming downstairs,” entertained by musicians. He left all of it — not because he could not have it, but because he “truly understood the origin, disappearance, gratification, drawback, and escape of sensual pleasures” and found a satisfaction “apart from sensual pleasures and unskillful qualities, which even equals heavenly pleasure.” Having tasted the higher, he does not envy the lower — as a god amusing himself in the Garden of Delight would never envy a human his earthly pleasures. Renunciation here is not loss but upgrade: one lets go of the candle because one has found the sun.

The leper at the coals

Then the discourse’s central image, and its sharpest. A leper, his limbs a mass of sores, finds relief by scratching them and searing his body over a pit of glowing coals. To him the fire feels good. But cure his leprosy, and he would not for anything go near those coals again — and if dragged toward them, he would writhe to escape. Was the fire pleasant before and painful now? No: “that fire is painful now and it was also painful previously.” What changed is not the fire but the man. His diseased nerves gave him a “distorted perception” (viparīta-saññā) that the burning was pleasant. This is the heart of the teaching: sensual pleasures “of the past, future, and present are painful to touch, fiercely burning and scorching.” Beings consumed by craving have impaired faculties, and so misread the burn as bliss. The ascetic is not the one who hates pleasure; he is the one who has been cured, and can finally feel the fire for what it is.

The itch that worsens with scratching

A second turn of the same image sharpens the trap. The more the leper scratches and burns his sores, the more they fester — and yet he gets a “degree of pleasure and gratification from the itchiness.” So too: the more one indulges sensual craving, “the more their craving grows, and the more they burn,” while still snatching a little gratification from each indulgence. Pleasure-seeking is not merely hollow; it is self-feeding. Each scratch deepens the wound it pretends to soothe. No king or minister, past or present, the Buddha notes, ever found a peaceful mind without giving up craving; the satisfied are only ever those who let go.

“Health is the ultimate blessing” — and the body that is not health

The Buddha utters an inspired verse: “Health is the ultimate blessing; extinguishment, the ultimate happiness; of paths the ultimate is eightfold — safe, and leading to the deathless.” Māgaṇḍiya is delighted — he has heard the old wanderers say the same. But asked what “health” and “extinguishment” he means, he strokes his own limbs: “This is health, this is extinguishment — I am healthy and happy right now!” He has heard the right words and filled them with the wrong meaning. He thinks the verse praises bodily comfort, the very sensual wellness it was meant to transcend. “This body,” the Buddha replies, “is a disease, a boil, a dart, an affliction” — the last thing “health” could name. Māgaṇḍiya lacks “the noble vision by which you might know health and see extinguishment.”

The man blind from birth

The final simile names Māgaṇḍiya’s condition exactly. A man blind from birth, who has never seen white, hears that “a white cloth is lovely, spotless, clean,” and goes looking for one — only to be cheated with a filthy, greasy rag, which he gladly wears, repeating the praise of its “whiteness.” He does this “not knowing or seeing, but out of faith.” So the wanderers of other paths recite “health, extinguishment” without the least idea of what they name. But the simile goes further: were a surgeon to actually cure the man’s sight, the moment he could see he would fling away the rag in disgust and turn on the cheat as an enemy. In the same way, when the vision of the Dhamma arises, one gives up desire for the “five grasping aggregates” and sees with a start: “For so long I’ve been cheated by this mind — what I grasped was only form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness.” The cure is not a new belief but a new sight — and the soiled rag of sensuality, once seen, loses all its charm.

The path: keep good company

How does the blindness lift? Not by being told the answer — the Buddha warns that to declare “this is health, this is extinguishment” to one who cannot yet see would only weary the teacher and cheat the hearer, like handing a colour-chart to the blind. The cure is a process, and it begins socially: “associate with true persons (sappurisa); then you will hear the true teaching; then you will practise in line with it; then you will know and see for yourself: ‘These are diseases, boils, and darts — and here is where they cease.’” Right company, right teaching, right practice, then direct sight. Convinced, Māgaṇḍiya goes for refuge, offers four years’ probation where four months would do, ordains, and — living “alone, withdrawn, diligent” — becomes one of the perfected. The reviler is cured, and sees the white cloth at last.

Key terms

bhūnahan — “destroyer of growth, slayer of life” (Sanskrit bhrūṇahan, “fetus-killer”). Māgaṇḍiya’s insult: to him, sense-restraint is an assault on life itself.
kāma — sensual pleasure, the enjoyment of the five senses. Māgaṇḍiya takes it for happiness itself; the Buddha shows it to be a burning misperceived as bliss.
viparīta-saññā — “distorted perception.” As the leper’s diseased nerves read scorching as pleasant, so craving-impaired faculties read sensual pleasure as happiness, when it is “painful to touch, fiercely burning.”
ārogya / nibbāna — “health” and “extinguishment.” In the verse, the ultimate blessing and the ultimate happiness — not the body’s wellness (Māgaṇḍiya’s error) but the unbinding of craving.
pañcupādānakkhandhā — the five grasping aggregates (form, feeling, perception, choices, consciousness). When sight arises, one sees that “what I grasped was only” these — and grasping drives the whole round of suffering.
sappurisa — a “true person,” a person of integrity. The path to seeing begins here: keep good company → hear the true teaching → practise → know for yourself.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The insult, the renunciation, the three great similes, and the path are given in full; the sense-by-sense lists and the repeated “I live rid of thirst” refrain are condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

“That baby-killer”

§1–6So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the land of the Kurus, near Kammāsadhamma, on a grass mat in the fire chamber of a brahmin of the Bhāradvāja clan. Returning from alms, he went to a forest grove to meditate. Meanwhile the wanderer Māgaṇḍiya, walking by, saw the grass mat and asked whose ascetic’s bed it was. “It is the ascetic Gotama’s,” said the brahmin. “It’s a sad sight, worthy Bhāradvāja,” said Māgaṇḍiya, “to see a bed for that baby-killer, Gotama!” “Be careful what you say,” warned the brahmin — but Māgaṇḍiya would say it to Gotama’s face. With purified hearing the Buddha overheard, and came to the fire chamber.

What restraint is for

§7–8The Buddha said to him: “Māgaṇḍiya, the eye likes sights, loves and enjoys them; that has been tamed, guarded, protected, and restrained by the Realized One, and he teaches Dhamma for its restraint. Is that what you meant when you called me a baby-killer?” “That is exactly what I meant. Why? For so it came to me while sleeping.” The Buddha said the same of the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind, and each time Māgaṇḍiya confirmed: that restraint is just why he called him a destroyer of life.

Renunciation from fullness

§9–10“Take someone who once amused themselves with delightful sights, sounds, smells, tastes, and touches. Later, having truly understood the origin, disappearance, gratification, drawback, and escape of these, they give up craving and live rid of thirst, their mind at peace. What would you say to them, Māgaṇḍiya?” “Nothing, worthy Gotama.” “When I was still a layperson, I had three stilt longhouses for the three seasons, and passed the four rainy months entertained by musicians, never coming downstairs. Later, understanding sensual pleasures truly, I gave up craving for them and live rid of thirst, my mind at peace. I see beings still burning with sensual craving, and I do not envy them — for there is a satisfaction apart from sensual pleasures that equals even heavenly pleasure.”

The Buddha drives the point home with two images of comparison. A god amusing himself in the Garden of Delight, attended by nymphs, would not envy a human his earthly pleasures — “heavenly pleasures are better.” In the same way, one who tastes the satisfaction beyond the senses does not envy the sensual. The point is not that pleasure is forbidden, but that something higher makes it lose its pull.

The leper at the coals

§13–16“Suppose a man had leprosy, his limbs covered in sores. Devoured by worms, scratching the wounds, he sears his body over a pit of glowing coals — and finds relief in it. Then a surgeon cures him, and he is healthy, happy, his own master. Would that cured man envy a leper his pit of coals, or wish to go near it?” “No, worthy Gotama — medicine is needed only when there is disease.” “And if two strong men dragged him toward the coals, wouldn’t he writhe to escape?” “Yes — for that fire is truly painful to touch.” “Was the fire painful only now, or before as well?” “Both. But before, his faculties were impaired by disease, so he had a distorted perception that the fire was pleasant. In the same way, sensual pleasures past, present, and future are painful to touch, fiercely burning — yet beings impaired by craving perceive them as pleasant.”

The itch, and the verse

§17–19“The more the leper scratches and burns his sores, the more they fester — yet he gets a little pleasure from the itch. So too, the more beings indulge sensual craving, the more their craving grows and the more they burn — yet they snatch a little gratification from it. No king or minister, past or present, ever lived with a peaceful mind without giving up craving.” Then the Buddha expressed this heartfelt verse: “Health is the ultimate blessing; extinguishment, the ultimate happiness. Of paths, the ultimate is eightfold — it’s safe, and leads to freedom from death.”

What “health” is not

§19–21“Wonderful, worthy Gotama!” said Māgaṇḍiya. “I too have heard the old wanderers say this.” “But what health, what extinguishment did they mean?” At this Māgaṇḍiya stroked his own limbs: “This is that health, this is that extinguishment — for I am now healthy and happy!” “Māgaṇḍiya, suppose a man blind from birth heard, ‘a white cloth is lovely, spotless, clean,’ and went searching for one — and someone cheated him with a filthy rag, which he gladly wore, praising its whiteness. Did he do this knowing and seeing, or out of faith?” “Out of faith, not seeing.” “Just so, the wanderers of other paths are blind: not knowing health, not seeing extinguishment, they recite the verse. This body is a disease, a boil, a dart — yet you call it ‘health.’ You lack the noble vision by which one knows health and sees extinguishment.”

The cure, and the cheat exposed

§22–24“I am confident the worthy Gotama can teach me to know health and see extinguishment.” “If I taught one who could not yet see, it would only weary me — like a surgeon whose medicine fails to cure a blind man. But suppose the medicine did cure him. The moment his eyes opened, he would lose all desire for that filthy rag, and turn on the one who cheated him as an enemy: ‘So long I was deceived by that man and his dirty cloth!’ In the same way, if you came to know health and see extinguishment, then as your vision arose you would give up desire for the five grasping aggregates and think: ‘So long I’ve been cheated by this mind — what I grasped was only form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness. Grasping conditions existence; existence, rebirth; rebirth, all this aging and death and sorrow. So this whole mass of suffering originates.’”

Keep good company, and see

§25–28“I am confident the worthy Gotama can teach me to rise from this seat cured of blindness.” “Then, Māgaṇḍiya, associate with true persons. Doing so, you’ll hear the true teaching; hearing it, you’ll practise in line with it; practising, you’ll know and see for yourself: ‘These are diseases, boils, and darts — and here they cease without remainder.’ When grasping ceases, existence ceases; when existence ceases, rebirth ceases; and so this whole mass of suffering ceases.” “Excellent, worthy Gotama! I go for refuge to you, to the teaching, and to the Saṅgha. May I receive the going forth?” Told that a former member of another sect spends four months on probation, Māgaṇḍiya answered, “If four months, I’ll spend four years.” He was ordained — and not long after, living alone, withdrawn, diligent, he realized the goal in this very life and became one of the perfected.

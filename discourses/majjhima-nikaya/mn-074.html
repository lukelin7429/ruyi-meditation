Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 74

With Dīghanakha

Dīghanakhasutta

Setting
Near Rājagaha, on Vulture’s Peak, in the Boar’s Cave
Speakers
The Buddha and the skeptic wanderer Dīghanakha (“Long-nails”), addressed by his clan name Aggivessana; with Venerable Sāriputta standing by
Form
A dismantling of views followed by a turn to practice — ending in two awakenings at once: Sāriputta to arahantship, Dīghanakha to the vision of the Dhamma.
Length
~9 minutes to read
Northern parallel
SĀ 969 (Saṁyukta-āgama 969)
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — subtle; the critique of clinging-to-views and the pivot to contemplating feeling reward careful reading.

Why this discourse, seventy-fourth

A man arrives announcing that he believes in nothing: “Nothing is acceptable to me.” The Buddha answers with a single disarming question — and is that view acceptable to you? — and from that small turn unfolds a teaching that does not argue the skeptic into a better opinion, but shows him how to set down opinion itself. Before the talk ends, two people are awakened: the visitor, and — quietly, while fanning the Buddha — the Buddha’s greatest disciple.

The wanderer is Dīghanakha, “Long-nails” — a nickname for an ascetic who let his nails grow as a mark of austerity — addressed throughout by his clan name, Aggivessana. By tradition he was Sāriputta’s nephew, and very likely a follower of the skeptic Sañjaya, whose whole method was to take no position on anything. His opening gambit, “nothing is acceptable to me,” is that skepticism worn as a badge: the pose of the man too clever to be caught holding a belief.

The Buddha’s reply is witty but goes deep. To deny all views is itself a view; the claim devours itself. More tellingly, the Buddha observes that most who say “it makes no difference” quietly cling to some other position anyway — for the mind cannot rest in pure unknowing, and a professed skepticism is often just how one hides one’s views from oneself. Only a few truly let a view go “without grasping another.” That phrase — letting go without grasping another — is the key the whole discourse turns.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Clinging to any view — “everything is acceptable,” “nothing is,” or “some things are” — breeds dispute and distress, so the wise let a view go without grasping another; and turning from views to the direct contemplation of the body and the three feelings as impermanent and not-self, one grows disillusioned and is freed — as Sāriputta is, on the spot, while Dīghanakha gains the eye of the Dhamma.

The skeptic’s self-refuting badge

Dīghanakha presents “nothing is acceptable to me” as a finished philosophy. The Buddha does not refute it; he simply asks whether that view is acceptable to its holder — and the trap springs shut. If Dīghanakha accepts his own view, he contradicts it (here is at least one thing he accepts); if he rejects it, it falls of its own weight. He dodges: “it would make no difference.” The Buddha lets the logic stand and adds a psychological observation sharper than any syllogism: the world is full of people who say “it makes no difference” and yet “grasp another view” in the same breath. Total skepticism is unstable; the mind seeks ground, and so the professed believer-in-nothing usually smuggles his convictions in by the back door. Genuine release is rare: to give up a view “by not grasping another.”

Three stances, and why even the least clinging must go

The Buddha lays out the field of possible positions:

The viewIts relation to grasping
“Everything is acceptable”close to greed, bondage, approving, attachment, grasping
“Nothing is acceptable”far from greed, bondage, attachment, grasping
“Some yes, some not”partly close, partly far

This looks, for a moment, like an endorsement — Dīghanakha crows, “Worthy Gotama commends my view!” because his “nothing is acceptable” is the one “far from grasping.” But the Buddha’s point is the opposite. Even the least entangling view, if clung to as “the only truth, anything else is futile,” sets its holder against the other two camps; “and when there’s arguing, there’s dispute; when there’s dispute, distress; when there’s distress, harm.” A sensible person, foreseeing this, “gives up that view by not grasping another.” The lesson is not which view to hold, but to stop holding — the problem was never the content of the opinion but the grip of the hand.

From views to the body

Then the Buddha pivots, with no transition, from the abstract to the intimate: “Aggivessana, this body…” Having shown that no view is worth clinging to, he turns to what we cling to most — this physical form, “made up of the four principal states, produced by mother and father, built up from rice and porridge, liable to… breaking up and destruction.” It is to be seen “as impermanent, as suffering, as diseased, as a boil, as a dart… as empty, as not-self.” Seen so, “desire, affection, and subservience to the body” fall away. For an ascetic like Dīghanakha, who may have tormented his body in the name of austerity, the medicine is exact: not to hate the body or to pamper it, but to see it clearly and so be freed of bondage to it.

The three feelings, one at a time

The contemplation narrows to feeling, the hinge of all craving. There are three kinds — pleasant, painful, neutral — and the Buddha notes a simple, telling fact: they are mutually exclusive in the moment. “When you feel a pleasant feeling, you don’t feel a painful or neutral one”; each excludes the others, and so each is fleeting, giving way to the next. All three are “impermanent, conditioned, dependently originated, liable to end, vanish, fade away, and cease.” This is the decisive seeing. The noble disciple who watches feeling arise and pass “grows disillusioned”; disillusioned, “desire fades away”; with the fading of desire, “they’re freed,” and know it: “Rebirth is ended… there is nothing further for this place.” The whole of liberation is compressed into the patient observation of how feeling comes and goes.

Free, and done with disputes

The liberated mind, the Buddha adds, “doesn’t side with anyone or dispute with anyone. They use the language of the world to communicate without getting stuck on it.” This closes the circle back to the opening. The discourse began with a man whose identity was a position to be defended; it ends with the portrait of one who has no position to defend, who can speak the ordinary words — “I,” “mine,” “acceptable,” “not” — as useful tools without being gripped by them. The cure for the disease of views is not a better view but a freedom that no longer needs one.

Two awakenings — and the irony

The drama is in who is listening. Sāriputta stands behind the Buddha, fanning him, and follows the teaching not as its object but as an overhearer. Reflecting — “the Buddha speaks of giving up all these things through direct knowledge” — “his mind was freed from the defilements by not grasping.” This is the moment of Sāriputta’s full awakening, a fortnight after his ordination. Meanwhile the words aimed at Dīghanakha land more modestly: “the stainless, immaculate vision of the Dhamma arose” in him — “everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease” — the breakthrough of stream-entry, and he goes for refuge. The teaching meant to convert the nephew brings the uncle to arahantship; the one being argued with gains the lesser fruit, the one quietly serving gains the greater. (Northern traditions add that Dīghanakha, too, went on to ordain and awaken fully — identifying him with the elder Mahākoṭṭhita.) The commentaries set this scene on the full-moon night of Māgha — the day later honoured across the Buddhist world as Māgha-pūjā.

Key terms

khamati — “is acceptable, pleasing, agreeable.” Dīghanakha’s slogan, “nothing is acceptable to me” (sabbaṁ me na khamati), is deliberately obscure — a skeptic’s refusal to commit.
diṭṭhi — a view, opinion, theory. The discourse’s target is not any particular view but the clinging to views — insisting “this alone is true, all else futile.”
anupādā(ya) — “by not grasping.” To give up a view “without grasping another” is true release; it is also exactly how Sāriputta’s mind “was freed from the defilements by not grasping.”
vivāda — dispute. The chain the Buddha traces: clinging → argument → dispute → distress → harm. Holding a view as absolute is the first link.
vedanā — feeling: pleasant, painful, neutral. Mutually exclusive in the moment, all impermanent and dependently arisen. Watching feeling arise and cease is the doorway to dispassion and freedom.
dhammacakkhu — the “eye of the Dhamma”: the breakthrough of stream-entry, “everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease.” It is this that arose in Dīghanakha.

The text

The full discourse. The dialogue on views and the teaching on body and feeling are given complete; the three-fold reflection on the stances of views is given once in pattern and condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

“Nothing is acceptable to me”

§1–3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, on Vulture’s Peak, in the Boar’s Cave. Then the wanderer Dīghanakha went up to the Buddha, exchanged greetings, stood to one side, and said, “Worthy Gotama, this is my doctrine and view: ‘Nothing is acceptable to me.’” “This view of yours, Aggivessana — is that acceptable to you?” “If I were to accept this view, worthy Gotama, it would make no difference, it would make no difference!” “Well, Aggivessana, there are many in the world who say ‘it would make no difference’ — yet they don’t give up that view, and they grasp another. And there are a scant few who say it, and give up that view by not grasping another.”

Three stances on what is acceptable

§4–5“There are ascetics and brahmins who hold ‘everything is acceptable to me’; others who hold ‘nothing is acceptable to me’; and others, ‘some things are acceptable and some are not.’ The view of those to whom everything is acceptable is close to greed, bondage, approving, attachment, and grasping. The view of those to whom nothing is acceptable is far from greed, bondage, attachment, and grasping.” At this Dīghanakha said, “Worthy Gotama commends my view! He recommends my view!” “As for those to whom some things are acceptable and some not — their view of what is acceptable is close to grasping; their view of what is not is far from it.”

Why even that view must be let go

§6–8“A sensible person reflects: ‘If I obstinately stick to my view and insist, “This alone is true, anything else is futile,” then I’ll clash with the other two — and where there’s arguing there’s dispute, where dispute distress, where distress harm.’ So, seeing in themselves the potential for argument, dispute, distress, and harm, they give up that view by not grasping another. That is how these views are given up and let go.” The Buddha says the same of all three stances in turn: whichever one is clung to as the only truth sets its holder at war with the rest, and the wise let it go without seizing a replacement.

The body, seen clearly

§9“Aggivessana, this body is formed. It’s made up of the four principal states, produced by mother and father, built up from rice and porridge, liable to impermanence, to wearing away and erosion, to breaking up and destruction. You should see it as impermanent, as suffering, as diseased, as a boil, as a dart, as gloom, as an affliction, as alien, as breaking apart, as empty, as not-self. Doing so, you’ll give up desire, affection, and subservience to the body.”

The three feelings

§10–12“There are these three feelings: pleasant, painful, and neutral. When you feel a pleasant feeling, you don’t feel a painful or neutral one; and so for each — you feel only one at a time. And all three are impermanent, conditioned, dependently originated, liable to end, vanish, fade away, and cease. Seeing this, a learned noble disciple grows disillusioned with pleasant, painful, and neutral feelings. Being disillusioned, desire fades away. When desire fades away they’re freed. When freed, they know they’re freed: ‘Rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey has been completed, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this place.’

Free of disputes

§13A mendicant whose mind is freed like this doesn’t side with anyone or dispute with anyone. They use the language of the world to communicate without getting stuck on it.”

Two awakenings

§14–16Now at that time Venerable Sāriputta was standing behind the Buddha, fanning him. He thought, “It seems the Buddha speaks of giving up and letting go all these things through direct knowledge.” Reflecting like this, Venerable Sāriputta’s mind was freed from the defilements by not grasping. And in the wanderer Dīghanakha there arose the stainless, immaculate vision of the Dhamma: “Everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease.” Then Dīghanakha saw, attained, and fathomed the Dhamma; he went beyond doubt and became self-assured regarding the Teacher’s instructions, and said: “Excellent, worthy Gotama! As if righting the overturned, or revealing the hidden, or pointing out the path to the lost, or lighting a lamp in the dark… I go for refuge to the worthy Gotama, to the teaching, and to the Saṅgha. From this day forth, may the worthy Gotama remember me as one gone for refuge for life.”

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 135

The Shorter Analysis of Deeds

Cūḷakammavibhaṅgasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, addressed to a visiting brahmin student
Speakers
The Buddha and the student Subha, son of Todeyya
Form
A question about human inequality, answered with a brief formula and then seven clean, paired examples
Length
~13 minutes to read
Northern parallel
The most widely attested discourse in the Majjhima Nikāya: six Chinese parallels, two Tibetan, two Sanskrit, and fragments in Khotanese, Sogdian, and Tocharian
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — clear and systematic, prized across the ancient Buddhist world for exactly that clarity

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-thirty-fifth

No discourse in the Majjhima Nikāya survives in more languages than this one. Beyond the expected Chinese and Tibetan versions, fragments have turned up in Khotanese, Sogdian, and Tocharian — the languages of Silk Road oasis cities, carried along trade routes that stretched from India to the edge of China. The question this sutta answers explains why: “Why are some people short-lived, some long-lived; some sickly, some healthy; some ugly, some beautiful; some poor, some rich?” It is, in other words, the single most universal question anyone has ever asked about fairness — and the Buddha’s answer, precise and cleanly structured, travelled about as far as a teaching can travel.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

“Sentient beings are the owners of their deeds and heir to their deeds” — and this single principle, unpacked into seven precise, paired examples, accounts for why people differ in lifespan, health, beauty, social standing, wealth, family status, and wisdom, without ever claiming that every misfortune traces back to a specific past deed.

A student’s question

Subha, a brahmin student and the son of Todeyya, comes to the Buddha with a question that has occurred to nearly everyone at some point: “What is the reason why even among those who are human beings some are seen to be inferior and superior?” He lists the contrasts precisely — “short-lived and long-lived, sickly and healthy, ugly and beautiful, insignificant and illustrious, poor and rich, from low and eminent families, witless and wise.” It is, notably, not his first encounter with the Buddha; a parallel account elsewhere describes his earlier conversion. Here, though, he arrives simply as an inquirer with a real question.

The formula, then the detail

The Buddha’s first answer is a single, celebrated line, one of five sayings the tradition holds should be recollected often by everyone, lay or ordained:

The formula. “Sentient beings are the owners of their deeds and heir to their deeds. Deeds are their womb, their relative, and their refuge. It is deeds that divide beings into inferior and superior.”

Subha, honest about not following the compressed statement, asks for the details — and the Buddha obliges with seven examples, each following an identical structure with startling precision.

Seven pairs, one clean structure

Each example matches its corresponding deed to its corresponding trait — in the exact order Subha originally listed them — and each is stated twice, once for the harmful version of the deed and once for its wholesome opposite:

Each pair, whichever direction, is also said to lead to rebirth in hell or in heaven respectively — the human trait is what appears if the person returns to human life rather than remaining in that other realm. Notice, too, that the text consistently frames every example as “some woman or man,” a phrasing the commentary points out is unusual and deliberate: kamma, agency, and the possibility of good or bad rebirth apply without distinction of gender, undercutting any assumption that a woman’s moral agency, or her prospects, are somehow lesser.

Not a rigid formula

It would be easy to read this sutta as claiming that every illness, every misfortune, traces to one identifiable past deed — but that stronger claim belongs to a different tradition entirely. Elsewhere the Buddha explicitly rejects the Jain position that all suffering is exclusively the fruit of past kamma; being born human at all, the tradition holds, already reflects good deeds, but ordinary causes — weather, accident, other people’s choices — still operate freely within that life. What this discourse establishes is a general, reliable tendency linking certain patterns of conduct to certain patterns of result, not an exhaustive forensic account of any single person’s every hardship.

A conversion on the spot

Subha’s response is the discourse’s closing note, using a formula that appears throughout the canon for a listener whose confusion has genuinely lifted: “As if he were righting the overturned, or revealing the hidden, or pointing out the path to the lost, or lighting a lamp in the dark so people with clear eyes can see what’s there — worthy Gotama has made the Teaching clear in many ways.” He goes for refuge on the spot, “from this day forth,” as a lay follower for life — a clean, satisfying resolution to a question that, as this sutta’s remarkable manuscript history shows, people across the ancient world found just as urgent as Subha did.

Key terms

kammassakā — “owners of their deeds”: the opening word of the discourse’s central formula, also recollected as one of five sayings recommended for everyone, lay or ordained, in AN 5.57.
“some woman or man” — the discourse’s consistent, deliberately gender-inclusive phrasing for each example, found only here and in a handful of other suttas dealing specifically with kamma.
the seven pairs — killing, hurting, irritability, jealousy, stinginess, arrogance, and indifference to learning, each matched with its opposite and with a specific resulting human trait.
the Jain view rejected — the position that all present suffering is strictly and exclusively the result of past kamma; the Buddha’s account here describes a strong tendency, not an exhaustive determinism.
“righting the overturned…” — the standard formula marking a listener’s genuine, immediate understanding, used here by Subha as he goes for refuge.

The text

The discourse in full: Subha’s question, the Buddha’s brief formula, and all seven paired examples given complete, since each is essential to the sutta’s carefully matched structure. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A student’s question

§1–4So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove. Then the student Subha, Todeyya’s son, approached the Buddha and said: “What is the cause, worthy Gotama, what is the reason why even among those who are human beings some are seen to be inferior and superior? For people are seen who are short-lived and long-lived, sickly and healthy, ugly and beautiful, insignificant and illustrious, poor and rich, from low and eminent families, witless and wise.”

“Student, sentient beings are the owners of their deeds and heir to their deeds. Deeds are their womb, their relative, and their refuge. It is deeds that divide beings into inferior and superior.” “I don’t understand the meaning of what the worthy Gotama has said in brief, without explaining the details. Worthy Gotama, please teach me this matter in detail so I can understand the meaning.” “Well then, student, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, worthy sir,” replied Subha. The Buddha said this:

Seven pairs, one clean structure

§5–6“Take some woman or man who kills living creatures — violent, bloody-handed, a hardened killer, merciless to living beings. Because of undertaking such deeds, when their body breaks up, they’re reborn in a place of loss, hell. If they’re not reborn in a place of loss, but return to the human realm, then wherever they’re reborn they’re short-lived. For killing living creatures is the path leading to a short lifespan.

But take some woman or man who gives up killing living creatures — who renounces the rod and the sword, who is scrupulous and kind, living full of sympathy for all living beings. Because of undertaking such deeds, they’re reborn in a heavenly realm. If they return to the human realm, wherever they’re reborn they’re long-lived. For not killing living creatures is the path leading to a long lifespan.

§7–8Take some woman or man who habitually hurts living creatures with a fist, stone, rod, or sword. Because of undertaking such deeds, after death they’re reborn in a place of loss — or if they return to the human realm, they’re sickly. But take some woman or man who would never hurt living creatures this way. Because of undertaking such deeds, they’re reborn in a heavenly realm — or if they return to the human realm, they’re healthy.

§9–10Take some woman or man who is irritable and bad-tempered. Even when lightly criticized they lose their temper, becoming annoyed, hostile, and hard-hearted, displaying annoyance, hate, and bitterness. Because of undertaking such deeds, after death they’re reborn in a place of loss — or if they return to the human realm, they’re ugly. But take some woman or man who isn’t irritable and bad-tempered — who, even when heavily criticized, doesn’t lose their temper. Because of undertaking such deeds, they’re reborn in a heavenly realm — or if they return to the human realm, they’re lovely.

§11–12Take some woman or man who is jealous — who envies, resents, and begrudges the possessions, honor, respect, reverence, homage, and veneration given to others. Because of undertaking such deeds, after death they’re reborn in a place of loss — or if they return to the human realm, they’re insignificant. But take some woman or man who is not jealous. Because of undertaking such deeds, they’re reborn in a heavenly realm — or if they return to the human realm, they’re illustrious.

§13–14Take some woman or man who doesn’t give to ascetics or brahmins such things as food, drink, clothing, vehicles, garlands, fragrance, makeup, bed, house, and lighting. Because of undertaking such deeds, after death they’re reborn in a place of loss — or if they return to the human realm, they’re poor. But take some woman or man who does give such things. Because of undertaking such deeds, they’re reborn in a heavenly realm — or if they return to the human realm, they’re rich.

§15–16Take some woman or man who is obstinate and arrogant — who doesn’t bow to those they should bow to, doesn’t rise up for them, offer them a seat, make way for them, or honor, respect, esteem, or venerate those worthy of it. Because of undertaking such deeds, after death they’re reborn in a place of loss — or if they return to the human realm, they’re reborn in a low class family. But take some woman or man who is not obstinate and arrogant. Because of undertaking such deeds, they’re reborn in a heavenly realm — or if they return to the human realm, they’re reborn in an eminent family.

§17–18Take some woman or man who doesn’t approach an ascetic or brahmin to ask: ‘Sir, what is skillful and what is unskillful? What is blameworthy and what is blameless? What should be cultivated and what should not be cultivated? What kind of action will lead to my lasting harm and suffering, or to my lasting welfare and happiness?’ Because of undertaking such deeds, after death they’re reborn in a place of loss — or if they return to the human realm, they’re witless.

But take some woman or man who does approach an ascetic or brahmin to ask these very questions. Because of undertaking such deeds, when their body breaks up, they’re reborn in a good place, a heavenly realm. If they’re not reborn in a heavenly realm, but return to the human realm, then wherever they’re reborn they’re very wise. For asking questions of ascetics or brahmins is the path leading to wisdom.

The formula, restated

§19–20So it is the way people live that makes them how they are, whether short-lived or long-lived, sickly or healthy, ugly or lovely, insignificant or illustrious, poor or rich, in a low class or eminent family, or witless or wise. Sentient beings are the owners of their deeds and heir to their deeds. Deeds are their womb, their relative, and their refuge. It is deeds that divide beings into inferior and superior.”

A conversion on the spot

§21When he had spoken, Subha said to him: “Excellent, worthy Gotama! Excellent! As if he were righting the overturned, or revealing the hidden, or pointing out the path to the lost, or lighting a lamp in the dark so people with clear eyes can see what’s there, worthy Gotama has made the Teaching clear in many ways. I go for refuge to the worthy Gotama, to the teaching, and to the mendicant Saṅgha. From this day forth, may the worthy Gotama remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.”

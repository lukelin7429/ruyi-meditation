Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 122

The Longer Discourse on Emptiness

Mahāsuññatasutta

Setting
Near Kapilavatthu, the Buddha’s home town, in the Banyan Tree Monastery
Speakers
The Buddha and Venerable Ānanda
Form
A warning against company, a diagnostic method for meditation, and emptiness extended into posture, speech, thought, and insight — closing with a famous vow
Length
~17 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A close counterpart survives in the Chinese Madhyama Āgama (MĀ 191, “Greater Discourse on Emptiness”)
Difficulty
★★★★★ — practical, psychologically exacting, and ends with one of the canon’s sharpest lines

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-twenty-second

Where MN 121 gave emptiness as a meditation technique, this companion discourse gives it as a whole way of living — and it opens with the Buddha, in his own home town, disturbed by a crowd. Robe-making season has filled Khemaka’s lodge with bedding; mendicants are everywhere, sewing, socializing, catching up. The Buddha’s response is not gentle. Company, he tells Ānanda, is simply incompatible with the freedom he is pointing toward — and from there the discourse widens emptiness out from a seated technique into how a mendicant should check their meditation, walk, talk, think, and watch their own mind, before closing with a vow that has unsettled readers for over two thousand years: “I shall not mollycoddle you… the core will stand the test.”

It is also, notably, a discourse the Buddha delivers in Kapilavatthu — among his own Sakyan relatives, in the community that raised him. That he chooses this setting to warn so bluntly against the pull of familiar company is not likely an accident.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Genuine emptiness requires physical seclusion, a diagnostic and self-checking approach to meditation, and its extension into every posture, every conversation, every thought, and finally insight into the five aggregates — and the Buddha will not spare his disciples the difficulty of any of it, because “the core will stand the test.”

A crowded lodge

The setting is disarmingly ordinary. Returning from almsround in his home town of Kapilavatthu, the Buddha notices an unusual number of resting places spread out at Khemaka the Dark’s lodge and wonders aloud whether many mendicants are staying there. Finding Ānanda sewing robes with a group at a nearby lodge, he learns the reason: it is kaṭhina season, the period after the rains retreat when the Saṅgha gathers to make and mend robes — a natural, sanctioned occasion for socializing. It is precisely this ordinary, blameless gathering that prompts the Buddha’s warning.

Why company is incompatible with freedom

“Ānanda,” the Buddha says, “a mendicant doesn’t shine who enjoys company and groups, who loves them and likes to enjoy them.” Such a person, he says flatly, cannot get “the pleasure of renunciation, seclusion, peace, awakening” at will, and cannot enter either the “temporary and pleasant” freedom of the absorptions or the “irreversible and unshakable” freedom of arahantship. Only someone who “lives alone, withdrawn from the group” can expect this. The reasoning beneath the warning is almost tender: “I do not see even a single sight which, with its decay and perishing, would not give rise to sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress in someone who has desire and lust for it.” Company is not condemned as sinful — it is simply, like any pleasant sight, something whose loss will hurt exactly as much as it was loved.

Even the Buddha manages the crowd

What follows is an unusually candid glimpse of the Buddha’s own practice. His own habitual meditation, he says, is “to enter and remain in emptiness internally by not focusing on any signs.” But he too is sought out — by monks, nuns, laypeople, rulers, ministers, and followers of other religions. When this happens, with his mind “slanting, sloping, and inclining to seclusion,” he “invariably gives a talk concerned only with sending them away.” Even the Buddha, in other words, has to actively protect his solitude — not through rudeness, but by steering every visit back toward its natural conclusion.

A diagnostic method, not a formula

The Buddha then gives Ānanda something closer to a troubleshooting guide than a technique. A mendicant wishing to “enter and remain in emptiness internally” first stills, settles, unifies, and immerses the mind through the four absorptions. Then they check: focusing on emptiness internally, externally, both internally and externally, or on “the imperturbable” (the formless attainments) — does the mind actually “leap forth, gain confidence, settle down, and become decided,” or not? Either way, the meditator simply notes what is true: “I am focusing on emptiness internally, but my mind does not leap forth” — or, once it does, “my mind leaps forth, gains confidence, settles down, and becomes decided.” If it doesn’t yet settle, they return to the same basis of immersion and try again. This is meditation as honest self-assessment, not as an assumption of automatic success — notably, the commentary observes, the order here (emptiness developed before the formless attainments) is the reverse of the sequence in MN 121, showing the method is not rigidly fixed.

Carrying it into posture, speech, and thought

The practice doesn’t end when the meditator gets up. Whatever posture the mind inclines toward — walking, standing, sitting, lying down — they take it up with the same watchfulness: “while I’m walking, bad, unskillful qualities of covetousness and displeasure will not overwhelm me.” The same applies to speech: a long list of ordinary conversational topics — politics, armies and wars, food and clothes, gossip about family and towns, talk of the departed, even idle speculation about future rebirths — is set aside as “low, crude, ordinary, ignoble, and pointless,” not because such talk is wicked but because it doesn’t lead toward “disillusionment, dispassion, cessation, peace, insight, awakening, and extinguishment.” In its place: “talk about self-effacement” — fewness of wishes, contentment, seclusion, energy, ethics, immersion, wisdom, and freedom. Thought receives the identical treatment: sensual, malicious, and cruel thoughts are set aside in favor of thoughts of renunciation, good will, and harmlessness.

Checking the senses and the aggregates

The meditator is told to “regularly check their own mind” against the five kinds of sensual stimulation — sights, sounds, smells, tastes, and touches — simply noting, without alarm, whether interest is still present or has genuinely faded. The practice culminates in observing the rise and fall of the five grasping aggregates themselves — form, feeling, perception, choices, consciousness — “such is its origin, such is its ending,” through which the conceit “I am” is gradually given up. This full sequence, the Buddha says, is “entirely skillful, with skillful outcomes; noble, transcendent, and inaccessible to the Wicked One” — beyond Māra’s reach.

A pointed question for Ānanda

The Buddha then asks Ānanda directly: why would a disciple bother following a teacher, even if driven away? Ānanda, ever deferential, defers the answer back to the Buddha. The reply carries a quiet edge, aimed at the mendicant famous above all others for his prodigious memory of the teachings: a disciple should not stay merely for “statements, mixed prose and verse, or discussions” — since Ānanda and the others have long since learned, remembered, and rehearsed those. What actually makes following a teacher worthwhile is “talk about self-effacement that helps open the heart” — living guidance, not accumulated text.

Three perils

This leads into a striking taxonomy of danger. A teacher (outside the Buddhist fold) who gains a reputation for seclusion attracts visitors, “enjoys infatuation, falls into greed, and returns to indulgence” — the tutor’s peril. A student who imitates that teacher’s seclusion suffers the same fate — the pupil’s peril. But there is a third, worse case: a fully awakened Buddha frequents solitary places and is likewise visited by crowds — yet he does not fall into indulgence. His disciple, however, imitating the Teacher’s reputation for seclusion without the Teacher’s attainment, is visited in turn and does fall — the peril of the spiritual practitioner. This third peril, the Buddha says, is worse than the other two, “with more painful, bitter results,” precisely because it trades on a standard the disciple has not actually reached.

“Treat me as a friend, not an enemy”

From here the Buddha turns personal. “Treat me as a friend, not as an enemy,” he tells Ānanda — and defines the difference with disarming simplicity: disciples who hear well-meant teaching and turn away, refusing to listen or try to understand, treat their teacher as an enemy; those who actively listen and engage treat their teacher as a friend. The discourse’s final line completes the thought with an image that has struck readers for millennia:

The potter’s pots. “I shall not mollycoddle you like a potter with their damp, unfired pots. I shall speak, correcting you again and again, pressing you again and again. The core will stand the test.”

An unfired clay pot, handled too gently, never becomes strong enough to hold anything; only the firing — and the testing that follows it — proves which pots are sound. The Buddha’s refusal to go easy on his disciples is presented, in this closing image, not as harshness but as the deepest form of respect: real friendship, real teaching, means trusting that the core can withstand the pressure.

Key terms

“temporary and pleasant” vs. “irreversible and unshakable” freedom — the four absorptions, versus arahantship: both are said to be out of reach for a mendicant who enjoys company.
āneñja — “the imperturbable”: a term for the formless attainments, checked here alongside internal, external, and internal-and-external emptiness as part of the diagnostic method.
sampajañña — situational awareness: extending the watchfulness of formal meditation into walking, standing, sitting, lying down, speaking, and thinking.
talk about self-effacement (sallekha-kathā) — the ten wholesome topics of conversation (fewness of wishes, contentment, seclusion, energy, ethics, immersion, wisdom, freedom, and the knowledge and vision of freedom) contrasted with a long list of ordinary but pointless talk.
the three perils — the tutor’s, the pupil’s, and the spiritual practitioner’s: the last, where a disciple trades on an unearned reputation for seclusion, is said to carry the worst consequences of the three.
“the core will stand the test” — the closing image of testing unfired pots: the Buddha’s refusal to spare his disciples difficulty, offered as the deepest form of friendship.

The text

The discourse in full: the frame story, the warning against company, the diagnostic meditation method, its extension into posture and speech and thought, the checks on sensuality and the five aggregates, the exchange with Ānanda, the three perils, and the closing vow. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A crowded lodge

§1–3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the land of the Sakyans, near Kapilavatthu, in the Banyan Tree Monastery. Returning from almsround, he went to the lodge of the Sakyan Khemaka the Dark for the day’s meditation, and saw that many resting places had been spread out. He wondered whether many mendicants were living there. Finding Ānanda making robes with many other mendicants at Ghaṭā’s lodge, he asked, and Ānanda confirmed: “Indeed there are, sir. It’s currently the time for making robes.”

Why company is incompatible with freedom

§3–5“Ānanda, a mendicant doesn’t shine who enjoys company and groups, who loves them and likes to enjoy them. It is quite impossible that such a mendicant will get the pleasure of renunciation, the pleasure of seclusion, the pleasure of peace, the pleasure of awakening when they want, without trouble or difficulty. But you should expect that a mendicant who lives alone, withdrawn from the group, will get these pleasures when they want. It is quite impossible that a mendicant who enjoys company will enter and remain in the freedom of heart — either that which is temporary and pleasant, or that which is irreversible and unshakable. But it is possible for one who lives alone, withdrawn from the group. Ānanda, I do not see even a single sight which, with its decay and perishing, would not give rise to sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress in someone who has desire and lust for it.

Even the Buddha manages the crowd

§6But this meditation has been understood by the Realized One, namely to enter and remain in emptiness internally by not focusing on any signs. Now, suppose that while the Realized One is practicing this meditation, monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen, rulers and their chief ministers, monastics of other religions and their disciples go to visit him. In that case, with a mind slanting, sloping, and inclining to seclusion, withdrawn, and loving renunciation, having totally eliminated defiling influences, he invariably gives a talk concerned only with sending them away.

A diagnostic method

§7–10Therefore, if a mendicant should wish, ‘May I enter and remain in emptiness internally!’ then they should still, settle, unify, and immerse their mind in samādhi internally — entering and remaining in the four absorptions. They focus on emptiness internally, but their mind does not leap forth, gain confidence, settle down, and become decided. In that case, they understand: ‘I am focusing on emptiness internally, but my mind does not leap forth.’ In this way they are aware of the situation. The same is said of focusing on emptiness externally, internally and externally, and on the imperturbable. Then that mendicant should still, settle, unify, and immerse their mind in samādhi internally in the same meditation subject as before. They focus again, and this time their mind leaps forth, gains confidence, settles down, and becomes decided. In that case, they understand: ‘My mind leaps forth, gains confidence, settles down, and becomes decided.’ In this way they are aware of the situation.

Posture, speech, and thought

§11While a mendicant is practicing such a meditation, if their mind inclines to walking, they walk, thinking: ‘While I’m walking, bad, unskillful qualities of covetousness and displeasure will not overwhelm me.’ In this way they are aware of the situation. The same applies to standing, sitting, and lying down.

§12If their mind inclines to talking, they think: ‘I will not engage in the kind of speech that is low, crude, ordinary, ignoble, and pointless… talk about kings, bandits, and chief ministers; talk about armies, threats, and wars; talk about food, drink, clothes, and beds; talk about garlands and fragrances; talk about family, vehicles, villages, towns, cities, and countries; talk about women and heroes; street talk and well talk; talk about the departed; motley talk; tales of land and sea; and talk about being reborn in this or that state of existence.’ In this way they are aware of the situation. ‘But I will take part in talk about self-effacement that helps open the heart and leads solely to disillusionment, dispassion, cessation, peace, insight, awakening, and extinguishment — talk about fewness of wishes, contentment, seclusion, aloofness, arousing energy, ethics, immersion, wisdom, freedom, and the knowledge and vision of freedom.’

§13If their mind inclines to thinking, they think: ‘I will not think the kind of thought that is low, crude, ordinary, ignoble, and pointless — sensual, malicious, or cruel thoughts.’ In this way they are aware of the situation. ‘But I will think the kind of thought that is noble and emancipating — thoughts of renunciation, good will, and harmlessness.’

Checking the senses and the aggregates

§14–17There are five kinds of sensual stimulation: sights, sounds, smells, tastes, and touches known by the eye, ear, nose, tongue, and body, which are likable, desirable, agreeable, pleasant, sensual, and arousing. So a mendicant should regularly check their own mind: ‘Does my mind take an interest in any of these?’ If it does, they understand: ‘I have not given up desire and greed for the five kinds of sensual stimulation.’ If it does not, they understand: ‘I have given up desire and greed.’ In this way they are aware of the situation either way.

A mendicant should meditate observing rise and fall in the five grasping aggregates: ‘Such is form, such is the origin of form, such is the ending of form. Such is feeling… perception… choices… Such is consciousness, such is the origin of consciousness, such is the ending of consciousness.’ As they do so, they give up the conceit ‘I am’ regarding the five grasping aggregates. These principles are entirely skillful, with skillful outcomes; they are noble, transcendent, and inaccessible to the Wicked One.

A pointed question for Ānanda

§19–20“What do you think, Ānanda? For what reason would a disciple deem it worthwhile to follow the Teacher, even if driven away?” “Our teachings are rooted in the Buddha. He is our guide and our refuge. Sir, may the Buddha himself please clarify the meaning of this.” “A disciple would not deem it worthwhile to follow the Teacher for the sake of statements, mixed prose and verse, or discussions — for a long time you have learned these teachings, remembering, rehearsing, and penetrating them theoretically. But a disciple would deem it worthwhile to follow the Teacher, even if driven away, for the sake of talk about self-effacement that helps open the heart and leads solely to disillusionment, dispassion, cessation, peace, insight, awakening, and extinguishment.

Three perils

§21–24This being so, Ānanda, there is a peril for the tutor, a peril for the pupil, and a peril for a spiritual practitioner. A teacher who frequents a secluded lodging — a wilderness, the root of a tree, a hill, a cave, a charnel ground — and while meditating withdrawn is visited by a stream of brahmins and householders, may enjoy infatuation, fall into greed, and return to indulgence: this is the tutor’s peril. A disciple who emulates the teacher’s fostering of seclusion, and is visited in the same way, may fall in the same way: this is the pupil’s peril. But when a Realized One arises in the world and frequents such places, visited in the same way, he does not fall into indulgence — yet a disciple of his, emulating his fostering of seclusion and visited in turn, may enjoy infatuation, fall into greed, and return to indulgence: this is the peril of the spiritual practitioner, and it has more painful, bitter results than the other two, even leading to the underworld.

“Treat me as a friend, not an enemy”

§25–27So, Ānanda, treat me as a friend, not as an enemy. That will be for your lasting welfare and happiness. Disciples treat their Teacher as an enemy when, taught Dhamma out of kindness and sympathy for their welfare and happiness, they don’t want to listen, don’t try to understand, and turn away from the instruction. They treat their Teacher as a friend when they want to listen, actively try to understand, and don’t turn away. So, Ānanda, treat me as a friend, not as an enemy. I shall not mollycoddle you like a potter with their damp, unfired pots. I shall speak, correcting you again and again, pressing you again and again. The core will stand the test.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Ānanda approved what the Buddha said.

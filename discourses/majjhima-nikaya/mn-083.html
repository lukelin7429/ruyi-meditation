Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 83

About King Maghadeva

Maghadevasutta

Setting
Near Mithilā, in the Maghadeva Mango Grove (the land of Videha)
Speakers
The Buddha and Venerable Ānanda; within the story, King Maghadeva, King Nimi, and Sakka, lord of gods
Form
A tale of a former age — a lineage of renouncing kings, and the Buddha’s revelation that he founded their practice, and has now founded a far better one.
Length
~12 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 67 (Madhyama-āgama 67)
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a gentle narrative; its point lands in the final contrast between heaven and liberation.

Why this discourse, eighty-third

In a mango grove named after a long-dead king, the Buddha smiles, and tells of an age when grey hair was a summons. A line of righteous kings, each at the first sign of ageing, handed over the crown and went forth — founding a noble custom that ran for thousands of generations. Then the Buddha reveals that he himself founded it, lifetimes ago, as that first king — and that the practice he started then, good as it was, reached only as far as heaven. The one he founds now reaches all the way to the deathless.

The hero is King Maghadeva, just and devout, who had asked his barber to tell him the moment grey hairs appeared. When the day came, the barber announced, “The messengers of the gods have shown themselves” — for the first grey hair is the herald of old age and death. Maghadeva had it plucked, rewarded the barber, settled the kingdom on his son, and went forth, saying: “I have enjoyed human pleasures; now it is time to seek the heavenly.” And he charged his heir to do the same in his turn, and to “keep up this good practice I have founded — do not be my final man.”

So began a lineage. Eighty-four thousand kings, son after son, renounced at the first grey hair, cultivated the four divine abidings in that same grove, and were reborn in the company of the gods. The chain held across an immense span — including the remarkable King Nimi, whom the gods themselves invited to heaven, and who chose instead to return to his duties — until at last one king refused to go forth and broke the custom: “he was their final man.” The Buddha’s closing turn reframes the whole tale, and lays a charge on Ānanda that still stands.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A noble custom of renouncing at the first grey hair, founded by the Buddha himself in a past life as King Maghadeva, carried a long line of kings to a heavenly rebirth through the divine abidings — but heaven is not freedom; so the Buddha has now founded a better practice, the noble eightfold path, which leads all the way to awakening, and he charges his disciples not to be the ones who let it die.

The messenger of the gods

The image that opens the story is unforgettable: a king who has arranged to be told the instant his first grey hair appears — and who hears it announced as “the messengers of the gods” (devadūta) have shown themselves. Ageing is a herald. The grey hair is not a trivial cosmetic event but a divine summons, the first visible notice that this body is on its way to old age and death. Where most people pluck the grey hair to deny the message, Maghadeva reads it rightly: it is time. “I have enjoyed human pleasures; now it is time to seek heavenly pleasures.” The wisdom is in receiving the messenger rather than turning him away — in letting the plain fact of ageing become a spur to the spiritual life rather than something to be cosmetically hidden. (The theme returns in full in MN 130, where ageing, sickness, and death are the three messengers who reproach those who ignored them.)

A lineage of righteous renunciation

What Maghadeva founds is a custom, a “good practice” (kalyāṇa-vatta) passed from king to king: rule justly, keep the observance days, and at the first grey hair hand over the throne and go forth to cultivate the four divine abidings — love, compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity — radiated boundlessly “to the whole world.” The lifespans are vast and symmetrical (84,000 years each as child, prince, king, and renunciant), and the line runs for 84,000 generations, every one reborn in a realm of the gods. It is a picture of a whole civilisation ordered toward the spiritual: power held lightly, surrendered gracefully when age calls, and crowned by a life of boundless goodwill. The repeated charge — “keep up this good practice; do not be my final man” — makes each king a link who must not be the one to let the chain break.

King Nimi, who chose duty over heaven

Near the end of the line stands King Nimi, and his episode is the story’s jewel. So admired is his justice that the gods of the Thirty-Three, gathered in their hall, sing his praises; and Sakka, their lord, sends his charioteer Mātali with a thousand-horse heavenly chariot to bring Nimi up to enjoy “heavenly glory.” (Mātali even offers to drive him by the road of the hells or the road of the heavens — “both ways,” says Nimi, wanting to see all of it.) But when Sakka invites him to stay and revel among the gods, Nimi declines: “Enough — send me back to Mithilā, so that I may rule my people justly and keep the observance days.” A king who would rather return to the work of righteous government than linger in paradise: it is a quiet rebuke to the idea that the goal is pleasure, even divine pleasure. Nimi’s greatness is that he treasures his duty and his practice above the reward they might earn.

The final man

The chain finally breaks. King Nimi’s son, Kaḷāra Janaka, “did not go forth… he broke that good practice. He was their final man.” The phrase has weight throughout the discourse: a “final man” (antima-purisa) is the one in whose hands a noble inheritance dies — not through some dramatic evil, but simply by failing to carry it on. It is a sobering note. The longest and finest tradition is only ever one generation from ending; it survives not by its past glory but by each person choosing, in turn, to keep it. To be a “final man” is to be the link that, by mere neglect, lets thousands of years of good practice come to nothing.

“I was Maghadeva” — and the great upgrade

Then the reframing. “Ānanda, you might think Maghadeva was someone else. But you should not: I myself was King Maghadeva.” The Buddha founded that ancient good practice. But — and here is the discourse’s real teaching — “that good practice does not lead to disillusionment, dispassion, cessation, peace, insight, awakening, and extinguishment. It leads only as far as rebirth in a realm of the gods.” For all its nobility, the old custom topped out at heaven; and heaven, however exalted, is still within the round of rebirth, still impermanent, still short of freedom. So the Buddha draws the contrast that the whole story exists to make:

The old good practiceThe new good practice
renounce at grey hair; cultivate the four divine abidingsthe noble eightfold path
leads as far as a heavenly rebirthleads to disillusionment, dispassion, cessation, peace, insight, awakening, extinguishment
still within the round of rebirththe way beyond it

The same Bodhisatta who once founded a heaven-bound practice has, as the Buddha, founded the path to the deathless. It is a striking statement about the trajectory of his own development across lives — and about the difference between even the best worldly good and liberation. A life of justice and boundless goodwill is genuinely good, and earns a genuinely good result; but it is not, by itself, the end of suffering.

The charge that still stands

The discourse closes by turning the old refrain on the listener. “Ānanda, I say to you: keep up this good practice that I have now founded. Do not be my final men.” The eightfold path is the new inheritance, and the warning is the same as Maghadeva’s to his son: a noble practice survives only if each generation chooses to carry it. We who read the discourse are, in a sense, addressed directly: the path the Buddha founded is now in our hands, and whether it continues depends on whether we keep it up — or become the final ones. The whole long tale of kings turns out to be a frame for a single, living charge: do not let this die with you.

Key terms

devadūta — “messenger of the gods.” Here, the first grey hair: a herald of old age and death, and a summons to the spiritual life. Received rightly, ageing becomes a spur, not a thing to hide.
kalyāṇa-vatta — a “good practice” or noble custom, passed from one to the next. Maghadeva founds one; the Buddha founds a far greater one in the eightfold path.
brahmavihāra — the four “divine abidings”: love, compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity, radiated boundlessly to all beings. The renouncing kings’ practice — which leads to a heavenly rebirth.
antima-purisa — the “final man”: the one in whose hands a noble inheritance dies, simply by failing to carry it on. Kaḷāra Janaka was the final man of the old line.
heaven is not freedom — the discourse’s pivot: the old practice reaches “only as far as” a heavenly rebirth, still within the round; the eightfold path reaches awakening, the way beyond.
Maghadeva = the Bodhisatta — the Buddha reveals he was the founding king — a statement about the long arc of his development, from founding a heaven-bound custom to founding the path to the deathless.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The founding by Maghadeva, the Nimi episode, and the closing revelation are given in full; the recurring lifespans and the repetition across the lineage are condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The smile, and the founding king

§1–5So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Mithilā in the Maghadeva Mango Grove. He smiled at a certain spot, and Ānanda asked the reason. “Once, Ānanda, right here in Mithilā there was a just and principled king named Maghadeva, who kept the observance days. He told his barber, ‘When you see grey hairs on my head, tell me.’ When at last the barber found them, he said, ‘The messengers of the gods have shown themselves.’ The king had the grey hair plucked, gave the barber a prize village, and summoned the crown prince: ‘The messengers of the gods have shown themselves. I have enjoyed human pleasures; now it is time to seek the heavenly. You rule the realm; I shall go forth. And when grey hairs show on your head, do the same. Keep up this good practice I have founded — do not be my final man.’ And Maghadeva went forth in this very grove.”

The divine abidings, and the lineage

§5–11“There he meditated spreading a heart full of love to all directions; and so too compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity — boundless, free of enmity. For 84,000 years he played as a child, 84,000 as viceroy, 84,000 as king, and 84,000 in the holy life; and having developed the four divine abidings, at death he was reborn in a realm of the gods. His son did likewise, and his son’s son — a lineage of 84,000 kings, each going forth at the first grey hair, each cultivating the divine abidings, each reborn among the gods.”

King Nimi chooses duty over heaven

§12–16“The last of them was King Nimi, just and devout. So admired was he that the gods of the Thirty-Three, gathered in their hall, sang his praises; and Sakka sent his charioteer Mātali with a thousand-horse heavenly chariot to fetch him. ‘Which way shall we go,’ asked Mātali, ‘the road of those who suffer for bad deeds, or of those who enjoy good ones?’ ‘Take me both ways.’ At the heavenly hall Sakka welcomed him: ‘Stay, great king, and enjoy heavenly glory among the gods!’ But Nimi replied, ‘Enough — send me back to Mithilā, that I may rule my people justly and keep the observance days.’ So Sakka sent him home; and there Nimi ruled justly, and in time went forth at the first grey hair, like the kings before him, and was reborn among the gods.”

The final man

§20“But King Nimi had a son named Kaḷāra Janaka. He did not go forth from the lay life to homelessness. He broke that good practice. He was their final man.”

“I was Maghadeva” — and the better path

§21“Ānanda, you might think Maghadeva was someone else. But you should not: I myself was King Maghadeva. I founded that good practice, kept up by those who came after. But that good practice does not lead to disillusionment, dispassion, cessation, peace, insight, awakening, and extinguishment. It leads only as far as rebirth in a realm of the gods. But now I have founded a good practice that does lead to all these — and what is it? Just this noble eightfold path: right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion. Ānanda, I say to you: keep up this good practice I have founded. Do not be my final men.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Ānanda approved what the Buddha said.

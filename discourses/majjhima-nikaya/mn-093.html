Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 93

With Assalāyana

Assalāyanasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speakers
The Buddha and Assalāyana, a sixteen-year-old brahmin prodigy sent to defend caste
Form
A debate the Buddha wins step by step — a chain of arguments against caste-supremacy, crowned by the story of the seven seers who could not prove their own lineage.
Length
~14 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 151 (Madhyama-āgama 151)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the canon’s fullest dismantling of caste, argued in the Buddha’s own voice.

Why this discourse, ninety-third

Five hundred brahmins, alarmed that the Buddha teaches purification is open to all four classes, look for a champion to debate him — and settle on a sixteen-year-old prodigy, Assalāyana, who has already mastered the whole Vedic curriculum. He goes reluctantly (“speakers of principle are hard to debate”), states the proud brahmin claim, and is taken apart, argument by patient argument, until he has unwittingly conceded the Buddha’s own position. It is the most thorough refutation of caste in the canon, spoken in the Buddha’s own voice.

The claim Assalāyana brings is the familiar one: “Only brahmins are the best class; only brahmins are pure; only brahmins are the true-born sons of Brahmā, born from his mouth.” What follows is a masterclass in dismantling a prejudice. The Buddha never raises his voice or appeals to his own authority; he simply asks question after question drawn from things Assalāyana already knows, and lets each answer chip away at the doctrine. After every blow, the young man can only repeat, like a refrain, “Even though you say this, still the brahmins maintain their belief” — the honest admission of a man who has run out of arguments but not out of inherited conviction.

The arguments march across every ground on which caste might rest — biology, geography, the moral law, spiritual capacity, even the brahmins’ own practice — and each one falls. And the discourse’s climax is a story within the story: the ancient seer Devala the Dark, grilling seven proud brahmin seers on their genealogy until they must admit they cannot even be sure what they are. The whole towering edifice of birth-purity rests, it turns out, on a foundation no one can actually verify.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The claim that brahmins are inherently the highest and purest class fails on every ground — biology, geography, the law of karma, spiritual capacity, and the brahmins’ own practice of honouring learning and ethics over birth — and finally collapses entirely, since no one can even verify their own lineage; purity is a matter of conduct, open to all four classes alike.

The chain of arguments

The Buddha’s refutation is a sequence of distinct attacks, each striking a different pillar of the doctrine. Taken together they leave nothing standing:

The argumentWhat it shows
BirthBrahmin women menstruate, conceive, give birth, and nurse like all women — brahmins come from a womb, not from Brahmā’s mouth.
GeographyIn foreign lands (Greece, Persia) there are only two classes, and one may pass from master to servant and back — caste is no universal law of nature.
Bad & good deedsAnyone of any class who does evil goes to a bad rebirth, and anyone who does good to a heaven — karma is caste-blind.
Loving-kindnessAnyone of any class can develop a heart of love free of enmity — the spiritual capacities belong to no caste.
BathingAnyone of any class can wash off dirt at the river — even ritual purity is not a brahmin monopoly.
The fireFire kindled by high-class men with fine wood, and by low-class men with poor wood, burns with the same flame, colour, and light — caste changes nothing about what one produces.
InterbreedingA child of an aristocrat and a brahmin can be called either — unlike a mule (a true crossbreed), caste leaves no biological mark; humans are one kind.

After each, the Buddha asks the same quiet question — “Then what is the source of the brahmins’ certainty in making this claim?” — and Assalāyana, unable to answer, can only fall back on the refrain: “still the brahmins maintain their belief.” The doctrine is shown to be not a truth but a tenacious habit of mind, held against the evidence.

The brahmins’ own practice betrays the doctrine

The cleverest stroke comes near the end, when the Buddha turns the brahmins’ own behaviour against their theory. Suppose two brothers from one womb, he asks — one a learned reciter, the other not: whom do the brahmins feed first at a sacred meal? “The learned one,” Assalāyana answers, “for how could an offering to the unlearned be fruitful?” So worth tracks education, not mere birth — and Assalāyana has just said so. Then the Buddha sharpens it: two brothers, one a learned reciter but immoral, the other unlearned but ethical — whom do they feed first? “The ethical one,” comes the answer, “for how could an offering to the immoral be fruitful?” So worth tracks conduct, above even learning. And with that, the Buddha names the trap Assalāyana has walked into: “First you relied on birth; then you switched to education; then you switched to ethical behaviour. Now you have come around to believing in purification for the four classes — just as I do.” The young prodigy has been led, by his own honest answers, to the Buddha’s exact position. He sits silent, “shoulders drooping, with nothing to say.” It is the kindest possible defeat: he is not crushed by superior cleverness, but shown that he already believes what he came to refute.

The seven seers who could not name themselves

To seal it, the Buddha tells a story. Seven brahmin seers, living in the wilderness, held exactly Assalāyana’s view — only brahmins are pure, Brahmā’s mouth-born sons. The seer Devala the Dark came to test them; when they tried to curse him to ashes, the curse rebounded — the more they cursed, the more handsome he grew — proving their vaunted spiritual power empty against a true sage. Then Devala questioned them on the one thing their whole claim depended on: their bloodline. Do you know your mother had relations only with a brahmin? Back seven generations? Your father? Do you know how an embryo is conceived — and whether the being that enters the womb is an aristocrat, a brahmin, a peasant, or a menial? To each, they had to answer: “We do not know.” “Then,” said Devala, “you do not even know what you are.” The point is devastating and simple. Caste-purity claims to rest on unbroken descent — but no one can actually trace or guarantee such descent, and the consciousness that takes rebirth carries no caste-label at all. The whole hierarchy stands on a genealogy no one can verify. “If even those seven seers could not prevail when grilled by Devala,” the Buddha concludes, “how could you?” And Assalāyana goes for refuge.

What the discourse establishes

Read whole, MN 93 is the Buddha’s definitive answer to the deepest social assumption of his world: that some human beings are, by birth, intrinsically superior to others. He answers it not with a competing dogma but with a battery of plain observations anyone can check — and the cumulative effect is overwhelming. Caste is not biology (we all come from wombs; we interbreed as one species). It is not natural law (other lands order themselves differently). It is not the moral order (karma ignores it). It is not spiritual capacity (love and purity are open to all). It is not even consistently held by the brahmins themselves (who honour learning and virtue above birth). And it cannot be verified (no lineage is provably pure). What remains, when caste is cleared away, is the Buddha’s steady teaching: that a person is purified by what they cultivate and do, and that the path to the highest goal is open, on identical terms, to everyone born. In an age — ancient or modern — still tempted to rank human beings by the accident of their birth, the discourse remains one of the most bracing things ever said.

Key terms

cātuvaṇṇa-suddhi — “purification for the four classes,” the Buddha’s teaching that the path is open to all castes alike. The brahmins gathered a champion precisely to attack it.
“still the brahmins maintain their belief” — Assalāyana’s refrain after each refutation: the honest admission of one who has run out of arguments but not out of inherited conviction. A doctrine held against the evidence.
the fire simile — fire from fine wood and from poor wood burns alike. Caste, like the wood, changes nothing about the flame — the quality of what a person produces is not set by birth.
birth → learning → conduct — the trap: by his own answers (whom brahmins feed first), Assalāyana shifts the basis of worth from birth, to education, to ethics — arriving at the Buddha’s own view.
Devala & the seven seers — the story-within: their curse rebounds, and they cannot answer Devala’s questions on their own lineage — “you do not even know what you are.” Caste-purity rests on an unverifiable genealogy.
the unknown rebirth-being — no one knows the “caste” of the consciousness that enters the womb. The claim of pure descent has, at its very root, a gap no one can close.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The chain of arguments, the two-brothers test, and the story of Devala are given; the repeated refrain and parallel cases are condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A champion is found

§1–5So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī. Five hundred visiting brahmins, troubled that the Buddha “advocates purification for all four classes,” sought a champion, and settled on the sixteen-year-old prodigy Assalāyana, a master of the Vedas. He went reluctantly — “speakers of principle are hard to debate” — but went, and put the brahmin claim: “Only brahmins are the best, the pure, the true-born sons of Brahmā, born from his mouth. What do you say?”

The chain of refutations

§6–11“But Assalāyana, brahmin women are seen menstruating, conceiving, giving birth, and nursing — yet they claim to be born from Brahmā’s mouth?” Argument by argument the Buddha presses, and to each Assalāyana can only reply, “Still the brahmins maintain their belief.” In foreign lands there are but two classes, and one may pass between them. Any of the four classes who does evil goes to hell, and any who does good to heaven — equally. Any can develop a heart of love; any can wash at the river. And fire kindled by high-born men with fine wood, and by low-born men with a dog’s trough for a drill-stick, has the same flame, colour, and light. “Then what is the source of the brahmins’ certainty?”

The trap of the brahmins’ own practice

§12–17“A child of an aristocrat father and brahmin mother — aristocrat or brahmin?” “Either.” “A mule, born of a mare and a donkey?” “A mule — a crossbreed; here I see a difference, but not in the human case.” “Two brothers from one womb, one a learned reciter, one not — whom do the brahmins feed first?” “The learned one.” “And if the learned one is immoral and the unlearned one ethical?” “The ethical one.” “So, Assalāyana: first you relied on birth, then on education, then on conduct — and now you have come around to purification for the four classes, just as I teach.” Assalāyana sat silent, shoulders drooping, with nothing to say.

The seven seers

§18“Once, seven brahmin seers in the wilderness held your very view. The seer Devala the Dark came to test them. When they tried to curse him to ashes, the more they cursed, the lovelier he grew — ‘our fervour is in vain!’ Then Devala asked them: ‘Do you know your mother had relations only with a brahmin? Back seven generations? Your father? Do you know how an embryo is conceived, and whether the being that enters the womb is aristocrat, brahmin, peasant, or menial?’ To each they answered, ‘We do not know.’ ‘Then you do not even know what you are.’ If even those seven seers could not prevail when grilled on their genealogy, Assalāyana — how could you?”

Refuge

§19“Excellent, worthy Gotama! From this day forth, may the worthy Gotama remember me as a lay follower gone for refuge for life.”

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 89

Shrines to the Teaching

Dhammacetiyasutta

Setting
The land of the Sakyans, at the town of Medaḷumpa — a late meeting, both men eighty years old
Speakers
The Buddha and King Pasenadi of Kosala
Form
A king’s testimony — reasons, not raptures: seven plain observations of the community from which Pasenadi infers that the Buddha is truly awakened.
Length
~13 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 213 (Madhyama-āgama 213)
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — warm and clear; a model of faith grounded in evidence, not feeling.

Why this discourse, eighty-ninth

An old king, walking in a park, sees a quiet grove of trees and thinks of the Buddha. He learns the Buddha is only a few leagues away, and goes — and when he reaches the door, he lays down his sword and crown, enters, and kisses the Buddha’s feet. Asked why a king shows such devotion to “this body,” he gives not a gush of feeling but a list of reasons — seven clear-eyed observations of the Buddha’s community from which he has reasoned his faith. The Buddha calls them “monuments to the Dhamma,” and tells the monks to learn them by heart.

The frame is poignant. King Pasenadi and the Buddha are now both eighty; this is among their last meetings. At the Buddha’s dwelling, the king hands his sword and royal turban to his general Dīgha Kārāyana — setting aside the very emblems of his power to come as a humble visitor — and inside, with extraordinary tenderness, he caresses and kisses the Buddha’s feet, naming himself aloud: “I am Pasenadi, king of Kosala!” The Buddha’s gentle question — why such devotion to this body? — invites the king to make his reasons explicit.

What follows is one of the canon’s finest pictures of reasoned faith. Pasenadi does not say “I simply believe.” He says, in effect, “I infer the Buddha’s awakening from what I can see” — and then lays out the visible evidence: the lifelong purity of the monks, their harmony, their unforced joy, their discipline without coercion, the way even hostile debaters are won over, the devotion of those who depend on the king yet revere the Buddha more. Each observation is a “monument” — a standing testimony to the truth of the teaching, read off the quality of the lives it produces.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A king’s devotion to the Buddha is grounded not in feeling but in evidence — in seven plain observations of the community the Buddha has formed: its members’ lifelong purity, harmony, unforced joy, self-discipline without coercion, the winning-over of even hostile critics, and a loyalty that outshines a king’s patronage — from all of which Pasenadi reasons that the Buddha is truly awakened, the teaching well explained, the Saṅgha practising well.

Faith you can reason to

The discourse’s signature phrase is the king’s refrain: “I infer this about the Buddha from the teaching — the Buddha is fully awakened, the teaching is well explained, the Saṅgha is practising well.” This is faith as inference, not as leap. Pasenadi cannot see the Buddha’s awakened mind directly; but he can see the lives that mind has shaped, and from those visible effects he reasons back to their invisible cause. The community is the evidence; the awakening is the conclusion. This is a profoundly important model of how a thoughtful person comes to trust a teacher: not by being told to believe, nor by a wave of emotion, but by watching, over years, the kind of human beings a teaching actually produces — and finding the fruit so good that the root must be sound. The Buddha himself endorses this: he calls the king’s words “monuments to the Dhamma” worth memorising.

The seven monuments

Each “monument” sets the Buddha’s community against the ordinary world or against rival ascetics, and reads its superiority as a sign:

The monumentWhat Pasenadi sees
1. Lifelong purityOther ascetics practise for a while, then return to luxury; the Buddha’s monks keep the holy life pure “to their last breath.”
2. HarmonyThe world is full of conflict — kings, families, friends at war; the monks live “blending like milk and water, regarding each other with kindly eyes.”
3. Unforced joyOther ascetics are “thin, haggard, pale” (dissatisfied, or hiding wrongdoing); the monks are “smiling, joyful… their hearts free as a wild deer.”
4. Discipline without coercionEven a king cannot stop people interrupting his court; the Buddha teaches hundreds in a silence so deep that one monk nudges another quiet — “trained without rod or sword.”
5. The undefeated teacherClever critics come sharpening questions to refute him; after one Dhamma talk they never even ask — they become disciples, and some awaken.
6. Loyalty beyond patronageThe king’s own officials, who owe him their living, revere the Buddha more — lying down with heads toward the Buddha, feet toward the king.
7. Shared kinshipThe Buddha and the king are both aristocrats, both Kosalan, both eighty — so deference and friendship are fitting.

Read together, they form a portrait of what a genuine spiritual community looks like from the outside — and a quiet checklist for judging any teacher by the company they keep.

“Without rod or sword” — the king’s own helplessness

The fourth monument is the most striking, because the king measures the Saṅgha against himself and finds himself wanting. He holds the power of life and death — “I can execute, fine, or banish” — and yet, sitting in his own court, he cannot get people to stop interrupting him until he has finished speaking. The Buddha, with no power to punish anyone, commands a silence so complete among hundreds that a single throat-clearing draws a gentle nudge to hush. “How incredible,” the king marvels, “that an assembly can be so well trained without rod or sword!” The phrase echoes Aṅgulimāla’s (MN 86): the deepest order is not imposed by force but arises freely from within, where each person disciplines themselves out of reverence and understanding. A king’s coercion cannot buy what the Dhamma’s authority gives freely. It is a humbling admission from the most powerful man in the land.

The joy that gives it away

The third monument deserves a second look, because it overturns a common assumption about spiritual seriousness. Pasenadi had supposed that religious people are gaunt and grim — and when he met sour, wasted ascetics, he reasoned they must be either secretly miserable or hiding some misdeed. But the Buddha’s monks are the opposite: relaxed, cheerful, openly happy, “their hearts free as a wild deer.” And from that he draws his inference: such ease can only mean they have “realised a higher distinction.” This quietly corrects a misconception as old as religion: that the holy life must be heavy, joyless, self-punishing. The genuine article, Pasenadi observes, looks like freedom and gladness, not gloom. A teaching is partly authenticated by the lightness it produces in those who live it.

The frame, and its shadow

The discourse is gentle, but its setting carries a tragic weight the text does not spell out. Pasenadi and the Buddha are old men near the end; this is nearly their last meeting. And the king’s act of handing his sword and turban to Dīgha Kārāyana, in order to enter the Buddha’s presence as a humble man, would — the tradition recounts — give Kārāyana the opening to seize the regalia and install Pasenadi’s son Viḍūḍabha as king, leaving the old king to die, deposed, outside a city gate. So the very humility that is the discourse’s beauty — a king laying down his power to honour the Dhamma — becomes, in the larger story, the hinge of his downfall. Whatever one makes of that, the contrast is its own teaching: the kingdom Pasenadi loses is exactly the impermanent, conflict-ridden world his “monuments” describe, while the refuge he comes to honour is the one thing that cannot be seized by a general at a gate.

Monuments to remember

When the king leaves, the Buddha turns to the monks and gives the discourse its name: “King Pasenadi spoke monuments to the Dhamma (dhammacetiya). Learn them, memorise them, remember them — they are beneficial and relate to the fundamentals of the spiritual life.” A cetiya is a shrine or monument, a thing built to be remembered and revered; the king’s words are verbal monuments — testimonies that mark and preserve the truth of the teaching. That the Buddha asks the monks to memorise a layman’s praise is telling: this outsider’s clear-eyed account of what the community looks like from without is itself a teaching the insiders should keep. It reminds the monks, and us, what the practice is for and what it should produce — and offers anyone weighing a spiritual path a sane and practical test: look not at the teacher’s claims, but at the lives of those who follow.

Key terms

dhammacetiya — “monuments / shrines to the Dhamma.” The king’s words of testimony, which the Buddha asks the monks to memorise — verbal monuments marking and preserving the truth of the teaching.
inferential faith — the king’s refrain, “I infer this about the Buddha from the teaching.” Faith reasoned to from visible evidence: the community is what is seen; the awakening is the conclusion.
“like milk and water” — the image of the monks’ harmony: blended without friction, “regarding each other with kindly eyes” — set against a world at war with itself.
“hearts free as a wild deer” — the monks’ unforced joy and ease. The holy life, genuinely lived, looks like freedom and gladness, not gloom — a sign of real attainment.
“without rod or sword” — the Saṅgha’s discipline, self-arising from reverence and understanding. A king’s coercion cannot buy the order the Dhamma gives freely.
Isidatta & Purāṇa — the king’s chamberlains who owe him their living yet revere the Buddha more — lying with heads toward the Buddha, feet toward the king. Loyalty that outshines patronage.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The frame and the seven monuments are given in full; the parallel lists of clever critics and the repeated refrain are condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The king lays down his sword

§1–9So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying among the Sakyans, near the town of Medaḷumpa. King Pasenadi, walking in a park, saw quiet groves that reminded him of the Buddha, and learned he was three leagues away. He drove there, was directed to the Buddha’s dwelling, and — handing his sword and turban to his general Dīgha Kārāyana — cleared his throat and knocked. The Buddha opened the door. The king entered, bowed, caressed and kissed the Buddha’s feet, and named himself: “Sir, I am Pasenadi, king of Kosala!” “But great king, for what reason do you show such utmost devotion to this body?”

The monuments to the Dhamma

§10–12“Sir, I infer this about the Buddha from the teaching: ‘The Buddha is fully awakened, the teaching well explained, the Saṅgha practising well.’ I see ascetics who lead the holy life only ten, twenty, thirty, or forty years, then return to bathing, anointing, and sensual pleasures; but here the monks keep it full and pure to their last breath. And while the world fights — kings, families, friends — the monks live in harmony, ‘blending like milk and water, regarding each other with kindly eyes.’ And while other ascetics are thin and haggard — ‘dissatisfied, or hiding some misdeed’ — the monks are smiling, joyful, relaxed, ‘their hearts free as a wild deer.’”

§13–18“As a king I can execute, fine, or banish — yet in my own court I cannot stop people interrupting me. But the Buddha teaches hundreds in such silence that when one monk cleared his throat, another nudged him to hush. How incredible, that an assembly is so well trained without rod or sword! Clever critics come to refute him — aristocrats, brahmins, householders, ascetics — yet after his Dhamma talk they never even ask their question; they become disciples, and some go forth and awaken, saying, ‘We were almost lost, claiming to be perfected when we were not — now we truly are!’ And my own chamberlains Isidatta and Purāṇa, who owe me their livelihood, revere the Buddha more than me — once they lay down with heads toward the Buddha and feet toward me.”

Kinship, and farewell

§19–20“And the Buddha is an aristocrat, and so am I; the Buddha is Kosalan, and so am I; the Buddha is eighty, and so am I. So it is fitting that I show such deference and friendship. Well, now, sir, I must go; I have much to do.” “Please, great king, go at your convenience.” And the king rose, bowed, and departed.

Learn these monuments

§21Soon after he left, the Buddha said to the monks: “Mendicants, King Pasenadi spoke monuments to the Dhamma, then got up and left. Learn these monuments to the Dhamma! Memorise them! Remember them! They are beneficial and relate to the fundamentals of the spiritual life.”

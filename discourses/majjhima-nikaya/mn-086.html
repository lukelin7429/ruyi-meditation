Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 86

With Aṅgulimāla

Aṅgulimālasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove; the road into the forest where the bandit lay in wait
Speakers
The Buddha, the bandit-turned-monk Aṅgulimāla, and King Pasenadi of Kosala
Form
The canon’s most dramatic conversion — a serial killer turned by a single word, ordained, awakened, and reconciled to the fruit of his deeds.
Length
~14 minutes to read
Northern parallel
SĀ 1077 (Saṁyukta-āgama 1077)
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a gripping narrative; its depths are in “stopping,” the act of truth, and the working of kamma.

Why this discourse, eighty-sixth

A man has murdered so many that he wears their fingers as a necklace; whole districts are emptied by terror of him. The Buddha walks down his road alone and unarmed — and turns him, not with force, but with a single sentence about what it means to “stop.” The killer throws away his weapons, is ordained on the spot, and becomes an arahant. It is the canon’s starkest proof that no one is beyond redemption, and that the deepest taming is done without rod or sword.

Aṅgulimāla — the name means “finger-garland” — is “violent, bloody-handed, a hardened killer, merciless,” who has “laid waste to villages, towns, and countries.” People dare the road through his forest only in armed bands of fifty, and still die by his hand. When the Buddha sets out down that very road, herders and farmers beg him to turn back. He goes on in silence — for he has seen that this monster is ripe for awakening, and that no one else can reach him.

What follows is told with unusual economy and force: the eerie chase in which the killer, sword drawn, cannot catch a Buddha walking at an ordinary pace; the exchange about “stopping” that breaks him open; the instant going-forth; King Pasenadi arriving with five hundred horse to hunt a man who is now sitting peacefully among the monks; the trembling new monk sent to bless a woman in agonising labour by an “act of truth”; and the stones and sticks that rain on the former murderer even after his awakening — the long arm of his own deeds, which he bears at last like “the moon freed from clouds.”

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

No one is beyond redemption — the Buddha tames even a mass murderer not with force but with a word about “stopping” (he has stopped all harming forever; the killer cannot stop himself); the man is reborn into “the noble birth,” becomes an arahant, and though he still reaps the fruit of his terrible deeds, he bears it free and at peace, “like the moon freed from clouds.”

“I have stopped”

The hinge of the conversion is a single, deceptively simple exchange. The Buddha walks at a normal pace, yet the sprinting, sword-drawn killer cannot close the distance — an outward sign of an inward truth. Baffled, Aṅgulimāla shouts, “Stop, ascetic!” And the Buddha replies, “I have stopped, Aṅgulimāla — now you stop.” The killer, who prides himself that the Sakyan ascetics never lie, is snagged on the paradox: how can a man still walking say he has stopped, while the one standing still is told he hasn’t? The Buddha unfolds the double meaning of the word: “I have stopped forever — I have laid aside all violence toward living beings. But you cannot stop yourself from harming. That is why I have stopped, and you have not.” True stillness is not of the feet but of the heart: it is the ceasing of the will to harm. The murderer, who has never in his life been “still” in that sense, hears his whole condition named in one stroke — and breaks. He flings his weapons off a cliff and asks for the going-forth, and the Buddha says simply, “Come, monk.”

Taming without rod or sword

The contrast with worldly power is drawn deliberately. King Pasenadi rides out with five hundred horse, armed to destroy the bandit — only to find him sitting, robed and serene, among the Buddha’s monks. The Buddha sets the king a question: if Aṅgulimāla had gone forth and become virtuous, what would you do? “I would honour and protect him — but how could such an evil man ever become so good?” Then the Buddha points: “This is Aṅgulimāla.” The king’s hair stands on end with fear; reassured, he approaches the man he came to kill, and ends up offering him robes and alms. His own verdict is the moral: “What I could not tame with rod and sword, the Buddha tamed without rod or sword.” Aṅgulimāla’s own verse echoes it: “Some tame by the rod, some by goads and whips — but the unaffected one tamed me without rod or sword.” The deepest transformation cannot be coerced; it is won by wisdom and compassion meeting a person at the exact point of their need.

The act of truth, and a new birth

Now a monk, Aṅgulimāla comes upon a woman in obstructed, agonising labour, and compassion stirs in the man who once felt none: “Oh, how beings suffer!” The Buddha sends him to perform an “act of truth” (saccakiriya) — a blessing made potent by the speaking of a true statement: “Sister, since I was born, I do not recall having intentionally killed a living being — by this truth, may you and your child be safe.” Aṅgulimāla balks: that would be a lie, for he has killed many. So the Buddha gives him the words that make it true: “Sister, since I was born in the noble birth…” — that is, since his spiritual rebirth as a disciple. And he speaks it, and the woman and her baby are safe. The amendment is the discourse’s tender heart. A person’s past need not define their present; in going forth, Aṅgulimāla was, in a real sense, born anew, and from that birth he has harmed no one. The blessing he speaks is still recited today over women in labour — words first uttered by the most unlikely man in the canon to bless a life.

Kamma is not escaped — but it is transformed

Aṅgulimāla soon becomes an arahant. But the discourse refuses a cheap redemption. Going for alms, he is met with stones, sticks, and gravel; he returns to the Buddha with a cracked, bleeding head, a broken bowl, a torn robe. The Buddha does not say his past is erased. He says: “Endure it, brahmin! You are experiencing in this very life the result of deeds that might have tormented you in hell for many thousands of years.” This is the discourse’s sober counterweight to its mercy. Awakening does not cancel kamma; the deeds were real and their fruit is real. But it transforms how that fruit is met. What could have been aeons of hell is compressed into bruises borne by a free mind — and Aṅgulimāla receives them without resentment, as a debt being paid: “The result of my deeds has already struck me, so I enjoy my food free of debt.” Redemption here is not the undoing of the past but the freedom to bear its consequences without adding to them — to receive the blows of one’s old life with the equanimity of one who has truly stopped.

The moon freed from clouds

The discourse closes with Aṅgulimāla’s verses of liberation, among the most moving in the canon. The governing image is light returning: “He who once was heedless but turned to heedfulness lights up the world, like the moon freed from clouds.” He reflects on his name — his birth-name was Ahiṁsaka, “Harmless,” a name once made a grim joke by his deeds, and now true again: “My name is ‘Harmless,’ though I used to be harmful… for I do no harm to anyone.” And his goodwill reaches even those who hate him: “May even my enemies hear the Dhamma! May even my enemies devote themselves to the Buddha’s teaching!” The man who took countless lives now wishes nothing but the Dhamma upon those who throw stones at him. “I used to have blood on my hands… see the refuge I have found — the leash to existence is cut. I have attained the three knowledges, and fulfilled the Buddha’s instructions.” That the cruellest figure in the discourses ends as a serene arahant radiating loving-kindness is the whole teaching in one life: the door is never shut, the cloud is never the moon, and even the worst of us can be tamed — not by punishment, but by being shown the way to stop.

Key terms

“I have stopped” — the Buddha’s pivotal word (ṭhita): not stopped of foot, but stopped of harm. “I have laid aside all violence toward living beings” — true stillness is the ceasing of the will to harm.
“Come, monk” — ehi bhikkhu, the Buddha’s direct ordination of one fully ripe. The killer is made a monk in a single word, on the spot.
saccakiriya — an “act of truth”: a blessing made potent by speaking a true statement. Aṅgulimāla’s blessing for safe childbirth is still recited today.
ariyāya jātiyā — “the noble birth.” The amendment that makes the blessing true: since his spiritual rebirth as a disciple, Aṅgulimāla has harmed no one. The past need not define the present.
tamed without rod or sword — King Pasenadi’s verdict and Aṅgulimāla’s own: what force could not do, the Dhamma did. The deepest transformation cannot be coerced.
“free of debt” — kamma is not escaped but transformed. The arahant still reaps his deeds (the stoning), but bears them with equanimity — aeons of hell compressed into bruises borne by a free mind.

The text

The full discourse, lightly abridged where the source repeats. The conversion, the meeting with King Pasenadi, the act of truth, the stoning, and the closing verses are given in full. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The Buddha walks toward the bandit

§1–4So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī. In King Pasenadi’s realm there was a bandit named Aṅgulimāla — violent, bloody-handed, a hardened killer, who wore his victims’ fingers as a necklace. After his meal the Buddha walked the road that led to him. Herders and farmers warned him: “Don’t take this road, ascetic! People travel it only in bands of fifty, and still die by his hand.” But the Buddha went on in silence.

“Stop”

§4–6Aṅgulimāla saw him coming and marvelled that this ascetic walked alone, “like a conqueror.” He took up sword and bow and gave chase — but the Buddha, by psychic power, walked at a normal pace and yet could not be overtaken, though Aṅgulimāla ran his hardest. Astonished, he cried, “Stop, stop, ascetic!” “I have stopped, Aṅgulimāla — now you stop.” And he asked in verse, “While walking you say ‘I’ve stopped’; I have stopped, yet you say I have not. How is it you have stopped and I have not?” “Aṅgulimāla, I have stopped forever — I have laid aside violence toward all creatures. But you cannot stop yourself from harming living things; that is why I have stopped, and you have not.” At these words the bandit cried, “At long last a great seer has come into this wood! Now that I have heard your verse, I shall live without evil” — and he hurled his weapons into an abyss, bowed at the Buddha’s feet, and asked for the going-forth. “Come, monk,” said the Buddha. And so he became a monk.

King Pasenadi comes to hunt him

§8–13King Pasenadi rode out with five hundred horse to destroy the bandit, and came to the Buddha. “Suppose, great king, Aṅgulimāla had gone forth, ethical and restrained — what would you do?” “I would honour and protect him — but how could such an evil man ever become so virtuous?” The Buddha pointed: “Great king, this is Aṅgulimāla.” The king’s hair stood on end with terror; reassured, he approached, learned the monk’s clan, and offered him robes and alms — which Aṅgulimāla, a forest monk content with three robes, declined. “It is incredible, sir,” said the king, “how the Buddha tames the untamed without rod or sword — what I could not do with an army.”

The act of truth

§14–15On alms-round Aṅgulimāla saw a woman in agonising, obstructed labour, and was moved: “Oh, how beings suffer!” The Buddha told him to go and say, “Sister, since I was born I do not recall intentionally killing a living being — by this truth, may you and your baby be safe.” “But sir, that would be a lie — I have killed many.” “Then say: ‘since I was born in the noble birth.’” He did so — and the woman and her child were safe.

The fruit of old deeds

§16–17Then Aṅgulimāla, living alone, withdrawn, diligent, soon became one of the perfected. But going for alms, he was struck by thrown stones, sticks, and gravel, and came to the Buddha with cracked head, broken bowl, and torn robe. “Endure it, brahmin! Endure it! You are experiencing in this very life the result of deeds that might have tormented you in hell for many thousands of years.”

The moon freed from clouds

§18Later, experiencing the bliss of release, Aṅgulimāla uttered these verses:

“He who once was heedless, but turned to heedfulness, lights up the world, like the moon freed from clouds… Some tame by the rod, some with goads and whips; but the unaffected one tamed me without rod or sword. My name is ‘Harmless,’ though I used to be harmful; the name I bear today is true, for I do no harm to anyone… May even my enemies hear the Dhamma! May even my enemies devote themselves to the Buddha’s teaching!… I used to have blood on my hands, the notorious Aṅgulimāla; see the refuge I have found — the leash to existence is cut. The result of my deeds has already struck me, so I enjoy my food free of debt… I have attained the three knowledges, and fulfilled the Buddha’s instructions.”

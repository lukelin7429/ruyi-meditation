Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 50

The Rebuke of Māra

Māratajjanīyasutta

Setting
The land of the Bhaggas at Crocodile’s Bellow (Suṃsumāragira), in the deer park at Bhesakaḷā’s Wood
Speaker
Venerable Mahāmoggallāna — the only Majjhima discourse spoken throughout by a disciple, not the Buddha
Form
A confrontation and an autobiographical warning — Māra possesses Moggallāna’s body; the great disciple expels him and recounts a past life in which he was himself the Māra Dūsī, who attacked a past Buddha’s disciples and fell into hell for it.
Length
~16 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 131 (Madhyama-āgama 131, “Subduing Māra”); also T 66 and T 67; Moggallāna’s verses parallel Thāg 20.1
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — narratively rich and mythic, but its core teachings are clear and practical: how to guard the mind against both hostility and flattery.

Why this discourse, fiftieth

This is the mirror image of MN 49. There the Buddha rebuked Māra in the heaven of Brahmā; here his foremost disciple in psychic power, Mahāmoggallāna, rebukes Māra on earth — and does it by telling a story only he could tell: that he himself, countless eons ago, was a Māra.

The discourse opens almost comically. Māra has slipped inside Moggallāna’s belly, and the monk feels heavy, “like I’ve just eaten a load of beans.” But it turns quickly serious. Moggallāna detects the intruder, names him, and then — to drive home why attacking the awakened is the worst of all gambles — recounts a past life as Māra Dūsī, who harassed the disciples of the previous Buddha Kakusandha. That earlier Māra tried abuse, then flattery, then finally violence; and for cracking the skull of the disciple Vidhura he was plunged, in that very instant, into the deepest hell, to roast for uncountable years.

Woven into the warning is a genuinely practical teaching: how a community guards its mind when it is under attack. Kakusandha met Māra’s two assaults — hostility and honor — with two complementary trainings: the boundless heart of love, compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity against the provocation to anger; and the contemplation of the body’s unattractiveness and of impermanence against the seduction of praise. The frame is mythic, but the lesson is for any practitioner: praise and blame are both Māra’s tools, and both are disarmed the same way — by a mind that cannot be hooked.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

When Māra invades his body, Mahāmoggallāna expels him and warns him with the one story that proves the point — that he himself was once the Māra Dūsī, who attacked a past Buddha’s disciples and was cast into the deepest hell — so Māra should give up all hope of harming the Buddha or those who follow him.

A discourse spoken by a disciple

Almost every discourse in the Majjhima is spoken by the Buddha. MN 50 is the great exception: from beginning to end it is Mahāmoggallāna who speaks. He was the disciple the Buddha named foremost in psychic power (iddhi), and this discourse is, in a sense, the demonstration of why — not only because he can feel and expel a Māra hiding in his own gut, but because his vision reaches back across eons to his own former lives. The teaching carries a special authority precisely because the one delivering the warning has stood on both sides of it.

The structure of the discourse

SectionWhat happens
1. The intruderMāra slips into Moggallāna’s belly. The monk feels the heaviness, investigates, sees Māra, and tells him to come out.
2. Māra’s disbeliefMāra thinks no mere disciple — not even the Teacher — could detect him so fast. Moggallāna reads the thought and repeats it back. Māra retreats and stands by the door.
3. “You are my nephew”Moggallāna reveals that long ago he was the Māra Dūsī, whose sister Kāḷī was this Māra’s mother.
4. The past Buddha’s disciplesIn the time of the Buddha Kakusandha, the chief disciples were Vidhura (foremost in teaching) and Sañjīva (master of the cessation of perception and feeling, once mistakenly cremated).
5. Two tacticsMāra Dūsī incites people first to abuse the monks, then to venerate them — both to unsettle their minds. Kakusandha counters each with a training.
6. The breaking pointDūsī possesses a boy and cracks Vidhura’s skull with a rock. Kakusandha gazes back “like an elephant”; in that instant Dūsī falls into the Great Hell.
7. The versesMoggallāna recounts the torments of that hell and his own later feats of power, ending with the simile of fire: to attack the Realized One is to burn yourself.

The two tactics, and the two trainings that defeat them

The heart of the practical teaching is Māra Dūsī’s strategy and Kakusandha’s reply. Unable to find any “vulnerability” in the virtuous monks, Dūsī tries to manufacture one by stirring their feelings — first through hostility, then through honor:

Māra’s tacticKakusandha’s training
Abuse — townspeople possessed to insult and attack the monks, hoping to provoke angerThe four boundless states — radiating love, compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity to the whole world, “abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will”
Honor — townspeople possessed to praise and venerate the monks, hoping to provoke conceit and attachmentThe contemplations of non-attachment — the ugliness of the body, the repulsiveness of food, dissatisfaction with the whole world, and the impermanence of all conditions

The symmetry is the lesson. We expect abuse to be the danger and praise to be the reward — but Māra uses both, because both can hook a mind that still grasps. Hostility tempts us to hatred; honor tempts us to pride and clinging. The antidotes are opposite in flavor but identical in aim: a heart so full of goodwill that insult finds nothing to inflame, and so clear about impermanence and the body’s nature that flattery finds nothing to swell.

Sañjīva and the cessation of perception and feeling

The cameo of Sañjīva is more than color. He could enter saññāvedayitanirodha, the “cessation of perception and feeling” — the deepest meditative attainment, in which the ordinary stream of mind stops entirely. So complete was his stillness that herders mistook him for dead and built a pyre on him; he emerged unharmed at dawn, earning the name “Sañjīva,” the Reviver. The point for the story is that such a practitioner is utterly beyond Māra’s reach: there is simply no opening in a mind that can rest in that depth. Māra’s frustration — “I don’t know the course of rebirth of these mendicants” — is the frustration of death itself before someone who has stepped outside its territory.

The cost of attacking the awakened

The climax is deliberately terrible. For the single act of cracking a disciple’s skull, Māra Dūsī does not merely accrue bad karma to ripen later — he falls into the Great Hell in that very instant, as Kakusandha simply turns to look. The hell’s three names, the iron spikes “each one uniquely painful,” the body in human form but with the head of a fish, the ten thousand years in its annex: all of it underlines a single warning, repeated as a refrain through the verses — “Dark One, if you attack a mendicant who directly knows this, a disciple of the Buddha, you’ll fall into suffering.” And Moggallāna can say it with absolute conviction, because the one who roasted there was himself.

The fire that does not intend to burn

The final simile reframes the whole drama in terms of natural law rather than divine punishment. “Though a fire doesn’t think, ‘I’ll burn the fool!’ still the fool who attacks the fire gets burnt.” The Buddha and his disciples bear Māra no ill will; they do not curse or retaliate. The harm Māra suffers is not a sentence handed down but the simple consequence of his own act, like a hand thrust into flame. This is the Buddhist understanding of karmic result at its cleanest: not a punishing God, but a fire that burns whoever grasps it. With that, Moggallāna tells Māra plainly: “Give up on the Buddha, Māra! And hold no hope for the mendicants!” — and Māra, downcast, vanishes.

The thread back to MN 49

One of Moggallāna’s verses recalls questioning a Brahmā who once believed himself permanent and eternal — and who has since admitted, “I don’t have that view that I had in the past… So how could I say today that I am permanent and eternal?” This is almost certainly the very god of MN 49, Baka Brahmā, now humbled and corrected. Read together, the two discourses close the first fifty of the Majjhima on a shared note: Māra and the eternalist god are both real powers, and both are surpassed — by the Buddha in the heavens, and by his disciple on the earth.

Key terms

Māra Dūsī — “Māra the Corrupter,” the Māra of a past eon — revealed here to have been a former life of Mahāmoggallāna himself. His downfall is the cautionary tale at the discourse’s center.
Kakusandha — the first of the four Buddhas of this present eon, long before Gotama. The events Moggallāna recounts took place during his dispensation, with his chief disciples Vidhura and Sañjīva.
saññāvedayitanirodha — the cessation of perception and feeling; the deepest meditative attainment, in which mental activity wholly stops. Sañjīva’s mastery of it left him untouched even by a funeral pyre.
brahmavihāra — the four “divine abidings”: love (mettā), compassion (karuṇā), rejoicing (muditā), and equanimity (upekkhā), radiated boundlessly. Kakusandha’s prescription against the provocation of abuse.
asubha & aniccasaññā — the perception of unattractiveness (of the body, of food) and the perception of impermanence. Kakusandha’s prescription against the seduction of honor and praise.
iddhi — psychic power. Moggallāna was the disciple foremost in iddhi; the verses recall his feats — shaking great mansions with his toe, touching the peak of Mount Meru, conversing with Sakka and Brahmā.

The text

The full discourse, given here complete. It is spoken throughout by Venerable Mahāmoggallāna. The closing portion is in verse, with the recurring refrain “Dark One, if you attack…” Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Māra inside the belly

§1So I have heard. At one time Venerable Mahāmoggallāna was staying in the land of the Bhaggas at Crocodile’s Bellow, in the deer park at Bhesakaḷā’s Wood.

§2At that time Moggallāna was walking mindfully in the open air. Now at that time Māra the Wicked had got inside Moggallāna’s belly. Moggallāna thought, “Why now is my belly so very heavy, like I’ve just eaten a load of beans?” Then he stepped down from the walking path, entered his dwelling, sat down on the seat spread out,

§3and investigated inside himself. He saw that Māra the Wicked had got inside his belly. So he said to Māra, “Come out, Wicked One, come out! Do not harass the Realized One or his disciple. Don’t create lasting harm and suffering for yourself!”

§4Then Māra thought, “This ascetic doesn’t really know me or see me when he tells me to come out. Not even the Teacher could recognize me so quickly, so how could a disciple?”

§5Then Moggallāna said to Māra, “I know you even when you’re like this, Wicked One. Do not think, ‘He doesn’t know me.’ You are Māra the Wicked. And you think, ‘This ascetic doesn’t really know me or see me when he tells me to come out. Not even the Teacher could recognize me so quickly, so how could a disciple?’”

§6Then Māra thought, “This ascetic really does know me and see me when he tells me to come out.” Then Māra came up out of Moggallāna’s mouth and stood against the door.

§7Moggallāna saw him there and said, “I see you even there, Wicked One. Do not think, ‘He doesn’t see me.’ That’s you, Wicked One, standing against the door.

“Once I was a Māra”

§8Once upon a time, Wicked One, I was a Māra named Dūsī, and I had a sister named Kāḷī. You were her son, which made you my nephew.

§9At that time Kakusandha, the Blessed One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha arose in the world. Kakusandha had a fine pair of chief disciples named Vidhura and Sañjīva. Of all the disciples of the Buddha Kakusandha, none were the equal of Venerable Vidhura in teaching Dhamma. And that’s how he came to be known as Vidhura.

§10But when Venerable Sañjīva had gone to a wilderness, or to the root of a tree, or to an empty hut, he easily attained the cessation of perception and feeling. Once upon a time, Sañjīva was sitting at the root of a certain tree having attained the cessation of perception and feeling. Some cowherds, shepherds, farmers, and passers-by saw him sitting there and said, ‘Oh lord, how incredible, how amazing! This ascetic passed away while seated. We should cremate him.’ They collected grass, wood, and cow-dung, heaped it all on Sañjīva’s body, set it on fire, and left.

§11Then, when the night had passed, Sañjīva emerged from that attainment, shook out his robes, and, since it was morning, he robed up and entered the village for alms. Those cowherds, shepherds, farmers, and passers-by saw him wandering for alms and said, ‘Oh lord, how incredible, how amazing! This ascetic passed away while seated, and now he has come back to life!’ And that’s how he came to be known as Sañjīva.

First tactic: abuse

§12Then it occurred to Māra Dūsī, ‘I don’t know the course of rebirth of these ethical mendicants of good character. Why don’t I take possession of these brahmins and householders and say, “Come, all of you, abuse, attack, harass, and trouble the ethical mendicants of good character. Hopefully by doing this we can upset their minds so that Māra Dūsī can find a vulnerability.”’ And that’s exactly what he did.

§13Then those brahmins and householders abused, attacked, harassed, and troubled the ethical mendicants of good character: ‘These shavelings, fake ascetics, primitives, black spawn from the feet of our kinsman, say, “We practice absorption meditation! We practice absorption meditation!” Shoulders drooping, downcast, and dopey, they meditate and concentrate and contemplate and ruminate.’

The abuse runs through a string of mocking similes: the monks are like an owl on a branch hunting a mouse, a jackal on a river-bank hunting a fish, a cat lurking by a drain, an unladen donkey by a dustbin — all “meditating and concentrating and contemplating and ruminating.” Most of the people who joined in this abuse, when they died, were reborn in a place of loss, a bad place, the underworld, hell.

§14Then Kakusandha the Blessed One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha, addressed the mendicants: ‘Mendicants, the brahmins and householders have been possessed by Māra Dūsī. He told them to abuse you in the hope of upsetting your minds so that he can find a vulnerability. Come, all of you mendicants, meditate spreading a heart full of love to one direction, and to the second, and to the third, and to the fourth. In the same way above, below, across, everywhere, all around, spread a heart full of love to the whole world—abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will. Meditate spreading a heart full of compassion … rejoicing … equanimity to the whole world—abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will.’

§15When those mendicants were instructed and advised by the Buddha Kakusandha in this way, they went to a wilderness, or to the root of a tree, or to an empty hut, where they meditated spreading a heart full of love … compassion … rejoicing … equanimity.

Second tactic: honor

§16Then it occurred to Māra Dūsī, ‘Even when I do this I don’t know the course of rebirth of these ethical mendicants of good character. Why don’t I take possession of these brahmins and householders and say, “Come, all of you, honor, respect, esteem, and venerate the ethical mendicants of good character. Hopefully by doing this we can upset their minds so that Māra Dūsī can find a vulnerability.”’

§17And that’s exactly what he did. Then those brahmins and householders honored, respected, esteemed, and venerated the ethical mendicants of good character. Most of the people who died at that time—when their body broke up, after death—were reborn in a good place, a heavenly realm.

§18Then Kakusandha the Blessed One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha, addressed the mendicants: ‘Mendicants, the brahmins and householders have been possessed by Māra Dūsī. He told them to venerate you in the hope of upsetting your minds so that he can find a vulnerability. Come, all you mendicants, meditate observing the ugliness of the body, perceiving the repulsiveness of food, perceiving dissatisfaction with the whole world, and observing the impermanence of all conditions.’

§19When those mendicants were instructed and advised by the Buddha Kakusandha in this way, they went to a wilderness, or to the root of a tree, or to an empty hut, where they meditated observing the ugliness of the body, perceiving the repulsiveness of food, perceiving dissatisfaction with the whole world, and observing the impermanence of all conditions.

The attack on Vidhura, and Dūsī’s fall

§20Then the Buddha Kakusandha robed up in the morning and, taking his bowl and robe, entered the village for alms with Venerable Vidhura as his second monk.

§21Then Māra Dūsī took possession of a certain boy, picked up a rock, and hit Vidhura on the head, cracking it open. Then Vidhura, with blood pouring from his cracked skull, still followed behind the Buddha Kakusandha. Then the Buddha Kakusandha turned to gaze back, the way that elephants do, saying, ‘This Māra Dūsī knows no limit.’ And as he was gazing, Māra Dūsī fell from that place and was reborn in the Great Hell.

§22Now that Great Hell is known by three names: ‘Related to the Six Fields of Contact’ and also ‘The Impaling With Spikes’ and also ‘Uniquely Painful’. Then the wardens of hell came to me and said, ‘When spike meets spike in your heart, you will know that you’ve been roasting in hell for a thousand years.’

§23I roasted for many years, many centuries, many millennia in that Great Hell. For ten thousand years I roasted in the annex of that Great Hell, experiencing the pain called ‘this is emergence’. My body was in human form, but I had the head of a fish.”

The verses of warning

§24What kind of hell was that,
where Dūsī was roasted
after attacking the disciple Vidhura
along with the brahmin Kakusandha?
There were 100 iron spikes,
each one uniquely painful.
That’s the kind of hell
where Dūsī was roasted…
Dark One, if you attack
a mendicant who directly knows this,
a disciple of the Buddha,
you’ll fall into suffering.

§25There are mansions that last an eon
standing in the middle of a lake.
Sapphire-colored, brilliant,
they sparkle and shine.
Dancing there are nymphs
shining in all different colors.
Dark One, if you attack
a mendicant who directly knows this,
a disciple of the Buddha,
you’ll fall into suffering.

§26I’m the one who, urged by the Buddha,
shook the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother
with his big toe
as the Saṅgha of mendicants watched.
Dark One, if you attack
a mendicant who directly knows this,
a disciple of the Buddha,
you’ll fall into suffering.

§27I’m the one who shook the Palace of Victory
with his big toe
owing to psychic power,
inspiring deities to awe.
Dark One, if you attack
a mendicant who directly knows this,
a disciple of the Buddha,
you’ll fall into suffering.

§28I’m the one who asked Sakka
in the Palace of Victory:
‘Vāsava, I hope you recall
the one who is freed through the ending of craving?’
And I’m the one to whom Sakka
admitted the truth when asked.
Dark One, if you attack
a mendicant who directly knows this,
a disciple of the Buddha,
you’ll fall into suffering.

§29I’m the one who asked the Divinity
in the Hall of Clear Right before the assembly:
‘Respectable sir, do you still have the same view
that you had in the past?
Or do you see the radiance
surpassing the realm of divinity?’
And I’m the one to whom the Divinity
truthfully admitted his progress:
‘Good fellow, I don’t have that view
that I had in the past.
I see the radiance
surpassing the realm of divinity.
So how could I say today
that I am permanent and eternal?’
Dark One, if you attack
a mendicant who directly knows this,
a disciple of the Buddha,
you’ll fall into suffering.

§30I’m the one who touched the peak of Mount Meru
using the power of meditative liberation.
I’ve visited the forests of the people
who dwell in the land east of Videha.
Dark One, if you attack
a mendicant who directly knows this,
a disciple of the Buddha,
you’ll fall into suffering.

§31Though a fire doesn’t think,
‘I’ll burn the fool!’
still the fool who attacks
the fire gets burnt.
In the same way, Māra,
in attacking the Realized One,
you’ll only burn yourself,
like a fool touching the flames.
Māra’s done a bad thing
in attacking the Realized One.
Wicked One, do you imagine that
your wickedness won’t bear fruit?
Your deeds heap up wickedness
that will last a long time, terminator!
Give up on the Buddha, Māra!
And hold no hope for the mendicants!”

That is how, in the Bhesakaḷā grove, the mendicant condemned Māra. That spirit, downcast, disappeared right there.

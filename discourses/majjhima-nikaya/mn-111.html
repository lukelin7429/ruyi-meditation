Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 111

One by One

Anupadasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, addressed to the mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha, praising Venerable Sāriputta
Form
A portrait of insight meditation at its summit — analysing each attainment factor by factor.
Length
~15 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No counterpart is known in the Chinese Āgamas
Difficulty
★★★★★ — the most technical portrait of insight in the collection.

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-eleventh

The Buddha praises his foremost disciple in wisdom — and then shows exactly what that wisdom did. For two weeks, Sāriputta climbed the whole ladder of meditative attainment, and at each rung he did something extraordinary: he turned insight back on the attainment itself, picking out every mental factor present — one by one — and watching each “come to be and flit away.” This is meditation observing meditation, the mind catching its own deepest states in the very act of arising and passing.

The discourse opens the twelfth chapter, the Anupadavagga, and gives it its name: anupada — “in succession,” “one after another,” “step by step.” Sāriputta’s practice was insight into phenomena one by one (anupada-dhamma-vipassanā): not a vague awareness of “being in jhāna,” but a precise, factor-by-factor seeing of what each state is actually made of. The Buddha lists the components — placing of mind, rapture, bliss, unification, contact, feeling, perception, intention, energy, mindfulness, equanimity, attention, and the rest — and describes how Sāriputta knew each “as it arose, as it remained, and as it went away.”

And to each, the same response: he met it “without getting involved or shying away — independent, untied, liberated, detached, with mind free of boundaries,” understanding “there is an escape beyond.” Rung by rung, through the four absorptions and the four formless states, the analysis holds — until, in the two highest attainments, where the mind is too subtle to dissect from within, he analyses them after emerging; and at the cessation of perception and feeling, the defilements end, and he understands at last: “there is no escape beyond.” The discourse closes with the Buddha’s highest tribute: Sāriputta is “the Buddha’s true-born son… heir to the teaching,” who “rightly keeps rolling the supreme Wheel of Dhamma.”

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Sāriputta, foremost in wisdom, practised “insight into phenomena one by one”: in each meditative attainment from the first absorption to the cessation of perception and feeling, he discerned every mental factor as it arose, remained, and vanished — “not having been, they come to be; having come to be, they vanish” — meeting each without attraction or aversion, until the defilements ended and there was “no escape beyond.”

The wisdom that is being shown

The Buddha opens with a cascade of praise: Sāriputta has “great wisdom, widespread wisdom, joyous wisdom, swift wisdom, sharp wisdom, penetrating wisdom.” But this is not flattery for its own sake — it is the heading for a demonstration. What follows is the Buddha showing, concretely, what such wisdom does when it meets the deepest states of meditation. The discourse is, in effect, a guided tour of the mind of the wisest disciple at work — and so a model held up for all.

Insight into phenomena one by one

The method, anupada-dhamma-vipassanā, is the discourse’s heart. In each attainment, Sāriputta does not simply rest in the state; he resolves it into its components. In the first absorption, for instance, he picks out the classic factors — placing of mind, keeping it connected, rapture, bliss, unification — and then a longer list that pervades every state: contact, feeling, perception, intention, mind, enthusiasm, decision, energy, mindfulness, equanimity, and attention. Each of these he sees individually, “one by one as they occur.”

Not a block, but a weave. What feels, from inside, like a single seamless state of absorption is seen by penetrating wisdom to be a composite — many distinct mental factors arising together. To see the jhāna this way is already to loosen its grip: a thing made of parts, each fleeting, cannot be the solid refuge it seemed.

The universal mark, and the response to it

Of every factor in every attainment, Sāriputta knows the same three moments — “as it arose, as it remained, and as it went away” — and draws the same conclusion, stated in one of the canon’s most exact descriptions of impermanence:

“So it seems that these phenomena, not having been, come to be; and having come to be, they flit away.” They were not, then they are, then they are gone. Even the rapture of the absorptions, even the vast peace of the formless realms, is caught in this flicker of arising and vanishing.

And the response to seeing this is the discourse’s most precious instruction. Sāriputta meditates on those phenomena “without getting involved or shying away — independent, untied, liberated, detached, with mind free of boundaries.” He neither grasps the pleasant factors nor recoils from their passing; he simply sees, free. And from this seeing comes the recurring recognition: “there is an escape beyond” (atthi uttari nissaraṇa) — this state, however high, is not the end; freedom lies further on. Insight does not let him settle anywhere.

The ladder, rung by rung

The same pattern repeats through the whole ascent — the four absorptions, then the four formless attainments: infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness. At each, he names its specific factors, sees them arise and vanish, stays detached, and finds “an escape beyond.” The repetition is the point: no attainment, however refined, escapes the analysis; each is shown to be conditioned, composite, and impermanent, and from each there is further to go.

Where the method must change

At the two highest reaches, something subtle happens. In the dimension of neither perception nor non-perception, the mind is too attenuated to dissect from within — perception is barely present. So Sāriputta “emerged from that attainment with mindfulness,” and only then contemplated the factors “that had passed, ceased, and perished.” Insight here works retrospectively, on the just-vanished state. The same with the summit:

The cessation of perception and feeling. Beyond all the formless states, Sāriputta enters the cessation of perception and feeling — and “having seen with wisdom, his defilements came to an end.” Emerging, he contemplates the phenomena that had ceased; and now, for the first time, he understands: “there is no escape beyond.” There is nowhere higher to go. The journey is complete.

The shift from “there is an escape beyond” to “there is no escape beyond” marks the whole arc of the path in two phrases. At every conditioned state, insight presses onward — not this, keep going. At the ending of the defilements, it rests — here, nothing further. The goal is not a higher attainment but the exhaustion of clinging itself; reached, it leaves nothing beyond to seek.

The Buddha’s true-born son

The discourse closes in tribute. Sāriputta has “attained mastery and perfection in noble ethics, immersion, wisdom, and freedom.” And then the words the tradition has cherished: he is “the Buddha’s true-born son, born from his mouth, born of the teaching, created by the teaching, heir to the teaching — not the heir in things of the flesh.” The kinship that matters is not blood but Dhamma; the true inheritance is awakening, passed from teacher to disciple through the teaching itself. And Sāriputta “rightly keeps rolling the supreme Wheel of Dhamma that was rolled forth by the Realized One” — the great wheel set turning at Benares now kept turning by a disciple who has fully understood it. The portrait of insight at its highest is, fittingly, also a portrait of the perfect heir.

Key terms

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats the factor-analysis for each of the nine attainments. The praise, the method shown in the first absorption, the formless states in brief, the two highest attainments, cessation, and the closing tribute are given closely. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The praise of Sāriputta

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, and addressed the mendicants. “Sāriputta is astute, mendicants. He has great wisdom, widespread wisdom, joyous wisdom, swift wisdom, sharp wisdom, and penetrating wisdom. For a fortnight he practised insight into phenomena one by one. And this is how he did it.”

The first absorption, factor by factor

§3–4“Quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unskillful qualities, he entered the first absorption. And he distinguished its phenomena one by one: placing of the mind and keeping it connected, rapture, bliss, and unification of mind; contact, feeling, perception, intention, mind, enthusiasm, decision, energy, mindfulness, equanimity, and attention. He knew those phenomena as they arose, as they remained, and as they went away. He understood: ‘So it seems that these phenomena, not having been, come to be; and having come to be, they flit away.’ Regarding them he meditated without getting involved or shying away — independent, untied, liberated, detached, with mind free of boundaries. He understood: ‘There is an escape beyond.’ And repeated practice confirmed this for him.”

Through the absorptions and the formless states

§5–16“In the same way he passed through the second, third, and fourth absorptions, naming the factors of each — and then the dimension of infinite space, the dimension of infinite consciousness, and the dimension of nothingness. At each, he distinguished its phenomena one by one, knew them arising, remaining, and vanishing, and understood: ‘not having been, they come to be; having come to be, they flit away.’ Detached and unbounded, he understood at each: ‘There is an escape beyond.’”

The two highest attainments

§17–18“Going beyond the dimension of nothingness, he entered the dimension of neither perception nor non-perception. And he emerged from that attainment with mindfulness, and contemplated the phenomena that had passed, ceased, and perished: ‘not having been, they come to be; having come to be, they flit away.’ Detached, he understood: ‘There is an escape beyond.’”

Cessation, and the end of the defilements

§19–20“Going beyond the dimension of neither perception nor non-perception, he entered the cessation of perception and feeling. And having seen with wisdom, his defilements came to an end. He emerged from that attainment with mindfulness, and contemplated the phenomena that had passed, ceased, and perished. Detached and unbounded, he understood: ‘There is no escape beyond.’ And cultivating that, he confirmed that there is not.”

The true-born son

§21–23“If there is anyone of whom it may rightly be said that they have attained mastery and perfection in noble ethics, immersion, wisdom, and freedom, it is Sāriputta. If there is anyone of whom it may rightly be said that they are the Buddha’s true-born son — born from his mouth, born of the teaching, created by the teaching, heir to the teaching, not the heir in things of the flesh — it is Sāriputta. Sāriputta rightly keeps rolling the supreme Wheel of Dhamma that was rolled forth by the Realized One.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said.

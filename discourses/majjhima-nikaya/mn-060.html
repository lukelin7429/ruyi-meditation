Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 60

Guaranteed

Apaṇṇakasutta

Setting
The Kosalan brahmin village of Sālā, on the Buddha’s wandering
Speakers
The Buddha and the brahmins and householders of Sālā, who follow no settled teacher
Form
A teaching by reasoning — not a creed to believe but a method: the “sure bet” by which the ethical life is the wise choice even amid uncertainty about the deepest questions.
Length
~20 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No full Āgama parallel; a Sanskrit fragment survives
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the reasoning is famous and clear; the length comes from applying the same “sure bet” logic across several contested doctrines.

Why this discourse, sixtieth

The Buddha arrives at a village whose people follow no particular teacher and admit they have no doctrine they trust. To them he offers something unusual: not a creed to accept on faith, but a method of reasoning — the apaṇṇaka-dhamma, the “sure bet” or “incontrovertible” teaching — by which a thoughtful person can choose wisely even when the deepest questions cannot be settled with certainty.

The method is often compared to Pascal’s famous wager, but it is sharper and more honest. Take the question of an afterlife. One cannot prove it either way. But weigh the options. The person who denies the afterlife and lives accordingly is, even by the nihilist’s own reckoning, “criticized by sensible people in this very life as an immoral person of wrong view” — and if there is an afterlife, they fall to a bad rebirth. They hold “a losing hand on both counts.” The person who affirms it and lives ethically is praised here and now — and if there is an afterlife, they rise to a good one. A “winning hand on both counts.” The wager pays even before death.

The Buddha runs this same logic across the three great nihilisms of his day — the denial of an afterlife and moral fruit, the denial that actions have any effect, and the denial of cause and human effort — and then, for two questions that turn on direct meditative knowledge, shifts to a different move: honest not-knowing plus a lean toward the aspiration that is “close to non-grasping.” The whole discourse is a master-class in how to live well without pretending to certainties one does not have.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

When you cannot know for certain whether there is an afterlife, whether deeds bear fruit, or whether there is causality, the wise choice is still clear: living ethically wins either way — praised by the wise here and now, and safe or rewarded hereafter — while the nihilist holds “a losing hand on both counts.”

A village with no teacher

The framing is important. The people of Sālā are not disciples; asked whether they have “any teacher you’re happy with, in whom you have acquired grounded faith,” they answer plainly, “No, sir.” The Buddha does not respond by demanding belief in him. Instead he says: since you have no teacher you trust, “you should undertake and implement this sure bet teaching” — a way of reasoning that needs no prior faith and works for anyone willing to think honestly. It is the Buddha at his most accommodating to the genuinely undecided: here is a tool you can use right now, on your own.

The sure-bet method

The method takes a contested metaphysical question — one that cannot be settled by ordinary means — and asks not “which is true?” but “which choice is wise, given that I cannot be sure?” For each question, two views directly contradict. The Buddha then shows that one choice dominates: it comes out at least as well under every possibility, and better under some. Crucially, the reckoning is not only about the afterlife payoff — it includes the consequences in this very life, in conduct and in the judgment of the wise. That is what makes it more than a gamble on the unknown.

The wager: the afterlife

Take the nihilist view: “there’s no meaning in giving, no fruit of good and bad deeds, no afterlife… no awakened ones who know it.” The Buddha lays out the matrix:

 If there’s no afterlifeIf there is an afterlife
The nihilist (denies it, lives badly)“Safe” after death — but already criticized now by the wise as immoralReborn in a bad place — and criticized now: a losing hand on both counts
The affirmer (affirms it, lives well)Already praised now by the wise as moral — loses nothingReborn in a good place — and praised now: a winning hand on both counts

The affirmer wins under both possibilities; the nihilist loses under both. And note the deeper point the Buddha adds: holding the false view does not stay neatly in the head. “Wrong view” gives rise to wrong intention, wrong speech, the disparaging of the noble ones, the misleading of others, and the abandonment of ethical conduct. The view shapes the life — which is exactly why the wager is decided as much here as hereafter.

The same logic: action and causality

The Buddha then applies the identical structure to two more of the era’s great nihilisms. First, the doctrine of non-action (the view that killing, torturing, and stealing incur no evil, and giving and restraint no merit — “reduce all beings to a heap of flesh, no evil comes of that”). Second, the doctrine of no-cause (the determinist view that there is “no power, no energy, no human strength,” that beings are “molded by destiny, circumstance, and nature” with no effort of their own). In each case the matrix comes out the same: the one who denies action’s efficacy, or denies causality and effort, holds a losing hand on both counts; the one who affirms them holds a winning hand. These three — nihilism about the afterlife, about moral action, and about causality — were the live doctrines of named teachers of the day, and the Buddha dismantles all three with one tool.

Two questions answered by honesty

The last two questions are handled differently, and the shift is instructive. They concern matters that turn on direct meditative knowledge — whether there are “totally formless states,” and whether there is a “total cessation of continued existence.” Here the Buddha does not offer a wager but models intellectual honesty: “Some say there are; I have not known that. Some say there are not; I have not seen that. Without knowing or seeing, it would not be appropriate for me to take one side and declare, ‘This alone is true.’” Then he leans — not toward the safer bet, but toward the wholesome aspiration. Of the two views about cessation, one is “close to greed, attachment, and grasping,” the other “close to non-greed, non-attachment, and non-grasping.” So, without claiming to know, the wise person “simply practices for disillusionment, dispassion, and cessation.” The orientation of one’s practice can be chosen wisely even when the metaphysics cannot be settled.

Is the safe bet just self-interest?

It can look, at first, like a calculation of personal advantage — bet on virtue because it pays. But the structure quietly undercuts that reading. The “winning hand” is not chiefly the heavenly reward; it is that right view already produces right intention, kind speech, ethical conduct, and freedom from the need to glorify oneself and put others down — goods that are real whether or not there is an afterlife. The person living this way is not gambling on a payout; they are simply living well, and would still be living well if the materialist turned out to be right. The wager is really a demonstration that the wholesome life needs no metaphysical guarantee to be worth living — it is its own reward, with the rest as a bonus.

The four persons and the path

The Buddha closes by setting the “sure bet” reasoning within the larger map of the four kinds of person — those who torment themselves, others, both, or neither — the same framework as MN 51. The fourth, who torments no one, is again unfolded as the whole gradual training: a Buddha arises, a seeker goes forth, abandons the five hindrances, enters the four absorptions, and realizes the three knowledges and liberation. The reasoning that began as a way for undecided villagers to choose wisely thus opens, at its far end, onto the complete path. The brahmins of Sālā go for refuge — not coerced by doctrine, but persuaded by a teaching that asked them only to think clearly.

Key terms

apaṇṇaka-dhamma — the “incontrovertible” or “sure bet” teaching; a way of reasoning that cannot lead one wrong. A method for choosing wisely under uncertainty, needing no prior faith.
natthika-vāda — nihilism: the doctrine that there is “no afterlife, no fruit of good and bad deeds, no awakened ones who know.” The first and most basic of the wrong views the wager addresses.
akiriya-vāda — the doctrine of non-action: that deeds have no moral effect — no evil from killing, no merit from giving. The denial of moral causation.
ahetuka-vāda — the doctrine of no-cause: strict determinism — “no power, no energy, no human strength,” beings “molded by destiny” alone. The denial of effort and causality.
sammā-diṭṭhi — right view, and the chain it conditions: right intention, right speech, ethical conduct. The discourse shows how a view “shapes the life” — which is why the wager is decided in this life too.
“a winning / losing hand on both counts” — Sujato’s rendering of the dice-throw idiom: the wise choice comes out well under every possibility (the lucky throw), the foolish one badly under all (the unlucky throw).

The text

The full discourse. The wager on the afterlife is given in full as the template; the parallel treatments of action and causality are condensed, as is the closing gradual training (given in full at MN 51). Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A teaching for those with no teacher

§1–3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was wandering in the land of the Kosalans when he arrived at a village of the Kosalan brahmins named Sālā. The brahmins and householders of Sālā, hearing of his good reputation, went up to him and sat down to one side.

§4The Buddha said to them: “So, householders, is there any teacher you’re happy with, in whom you have acquired grounded faith?” “No, sir.” “Since you haven’t found a teacher you’re happy with, you should undertake and implement this sure bet teaching. For when the sure bet teaching is undertaken, it will be for your lasting welfare and happiness. And what is the sure bet teaching?

The wager: is there an afterlife?

§5–6There are some ascetics and brahmins who have this view: ‘There’s no meaning in giving, sacrifice, or offerings. There’s no fruit or result of good and bad deeds. There’s no afterlife. There’s no such thing as mother and father, or beings reborn spontaneously. And there’s no ascetic or brahmin who has realized the afterlife with their own insight.’ And there are some whose doctrine directly contradicts this, affirming all these things. Don’t these doctrines directly contradict each other?” “Yes, sir.”

§7–8“Consider those whose view is that there is no afterlife. You can expect that they will shun good conduct and undertake bad conduct — because they don’t see the drawbacks of unskillful qualities or the benefit of the skillful. Moreover, since there actually is another world, their view that there is none is wrong view; their thought, wrong purpose; their speech, wrong speech. In saying it they contradict the perfected ones who know, and persuade others of an untrue teaching, glorifying themselves and putting others down. So many bad qualities come to be, with wrong view as their condition.

§9A sensible person reflects: ‘If there is no afterlife, then when this person’s body breaks up they keep themselves safe. But if there is an afterlife, they will be reborn in a bad place, in hell. And even granting the nihilists are right, that person is still criticized in this very life by sensible people as immoral, a nihilist of wrong view.’ So if there really is another world, they hold a losing hand on both counts: criticized now, and reborn in hell. They have wrongly undertaken the teaching, grasping only one side’s outcome and leaving out the skillful premise.

§10–12Now consider those whose view is that there is an afterlife. You can expect that they will shun bad conduct and undertake good conduct — because they see the drawbacks of the unskillful and the benefit of the skillful. Their view is right view, their thought right purpose, their speech right speech; they don’t contradict the perfected ones, don’t mislead others, don’t glorify themselves or put others down. A sensible person reflects: ‘If there is an afterlife, this person will be reborn in a good place, a heaven. And even granting the nihilists are right, that person is still praised in this very life by sensible people as moral, of right view.’ So if there really is another world, they hold a winning hand on both counts: praised now, and reborn in heaven. They have rightly undertaken the teaching, encompassing the outcomes of both sides.”

The same logic: action, and causality

The Buddha applies the identical reasoning to two more nihilisms. First, the doctrine of non-action: that killing, torturing, and stealing incur no evil and giving no merit — “if you reduced all the creatures of this earth to one heap of flesh, no evil comes of that.” Second, the doctrine of no-cause: that there is “no power, no energy, no human strength,” and beings are merely “molded by destiny, circumstance, and nature.” In each case: the one who denies action’s effect, or denies causality and effort, holds a losing hand on both counts; the one who affirms them holds a winning hand on both counts — criticized or praised now, and reborn badly or well hereafter.

Two questions answered by honesty

§29–31“There are some who say ‘there are no totally formless states of meditation,’ and some who say ‘there are.’ A sensible person reflects: ‘I have not known the one, nor seen the other. Without knowing or seeing, it would not be appropriate for me to declare, “This alone is true, anything else is futile.” Now, owing to form, bad things are seen — taking up the rod and sword, quarrels, disputes, lies — but these do not exist where it is totally formless.’ Reflecting thus, they simply practice for disillusionment, dispassion, and cessation regarding forms.

§32–34There are some who say ‘there is no total cessation of continued existence,’ and some who say ‘there is.’ A sensible person reflects: ‘I have not known the one, nor seen the other. Without knowing or seeing, it would not be appropriate to declare, “This alone is true.” The view that there is no cessation of existence is close to greed, yoking, relishing, attachment, and grasping. The view that there is a cessation of existence is close to non-greed, non-yoking, non-attachment, and non-grasping.’ Reflecting thus, they simply practice for disillusionment, dispassion, and cessation regarding future lives.”

The four persons, and the path

§35–39“Householders, these four individuals are found in the world: one who mortifies themselves; one who mortifies others; one who mortifies both; and one who mortifies neither — who lives without wishes, quenched, cooled, experiencing bliss, with self become divine. The self-tormentor is the naked ascetic… the other-tormentor lives by a cruel trade… the one who torments both is the king performing a great sacrifice… And the one who torments neither?”

The fourth person is unfolded as the whole gradual training (given in full at MN 51): a Realized One arises and teaches; a householder hears, gains faith, and goes forth; abandoning the five hindrances, they enter the four absorptions, and with the purified mind realize the three knowledges — recollection of past lives, the divine eye, and the ending of the defilements — knowing: “Rebirth is ended… there is nothing further for this place.” This is the one who torments neither themselves nor others, “quenched, cooled… with self become divine.”

§57When he had spoken, the brahmins and householders of Sālā said, “Excellent, worthy Gotama! Excellent! As if he were righting the overturned, or revealing the hidden, or pointing out the path to the lost, or lighting a lamp in the dark… We go for refuge to the worthy Gotama, to the teaching, and to the mendicant Saṅgha. From this day forth, may the worthy Gotama remember us as lay followers who have gone for refuge for life.”

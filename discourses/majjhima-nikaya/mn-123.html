Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 123

Incredible and Amazing

Acchariyaabbhutasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, following an unfinished mendicants’ conversation
Speakers
Venerable Ānanda, narrating at length, with the Buddha framing and closing the discourse
Form
A devotional catalogue of miraculous events at the Buddha’s conception and birth, capped by a quiet redirection to ordinary mindfulness practice
Length
~14 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A close counterpart survives in the Chinese Madhyama Āgama (MĀ 32, “Discourse on Unprecedented and Marvelous Qualities”)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — vivid and narrative rather than argumentative, but easy to miss the sutta’s real point

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-twenty-third

This is the discourse behind the Buddha nativity story as most of the Buddhist world has come to know it — the cosmic light, the newborn who takes seven steps and speaks, the miraculous purity surrounding his birth. Ānanda, revered in the tradition as something like the inventor of this entire genre of “amazing stories” (abbhutadhamma), narrates it here at length, building marvel upon marvel. It would be easy to read this as simple hagiography, awe for its own sake.

But the discourse doesn’t end where Ānanda’s catalogue ends. After a stream of cosmic wonders, the Buddha adds one more item to the list — and it is deliberately, almost comically down to earth. The contrast is the entire point: the sutta stages a magnificent display of devotion, then gently redirects it back toward the plain, unglamorous work of watching one’s own mind.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

After Ānanda catalogues an extraordinary sequence of marvels surrounding the Buddha’s conception and birth, the Buddha adds one final “incredible and amazing quality” of his own — that he simply knows feelings, perceptions, and thoughts as they arise, remain, and pass away — quietly redirecting all that cosmic wonder back to the ordinary discipline of present-moment awareness.

An unfinished conversation, again

As in MN 119, the frame is a mendicants’ conversation the Buddha walks in on mid-sentence. This time they are marveling at the Buddha’s psychic power to recall every detail of the countless Buddhas of the past — their births, names, clans, teachings, and attainments. Ānanda adds simply, “The Realized Ones are incredible… and they have incredible qualities.” When the Buddha arrives and hears what they were discussing, he turns directly to Ānanda: “Well then, Ānanda, say some more about the incredible and amazing qualities of the Realized One.” What follows is Ānanda’s own extended recitation — not something he invents on the spot, but a catalogue he says he “heard and learned in the presence of the Buddha.”

Ānanda’s catalogue

What Ānanda recites became, in time, the seed of the entire traditional nativity story of the Buddha. It unfolds as an escalating sequence of marvels, item by item, each one closed with the same refrain: “This too I remember as an incredible quality of the Buddha.”

The mother spontaneously becomes ethical, loses sexual desire, yet enjoys the five sensual pleasures freely and remains free of affliction — seeing the child whole in her womb, “like a beryl gem strung on a thread,” examined by a person with clear eyes.
BirthAfter a full ten months (not the usual nine), the mother gives birth standing up. Gods receive the child before humans do; four godlings catch him before he touches the ground and present him to his mother: “Rejoice, O Queen! An illustrious son is born to you.” He emerges already clean, “as a jewel does not soil the cloth it rests on, nor the cloth the jewel” — and two showers, one cool, one warm, fall from the sky to bathe mother and child.
The declarationStanding firm on his own feet, facing north, sheltered by a white parasol, the newborn takes seven strides, surveys all directions, and proclaims: “I am the foremost in the world! I am the eldest! I am the first! This is my last rebirth; now there’ll be no more future lives.” The cosmic light and trembling repeat.

One detail breaks the pattern of pure marvel: “Seven days after the being intent on awakening is born, his mother passes away and is reborn in the host of joyful gods.” This is not legend but history — Queen Māyā did die shortly after childbirth, and the future Buddha was raised by his aunt and stepmother, Mahāpajāpatī. Even in a catalogue built for wonder, the discourse does not flinch from this loss.

Decoding the newborn’s proclamation

The scene of the infant’s seven steps and declaration is one of the most famous images in Buddhist art, and the tradition reads it as thoroughly symbolic rather than merely spectacular. “North” (uttara) also means “beyond,” foreshadowing Nibbāna. The seven strides are read as the seven awakening factors he would later teach. The white parasol signifies purity and royalty; surveying all quarters signifies the universal knowledge of awakening; and the “dramatic proclamation” is literally, in Pali, an āsabhā vācā, a “bull’s voice” — the register of an assertion made with total, unshakable confidence. Read this way, the newborn is not simply boasting; the scene compresses the entire trajectory of the path to awakening into a single infant gesture.

There is a real tension worth noticing here, one the commentary itself flags: this scene implies Buddhahood was settled and predestined from the moment of birth — in some contrast to the rest of the canon, where the bodhisatta’s awakening is consistently presented as something hard-won through years of strenuous effort. The genre of devotional “amazing stories” and the genre of practical spiritual instruction do not always sit at quite the same angle.

The reversal

Once Ānanda has finished — a sustained, escalating performance of devotion — the Buddha does something unexpected. Rather than simply approving and moving on, he adds one further item of his own: “you should also remember this as an incredible and amazing quality of the Realized One. It’s that the Realized One knows feelings as they arise, as they remain, and as they go away. He knows perceptions as they arise, as they remain, and as they go away. He knows thoughts as they arise, as they remain, and as they go away.”

The turn. After cosmic light, trembling galaxies, and a talking newborn, the Buddha’s own candidate for “most incredible and amazing” is simply this: paying continuous, precise attention to the arising and passing of one’s own feelings, perceptions, and thoughts.

The move is not a rejection of Ānanda’s devotion — the Buddha explicitly “approved” what Ānanda said, and Ānanda immediately agrees with the addition. It is a reframing. This capacity for continuous present-moment awareness (satisampajañña) is elsewhere described as something the Buddha shares with his awakened disciples, not a unique cosmic privilege. And it closes an elegant circle: Ānanda’s very first item, describing the bodhisatta’s rebirth in Tusita, was that he was “mindful and aware” (sato sampajāno) at that moment. The discourse ends by pointing straight back to where it began — not the miracle of the event, but the ordinary, replicable quality of mind present within it.

Key terms

abbhutadhamma — “amazing stories,” one of the nine traditional categories of Buddhist teaching, and a genre closely associated with Ānanda, who may be regarded as its originator.
bodhisatta — “being intent on awakening”: here applied to the future Buddha from the very start of his final rebirth in Tusita, an earlier and broader usage than in some other early texts.
the interstellar darkness — the “boundless gloom” between world-systems, so total that sun and moon make no impression there; the cosmic light at the bodhisatta’s conception and birth reaches even this realm, letting its inhabitants see each other for the first time.
āsabhā vācā — the newborn’s “dramatic proclamation,” literally a “bull’s voice”: speech delivered with total, unshakable confidence.
satisampajañña — “mindfulness and awareness”: the Buddha’s own closing candidate for “incredible and amazing,” knowing feelings, perceptions, and thoughts as they arise, remain, and pass — a quality he shares with his awakened disciples.
the circle closed — Ānanda’s catalogue opens with the bodhisatta “mindful and aware” at his rebirth in Tusita; the Buddha’s final addition returns to exactly this quality, reframing wonder at results into attention to cause.

The text

The discourse in full: the unfinished conversation, the Buddha’s invitation to Ānanda, the complete catalogue of marvels from conception through birth, and the Buddha’s closing addition. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

An unfinished conversation

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove. After the meal, several mendicants sat together in the assembly hall and this discussion came up: “It’s incredible, reverends, it’s amazing! The Realized One has such psychic power and might! For he is able to know the Buddhas of the past… their birth, names, clans, conduct, teaching, wisdom, meditation, and freedom.” Ānanda said: “The Realized Ones are incredible, reverends, and they have incredible qualities. They’re amazing, and they have amazing qualities.” But their conversation was left unfinished. Then the Buddha came out of retreat and asked what they had been discussing. When they told him, he said: “Well then, Ānanda, say some more about the incredible and amazing qualities of the Realized One.”

Conception

§3–7“Sir, I have heard and learned this in the presence of the Buddha: ‘Mindful and aware, the being intent on awakening was reborn in the host of joyful gods.’ This I remember as an incredible quality of the Buddha. Mindful and aware, he remained there for the whole of that life. Mindful and aware, the being intent on awakening passed away from the host of joyful gods and was conceived in his mother’s womb. And then — in this world with its gods, Māras, and divinities — an immeasurable, magnificent light appears, surpassing the glory of the gods. Even in the boundless gloom of interstellar space, so utterly dark that even the luminosity of the moon and the sun makes no impression, an immeasurable, magnificent light appears. And even the sentient beings reborn there recognize each other by that light: ‘So, it seems other sentient beings have been reborn here!’ And this ten-thousandfold galaxy shakes and rocks and trembles.

Protection and gestation

§8–12When the being intent on awakening is conceived in his mother’s belly, four godlings approach to guard the four quarters, so that no one shall harm him or his mother. His mother becomes naturally ethical, refraining from killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying, and intoxicants. She no longer thinks of men in a sexual way, and cannot be violated by a man of lustful intent. She obtains the five kinds of sensual stimulation and amuses herself, supplied and provided with them. No afflictions beset her; she’s happy and free of bodily fatigue, and she sees the being intent on awakening in her womb, whole in his major and minor limbs, not deficient in any faculty — as a person with clear eyes might take in hand a naturally lustrous, eight-faceted beryl gem strung on a thread of blue, yellow, red, white, or golden brown, and examine it.

Birth

§13–19Seven days after the being intent on awakening is born, his mother passes away and is reborn in the host of joyful gods. Other women carry the infant for nine or ten months; not so his mother — she gives birth after exactly ten months. Other women give birth sitting or lying down; not so his mother — she gives birth standing up. When he emerges from the womb, gods receive him first, then humans. Before he reaches the ground, four godlings receive him and place him before his mother, saying: ‘Rejoice, O Queen! An illustrious son is born to you.’ He emerges already clean, unsoiled by any impurity — as a jewel-treasure placed on a cloth from Kāsi would not soil the cloth, nor the cloth the jewel, because of the cleanliness of them both. And two showers of water appear from the sky, one cool, one warm, for bathing the being intent on awakening and his mother.

The declaration

§20–21As soon as he’s born, the being intent on awakening stands firm with his own feet on the ground. Facing north, he takes seven strides with a white parasol held above him, surveys all quarters, and makes this dramatic proclamation: ‘I am the foremost in the world! I am the eldest in the world! I am the first in the world! This is my last rebirth; now there’ll be no more future lives.’ And then, once more, an immeasurable, magnificent light appears throughout the cosmos, even in the boundless gloom of interstellar space, and this ten-thousandfold galaxy shakes and rocks and trembles. This too I remember as an incredible and amazing quality of the Buddha.”

The reversal

§22–23“Well then, Ānanda, you should also remember this as an incredible and amazing quality of the Realized One. It’s that the Realized One knows feelings as they arise, as they remain, and as they go away. He knows perceptions as they arise, as they remain, and as they go away. He knows thoughts as they arise, as they remain, and as they go away. This too you should remember as an incredible and amazing quality of the Realized One.” “Sir, the Buddha knows feelings as they arise, as they remain, and as they go away. He knows perceptions… He knows thoughts… This too I remember as an incredible and amazing quality of the Buddha.” That’s what Ānanda said, and the teacher approved. Satisfied, those mendicants approved what Venerable Ānanda said.

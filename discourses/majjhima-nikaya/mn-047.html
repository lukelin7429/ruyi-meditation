Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 47

The Inquirer

Vīmaṃsakasutta

Setting
Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speakers
The Buddha and the monks
Form
Teaching to monks — the Buddha lays out a step-by-step method by which a disciple who cannot read minds may investigate the Buddha himself, testing him through what can be seen and heard until faith rests on evidence.
Length
~10 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 186 (Madhyama-āgama 186, “Seeking Understanding”)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — accessible but subtle. The content is concrete — a checklist of investigation — but its implication is radical: the teacher invites his own examination, and faith is to be earned, not demanded.

Why this discourse, forty-seventh

MN 47 is one of the most remarkable invitations in religious literature: the Buddha tells his disciples to scrutinize him. A serious inquirer should not take his awakening on trust. They should examine the Realized One through everything that can be seen and heard, and place confidence in him only when the evidence holds.

The discourse opens from the disciple’s side. An ordinary mendicant cannot read another person’s mind, so how could they ever know whether their teacher is genuinely awakened? The Buddha’s answer is that they do not need to read his mind. Awakening leaves traces. A purified mind shows in conduct and speech over time, under pressure, in private and in public. These traces can be observed. The inquirer’s task is to look carefully and patiently at what is observable, and to draw a conclusion from it.

What emerges is a model of faith that is the opposite of blind belief. The discourse ends with the famous formula of grounded faith based on evidence (ākāravatī saddhā dassanamūlikā) — a conviction that is strong precisely because it was tested and earned, and so cannot be shaken by any ascetic, brahmin, god, or Māra. MN 47 is the Buddhist charter for critical inquiry: trust, but verify; and verify even the teacher.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A disciple who cannot read minds should not take their teacher’s awakening on faith — they should scrutinize the Realized One through what can be seen and heard, over time and under the pressure of fame, until their confidence rests on evidence rather than belief.

The structure of the discourse

SectionWhat happens
1. The problemAn inquiring mendicant who cannot encompass another’s mind should still scrutinize the Realized One to determine whether he is fully awakened. The monks ask the Buddha to explain how.
2. The two channelsThe Buddha is to be examined for “two things — things that can be seen and heard”: his observable conduct and his observable speech.
3. Corrupt, mixed, cleanThree successive questions: are there corrupt states in him? mixed states? clean states? The inquirer finds nothing corrupt, nothing mixed — only clean states.
4. Long-establishedHas he had these clean states for a long time, or only recently? They are long-established — not a passing or recent show.
5. The test of fameFame and renown bring dangers that expose a person. Does this venerable, now famous, show those dangers? He does not.
6. Secure, not insecureIs his restraint “securely stilled” — the freedom of one in whom greed has truly ended — or merely held in check? It is secure: greed has ended.
7. Asking the teacher directlyThe inquirer puts the same questions to the Realized One himself, who confirms them — adding that he is “the scope and the range” of clean states “but not determined by that.”
8. Grounded faithThe disciple then listens to the teaching, directly knows part of it for themselves, and arrives at confidence. This is “grounded faith based on evidence” — unshakeable.

The two channels: seen and heard

The Buddha frames the whole investigation around diṭṭha-suta — what is seen and what is heard. The inquirer cannot peer inside the teacher’s mind, so they work entirely from the outside: from how he behaves and how he speaks. This is a deliberately empirical method. It does not ask the disciple to take anything on report or intuition. It asks them to watch and listen, and to keep watching and listening, because a single impressive moment proves little. Character reveals itself across time and circumstance.

The three-fold filter — corrupt, mixed, clean — is a graduated test. First the gross fault: is there anything plainly defiled in his conduct or speech? Then the subtle fault: is there anything mixed, where good and bad alternate, the mark of someone not yet fully purified? Only when both come back empty does the inquirer look for the positive sign: clean states, consistently present. The order matters. Disqualifying faults are ruled out before any positive verdict is allowed.

Why fame is the crucial test

The most psychologically acute step is the test of fame. The Buddha observes that “so long as a mendicant has not achieved fame and renown, certain dangers are not found in them; but when they achieve fame and renown, those dangers appear.” Obscurity protects a person from temptation. Recognition, a following, gifts, and praise are the conditions under which hidden flaws surface — pride, the craving for status, the exploitation of devotees. A teacher who remains unchanged by fame has passed a test that an unknown person never even faces. This is one of the discourse’s sharpest contributions to the question of how to judge a spiritual teacher.

“Securely stilled, not insecurely stilled”

The final check distinguishes two reasons a person might abstain from sensual pleasure. One is suppression — desire is still present but held down by willpower or circumstance; this is “insecurely stilled,” and it can collapse. The other is eradication — greed has actually ended, so there is nothing left to indulge; this is “securely stilled.” From the outside these can look identical, which is why the inquirer must observe the teacher across situations — in community and alone, surrounded by people in every condition — and note whether his evenness is genuine or merely a performance that pressure would crack.

Grounded faith, not blind faith

The discourse’s destination is its key term: ākāravatī saddhā dassanamūlikā, “grounded faith that is based on evidence” — literally, faith that has reasons and is rooted in seeing. This is not credulity. The disciple investigated the teacher, then listened to the teaching, then verified a portion of it in their own experience. Faith built this way “cannot be shifted by any ascetic or brahmin or god or Māra or divinity or by anyone in the world.” The strength of the conviction comes from the rigor of the testing. MN 47 is the canonical answer to the charge that faith and inquiry are opposed: here, faith is the result of inquiry.

“The scope and the range, but not determined by that”

When the inquirer asks the Buddha directly about his clean states, he confirms them but adds a striking qualification: “I am the scope and the range of that, but I am not determined by that.” The clean qualities are fully his — he embodies them — yet he does not identify with them or grasp them as “mine.” Even the purest states are not made into a self. It is a quiet reminder that the awakened mind holds its own virtues without clinging, the very non-grasping that makes them clean in the first place.

Key terms

vīmaṃsaka — an inquirer; an investigator; one who examines and tests. The title figure of the discourse. The related noun vīmaṃsā means investigation or scrutiny — one of the four bases of spiritual power.
diṭṭha · suta — the seen and the heard. The two channels through which the teacher is examined: observable conduct and observable speech. The method is deliberately empirical — not mind-reading, but watching and listening.
saṅkiliṭṭha / vītimissa / vodāta — corrupt / mixed / clean. The three-fold graduated filter. The inquirer rules out gross defilement, then subtle alternation of good and bad, before confirming the positive presence of clean states.
anupakuṭṭho / supakuṭṭho — insecurely stilled / securely stilled. The distinction between desire merely suppressed and desire actually ended. Only the latter is the mark of one “free of greed because greed has ended.”
ākāravatī saddhā — grounded faith; faith that has reasons. With dassanamūlikā (“rooted in seeing”), the discourse’s culminating term: a conviction built on tested evidence, strong enough that no power in the world can shift it.
tathāgata — the Realized One; the Buddha’s usual term for himself. Here it carries weight: the inquirer scrutinizes the Tathāgata — the very figure who, in most traditions, would be placed beyond examination.

The text

The full discourse, given here complete. The investigation unfolds as a sequence of questions the inquirer asks, each answered by observation. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The inquirer’s task

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. There the Buddha addressed the mendicants, “Mendicants!” “Venerable sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

§2“Mendicants, a mendicant who is an inquirer, unable to encompass another’s mind, should scrutinize the Realized One to understand whether he is a fully awakened Buddha or not.”

§3“Our teachings are rooted in the Buddha. He is our guide and our refuge. Sir, may the Buddha himself please clarify the meaning of this. The mendicants will listen and remember it.” “Well then, mendicants, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

Things that can be seen and heard

§4“Mendicants, a mendicant who is an inquirer, unable to encompass another’s mind, should scrutinize the Realized One for two things—things that can be seen and heard: ‘Can anything corrupt be seen or heard in the Realized One or not?’ Scrutinizing him they find that nothing corrupt can be seen or heard in the Realized One.

§5They scrutinize further: ‘Can anything mixed be seen or heard in the Realized One or not?’ Scrutinizing him they find that nothing mixed can be seen or heard in the Realized One.

§6They scrutinize further: ‘Can anything clean be seen or heard in the Realized One or not?’ Scrutinizing him they find that clean things can be seen and heard in the Realized One.

§7They scrutinize further: ‘Did the venerable attain this skillful state a long time ago, or just recently?’ Scrutinizing him they find that the venerable attained this skillful state a long time ago, not just recently.

§8They scrutinize further: ‘Are certain dangers found in that venerable mendicant who has achieved fame and renown?’ For, mendicants, so long as a mendicant has not achieved fame and renown, certain dangers are not found in them. But when they achieve fame and renown, those dangers appear. Scrutinizing him they find that those dangers are not found in that venerable mendicant who has achieved fame and renown.

§9They scrutinize further: ‘Is this venerable securely stilled or insecurely stilled? Is the reason they don’t indulge in sensual pleasures that they’re free of greed because greed has ended?’ Scrutinizing him they find that that venerable is securely stilled, not insecurely stilled. The reason they don’t indulge in sensual pleasures is that they’re free of greed because greed has ended.

§10If others should ask that mendicant, ‘But what reason and evidence does the venerable have for saying this?’ Answering rightly, the mendicant should say, ‘Because, whether that venerable is staying in a community or alone, some people there are in a good state or a sorry state, some instruct a group, and some are seen among pleasures of the flesh while others remain unsullied. Yet that venerable doesn’t look down on them for that. Also, I have heard and learned this in the presence of the Buddha: “I am securely stilled, not insecurely stilled. The reason I don’t indulge in sensual pleasures is that I’m free of greed because greed has ended.”’

Asking the Realized One directly

§11Next, they should ask the Realized One himself about this, ‘Can anything corrupt be seen or heard in the Realized One or not?’ The Realized One would answer, ‘Nothing corrupt can be seen or heard in the Realized One.’

§12‘Can anything mixed be seen or heard in the Realized One or not?’ The Realized One would answer, ‘Nothing mixed can be seen or heard in the Realized One.’

§13‘Can anything clean be seen or heard in the Realized One or not?’ The Realized One would answer, ‘Clean things can be seen and heard in the Realized One. I am the scope and the range of that, but I am not determined by that.’

Confidence grounded in evidence

§14A disciple ought to approach a teacher who has such a doctrine in order to listen to the teaching. The teacher explains Dhamma with its higher and higher stages, with its better and better stages, with its dark and bright sides. When they directly know a certain principle of those teachings, in accordance with how they were taught, the mendicant comes to a conclusion about the teachings. They have confidence in the teacher: ‘The Blessed One is a fully awakened Buddha! The teaching is well explained! The Saṅgha is practicing well!’

§15If others should ask that mendicant, ‘But what reason and evidence does the venerable have for saying this?’ Answering rightly, the mendicant should say, ‘Reverends, I approached the Buddha to listen to the teaching. He explained Dhamma with its higher and higher stages, with its better and better stages, with its dark and bright sides. When I directly knew a certain principle of those teachings, in accordance with how I was taught, I came to a conclusion about the teachings. I had confidence in the Teacher: “The Blessed One is a fully awakened Buddha! The teaching is well explained! The Saṅgha is practicing well!”’

§16When someone’s faith is settled, rooted, and planted in the Realized One in this manner, with these words and phrases, it’s said to be grounded faith that’s based on evidence. It is strong, and cannot be shifted by any ascetic or brahmin or god or Māra or divinity or by anyone in the world. That is how there is legitimate scrutiny of the Realized One, and that is how the Realized One is legitimately well-scrutinized.”

That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said.

Question 1 of 10
What is the striking premise of MN 47?
Correct: C. MN 47 is remarkable precisely because the Buddha invites examination of himself. A serious inquirer should not take his awakening on trust but should test it. Faith is to be earned through investigation, not demanded in advance.
Question 2 of 10
Through what two channels is the inquirer told to examine the Realized One?
Correct: B. The mendicant “unable to encompass another’s mind” cannot peer inside the teacher. So the method is empirical: examine the two things that can be observed — what is seen (conduct) and what is heard (speech) — and keep observing over time.
Question 3 of 10
What is the three-fold filter the inquirer applies, in order?
Correct: A. The inquirer first rules out anything corrupt (gross defilement), then anything mixed (the subtler fault of good and bad alternating), and only then confirms the positive presence of clean states. Disqualifying faults are excluded before any positive verdict is allowed.
Question 4 of 10
After finding clean states, what does the inquirer check about their duration?
Correct: D. A recent or temporary display proves little. The inquirer confirms that the venerable “attained this skillful state a long time ago, not just recently” — the purity is established and stable, not a passing show.
Question 5 of 10
Why does the Buddha single out fame and renown as a crucial test?
Correct: B. “So long as a mendicant has not achieved fame and renown, certain dangers are not found in them; but when they achieve fame and renown, those dangers appear.” Obscurity shields a person from temptation; fame exposes pride and the craving for status. A teacher unchanged by fame has passed a test the unknown never face.
Question 6 of 10
What does “securely stilled” (as opposed to “insecurely stilled”) mean here?
Correct: C. “Insecurely stilled” is desire merely suppressed — it can collapse. “Securely stilled” is desire eradicated: “free of greed because greed has ended.” From the outside these can look alike, which is why the inquirer must watch the teacher across many situations.
Question 7 of 10
After examining the teacher, what does the inquirer do next?
Correct: A. The inquirer puts the same questions — about corrupt, mixed, and clean states — to the Realized One himself, who confirms them. Then the disciple approaches him to listen to the Dhamma, taught “with its higher and higher stages… with its dark and bright sides.”
Question 8 of 10
When asked directly about his clean states, the Buddha confirms them but adds a qualification. What is it?
Correct: D. The clean qualities are fully his — he embodies them — yet he does not grasp them as “mine” or build a self from them. Even the purest states are not made into an identity. This non-grasping is part of what makes them clean.
Question 9 of 10
What kind of faith does the disciple arrive at by the end of the discourse?
Correct: B. The destination is “grounded faith that’s based on evidence” — faith with reasons, rooted in seeing. The disciple investigated the teacher, listened to the teaching, and verified part of it in their own experience. Faith built this way is the result of inquiry, not a substitute for it.
Question 10 of 10
According to the discourse, why is this kind of faith so strong?
Correct: C. The strength of the conviction comes from the rigor of the testing. Because it rests on evidence the disciple checked for themselves, no power in the world can shake it. MN 47 is the canonical reply to the idea that faith and inquiry are opposed: here, faith is what inquiry produces.

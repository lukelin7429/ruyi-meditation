Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 38

The Greater Discourse on the Ending of Craving

Mahātaṇhāsaṅkhayasutta

Setting
Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery
Speakers
The Buddha, Sāti (a monk, fisherman’s son), a group of monks
Form
Corrective discourse — the Buddha demolishes a monk’s wrong view that consciousness transmigrates, then gives an extended teaching on dependent origination, conception, nutriments, and the arising and cessation of the entire mass of suffering
Length
~25 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 201 (Madhyama-āgama 201)
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — one of the most doctrinally dense discourses in the Majjhima Nikāya. The correction of Sāti is vivid; the dependent origination teaching that follows demands slow, careful reading. Essential for understanding the Buddhist position on consciousness and rebirth.

Why this discourse, thirty-eighth

A monk named Sāti, a fisherman’s son, has been telling everyone that the Buddha teaches “this very same consciousness transmigrates from life to life, not another.” The monks try to correct him. He refuses to budge. When the Buddha himself summons Sāti and asks him to explain, Sāti crumbles — he cannot say what this “consciousness” actually is. The Buddha rebukes him sharply: “Foolish man! In many ways I have stated consciousness to be dependently arisen — without conditions, there is no arising of consciousness.”

What follows is one of the canon’s most important teachings on dependent origination. The Buddha shows that consciousness is not a thing that travels but an event that arises whenever conditions come together — named after its conditions, just as a fire is named after its fuel. He then traces the full chain of dependent origination: from ignorance through formations, consciousness, name-and-form, the six sense bases, contact, feeling, craving, clinging, becoming, birth, to aging-and-death — and its cessation in reverse. The discourse also contains a striking passage on conception (the three conditions needed for an embryo to form) and on the four nutriments that sustain living beings.

This is the companion to MN 37. Where MN 37 gave the teaching on the ending of craving “in brief,” MN 38 gives it at length — and begins by clearing away the most dangerous misunderstanding a student can have about the Buddha’s teaching on consciousness and continuity.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Consciousness does not transmigrate — it arises dependent on conditions, and when those conditions cease, the entire mass of suffering ceases with them.

The core teaching: consciousness is not a transmigrating self

Sāti’s error is the most natural mistake a person can make about the Buddhist teaching. If there is no permanent self (attā), and yet the Buddha teaches rebirth, then something must travel from life to life. Sāti reasons that this something must be consciousness — the stream of awareness that knows and feels. He turns consciousness into a soul by another name.

The Buddha’s correction is unequivocal. Consciousness does not travel. It arises fresh each moment, dependent on conditions. Eye-consciousness arises when the eye meets a visible form; ear-consciousness arises when the ear meets a sound. Remove the conditions, and that consciousness does not arise. There is no underlying “consciousness substance” that moves from one body to the next. What continues across lives is not a thing but a process — a causal chain in which each moment of consciousness conditions the next, without anything being transferred.

The fire analogy

The Buddha’s metaphor is elegant. When wood burns, we call it a “wood fire.” When grass burns, we call it a “grass fire.” The fire is named after its fuel. But we do not say that the “wood fire” is the same fire as the “grass fire,” nor that a single fire-entity traveled from the wood to the grass. Similarly, eye-consciousness is not the same thing as ear-consciousness; each arises from its own conditions and is named accordingly. There is no single “consciousness” that moves from sense door to sense door, much less from life to life.

Dependent origination: the full chain

After correcting Sāti, the Buddha gives one of the most complete treatments of dependent origination (paṭiccasamuppāda) in the Majjhima Nikāya. The twelve links — ignorance, formations, consciousness, name-and-form, the six sense bases, contact, feeling, craving, clinging, becoming, birth, and aging-and-death — are not presented as abstract metaphysics but as a description of how suffering arises in real experience: in conception, in childhood, in the moment-to-moment arising of craving from feeling.

The cessation of the chain is equally concrete: when ignorance ceases, formations cease; when formations cease, consciousness (as conditioned) ceases; and so on, until the entire mass of suffering ceases. The discourse does not present this as a distant hope but as something a monk can verify through direct knowledge.

Three questions Western students often ask

“If consciousness doesn’t transmigrate, how does rebirth work?” The Buddhist answer is causal continuity without identity. A flame can light another flame — the second flame is caused by the first, but it is not the same flame. Similarly, the last moment of consciousness in one life conditions the first moment of consciousness in the next, through the mechanism of craving and clinging (taṇhā and upādāna). What “transmigrates” is not a thing but a causal influence. This discourse is the canonical locus for that distinction.

“What is the gandhabba mentioned in the conception passage?” The term is debated. In context, it refers to the being who is seeking rebirth — the consciousness-stream that is about to take a new birth. The commentary glosses it as the being driven by kamma to enter the womb. It is not a soul or spirit in the Hindu sense. The Buddha uses the term pragmatically: three conditions must converge for conception, and the gandhabba is one of them.

“Is this discourse anti-materialist as well as anti-eternalist?” Yes. Sāti represents the eternalist extreme — positing a permanent consciousness that survives death. But the Buddha’s teaching also refutes the annihilationist view that consciousness is simply a product of the brain that ends at death. The twelve-link chain shows that consciousness is neither permanent nor randomly produced; it arises from conditions and ceases when those conditions cease. The middle way of dependent origination avoids both extremes.

Key terms

Mahātaṇhāsaṅkhaya — the greater discourse on the ending of craving. “Greater” (mahā) distinguishes it from MN 37, the shorter treatment. The “ending of craving” is the theme shared by both discourses.
viññāṇa — consciousness; cognition; awareness. The central term in Sāti’s wrong view and the Buddha’s correction. In the Pāli analysis, consciousness is not a self or soul but one of the five aggregates, arising dependent on conditions at each of the six sense doors.
paṭiccasamuppāda — dependent origination; dependent arising. The teaching that all phenomena arise in dependence on conditions. In this discourse, presented as the twelve-link chain from ignorance to aging-and-death.
Sāti kevaṭṭaputta — Sāti, the fisherman’s son. A monk who holds the wrong view that consciousness transmigrates. His name and origin are preserved in the text — a fisherman’s son who, perhaps shaped by his background, grasps at the idea that something enduring travels through the waters of existence.
gandhabba — the being seeking rebirth. One of the three conditions for conception. In this context, not a celestial musician (another meaning of the same word) but the consciousness-stream that, driven by kamma, is about to enter a new life.
āhāra — nutriment; food. The Buddha teaches four nutriments that sustain beings: (1) physical food (kabaḷīkāra āhāra), (2) contact (phassa), (3) mental intention (manosañcetanā), (4) consciousness (viññāṇa).
nāmarūpa — name-and-form; mind-and-body. The compound of mental and physical phenomena that, together with consciousness, constitutes a sentient being. In the twelve-link chain, consciousness and name-and-form condition each other reciprocally.
avijjā — ignorance. The first link in the standard twelve-link chain of dependent origination. Not mere lack of information but a fundamental misapprehension of reality — seeing permanence in the impermanent, satisfaction in the unsatisfying, self in the not-self.

The text

MN 38 is one of the longest and most doctrinally important discourses in the Majjhima Nikāya. Repetitive formula passages are condensed where indicated. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Sāti’s wrong view

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery.

§2Now at that time a pernicious view had arisen in a monk named Sāti, a fisherman’s son: “As I understand the Buddha’s teaching, it is this very same consciousness that transmigrates, not another.”

§3Several monks heard about this and went to Sāti, saying: “Don’t say that, Reverend Sāti. Don’t misrepresent the Buddha. It is not good to misrepresent the Buddha. The Buddha would not say such a thing. For in many ways the Buddha has stated consciousness to be dependently arisen — without conditions, there is no arising of consciousness.”

§4But even after being spoken to by those monks, Sāti stubbornly held on to that pernicious view: “As I understand the Buddha’s teaching, it is this very same consciousness that transmigrates, not another.”

§5Since the monks could not dissuade Sāti from that pernicious view, they went to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and told him everything.

The Buddha summons Sāti

§6Then the Buddha had a monk summon Sāti. Sāti came, bowed, and sat down to one side. The Buddha said to him:

“Is it true, Sāti, that a pernicious view has arisen in you: ‘As I understand the Buddha’s teaching, it is this very same consciousness that transmigrates, not another’?”

“Yes, sir. As I understand the Buddha’s teaching, it is this very same consciousness that transmigrates, not another.”

§7“What is that consciousness, Sāti?”

“Sir, it is that which speaks and feels and experiences the results of good and bad deeds here and there.”

§8“Foolish man! To whom have you ever known me to teach the Dhamma in that way? Haven’t I said in many ways that consciousness arises dependent on conditions, and that without conditions there is no arising of consciousness? But still you misrepresent me by your wrong grasp, harming yourself and storing up much demerit. This will be for your lasting harm and suffering.”

Consciousness is conditioned

§9Then the Buddha addressed the monks: “Monks, do you understand my teaching in the same way as Sāti?”

“No, sir. For in many ways the Buddha has stated consciousness to be dependently arisen — without conditions, there is no arising of consciousness.”

“Good, monks. It is good that you understand my teaching in this way. For in many ways I have stated consciousness to be dependently arisen — without conditions, there is no arising of consciousness.”

§10“Monks, consciousness is named after the condition on which it arises. When consciousness arises dependent on the eye and forms, it is called eye-consciousness. When consciousness arises dependent on the ear and sounds, it is called ear-consciousness. When consciousness arises dependent on the nose and odors, it is called nose-consciousness. When consciousness arises dependent on the tongue and tastes, it is called tongue-consciousness. When consciousness arises dependent on the body and tactile objects, it is called body-consciousness. When consciousness arises dependent on the mind and mental phenomena, it is called mind-consciousness.”

The fire analogy

§11“It is just like fire, which is named after the condition on which it burns. A fire that burns dependent on logs is called a log fire. A fire that burns dependent on sticks is called a stick fire. A fire that burns dependent on grass is called a grass fire. A fire that burns dependent on cow dung is called a cow-dung fire. A fire that burns dependent on husks is called a husk fire. A fire that burns dependent on rubbish is called a rubbish fire.”

“In the same way, consciousness is named after the condition on which it arises.”

Dependent origination

§12“Monks, do you see: ‘This has come to be’?”

“Yes, sir.”

“Do you see: ‘It has come to be with that as nutriment’?”

“Yes, sir.”

“Do you see: ‘With the cessation of that nutriment, what has come to be is liable to cease’?”

“Yes, sir.”

§13“Monks, there are these four nutriments for the sustenance of beings that have come to be, and for the support of beings seeking a new existence. What four? Physical food, whether gross or subtle; contact as the second; mental intention as the third; and consciousness as the fourth. These are the four nutriments.”

§14“And what is the source, what is the origin, what is the birthplace, what is the provenance of these four nutriments? These four nutriments have craving as their source, craving as their origin, craving as their birthplace, craving as their provenance.”

“And what is the source of craving? Craving has feeling as its source. And what is the source of feeling? Feeling has contact as its source. And what is the source of contact? Contact has the six sense bases as its source. And what is the source of the six sense bases? The six sense bases have name-and-form as their source. And what is the source of name-and-form? Name-and-form has consciousness as its source. And what is the source of consciousness? Consciousness has formations as its source. And what is the source of formations? Formations have ignorance as their source.”

§15“So: with ignorance as condition, formations come to be. With formations as condition, consciousness comes to be. With consciousness as condition, name-and-form comes to be. With name-and-form as condition, the six sense bases come to be. With the six sense bases as condition, contact comes to be. With contact as condition, feeling comes to be. With feeling as condition, craving comes to be. With craving as condition, clinging comes to be. With clinging as condition, becoming comes to be. With becoming as condition, birth comes to be. With birth as condition, aging-and-death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress come to be. That is how this whole mass of suffering originates.”

Three conditions for conception

§16“Monks, when three things come together, an embryo is conceived. If the mother and father come together but the mother is not in her season, and the being seeking rebirth (gandhabba) is not present — in that case, no embryo is conceived. If the mother and father come together and the mother is in her season, but the being seeking rebirth is not present — in that case too, no embryo is conceived. But when the mother and father come together, the mother is in her season, and the being seeking rebirth is present — when these three things come together, an embryo is conceived.”

§17“The mother carries that embryo in her womb for nine or ten months with great anxiety, as a heavy burden. After nine or ten months the mother gives birth with great anxiety, as a heavy burden. When the child is born, she nourishes it with her own blood — for mother’s milk is called blood in the training of the noble ones.”

From contact to craving

§18“As the child grows and the sense faculties mature, they play with the various toys of childhood — toy ploughs, toy games, upside-down tricks, toy windmills, toy measures, toy carts, and toy bows.”

§19“As the child grows further and the sense faculties fully mature, they enjoy themselves, supplied and provided with the five kinds of sensual stimulation — pleasing forms known by the eye, pleasing sounds known by the ear, pleasing odors known by the nose, pleasing tastes known by the tongue, pleasing touches known by the body — desirable, lovely, agreeable, pleasant, sensual, and arousing.”

§20“On seeing a form with the eye, they are attracted to pleasing forms and repelled by displeasing forms. They dwell without establishing mindfulness of the body, with a limited mind. They do not truly understand the freedom of mind and freedom by wisdom where those bad, unwholesome qualities cease without anything remaining.”

The same pattern is repeated for the ear and sounds, the nose and odors, the tongue and tastes, the body and touches, the mind and mental phenomena. In each case: contact with a sense object, without mindfulness, leads to feeling, then to craving.

§21“So, being attracted and repelled in this way, whatever feeling they experience — pleasant, painful, or neutral — they approve of that feeling, welcome it, and keep holding on to it. As they do so, delight arises. Delight in feelings is clinging. With clinging as condition, becoming comes to be. With becoming as condition, birth comes to be. With birth as condition, aging-and-death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress come to be. That is how this whole mass of suffering originates.”

The cessation of suffering

§22“But monks, on seeing a form with the eye, a monk is not attracted to pleasing forms and not repelled by displeasing forms. They dwell having established mindfulness of the body, with a limitless mind. They truly understand the freedom of mind and freedom by wisdom where those bad, unwholesome qualities cease without anything remaining.”

The same pattern is repeated for all six sense bases: the monk dwells with mindfulness established, not carried away by attraction or repulsion.

§23“Having given up attraction and repulsion in this way, whatever feeling they experience — pleasant, painful, or neutral — they do not approve of that feeling, do not welcome it, and do not keep holding on to it. As they do not, delight ceases. With the cessation of delight, clinging ceases. With the cessation of clinging, becoming ceases. With the cessation of becoming, birth ceases. With the cessation of birth, aging-and-death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress cease. That is how this whole mass of suffering ceases.”

§24“With the fading away of ignorance and with the arising of true knowledge, they do not cling to sensual pleasures, they do not cling to views, they do not cling to precepts and observances, they do not cling to a doctrine of self. Not clinging, they are not agitated. Not being agitated, they personally attain nibbāna. They understand: ‘Birth is ended, the spiritual life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is no more coming back to any state of being.’”

That is what the Buddha said. The monks were satisfied and delighted in the Buddha’s words.

Self-check quiz

Ten questions. Click an answer to see immediate feedback. No score is recorded — this is for your own checking.

Question 1 of 10
What wrong view does Sāti hold?
Correct: B. Sāti’s view is essentially eternalist: he posits a single, enduring consciousness that travels from one life to the next. This turns consciousness into a self or soul by another name — exactly the view the Buddha rejects throughout his teaching.
Question 2 of 10
When the monks try to correct Sāti, what happens?
Correct: C. Sāti is unmoved by the monks’ correction. He continues to insist on his wrong view, which is why the monks must escalate the matter to the Buddha himself. His stubbornness illustrates how deeply wrong views can take root.
Question 3 of 10
When the Buddha asks Sāti what consciousness is, how does Sāti define it?
Correct: A. Sāti defines consciousness as a unitary agent that speaks, feels, and reaps the results of actions — essentially describing a soul-like entity. This is precisely what the Buddha rejects: consciousness is not an agent but an event that arises and passes dependent on conditions.
Question 4 of 10
What analogy does the Buddha use to explain how consciousness is named?
Correct: D. The fire analogy is central to the discourse. Just as a fire is named after what it burns — a log fire, a grass fire — consciousness is named after the condition on which it arises: eye-consciousness, ear-consciousness, etc. No single fire-entity travels from fuel to fuel; no single consciousness-entity travels from sense door to sense door or from life to life.
Question 5 of 10
How many types of consciousness does the Buddha enumerate by sense door?
Correct: B. The Buddha names six types: eye-consciousness, ear-consciousness, nose-consciousness, tongue-consciousness, body-consciousness, and mind-consciousness. Each arises at its own sense door dependent on its own conditions. The sixth, mind-consciousness, arises dependent on the mind and mental phenomena.
Question 6 of 10
What are the three conditions required for conception according to this discourse?
Correct: C. The three conditions are: (1) the mother and father come together, (2) the mother is in her season, and (3) the gandhabba — the being seeking rebirth — is present. All three must converge for an embryo to be conceived. This is one of the few passages in the Pāli canon that directly addresses the mechanics of rebirth and conception.
Question 7 of 10
What are the four nutriments that sustain living beings?
Correct: A. The four nutriments (āhāra) are: (1) physical food (kabaḷīkāra āhāra), whether gross or subtle; (2) contact (phassa); (3) mental intention (manosañcetanā); (4) consciousness (viññāṇa). This teaching shows that life is sustained not just by physical food but by the interplay of mental and physical conditions.
Question 8 of 10
In the twelve-link chain of dependent origination, what is the first link?
Correct: D. Ignorance (avijjā) is the first link: “With ignorance as condition, formations come to be.” This is not ignorance in the ordinary sense of lacking information, but a fundamental misapprehension of reality — not seeing things as they truly are. When ignorance is present, the entire chain of suffering unfolds.
Question 9 of 10
According to this discourse, how does the entire mass of suffering cease?
Correct: B. The cessation runs in reverse: “With the fading away and cessation of ignorance, formations cease. With the cessation of formations, consciousness ceases” — and so on through the chain until “aging-and-death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress cease.” The process is internal, conditional, and verifiable through practice.
Question 10 of 10
What is the central doctrinal lesson of MN 38?
Correct: C. The discourse rejects both eternalism (Sāti’s view of a transmigrating consciousness) and annihilationism (the view that consciousness simply ends). Instead, it teaches that consciousness arises dependent on conditions, is named after those conditions, and ceases when those conditions cease. This is the middle way of dependent origination — one of the most fundamental principles of the entire Buddhist teaching.

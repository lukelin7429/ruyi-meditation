Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 104

At Sāmagāma

Sāmagāmasutta

Setting
The Sakyan land near the village of Sāma; prompted by news of a rival sect’s collapse
Speakers
The Buddha and Venerable Ānanda, with the novice Cunda
Form
A practical charter for community: the roots of dispute, the kinds of issue, and how to settle them.
Length
~18 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A counterpart is preserved in the Chinese Madhyama Āgama (MĀ 196)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — list-rich, but the foundational text on Saṅgha harmony.

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-fourth

A rival teacher has just died, and his community has torn itself apart — two factions “fighting, quarrelling, and wounding each other with barbed words,” their lay followers disgusted and disillusioned. The young monk who brings the news, and the Venerable Ānanda who hears it, are alarmed by the obvious question: could this happen to us? The Buddha’s answer is this discourse — the most systematic charter in the canon for how a spiritual community keeps from coming apart.

The dead teacher is the Jain leader of the Ñātika clan (Mahāvīra), and the scene at Pāvā is drawn with unsparing realism: “You don’t understand this teaching, I do… your doctrine is refuted, save it if you can!” — until “you’d think there was nothing but slaughter going on.” The white-robed lay disciples conclude that the teaching itself must have been “poorly explained,” with “broken monument and no refuge.” It is a portrait of what a community looks like when its founder is gone and nothing holds it together.

Against this, the Buddha builds his reply in four parts: the six roots from which disputes grow; the four kinds of issue a community faces; the seven methods for settling any issue that arises; and the six warm-hearted qualities that make for “fondness and respect, harmony and unity, without dispute.” Reassuringly, he first notes that no two of his monks even differ about the teaching itself — the real danger is conduct and character, not doctrine. What keeps a community whole, in the end, is less a perfect procedure than people who treat one another with kindness, share what they have, and hold a common virtue and vision.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A rival sect’s self-destruction prompts the Buddha to set out how his own community can stay whole: dispute over the path would be far graver than dispute over rules; it grows from six roots of bad character; it is settled by seven methods suited to different issues; and it is prevented above all by six warm-hearted qualities — kindness in body, speech, and mind, shared possessions, shared virtue, and a shared view.

The cautionary scene at Pāvā

The discourse begins not with the Buddha but with a disaster. The Jain leader has died, and his followers have split into warring camps, flinging the stock taunts of the debating hall — “I stay on topic, you don’t; your doctrine is refuted; get out of it if you can!” The lay supporters draw the natural conclusion: a teaching that ends like this must have been hollow all along. The novice Cunda carries the news to Ānanda, who carries it to the Buddha with a heartfelt wish: when the Buddha is gone, may no such dispute arise among us. The whole teaching that follows is the Buddha taking that fear seriously and answering it.

What is worth disputing — and what is not

The Buddha first calms the fear, then sharpens it. Do even two of his monks differ about the things he taught from direct knowledge — the wings of awakening? No. So the core is sound. After he is gone, some might quarrel over livelihood or the monastic code — but, he says, “that is a minor matter.” The truly dangerous dispute is one “concerning the path or the practice” — that would be “for the harm and suffering of gods and humans.” The ranking is telling: rules and procedures can be disagreed over without catastrophe; it is division about the very way to liberation that tears a community at the root.

The six roots of dispute

Disputes do not really come from issues; they come from people — specifically, from six faults of character. A monk who is (1) irritable and hostile, (2) contemptuous and domineering, (3) jealous and stingy, (4) devious and deceitful, (5) of corrupt wishes and wrong view, or (6) attached to his own views, holding them tight and refusing to let go — such a monk “lacks respect for the Teacher, the teaching, and the Saṅgha,” and breeds conflict. The instruction is diagnostic and personal: look for these roots “in yourselves or others”; if found, work to abandon them; if not, guard against their arising. Notice that the sixth — clinging to one’s views — is the same fault that closed the survey of MN 102. The deepest seed of quarrel is not having a view but gripping it.

The four issues and the seven settlements

The Buddha then names the four kinds of disciplinary issue a community must handle — arising from disputes, from accusations, from offenses, and from formal business — and the seven methods for settling any of them. This is the foundation of the monastic procedure for conflict, still used today:

MethodWhat it does
Face-to-face resolutionAll sit together in harmony and settle the matter against the guidelines of the teaching.
Majority decisionIf a monastery cannot resolve it, refer it to a larger assembly.
Resolution by recollectionGranted to one who genuinely does not recall the alleged offense.
Resolution for past insanityGranted to one who acted while out of their mind and remembers nothing.
Acting on what is admittedHonest confession of a fault to a senior, with a pledge to restrain oneself.
Further penaltyFor one who lied and only admitted the offense under questioning.
Covering over as with grassFor an entrenched mutual quarrel: both sides make a blanket confession to bury the lesser offenses and start clean.

The range is humane and realistic. It protects the innocent (recollection, insanity), rewards honesty and penalises evasion (confession versus further penalty), and — most beautifully — offers an exit from quarrels too tangled to adjudicate: “covering over as with grass,” in which a respected monk from each side asks the assembly to let both parties confess their faults together and lay the whole matter to rest (excepting only grave offenses and those involving laypeople). Sometimes the wise thing is not to assign blame but to spread grass over the mud and walk on.

The six warm-hearted qualities

Procedures settle disputes once they arise; the real cure is to keep them from arising. The discourse closes with the six warm-hearted qualities (sāraṇīya-dhammā) that “make for fondness and respect, conducing to inclusion, harmony, and unity”:

  • Kindness in body toward one’s companions, “in public and in private.”
  • Kindness in speech, the same.
  • Kindness in mind, the same.
  • Sharing material things without reserve — “even the food in the alms-bowl” — held in common.
  • Shared virtue: living by the same precepts as one’s companions, “in public and in private.”
  • Shared view: holding the same noble, liberating view that “leads to the complete ending of suffering.”

The recurring phrase “in public and in private” is the heart of it: harmony is not a performance for the assembly but a consistency of kindness whether or not anyone is watching. The first three turn the three doors of action — body, speech, mind — toward one’s companions; the next binds the community through generosity; the last two give it a shared foundation in ethics and understanding. Where the Jains had only a charismatic founder and fell apart at his death, the Buddha leaves a community held together by these — able to outlast him because its bonds are woven of kindness, sharing, and a common path, not of any single person.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats the six roots and details the seven settlements. The scene at Pāvā, Ānanda’s concern, the ranking of disputes, the roots, the settlements, and the six qualities are given closely. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A community torn apart

§1–4So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the Sakyan land near Sāma village. Now the Jain leader of the Ñātika clan had just died at Pāvā, and his followers had split into two factions, “fighting, quarrelling, and wounding each other with barbed words — you’d think there was nothing but slaughter going on,” while his lay disciples grew disillusioned with a teaching “poorly explained… with broken monument and no refuge.” The novice Cunda told Venerable Ānanda, and together they went to the Buddha. Ānanda said: “Sir, I think: when the Buddha has passed away, let no such dispute arise in the Saṅgha — it would harm gods and humans.”

What is worth disputing

§5“Ānanda, do even two mendicants differ about the things I taught from direct knowledge — the wings of awakening?” “No, sir. But some might, after you pass, create a dispute over livelihood or the monastic code.” “That, Ānanda, is a minor matter. But a dispute concerning the path or the practice — that would be for the harm and suffering of gods and humans.”

The six roots of dispute

§6–11“There are six roots of dispute. A mendicant is irritable and hostile; or contemptuous and domineering; or jealous and stingy; or devious and deceitful; or of corrupt wishes and wrong view; or attached to his own views, holding them tight and refusing to let go. Such a one lacks respect for the Teacher, the teaching, and the Saṅgha, and creates disputes that harm gods and humans. If you see such a root in yourselves or others, strive to give it up; if you do not, practise so it does not arise.”

The four issues and seven settlements

§12–20“There are four kinds of disciplinary issue — arising from disputes, accusations, offenses, or business — and seven methods to settle them. Face-to-face resolution: all sit in harmony and settle the matter against the guidelines. Majority decision: refer it to a larger assembly. Resolution by recollection: granted to one who truly does not recall the offense. Resolution for past insanity: granted to one who acted while out of his mind. Acting on what is admitted: honest confession to a senior, with a pledge to restrain oneself. Further penalty: for one who only admits the offense under questioning. Covering over as with grass: when a quarrel is entrenched, a respected monk of each side asks the assembly to let both parties confess together and bury the lesser offenses, excepting grave ones and those involving laypeople.”

The six warm-hearted qualities

§21–22“Ānanda, these six warm-hearted qualities make for fondness and respect, for harmony and unity, without dispute: a mendicant treats their companions with kindness of body, of speech, and of mind, in public and in private; shares without reserve whatever they rightly gain, even the food in their bowl; lives by the same precepts as their companions, in public and in private; and holds the same noble view that leads to the complete ending of suffering. If you undertake these six, is there any criticism, large or small, you could not endure?” “No, sir.” “Then undertake them, Ānanda, for your lasting welfare and happiness.” Satisfied, Ānanda approved what the Buddha said.

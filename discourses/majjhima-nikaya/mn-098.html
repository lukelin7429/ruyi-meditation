Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 98

With Vāseṭṭha

Vāseṭṭhasutta

Setting
A forest near Icchānaṅgala, in a district crowded with eminent brahmins
Speakers
The Buddha and the two brahmin students Vāseṭṭha and Bhāradvāja
Form
A dispute settled in verse — the Buddha’s reply is almost entirely poetry.
Length
~16 minutes to read
Northern parallel
Also preserved in the Pāli Sutta Nipāta (Sn 3.9); Sanskrit fragments of a parallel survive
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — a poem on caste, deeds, and who is truly noble.

Why this discourse, ninety-eighth

Two young scholars, equally matched in the Vedas, fall out over an old question and cannot settle it: is a person a brahmin by birth, or by what they do? They bring it to the Buddha, who answers not in argument but in verse — beginning, startlingly, with biology. Look at the living world, he says: grasses, insects, fish, birds are genuinely sorted into kinds by birth. But among humans there are no such kinds. “Brahmin” is not a species; it is a name for a way of living.

The two students stand at opposite poles of the debate. Bhāradvāja holds the orthodox line: a brahmin is one of pure descent “back to the seventh paternal generation.” Vāseṭṭha holds the reformist view: a brahmin is one “ethical and accomplished in doing their duties.” Neither can convince the other, so they go to the forest near Icchānaṅgala — a district thick with the most famous brahmins of the age — to put it to the renunciant whose reputation has reached even there.

The Buddha’s reply, shared with the Sutta Nipāta, is one of the great poems of the canon. It moves in three sweeps: a survey of the natural world to show that human beings are a single species, not a ladder of breeds; a demonstration that the words farmer, trader, priest, ruler — and brahmin — all name deeds, not wombs; and a long, luminous litany redefining the “true brahmin” as the one wholly freed — of greed, of hate, of clinging, of rebirth itself. Caste names nothing real; it is kamma, action, that shapes a person and the world.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Unlike plants and animals, which are truly divided into species by birth, human beings are one kind; so “brahmin,” like “farmer” or “ruler,” names not a womb but a way of acting — and the brahmin worthy of the name is not the high-born but the one freed of greed, hate, and rebirth, “a brahmin by deeds.”

The argument from biology

The Buddha’s opening move is unexpected and quietly modern. Before saying anything about virtue, he surveys the living world: grasses and trees, insects, “ants and termites,” quadrupeds, snakes, fish, birds. Each of these, he says, is genuinely “defined by birth, for species are indeed diverse” — a beetle is born a beetle, a fish a fish, and the marks of the kind are in the body. Then the turn:

Humans are one species. “While the differences between these species are defined by birth, the differences between humans are not.” Not by hair or head, eye or ear, mouth or nose, hand or foot, colour or voice — “in specific human bodies you cannot find such distinctions.”

The point is biological and devastating to caste: a brahmin and a menial do not differ as a sparrow differs from a snake. There is no bodily mark that sorts humanity into breeds. Therefore “the distinctions among humans are spoken of by convention” (samaññā) — they are agreed labels, not facts of nature. Twenty-five centuries before the biology was known, the sutta asserts the unity of the human species and draws the moral consequence.

Every class-name is a name for deeds

If birth does not make the categories, what does? Doing. The Buddha runs through the occupations: one who keeps cattle is a farmer; one who lives by a craft, a professional; by trade, a trader; by service, a servant; by stealing, a bandit; by arms, a warrior; by priestcraft, a sacrificer; by taxing the realm, a ruler. None of these is a thing one is born; each is a thing one does. The same is true of “brahmin.” It is a description of conduct that has been frozen, by long misunderstanding, into a claim of blood.

The true brahmin: a portrait of the freed

The heart of the poem is a long litany — more than thirty verses — each ending “that’s who I declare a brahmin.” (The same verses close the Dhammapada and the Sutta Nipāta.) Here “brahmin” is filled with an entirely new content: it is the arahant, the fully freed person. The portrait gathers many strokes:

  • Having nothing and taking nothing; all fetters cut; no anxiety, detached.
  • Enduring abuse, blows, and imprisonment without anger — “patience their powerful army.”
  • Sensual pleasures slipping off them “like rain off a lotus leaf, like a mustard seed off the point of a pin.”
  • Laying aside all violence “against creatures firm and frail”; harming none.
  • Greed, hate, conceit, and contempt discarded; speech true and harmless; nothing stolen, nothing hoped for in this world or the next.
  • Knowing their own past lives, seeing the passing and rebirth of beings, defilements ended, “quenched by not grasping,” with rebirth at an end.

Every line measures the brahmin by inner freedom, not outer birth. The genuinely noble person is recognised by what they have let go of — and the highest of them, “accomplished in the three knowledges, peaceful, with rebirth ended,” is the true “Divinity” and the true “Sakka” to the wise. The word the brahmins reserved for their bloodline is handed instead to the saint.

Deeds make the world go on

The closing verses lift the teaching from social critique to its metaphysical root. “Name and clan are formulated as mere convention in the world, produced by mutual agreement” — and the misconception that birth makes the brahmin has “for a long time prejudiced those who do not understand.” What is real is kamma:

The linchpin of the chariot. “Deeds make the world go on, deeds make people go on; sentient beings are bound by deeds, like a moving chariot’s linchpin.” What you are is the running total of what you have done — and what you may yet become is open, by “fervour, restraint, and self-control.”

This is the deep reason caste cannot be a fixed essence: a person is not a substance stamped at birth but a stream shaped by action, always remaking itself. The reformist Vāseṭṭha was right — but the Buddha grounds his answer far deeper than ethics, in the law of kamma and dependent origination that “the astute regard in accord with truth.” Both students, hearing it, go for refuge for life.

Key terms

jāti — “birth” or “species.” Real among plants and animals, where the kind is marked in the body; but among humans, the Buddha says, no such birth-distinction exists.
samaññā — convention, agreed designation. The class-names of humankind — including “brahmin” — are labels produced “by mutual agreement,” not facts of nature.
a brahmin by deeds, not birth — the verdict: as “farmer” and “ruler” name what one does, so does “brahmin.” One becomes one by acting, not by being born.
“that’s who I declare a brahmin” — the refrain of the great litany, refilling the word with a new meaning: the brahmin is the arahant, freed of greed, hate, clinging, and rebirth.
kamma — action. “Deeds make the world go on… beings are bound by deeds, like a moving chariot’s linchpin.” What a person is, is shaped by what they do — never fixed at birth.
like rain off a lotus leaf — the image of one for whom sensual pleasures find no purchase, sliding away “like a mustard seed off the point of a pin.” A mark of the truly noble.

The text

The Buddha’s reply is in verse. The prose frame and the species-survey are given closely; the long litany of the “true brahmin” is represented by a selection of its verses, with the close given in full. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The dispute, brought to the Buddha

§1–7So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in a forest near Icchānaṅgala, where many famous brahmins were then residing. The students Vāseṭṭha and Bhāradvāja, walking together, fell to disputing: “How does one become a brahmin?” Bhāradvāja said: “By pure descent on both sides, back seven generations.” Vāseṭṭha said: “By being ethical and accomplished in one’s duties.” Neither could convince the other, so they went to the Buddha. Vāseṭṭha addressed him in verse: “We are both masters of the three Vedas; Bhāradvāja says one is a brahmin by birth, but I say it is by deeds. We ask you, Clear-eyed One: is one a brahmin by birth, or by deeds?”

The species of the living world

§8“I shall explain to you, accurately and in sequence, the taxonomy of living creatures — for species are indeed diverse. Know the grass and trees: though they lack self-awareness, they are defined by birth, for species are diverse. So too the insects, the ants and termites; the quadrupeds small and large; the long-backed snakes; the fish whose range is water; the birds, winged chariots in flight — each is defined by birth, for species are indeed diverse.”

But humankind is one

§9“While the differences between these species are defined by birth, the differences between humans are not. Not by hair nor head, not by ear nor eye, not by mouth nor nose, not by lips nor brow, not by shoulder, belly, back, or breast, not by hand nor foot, not by colour nor voice — none of these is defined by birth as it is for other species. In specific human bodies you cannot find such distinctions. The distinctions among humans are spoken of by convention.”

Class-names name deeds

§10“Whoever lives off keeping cattle, know them as a farmer, not a brahmin. Whoever lives off a craft, a professional; off trade, a trader; off service, a servant; off stealing, a bandit; off archery, a warrior; off priestcraft, a sacrificer; off taxing the realm, a ruler — not a brahmin.”

That’s who I declare a brahmin

§11“I do not call someone a brahmin merely for the womb they are born from; if they still have attachments, they are just someone who says ‘worthy.’

Having nothing, taking nothing:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

Having cut off all fetters, free of anxiety,
slipped their chains, detached:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

Abuse, killing, caging —
they endure these without anger;
patience their powerful army:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

Like rain off a lotus leaf,
like a mustard seed off the point of a pin,
sensual pleasures slip off them:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

They’ve laid aside violence
against creatures firm and frail,
not killing or making others kill:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

They’ve discarded greed and hate,
along with conceit and contempt:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.

They who know their past lives,
see heaven and places of loss,
and have attained the end of rebirth:
that’s who I declare a brahmin.”

Convention, and the truth of deeds

§12–13“Name and clan are formulated as mere convention in the world, produced by mutual agreement. For a long time this misconception has prejudiced those who do not understand, who declare one a brahmin by birth. You are not a brahmin by birth, nor a non-brahmin by birth: you are a brahmin by your deeds, a non-brahmin by your deeds — a farmer, trader, servant, ruler, all by deeds. The astute, seeing dependent origination, regard deeds in accord with truth. Deeds make the world go on; beings are bound by deeds, like a moving chariot’s linchpin. By fervour, restraint, and self-control — that is how one becomes a brahmin, this is the supreme brahmin.”

Refuge

§14“Excellent, worthy Gotama! As if righting the overturned, or lighting a lamp in the dark… We go for refuge to the worthy Gotama, to the teaching, and to the Saṅgha. From this day forth, may he remember us as lay followers gone for refuge for life.”

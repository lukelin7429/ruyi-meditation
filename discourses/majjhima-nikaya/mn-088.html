Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 88

The Imported Cloth

Bāhitikasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī; a conversation on the bank of the Aciravatī river
Speakers
Venerable Ānanda and King Pasenadi of Kosala
Form
A king’s question drawn out into a definition — what conduct the wise blame, and what they praise — followed by a graceful exchange over a gift.
Length
~9 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No counterpart survives in the Chinese Āgamas
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — accessible; a clear lexicon of what makes conduct good or bad.

Why this discourse, eighty-eighth

King Pasenadi stops Venerable Ānanda on the road and asks a simple-sounding question: would the Buddha ever do anything that ascetics and brahmins would fault? Ānanda’s answer adds a single, decisive word — not what fools fault, but what the wise do — and from that small correction unfolds one of the canon’s clearest accounts of what actually makes conduct blameworthy or blameless, and the measure by which the truly perfected person can be known.

The setting is warm. The king, riding out, spots Ānanda and so wants to talk that he sends a messenger ahead, dismounts to approach on foot, and invites the elder to sit by the river. He offers his own elephant-rug; Ānanda keeps his own seat. Then comes the question, and the king cannot even finish phrasing it — but Ānanda completes it for him, adding the qualifier that the king himself most values: the judgement that matters is not that of incompetent fools who praise and blame without examining, but that of “competent and intelligent people” who judge after scrutiny.

From there Ānanda lays out a chain of plain equivalences — what the wise blame is unskilful conduct, which is to say blameworthy, hurtful, and productive of suffering; and what they praise is its mirror. The test, in the end, is consequence and character: does an act harm oneself, others, or both, and does it make the unskilful grow and the skilful wane — or the reverse? The discourse closes with a small comedy of generosity, as the king tries to give the contented monk a length of fine imported cloth, and is gently refused until he finds the one argument a monk cannot decline.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The conduct the wise blame is the unskilful — that is, the blameworthy, the hurtful, the suffering-producing: whatever harms oneself, others, or both, and makes unskilful qualities grow and skilful ones wane — and the Buddha is one who has given up all of it and possesses all its opposite; and what matters is not the praise or blame of fools, but the examined judgement of the discerning.

The one word that completes the question

The king asks whether the Buddha would do anything “faulted by ascetics and brahmins.” Ānanda answers: he would not do what is faulted by sensible ascetics and brahmins — and the king is delighted, because that single addition is exactly the distinction he himself holds dear. “I do not believe the praise or criticism of incompetent fools, spoken without examining, is the important thing,” he says; “rather, the praise or criticism of competent and intelligent people, after examining and scrutinising.” This is a small but important point about the moral life. The world is full of approval and disapproval, and most of it is noise — the reflexive likes and dislikes of people who have not looked carefully. To live well is not to chase praise or flee blame as such, but to care only for the verdict of those who actually understand. A wise person’s blame is worth more than a thousand fools’ applause; and the Buddha’s conduct is such that no wise observer could fault it.

The chain of equivalences

Asked what conduct the wise blame, Ānanda answers through a descending ladder of synonyms, each unpacking the last — and the same in reverse for the praiseworthy:

What the wise blameWhat the wise praise
unskilful conductskilful conduct
… which is blameworthy… which is blameless
… which is hurtful… which is pleasing
… which results in suffering… which results in happiness

These are not four different things but one thing seen from four sides: the unskilful is the blameworthy is the hurtful is the suffering-producing. The chain dissolves any gap between “morally wrong” and “actually harmful”: in the Dhamma, an act is not blamed by arbitrary decree but because of what it genuinely does. Bad conduct is not bad because a rule forbids it; it is forbidden because it hurts. This holds across body, speech, and mind alike — the same analysis runs through all three doors of action.

The final test: harm and growth

At the bottom of the ladder lies the actual criterion, stated twice over. Conduct “results in suffering” when it “leads to hurting yourself, hurting others, and hurting both, and makes unskilful qualities grow while skilful qualities decline.” And conduct “results in happiness” when it “leads to pleasing yourself, others, and both, and makes skilful qualities grow while unskilful decline.” Two measures are joined here. The first is consequence: whom does this act harm or benefit — oneself, others, or both? The second is character: what does this act do to the mind that does it, growing or shrinking its skilful qualities? An act is fully good only when it passes both tests — when it harms no one and also leaves the doer better, more generous, more clear. This double criterion — outward effect and inward effect together — is the heart of Buddhist ethics, and it is why the moral life and the meditative life are not two things: every action either purifies or pollutes the very mind one is trying to free.

The Buddha, who has done it completely

Twice the king asks whether the Buddha praises giving up all the unskilful and embracing all the skilful, and twice Ānanda gives the same quiet, total answer: “The Realized One has given up all unskilful things and possesses skilful things.” The Buddha is not merely a teacher of this standard but its complete fulfilment — one in whom the whole of the unskilful has been abandoned and the whole of the skilful is present. This is what makes him beyond the fault of even the most discerning critic: there is simply nothing harmful left in him to find. The standard the king was asking about is, in the Buddha, a finished fact.

The gift, and contentment that overflows

The closing scene is a small masterpiece of social grace. So pleased is the king that he wishes to give Ānanda a fitting reward — an elephant, a horse, a village — but knows none of these suits a monk. So he offers the bāhitika, a length of fine imported cloth sent to him by King Ajātasattu, sixteen cubits by eight. Ānanda declines: “Enough, great king. My three robes are complete.” Here contentment meets generosity, and at first they seem to block each other. But the king finds the resolution, with a simile from the very river beside them: as the Aciravatī, swollen by mountain rains, overflows both its banks, so let Ānanda make new robes from this cloth and pass his old robes on to his fellow monks — “in this way my gift will come to overflow.” And Ānanda accepts. The lesson is delicate: the contented monk need not refuse a gift outright, for a gift accepted and then shared onward multiplies into the whole community’s benefit. Contentment is not the rejection of all receiving, but freedom from grasping — and such freedom can let a gift flow through it to others rather than pool in oneself.

The Buddha’s praise of Ānanda

Ānanda reports the conversation and presents the cloth to the Buddha, who says to the monks: “King Pasenadi is lucky, so very lucky, to get to see Ānanda and pay homage to him.” It is a generous word about the great attendant, who throughout this discourse teaches in the Buddha’s absence with perfect fidelity — completing the king’s question, unfolding the analysis clearly, declining and then rightly accepting the gift. The discourse is, among other things, a portrait of Ānanda as a worthy representative of the Teacher: when the king meets him, he meets the Dhamma, and the good fortune is the king’s.

Key terms

viññū — the “sensible, discerning” person. The one word Ānanda adds: the Buddha never does what the wise would blame. The verdict that matters is the examined one, not the fools’ noise.
akusala — “unskilful.” The root of the chain: unskilful = blameworthy = hurtful = suffering-producing. One fact seen from four sides; an act is blamed because of what it does, not by arbitrary decree.
the double criterion — conduct is bad if it harms (self, others, or both) and grows the unskilful in the mind; good if it benefits and grows the skilful. Outward effect and inward effect, joined.
three doors — the same analysis runs through body, speech, and mind. Conduct is not only outward deed; thought too is action, judged by the same standard of harm and growth.
“given up all, possesses all” — the Buddha is the standard fulfilled: he has abandoned the whole of the unskilful and possesses the whole of the skilful — nothing harmful left for any critic to find.
bāhitika — the “imported cloth”: the king’s gift. Contentment (“my three robes are complete”) meets generosity, resolved when the gift is accepted to be shared onward — “so my donation will overflow.”

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The meeting, the analysis of conduct, and the gift are given in full; the parallel treatment of speech and mind is condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The king stops Ānanda

§1–8So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī. King Pasenadi, riding out, saw Venerable Ānanda and sent word asking him to wait, then invited him to the bank of the Aciravatī. He offered his elephant-rug; Ānanda kept his own seat. Then the king asked, “Honourable Ānanda, would the Buddha engage in any conduct — of body, speech, or mind — that is faulted by ascetics and brahmins?” “No, great king — the Buddha would not engage in conduct faulted by sensible ascetics and brahmins.”

The verdict that matters

§9“Incredible, sir! I couldn’t fully express my question, yet your answer completed it. I do not believe the praise or criticism of incompetent fools, spoken without examining, is the important thing — but rather the praise or criticism of competent, intelligent people, after examining and scrutinising.”

What the wise blame

§10–13“But what bodily conduct is faulted by the wise?” “Unskilful conduct.” “And what is that?” “Blameworthy conduct.” “And that?” “Hurtful conduct.” “And that?” “Conduct that results in suffering — that leads to hurting yourself, hurting others, and hurting both, and makes unskilful qualities grow while skilful qualities decline. That is faulted by the wise.” The same is said of speech and of mind. “Does the Buddha praise giving up all these unskilful things?” “Great king, the Realized One has given up all unskilful things and possesses skilful things.”

What the wise praise

§14–17“And what bodily conduct is not faulted by the wise?” “Skilful conduct — blameless, pleasing, resulting in happiness: conduct that leads to pleasing yourself, others, and both, and makes skilful qualities grow while unskilful decline.” The same of speech and mind. “Does the Buddha praise embracing all these skilful things?” “Great king, the Realized One has given up all unskilful things and possesses skilful things.”

The imported cloth

§18–20“Incredible, sir! I am so delighted that, were an elephant, a horse, or a village suitable for you, I would give it. But knowing these are not, may you accept this imported cloth, sent to me by King Ajātasattu — sixteen cubits by eight.” “Enough, great king. My three robes are complete.” “Sir, as the Aciravatī, swollen by mountain rains, overflows both banks, so make new robes from this cloth and pass your old robes to your fellow monks — thus my gift will overflow. Please accept it.” So Ānanda accepted.

The Buddha’s praise

§21–22The king took his leave, and Ānanda went to the Buddha, reported the conversation, and presented the cloth. The Buddha said to the monks, “King Pasenadi is lucky, so very lucky, to get to see Ānanda and pay homage to him.”

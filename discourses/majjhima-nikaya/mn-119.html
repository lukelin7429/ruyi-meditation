Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 119

Mindfulness of the Body

Kāyagatāsatisutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, following a mendicants’ conversation the Buddha overhears and completes
Speakers
The Buddha, prompted by an unfinished discussion among the mendicants
Form
Fourteen ways to be mindful of the body, the four absorptions folded into body-mindfulness, and ten concrete benefits
Length
~17 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A close counterpart survives in the Chinese Madhyama Āgama (MĀ 81, “Discourse on Mindfulness of the Body”)
Difficulty
★★★★★ — long, vivid, and cumulative; the single most complete portrait of body-based practice in the Majjhima

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-nineteenth

The mendicants can’t finish their own sentence. Sitting together after almsround, they start marveling at how “fruitful and beneficial” the Buddha has said mindfulness of the body is — and their conversation is “left unfinished” when he walks in. What follows is the Buddha finishing it for them: the single most complete inventory of body-based practice anywhere in the Majjhima Nikāya, fourteen distinct ways of being mindful of the body, folded together with the four absorptions themselves, and capped with a list of ten benefits so concrete they read almost like a job description for what this practice is actually for.

Where MN 118 traced one practice (the breath) upward through mindfulness to awakening, this discourse does something different: it gathers everything the body can teach — posture, anatomy, decay, and finally jhāna itself — under a single heading, then argues that this one heading, properly developed, already contains every other quality needed for realization.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Fourteen distinct practices — from the breath, through posture and anatomy and the decay of a corpse, to the four absorptions themselves pervading the body with bliss — all count as “mindfulness of the body,” a single practice the Buddha says already includes every other skillful quality needed for awakening, shields the mind from Māra, and yields ten concrete benefits culminating in liberation.

An unfinished conversation

The frame is almost comic in its ordinariness. After the meal, mendicants sit together in the assembly hall marveling: “It’s incredible, reverends, it’s amazing, how the Buddha has said that mindfulness of the body, when developed and cultivated, is very fruitful and beneficial.” They don’t get further than that — the Buddha himself comes out of retreat, asks what they were discussing, and simply answers the question they hadn’t finished asking. Everything that follows is the actual content behind a claim the mendicants could only gesture at.

Fourteen ways to be mindful of the body

Each practice below ends in the identical refrain: meditating “diligent, keen, and resolute,” memories and thoughts tied to lay life are given up, and the mind “becomes stilled internally; it settles, unifies, and becomes immersed in samādhi.” The refrain matters as much as the list — whatever the object, the destination is the same interior stillness.

  1. The breath — the same opening steps as MN 118’s first tetrad: knowing the breath as heavy or light, experiencing the whole breath, stilling it.
  2. The four postures — knowing “I am walking,” standing, sitting, or lying down, whatever posture the body is actually in.
  3. Situational awareness (sampajañña) — clear comprehension while going out and returning, dressing, eating, using the toilet, speaking or staying silent: ordinary activity done with full presence, laying the groundwork for meditation rather than being meditation itself.
  4. The 31 anatomical parts — head hair, nails, teeth, skin, flesh, bones, organs, fluids, examined the way a person with clear eyes sorts a mixed bag of grains into its kinds.
  5. The four elements — earth, water, fire, and air within the body, examined the way a butcher lays out a cow’s carcass at the crossroads, cut into pieces — the body seen as impersonal parts rather than a whole.
  6. Nine charnel-ground contemplations — a corpse observed through progressive stages of decay (bloated and festering; devoured by animals; a flesh-and-blood skeleton; a blood-smeared skeleton; a bare skeleton; scattered bones; whitened bones; a heap of bones; bones crumbled to dust), each time compared directly to one’s own body: “this body is also of that same nature.”

The commentary is careful to note that clear comprehension while going to the toilet does not mean entering deep meditation there — these earlier practices, in the spirit of the Gradual Training, prepare the ground rather than constituting absorption themselves. The charnel-ground contemplations, though their object is deliberately unpleasant, are said to lead to immersion precisely because a mind freed of desire toward the body discovers a lightness that itself becomes the basis for stillness.

The body pervaded by jhāna

The discourse then does something unusual: it folds the four absorptions themselves into “mindfulness of the body,” describing each one not by its mental factors but by how it physically saturates the body, each with its own simile:

AbsorptionWhat pervades the bodySimile
Firstrapture and bliss born of seclusiona ball of bath powder kneaded with water until moist through and through, yet nothing drips out
Secondrapture and bliss born of immersiona deep lake with no inlet, filled entirely by its own spring welling up from within
Thirdbliss free of rapturelotuses growing fully submerged, drenched with cool water from tip to root
Fourthpure, bright minda person sitting wrapped head to foot in white cloth

Each simile tracks the same progression — from tactile bodily sensation, to interior bliss and light, to a final pervasive purity — making explicit that the “body” being pervaded is not just muscle and bone but the entire field of felt experience.

The ocean that gathers every river

Having laid out all fourteen practices and the four absorptions, the Buddha makes a striking claim: “Anyone who has developed and cultivated mindfulness of the body includes all of the skillful qualities that play a part in realization — just as anyone who brings to mind the great ocean includes all of the streams that run down into it.” Mindfulness of the body is not merely the first of the four establishments of mindfulness; it is the ground in which feelings, mind, and principles all become visible in the first place.

Māra’s opportunity

Three pairs of similes then make the stakes vivid. Without this practice, a mendicant is like wet clay that a thrown stone sinks straight into, a withered log that catches fire easily from a drill-stick, an empty jar that can simply be filled — “Māra finds a vulnerability and gets an opportunity.” With this practice developed, the same mendicant is like a solid hardwood door that a soft ball of string cannot penetrate, a green sappy log that friction cannot ignite, a jar already filled to the brim that no more water can enter — Māra “cannot find a vulnerability.”

A mind fit for anything

But being closed to Māra does not mean being closed to insight. A further set of similes — a full jar that pours freely from any side when tipped, a walled pond that releases water the moment any wall is opened, a master charioteer who can drive his chariot wherever and whenever he wishes — shows that a mendicant who has developed mindfulness of the body can direct the mind to realize, by insight, whatever can be realized: nothing is out of reach.

Ten benefits

The discourse closes with a list of concrete rewards for cultivating this one practice: mastery over desire and discontent; mastery over fear and dread; the physical endurance to bear cold, heat, hunger, thirst, pests, harsh criticism, and even severe pain; the four absorptions attained “when they want, without trouble or difficulty”; and finally the six higher knowledges — psychic power, clairaudience, reading others’ minds, recollecting past lives, seeing beings pass on according to their deeds, and, last of all, “the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom” through the ending of the defilements. The list moves, in other words, from ordinary emotional resilience all the way to arahantship — ten rungs on a single ladder, all resting on one practice.

Key terms

kāyagatāsati — “mindfulness of the body”: not one technique but a family of fourteen, unified by the same refrain of stilling and immersion, and by the claim that it grounds every other quality needed for awakening.
sampajañña — “situational awareness” or clear comprehension: full presence brought to ordinary daily activity, preparing the mind rather than constituting meditation itself.
the nine charnel-ground stages — a corpse observed decaying from bloated to bone-dust, each stage compared directly to one’s own body; an unpleasant object that, once desire toward the body fades, yields an unexpected lightness leading to samādhi.
the ocean simile — mindfulness of the body “includes all of the skillful qualities that play a part in realization,” the way the ocean gathers every river that flows into it.
“Māra finds a vulnerability” — six paired similes (wet clay/hardwood door, dry log/green log, empty jar/full jar) contrasting a mind undefended by body-mindfulness with one that is.
the ten benefits — mastery of desire, fear, and physical hardship; the four absorptions at will; and the six higher knowledges, culminating in the ending of the defilements.

The text

The discourse in full: the frame story, all fourteen ways of being mindful of the body, the four absorptions pervading the body, the ocean simile, the Māra similes, the similes of flexible realization, and the ten benefits. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

An unfinished conversation

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove. After the meal, returning from almsround, several mendicants sat together in the assembly hall and this discussion came up: “It’s incredible, reverends, it’s amazing, how the Buddha has said that mindfulness of the body, when developed and cultivated, is very fruitful and beneficial.” But their conversation was left unfinished. Then the Buddha came out of retreat, sat down, and asked, “Mendicants, what were you sitting talking about just now? What conversation was left unfinished?” So the mendicants told him. The Buddha said:

Fourteen ways to be mindful of the body

§3–4“And how is mindfulness of the body developed and cultivated to be very fruitful and beneficial? It’s when a mendicant — gone to a wilderness, or to the root of a tree, or to an empty hut — sits down cross-legged, sets their body straight, and brings mindfulness to the present. Just mindful, they breathe in. Mindful, they breathe out. Breathing in heavily they know: ‘I’m breathing in heavily’… they practice: ‘I’ll breathe in experiencing the whole body’… ‘I’ll breathe in stilling the physical process.’ As they meditate like this — diligent, keen, and resolute — memories and thoughts tied to lay life are given up. Their mind becomes stilled internally; it settles, unifies, and becomes immersed in samādhi. That’s how a mendicant develops mindfulness of the body.

§5–6Furthermore, when walking a mendicant knows ‘I am walking’; when standing, sitting, or lying down, they know it too — whatever posture the body is in, they know it… That too is how a mendicant develops mindfulness of the body.

Furthermore, a mendicant acts with situational awareness when going out and coming back, when looking ahead and aside, when bending and extending the limbs, when bearing robes and bowl, when eating and drinking, when urinating and defecating, when walking, standing, sitting, sleeping, waking, speaking, and keeping silent… That too is how a mendicant develops mindfulness of the body.

§7–8Furthermore, a mendicant examines their own body, up from the soles of the feet and down from the tips of the hairs, wrapped in skin and full of many kinds of filth: ‘In this body there is head hair, body hair, nails, teeth, skin, flesh, sinews, bones, bone marrow, kidneys, heart, liver, diaphragm, spleen, lungs, intestines, mesentery, undigested food, feces, bile, phlegm, pus, blood, sweat, fat, tears, grease, saliva, snot, synovial fluid, urine.’ It’s as if a person with clear eyes were to open a bag filled with various kinds of grains — fine rice, wheat, mung beans, peas, sesame, ordinary rice — and examine the contents, distinguishing each kind. In the same way, a mendicant examines their own body… That too is how a mendicant develops mindfulness of the body.

Furthermore, a mendicant examines their own body, whatever its placement or posture, according to the elements: ‘In this body there is the earth element, the water element, the fire element, and the air element.’ It’s as if a deft butcher or their apprentice were to kill a cow and sit at the crossroads with the meat cut into chops. In the same way, a mendicant examines their own body according to the elements… That too is how a mendicant develops mindfulness of the body.

§9–17Furthermore, suppose a mendicant were to see a corpse discarded in a charnel ground — dead for one, two, or three days, bloated, livid, and festering — and compare it with their own body: ‘This body is also of that same nature, that same kind, and cannot go beyond that.’ Or a corpse being devoured by crows, hawks, vultures, dogs, and jackals. Or a skeleton with flesh and blood, held together by sinews; or without flesh but smeared with blood; or rid of flesh and blood entirely, still held by sinews; or bones scattered in every direction — a hand-bone here, a foot-bone there, a skull elsewhere; or white bones, the color of shells; or bones heaped in a pile after a year or more; or bones rotted and crumbled to powder. Each time comparing it with their own body: ‘This body is also of that same nature, that same kind, and cannot go beyond that.’ As they meditate like this — diligent, keen, and resolute — memories and thoughts tied to lay life are given up, and the mind becomes stilled, settled, unified, and immersed in samādhi. That too is how a mendicant develops mindfulness of the body.

The body pervaded by jhāna

§18–21Furthermore, a mendicant, quite secluded from sensual pleasures and unskillful qualities, enters and remains in the first absorption, and drenches, steeps, fills, and spreads their body with the rapture and bliss born of seclusion — there’s no part of the body not spread with it. It’s like a deft bathroom attendant kneading bath powder with water into a ball, soaked and saturated through inside and out, yet no moisture oozes out.

Furthermore, as placing the mind and keeping it connected are stilled, a mendicant enters the second absorption, and pervades the body with the rapture and bliss born of immersion. It’s like a deep lake fed by spring water, with no inlet from any direction, filled entirely by the cool stream welling up from within — there’s no part of the lake not spread through with it.

Furthermore, with the fading away of rapture, a mendicant enters the third absorption, equanimous, mindful, and aware, and pervades the body with bliss free of rapture. It’s like lotuses growing and thriving fully submerged in a pool, drenched with cool water from tip to root — there’s no part of them not soaked through.

Furthermore, with the giving up of pleasure and pain, a mendicant enters the fourth absorption, without pleasure or pain, with pure equanimity and mindfulness, and sits spreading their body through with a pure, bright mind. It’s like someone sitting wrapped from head to foot in white cloth — there’s no part of the body not spread over with it. As they meditate like this, memories and thoughts tied to lay life are given up, and the mind becomes immersed in samādhi. That too is how a mendicant develops mindfulness of the body.

The ocean that gathers every river

§22Anyone who has developed and cultivated mindfulness of the body includes all of the skillful qualities that play a part in realization. Anyone who brings into their mind the great ocean includes all of the streams that run down into it. In the same way, anyone who has developed mindfulness of the body includes all of the skillful qualities that play a part in realization.

Māra’s opportunity

§23–28When a mendicant has not developed mindfulness of the body, Māra finds a vulnerability and gets an opportunity — like a heavy stone ball thrown at wet clay finds an entry, like a person drilling a withered log can light a fire, like a person with a load of water can pour it into an empty jar. But when a mendicant has developed mindfulness of the body, Māra cannot find a vulnerability — like a light ball of string thrown at a hardwood door cannot enter, like drilling a green, sappy log cannot light a fire, like a person cannot pour more water into a jar already full to the brim.

A mind fit for anything

§29–31When a mendicant has developed mindfulness of the body, they extend the mind to realize by insight each and every thing that can be realized by insight, and are capable of realizing it, since each and every one is within range. It’s like a jar full to the brim: tip it on any side and water pours out. It’s like a walled lotus pond, full to the brim: open the wall on any side and water pours out. It’s like a chariot harnessed to thoroughbreds at a crossroads, with a master charioteer who can drive it out and back wherever and whenever he wishes.

Ten benefits

§32–43You can expect ten benefits when mindfulness of the body has been cultivated, developed, and practiced, made a vehicle and a basis, kept up, consolidated, and properly implemented. They prevail over desire and discontent whenever they arise. They prevail over fear and dread whenever they arise. They endure cold, heat, hunger, thirst, the touch of flies and mosquitoes, wind, sun, reptiles, rude criticism, and sharp, severe, life-threatening physical pain. They get the four absorptions whenever they want, without trouble or difficulty. They wield the many kinds of psychic power. With clairaudience purified and superhuman, they hear both human and heavenly sounds, near and far. They understand the minds of other beings, having encompassed them with their own mind. They recollect many kinds of past lives, with features and details. With clairvoyance purified and superhuman, they see beings passing away and being reborn, understanding how they pass on according to their deeds. And they realize the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom in this very life, having realized it with their own insight due to the ending of the defilements. You can expect these ten benefits when mindfulness of the body has been cultivated, developed, and practiced.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said.

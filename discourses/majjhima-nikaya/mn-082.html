Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 82

With Raṭṭhapāla

Raṭṭhapālasutta

Setting
The land of the Kurus, at the town of Thullakoṭṭhika; later, King Koravya’s deer park
Speakers
The Buddha, Venerable Raṭṭhapāla, his parents and former wives, and King Koravya
Form
A renunciation epic in three movements — a hard-won going-forth, a homecoming that refuses wealth and love, and a royal dialogue on the four truths that drove it.
Length
~20 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 132 (Madhyama-āgama 132)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — a long narrative; the four summaries of the Dhamma at its centre are its enduring teaching.

Why this discourse, eighty-second

A young man who has everything — wealth, family, youth, health — gives it all up, and no one can understand why. His parents refuse to release him; he starves himself until they relent. Years later he comes home an awakened one, turns away a heap of gold and his own former wives, and finally tells a baffled king the four plain truths about the world that made staying impossible. It is the Majjhima’s great portrait of renunciation from fullness, not from loss.

Raṭṭhapāla is the only son of the foremost family of Thullakoṭṭhika. Hearing the Buddha, he is seized by a single thought: the holy life cannot be lived “utterly full and pure, like a polished shell” by one still at home. The Buddha will not ordain him without his parents’ consent — and his parents, who have everything invested in their beloved, pampered heir, refuse three times over. So Raṭṭhapāla lies down on the bare ground and declares, “I will either die right here or go forth,” refusing food to the seventh meal. Only when his friends warn the parents that they will lose him either way — to death now, or to the robe with a chance of seeing him again — do they consent. He ordains, and soon becomes an arahant. The Buddha, the tradition records, would name him foremost of those who went forth out of pure faith.

The second movement is the homecoming, and it is unsparing. At his own father’s door he is abused as a “fake ascetic” and given nothing; he eats a servant’s discarded porridge by a wall. His father heaps up the family gold and dresses the former wives in their finest to lure him back. Raṭṭhapāla’s answers — dump the gold in the Ganges; “I do not live the holy life for nymphs, sisters” — and his stark verses on the painted body show a man wholly free of what once held him. The third movement, with King Koravya, supplies the reasons: the four summaries of the Dhamma — the world is unstable, unsheltering, unowned, and unsatisfiable — truths visible to anyone, that turn a life of plenty into a reason to leave.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A young man of every advantage renounces it all, not because life has failed him but because he has seen the four plain truths the Buddha summarised — the world is unstable and swept away, without shelter or saviour, without an owner (you leave it all behind), and forever wanting, a slave to craving — and these, not poverty or age or loss, are the real reasons the wise go forth.

Renunciation that had to fight for itself

The going-forth is hard-won, and the discourse lingers on the cost. The requirement of parental consent is no formality: the Buddha simply will not ordain a son over his parents’ refusal. And the parents’ refusal is wholly human — not villainy but love: “You are our only child, dear to us, pampered, raised in comfort; you know nothing of suffering. Even when you die we will lose you against our wishes — how can we let you go while you still live?” Against this, Raṭṭhapāla has only his body to stake, and he stakes it: he lies on the bare ground and stops eating. His resolve is total — “die here or go forth” — and it is what finally moves his parents, persuaded by his friends that a living monk is better than a dead son. The episode honours both the depth of family love and the deeper pull that can outweigh it. Renunciation is not shown as easy or cold; it costs, and it costs those who love the renunciant too.

The homecoming, and what it reveals

When Raṭṭhapāla returns — now an arahant, keeping his promise to visit — the contrast between his old world and his freedom is laid bare. He does not announce himself; he simply walks the alms-round, and comes to his father’s house, where he is not recognised and is abused as one of “these shavelings, these fake ascetics” who stole the family’s son. He accepts a servant’s three-day-old porridge, destined for the bin, and eats it against a wall — the heir of the leading clan, perfectly content. “We who have gone forth have no home,” he tells his astonished father. The two great hooks of the household life are then dangled before him and find nothing to catch on:

  • Wealth. His father reveals the heaped family gold — maternal, paternal, ancestral — and begs him to “return to a lesser life, enjoy wealth, make merit.” Raṭṭhapāla’s reply: “Load it on a cart and dump it in the Ganges — it will bring you nothing but sorrow.”
  • Love. His former wives, dressed to their old allure, clasp his feet and ask, “What are the nymphs like, for whom you live the holy life?” — assuming only some heavenly reward could explain his leaving. “I do not live the holy life for the sake of nymphs, sisters,” he answers; and at the single word “sisters” they faint.

He then speaks the famous verses on the painted body: it is “a fancy puppet, a body built of sores… bones encased in skin, made pretty by its clothes”; rouged feet and powdered face “may beguile a fool, but not a seeker of the far shore.” And the image that seals it: “The hunter laid his snare, but the deer did not spring the trap. I’ve eaten the bait and now I go, leaving the trapper to lament.” He takes the family’s food but not its hook.

The king’s puzzle: why this man?

King Koravya, who had long admired Raṭṭhapāla, comes to see him and poses a sharp question. In his experience, people renounce because of one of four kinds of decay (pārijuñña) — the push-factors of misfortune:

The four decaysRaṭṭhapāla’s case
old agehe is young, in his prime
sicknesshe is healthy, rarely ill
loss of wealthhe is heir to the leading clan
loss of relativeshe has abundant family and friends

Raṭṭhapāla has suffered none of them. “So what,” the king asks, “did you know or see or hear that made you go forth?” The question is the discourse’s hinge: it forces the distinction between renouncing because life has gone wrong and renouncing because one has seen something true. Raṭṭhapāla went forth not from any decay but from insight — from fullness, exactly as the Buddha had.

The four summaries of the Dhamma

His answer is the heart of the sutta: the Buddha’s four summaries of the Dhamma (dhammuddesa), and to each Raṭṭhapāla draws from the king himself the evidence that proves it:

SummaryHow the king himself confirms it
1. The world is unstable, swept awayAt eighty the king, once a peerless warrior, now “means to step one place, but his foot goes elsewhere.”
2. The world has no shelter, no saviourHis armies cannot defend him against disease; no friend can take a share of his pain.
3. The world has no owner — you leave it all and pass onHis vast treasure cannot follow him; others will spend it while he fares on by his deeds.
4. The world is wanting, insatiable, a slave to cravingMaster of all Kuru, he would still conquer kingdom after kingdom in every direction, never satisfied.

The brilliance of the method is that Raṭṭhapāla proves nothing by argument; he simply asks the king about his own life, and the king supplies each truth in his own words, exclaiming each time, “How well said by the Buddha!” These four truths are not esoteric doctrine; they are the visible condition of every human life, the king’s included. To see them clearly is already to have a reason to seek the deathless — and that seeing, not any misfortune, is why Raṭṭhapāla left.

The closing verses

Raṭṭhapāla ends in verse, drawing the threads together. The rich hoard and die unsatisfied; a king who rules “from sea to sea” still yearns for the farther shore; death takes all and “riches don’t follow you… nor children, wife, wealth, nor kingdom.” Therefore “wisdom is much better than wealth, since by wisdom one reaches consummation in this very life.” And the final images: sensual pleasures “appearing in disguise disturb the mind”; “as fruit falls from a tree, so the sons of Manu fall, young and old, when the body breaks up.” Seeing this, he says, “I went forth, O King — it is a sure bet that the ascetic life is better.” The discourse leaves us not with a rejection of life but with a clear-eyed reckoning of it: precisely because the world is unstable, unsheltering, unowned, and unsatisfiable, the one thing worth pursuing is the freedom that none of those four can touch.

Key terms

dhammuddesa — “summaries of the Dhamma”: the four pithy truths Raṭṭhapāla gives as his reason for going forth — the world is unstable, unsheltering, unowned, and a slave to craving.
pārijuñña — “decay, loss”: the four worldly misfortunes (age, sickness, lost wealth, lost relatives) that usually drive people to renounce. Raṭṭhapāla has none — he renounces from insight, not loss.
saddhā-pabbajita — “gone forth out of faith.” The tradition names Raṭṭhapāla foremost of those who renounced from pure faith — not pushed by hardship but pulled by conviction.
“like a polished shell” — Raṭṭhapāla’s image for the holy life lived “utterly full and pure” — which, he judges, cannot be lived complete while one is still at home.
the fancy puppet — Raṭṭhapāla’s verse-image of the adorned body: “bones encased in skin, made pretty by clothes”; rouge and braids “beguile a fool, but not a seeker of the far shore.”
the snare and the bait — “The hunter laid his snare, but the deer didn’t spring the trap; I’ve eaten the bait and now I go.” He accepts his family’s food, but is not caught by their wealth or love.

The text

The full discourse, substantially abridged — this is among the longer narratives in the collection. The going-forth, the homecoming, the king’s puzzle, and the four summaries are given in full; the threefold repetitions and the long closing verses are condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The going-forth

§1–13So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was wandering in the land of the Kurus and came to the town of Thullakoṭṭhika. Hearing him teach, Raṭṭhapāla — only son of the leading clan — thought, “The holy life can’t be lived utterly full and pure, like a polished shell, by one who stays at home,” and asked to go forth. “Do you have your parents’ permission?” “No, sir.” “Buddhas do not ordain a child without it.” His parents refused three times: “You are our only child, dear, pampered — even your death would take you from us against our will; how can we let you go while you live?” So Raṭṭhapāla lay on the bare ground: “I will die here or go forth,” and refused food to the seventh meal. At last his friends persuaded the parents — “let him go, and you may see him again” — and they consented, on condition he visit them after. He was ordained, and soon, “living alone, withdrawn, diligent,” became one of the perfected.

The homecoming

§16–22Returning to keep his promise, Raṭṭhapāla walked the alms-round to his own father’s house, where — unrecognised — he was abused: “Our dear son was made to go forth by these fake ascetics!” He received nothing, and ate a servant’s discarded porridge by a wall. The servant knew him; his parents came. “Why eat stale porridge? Come home!” “We who have gone forth have no home, householder.” He accepted the next day’s meal. His father heaped up the family gold and dressed the former wives in their finest. At the meal he revealed the gold: “This is your fortune — return to lay life, enjoy wealth, make merit!” “Householder, load it on a cart and dump it in the Ganges — it will bring you nothing but sorrow.”

“I do not live the holy life for nymphs”

§23–26His former wives clasped his feet: “What are the nymphs like, master, for whom you live the holy life?” “I do not live the holy life for the sake of nymphs, sisters.” At the word “sisters” they fainted away. Having eaten, Raṭṭhapāla spoke this verse, standing:

“See this fancy puppet, a body built of sores, diseased, obsessed over, in which nothing lasts at all… it is bones encased in skin, made pretty by its clothes. Rouged feet and powdered face may beguile a fool, but not a seeker of the far shore… The hunter laid his snare, but the deer didn’t spring the trap. I’ve eaten the bait and now I go, leaving the trapper to lament.”

Then he went to King Koravya’s deer park and sat at the root of a tree.

The king’s puzzle: the four decays

§27–34King Koravya came to pay homage. “Worthy Raṭṭhapāla, there are four kinds of decay that make people go forth — old age, sickness, loss of wealth, loss of relatives. But you are young, healthy, heir to the leading clan, rich in kin. You have none of these. So what did you know or see or hear that made you go forth?”

The four summaries of the Dhamma

§35–41“Great king, the Buddha taught four summaries of the Dhamma; it was after knowing them that I went forth. The world is unstable and swept away — as you yourself, once a peerless warrior at twenty-five, now at eighty mean to step one place and your foot goes elsewhere. The world has no shelter and no saviour — for your armies cannot guard you from sickness, and no friend can take a share of your pain. The world has no owner; you must leave it all behind and pass on — for your treasure cannot follow you; others will spend it while you fare on by your deeds. The world is wanting, insatiable, a slave to craving — for though you rule all Kuru, you would still conquer kingdom after kingdom in every direction, and never be satisfied.” To each the king exclaimed, “How well said by the Buddha! For so the world indeed is.”

Closing verses

§42Then Raṭṭhapāla spoke at length in verse: the rich hoard and die unsatisfied; a king who rules “from sea to sea” still yearns for the farther shore; death takes all, and “riches don’t follow you when you die, nor children, wife, wealth, nor kingdom.” “Therefore wisdom is much better than wealth, since by wisdom one reaches consummation in this very life… Seeing danger in sensual stimulations, I went forth, O King. As fruit falls from a tree, so the sons of Manu fall, young and old, when the body breaks up. Seeing this too, I went forth — it is a sure bet that the ascetic life is better.”

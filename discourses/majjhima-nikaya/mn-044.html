Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 44

The Shorter Series of Questions and Answers

Cūḷavedallasutta

Setting
Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the Squirrels’ Feeding Ground
Speakers
Bhikkhunī Dhammadinmā (answering) and the lay follower Visākha (asking)
Form
Doctrinal dialogue — a bhikkhunī teaches her former husband on identity, the eightfold path, the three formations, feeling, underlying tendencies, and the attainment of cessation. The Buddha endorses her answers without alteration.
Length
~18 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 210 (Madhyama-āgama 210)
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — demanding. Like its twin MN 43, this is a philosophical dialogue requiring familiarity with the aggregates and the path. The definitions are precise and reward careful attention.

Why this discourse, forty-fourth

MN 44 is one of the most remarkable discourses in the Pāli canon — not only for its doctrinal content but for who delivers it. The teacher here is Bhikkhunī Dhammadinmā, a nun whom the Buddha declared foremost among bhikkhunīs who are Dhamma-speakers. The questioner is the lay follower Visākha — her former husband.

Before ordaining, Dhammadinmā was Visākha’s wife. When Visākha attained the stage of non-returner after hearing the Buddha teach, he offered her their household and possessions. She refused, seeing that what a non-returner relinquishes must be worth relinquishing, and she went forth as a bhikkhunī. She quickly attained arahantship. Now Visākha, a non-returner, comes to his former wife — now an arahant — and sits at her feet to learn.

The discourse forms a pair with MN 43. Both are vedalla — dialogues that proceed by question and answer. In MN 43, two monks discuss topics at the highest philosophical level. In MN 44, a nun teaches a layman — a different social configuration that the canon preserves without comment, as though it were entirely natural. It is. The teaching authority in early Buddhism rested on realization, not on gender or ordained status.

At the end, Visākha reports Dhammadinmā’s answers to the Buddha. The Buddha says: “Dhammadinmā is wise. If you had asked me these questions, I would have answered in exactly the same way.” This is the strongest endorsement a teacher can receive in the canon.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Through a series of precise questions and answers, Dhammadinmā defines identity as the five aggregates affected by clinging, maps the noble eightfold path onto the three trainings, distinguishes the three formations, analyzes feeling and its underlying tendencies, and describes the attainment of cessation.

The structure of the discourse

TopicWhat Dhammadinmā teaches
1. IdentityIdentity (sakkāya) is the five aggregates affected by clinging: form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness.
2. Identity viewIdentity view arises when an uninstructed ordinary person regards any of the five aggregates as self, as belonging to self, as containing self, or as being within self — twenty types in all.
3. The noble eightfold pathThe path is not included in the three trainings, but the three trainings are included in the path. Right speech, right action, and right livelihood belong to the training in virtue. Right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration belong to the training in concentration. Right view and right intention belong to the training in wisdom.
4. ConcentrationConcentration is one-pointedness of mind. Its sign is the four establishments of mindfulness. Its equipment is the four right efforts. The cultivation of these is the development of concentration.
5. The three formationsIn-and-out breathing is the bodily formation. Applied and sustained thought is the verbal formation. Perception and feeling are the mental formation.
6. The attainment of cessationOn entering cessation, verbal formation ceases first, then bodily formation, then mental formation. On emerging, mental formation arises first, then bodily formation, then verbal formation.
7. Emergence from cessationThe mind on emerging inclines to seclusion, leans toward seclusion, slopes toward seclusion.
8. FeelingThree feelings: pleasant, painful, and neither-pleasant-nor-painful. Pleasant feeling is pleasant in persisting and painful in changing. Painful feeling is the reverse. Neutral feeling is pleasant when known with understanding and painful when not.
9. Underlying tendenciesThe underlying tendency to lust underlies pleasant feeling. The underlying tendency to aversion underlies painful feeling. The underlying tendency to ignorance underlies neutral feeling. But not in every case — an arahant does not have these tendencies.
10. The counterpart of identity viewThe noble eightfold path is the counterpart of identity view — identity view ceases through the practice of the path.

Dhammadinmā: a woman’s teaching authority

Dhammadinmā’s appearance as the teacher in MN 44 is not presented as exceptional. The discourse does not introduce her with qualifications or justifications; it simply records that Visākha went to see her, asked his questions, and received authoritative answers. The Buddha’s endorsement at the end confirms what the discourse already assumes: that a bhikkhunī’s teaching has the same authority as a bhikkhu’s when grounded in realization.

This matters for understanding early Buddhism. The canon preserves relatively few discourses with female teachers — not because women did not teach, but because the monastic editorial tradition that compiled the canon was predominantly male. MN 44 survives as evidence that the teaching role of bhikkhunīs was recognized and recorded when the discourse was too doctrinally important to omit.

The three formations: a key analysis

Dhammadinmā’s analysis of the three formations (saṅkhāra) is one of the most cited passages in the entire Majjhima Nikāya. She defines:

  • Bodily formation (kāyasaṅkhāra): in-and-out breathing. Breathing is bodily because it is “a thing bound up with the body” — it belongs to the body and conditions the body.
  • Verbal formation (vacīsaṅkhāra): applied thought (vitakka) and sustained thought (vicāra). One first thinks and considers, and then breaks into speech — thought conditions speech.
  • Mental formation (cittasaṅkhāra): perception (saññā) and feeling (vedanā). These are “things bound up with the mind” — they condition the mind’s state.

This analysis is crucial for understanding the attainment of cessation. The three formations cease in a specific order: verbal formation first (thought stops), then bodily formation (breathing stops), then mental formation (perception and feeling stop). On emerging, they arise in reverse order. This sequence explains why the attainment of cessation is not death: the formations cease in an orderly way and resume in an orderly way, with vitality and warmth preserved throughout.

Feeling and underlying tendencies

Dhammadinmā’s analysis of feeling and its underlying tendencies (anusaya) is subtle and clinically important. She teaches that each of the three feelings has a corresponding underlying tendency: lust underlies pleasant feeling, aversion underlies painful feeling, and ignorance underlies neutral feeling. But — and this is the critical qualifier — “not in every case.”

A monk abandons the underlying tendency to lust in response to pleasant feeling by meditating on its impermanence. A monk abandons the underlying tendency to aversion in response to painful feeling by meditating on its cessation. And a monk abandons the underlying tendency to ignorance in response to neutral feeling by cultivating understanding of that very feeling. The underlying tendencies are not automatic; they are habitual patterns that can be broken through practice.

Key terms

sakkāya — identity; the existing body. Defined as the five aggregates affected by clinging (pañc’upādānakkhandhā). Not a self but the totality of what one identifies with.
sakkāyadiṭṭhi — identity view. The deeply ingrained assumption that one of the five aggregates is self, belongs to self, contains self, or is within self. Abandoned at stream-entry.
saṅkhāra — formation; conditioned thing; volitional activity. In this discourse, used specifically for the three formations: bodily (breathing), verbal (thought), and mental (perception and feeling).
vedanā — feeling; feeling-tone. Three kinds: pleasant, painful, and neither-pleasant-nor-painful. Each has a characteristic impermanence and a corresponding underlying tendency.
anusaya — underlying tendency; latent disposition. Not a conscious thought but a dormant tendency that is activated by experience. The three discussed here: lust (rāgānusaya), aversion (paṭighānusaya), and ignorance (avijjānusaya).
saññāvedayitanirodha — the cessation of perception and feeling. The deepest meditative attainment, as in MN 43. Dhammadinmā adds the crucial detail of the order in which formations cease and resume.
vedalla — a genre of discourse that proceeds by question and answer. MN 44 is the “shorter” (cūḷa) vedalla, paired with MN 43, the “greater” (mahā) vedalla.
sikkhā — training. The three trainings: virtue (sīla), concentration (samādhi), and wisdom (paññā). Dhammadinmā maps the eight path factors onto these three trainings.

The text

MN 44 is a dense doctrinal dialogue. Questions and key answers are given in full; some repetitive formulae are condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Setting

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the Squirrels’ Feeding Ground.

§2Then the lay follower Visākha went to Bhikkhunī Dhammadinmā, bowed, sat down to one side, and said:

Identity

§3“Noble lady, ‘identity, identity’ is said. What is identity as declared by the Buddha?”

“Friend Visākha, these five aggregates affected by clinging are declared by the Buddha to be identity. That is: the aggregate of form affected by clinging, the aggregate of feeling affected by clinging, the aggregate of perception affected by clinging, the aggregate of formations affected by clinging, and the aggregate of consciousness affected by clinging. These five aggregates affected by clinging are declared by the Buddha to be identity.”

Visākha approved and rejoiced in her answer. Then he asked a further question:

Identity view

§4“Noble lady, ‘identity view, identity view’ is said. What is identity view?”

“Here, friend Visākha, an uninstructed ordinary person regards form as self, or self as possessing form, or form as in self, or self as in form. They regard feeling as self … perception as self … formations as self … consciousness as self, or self as possessing consciousness, or consciousness as in self, or self as in consciousness. This is called identity view.”

§5“And how does identity view not come to be?”

“Here, a well-instructed noble disciple does not regard form as self, or self as possessing form, or form as in self, or self as in form. They do not regard feeling … perception … formations … consciousness as self. This is how identity view does not come to be.”

The noble eightfold path and the three trainings

§6“Noble lady, what is the noble eightfold path?”

“It is: right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration.”

§7“Is the noble eightfold path conditioned or unconditioned?”

“The noble eightfold path is conditioned.”

§8“Are the three trainings included in the noble eightfold path, or is the noble eightfold path included in the three trainings?”

“The noble eightfold path is not included in the three trainings. Rather, the three trainings are included in the noble eightfold path. Right speech, right action, and right livelihood: these are included in the training of virtue. Right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration: these are included in the training of concentration. Right view and right intention: these are included in the training of wisdom.”

Concentration

§9“Noble lady, what is concentration? What is its sign? What is its equipment? What is its development?”

“Friend Visākha, one-pointedness of mind is concentration. The four establishments of mindfulness are its sign. The four right efforts are its equipment. The cultivation, development, and practice of these is the development of concentration.”

The three formations

§10“Noble lady, how many formations are there?”

“There are three formations, friend Visākha: the bodily formation, the verbal formation, and the mental formation.”

“But what is the bodily formation? What is the verbal formation? What is the mental formation?”

“In-and-out breathing is the bodily formation. Applied thought and sustained thought are the verbal formation. Perception and feeling are the mental formation.”

§11“But why is in-and-out breathing the bodily formation? Why are applied and sustained thought the verbal formation? Why are perception and feeling the mental formation?”

“Friend Visākha, in-and-out breathing is bodily — it is a thing bound up with the body. That is why in-and-out breathing is the bodily formation. First one thinks and considers, and then one breaks into speech. That is why applied and sustained thought are the verbal formation. Perception and feeling are mental — they are things bound up with the mind. That is why perception and feeling are the mental formation.”

The attainment of cessation

§12“Noble lady, how does the attainment of the cessation of perception and feeling come about?”

“Friend Visākha, a monk who is attaining the cessation of perception and feeling does not think: ‘I will attain cessation,’ or ‘I am attaining cessation,’ or ‘I have attained cessation.’ Rather, the mind has been previously developed in such a way that it leads to that state.”

§13“When a monk is attaining the cessation of perception and feeling, which formations cease first: the bodily, the verbal, or the mental?”

“When a monk is attaining cessation, the verbal formation ceases first, then the bodily formation, then the mental formation.”

§14“When a monk is emerging from the attainment of cessation, which formations arise first?”

“The mental formation arises first, then the bodily formation, then the verbal formation.”

§15“When a monk has emerged from cessation, how many contacts touch him?”

“Three contacts: emptiness contact, signless contact, and undirected contact.”

§16“When a monk has emerged from cessation, to what does the mind incline?”

“The mind inclines to seclusion, leans toward seclusion, slopes toward seclusion.”

Feeling

§17“Noble lady, how many kinds of feeling are there?”

“There are three kinds of feeling: pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and neither-pleasant-nor-painful feeling.”

§18“What is pleasant feeling? What is painful feeling? What is neither-pleasant-nor-painful feeling?”

“Whatever is felt bodily or mentally as pleasant and agreeable — that is pleasant feeling. Whatever is felt bodily or mentally as painful and disagreeable — that is painful feeling. Whatever is felt bodily or mentally as neither agreeable nor disagreeable — that is neither-pleasant-nor-painful feeling.”

§19“In what way is pleasant feeling pleasant, and in what way painful? In what way is painful feeling painful, and in what way pleasant? In what way is neutral feeling pleasant, and in what way painful?”

“Pleasant feeling is pleasant when it persists and painful when it changes. Painful feeling is painful when it persists and pleasant when it changes. Neither-pleasant-nor-painful feeling is pleasant when known with understanding and painful when not known with understanding.”

Underlying tendencies

§20“What underlying tendency underlies pleasant feeling? What underlying tendency underlies painful feeling? What underlying tendency underlies neutral feeling?”

“The underlying tendency to lust underlies pleasant feeling. The underlying tendency to aversion underlies painful feeling. The underlying tendency to ignorance underlies neither-pleasant-nor-painful feeling.”

§21“Does the underlying tendency to lust underlie all pleasant feeling? Does the underlying tendency to aversion underlie all painful feeling? Does the underlying tendency to ignorance underlie all neutral feeling?”

“No, friend Visākha, not in every case.”

Dhammadinmā explains: a monk who meditates on the impermanence of pleasant feeling abandons the underlying tendency to lust. A monk who meditates on the cessation of painful feeling abandons the underlying tendency to aversion. A monk who meditates with understanding on neutral feeling abandons the underlying tendency to ignorance.

The counterpart of identity view

§22“Noble lady, what is the counterpart of identity view?”

“The noble eightfold path is the counterpart of identity view: right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration.”

The Buddha’s endorsement

§23Then the lay follower Visākha approved and rejoiced in Bhikkhunī Dhammadinmā’s answer. He rose from his seat, bowed, and keeping her on his right, he left.

§24Then Visākha went to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and reported his entire conversation with Bhikkhunī Dhammadinmā.

§25“Dhammadinmā is wise, Visākha. Dhammadinmā has great wisdom. If you had asked me those same questions, I would have answered in exactly the same way that Dhammadinmā answered. That is the meaning of those things. Remember them in that way.”

Self-check quiz

Ten questions. Click an answer to see immediate feedback. No score is recorded — this is for your own checking.

Question 1 of 10
Who is the teacher in MN 44?
Correct: C. Bhikkhunī Dhammadinmā, declared by the Buddha as foremost among bhikkhunīs who are Dhamma-speakers, answers all the questions. The questioner is the lay follower Visākha, her former husband. The Buddha appears only at the end to endorse her answers.
Question 2 of 10
How does Dhammadinmā define “identity” (sakkāya)?
Correct: A. Identity is not a self but the five aggregates affected by clinging (pañc’upādānakkhandhā). It is what one takes to be “me” or “mine” — the totality of psychophysical experience that one identifies with. This definition is foundational: identity view arises precisely when one regards any of these aggregates as self.
Question 3 of 10
How does Dhammadinmā relate the noble eightfold path to the three trainings?
Correct: D. The three trainings are included in the eightfold path, not the reverse. Right speech, action, and livelihood fall under virtue. Right effort, mindfulness, and concentration fall under concentration. Right view and intention fall under wisdom. The eightfold path is the more comprehensive framework.
Question 4 of 10
What is the “bodily formation,” according to Dhammadinmā?
Correct: B. In-and-out breathing is the bodily formation (kāyasaṅkhāra) because it is “a thing bound up with the body.” This definition is crucial for understanding the attainment of cessation: when the bodily formation ceases, breathing stops — yet the monk is not dead, because vitality and warmth remain.
Question 5 of 10
In what order do the formations cease when entering the attainment of cessation?
Correct: A. The verbal formation (applied and sustained thought) ceases first — thinking stops. Then the bodily formation (breathing) ceases. Finally the mental formation (perception and feeling) ceases. On emerging, they arise in the reverse order: mental first, then bodily, then verbal. This orderly sequence is what distinguishes cessation from death.
Question 6 of 10
How does Dhammadinmā describe the impermanence of pleasant feeling?
Correct: C. Dhammadinmā’s analysis is precise: pleasant feeling is genuinely pleasant while it lasts, but becomes painful when it changes. Painful feeling is genuinely painful while it persists, but becomes pleasant when it changes. Neutral feeling is pleasant when understood and painful when not understood. This is not a denial that pleasure exists but an analysis of its impermanent nature.
Question 7 of 10
What underlying tendency does Dhammadinmā say underlies neutral feeling?
Correct: B. Ignorance (avijjā) underlies neutral feeling because neutral feeling is subtle and hard to notice — one tends to be unaware of it. Lust underlies pleasant feeling (one wants it to continue). Aversion underlies painful feeling (one wants it to stop). But Dhammadinmā adds the critical qualifier: “not in every case” — a practitioner can break these patterns.
Question 8 of 10
Does the underlying tendency to lust underlie all pleasant feeling?
Correct: D. Dhammadinmā’s “not in every case” is one of the most important qualifications in the discourse. The underlying tendencies are not automatic. A practitioner who understands the impermanence of pleasant feeling does not activate the underlying tendency to lust. The tendencies are habitual, not inevitable — and practice is what breaks them.
Question 9 of 10
What does Dhammadinmā say is the counterpart of identity view?
Correct: A. The noble eightfold path is the “counterpart” (paṭipakkha) of identity view — the practice that directly opposes and removes it. Identity view ceases through the cultivation of the path. This connects the discourse’s opening topic (identity) to its closing one, creating a complete circle.
Question 10 of 10
What does the Buddha say about Dhammadinmā’s answers?
Correct: C. The Buddha’s endorsement is unqualified: “Dhammadinmā is wise. Dhammadinmā has great wisdom. If you had asked me those same questions, I would have answered in exactly the same way.” This is the strongest form of validation in the canon — the teacher’s answers are declared identical to what the Buddha himself would have said.

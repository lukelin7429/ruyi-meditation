Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 33

The Greater Discourse on the Cowherd

Mahāgopālakasutta

Setting
Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speaker
The Buddha, addressing the monks
Form
An extended simile — eleven qualities of a skilled cowherd are mapped one-to-one onto eleven qualities of a monk who grows in the Dhamma
Length
~12 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 185 (Madhyama-āgama 185)
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — no technical argument; the simile is concrete and systematic. An excellent entry point for understanding how the Buddha uses occupational metaphors to teach monastic training.

Why this discourse, thirty-third

The Buddha takes one of ancient India’s most common occupations — tending cattle — and turns it into a complete curriculum for monastic growth. A cowherd who lacks any of eleven skills will fail to maintain and expand the herd; a monk who lacks the corresponding eleven qualities will fail to grow in the Dhamma.

The discourse’s power lies in the completeness and concreteness of the analogy. Each of the eleven cowherd skills maps to a specific monastic quality, and the correspondence is vivid enough to remember. The result is something rare in the canon: a mnemonic checklist that is also a teaching, and a teaching that is also a portrait of the complete practitioner.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Just as a cowherd who possesses eleven qualities can maintain and expand the herd, a monk who possesses the corresponding eleven qualities can grow in the Dhamma — and just as a cowherd who lacks them will see the herd dwindle, a monk who lacks them will decline.

The eleven qualities: cowherd to monk

The discourse’s structure is unusually systematic. The Buddha first lists the eleven qualities a cowherd needs, then maps each one to a monastic quality. The following table captures the full correspondence:

#The cowherd’s skillThe monk’s quality
1Knows forms — recognizes each cow by appearanceUnderstands the four elements (earth, water, fire, air) that make up bodily form
2Knows marks — identifies cattle by their distinguishing marksUnderstands that a fool is known by their actions (deeds of body, speech, and mind) and a wise person likewise
3Picks off flies’ eggs — removes parasites before they hatchRemoves sensual thoughts as soon as they arise, before they can take hold
4Tends wounds — dresses and bandages injuriesGuards the sense doors — does not let contact with sense objects produce unwholesome states
5Makes fires — smokes out insects and pestsTeaches the Dhamma to others as learned and understood
6Knows the ford — knows where to cross the river safelyGoes to learned monks and asks: “How is this? What does this mean?” — they open up what is hidden
7Knows where to drink — leads cattle to clean waterFinds joy and gladness when the Dhamma and Vinaya are taught
8Knows the road — knows which paths are safeKnows the noble eightfold path
9Skilled in pastures — knows where good grazing isKnows the four establishments of mindfulness as the proper pasture for a monk
10Milks without leaving a remainder — leaves enough for the calvesKnows moderation in accepting gifts from lay supporters — neither greedy nor negligent
11Shows extra care for the bulls — honors the fathers and leaders of the herdShows extra respect for senior monks who are long-ordained, experienced, and the community’s elders

The negative frame comes first

Unusually, the Buddha begins with the negative case: a cowherd who lacks these eleven qualities cannot maintain the herd. He then runs through the positive case. This rhetorical move — showing the failure first, then the success — has a pedagogical purpose: the listener recognizes the cost of each missing skill before hearing what the skill actually is. The structure creates urgency. You don’t just learn the eleven qualities; you learn what happens without each one.

Why a cowherd?

Cattle-herding was one of the most widely understood occupations in the Buddha’s world. A cowherd’s work was tangible, observable, and required genuine skill. The analogy works because it is not lofty — it is earthy. The Buddha consistently chose occupations his audience knew from daily life (potters, charioteers, archers, carpenters) to make abstract training concrete. The cowherd simile is the most fully developed of these.

Relation to MN 34

MN 34 (The Shorter Discourse on the Cowherd) is a companion piece, but it takes a completely different approach. Where MN 33 maps cowherd skills to monastic qualities, MN 34 tells a story: a foolish cowherd tries to cross his cattle at a dangerous ford, and the herd is scattered; a wise cowherd crosses at the right place, leading the strongest bulls first, then the trained cattle, then the calves and weak cows. The Buddha uses this to describe how beings cross the flood of saṃsāra — the most developed practitioners go first and open the way.

Three questions Western students often ask

“Is this discourse just a list?” It is a list — and deliberately so. The Buddha organized his teachings for oral memorization. The eleven qualities form a systematic checklist: a monk can review them and identify what is missing. The checklist format is itself a teaching method: it says that spiritual growth is not one dramatic insight but eleven concurrent capacities, each needing attention.

“Why does ‘making fires’ correspond to teaching the Dhamma?” The correspondence is not arbitrary. A cowherd makes fires to smoke out insects and pests that would harm the cattle. A monk “makes fires” by teaching the Dhamma: the act of teaching disperses the pests of ignorance and unwholesome states — in others and in oneself. Teaching is not just output; it is an act of clarification that burns away confusion.

“What does ‘milking without leaving a remainder’ mean for a monk?” A greedy cowherd milks every drop and the calves starve. A negligent cowherd leaves too much and the owner loses out. The middle way: take enough, leave enough. For a monk, this means accepting lay offerings with moderation — neither burdening supporters with excessive demands nor refusing what is freely given. It is the ethics of dependence: a monk depends on the lay community and must be careful not to exhaust that goodwill.

Key terms

gopālaka — cowherd; cattle-keeper. The central simile of the discourse. The Pāli literally means “cow-protector” — the cowherd’s role is not mere herding but guardianship.
rūpaṃ jānāti — knows forms. The first cowherd skill: recognizing each animal by its physical appearance. For the monk: understanding the four great elements (earth, water, fire, air) that constitute material form.
lakkhaṇaṃ jānāti — knows marks. The second skill: distinguishing cattle by their brands or distinguishing features. For the monk: recognizing the moral character of a person by the “marks” of their actions.
āsaṭikaṃ hāreti — picks off flies’ eggs. Removing parasites before they can develop. For the monk: removing unwholesome thoughts at the moment of arising, before they proliferate.
vaṇaṃ paṭicchādeti — tends wounds; bandages injuries. For the monk: guarding the sense doors so that contact does not become a wound through which defilements enter.
dhūmaṃ kattabbaṃ jānāti — knows when to make fires (smoke). For the monk: teaching the Dhamma to others — creating the “smoke” that drives out the pests of ignorance.
titthaṃ jānāti — knows the ford. For the monk: approaching learned monks who can open up what is obscure — finding the crossing-place in the teachings.
ariya aṭṭhaṅgika magga — the noble eightfold path. The “road” that the monk must know, corresponding to the cowherd’s knowledge of safe paths.
cattāro satipaṭṭhānā — the four establishments of mindfulness (body, feelings, mind, and mind-objects). The monk’s “pasture” — the domain where growth happens.

The text

MN 33 is a systematic simile discourse. The Buddha presents the negative case (a cowherd lacking eleven qualities) and then the positive case, mapping each cowherd skill to a monastic quality. Repetitive enumerations are condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The cowherd who fails

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery.

§2There the Buddha addressed the monks: “Monks!” “Venerable sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

“Monks, a cowherd with eleven qualities is incapable of maintaining and expanding a herd of cattle. What eleven? It’s when a cowherd doesn’t know forms, is unskilled in marks, doesn’t pick off flies’ eggs, doesn’t tend wounds, doesn’t make fires, doesn’t know the ford, doesn’t know where to drink, doesn’t know the road, isn’t skilled in pastures, milks dry without leaving a remainder, and doesn’t show extra respect to the bulls who are fathers and leaders of the herd.

A cowherd with these eleven qualities is incapable of maintaining and expanding a herd of cattle.

§3In the same way, a monk with eleven qualities is incapable of growing in this teaching and training.

The Buddha maps each of the eleven lacks to a monk’s deficiency. A monk who doesn’t understand forms (the four elements), doesn’t know marks (judging character by actions), doesn’t pick off flies’ eggs (remove sensual thoughts at arising), doesn’t tend wounds (guard the senses), doesn’t make fires (teach the Dhamma), doesn’t know the ford (consult learned monks), doesn’t know where to drink (find no joy in the teachings), doesn’t know the road (the noble eightfold path), isn’t skilled in pastures (the four establishments of mindfulness), milks dry (immoderately accepts gifts), and doesn’t show extra respect for senior monks — such a monk is incapable of growth. Each quality is explained in turn.

The cowherd who succeeds

§4“A cowherd with eleven qualities is capable of maintaining and expanding a herd of cattle. What eleven? It’s when a cowherd knows forms, is skilled in marks, picks off flies’ eggs, tends wounds, makes fires, knows the ford, knows where to drink, knows the road, is skilled in pastures, milks without leaving a remainder, and shows extra respect to the bulls who are fathers and leaders of the herd.

A cowherd with these eleven qualities is capable of maintaining and expanding a herd of cattle.

The monk who grows

§5In the same way, a monk with eleven qualities is capable of growing in this teaching and training. What eleven?

§6And how does a monk know forms? It’s when a monk truly understands that all form is made up of the four great elements and form derived from the four great elements. That is how a monk knows forms.

§7And how does a monk know marks? It’s when a monk truly understands that a fool is marked by their deeds and a wise person is also marked by their deeds. That is how a monk knows marks.

§8And how does a monk pick off flies’ eggs? It’s when a monk doesn’t tolerate a sensual thought that has arisen. They give it up, get rid of it, eliminate it, and obliterate it. They don’t tolerate a thought of ill will that has arisen … They don’t tolerate a thought of cruelty that has arisen … They don’t tolerate any bad, unwholesome qualities that have arisen. They give them up, get rid of them, eliminate them, and obliterate them. That is how a monk picks off flies’ eggs.

§9And how does a monk tend wounds? It’s when a monk, on seeing a form with the eye, doesn’t grasp at features and details. If the faculty of sight were left unguarded, bad, unwholesome qualities of desire and aversion would become overwhelming. For this reason they guard the faculty of sight. They do the same with the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind. That is how a monk tends wounds.

§10And how does a monk make fires? It’s when a monk teaches others the Dhamma in detail as they have learned and understood it. That is how a monk makes fires.

§11And how does a monk know the ford? It’s when a monk from time to time goes to the monks who are very learned, who have memorized the tradition, and who are experts in the teaching, the monastic law, and the summaries. He asks them: “How is this, sir? What does this mean?” Those venerables open up what is hidden, clarify what is obscure, and resolve doubt on many doubtful matters. That is how a monk knows the ford.

§12And how does a monk know where to drink? It’s when a monk, whenever the teaching and training proclaimed by the Realized One is being taught, finds joy and gladness in the meaning, joy and gladness in the teaching. That is how a monk knows where to drink.

§13And how does a monk know the road? It’s when a monk truly understands the noble eightfold path. That is how a monk knows the road.

§14And how is a monk skilled in pastures? It’s when a monk truly understands the four establishments of mindfulness. That is how a monk is skilled in pastures.

§15And how does a monk milk without leaving a remainder? It’s when faithful laypeople invite a monk to accept robes, almsfood, lodgings, and medicines. That monk knows moderation in accepting. That is how a monk milks without leaving a remainder.

§16And how does a monk show extra respect to the bulls who are fathers and leaders of the herd? It’s when a monk sets up acts of body, speech, and mind filled with loving-kindness toward those senior monks who are long-ordained, long gone forth, fathers and leaders of the community. That is how a monk shows extra respect to the bulls who are fathers and leaders of the herd.

§17A monk with these eleven qualities is capable of growing in this teaching and training.”

That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the monks approved what the Buddha said.

Self-check quiz

Ten questions. Click an answer to see immediate feedback. No score is recorded — this is for your own checking.

Question 1 of 10
How many qualities does the Buddha enumerate for a cowherd (and correspondingly for a monk)?
Correct: B. The discourse is built on a systematic analogy of eleven qualities. This number is distinctive — most lists in the Pāli canon use round numbers (4, 5, 8, 10). The oddness of eleven itself makes the list memorable.
Question 2 of 10
What does “knowing forms” correspond to for a monk?
Correct: C. Just as a cowherd recognizes each cow by its physical appearance, a monk understands that all bodily form is composed of the four great elements (mahābhūta) and form derived from them. This is foundational material analysis.
Question 3 of 10
What does “picking off flies’ eggs” correspond to for a monk?
Correct: A. Flies’ eggs become maggots if left alone. Sensual thoughts become obsessions if not removed at the moment of arising. The vividness of the image is the teaching: catch them early, before they hatch.
Question 4 of 10
What does “tending wounds” correspond to for a monk?
Correct: D. An untended wound on a cow lets infection in. An unguarded sense door lets defilements in. The cowherd bandages the wound; the monk practices sense restraint (indriyasaṃvara) so that contact with sense objects does not become a pathway for desire and aversion.
Question 5 of 10
Why does the Buddha present the negative case (the cowherd who fails) before the positive case?
Correct: B. By showing the failure first, the Buddha ensures that the listener feels the stakes. You don’t just learn what to do; you first see what happens if you don’t do it. The negative frame creates urgency — a pedagogical technique the Buddha uses in several discourses.
Question 6 of 10
What does “knowing the ford” correspond to for a monk?
Correct: C. A ford is a safe crossing-place in a river. For a monk, the “ford” is the wisdom of senior practitioners who can show the way through difficult teachings. The image captures the humility of asking: you do not cross the river by guessing, but by going to someone who knows where the riverbed is firm.
Question 7 of 10
What is the monk’s “pasture,” according to the discourse?
Correct: A. The satipaṭṭhāna (establishments of mindfulness) are the monk’s proper grazing ground. Just as cattle grow strong in good pasture, a monk grows in wisdom by cultivating mindfulness of body, feelings, mind, and mind-objects. This connection between “pasture” and satipaṭṭhāna appears in several discourses.
Question 8 of 10
What does “milking without leaving a remainder” mean for a monk?
Correct: D. A cowherd who milks every drop starves the calves; one who leaves too much cheats the owner. The monk likewise must find moderation: accepting enough to sustain the practice, but not so much as to exhaust the goodwill of lay supporters. This is the ethics of dependence — a cornerstone of monastic life.
Question 9 of 10
What does “making fires” correspond to for a monk?
Correct: C. A cowherd makes fires to smoke out insects and pests. A monk “makes fires” by teaching: the act of explaining the Dhamma drives out the pests of ignorance and confusion — in others and, through the clarification involved, in oneself.
Question 10 of 10
How does MN 33 relate to MN 34 (The Shorter Discourse on the Cowherd)?
Correct: B. MN 33 is systematic: a point-by-point analogy. MN 34 is narrative: a story about river-crossing that becomes a metaphor for how beings cross the flood of saṃsāra. Together they show two ways the Buddha uses the same pastoral imagery — as a checklist and as a parable.

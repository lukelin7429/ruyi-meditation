Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 56

With Upāli

Upālisutta

Setting
Nāḷandā, in Pāvārika’s mango grove
Speakers
The Buddha, the Jain ascetics Dīgha Tapassī and the Jain leader Nātaputta, and the wealthy lay-disciple Upāli
Form
Debate and conversion — a doctrinal dispute over whether body or mind bears the greater moral weight, in which a Jain layman sent to refute the Buddha is won over and becomes a stream-enterer.
Length
~22 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 133 (Madhyama-āgama 133, “Upāli”), with Sanskrit fragments
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — long but vivid and dramatic. Its doctrinal core — the primacy of intention over the physical act — is one of the clearest in the canon.

Why this discourse, fifty-sixth

A wealthy and clever Jain layman boasts that he will go and drag the Buddha around in debate “like a strong man dragging a sheep” — and comes home a disciple. MN 56 is at once a gripping human drama and the clearest statement of the deepest difference between the Buddha’s teaching and the Jainism of his day: does the body or the mind carry the greatest moral weight?

The Jains, led by Niganṭha Nātaputta (the teacher we know as Mahāvīra), spoke of three “rods” (daṇḍa) — instruments of harm by body, speech, and mind — and held the physical rod the most blameworthy. The Buddha spoke not of “rods” but of “deeds” (kamma), and held the mental deed — intention — the weightiest of all. This is not a quibble over words. It is the whole question of whether morality lives in outward acts or in the heart that drives them. The Buddha’s answer — that cetanā, intention, is the real engine of karma — reshapes the entire understanding of action.

What makes the discourse unforgettable is how the Buddha wins. He never grasps for the convert. When Upāli, overwhelmed, asks to go for refuge, the Buddha tells him to “act after careful consideration,” and even urges him to keep supporting the Jain ascetics his family has long fed. It is precisely this refusal to claim him that wins Upāli completely. The discourse ends in a cascade of drama: a graduated teaching that opens the “Dhamma-eye,” a closed gate, the simile of the dyed monkey, a spontaneous hymn of a hundred praises — and the Jain leader, unable to bear the honor paid to the Buddha, coughing up hot blood on the spot.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Against the Jain doctrine that the “bodily rod” is the most blameworthy, the Buddha argues that mental action — intention — carries the greatest moral weight; and the wealthy Jain Upāli, sent to refute him, is so won over that he becomes a stream-enterer, while the Buddha’s refusal to grasp for the convert only deepens his faith.

“Rods” and “deeds”

The discourse opens with a precise terminological clash. Asked how he classifies wrongdoing, the Jain envoy Dīgha Tapassī explains that his teacher speaks not of “deeds” but of “rods” (daṇḍa) — literally cudgels, instruments of violence — of which there are three: bodily, verbal, and mental. And of the three, the Jains hold the bodily rod the most blameworthy. The Buddha, by contrast, speaks of “deeds” (kamma), and of the three deeds — bodily, verbal, mental — he holds the mental deed the weightiest. The very choice of words is revealing: a “rod” is an external instrument; a “deed” is an act with an intention behind it. The two schools are not merely ranking the same three things differently; they are looking at morality through two different lenses.

The four arguments that the mind weighs most

When Upāli comes to debate, the Buddha does not lecture him; he asks questions that lead Upāli, step by step, to contradict his own position — drawing the conclusion out of Jain doctrine itself:

  1. The dying ascetic. A scrupulous Jain who refuses cold water (believing it contains life) and dies is, by Jain teaching, reborn among “mind-bound gods” — because “he died with mental attachment.” So it was the mind that determined his fate.
  2. The unintentional killing. A Jain perfectly restrained in conduct still crushes tiny creatures while walking. Jain teaching says such unintentional acts are “not very blameworthy” — while intentional ones are. And intention, Upāli admits, belongs to the mental rod. His own doctrine makes mind decisive.
  3. The power of will. One man with a sword could not slaughter all of Nāḷandā in a day — but a sage with psychic mastery could “reduce it to ashes with a single malevolent act of will.” The mind’s power vastly exceeds the body’s.
  4. The cursed wildernesses. The famous deserts of Daṇḍaka, Kaliṅga, Mejjha, and Mātaṅga became barren, Upāli himself has heard, “because of a malevolent act of will by seers” — a mental act that laid waste whole regions.

Four times Upāli gamely repeats “still the bodily rod is most blameworthy,” and four times the Buddha gently notes, “what you said before and after don’t match.” In the end Upāli confesses he was convinced by the very first argument but kept objecting only to hear all the Buddha’s “solutions” — the honest delight of someone who has been beaten and is glad of it.

The Buddha’s three surprising kindnesses

What turns conviction into devotion is not the argument but the Buddha’s conduct. Three times Upāli asks to go for refuge, and three times the Buddha gives a response no other teacher would:

  • “Act after careful consideration.” Rather than seizing a famous convert, the Buddha tells him not to rush — advice that astonishes Upāli, who knew that rival sects would “carry a banner all over Nāḷandā” boasting of him.
  • “Keep giving to the Jains.” “Your family has long been a well-spring of support for the Jain ascetics; you should still consider giving to them when they come.” The Buddha, far from demanding exclusive loyalty, urges continued generosity to Upāli’s former teachers.

These responses overturn the rumor Dīgha Tapassī had spread — that the Buddha used a “conversion trick” to snatch other sects’ followers. The only “trick” is a complete absence of grasping. By not trying to win Upāli, the Buddha wins him utterly.

The gradual teaching and the Dhamma-eye

Only now does the Buddha give Upāli the “graduated talk” (anupubbi-kathā): on giving, on ethical conduct, on heaven; then the drawbacks of sensual pleasures and the benefit of renunciation. When Upāli’s mind is “ready, pliable, rid of hindrances, elated, and confident,” the Buddha reveals “the special teaching of the Buddhas” — the four noble truths. And “just as a clean cloth rid of stains would properly absorb dye,” the “stainless, immaculate vision of the Dhamma” arises in Upāli: “Everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease.” This is the moment of stream-entry — the irreversible first glimpse of the unconditioned.

The reversal at the gate, and the dyed monkey

Home again, Upāli instructs his gatekeeper to close the gate to Jain monastics and open it to the Buddha’s followers. When Nātaputta himself comes, Upāli — who once would have given his teacher the finest seat — takes the high seat himself and coolly invites the Jain leader to sit. Stung, Nātaputta calls him a fool “caught in the net” of Gotama’s doctrine. Upāli replies with the discourse’s sharpest simile: a baby monkey can be dyed but falls apart if “pounded and pressed” — like Jain doctrine, which “looks fine to fools but can’t endure being scrutinized.” A sturdy new cloth, by contrast, can be dyed, pounded, and pressed — like the Buddha’s teaching, which “can endure being scrutinized and pressed.” The test of a true teaching is whether it survives examination.

Whose disciple I am

Asked whose disciple he now is, Upāli rises, faces the direction of the Buddha with cupped hands, and pours out a spontaneous hymn of some hundred epithets — “the attentive one, free of delusion… victor in battle… he is the Buddha, and I am his disciple” — verse after verse. When Nātaputta asks when he composed all this, Upāli answers with a final image: like a garland-maker tying many flowers into one wreath, he has simply gathered the Buddha’s “many hundreds” of praiseworthy qualities — “who would not praise the praiseworthy?” And the Jain leader, unable to bear this honor paid to his rival, “spewed hot blood from his mouth there and then.” The image is stark, even shocking — the bodily collapse of a doctrine that staked everything on the body, undone by the power of a converted heart.

Key terms

daṇḍa — “rod”; a cudgel or instrument of violence. The Jain term for the three channels of wrongdoing (bodily, verbal, mental). Its imagery is of an external weapon — revealing a focus on the outward act.
kamma — “deed, action.” The Buddhist term, which the Buddha prefers to “rod.” A deed includes the intention behind it — which is why, for the Buddha, the mental deed is weightiest.
cetanā — intention, volition. The Buddha’s key principle: “It is intention that I call deed.” Karma follows the will behind an action, not the bodily motion alone — the very point Upāli is led to see.
Niganṭha Nātaputta — the leader of the Jains (known to history as Mahāvīra), “the Jain ascetic of the Ñātika clan.” His doctrine of the three rods is the foil for the Buddha’s teaching on deeds.
anupubbi-kathā — the graduated talk: giving, ethics, heaven, the drawbacks of sensual pleasures, the benefit of renunciation — preparing the mind before the four noble truths are revealed.
dhamma-cakkhu — the “Dhamma-eye”: the vision that arises at stream-entry — “Everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease.” Upāli attains it “in that very seat.”

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The opening debate, the four arguments, and the Buddha’s three responses are given in full; Upāli’s long hymn of praise is sampled, with the rest noted. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Rods and deeds

§1–3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Nāḷandā in Pāvārika’s mango grove. At that time the Jain ascetic of the Ñātika clan was residing at Nāḷandā with a large assembly. The Jain ascetic Dīgha Tapassī came to the Buddha. The Buddha asked him: “Tapassī, how many kinds of deed does the Jain Ñātika describe for performing bad deeds?” “Reverend Gotama, the Jain Ñātika doesn’t usually speak of ‘deeds’. He speaks of ‘rods’: the physical rod, the verbal rod, and the mental rod.” “And which rod does he describe as the most blameworthy?” “The physical rod, Reverend Gotama, not so much the verbal or mental rod.” The Buddha made him stand by this point up to the third time.

§4Then Dīgha Tapassī asked the Buddha, “And how many kinds of rod do you describe?” “Tapassī, the Realized One doesn’t usually speak of ‘rods’. He speaks of ‘deeds’: physical deeds, verbal deeds, and mental deeds.” “And which deed do you describe as the most blameworthy?” “I describe mental deeds as the most blameworthy, not so much physical deeds or verbal deeds.” Thus Dīgha Tapassī made the Buddha stand by this point up to the third time, then went to report to the Jain Ñātika.

Upāli’s boast

§5–7The Jain Ñātika was sitting with a large assembly headed by the householder Upāli. Hearing the report, he praised Dīgha Tapassī: “How impressive is the measly mental rod compared with the solid physical rod?” Upāli agreed, and boasted: “I’d better go and refute the ascetic Gotama’s doctrine. If he stands by what he told Tapassī, I’ll take him on in debate and drag him to and fro and round about, like a strong man would grab a fleecy sheep and drag it to and fro!”

§8–9Dīgha Tapassī warned him, three times: “Sir, I don’t think it’s a good idea for Upāli to go. For the ascetic Gotama is deceitful; he knows a conversion trick by which he converts the disciples of other religions.” But the Jain Ñātika said, “It is impossible that Upāli could become Gotama’s disciple. But it is possible that Gotama could become Upāli’s disciple. Go, householder.” So Upāli went to the Buddha, and praised Dīgha Tapassī’s answer. The Buddha said, “Householder, so long as you debate on the basis of truth, we can discuss this.” “I will debate on the basis of truth, sir.”

The four arguments

§11“What do you think, householder? Take a Jain ascetic who is sick, and refuses cold water, using only hot water. Not getting cold water, he dies. Where does the Jain Ñātika say he is reborn?” “Sir, there are gods called ‘mind-bound’. He is reborn there—because he died with mental attachment.” “Think, householder! What you said before and after don’t match. Yet you said you would debate on the basis of truth.” “Even so, sir, the physical rod is the most blameworthy.”

§12“What do you think? Take a Jain restrained in the fourfold constraint. Going to and fro, he unintentionally injures many tiny creatures. What result does the Jain Ñātika say this brings?” “Sir, what is unintentional is not very blameworthy.” “But if it is intentional?” “Then it is very blameworthy.” “And where does the Jain Ñātika classify intention?” “In the mental rod, sir.” “Think, householder! What you said before and after don’t match.” “Even so, sir, the physical rod is the most blameworthy.”

§13“What do you think? Is this Nāḷandā prosperous and populous?” “Indeed it is, sir.” “Suppose a man came with a drawn sword and said, ‘In one hour I will reduce all the creatures of Nāḷandā to one mass of flesh!’ Could he?” “Sir, even fifty men couldn’t—how significant is one measly man?” “And suppose an ascetic with psychic power came and said, ‘I will reduce Nāḷandā to ashes with a single malevolent act of will’—could he?” “Sir, he could reduce ten, twenty, fifty Nāḷandās to ashes—how significant is one measly Nāḷandā?” “Think, householder! What you said before and after don’t match.”

§14“Have you heard how the wildernesses of Daṇḍaka, Kaliṅga, Mejjha, and Mātaṅga came to be that way?” “I have, sir—it was because of a malevolent act of will by seers.” “Think, householder! What you said before and after don’t match. Yet you said you would debate on the basis of truth.”

Upāli goes for refuge

§15“Sir, I was already delighted and satisfied by the Buddha’s very first simile. But I wanted to hear the Buddha’s various solutions to the problem, so I pretended to oppose you. Excellent, sir! As if he were righting the overturned, or revealing the hidden, or pointing out the path to the lost, or lighting a lamp in the dark, the Buddha has made the teaching clear. I go for refuge to the Buddha, to the teaching, and to the mendicant Saṅgha. May the Buddha remember me as a lay follower gone for refuge for life.”

§16–17“Householder, you should act after careful consideration. It’s good for well-known people such as yourself to act after careful consideration.” “Now I’m even more delighted, sir—for other sects would carry a banner all over Nāḷandā boasting that I had become their disciple, yet you tell me to consider carefully. For a second time I go for refuge.” “For a long time, householder, your family has been a well-spring of support for the Jain ascetics. You should consider giving to them when they come.” “Now I’m even more delighted, sir. I had heard that the ascetic Gotama says ‘Gifts should be given only to me… only to my disciples.’ Yet the Buddha encourages me to give to the Jains. We’ll know the proper time for that. For a third time I go for refuge to the Buddha, to the teaching, and to the mendicant Saṅgha.”

The Dhamma-eye

§18Then the Buddha taught Upāli step by step, with a talk on giving, ethical conduct, and heaven. He explained the drawbacks of sensual pleasures and the benefit of renunciation. And when he knew that Upāli’s mind was ready, pliable, rid of hindrances, elated, and confident, he explained the special teaching of the Buddhas: suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path. Just as a clean cloth rid of stains would properly absorb dye, in that very seat the stainless, immaculate vision of the Dhamma arose in Upāli: “Everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease.” Then Upāli saw, attained, understood, and fathomed the Dhamma. He went beyond doubt and became self-assured and independent of others regarding the Teacher’s instructions.

The closed gate

§19Upāli returned home and told his gatekeeper, “From this day forth I close the gate to Jain monks and nuns, and open it for the Buddha’s monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen. If any Jain ascetics come, say: ‘Wait, sir, do not enter. The householder Upāli has become a disciple of the ascetic Gotama. If you require almsfood, wait here, they will bring it to you.’”

Dīgha Tapassī and then the Jain Ñātika hear the news. Each time, the Jain Ñātika insists: “It is impossible that Upāli could become Gotama’s disciple; rather, Gotama might become Upāli’s.” Dīgha Tapassī goes to confirm, is turned away at the gate, and reports back. At last the Jain Ñātika goes himself, with a large following of Jain ascetics.

The reversal, and the dyed monkey

§23–25Upāli had the seats prepared and sat himself on the highest and finest seat — whereas before he would have risen to greet the Jain Ñātika and seated him there. “There are seats, sir. Please sit if you wish.” The Jain Ñātika said: “You’re mad, householder! You said, ‘I’ll refute the ascetic Gotama,’ but you come back caught in the vast net of his doctrine. Suppose a man went to deliver a pair of testicles but came back castrated—you’ve been converted by Gotama’s conversion trick!”

§26–27“Sir, this conversion trick is excellent, this conversion trick is lovely! If my loved ones were converted by it, it would be for their lasting welfare and happiness… Let me give you a simile. Once an old brahmin’s young wife wanted a baby monkey dyed, pounded, and pressed. The dyer said: ‘This monkey can endure a dyeing, but not a pounding or a pressing.’ In the same way, the doctrine of the foolish Jains looks fine initially—for fools, not the astute—but can’t endure being scrutinized or pressed. But a new pair of garments can endure a dyeing, a pounding, and a pressing. In the same way, the doctrine of the Buddha looks fine—for the astute, not for fools—and can endure being scrutinized and pressed.”

Whose disciple I am

§28–29“Householder, the king knows you as a disciple of the Jain Ñātika. Whose disciple should we remember you as?” Then Upāli rose, arranged his robe over one shoulder, raised his cupped palms toward the Buddha, and said, “Hear, then, whose disciple I am:

The attentive one, free of delusion,
with hard-heartedness dissolved, victor in battle;
he’s untroubled and very even-minded,
with the virtue of an elder and the wisdom of a saint —
he is the Buddha, and I am his disciple.

He has no indecision, he’s content,
joyful, he has spat out the world’s bait;
he has completed the ascetic’s task,
a man who bears his final body —
he is the Buddha, and I am his disciple.

The hymn continues through some ten verses — praising the Buddha as the trainer and charioteer, the “boss bull” profound and sagacious, free of conceit, possessing the three knowledges, peaceful and of vast wisdom, “the Realized One, the Holy One, unrivaled, unequaled” — each verse ending, “he is the Buddha, and I am his disciple.”

§30“But when did you compose these praises of the ascetic Gotama’s beautiful qualities, householder?” “Sir, suppose there was a great heap of many different flowers. A deft garland-maker could tie them into a colorful garland. In the same way, the Buddha has many beautiful qualities to praise, many hundreds of them. Who, sir, would not praise the praiseworthy?”

§31Unable to bear this honor paid to the Buddha, the Jain Ñātika spewed hot blood from his mouth there and then.

Who is Upāli in this discourse?
Correct: A. This Upāli is a prominent and prosperous lay follower of the Jains (not the monk Upāli of vinaya fame). Confident he will demolish the Buddha in debate, he ends up the Buddha’s devoted disciple.

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 48

The Mendicants of Kosambī

Kosambiyasutta

Setting
Kosambī, in Ghosita’s Monastery
Speakers
The Buddha and the quarreling monks of Kosambī
Form
Rebuke and teaching — with his own community split by a bitter dispute, the Buddha lays out the six “warm-hearted” qualities that hold a community together, then unfolds the noble view, their chief, into the seven knowledges of stream-entry.
Length
~13 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No direct Āgama parallel to the whole discourse; the six warm-hearted qualities recur at AN 6.11–12 and DN 33, and the Kosambī quarrel continues at MN 128.
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — a warm, accessible opening on communal harmony, followed by a more technical second half: the seven knowledges by which a disciple verifies their own stream-entry.

Why this discourse, forty-eighth

The most famous quarrel in the early community is its setting. The monks of Kosambī had split into factions over a trifling disciplinary matter and were “continually wounding each other with barbed words.” Even the Buddha’s presence could not calm them. MN 48 is his teaching to those monks: how a community holds together, and what holds the qualities of community together in turn.

The discourse has two movements. First, the Buddha names six warm-hearted qualities — kindness of body, speech, and mind toward one’s companions; sharing one’s gains in common; shared ethics; and shared view. These are the load-bearing beams of any harmonious saṅgha. Then he singles out the sixth, right view, as the chief among them — the roof-peak that holds the whole structure together — and shows how that view, when truly noble and emancipating, ripens into seven distinct knowledges that culminate in the fruit of stream-entry.

So MN 48 moves from the most practical of problems — people who cannot stop fighting — to the most refined of attainments. The bridge between them is view. A community quarrels because its members do not yet share the view that ends quarreling at its root. Heal the view, and harmony follows; deepen the view, and liberation follows. The same noble view that binds a community together is the very thing that delivers the individual to the end of suffering.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Six warm-hearted qualities — kindness in body, speech, and mind, sharing in common, shared ethics, and shared view — make a community harmonious; and the chief of them, the noble view, ripens through seven knowledges into the fruit of stream-entry.

The Kosambī quarrel

The backdrop is a real and notorious crisis. As the canon records it, the monks of Kosambī divided over a minor point of discipline, hardened into factions, and refused to be reconciled. The Buddha himself tried three times to settle them and failed; eventually he left them and withdrew alone to the forest. MN 48 belongs to this episode. The Buddha summons the monks, confronts them — “you futile men” — and then, rather than adjudicating the dispute, redirects them entirely. He does not say who was right. He teaches them what makes quarreling impossible in the first place.

The structure of the discourse

SectionWhat happens
1. The quarrelThe monks of Kosambī are fighting and wounding each other with words, unable to persuade or be persuaded. A monk reports this to the Buddha.
2. The summonsThe Buddha calls them and confronts them: when you quarrel, are you treating your companions with kindness in body, speech, and mind? They admit they are not.
3. The six qualitiesThe Buddha teaches the six warm-hearted qualities that make for fondness, respect, harmony, and unity, without quarreling.
4. The roof-peakOf the six, the chief is the noble view. Like the roof-peak of a building, it holds and binds all the others together.
5. The seven knowledgesThe Buddha unfolds how the noble view leads to the end of suffering: through seven knowledges by which a disciple confirms their own attainment.
6. Stream-entryA disciple endowed with these seven factors has realized the fruit of stream-entry.

The six warm-hearted qualities

The Pāli term is saāranīyā dhammā — qualities that make beings “dear and respected,” the building blocks of communal warmth. Sujato renders them “warm-hearted qualities.” They fall into a natural order:

#Quality
1Bodily kindness — acting toward companions with loving-kindness, in public and in private.
2Verbal kindness — speaking to and about companions with loving-kindness.
3Mental kindness — holding companions in mind with loving-kindness.
4Sharing in common — sharing legitimately gained goods, “even the food in the alms-bowl,” without holding back.
5Shared ethics — keeping the same precepts as one’s companions, intact and unbroken, in public and in private.
6Shared view — holding the same noble, emancipating view that leads to the complete ending of suffering.

The list moves outward and then inward: from how we act (1–3), to how we share what we have (4), to the ethical ground we stand on (5), to the deepest layer of all — how we see (6). The phrase “both in public and in private” recurs deliberately. Harmony is not a public performance; it must hold when no one is watching.

Why right view is the roof-peak

The Buddha gives the six an internal hierarchy. The chief (agga) is the sixth, right view, and his image is precise: in a peaked building, the roof-peak (kūṭa) is the topmost piece that draws all the rafters together and holds the structure. Remove it and the building collapses. So too with a community: kindness, generosity, and shared precepts are real and necessary, but it is shared view — a common understanding of what suffering is and how it ends — that ultimately holds them together. Where people see the goal the same way, the smaller frictions cannot pull them apart. Where they do not, no amount of surface courtesy will hold.

The seven knowledges of stream-entry

The discourse’s second half answers a single question: how does the noble view actually lead to the end of suffering? The answer is given as seven knowledges (ñāṇa) — not doctrines to believe but things a disciple comes to know for themselves through honest self-examination, going to a quiet place and reflecting:

  1. No hindrance remains — “Is there anything I am overcome with internally and have not given up?” Finding none of the five hindrances (or speculation, or the urge to quarrel) ruling the mind, they know their heart is “properly disposed for awakening to the truths.”
  2. The view brings peace — cultivating this view, they personally gain “serenity and quenching.”
  3. The view is unique — no ascetic or brahmin outside the Buddha’s teaching holds the same view.
  4. Quick to confess — they have the nature of one “accomplished in view”: a fault is disclosed at once, like a baby snatching its hand off a coal.
  5. Devoted to training — however busy with duties, they keep a keen regard for the three trainings, like a cow grazing while watching her calf.
  6. Eager to listen — when the teaching is taught, they listen with their whole mind.
  7. Inspired by the teaching — they find joy and inspiration in its meaning and its words.

A disciple in whom all seven are present, the Buddha concludes, “has the fruit of stream-entry.” This is one of the canon’s clearest checklists for a practitioner to verify their own first awakening — not by feeling, claim, or another’s endorsement, but by the demonstrable presence of these seven knowledges.

The two similes

The fourth and fifth knowledges each carry a vivid image. The baby and the burning coal: a stream-enterer may still slip into a minor offense, but the instant they notice it they pull back and disclose it — reflexively, the way an infant who touches fire yanks its hand away. There is no concealment, no defending the fault. The cow and her calf: even fully occupied with the many tasks of community life, the disciple never loses sight of the training in higher ethics, higher mind, and higher wisdom — as a grazing cow keeps one eye always on her calf. Both similes describe an effortless, instinctive orientation: the awakened priority has become second nature.

Key terms

saāranīyā dhammā — warm-hearted qualities; qualities that make one “dear and respected.” The six communal virtues that make for fondness, respect, harmony, and unity, without quarreling.
mettā — loving-kindness. The first three qualities are mettā expressed through the three doors of action: body, speech, and mind — held toward one’s spiritual companions “in public and in private.”
sammā-diṭṭhi — right view; here “the view that is noble and emancipating (ariyā niyyānikā), and delivers one who practices it to the complete ending of suffering.” The chief of the six and the engine of the seven knowledges.
kūṭa — the roof-peak of a building; the topmost member that binds all the rafters. The Buddha’s image for how right view holds the other five qualities together.
ñāṇa — knowledge; direct knowing. The seven knowledges are not beliefs but verifiable realizations — “noble and transcendent, and distinct from that of ordinary people.”
sotāpatti-phala — the fruit of stream-entry; the first of the four stages of awakening. One who has “entered the stream” will reach full liberation and can never fall back to a lower destination. This discourse gives its seven marks.

The text

The full discourse, given here complete. The repeated refrains for the warm-hearted qualities and the closing formula of each knowledge are abbreviated with “…” in the source, as below. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The quarrel at Kosambī

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Kosambī, in Ghosita’s Monastery.

§2Now at that time the mendicants of Kosambī were fighting, quarreling, and disputing, continually wounding each other with barbed words. They couldn’t persuade each other or be persuaded, nor could they convince each other or be convinced.

§3Then a mendicant went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and told him what was happening.

§4So the Buddha addressed one of the monks, “Please, monk, in my name tell those mendicants that the teacher summons them.” “Yes, sir,” that monk replied. He went to those monks and said, “Venerables, the teacher summons you.” “Yes, reverend,” those monks replied. They went to the Buddha, bowed, and sat down to one side. The Buddha said to them, “Is it really true, mendicants, that you have been fighting, quarreling, and disputing, continually wounding each other with barbed words? And that you can’t persuade each other or be persuaded, nor can you convince each other or be convinced?” “Yes, sir,” they said.

§5“What do you think, mendicants? When you’re fighting, quarreling, and disputing, continually wounding each other with barbed words, are you treating your spiritual companions with kindness by way of body, speech, and mind, both in public and in private?” “No, sir.” “So it seems that when you’re arguing you are not treating each other with kindness. So what then do you know and see, you futile men, that you behave in such a way? This will be for your lasting harm and suffering.”

The six warm-hearted qualities

§6Then the Buddha said to the mendicants: “Mendicants, these six warm-hearted qualities make for fondness and respect, conducing to inclusion, harmony, and unity, without quarreling. What six? Firstly, a mendicant consistently treats their spiritual companions with bodily kindness, both in public and in private. This warm-hearted quality makes for fondness and respect, conducing to inclusion, harmony, and unity, without quarreling.

Furthermore, a mendicant consistently treats their spiritual companions with verbal kindness … Furthermore, a mendicant consistently treats their spiritual companions with mental kindness …

Furthermore, a mendicant shares without reservation any material things they have gained by legitimate means, even the food placed in the alms-bowl, using them in common with their ethical spiritual companions …

Furthermore, a mendicant lives according to the precepts shared with their spiritual companions, both in public and in private. Those precepts are intact, impeccable, spotless, and unmarred, liberating, praised by sensible people, not mistaken, and leading to immersion. …

Furthermore, a mendicant lives according to the view shared with their spiritual companions, both in public and in private. That view is noble and emancipating, and delivers one who practices it to the complete ending of suffering. This warm-hearted quality makes for fondness and respect, conducing to inclusion, harmony, and unity, without quarreling. These six warm-hearted qualities make for fondness and respect, conducing to inclusion, harmony, and unity, without quarreling.”

The roof-peak

§7“Of these six warm-hearted qualities, the chief is the view that is noble and emancipating, and delivers one who practices it to the complete ending of suffering. It holds and binds everything together. It’s like a bungalow. The roof-peak is the chief point, which holds and binds everything together. In the same way, of these six warm-hearted qualities, the chief is the view that is noble and emancipating, and delivers one who practices it to the complete ending of suffering. It holds and binds everything together.”

How the noble view ends suffering — the seven knowledges

§8“And how does the view that is noble and emancipating lead one who practices it to the complete ending of suffering? It’s when a mendicant has gone to a wilderness, or to the root of a tree, or to an empty hut, and reflects like this, ‘Is there anything that I’m overcome with internally and haven’t given up, because of which I might not accurately know and see?’ If a mendicant is overcome with sensual desire, it’s their mind that’s overcome. If a mendicant is overcome with ill will, dullness and drowsiness, restlessness and remorse, doubt, pursuing speculation about this world, pursuing speculation about the next world, or fighting, quarreling, and disputing, continually wounding others with barbed words, it’s their mind that’s overcome. They understand, ‘There is nothing that I’m overcome with internally and haven’t given up, because of which I might not accurately know and see. My heart is properly disposed for awakening to the truths.’ This is the first knowledge they have achieved that is noble and transcendent, and is distinct from that of ordinary people.

§9Furthermore, a noble disciple reflects, ‘When I develop, cultivate, and make much of this view, do I personally gain serenity and quenching?’ They understand, ‘When I develop, cultivate, and make much of this view, I personally gain serenity and quenching.’ This is their second knowledge …

§10Furthermore, a noble disciple reflects, ‘Are there any ascetics or brahmins outside of the Buddhist community who have the same kind of view that I have?’ They understand, ‘There are no ascetics or brahmins outside of the Buddhist community who have the same kind of view that I have.’ This is their third knowledge …

§11Furthermore, a noble disciple reflects, ‘Do I have the same nature as an individual accomplished in view?’ And what, mendicants, is the nature of an individual accomplished in view? This is the nature of an individual accomplished in view. Though they may fall into a kind of offense for which rehabilitation is possible, they quickly disclose, clarify, and reveal it to the Teacher or a sensible spiritual companion. And having revealed it they restrain themselves in the future. Suppose there was a little baby boy. If he puts his hand or foot on a burning coal, he quickly pulls it back. In the same way, this is the nature of an individual accomplished in view. Though they may still fall into a kind of offense for which rehabilitation is possible, they quickly reveal it to the Teacher or a sensible spiritual companion. And having revealed it they restrain themselves in the future. They understand, ‘I have the same nature as an individual accomplished in view.’ This is their fourth knowledge …

§12Furthermore, a noble disciple reflects, ‘Do I have the same nature as an individual accomplished in view?’ And what, mendicants, is the nature of an individual accomplished in view? This is the nature of an individual accomplished in view. Though they might manage a diverse spectrum of duties for their spiritual companions, they still feel a keen regard for the training in higher ethics, higher mind, and higher wisdom. Suppose there was a cow with a baby calf. She watches her calf devotedly as she grazes. In the same way, this is the nature of an individual accomplished in view. Though they might manage a diverse spectrum of duties for their spiritual companions, they still feel a keen regard for the training in higher ethics, higher mind, and higher wisdom. They understand, ‘I have the same nature as an individual accomplished in view.’ This is their fifth knowledge …

§13Furthermore, a noble disciple reflects, ‘Do I have the same strength as an individual accomplished in view?’ And what, mendicants, is the strength of an individual accomplished in view? The strength of an individual accomplished in view is that, when the teaching and training proclaimed by the Realized One are being taught, they pay attention, apply the mind, concentrate wholeheartedly, and actively listen to the teaching. They understand, ‘I have the same strength as an individual accomplished in view.’ This is their sixth knowledge …

§14Furthermore, a noble disciple reflects, ‘Do I have the same strength as an individual accomplished in view?’ And what, mendicants, is the strength of an individual accomplished in view? The strength of an individual accomplished in view is that, when the teaching and training proclaimed by the Realized One are being taught, they find inspiration in the meaning and the teaching, and find joy connected with the teaching. They understand, ‘I have the same strength as an individual accomplished in view.’ This is the seventh knowledge they have achieved that is noble and transcendent, and is distinct from that of ordinary people.

The fruit of stream-entry

§15When a noble disciple has these seven factors, they have properly investigated their nature through the realization of the fruit of stream-entry. A noble disciple with these seven factors has the fruit of stream-entry.”

That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said.

Question 1 of 10
What is the situation that prompts this discourse?
Correct: B. The monks of Kosambī had split into factions over a disciplinary dispute and could not be reconciled — one of the most famous quarrels in the early community. The Buddha summons them and, rather than judging who is right, teaches them what makes quarreling impossible.
Question 2 of 10
When the Buddha confronts the monks, what does he ask them to consider?
Correct: D. The Buddha does not adjudicate the dispute. He turns their attention to their own conduct: while fighting, are they acting with mettā toward their companions by body, speech, and mind? They admit they are not.
Question 3 of 10
How many “warm-hearted qualities” (saāranīyā dhammā) does the Buddha teach?
Correct: A. Six: bodily kindness, verbal kindness, and mental kindness toward companions; sharing one’s gains in common; shared ethics; and shared view. Together they “make for fondness and respect, conducing to inclusion, harmony, and unity, without quarreling.”
Question 4 of 10
Which of the six qualities does the Buddha call the chief?
Correct: C. The chief (agga) is the sixth quality, right view — “noble and emancipating.” It “holds and binds everything together.” Where people share an understanding of how suffering ends, the smaller frictions cannot pull them apart.
Question 5 of 10
What simile does the Buddha use for how right view relates to the other qualities?
Correct: B. “It’s like a bungalow. The roof-peak is the chief point, which holds and binds everything together.” Just as the roof-peak (kūṭa) draws the whole structure into one, right view binds the other five warm-hearted qualities together.
Question 6 of 10
In the first knowledge, what does the disciple examine in seclusion?
Correct: C. Going to a quiet place, the disciple asks: “Is there anything that I’m overcome with internally and haven’t given up?” — checking for sensual desire, ill will, dullness, restlessness, doubt, speculation, or the urge to quarrel. Finding none ruling the mind, they know their “heart is properly disposed for awakening.”
Question 7 of 10
The simile of the baby and the burning coal illustrates which quality of one “accomplished in view”?
Correct: A. A stream-enterer may still slip into a minor, rehabilitable offense — but the moment they notice, they reveal it to the teacher or a wise companion and restrain themselves, “just as a little baby quickly pulls back its hand from a burning coal.” The point is reflexive honesty, not flawlessness.
Question 8 of 10
What does the simile of the cow watching her calf illustrate?
Correct: D. Just as a grazing cow keeps one eye always on her calf, the disciple — even fully occupied with community tasks — never loses sight of the three trainings. The awakened priority has become second nature.
Question 9 of 10
The seven items the disciple confirms are called “knowledges” (ñāṇa). Why does that word matter?
Correct: B. Each is something the disciple comes to know through honest self-examination, not something taken on faith. This makes the seven a practical checklist by which a practitioner can verify their own stream-entry — by demonstrable presence, not by feeling or another’s endorsement.
Question 10 of 10
What does the Buddha conclude about a disciple endowed with all seven factors?
Correct: C. “A noble disciple with these seven factors has the fruit of stream-entry.” Stream-entry is the first of the four stages of awakening: such a person is bound for full liberation and can never fall to a lower destination. The discourse gives its seven verifiable marks.

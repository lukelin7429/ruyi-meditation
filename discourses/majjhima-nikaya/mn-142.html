Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 142

The Analysis of Religious Donations

Dakkhiṇāvibhaṅgasutta

Setting
Near Kapilavatthu, in the land of the Sakyans, at the Banyan Tree Monastery
Speakers
The Buddha, his foster-mother Mahāpajāpati Gotamī, and Venerable Ānanda
Form
A domestic scene over a hand-woven robe opens into a systematic doctrinal analysis of giving
Length
~18 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No specific Āgama parallel is confidently identified in the available commentary
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — cumulative and systematic; its hierarchy of recipients and its four-part purification scheme reward careful attention

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-forty-second

This is the last discourse of the Vibhaṅgavagga, the fourteenth chapter of “Analyses” that runs from MN 131 to here — and it closes that run of doctrinal dissections with something unexpectedly domestic: an aunt trying three times to give her nephew a robe she wove with her own hands. Out of that small, warm scene comes one of the canon’s clearest statements on generosity — not a theory of giving in the abstract, but a precise, rung-by-rung account of what makes an offering fruitful, and why giving to a community will always outlast and outreach giving to any single person within it, however accomplished.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A gift to the collective Saṅgha is always more fruitful than the same gift given to any one person, however spiritually advanced — because what purifies an offering depends on the ethical clarity of both giver and recipient, not only on the recipient’s rank, and because a gift given to “the Saṅgha” outlives and outreaches any single teacher who happens to receive it on the community’s behalf.

A robe, offered three times

The scene that opens the discourse is intimate rather than doctrinal. Mahāpajāpati Gotamī — the Buddha’s maternal aunt, who nursed him at her own breast after his mother Māyā died within a week of his birth, and who would later become the first bhikkhunī — has spun and woven a new pair of robes specially for him. She brings them to the Banyan Tree Monastery near Kapilavatthu and asks him, three times, in nearly identical words, to accept the gift “out of sympathy.” Three times he gives her the same answer: “Give it to the Saṅgha, Gotamī. When you give to the Saṅgha, both the Saṅgha and I will be honored.” He does not refuse the robe. He refuses to receive it apart from the community he leads.

Ānanda intervenes

Watching this exchange, Ānanda speaks up on Gotamī’s behalf, and in doing so gives the discourse its emotional center. He reminds the Buddha, point by point, of everything she has done for him — raising him, nursing him, standing in for the mother he lost — and everything the Buddha has done for her in return: bringing her to go for refuge, to ethical conduct, to confidence in the Three Jewels, and to freedom from doubt about suffering and its ending. The Buddha agrees entirely, and then sharpens the point: a debt of that second kind — being set by someone else on the path to awakening — cannot be repaid merely by bowing, rising, or providing robes, almsfood, lodging, and medicine. It belongs to a different order of obligation than any material gift can settle.

Fourteen gifts to an individual

With that scene as its frame, the Buddha turns to a full analysis of giving. He first lists fourteen kinds of gift to an individual (puggalika dakkhiṇā), ranked strictly by the spiritual attainment of the recipient: from a gift to an animal, at the bottom, through the “four pairs” of noble persons recognized on the path — those practicing toward, and those who have reached, stream-entry, once-return, non-return, and full awakening — up to a gift to an independent Buddha, and finally to a fully awakened Buddha at the summit.

The jump at the top of the ladder is deliberate: below it, returns simply multiply; above it, the Buddha declines to multiply at all, calling the returns incalculable rather than merely large.

Seven gifts to the Saṅgha as a whole

Individual gifts, however, are not the discourse’s real destination. Ānanda is next given seven kinds of gift “bestowed on a Saṅgha” (saṅghagata dakkhiṇā): to the combined communities of monks and nuns while the Buddha is alive; to the combined communities after his final passing; to the monks’ Saṅgha alone; to the nuns’ Saṅgha alone; and three further categories in which the donor asks the Saṅgha itself to appoint however many monks and nuns, monks alone, or nuns alone are needed, rather than naming any individual. What unites all seven is that the gift belongs to the institution, not to whichever person the institution happens to send to receive it.

Even in a corrupted future

The Buddha then makes an unusually blunt prediction: that in times to come there will be “lambs of the flock wearing a scrap of ocher cloth, unethical and of bad character” — monastics in robes without the corresponding practice — and that people will still give to them in the Saṅgha’s name. Even then, he says, a gift bestowed on the Saṅgha remains incalculable and immeasurable in its fruit. The logic is structural rather than sentimental: because such a gift is given to the institution across time, its worth cannot be reduced to the failings of whichever individual happens to receive it on the community’s behalf. “There is no way,” he concludes, “that a personal offering can be more fruitful than one bestowed on a Saṅgha.”

What actually purifies a gift

The discourse’s final teaching shifts from the recipient’s rank to something more basic: the ethical state of both parties. Four combinations are possible — an offering purified by the giver alone (an ethical giver, an unethical recipient); by the recipient alone (an unethical giver, an ethical recipient); by neither (both unethical); or by both (both ethical). The closing verses add a further, superlative case beyond these four: when someone free of passion gives, with a trusting heart, to another who is likewise free of passion, “that’s truly the best of material gifts.” A recipient’s rank on the fourteen-step ladder and the ethical quality of a specific exchange turn out to be two separate measures, both bearing on what any single gift becomes.

Key terms

dakkhiṇā — a religious donation or offering, specifically one given with a spiritual rather than merely social intention; the discourse’s central term, appearing in its very title.
puggalika dakkhiṇā & saṅghagata dakkhiṇā — offerings “to an individual” versus offerings “gone to the Saṅgha,” i.e. given to the monastic community as an institution rather than to any named person within it.
paccekabuddha — an “independent Buddha,” who awakens without a teacher but, unlike a fully awakened Buddha, does not found a teaching for others; ranked second only to a Buddha in this discourse’s ladder of recipients.
the four pairs — the eight kinds of noble persons recognized in the texts: those practicing toward, and those who have realized, stream-entry, once-return, non-return, and full awakening (arahantship).
Mahāpajāpati Gotamī — the Buddha’s maternal aunt, who raised him after his mother’s death and later became the first bhikkhunī, founding the order of nuns.

The text

The discourse in full: Gotamī’s three attempts to give the Buddha a robe, Ānanda’s intervention, and the Buddha’s complete analysis of fourteen individual gifts, seven communal gifts, and four modes of purification, closing with his verses on giving. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A robe, offered three times

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the land of the Sakyans, near Kapilavatthu in the Banyan Tree Monastery.

Then Mahāpajāpati Gotamī approached the Buddha bringing a new pair of garments. She bowed, sat down to one side, and said to the Buddha, “Sir, I have spun and woven this new pair of garments specially for the Buddha. May the Buddha please accept this from me out of sympathy.” When she said this, the Buddha said to her, “Give it to the Saṅgha, Gotamī. When you give to the Saṅgha, both the Saṅgha and I will be honored.”

For a second time… For a third time, Mahāpajāpati Gotamī said to the Buddha, “Sir, I have spun and woven this new pair of garments specially for the Buddha. May the Buddha please accept this from me out of sympathy.” And for a third time, the Buddha said to her, “Give it to the Saṅgha, Gotamī. When you give to the Saṅgha, both the Saṅgha and I will be honored.”

Ānanda intervenes

§3–4When he said this, Venerable Ānanda said to the Buddha, “Sir, please accept the new pair of garments from Mahāpajāpati Gotamī. Sir, Mahāpajāpati was very helpful to the Buddha. As his aunt, she raised him, nurtured him, and gave him her milk. When the Buddha’s birth mother passed away, she nurtured him at her own breast. And the Buddha has been very helpful to Mahāpajāpati. It is owing to the Buddha that Mahāpajāpati has gone for refuge to the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha. It’s owing to the Buddha that she refrains from killing living creatures, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying, and intoxicants of beer, wine, and liquor. It’s owing to the Buddha that she has experiential confidence in the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha, and has the ethics loved by the noble ones. It’s owing to the Buddha that she is free of doubt regarding suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the practice that leads to its cessation. The Buddha has been very helpful to Mahāpajāpati.”

“That’s so true, Ānanda. When someone has enabled you to go for refuge, it’s not easy to repay them by bowing down to them, rising up for them, greeting them with cupped palms, and observing proper etiquette for them; or by providing them with robes, almsfood, lodgings, and medicines and supplies for the sick. When someone has enabled you to refrain from killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying, and intoxicants of beer, wine, and liquor, it’s not easy to repay them… When someone has enabled you to have experiential confidence in the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha, and the ethics loved by the noble ones, it’s not easy to repay them… When someone has enabled you to be free of doubt regarding suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the practice that leads to its cessation, it’s not easy to repay them by bowing down to them, rising up for them, greeting them with cupped palms, and observing proper etiquette for them; or by providing them with robes, almsfood, lodgings, and medicines and supplies for the sick.

Fourteen gifts to an individual

§5–6Ānanda, there are these fourteen religious donations to individuals. What fourteen? One gives a gift to the Realized One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha. This is the first religious donation to an individual. One gives a gift to an independent Buddha. This is the second religious donation to an individual. One gives a gift to a perfected one. This is the third religious donation to an individual. One gives a gift to someone practicing to realize the fruit of perfection. This is the fourth religious donation to an individual. One gives a gift to a non-returner. This is the fifth religious donation to an individual. One gives a gift to someone practicing to realize the fruit of non-return. This is the sixth religious donation to an individual. One gives a gift to a once-returner. This is the seventh religious donation to an individual. One gives a gift to someone practicing to realize the fruit of once-return. This is the eighth religious donation to an individual. One gives a gift to a stream-enterer. This is the ninth religious donation to an individual. One gives a gift to someone practicing to realize the fruit of stream-entry. This is the tenth religious donation to an individual. One gives a gift to an outsider who is free of desire for sensual pleasures. This is the eleventh religious donation to an individual. One gives a gift to an ordinary person who has good ethical conduct. This is the twelfth religious donation to an individual. One gives a gift to an ordinary person who has bad ethical conduct. This is the thirteenth religious donation to an individual. One gives a gift to an animal. This is the fourteenth religious donation to an individual.

Now, Ānanda, gifts to the following persons may be expected to yield the following returns. To an animal, a hundred times. To an unethical ordinary person, a thousand. To an ethical ordinary person, a hundred thousand. To an outsider free of desire for sensual pleasures, 10,000,000,000. But a gift to someone practicing to realize the fruit of stream-entry may be expected to yield incalculable, immeasurable returns. How much more so a gift to a stream-enterer, someone practicing to realize the fruit of once-return, a once-returner, someone practicing to realize the fruit of non-return, a non-returner, someone practicing to realize the fruit of perfection, a perfected one, or an independent Buddha? How much more so a Realized One, a perfected one, a fully awakened Buddha?

Seven gifts to the Saṅgha as a whole

§7–8But there are, Ānanda, seven religious donations bestowed on a Saṅgha. What seven? One gives a gift to the communities of both monks and nuns headed by the Buddha. This is the first religious donation bestowed on a Saṅgha. One gives a gift to the communities of both monks and nuns after the Buddha has finally become quenched. This is the second religious donation bestowed on a Saṅgha. One gives a gift to the Saṅgha of monks. This is the third religious donation bestowed on a Saṅgha. One gives a gift to the Saṅgha of nuns. This is the fourth religious donation bestowed on a Saṅgha. One gives a gift, thinking: ‘Appoint this many monks and nuns for me from the Saṅgha.’ This is the fifth religious donation bestowed on a Saṅgha. One gives a gift, thinking: ‘Appoint this many monks for me from the Saṅgha.’ This is the sixth religious donation bestowed on a Saṅgha. One gives a gift, thinking: ‘Appoint this many nuns for me from the Saṅgha.’ This is the seventh religious donation bestowed on a Saṅgha.

In times to come there will be lambs of the flock wearing a scrap of ocher cloth, unethical and of bad character. People will give gifts to those unethical people in the name of the Saṅgha. Then too, I say, a religious donation bestowed on the Saṅgha is incalculable and immeasurable. But in any case, there is no way a personal offering can be more fruitful than one bestowed on a Saṅgha, I say.

What actually purifies a gift

§9–13Ānanda, there are these four ways of purifying a religious donation. What four? There’s a religious donation that’s purified by the giver, not the recipient. There’s a religious donation that’s purified by the recipient, not the giver. There’s a religious donation that’s purified by neither the giver nor the recipient. There’s a religious donation that’s purified by both the giver and the recipient.

And how is a religious donation purified by the giver, not the recipient? It’s when the giver is ethical, of good character, but the recipient is unethical, of bad character.

And how is a religious donation purified by the recipient, not the giver? It’s when the giver is unethical, of bad character, but the recipient is ethical, of good character.

And how is a religious donation purified by neither the giver nor the recipient? It’s when both the giver and the recipient are unethical, of bad character.

And how is a religious donation purified by both the giver and the recipient? It’s when both the giver and the recipient are ethical, of good character. These are the four ways of purifying a religious donation.”

The closing verses

§14That is what the Buddha said. Then the Holy One, the Teacher, went on to say:

“When an ethical person with trusting heart
gives a proper gift to unethical persons,
trusting in the ample fruit of deeds,
that offering is purified by the giver.

When an unethical and untrusting person,
gives an improper gift to ethical persons,
not trusting in the ample fruit of deeds,
that offering is purified by the receivers.

When an unethical and untrusting person,
gives an improper gift to unethical persons,
not trusting in the ample fruit of deeds,
I declare that gift is not very fruitful.

When an ethical person with trusting heart
gives a proper gift to ethical persons,
trusting in the ample fruit of deeds,
I declare that gift is abundantly fruitful.

But when a passionless one gives to the passionless
a proper gift with trusting heart,
trusting in the ample fruit of deeds,
that’s truly the best of material gifts.”

According to the Buddha's reply to Ānanda, why is it "not easy" to repay someone who set you on the path to awakening?
Correct: D. The Buddha lists the usual gestures of gratitude and states plainly that none of them are equal to the debt.
Question 5 of 10
How many kinds of "individual gift" does the Buddha list, and who ranks highest?
Correct: B. The fourteen recipients are ranked strictly by spiritual attainment.
Question 6 of 10
What is the expected multiplier for a gift to an ethical ordinary person, compared to an unethical one?
Correct: C. Above these lower rungs, the noble persons and the two kinds of Buddha are described as yielding incalculable, immeasurable returns rather than a further multiple.
Question 7 of 10
What distinguishes the seven "gifts to the Saṅgha" from the fourteen "gifts to an individual"?
Correct: D. Some of the seven even leave the choice of recipient entirely up to the Saṅgha.
Question 8 of 10
What does the Buddha predict about "lambs of the flock" in a future, corrupted Saṅgha, and what does he conclude?
Correct: B. The point is structural: a gift to the institution cannot be devalued by any one recipient's failings.
Question 9 of 10
According to the four ways of purifying a gift, what happens when the giver is ethical but the recipient is not?
Correct: C. The four combinations depend on the ethical state of giver and recipient independently of each other.
Question 10 of 10
What superlative case do the closing verses add beyond the basic four ways of purifying a gift?
Correct: A. This fifth case sits above the ordinary four, describing an exchange between two people already free of greed, hatred, and delusion.

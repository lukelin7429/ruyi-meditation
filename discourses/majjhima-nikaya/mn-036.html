Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 36

The Greater Discourse with Saccaka

Mahāsaccakasutta

Setting
Vesālī, in the Great Wood, at the hall with the peaked roof
Speakers
The Buddha and Saccaka (Aggivessana)
Form
Autobiographical narrative — the Buddha tells the story of his own path to awakening: six years of austerity, their abandonment, the rediscovery of the jhānas, and the three knowledges attained on the night of enlightenment
Length
~20 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 204 (Madhyama-āgama 204)
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — the narrative is vivid and gripping. Some familiarity with the jhāna framework helps, but the discourse is largely self-explanatory. One of the most important autobiographical discourses in the canon.

Why this discourse, thirty-sixth

When Saccaka returns after his defeat in MN 35, the Buddha does not repeat the philosophical argument. Instead he tells his own story: the six years of extreme austerity that nearly killed him, the pivotal realization that self-mortification is futile, the childhood memory of a jhāna under a rose-apple tree, and the three knowledges — recollection of past lives, the divine eye seeing beings reborn according to their deeds, and the destruction of the taints — attained on the night of awakening.

MN 36 is the canon’s most detailed autobiographical account of the Buddha’s path from renunciation to enlightenment. Where MN 35 proved the not-self teaching through logic, MN 36 proves it through experience: the Buddha tried the path of total self-control (asceticism) and found it empty, then discovered that liberation comes not through mastering the body but through understanding the mind.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The path to awakening is not self-mortification but the middle way — the Buddha discovered this by nearly dying in austerity, then remembering a childhood jhāna of effortless ease, and realizing that the jhānas, not the deprivations, are the vehicle of liberation.

The narrative arc

PhaseWhat happens
Opening frameSaccaka revisits the question of “development of body” and “development of mind.” The Buddha distinguishes true development (remaining unmoved when pleasant or painful feelings arise) from its absence.
The austeritiesThe Bodhisatta describes three categories of extreme practice: (1) extreme diet — eating only a single grain of rice, a single jujube, reducing food to almost nothing until his limbs became like jointed bamboo and his eyes sank deep; (2) extreme breath control — suppressing breathing until violent pains pierced his head; (3) extreme exertion — clenching teeth and pressing tongue against palate until sweat poured from the armpits.
The turning pointAfter six years, the Bodhisatta realizes: “Whatever ascetics or brahmins have ever felt painful, racking, piercing feelings due to exertion — this is the utmost, there is none beyond this. But by this fierce austerity I have not attained any superhuman distinction in knowledge and vision worthy of the noble ones. Could there be another path to awakening?”
The childhood memoryHe recalls sitting under a rose-apple tree as a child during his father’s plowing festival, spontaneously entering the first jhāna. He asks himself: “Could that be the path?” And a knowledge arises: “That is the path to awakening.”
The four jhānasHe eats solid food again, regains strength, and enters the four jhānas in sequence — secluded from sensual pleasures, with applied and sustained thought, then without them, culminating in equanimity and mindfulness purified.
The three knowledgesIn the first watch of the night: recollection of past lives. In the middle watch: the divine eye — seeing beings dying and being reborn according to their deeds. In the last watch: the destruction of the taints — direct knowledge of the four noble truths and the realization “Birth is ended, the spiritual life has been lived, what had to be done has been done.”

The rose-apple tree: the pivotal memory

The most psychologically striking moment in the discourse is the childhood memory. After six years of the most extreme austerity ever attempted, the Bodhisatta does not have a philosophical breakthrough. He has a memory: sitting under a tree as a boy, effortlessly falling into a state of joy and ease. The contrast is total. He has been torturing himself for years, and the answer was something he experienced as a child without trying. The teaching embedded in this image: liberation is not achieved by force but by a kind of natural clarity that the mind already knows how to produce — you have to stop fighting it and let it arise.

Why the austerities matter

The Buddha does not describe his austerities to shock. He describes them to prove a negative: he tried the path of self-mortification to its absolute extreme — no one has ever gone further — and it did not work. The six years are not a detour but an essential experiment. The middle way is credible precisely because the Buddha exhausted the alternative. He does not reject asceticism from ignorance but from thorough, personal experience.

Relation to MN 35

MN 35 and MN 36 form one of the great pairs in the Majjhima Nikāya. In MN 35, the Buddha refutes Saccaka’s self-view through cross-examination: the sovereignty test. In MN 36, Saccaka returns, and the Buddha responds not with further argument but with autobiography. The two discourses answer the same question from different angles: MN 35 asks “why is the self-view wrong?” (because you have no sovereignty over the aggregates); MN 36 asks “how did you discover this?” (by pushing self-control to its limit and finding it empty).

Three questions Western students often ask

“Did the Buddha really eat only one grain of rice a day?” The descriptions of extreme austerity are likely hyperbolic in their details — a single grain of rice, a single jujube. But the pattern is well-attested across traditions: severe fasting, breath suppression, and physical endurance were standard practices among samaṇa ascetics in the Buddha’s time. The point is not the literal quantity but the extremity: the Bodhisatta went as far as anyone could go.

“What exactly is a jhāna?” In this discourse, the four jhānas are described as progressive states of meditative absorption. The first jhāna involves seclusion from sensual pleasures, with joy and ease born of seclusion, together with applied and sustained thought. The second drops applied and sustained thought and is characterized by inner confidence and unification. The third moves beyond joy to equanimity and pleasure. The fourth is equanimity and mindfulness purified, beyond pleasure and pain. These are not trances but states of increasing clarity and stability.

“Is this discourse historical?” MN 36 is one of the closest things we have to an autobiography of the Buddha. Whether it records his exact words or is a later formalization of his oral teaching is debated. But the discourse’s psychological realism — the failure of austerity, the surprise of the childhood memory, the systematic progression from jhāna to insight — is internally consistent and does not read like hagiography. It reads like someone recounting a genuine process of trial, failure, and discovery.

Key terms

dukkarakārikā — difficult practices; austerities. The Bodhisatta’s six years of extreme self-mortification. The term literally means “doing what is difficult” — it is the path of effort pushed to its limit and found wanting.
jhāna — meditative absorption. The four jhānas are the vehicle through which the Bodhisatta attains the three knowledges. The childhood rose-apple tree memory is the first jhāna rediscovered.
pubbenivāsānussati — recollection of past lives. The first of the three knowledges, attained in the first watch of the night. The Bodhisatta recalls many hundreds of thousands of past lives with their details.
dibbacakkhu — the divine eye. The second knowledge, attained in the middle watch. He sees beings dying and being reborn in accordance with their actions (kamma).
āsavakkhaṇa — destruction of the taints. The third and decisive knowledge, attained in the last watch. Direct knowledge of the four noble truths and the realization that birth is ended.
majjhimā paṭipadā — the middle way. Not explicitly named in MN 36 but implicit throughout: the path between self-indulgence and self-mortification. The jhānas are the middle way in practice — they require renunciation of sensual pleasures but not the torment of the body.
kāya-bhāvanā / citta-bhāvanā — development of body / development of mind. The opening frame of the discourse. Saccaka claims that development of body and mind means extreme asceticism. The Buddha redefines both: true development means remaining unmoved by pleasant and painful feelings, not by suppressing them but by understanding them.

The text

MN 36 is a long autobiographical narrative. The opening exchange with Saccaka and the austerity descriptions are given in full. The four jhānas and three knowledges follow the standard formulas and are presented here in condensed form where the pattern is established. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The opening exchange

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Vesālī, in the Great Wood, at the hall with the peaked roof.

§2Then in the morning the Buddha robed up, took his bowl and robe, and entered Vesālī for alms. Then Saccaka, walking and wandering for exercise, went to the hall with the peaked roof. Venerable Ānanda saw him coming and said to the Buddha: “Sir, here comes Saccaka — a debater, a clever speaker, well-regarded by many.”

§3Then Saccaka went to the Buddha, exchanged greetings, sat down to one side, and said:

“Master Gotama, there are some ascetics and brahmins who practice development of body but not development of mind. They are stricken by painful bodily feelings. In the past, when one was stricken by painful bodily feelings, one’s thighs would become rigid, the heart might burst, hot blood might gush from the mouth, one might go mad or lose one’s mind. In their case the mind was subservient to the body; the body had mastery. Why is that? Because the mind was not developed. But there are also some ascetics and brahmins who practice development of mind but not development of body … In their case the body was subservient to the mind. Why is that? Because the body was not developed.

Master Gotama, it occurs to me that the disciples of Master Gotama practice development of mind but not development of body.”

§4“But, Aggivessana, what have you learned about development of body?”

Saccaka described the practices of Nanda Vaccha, Kisa Saṅkicca, and Makkhali Gosāla — going naked, licking their hands, refusing invitations, eating various restricted diets.

“But, Aggivessana, do those who practice like this truly have a developed body?” “Indeed they do, Master Gotama.”

§5“Aggivessana, that is like an unbroken colt that you call a broken-in horse. It is not development of body in the training of the noble ones.”

“Then how is development of body in the training of the noble ones?”

The Buddha defines true development of body and mind: a well-taught noble disciple, when pleasant feelings arise, is not overwhelmed by them; when painful feelings arise, is not overwhelmed by them. They neither pursue pleasure nor avoid pain; they understand both without being mastered by either. This is true development.

The austerities

§6“Before my awakening, while I was still an unawakened Bodhisatta, I too thought: ‘Household life is crowded and dusty; the life gone forth is wide open. It is not easy living in a house to lead the spiritual life utterly perfect and pure as a polished shell. Let me shave off my hair and beard, put on ocher robes, and go forth from the lay life to homelessness.’

And so, while still young, a black-haired youth in the prime of life, though my mother and father wished otherwise and wept with tearful faces, I shaved off my hair and beard, put on ocher robes, and went forth from the lay life to homelessness.

§7Having gone forth, seeking what is wholesome, seeking the supreme state of sublime peace, I went to Āḷāra Kālāma … I quickly realized his teaching. But I thought: ‘This teaching does not lead to disenchantment, dispassion, cessation, peace, insight, awakening, or nibbāna.’ So I left.

Then I went to Udāka Rāmaputta … I quickly realized his teaching. But I thought the same: this does not lead to nibbāna. So I left.

§8Seeking what is wholesome, I wandered in stages through Magadha and came to Uruvelā, a pleasant spot by the river Nerañjarā — with a delightful grove, a clear-flowing river, and villages nearby for almsround. And I thought: ‘This is good enough for striving.’ So I sat down there.

The Buddha describes three forms of extreme practice:
(1) Breath suppression. He suppressed breathing through mouth and nose until violent winds tore through his body, cutting pains pierced his head as if struck by a sharp sword, and his belly burned as if over a charcoal pit.
(2) Extreme diet. He reduced food to a single grain of rice, a single sesame seed, a single jujube a day. His body became emaciated: his limbs like jointed bamboo, his ribs like rafters of a ruined hall, his eyes sank deep in their sockets like water in a deep well, his scalp shriveled like a green bitter gourd in the wind and sun. When he touched his belly, he felt his backbone; when he touched his backbone, he felt his belly.
(3) Extreme exertion. He clenched his teeth, pressed tongue against palate, and beat down, constrained, and crushed mind with mind until sweat poured from his armpits.

§9“But by these fierce austerities I did not attain any superhuman distinction in knowledge and vision worthy of the noble ones. Why was that? Because I had not reached the noble wisdom that, when reached, is noble and emancipating and leads the one who practices accordingly to the complete ending of suffering.”

The childhood memory

§10“I thought: ‘I recall that when my father the Sakyan was working, I was sitting in the cool shade of a rose-apple tree. Quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unwholesome qualities, I entered and remained in the first jhāna, which has the rapture and pleasure born of seclusion, while applied and sustained thought continue. Could that be the path to awakening?’

Then, following on that memory, came the realization: ‘That is the path to awakening.’

§11I thought: ‘Why am I afraid of that pleasure that has nothing to do with sensual pleasures and unwholesome qualities?’ I thought: ‘I am not afraid of that pleasure, for it has nothing to do with sensual pleasures and unwholesome qualities.’

I thought: ‘It is not possible to attain that pleasure with a body so extremely emaciated. Let me eat some solid food — some boiled rice and bread.’ And so I ate solid food.

The five monks who had been attending the Bodhisatta left in disgust: “The ascetic Gotama has become indulgent, he has given up the struggle and returned to luxury.” These are the same five monks who will become the Buddha’s first audience at Benares.

The four jhānas

§12“When I had eaten solid food and regained my strength, quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unwholesome qualities, I entered and remained in the first jhāna, which has the rapture and pleasure born of seclusion, while applied and sustained thought continue. But the pleasant feeling that arose in me did not invade my mind and remain.

The Bodhisatta enters the second, third, and fourth jhānas in succession. In each case, the text notes: “the pleasant feeling that arose did not invade my mind and remain” — meaning he experienced the pleasure of each jhāna without being mastered by it. This directly answers Saccaka’s opening question about development of body and mind: the Bodhisatta is pleasant but not overwhelmed.

The three knowledges

§13“When my mind was thus composed, purified, bright, unblemished, rid of imperfection, malleable, wieldy, steady, and imperturbable, I directed it to the knowledge of recollection of past lives. I recollected many past lives: one birth, two … five, ten … a hundred, a thousand … many eons of cosmic contraction and expansion. I remembered: ‘There I was so named, of such a clan, of such an appearance, eating such food, experiencing such pleasure and pain, living for so long.’ This was the first knowledge attained by me in the first watch of the night.

§14I directed my mind to the knowledge of the death and rebirth of beings. With clairvoyance that is purified and surpasses the human, I saw beings passing away and being reborn — inferior and superior, beautiful and ugly, in a good place or a bad place — and I understood how beings fare according to their deeds. This was the second knowledge attained by me in the middle watch of the night.

§15I directed my mind to the knowledge of the ending of the taints. I truly understood: ‘This is suffering. This is the origin of suffering. This is the cessation of suffering. This is the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering.’ I truly understood: ‘These are the taints. This is the origin of the taints. This is the cessation of the taints. This is the practice that leads to the cessation of the taints.’

Knowing and seeing thus, my mind was freed from the taint of sensual desire, the taint of continued existence, and the taint of ignorance. When it was freed, there came the knowledge: ‘It is freed.’ I understood: ‘Birth is ended, the spiritual life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is no more coming back to any state of being.’

This was the third knowledge attained by me in the last watch of the night. But the pleasant feeling that arose in me did not invade my mind and remain.”

Saccaka’s response

§16“I am convinced that Master Gotama truly has a developed body and a developed mind.”

“Certainly these words of yours are offensive and disrespectful, Aggivessana. Nevertheless I shall answer you. Since I shaved my hair and beard, put on ocher robes, and went forth, it has not been possible for any pleasant feeling to invade my mind and remain, nor any painful feeling.”

“It is plausible that no pleasant or painful feeling would invade Master Gotama’s mind and remain. It is truly plausible.”

That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Saccaka approved what the Buddha said.

Self-check quiz

Ten questions. Click an answer to see immediate feedback. No score is recorded — this is for your own checking.

Question 1 of 10
What is Saccaka’s opening claim in MN 36?
Correct: C. Saccaka’s opening move is different from MN 35. Here he challenges the Buddha on the distinction between developing the body and developing the mind, implying that Buddhist monks neglect physical discipline. The Buddha redefines both terms: true development means not being overwhelmed by pleasant or painful feelings.
Question 2 of 10
Who were the Bodhisatta’s two teachers before the period of austerity?
Correct: A. The Bodhisatta went first to Āḷāra Kālāma, who taught the base of nothingness, then to Udāka Rāmaputta, who taught the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. He quickly mastered both attainments but recognized they did not lead to liberation. These are formless attainments beyond the four jhānas — refined but not emancipating.
Question 3 of 10
What three forms of extreme practice does the Bodhisatta describe?
Correct: D. The three forms are vivid and physically detailed. Breath suppression produced sensations like a sword splitting the head; extreme diet reduced the body to a skeleton; extreme exertion was a battle of will against the body. All three failed to produce any superhuman distinction in knowledge.
Question 4 of 10
What is the Bodhisatta’s conclusion after six years of austerity?
Correct: B. The Bodhisatta’s conclusion is empirical, not philosophical: he tried the path of austerity to its absolute limit, and it produced nothing. The question “Could there be another path?” is the hinge of the entire narrative — the moment he opens himself to a different possibility.
Question 5 of 10
What is the childhood memory that becomes the turning point?
Correct: A. The rose-apple tree memory is the discourse’s emotional and intellectual center. After years of extreme pain, the answer comes from a moment of childhood ease. The Bodhisatta realizes that the pleasure of the first jhāna — which has nothing to do with sensual pleasures — is not something to fear but something to cultivate. This is the discovery of the middle way.
Question 6 of 10
Why did the five monks leave the Bodhisatta?
Correct: C. The five monks had been attending the Bodhisatta because they admired his extreme austerity. When he ate solid food, they saw it as a failure of discipline. Ironically, the moment they left was the moment he turned toward the path that would actually work. These same five monks became his first audience at Benares after his awakening.
Question 7 of 10
What are the three knowledges attained on the night of awakening?
Correct: B. The three knowledges (tevijjā) are attained in the three watches of the night, in the fourth jhāna. The first two (past lives, divine eye) are shared with other accomplished meditators. The third — the destruction of the taints (āsavakkhaṇa) — is unique to a Buddha’s awakening and constitutes full liberation.
Question 8 of 10
What phrase recurs after each jhāna and each knowledge?
Correct: D. This refrain is the discourse’s structural backbone. It directly answers Saccaka’s opening challenge about development of body and mind: the Bodhisatta experienced intense pleasure in each jhāna and each knowledge, but was never overwhelmed by it. This is the proof that his body and mind were truly developed.
Question 9 of 10
Why does the Buddha describe the austerities in such detail?
Correct: A. The detailed description of austerity is not self-aggrandizement. It is evidence. The Buddha’s authority to reject self-mortification comes from having tried it more thoroughly than anyone. The middle way is not a compromise but a discovery born from the failure of the extreme.
Question 10 of 10
How do MN 35 and MN 36 complement each other?
Correct: C. The pair answers the same question from two angles. MN 35: “Why is the self-view wrong?” — because you have no sovereignty over the aggregates. MN 36: “How did you discover this?” — by pushing sovereignty to its limit (austerity) and finding it empty, then discovering that liberation comes through understanding (the jhānas and the three knowledges), not through control.

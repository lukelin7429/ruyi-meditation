Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 92

With Sela

Selasutta

Setting
The land of the Aṅguttarāpans, at the town of Āpaṇa; the hermitage of Keṇiya the ascetic
Speakers
The Buddha and the brahmin Sela, teacher of three hundred students; with Keṇiya the matted-hair ascetic
Form
A verse dialogue — a learned brahmin praises the Buddha as a would-be emperor; the Buddha answers as the king of the Dhamma, and Sela and all his students go forth.
Length
~12 minutes to read
Northern parallel
Snp 3.7 (the same discourse, also in the Sutta Nipāta)
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a stirring poetic exchange culminating in a mass awakening.

Why this discourse, ninety-second

A brahmin scholar with three hundred students hears one electrifying word — “Buddha” — and cannot quite believe it: such a word is hardly to be found in the world. He goes to look, finds the marks of a great man, and praises the radiant figure before him as fit to rule the earth as a world-emperor. The Buddha’s reply turns the praise inside out: “I am a king, Sela — the supreme king of the Dhamma, who rolls the wheel that cannot be rolled back.” By the day’s end, the brahmin and all three hundred have gone forth; within a week, all are awakened.

The discourse is a verse companion to MN 91 — the same recognition of the marks, but here carried in poetry, and ending not in a single conversion but in a mass one. The learned Sela is no fool; he is a master of the Vedas and of the very science of the marks, and he tests carefully. But where Brahmāyu’s pupil watched the Buddha’s conduct for seven months, Sela uses a different key, drawn from old brahmin lore: “The fully awakened reveal themselves when praised.” So he praises — and the Buddha’s answer is the reveal.

What the exchange unfolds is one of the canon’s great images: the Buddha as a cakkavatti of the spirit. Where a world-emperor conquers the four quarters by force and rolls his chariot-wheel across the earth, the Buddha conquers by truth and rolls the wheel of the Dhamma — “the wheel that cannot be rolled back.” And when Sela asks who is the Buddha’s “general,” the answer names Sāriputta, the chief disciple who keeps the wheel turning. The whole vocabulary of kingship and conquest is lifted out of the political world and re-set in the spiritual: the only empire worth ruling is the mind, and the only conquest that lasts is over Māra.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Buddha is a king — not of any land, but of the Dhamma, who by truth rolls forth the wheel that cannot be rolled back, with Sāriputta as his general; and the learned brahmin Sela, praising him as a would-be world-emperor and hearing this answer, goes forth with all three hundred of his students and reaches awakening within a week.

The rare word

The story turns on the power of a single word. When Keṇiya says the “awakened one” (buddha) is coming, Sela is so struck that he asks twice to be sure he heard right, and reflects: “It is hard even to find the word ‘awakened one’ in the world.” This is easy to pass over, but it carries the discourse’s sense of rarity. A buddha is not merely a good teacher or a holy man — categories the world has many of — but something the world goes whole ages without: one who has discovered the path to the deathless by himself and opened it for others. The Buddha himself presses the point in his reply: “The sight of a Buddha is hard to find again… one of those whose appearance in the world is hard to find.” The discourse asks us to feel the preciousness of the encounter — that to stand before a fully awakened being is a once-in-aeons chance, not to be wasted in doubt.

Praise as a test

Sela’s method is worth noting. Having confirmed the marks, he still does not know whether this is truly a Buddha; the marks alone, as in MN 91, settle only that here is a “great man.” So he draws on a tradition: “the fully awakened reveal themselves when praised.” The idea is subtle. A pretender, praised, will preen and accept the flattery; an awakened one, beyond all conceit, will neither be inflated by praise nor disturbed by it, but will simply, calmly, redirect it to the truth. So Sela praises the Buddha lavishly — golden, radiant, formidable, fit to be emperor of the world — and watches the response. And the response is perfect: the Buddha does not deny he is a king, nor accept the worldly crown, but transposes the whole praise into a higher key. He passes the test not by humility alone but by the unshakable accuracy of his self-knowledge.

“I am a king — of the Dhamma”

Sela’s praise ends, “You are fit to be a wheel-turning monarch… may you reign as king of kings, lord of mankind!” The Buddha’s reply is one of the canon’s most resonant lines:

“I am a king, Sela, the supreme king of the teaching. By the teaching I roll forth the wheel which cannot be rolled back.”

Here the central Buddhist re-imagining of power is stated in a breath. The cakkavatti, the ideal world-emperor, was the highest worldly destiny imaginable — ruler of the four quarters, possessed of the seven treasures, conquering “without rod or sword.” The Buddha claims exactly that title and empties it of territory: he is the wheel-turner, but the wheel he rolls is the Dhamma, and the realm he wins is awakening. And his wheel “cannot be rolled back” — unlike any earthly conquest, which time and rivals always undo, the turning of the Dhamma-wheel, once set in motion, cannot be reversed. When Sela asks who is the Buddha’s “general” — the disciple who keeps the wheel rolling — the Buddha names Sāriputta, “taking after the Realized One.” The army of this king is the Saṅgha; its campaign is the spread of the teaching; its enemy is Māra, whose army the Buddha has crushed. Every word of conquest is kept — and transfigured.

“I am the supreme surgeon”

The Buddha’s self-declaration adds a second image to the king: the surgeon. “I am a Buddha, brahmin, the supreme surgeon.” The pairing is telling. A king conquers; a surgeon heals. The Buddha is both — he overcomes (Māra, the defilements) and he cures (the wound of suffering, the dart of craving). And his credential is given plainly, the same line as in MN 91: “I have known what should be known, developed what should be developed, given up what should be given up — and so, brahmin, I am a Buddha.” This is the whole of awakening in three clauses: full knowledge of the four truths, full development of the path, full abandonment of the defilements. There is no mystery in the claim, only completeness. To be a Buddha is simply to have finished, perfectly, the work that the path lays out.

The mass going-forth

What follows is swift and stirring. Sela turns to his three hundred students: “Pay heed to the Clear-eyed One… who would not be inspired by him? Those who wish may follow me; I will go forth here.” And the students answer as one: “If you endorse this teaching, we too will go forth.” Three hundred raise their cupped palms together, and the Buddha receives them all with the words that authenticate his whole path: “The spiritual life is well explained, apparent in the present life, immediately effective; the going-forth is not in vain for one who trains with diligence.” The conversion of a respected teacher carrying his whole school with him is a vivid picture of the teaching’s drawing power — and of the right relation between a teacher and his pupils, who follow Sela’s lead in conviction, not blindly but because they too have seen.

The Saṅgha, foremost field of merit — and seven days to freedom

At Keṇiya’s meal the next day, the Buddha gives verses of thanks that quietly re-rank the brahmin world’s hierarchy of “foremost” things — fire foremost of sacrifices, the Sāvitrī of prayers, the king of men, the ocean of rivers, the moon of stars, the sun of lights — and crowns the list: “for those who sacrifice seeking merit, the Saṅgha is foremost.” The supreme field in which to plant generosity is not the sacred fire but the community of the awakened and awakening. And then the close: Sela and his three hundred, “living alone, withdrawn, diligent,” reach the goal in this very life. Sela returns on the eighth day to give thanks in verse: “In these seven days, Blessed One, we have become tamed in your teaching… you have crossed over, and you bring humanity across. Stretch out your feet, great hero: let these giants bow to the Teacher.” A scholar who came to test a rumour leaves, a week later, an arahant — and brings three hundred with him across to the far shore.

Key terms

buddha — “the awakened one.” A word “hard even to find in the world”: not a good teacher or holy man (of which there are many) but one who appears only across aeons. The discourse asks us to feel the rarity.
dhamma-rāja — “king of the Dhamma.” The Buddha’s answer to being hailed as a would-be emperor: he is a king, but his realm is awakening, won by truth, not territory.
dhamma-cakka — the “wheel of the teaching,” rolled forth by the Buddha — “the wheel that cannot be rolled back.” Unlike any earthly conquest, its turning, once begun, cannot be reversed.
dhamma-senāpati — the “general of the Dhamma”: Sāriputta, who “taking after the Realized One, keeps the wheel rolling.” The army of this king is the Saṅgha; its enemy is Māra.
the supreme surgeon — the Buddha’s second self-image, paired with the king: he both conquers (Māra) and heals (the dart of suffering). “I am a Buddha… the supreme surgeon.”
“known, developed, given up” — the whole of awakening in three clauses: full knowledge of the truths, full development of the path, full abandonment of the defilements. “And so, brahmin, I am a Buddha.”

The text

The full discourse, lightly abridged. The verse dialogue, the going-forth, and the closing verses are given; the marks and the meal-frame are condensed. (Identical to Snp 3.7.) Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The rare word

§1–11So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was wandering among the Aṅguttarāpans with 1,250 mendicants, and came to Āpaṇa. The ascetic Keṇiya invited him for a meal. Now the brahmin Sela, a master of the Vedas and the marks, teaching three hundred students, saw the preparations and learned that “the awakened one” was coming. “Did you say ‘the awakened one’?” he asked twice, struck that such a word could even be found in the world. Learning the Buddha’s whereabouts, he went with his students — “Come quietly; the Buddhas are intimidating, like a lion living alone” — examined the marks, and saw all but two, which the Buddha revealed.

Sela’s praise, and the king of the Dhamma

§15–19Recalling that “the fully awakened reveal themselves when praised,” Sela extolled him: “Your body is perfect, golden, radiant… you bear all the marks… but with such excellence, what do you want with the ascetic life? You are fit to be a wheel-turning monarch — may you reign as king of kings, lord of mankind!” The Buddha replied: “I am a king, Sela — the supreme king of the teaching. By the teaching I roll forth the wheel which cannot be rolled back.” “Then who is your general, who keeps the wheel rolling?” “Sāriputta, taking after the Realized One, keeps it rolling. I have known what should be known, developed what should be developed, given up what should be given up — and so, brahmin, I am a Buddha. Dispel your doubt; the sight of a Buddha is hard to find. I am the supreme surgeon, crusher of Māra’s army.”

The going-forth of three hundred

§20–24Sela turned to his students: “Pay heed to the Clear-eyed One… who would not be inspired? Those who wish may follow me; right here I will go forth.” “If you endorse this teaching,” they replied, “we too will go forth.” “These three hundred brahmins, with cupped palms raised, ask: may we lead the spiritual life in your presence?” “The spiritual life is well explained,” said the Buddha, “apparent in the present life, immediately effective; the going-forth is not in vain for one who trains with diligence.” And Sela with all his assembly received the going-forth.

The Saṅgha foremost, and seven days to freedom

§25–28At Keṇiya’s meal the Buddha gave thanks in verse: “Fire is foremost of sacrifices, the Sāvitrī of prayers, the king of men, the ocean of rivers, the moon of stars, the sun of lights — and for those who sacrifice seeking merit, the Saṅgha is foremost.” Then Sela and his three hundred, living withdrawn and diligent, soon became perfected. On the eighth day Sela returned: “This is the eighth day since we went for refuge; in these seven days we have become tamed in your teaching. You are the Buddha, the Teacher, the sage who overcame Māra… you have crossed over, and you bring humanity across. Stretch out your feet, great hero: let these giants bow to the Teacher.”

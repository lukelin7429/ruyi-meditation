Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 131

One Fine Night

Bhaddekarattasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, addressed to the mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha alone; the mendicants only reply “yes, sir”
Form
A short, famous verse followed by a line-by-line analysis; the first of a linked set of four discourses on the same words
Length
~6 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No specific Āgama match is noted in the available commentary, though the verse recurs across MN 132–134
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — short and quotable, but its analysis repays close attention

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-thirty-first

Few verses in the Pali canon are quoted as often, in as many contexts, as the one that opens this discourse. “Don’t run back to the past, don’t anticipate the future” sounds, on first hearing, like generic advice to live in the moment — the kind of thing that could appear on a poster. What the Buddha does here is take that same verse and show it is something much more precise: a technical description of insight meditation, with an exact account of what “running to the past” and “anticipating the future” actually consist of, psychologically, and what it means to stand unshaken in the present instead.

This is the first of four discourses built around the identical verse — the Buddha teaches it here in its plainest form; the next three discourses will have it retold, expanded, and explained by other means, including the story of how it came to be transmitted at all.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Whoever meditates without delighting in memories of the past, without craving imagined futures, and without falling into the twenty ways an untrained mind mistakes the five aggregates for a self — working diligently today, because tomorrow’s death cannot be bargained away — is the one who truly “has one fine night,” regardless of which calendar night it happens to be.

The verse

The Buddha announces he will teach “the summary recital and the analysis of the one who has one fine night,” and opens with the verse itself, given here in full because everything that follows is simply an unpacking of these lines:

The verse. “Don’t run back to the past, don’t anticipate the future. What’s past is left behind, the future has not arrived; and any present phenomenon you clearly discern in every case. The unfaltering, the unshakable: having known that, foster it. Today’s the day to keenly work — who knows, tomorrow may bring death! For there is no bargain to be struck with Death and his mighty horde. One who keenly meditates like this, tireless all night and day: that’s who has one fine night — so declares the peaceful sage.”

Running to the past

The Buddha then defines each line with precision. “Running back to the past” is not simply remembering — it is “mustering delight” in that memory: thinking, “I had such form in the past… such feeling… perception… choices… consciousness in the past,” and taking pleasure in the thought. The five terms are the five aggregates (khandha) that make up a person’s experience — and the fault isn’t memory itself, but the emotional grip of identifying with how one used to be.

Anticipating the future

“Anticipating the future” works the same way, aimed forward instead of back: “mustering delight” in the thought “may I have such form… such feeling… perception… choices… consciousness in the future.” Ordinary planning and foresight aren’t the target — the commentary notes that “anticipating” danger, or awaiting one’s time with equanimity, is treated as entirely appropriate elsewhere in the canon. What’s being named here is the specific craving that clings to an imagined future self.

Faltering amid the present

The most detailed section concerns the present itself. “Faltering” happens, the Buddha says, in “an unlearned ordinary person” who has never studied the teaching of the noble ones, and who “regards form as self, self as having form, form in self, or self in form” — and repeats the same four-part confusion for feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness. That gives twenty distinct ways an untrained mind can knot itself around the five aggregates as if one of them, or some relationship to one of them, constituted a fixed self. “Not faltering” is simply the absence of all twenty — the clear, direct discernment of a “learned noble disciple” who has trained in exactly this teaching and no longer makes any of these moves.

“One fine night”

The title itself deserves unpacking. Bhaddekaratta literally means something like “one who has a single excellent night” — but as the verse makes clear, this isn’t a claim about any particular calendar night at all. Whoever “keenly meditates like this, tireless all night and day” — free of nostalgia for the past, free of craving for the future, undistorted about the present — is, by that very fact, someone who “has one fine night,” whichever night it happens to be. The urgency running underneath the whole verse is mortality itself: “who knows, tomorrow may bring death” — and, starkly, “there is no bargain to be struck with Death and his mighty horde.” The practice described is not a leisurely ideal but a response to that fact.

This discourse is the plainest of four built around these same words. The next three will show the verse being taught to a different audience, unpacked with a different emphasis by one of the Buddha’s foremost disciples in doctrinal analysis, and traced back to the very night a wandering deity first brought it to a mendicant’s attention.

Key terms

bhaddekaratta — “one who has one fine night”: not a calendar designation but a description of whoever meditates undistracted by past or future, day and night, without pause.
the five aggregates (khandha) — form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness: the five terms repeated throughout the analysis, the basic components of experience that can be either clung to or clearly discerned.
“mustering delight” (samanvāneti) — the precise fault named in running to the past or anticipating the future: not memory or foresight themselves, but the emotional grip of pleasure taken in an imagined self, past or future.
the twenty-fold self-view — for each of the five aggregates, four possible confusions (as self, self as having it, it in self, self in it) — the technical description of “faltering amid presently arisen phenomena.”
the peaceful sage — the verse’s own epithet for the Buddha, who is both its speaker and, within the verse, its authority.

The text

The discourse in full: the opening verse, the analysis of each line, and the verse repeated in closing. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The verse

§1–3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, and addressed the mendicants: “I shall teach you the summary recital and the analysis of the one who has one fine night. Listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

“Don’t run back to the past, don’t anticipate the future. What’s past is left behind, the future has not arrived; and any present phenomenon you clearly discern in every case. The unfaltering, the unshakable: having known that, foster it. Today’s the day to keenly work — who knows, tomorrow may bring death! For there is no bargain to be struck with Death and his mighty horde. One who keenly meditates like this, tireless all night and day: that’s who has one fine night — so declares the peaceful sage.

The analysis

§4–9And how do you run back to the past? You muster delight there, thinking: ‘I had such form in the past… such feeling… perception… choices… consciousness in the past.’ That’s how you run back to the past. And how do you not run back to the past? You don’t muster delight there in the same way. That’s how you don’t run back to the past.

And how do you anticipate the future? You muster delight there, thinking: ‘May I have such form in the future… such feeling… perception… choices… consciousness in the future.’ That’s how you anticipate the future. And how do you not anticipate the future? You don’t muster delight there in the same way. That’s how you don’t anticipate the future.

And how do you falter amid presently arisen phenomena? It’s when an unlearned ordinary person has not seen the noble ones, and is neither skilled nor trained in the teaching of the noble ones or of true persons. They regard form as self, self as having form, form in self, or self in form. They regard feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness in the same fourfold way. That’s how you falter amid presently arisen phenomena.

And how do you not falter amid presently arisen phenomena? It’s when a learned noble disciple has seen the noble ones, and is skilled and trained in the teaching of the noble ones and of true persons. They don’t regard form as self, self as having form, form in self, or self in form — nor feeling, perception, choices, or consciousness in any of these four ways. That’s how you don’t falter amid presently arisen phenomena.

The verse, repeated

§10–11‘Don’t run back to the past, don’t anticipate the future. What’s past is left behind, the future has not arrived; and any present phenomenon you clearly discern in every case. The unfaltering, the unshakable: having known that, foster it. Today’s the day to keenly work — who knows, tomorrow may bring death! For there is no bargain to be struck with Death and his mighty horde. One who keenly meditates like this, tireless all night and day: that’s who has one fine night — so declares the peaceful sage.’ And that’s what I meant when I said: ‘I shall teach you the summary recital and the analysis of the one who has one fine night.’” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said.

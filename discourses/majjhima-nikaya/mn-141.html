Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 141

The Analysis of the Truths

Saccavibhaṅgasutta

Setting
Near Varanasi, in the deer park at Isipatana — the very place of the Buddha’s first sermon
Speakers
The Buddha gives a brief statement and withdraws; Venerable Sāriputta then delivers the full exposition to the assembled mendicants
Form
A short narrative frame around the canonical, complete definitions of the four noble truths
Length
~15 minutes to read
Northern parallel
Confidently identified as MĀ 31 in the Madhyama-āgama, which likewise presents Sāriputta’s detailed exposition of the four noble truths
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — technically dense but exceptionally clear and systematic, the doctrinal core of the entire teaching in analytical form

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-forty-first

This is the sutta that goes back to where everything began. Isipatana, the deer park near Varanasi, is where the Buddha gave his first teaching to the five ascetics after his awakening — the famous “setting in motion of the Wheel of Dhamma.” Here, decades into his teaching career, he returns to that same ground and to that same four-part structure, but this time hands the detailed exposition to his foremost disciple in wisdom, Sāriputta. What follows is not a summary or a simplified version but the full canonical definition of suffering, its cause, its end, and the path that ends it — the doctrinal skeleton beneath nearly everything else the Buddha taught, laid out here with no shortcuts.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

At Isipatana, where the Wheel of Dhamma was first set rolling, the Buddha briefly restates the four noble truths and then withdraws, leaving Sāriputta to give their complete technical definition — suffering as birth, aging, death, sorrow, and the five grasping aggregates; its origin as craving in three forms; its cessation as that craving’s complete fading away; and the path to that cessation as the noble eightfold path, each factor defined in full.

Back at the deer park

The opening lines are almost liturgical, and deliberately so: “Near Varanasi, in the deer park at Isipatana, the Realized One… rolled forth the supreme Wheel of Dhamma. And that wheel cannot be rolled back by any ascetic or brahmin or god or Māra or divinity or by anyone in the world.” This is the same language used to describe the Buddha’s first sermon (recorded separately as SN 56.11), and its reappearance here is not incidental. By anchoring this exposition to that founding moment and that founding formula, the discourse presents itself as a direct continuation of the very first teaching — not a later addition, but the same wheel, still turning.

Two models for training

Before withdrawing, the Buddha pauses to commend two of his disciples: “You should cultivate friendship with Sāriputta and Moggallāna… Sāriputta is like one who gives birth, while Moggallāna is like one who raises the child. Sāriputta guides people to the fruit of stream-entry, Moggallāna to the highest goal.” The image is deliberately domestic — two roles in raising a single child, not two competitors. Sāriputta gets a disciple started on the path; Moggallāna sees them through to its end. The Buddha adds, as if handing over a credential, that Sāriputta specifically “is able to explain, teach, assert, establish, clarify, analyze, and reveal the four noble truths in detail” — which is exactly what happens next.

The Buddha withdraws, Sāriputta continues

Having said this, the Buddha rises and enters his dwelling, and Sāriputta immediately takes over, repeating the identical “Wheel of Dhamma” formula before beginning his exposition. The literary effect is striking: the teacher steps back entirely, and the discourse becomes proof, in real time, of the very praise just given. Tradition remembers Sāriputta by the epithet “General of the Dhamma” for exactly this kind of role — carrying out, in full technical detail, what the Buddha himself had only sketched.

Suffering, defined completely

Sāriputta’s definition of the first truth does not stop at the famous short list. Each item is defined in turn: rebirth as “the rebirth, inception, conception… of the various sentient beings”; old age as “decrepitude, broken teeth, grey hair, wrinkly skin…”; death as the “disintegration… breaking up of the aggregates”; sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress each given their own careful gloss (sorrow and lamentation as inner and outer expressions of loss; pain and sadness as physical and mental unpleasant feeling); and “not getting what you wish for” illustrated with the plainly human wish, “oh, if only we were not liable to be reborn.” The whole list is then compressed back down: “in brief, the five grasping aggregates are suffering” — form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness, whenever grasped at as one’s own.

The origin: craving in three forms

The second truth names a single cause — craving (taṇhā) — but specifies three directions it takes: craving for sensual pleasures, craving for continued existence, and, just as tellingly, craving for nonexistence. That third form matters: even the wish to simply stop existing is still, on this analysis, a form of craving, not an escape from it. The origin of suffering is not any particular desired object but the underlying momentum of relishing and grasping wherever it lands.

Cessation: craving’s fading away

The third truth is defined with matching precision as the complete fading away of that same craving, “with no residue left behind,” described through a cluster of near-synonyms — giving it away, letting it go, releasing it, not clinging to it. Cessation, notably, is not described as the arising of some new, separate state, but as the exhaustion of the very thing named in the second truth.

The path, factor by factor

The fourth truth, the noble eightfold path, receives the fullest treatment of all — each of its eight factors given a working definition rather than left as a label.

Path factorDefinition given
Right viewKnowing the four truths themselves
Right purposePurposes of renunciation, good will, and harmlessness
Right speechRefraining from lying, backbiting, harsh speech, and nonsense
Right actionRefraining from killing, stealing, and sexual misconduct
Right livelihoodGiving up wrong livelihood, earning a living rightly
Right effortPreventing, abandoning, developing, and maintaining skillful/unskillful states
Right mindfulnessObserving body, feelings, mind, and principles
Right immersionThe four absorptions, from the first through the fourth

Right immersion is spelled out especially fully — the same four-jhāna formula that recurs across the canon, from the rapture and bliss of the first absorption through to the “pure equanimity and mindfulness” of the fourth, “without pleasure or pain.” Nothing in this list is left as shorthand for the listener to fill in privately.

Closing: the wheel rolls on

Sāriputta closes precisely where he began, repeating the Wheel of Dhamma formula a final time. The discourse’s structure — frame, withdrawal, full exposition, frame again — mirrors its content: the four truths are shown, not merely told, to be sturdy enough that even a senior disciple, not the Buddha himself, can carry them through in full and have the mendicants approve what he said.

Key terms

ariya sacca — “noble truth”: the four truths, given here in their full canonical technical definition rather than the familiar short formula alone.
taṇhā — “craving”: the origin of suffering, specified in three forms — for sensual pleasures, for existence, and for nonexistence.
Isipatana — the deer park near Varanasi where the Buddha’s first sermon was given; this discourse deliberately echoes that occasion’s opening formula.
Sāriputta — the Buddha’s chief disciple in wisdom, traditionally called “General of the Dhamma,” entrusted here to deliver the full technical exposition after the Buddha withdraws.
“the Wheel of Dhamma… cannot be rolled back” — the discourse’s frame, spoken at the very start and repeated verbatim at the very end.

The text

The discourse in full, given complete and without abbreviation: the Buddha’s brief statement and praise of Sāriputta and Moggallāna, and then Sāriputta’s entire detailed exposition of the four noble truths, including every item of the first truth’s list and every factor of the eightfold path. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

At the deer park

§1–3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Varanasi, in the deer park at Isipatana. There the Buddha addressed the mendicants, “Mendicants!” “Venerable sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

“Near Varanasi, in the deer park at Isipatana, the Realized One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha rolled forth the supreme Wheel of Dhamma. And that wheel cannot be rolled back by any ascetic or brahmin or god or Māra or divinity or by anyone in the world. It is the teaching, advocating, establishing, clarifying, analyzing, and revealing of the four noble truths. What four? The noble truths of suffering, the origin of suffering, the cessation of suffering, and the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering.

Two models for training

§4–6Near Varanasi, in the deer park at Isipatana, the Realized One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha rolled forth the supreme Wheel of Dhamma. And that wheel cannot be rolled back by any ascetic or brahmin or god or Māra or divinity or by anyone in the world. It is the teaching, advocating, establishing, clarifying, analyzing, and revealing of the four noble truths.

Mendicants, you should cultivate friendship with Sāriputta and Moggallāna. You should associate with Sāriputta and Moggallāna. They’re astute, and they support their spiritual companions. Sāriputta is like one who gives birth, while Moggallāna is like one who raises the child. Sāriputta guides people to the fruit of stream-entry, Moggallāna to the highest goal. Sāriputta is able to explain, teach, assert, establish, clarify, analyze, and reveal the four noble truths in detail.”

That is what the Buddha said. When he had spoken, the Holy One rose from his seat and entered his dwelling.

Sāriputta continues

§7–9Then soon after the Buddha left, Venerable Sāriputta said to the mendicants, “Reverends, mendicants!” “Reverend,” they replied. Sāriputta said this:

“Near Varanasi, in the deer park at Isipatana, the Realized One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha rolled forth the supreme Wheel of Dhamma. And that wheel cannot be rolled back by any ascetic or brahmin or god or Māra or divinity or by anyone in the world. It is the teaching, advocating, establishing, clarifying, analyzing, and revealing of the four noble truths. What four? The noble truths of suffering, the origin of suffering, the cessation of suffering, and the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering.

Suffering, defined completely

§10–20And what is the noble truth of suffering? Rebirth is suffering; old age is suffering; death is suffering; sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress are suffering; not getting what you wish for is suffering. In brief, the five grasping aggregates are suffering.

And what is rebirth? The rebirth, inception, conception, regeneration, manifestation of the aggregates, and acquisition of the sense fields of the various sentient beings in the various orders of sentient beings. This is called rebirth.

And what is old age? The old age, decrepitude, broken teeth, grey hair, wrinkly skin, diminished vitality, and failing faculties of the various sentient beings in the various orders of sentient beings. This is called old age.

And what is death? The passing away, passing on, disintegration, demise, mortality, death, decease, breaking up of the aggregates, laying to rest of the corpse, and cutting off of the life faculty of the various sentient beings in the various orders of sentient beings. This is called death.

And what is sorrow? The sorrow, sorrowing, state of sorrow, inner sorrow, inner deep sorrow in someone who has undergone misfortune, who has experienced suffering. This is called sorrow.

And what is lamentation? The wail, lament, wailing, lamenting, state of wailing and lamentation in someone who has undergone misfortune, who has experienced suffering. This is called lamentation.

And what is pain? Physical pain, physical unpleasantness, the painful, unpleasant feeling that’s born from physical contact. This is called pain.

And what is sadness? Mental pain, mental displeasure, the painful, unpleasant feeling that’s born from mind contact. This is called sadness.

And what is distress? The stress, distress, state of stress and distress in someone who has undergone misfortune, who has experienced suffering. This is called distress.

And what is ‘not getting what you wish for is suffering’? In sentient beings who are liable to be reborn, such a wish arises: ‘Oh, if only we were not liable to be reborn! If only rebirth would not come to us!’ But you can’t get that by wishing. This is: ‘not getting what you wish for is suffering.’ In sentient beings who are liable to grow old… fall ill… die… experience sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress, such a wish arises: ‘Oh, if only we were not liable to experience sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress! If only sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress would not come to us!’ But you can’t get that by wishing. This is: ‘not getting what you wish for is suffering.’

And what is ‘in brief, the five grasping aggregates are suffering’? They are the grasping aggregates that consist of form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness. This is called ‘in brief, the five grasping aggregates are suffering.’ This is called the noble truth of suffering.

The origin: craving in three forms

§21And what is the noble truth of the origin of suffering? It’s the craving that leads to future lives, mixed up with relishing and greed, taking pleasure there wherever it alights. That is, craving for sensual pleasures, craving for existence, and craving for nonexistence. This is called the noble truth of the origin of suffering.

Cessation: craving’s fading away

§22And what is the noble truth of the cessation of suffering? It’s the fading away and cessation of that very same craving with no residue left behind; giving it away, letting it go, releasing it, and not clinging to it. This is called the noble truth of the cessation of suffering.

The path, factor by factor

§23–31And what is the noble truth of the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering? It is simply this noble eightfold path, that is: right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion.

And what is right view? Knowing about suffering, the origin of suffering, the cessation of suffering, and the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering. This is called right view.

And what is right purpose? Purposes of renunciation, good will, and harmlessness. This is called right purpose.

And what is right speech? Refraining from lying, backbiting, harsh speech, and talking nonsense. This is called right speech.

And what is right action? Refraining from killing living creatures, stealing, and sexual misconduct. This is called right action.

And what is right livelihood? It’s when a noble disciple gives up wrong livelihood and earns a living by right livelihood. This is called right livelihood.

And what is right effort? It’s when a mendicant generates enthusiasm, tries, makes an effort, exerts the mind, and strives so that bad, unskillful qualities don’t arise. They generate enthusiasm, try, make an effort, exert the mind, and strive so that bad, unskillful qualities that have arisen are given up. They generate enthusiasm, try, make an effort, exert the mind, and strive so that skillful qualities arise. They generate enthusiasm, try, make an effort, exert the mind, and strive so that skillful qualities that have arisen remain, are not lost, but increase, mature, and are completed by development. This is called right effort.

And what is right mindfulness? It’s when a mendicant meditates by observing an aspect of the body—keen, aware, and mindful, rid of covetousness and displeasure for the world. They meditate observing an aspect of feelings… mind… principles—keen, aware, and mindful, rid of covetousness and displeasure for the world. This is called right mindfulness.

And what is right immersion? It’s when a mendicant, quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unskillful qualities, enters and remains in the first absorption, which has the rapture and bliss born of seclusion, while placing the mind and keeping it connected. As the placing of the mind and keeping it connected are stilled, they enter and remain in the second absorption, which has the rapture and bliss born of immersion, with internal clarity and mind at one, without placing the mind and keeping it connected. And with the fading away of rapture, they enter and remain in the third absorption, where they meditate with equanimity, mindful and aware, personally experiencing the bliss of which the noble ones declare, ‘Equanimous and mindful, one meditates in bliss.’ With the giving up of pleasure and pain and the disappearance of former happiness and sadness, they enter and remain in the fourth absorption, without pleasure or pain, with pure equanimity and mindfulness. This is called right immersion. This is called the noble truth of the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering.

Closing: the wheel rolls on

§32Near Varanasi, in the deer park at Isipatana, the Realized One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha rolled forth the supreme Wheel of Dhamma. And that wheel cannot be rolled back by any ascetic or brahmin or god or Māra or divinity or by anyone in the world. It is the teaching, advocating, establishing, clarifying, analyzing, and revealing of the four noble truths.” That’s what Venerable Sāriputta said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what Sāriputta said.

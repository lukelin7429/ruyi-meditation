Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 102

The Five and Three

Pañcattayasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, addressed to the mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha, surveying the speculative views of his time
Form
A map of all theories of self and afterlife, each shown to be conditioned and grasped — and transcended.
Length
~17 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No counterpart is known in the Chinese Āgamas
Difficulty
★★★★★ — the canon’s densest survey of views; read slowly.

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-second

What happens to the self after death? Was the cosmos there from the beginning? Every culture has its answers, and the Buddha’s India had dozens. Here he lays them all out on a single map — five theories of where we end, sixteen of where we began — and shows that each one, however subtle, is built of the same flawed material: it is conditioned, it is grasped at, and so it cannot be the peace it promises. Even the meditator’s serene “I am free of grasping” turns out to be one more handhold.

This is among the most demanding discourses in the collection — a companion to the great net of views in the Brahmajāla (DN 1), but turned toward a single practical purpose. The Buddha is not refuting each theory on its own terms; he is showing the structure they share. A refrain tolls through the whole text like a bell: “All that is conditioned and crude. But there is the cessation of conditions — that is real.” Whatever the view, the Realized One “understands it, sees the escape from it, and has gone beyond.”

The title, “The Five and Three,” names a counting trick: five theories of the self’s destiny that collapse into three, and expand back into five. But the heart of the discourse is its closing turn. After dismantling every position — including the highest meditative states, which rise and fall “like sunlight and shadow,” and even the refined conceit of one who thinks himself already free — the Buddha names the one thing that is not a view at all: liberation by not grasping, won through understanding the six fields of sense-contact. Freedom is not a better theory of the self; it is the release that comes when grasping itself is let go.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Every theory of the self’s destiny and the cosmos’s origin — and every refined meditative state taken as final — is conditioned, crude, and grasped at, even the thought “I am free of grasping”; the Realized One alone is freed, not by a better view but by liberation through not grasping, having understood the six fields of contact.

The refrain to hold onto

Before the details, fix the sentence that returns after every position the Buddha examines:

“All that is conditioned and crude. But there is the cessation of conditions — that is real.” Whatever is fabricated by thought, perception, or attainment is saṅkhata — put together, and therefore fragile, coarse, bound to pass. Only the stilling of all such fabricating is the genuine peace. “Understanding this and seeing the escape, the Realized One has gone beyond.”

This is the lens through which the whole survey is read. The Buddha does not argue that the eternalist is wrong and the annihilationist right, or vice versa. He shows that all of them are handling conditioned things and mistaking one for the unconditioned.

The five theories of destiny (and why “five and three”)

First, the theories of the “final end” — what becomes of the self after death:

  1. The self survives death percipient (conscious), “healthy,” in some form.
  2. The self survives death non-percipient.
  3. The self survives death neither percipient nor non-percipient.
  4. Annihilation: the being is utterly cut off and ceases at death.
  5. Extinguishment here and now: liberation in this very life.

The first three all affirm a self that survives — so they fold into one family (“an existent self after death”), giving three broad positions: survival, annihilation, present extinguishment. Expand the survival-family back out and you have five. Hence “five become three, and three become five.” The counting is not the point; it is a way of showing that the whole crowded field reduces to a few moves repeated with variations — each endlessly subdivided (formed or formless, unified or diverse perception, and so on) without ever escaping the conditioned.

How the camps refute each other

The Buddha lets the theorists expose one another. The non-perceptionists reject the perceptionists because they hold “perception is a disease, a boil, a dart,” and prize a perception-free survival. The neither-perceptionists reject both, calling perception a disease and bare non-perception a “stupor.” And the annihilationists mock all the survivalists as wishful traders:

The trader, and the leashed dog. The survivalists are like a merchant muttering “with this, that shall be mine” — greedily projecting a future self: “After death I shall be thus!” But the annihilationists are no freer: “from fear and disgust with substantial reality, they keep running and circling around it” — like a dog leashed to a post, forever orbiting the very self it dreads. Both remain bound to identity; one by craving it, one by recoiling from it.

The Buddha’s deeper observation about the perceptionists is worth noting: you cannot describe a consciousness “coming and going, growing and maturing” apart from form, feeling, perception, and choices. A disembodied surviving consciousness is not a coherent idea — it is always parasitic on the very aggregates it claims to transcend.

The sixteen theories of origin

Next, the theories of the “first beginning” — the self and cosmos as eternal or not, finite or infinite, of unified or diverse perception, experiencing only happiness or only suffering, and so on: sixteen positions, each insisting “this alone is true, all else is futile.” The Buddha’s critique is the epistemology of MN 95 applied here: no one can have “pure and bright personal knowledge” of such cosmic claims; they rest only on the five unreliable grounds (faith, endorsement, transmission, reasoning, accepted opinion). And in the absence of real knowing, “even the portion they illuminate is grasping on their part.” Without seeing, every confident assertion about origins is just another grip.

When even the meditator grasps

Here the discourse turns subtle and personal. Consider those who wisely drop all such theories and turn to practice. One enters the rapture of seclusion (the joy of the lower absorptions): “This is peaceful, this is sublime!” — but it ceases, and when it ceases, sadness arises; and when sadness ceases, the rapture returns:

Sunlight and shadow. “As sunlight fills the space when the shadow leaves, and shadow when the sunlight leaves” — so these refined states and their absence keep displacing one another. To take any of them as the final peace is to be caught in the very alternation.

The seeker climbs higher — beyond rapture to pleasure not of the flesh (the third absorption’s bliss), beyond that to neutral feeling (the equanimity of the fourth) — and each, taken as final, still rises and falls. Finally one goes beyond even these and thinks: “I am at peace; I am quenched; I am free of grasping.” This, the Buddha grants, “is a practice conducive to extinguishment.” And yet — the sharpest point in the sutta — the very self-regard “I am free of grasping” is itself a grasping. The last subtle clinging is the conceit of being free.

The one thing that is not a view

After every position — crude and refined alike — has been seen as conditioned and let go, the discourse closes on what the Realized One actually awakened to:

“Liberation by not grasping, after truly understanding the six fields of contact’s origin, disappearance, gratification, drawback, and escape.”

The contrast is total. Every theory tried to secure something — a surviving self, a known origin, an attained state — and securing is grasping. Freedom is not a finer thing to hold but the laying-down of the hand. And the way to it is not metaphysical speculation about self and cosmos but direct, experiential understanding of the six fields of contact (eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mind, and their objects) — how they arise, fade, allure, endanger, and can be escaped. Look not outward to theories of the universe, but inward to the points where experience touches the world; understand them fully; grasp at nothing — that is the supreme peace the theorists were all groping for and could not, by grasping, ever reach.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats the long lists of variants. The five theories, the trader and the dog, the sixteen origin-views, the rise and fall of meditative states, and the closing on non-grasping are given closely. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The five and three

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, and addressed the mendicants. “Mendicants, there are ascetics and brahmins who theorize about the final end. Some say: ‘The self is percipient and healthy after death’; some, ‘non-percipient’; some, ‘neither percipient nor non-percipient.’ Some assert the annihilation of an existing being; and some propose extinguishment in this life. So they assert a self that survives, or its annihilation, or present extinguishment. In this way five become three, and three become five.”

The camps reject each other

§3–10“Those who assert a percipient self describe it as formed or formless, of unified or diverse perception, and so on. The non-perceptionists reject them, holding ‘perception is a disease, a boil, a dart,’ and that non-perception is peaceful. The neither-perceptionists reject both. But none can describe a consciousness ‘coming and going, growing and maturing’ apart from form, feeling, perception, and choices — that is not possible. In each case: ‘All that is conditioned and crude; but there is the cessation of conditions — that is real.’ Seeing the escape, the Realized One has gone beyond.”

The trader and the leashed dog

§11–12“The annihilationists reject all the survivalists, thinking: ‘These are attached to heading upstream — after death I shall be thus! — like a trader at market: with this, that shall be mine.’ But the Realized One sees that the annihilationists too, ‘from fear and disgust with substantial reality, keep running and circling around it — like a dog leashed to a post, circling that very post.’ All that is conditioned and crude; the cessation of conditions is real. The Realized One has gone beyond.”

The sixteen theories of origin

§14–16“Others theorize about the first beginning: ‘The self and cosmos are eternal’ — or not eternal, both, neither; finite, infinite, both, neither; of unified or diverse or limited or limitless perception; experiencing only happiness, only suffering, both, or neither — each insisting ‘this alone is true.’ But it is not possible for them to have pure personal knowledge of this apart from faith, endorsement, oral transmission, reasoning, or accepted opinion. Lacking such knowledge, even the portion they illuminate is grasping. All that is conditioned and crude; the cessation of conditions is real.”

Sunlight and shadow

§17–22“Now, some let go of theories of beginning and end, and enter the rapture of seclusion: ‘This is peaceful, this is sublime!’ But it ceases; and when it ceases, sadness arises; and when sadness ceases, the rapture returns — as sunlight fills the space when the shadow leaves, and shadow when the sunlight leaves. So too with pleasure not of the flesh, and with neutral feeling: each, taken as final, still rises and falls. All that is conditioned and crude; the cessation of conditions is real.”

Even “I am free of grasping”

§23–24“And some, going beyond even these, regard themselves: ‘I am at peace; I am quenched; I am free of grasping.’ This is indeed a practice conducive to extinguishment. Yet they still grasp — at theories, at the fetters of sensuality, at the states they have passed through; and the very thought ‘I am free of grasping’ is itself grasping. All that is conditioned and crude; the cessation of conditions is real. The Realized One has gone beyond.”

Liberation by not grasping

§25“But the Realized One has awakened to the supreme state of sublime peace: liberation by not grasping, after truly understanding the six fields of contact’s origin, disappearance, gratification, drawback, and escape.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what he said.

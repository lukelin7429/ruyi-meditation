Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 80

With Vekhanasa

Vekhanasasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speakers
The Buddha and the wanderer Vekhanasa, addressed by his clan name Kaccāna
Form
A compact companion to MN 79 — the same exposure of an empty ideal, then a teaching on the pleasure beyond the senses, a flash of wounded pride, and the simile of a child outgrowing its swaddling.
Length
~8 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No counterpart survives in the Chinese Āgamas
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — accessible; closes the Wanderers’ Chapter by gathering its themes.

Why this discourse, eightieth

Another wanderer arrives with the same grand slogan that undid Sakuludāyī — “This is the ultimate splendour!” — and the Buddha gives it the same patient dismantling. But the conversation then turns somewhere new: to the difference between sensual pleasure and the pleasure that rises above it, to the proud anger of a man told he cannot yet understand, and to a quiet image of how freedom actually comes — not from knowing the cosmos’s edges, but from growing, like a child, out of a binding.

Vekhanasa — of the Kaccāna clan, and clearly of the same school as Sakuludāyī — opens not with a question but with a declaration of faith: “This is the ultimate splendour, this is the ultimate splendour.” The Buddha asks the obvious thing: what splendour? And the doctrine unravels exactly as in the previous discourse: Vekhanasa can only say “the splendour than which none is finer,” cannot describe it, is shown by the simile of the man in love with an unseen woman that his claim “has no demonstrable basis,” and is walked up the ladder of brightnesses — firefly, lamp, bonfire, morning star, moon, sun — until his “ultimate” stands revealed as dimmer than a firefly.

Where MN 79 then turned to “perfect happiness,” this discourse turns to pleasure itself. The Buddha distinguishes the pleasure born of the five senses from “the pleasure that surmounts the sensual” — and says this latter is the best of the two. Vekhanasa eagerly agrees — until the Buddha adds that only those who are fully liberated truly understand it. That gentle limit stings his pride, and his reaction, and the Buddha’s response to it, give the discourse its real teaching.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

An ideal that cannot be described is empty (the “ultimate splendour,” dimmer than a firefly); the highest pleasure is not the sensual but the pleasure that surmounts it, which only the liberated truly know; and liberation is verified not by knowing the cosmos’s beginning or end but by honest practice — growing free of the bond of ignorance as a child grows out of its swaddling.

The empty ideal, exposed again

The discourse opens by re-running, almost word for word, the critique of “ultimate splendour” (paramavaṇṇa) from MN 79. Vekhanasa proclaims it; cannot define it beyond “the splendour than which none is finer”; is shown by the unseen-lady simile that desiring what one cannot describe “has no demonstrable basis”; and finally offers content — a flawless beryl gem on a cream rug, “the splendour of the self that is free of disease after death.” The Buddha then climbs the same ladder of brightnesses (gem < firefly < lamp < bonfire < morning star < full moon < midday sun < radiant gods), and the conclusion bites the same way: the thing called “ultimate” is fainter than a firefly, and still undescribed. That this critique is given twice, to two members of the same school, suggests it was a stock teaching for a stock doctrine — a recognised piece of philosophical hygiene against impressive words with nothing behind them.

The pleasure that surmounts the sensual

Here the discourse breaks new ground. The Buddha names the five kinds of sensual stimulation — pleasing sights, sounds, smells, tastes, and touches — and the “sensual pleasure” (kāma-sukha) that arises from them. Then the key line: “From the senses comes sensual pleasure. Beyond sensual pleasure is the pleasure that surmounts the sensual — and that is said to be the best of these.” This is the heart of the Buddhist reframing of happiness: there is a real, higher joy — the bliss of seclusion, the absorptions, and ultimately liberation — that does not depend on the senses at all, and is finer than any sensual delight. It is the positive counterpart to the leper simile of MN 75: not only is sensual pleasure a fire misperceived as bliss, there is an actual bliss elsewhere, worth more.

The limit that wounds pride

Vekhanasa is delighted and repeats the line approvingly. But the Buddha adds a frank caveat: “Since you have a different view, creed, and belief, it is hard for you to understand the senses, sensual pleasure, and the pleasure that surmounts the sensual — unless you dedicate yourself to practice. It is the perfected ones, with defilements ended, who understand it.” This is not contempt but accuracy: a higher pleasure that lies entirely beyond the senses cannot be grasped by someone who still measures all pleasure by the senses; it has to be tasted, through practice, to be known. But to Vekhanasa it lands as an insult. He “becomes angry and upset,” even badmouths the Buddha — “The ascetic Gotama will be worsted!” — and lashes out with a counter-charge: that some ascetics claim “rebirth is ended” while “not knowing the first beginning or seeing the final end,” so their claim is “a joke, mere hollow words.” Wounded pride reaches, as it often does, for the accusation that the other is the empty one.

Liberation is not knowledge of beginnings and ends

The Buddha’s reply is the discourse’s real teaching, and it quietly demolishes Vekhanasa’s premise. He grants the narrow point: yes, some who claim awakening without basis can be legitimately refuted. But then — the familiar move — “leave aside the first beginning and the final end.” Liberation has nothing to do with knowing how the cosmos started or how it will end. “Let a sensible person come — honest, of integrity, neither devious nor deceitful. I teach them; and practising as instructed, they will soon know and see for themselves how to be released from the bond of ignorance.” The validity of awakening is shown not by metaphysical credentials but by a transformation the practitioner can verify in their own experience. Vekhanasa’s objection assumes the goal is cosmic knowledge; the Buddha answers that the goal is freedom, and freedom is its own evidence.

The child and the swaddling

The closing simile is tender and exact. A newborn lies bound in swaddling-cloth, wrapped tight to the neck. As the child grows and its faculties mature, it is “released from those bonds” — not by being taught the history of cloth, but simply by growing; and then it knows, “I am released,” and the bonds are gone. So too the practitioner: bound by the “bond of ignorance” (avijjā-yoga), they are released through the maturing that practice brings, and come to know their own freedom directly. Liberation is organic and verifiable, like growing up — not a doctrine to be asserted but a development to be lived through. On hearing this, Vekhanasa’s anger dissolves; he says, “Excellent!” and goes for refuge for life. Unlike Sandaka and Sakuludāyī, who saw the truth and were held back — by reputation, by their followers — Vekhanasa moves through his flash of pride and arrives. The Wanderers’ Chapter, which has shown so many who came close, closes on one who makes it.

Key terms

paramavaṇṇa — “ultimate splendour,” the same undefined ideal exposed in MN 79: a grand word shown, by the unseen-lady simile and the ladder of brightnesses, to be dimmer than a firefly.
kāma-sukha — “sensual pleasure,” the happiness arising from the five kinds of sensual stimulation. Real, but not the highest.
the pleasure that surmounts the sensual — the higher joy beyond the senses (the bliss of seclusion, absorption, and liberation), “said to be the best.” Only the liberated truly understand it.
pubbanta / aparanta — “the first beginning” and “the final end.” Vekhanasa makes them the test of true awakening; the Buddha sets them aside — liberation does not depend on cosmic knowledge.
avijjā-yoga — the “bond of ignorance,” the real binding from which one is freed. Not undone by knowing beginnings and ends, but outgrown through practice.
the swaddled child — the closing simile: as a child grows out of its swaddling-bands simply by maturing, so the practitioner is released from ignorance and knows directly, “I am released.”

The text

The full discourse. The opening critique of “ultimate splendour” closely repeats MN 79 and is condensed here; the teaching on pleasure, the angry exchange, and the swaddling simile are given in full. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

“The ultimate splendour” — again

§1–11So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove. The wanderer Vekhanasa came and, standing to one side, declared, “This is the ultimate splendour, this is the ultimate splendour!” “But Kaccāna, what is that ultimate splendour?” “The splendour compared to which no other is finer.” As in the previous discourse, the Buddha shows by the simile of the man who loves a woman he has never seen that such a claim “has no demonstrable basis,” and then leads Vekhanasa up the ladder of brightnesses — gem, firefly, oil lamp, bonfire, morning star, full moon, midday autumn sun, and gods beyond sun and moon — concluding: “Of a splendour dimmer than a firefly you say, ‘This is the ultimate’ — and you cannot describe it.”

The pleasure beyond the senses

§12–14“Kaccāna, there are five kinds of sensual stimulation: pleasing sights, sounds, smells, tastes, and touches. The pleasure and happiness that arise from these is called sensual pleasure. From the senses comes sensual pleasure; but beyond sensual pleasure is the pleasure that surmounts the sensual — and that is said to be the best of these.” “It’s incredible, worthy Gotama, how well said! From the senses comes sensual pleasure; beyond it is the pleasure that surmounts the sensual, the best of these.” “But Kaccāna, since you have a different view, creed, and belief, it is hard for you to understand the senses, sensual pleasure, and the pleasure that surmounts the sensual — unless you dedicate yourself to practice. It is the perfected ones, with defilements ended, who understand it.”

Wounded pride

§15At this Vekhanasa became angry and upset, and even badmouthed the Buddha: “The ascetic Gotama will be worsted!” He said, “This is just what some ascetics and brahmins do: not knowing the first beginning or seeing the final end, they still claim, ‘Rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey completed… there is nothing further for this place.’ Their statement turns out to be a joke — mere words, vacuous and hollow.”

Not knowledge of beginnings, but release

§16“Kaccāna, some who claim awakening without basis can indeed be legitimately refuted. But leave aside the first beginning and the final end. Let a sensible person come — honest, of integrity, neither devious nor deceitful. I teach and instruct them; and practising as instructed, they will soon know and see for themselves, ‘So this is how to be rightly released from the bond — the bond of ignorance.’ Suppose a little baby were bound with swaddling up to the neck. As they grow up and their faculties mature, they are released from those bonds, and they know, ‘I am released,’ and there are no more bonds. In the same way, let a sensible person of integrity come; practising as I instruct, they will soon know and see for themselves how to be released from the bond of ignorance.”

Refuge

§17At this Vekhanasa said, “Excellent, worthy Gotama! Excellent! As if righting the overturned, or revealing the hidden, or lighting a lamp in the dark… From this day forth, may the worthy Gotama remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.”

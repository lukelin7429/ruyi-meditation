Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 107

With Moggallāna the Accountant

Gaṇakamoggallānasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in the Eastern Monastery, the longhouse of Migāra’s mother
Speakers
The Buddha and the brahmin Moggallāna the Accountant
Form
A step-by-step map of the training, and a frank answer to why not everyone arrives.
Length
~14 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A counterpart is preserved in the Chinese Madhyama Āgama (MĀ 144)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the classic outline of the gradual training.

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-seventh

An accountant notices that everything worth learning is learned by stages — the steps of a staircase, the recitation of the brahmins, the drawing of a bow, the counting tables he drills into his own pupils. Is the Buddha’s teaching like that too — a gradual training? It is, the Buddha replies, and lays out the path one rung at a time. But the discourse’s sharpest moment comes after: if the path is so clear, why do some who walk it arrive and others do not?

Moggallāna the Accountant is a practical man, and his question is the practical one: does liberation have a curriculum? The Buddha’s answer is one of the canon’s clearest statements of the gradual training (anupubbasikkhā) — the same sequence found in the long discourses, here told briefly and vividly, beginning with the image of a horse-trainer who first gets a fine colt used to the bit before anything else.

Then the honest question: with the goal real, the path laid out, and the Buddha himself there to encourage, why do some disciples reach extinguishment and some fall short? The Buddha does not pretend otherwise — “some succeed, some fail” — and answers with the famous simile of the road to Rājagaha. A man who knows the road perfectly can describe every turn; yet one traveller still goes west and another arrives. “What can I do? The Realized One is the one who shows the way.” The teaching is a map, complete and reliable; but the walking is the disciple’s own.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Like any craft, the Buddha’s path is a gradual training — ethics, sense-restraint, moderation in eating, wakefulness, mindfulness, seclusion, and the absorptions, step building on step; yet whether a disciple reaches the goal is not guaranteed by the path’s clarity, for the Realized One is only the one who shows the way, and the walking is each person’s own.

Does liberation have a curriculum?

Moggallāna’s opening is delightful and shrewd. In this very longhouse, he says, you can see “gradual progress down to the last step of the staircase”; brahmins progress by stages in recitation, archers in archery, and accountants — “we even make our apprentices count up to a hundred” — in mathematics. Everything skilful is mastered in order. Is it so in the Dhamma too? The Buddha’s “It is possible, brahmin” affirms something important: awakening is not a random gift or a sudden lightning-strike but the fruit of a trainable sequence. The path can be taught the way a craft is taught.

The horse-trainer and the bit

The image that opens the training sets its tone: a skilled trainer who gets a fine thoroughbred “first makes it get used to wearing the bit.” Before speed, before manoeuvres, comes the basic discipline that makes everything else possible. So the Realized One, taking a person “fit for training,” begins not with meditation or insight but with conduct.

The gradual training, rung by rung

The sequence each step makes possible the next:

StepWhat it is
EthicsRestraint in the monastic code, “seeing danger in the slightest fault.”
Sense-restraintGuarding the six sense-doors, not “caught up in the features and details,” so covetousness and displeasure don’t flood in.
Moderation in eatingEating “not for fun or adornment but only to sustain the body” and support practice.
WakefulnessMeditating through the watches of the night, sleeping mindfully in the lion’s posture.
Mindfulness & awarenessActing with clear comprehension in every movement — going, looking, eating, even resting.
Seclusion & the absorptionsResorting to a quiet place, abandoning the five hindrances, and entering the four absorptions in turn.

Each rung rests on the one below: sense-restraint is hard without ethics; mindfulness falters on a body dulled by overeating or starved of sleep; the absorptions cannot settle while the hindrances rage. The path is not a set of independent virtues but a single graded ascent — which is exactly why it can be taught like a craft.

For trainees, and for the perfected

The Buddha adds a careful distinction. This whole training, he says, is “how I instruct mendicants who are trainees” — those still aspiring to the goal. For the perfected, the arahants who have already “done what had to be done,” these same practices remain — but now “they lead to blissful meditation in this life, and to mindfulness and awareness.” The training does not fall away with awakening; it is simply no longer a means to an end. What was once the road becomes the home.

Why some arrive and some do not

Then Moggallāna asks the question that every teacher and student eventually faces: with the goal real, the path laid out, and the teacher present to encourage, why do some reach extinguishment and others fail? The Buddha turns it back with the simile that gives the discourse its force:

The road to Rājagaha. A man who knows the road perfectly gives flawless directions — this village in an hour, that town an hour on, then Rājagaha with its parks and ponds. One traveller, so instructed, still takes the wrong road and heads west; another, identically instructed, arrives safely. “What can I do? I am the one who shows the way.”

The point is liberating and sobering at once. The path’s reliability does not override the walker’s freedom. The Buddha is “the one who shows the way” (maggakkhāyī) — he can map every turn with perfect accuracy — but he cannot take the steps for anyone. No teacher can. Awakening is not dispensed by a guru’s grace; it is reached by one’s own walking on a road another has charted. This places responsibility squarely, and kindly, where it belongs.

The fragrance of the teaching

Moggallāna grasps it at once — “What can I do about that? I am the one who shows the way” is, after all, exactly his own situation as a teacher of accounts. He then draws his own distinction: the faithless who go forth merely “to earn a living,” devious and slack, the Buddha does not really live among; but the faithful who go forth from conviction, energetic and mindful, he does. And he ends with three fragrances — spikenard the best of roots, red sandalwood the best of heartwoods, jasmine the best of flowers — “in the same way, the worthy Gotama’s advice is the best of contemporary teachings,” and goes for refuge for life. The accountant who came asking for a curriculum leaves having found a path — and the honesty that it is his to walk.

Key terms

anupubbasikkhā — the “gradual training”: awakening reached not by a sudden gift but by a trainable sequence of steps, each building on the last — the path taught the way a craft is taught.
the horse-trainer’s bit — before anything else, the basic discipline. The Buddha takes one “fit for training” and begins not with meditation but with conduct.
the six steps — ethics, sense-restraint, moderation in eating, wakefulness, mindfulness and awareness, then seclusion and the four absorptions. Each rung rests on the one below.
sense-restraint — not getting “caught up in the features and details” of what is seen, heard, and so on, so that covetousness and displeasure do not flood the mind.
trainee & perfected — for the trainee these practices are the road to the goal; for the perfected arahant, the same practices remain as “blissful meditation in this life.” The road becomes the home.
the road to Rājagaha — why some arrive and some do not: the guide gives flawless directions, yet one traveller heads west and another arrives. The path’s reliability does not override the walker’s freedom.
maggakkhāyī — “the one who shows the way.” The Buddha can map every turn but cannot take the steps for anyone. Awakening is not dispensed by grace but reached by one’s own walking.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source spells out each training step (given fully in MN 27 and MN 39). The accountant’s question, the gradual training, the road-to-Rājagaha simile, and the closing praise are given closely. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Is there a gradual training?

§1–3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in the Eastern Monastery. The brahmin Moggallāna the Accountant came and said: “Worthy Gotama, in this longhouse we see gradual progress down to the last step of the stair; brahmins progress by stages in recitation, archers in archery, and we accountants in mathematics — we make our apprentices count, ‘one one, two twos…’ up to a hundred. Is there a comparable gradual training in your teaching?” “There is, brahmin. As a deft trainer first gets a fine thoroughbred used to the bit, so the Realized One first guides a person fit for training.”

The gradual training

§3–10“First: ‘Come, mendicant, be ethical, restrained in the code, seeing danger in the slightest fault.’ Then: ‘Guard your sense doors; when you see a sight, don’t get caught up in its features and details,’ and so for all six senses. Then: ‘Eat in moderation, not for fun or adornment but to sustain the body and support practice.’ Then: ‘Be devoted to wakefulness,’ meditating through the watches of the night. Then: ‘Have mindfulness and situational awareness’ in every act. Then: ‘Frequent a secluded lodging.’ There, abandoning the five hindrances — covetousness, ill will, dullness, restlessness, doubt — the mendicant enters the first, second, third, and fourth absorptions.”

For trainees and for the perfected

§11“That is how I instruct the mendicants who are trainees, aspiring to the supreme sanctuary. But for those who are perfected, with defilements ended and the journey completed, these same things lead to blissful meditation in this life, and to mindfulness and awareness.”

Why do some fail?

§12–14Moggallāna asked: “When your disciples are instructed like this, do all reach extinguishment, or do some fail?” “Some succeed, some fail.” “What is the cause, when extinguishment exists, the path exists, and you are there to encourage?” “I’ll ask you in return. Are you skilled in the road to Rājagaha?” “I am.” “Suppose you gave a traveller flawless directions, yet he took the wrong road and headed west, while another, identically instructed, arrived safely. What is the cause?” “What can I do, worthy Gotama? I am the one who shows the way.” “In the same way, though extinguishment exists and the path exists and I encourage them, some of my disciples arrive and some fail. What can I do, brahmin? The Realized One is the one who shows the way.”

The fragrance of the teaching

§15–17Moggallāna said: “The faithless who go forth merely to earn a living — devious, restless, slack — the worthy Gotama does not live with. But the faithful who go forth from conviction — energetic, mindful, devoted — with these he does. As spikenard is the best of fragrant roots, red sandalwood of heartwoods, and jasmine of flowers, so the worthy Gotama’s advice is the best of contemporary teachings. Excellent, worthy Gotama! I go for refuge to the worthy Gotama, to the teaching, and to the Saṅgha. From this day forth, may he remember me as a lay follower gone for refuge for life.”

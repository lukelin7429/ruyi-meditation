Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 113

A True Person

Sappurisasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, addressed to the mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha and the mendicants
Form
A graded contrast: every distinction the untrue exalt themselves by, and the true refuse to.
Length
~14 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A counterpart is preserved in the Chinese Madhyama Āgama (MĀ 85)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — how even spiritual attainment can feed conceit.

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-thirteenth

Pride is easy to spot when it leans on money or birth. But what about the subtler pride that leans on virtue, learning, austerity — even on the deep meditative attainments themselves? This discourse follows conceit all the way up the spiritual ladder, showing that an untrue person can turn anything, however holy, into a reason to exalt themselves and look down on others — while the true person, holding the very same attainment, refuses to be defined by it at all.

Where MN 110 contrasted the true and untrue person by their moral conduct, this companion piece (the “true person” discourse that gives the whole twelfth chapter much of its theme) sharpens the comparison to a single recurring move: “glorifying oneself and putting others down” (attukkaṁsana-paravambhana). The untrue person reaches for whatever distinguishes them — and uses it as a measuring-stick to rise above their companions. The true person, given the identical distinction, declines the comparison.

The list of distinctions is the heart of the discourse, and it climbs steadily: from the worldly (noble birth, fame, gifts) through the religious (great learning, mastery of the monastic law, being a teacher, the rigorous ascetic practices) to the rarest spiritual heights (the four absorptions, the four formless attainments). At every rung the untrue thinks “I have this, unlike these others,” while the true reflects: “it is not because of this that greed, hate, and delusion end” — and, of the deep attainments, that the Buddha taught “not being determined” even by them. The summit comes when the true person passes beyond all attainments into the cessation of perception and feeling, the defilements ending — and is, the Buddha says, “one who does not conceive” at all. The discourse traces conceit to the very top of the path, and shows the one place it finally has nothing left to stand on.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The untrue person turns every distinction — from noble birth to the deepest meditative attainment — into a reason to exalt themselves and disparage others; the true person knows that none of these ends greed, hate, or delusion, refuses to be defined by any of them, and at the summit, beyond all attainments, does not conceive at all.

The one diagnostic move

The whole discourse turns on a single behaviour, repeated at every level: the untrue person “glorifies themselves and puts others down” on account of some distinction they possess. This is the signature of conceit (māna) — not merely thinking well of oneself, but using oneself as a yardstick against others: “I have this, unlike these other mendicants.” The comparison is the poison. And the true person’s answer, repeated just as faithfully, is to decline the comparison — not by false modesty about their attainments, but by seeing clearly that the attainment is simply not what makes a person worthy.

The climbing ladder of distinctions

The genius of the discourse is its ascending list. Conceit is first shown leaning on the obvious worldly supports, then on increasingly refined religious ones:

LevelThe distinction leant on
Worldlygoing forth from an eminent or wealthy family; being famous; receiving ample requisites
Religious learningbeing very learned; an expert in the monastic law; a Dhamma teacher
Ascetic practicewilderness-dwelling, rag-robes, almsfood-only, tree-root dwelling, the charnel ground, never lying down, one meal a day
Meditative attainmentthe four absorptions; the four formless states up to neither-perception-nor-non-perception

The point of the ascent is sobering: there is no height so holy that conceit cannot perch on it. The wilderness ascetic can look down on the village monk; the deep meditator can look down on the beginner. The further up the path, the subtler and more “spiritual” the pride becomes — and the more it disguises itself as genuine worth. The discourse refuses to let any rung be exempt.

The true person’s reflection

Against each distinction, the true person reflects the same way. For the worldly and ascetic distinctions: “It is not because of one’s eminent family” — or fame, or learning, or eating one meal a day — “that thoughts of greed, hate, or delusion come to an end. Even someone without this distinction, if they practise in line with the teaching, is worthy of honour.” The test of worth is not the distinction but whether the defilements are being uprooted — and that test is open to anyone, with or without the advantage. So the true person “keeps the practice to themselves,” not advertising it as a badge of superiority.

The real measure. A thing is spiritually valuable only insofar as it ends greed, hate, and delusion. Birth, fame, learning, and austerity do not, by themselves, do this — so to rank oneself above others by them is to mistake the scaffolding for the building.

“Not being determined” by the attainments

When the ladder reaches the absorptions and formless states, the true person’s reflection deepens into something more subtle still. Here they recall that “the Buddha has spoken of not being determined even by the attainment of the first absorption” (and so up through every state): “for whatever they imagine it to be, it turns out to be something else.” This is the principle the tradition calls atammayatā — “not being made of that,” not identifying oneself with or through any state. Even a genuine, hard-won attainment must not become a thing one is, a position from which to look out at others. The moment a meditator thinks “I am one who has the fourth absorption,” they have turned a fleeting, conditioned state into a self — and missed its real lesson. The true person enjoys the attainment without being fashioned into someone by it.

The summit: not conceiving at all

The discourse ends not with a quality the untrue person also has-and-misuses, but with one only the true person reaches. Going beyond even neither-perception-nor-non-perception, the true person “enters the cessation of perception and feeling, and, having seen with wisdom, their defilements come to an end.” And of such a one the Buddha says the final word:

“This is a mendicant who does not conceive with anything, does not conceive regarding anything, does not conceive through anything.” All conceiving (maññanā) — the whole machinery of measuring “I am better, worse, equal” — has stopped. There is no longer a self being constructed out of any distinction, because there is no longer the activity of self-construction at all.

This is the perfect end of the path the discourse has been climbing. At every rung below, conceit could still find a foothold — even in the formless attainments. Only here, where the defilements themselves are ended, does it run out of ground entirely. The truly true person is not one who has the best attainment and stays humble about it, but one in whom the very impulse to rank, to measure, to be someone in comparison, has been uprooted at the source. The discourse’s long ascent ends exactly where conceit can no longer follow.

Key terms

sappurisa / asappurisa — the true (good, real) person and the untrue. Here distinguished not by morality (as in MN 110) but by the subtle pride that can attach even to genuine spiritual attainment.
attukkaṁsana-paravambhana — “glorifying oneself and putting others down.” The signature of the untrue person, repeated at every level: using a distinction as a yardstick to rise above one’s companions.
the climbing ladder — the distinctions conceit leans on, ascending from the worldly (birth, fame, gifts) through the religious (learning, asceticism) to the meditative (the absorptions and formless states). No height is exempt.
the real measure — a thing is spiritually valuable only insofar as it ends greed, hate, and delusion. Birth, fame, learning, and austerity do not by themselves do this — so to rank oneself by them is to mistake the scaffolding for the building.
atammayatā — “not being determined / not made of that”: not identifying oneself with or through any state. Even a genuine attainment must not become a thing one is, “for whatever they imagine it to be, it turns out otherwise.”
keeping the practice to themselves — the true person does not advertise their attainments as a badge of superiority; worth is measured by the uprooting of defilements, open to anyone with or without the distinction.
not conceiving (na maññati) — the summit: beyond all attainments, in the cessation of perception and feeling with defilements ended, the whole machinery of measuring “I am better, worse, equal” has stopped. Conceit runs out of ground.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats the contrast for each of the many distinctions. The diagnostic move, representative rungs of the ladder (birth, fame, asceticism), the “not being determined” reflection for the attainments, and the summit are given closely. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The two kinds of person

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, and addressed the mendicants. “Mendicants, I will teach you the qualities of a true person and the qualities of an untrue person. Listen well.”

Birth, family, fame, and gifts

§3–8“An untrue person who has gone forth from an eminent family reflects: ‘I am of eminent family, unlike these other mendicants’ — and glorifies themselves and puts others down. But a true person reflects: ‘It is not because of an eminent family that greed, hate, or delusion come to an end. Even one not of eminent family, if they practise in line with the teaching, is worthy of honour.’ And keeping the practice to themselves, they do not exalt themselves or disparage others. The same holds for one from a great or wealthy family, for one who is famous, and for one who receives ample robes, almsfood, lodgings, and medicines.”

Learning and ascetic practice

§9–20“So too with one who is very learned, an expert in the monastic law, or a Dhamma teacher; and with one who dwells in the wilderness, wears rag-robes, eats only almsfood, stays at the root of a tree or in a charnel ground or the open air, never lies down, sleeps wherever they can, or eats one meal a day. The untrue person reflects, ‘I do this, unlike these others,’ and exalts themselves. The true person reflects: ‘It is not because of this that greed, hate, or delusion come to an end. Even one who does not do this, if they practise in line with the teaching, is worthy of honour’ — and does not exalt themselves or disparage others.”

The absorptions and formless states

§21–28“An untrue person who attains the first absorption reflects: ‘I have attained it, unlike these others,’ and exalts themselves. But a true person reflects: ‘The Buddha has spoken of not being determined even by the attainment of the first absorption; for whatever they imagine it to be, it turns out to be something else.’ Keeping to themselves that they are not determined by it, they do not exalt themselves or disparage others. The same holds for the second, third, and fourth absorptions, and for the dimensions of infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness, and neither perception nor non-perception.”

The summit: not conceiving

§29“Furthermore, a true person, going beyond the dimension of neither perception nor non-perception, enters the cessation of perception and feeling. And, having seen with wisdom, their defilements come to an end. This is a mendicant who does not conceive with anything, does not conceive regarding anything, does not conceive through anything.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said.

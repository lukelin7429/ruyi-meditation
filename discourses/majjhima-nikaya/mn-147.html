Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 147

The Shorter Advice to Rāhula

Cūḷarāhulovādasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in the Dark Forest, where the Buddha leads his son Rāhula for a private teaching
Speakers
The Buddha and the mendicant Rāhula
Form
A compact catechism on the impermanence, suffering, and non-self nature of the six senses, their objects, and everything that arises from them
Length
~11 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No specific Āgama match is confidently identified in the available commentary; catechisms of this kind addressed to Rāhula recur across the Saṃyukta-āgama material, but no single confirmed parallel is noted here
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — short and formulaic, but its outcome is one of the highest stakes in the whole collection

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-forty-seventh

The Buddha notices, privately, that his own son is finally ready — and instead of announcing it, he simply takes Rāhula for a walk. What follows is a short, familiar-sounding catechism, the same three questions asked again and again about each sense and its object. But this time the questions land differently: by the end of it, Rāhula’s mind is “freed from defilements by not grasping,” full awakening arriving in real time, in the middle of the conversation, rather than as something reported afterward.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Every sense faculty, every sense object, every resulting consciousness, contact, and anything at all bound up with feeling, perception, choices, or consciousness that arises from that contact — each one, run through the same three-question test, turns out to be impermanent, therefore suffering, and therefore not a fit basis for “this is mine, I am this, this is my self”; seeing this thoroughly enough, in one sitting, is what finally brings Rāhula to full awakening.

A private recognition

The discourse opens inside the Buddha’s own mind, in retreat: “The qualities that ripen in freedom have ripened in Rāhula. Why don’t I lead him further to the ending of defilements?” Rāhula was ordained as a novice at the age of seven, not long after the Buddha’s awakening, making him possibly the youngest person ever to enter the monastic order under the Buddha. By the time of this sutta he has trained for years, including through the more foundational teaching recorded as the Longer Advice to Rāhula (MN 62) — on truthfulness, on meditating on the elements to loosen the sense of ownership over the body, on mindfulness of breathing. This discourse assumes all of that groundwork is already in place, and moves straight to the final push.

Into the forest, watched by thousands

After his usual alms round, the Buddha simply says, “Rāhula, get your sitting cloth. Let’s go to the Dark Forest for the day’s meditation.” No explanation is given to Rāhula himself. But the text tells us something Rāhula doesn’t yet know: many thousands of deities are following along, having sensed what is about to happen — “Today the Buddha will lead Rāhula further to the ending of defilements!” The scene is deliberately quiet and undramatic on the surface — a teacher and a student sitting at the root of a tree — while an enormous unseen audience gathers to witness it.

The same three questions, run through everything

The catechism itself will be familiar to anyone who has read the account of Nandaka teaching the nuns (MN 146): is it permanent or impermanent? if impermanent, is it suffering or happiness? if impermanent and suffering, is it fit to be regarded as “mine,” as “I,” as “my self”? What sets this version apart is its completeness. For the eye alone, the Buddha runs the question not just over the eye itself, but over sights, eye consciousness, eye contact, and then — in a single further step — “anything included in feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness that arises dependent on eye contact.” That last phrase quietly folds in all five aggregates: form (the eye and sights), consciousness (eye consciousness), and then feeling, perception, and choices arising from contact. The same five-part chain is then run, in brief, through the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind. Nothing in experience is left unexamined; every link in the chain from sense faculty to full-blown mental reaction receives the identical scrutiny.

Disillusionment, and its result

The Buddha closes with the standard formula for what this scrutiny is meant to produce: a learned noble disciple, seeing all of this clearly, “grows disillusioned” with each sense chain in turn. Disillusionment causes desire to fade; when desire fades, one is freed; and one knows one is freed, with the classic declaration of full awakening — “rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey has been completed, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this place.” Then comes the detail that makes this Rāhula’s own arrival, not just a repeated formula: “while this discourse was being spoken, Rāhula’s mind was freed from defilements by not grasping.” He does not need to go away and practice further. The freedom arrives inside the conversation itself. The thousands of deities listening in receive a related but distinct gift — not full liberation, but “the stainless, immaculate vision of the Dhamma,” the insight traditionally associated with stream-entry, summarized in a single line: “Everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease.”

Key terms

the Dark Forest — (Andhavana) a forest near Sāvatthī used repeatedly in the canon as a quiet setting for decisive private teachings.
the three questions — permanent or impermanent? suffering or happiness? fit to be regarded as self? — the same method used by Nandaka with the nuns in MN 146, here run through all six sense chains.
“anything included in feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness” — the phrase that folds the remaining three aggregates into the analysis of contact at each sense door.
“freed from defilements by not grasping” — the discourse’s description of Rāhula’s awakening, occurring in real time as the teaching is spoken rather than reported afterward.
the stainless vision of the Dhamma — the insight gained by the listening deities, traditionally the mark of stream-entry: “everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease.”

The text

The discourse in full: the Buddha’s private recognition, the walk to the Dark Forest, the complete catechism across all six senses, and Rāhula’s liberation. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A private recognition

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Then as he was in private retreat this thought came to his mind, “The qualities that ripen in freedom have ripened in Rāhula. Why don’t I lead him further to the ending of defilements?” Then the Buddha robed up in the morning and, taking his bowl and robe, entered Sāvatthī for alms. Then, after the meal, on his return from almsround, he addressed Venerable Rāhula, “Rāhula, get your sitting cloth. Let’s go to the Dark Forest for the day’s meditation.” “Yes, sir,” replied Rāhula. Taking his sitting cloth he followed behind the Buddha. Now at that time many thousands of deities followed the Buddha, thinking, “Today the Buddha will lead Rāhula further to the ending of defilements!” Then the Buddha plunged deep into the Dark Forest and sat at the root of a tree on the seat spread out. Rāhula bowed to the Buddha and sat down to one side. The Buddha said to him:

The eye, and everything that follows from it

§3“What do you think, Rāhula? Is the eye permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.” “What do you think, Rāhula? Are sights permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if they’re impermanent, are they suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if they’re impermanent, suffering, and perishable, are they fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.” “What do you think, Rāhula? Is eye consciousness permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.” “What do you think, Rāhula? Is eye contact permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.” “What do you think, Rāhula? Anything included in feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness that arises dependent on eye contact: is that permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.”

The remaining five senses

§4–8“What do you think, Rāhula? Is the ear … nose … tongue … body … mind permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.” “What do you think, Rāhula? Are ideas permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if they’re impermanent, are they suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if they’re impermanent, suffering, and perishable, are they fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.” “What do you think, Rāhula? Is mind consciousness permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.” “What do you think, Rāhula? Is mind contact permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.” “What do you think, Rāhula? Anything included in feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness that arises dependent on mind contact: is that permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.”

Disillusionment, and liberation

§9“Seeing this, a learned noble disciple grows disillusioned with the eye, sights, eye consciousness, and eye contact. And they grow disillusioned with anything included in feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness that arises dependent on eye contact. They grow disillusioned with the ear … nose … tongue … body … mind, ideas, mind consciousness, and mind contact. And they grow disillusioned with anything included in feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness that arises dependent on mind contact. Being disillusioned, desire fades away. When desire fades away they’re freed. When they’re freed, they know they’re freed. They understand: ‘Rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey has been completed, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this place.’” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Rāhula approved what the Buddha said. And while this discourse was being spoken, Rāhula’s mind was freed from defilements by not grasping. And the stainless, immaculate vision of the Dhamma arose in those thousands of deities: “Everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease.”

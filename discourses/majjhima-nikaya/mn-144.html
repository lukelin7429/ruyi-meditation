Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 144

Advice to Channa

Channovādasutta

Setting
Vulture’s Peak Mountain, near Rājagaha
Speakers
Venerable Sāriputta, Venerable Mahācunda, Venerable Channa, and finally the Buddha
Form
A bedside visit to a gravely ill monk, a searching examination of his understanding, a narrative outcome, and the Buddha’s verdict
Length
~11 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A close Chinese parallel survives in the Saṃyukta-āgama (SĀ 1266); the same episode also appears in Pali at SN 35.87
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — doctrinally compact, but the subject matter is grave and has been debated by commentators for centuries; read the reading guide’s framing before the text itself

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-forty-fourth

A note before beginning: this discourse concerns a terminally ill monk who ends his own life, and the Buddha’s subsequent verdict on that act. It is one of the most carefully studied and debated passages in the Pali canon, precisely because it resists a simple reading in either direction — it is not a general endorsement of suicide, and it is not a straightforward condemnation of Channa either. If this subject is difficult for you right now, it is entirely fine to set this discourse aside. For those who continue: the text repays a slow, careful reading, and the reading guide below tries to set out the traditional interpretive nuance before you reach the narrative itself.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Venerable Channa, in unbearable and incurable pain, tells Sāriputta he intends to end his life; rather than simply forbidding it, Sāriputta carefully examines whether Channa still clings to any sense of self in his experience, and only after Channa demonstrates clear, blameless understanding does the Buddha later declare that his death, when it comes, was without fault — a verdict the Buddha ties explicitly to the absence of clinging in Channa’s mind, not to the act of taking his own life as such.

Reading this discourse responsibly

This is one of a small handful of canonical episodes — alongside the cases of the monk Vakkali and, elsewhere, Godhika — in which a disciple ends his own life while gravely and irreversibly ill, and the Buddha is later reported to comment on the outcome without condemning the disciple. Scholars and teachers across the tradition have long cautioned against two opposite misreadings. The first misreading takes the Buddha’s verdict as blanket permission: it is not. The Buddha does not say that ending one’s life is acceptable whenever suffering is severe; the discourse does not function as a general teaching on when suicide is permitted, and elsewhere the canon treats the taking of life, including one’s own, as a grave matter with serious ethical weight. The second misreading treats Channa’s case as straightforwardly wrong and glosses over what the text actually says: the Buddha’s judgment is not that the act was fine because Channa was in pain, but that Channa, at the specific moment of his death, held no grasping at a self to be reborn, and it is this fact — verified beforehand by Sāriputta’s careful questioning — that the Buddha treats as decisive. The discourse is a case study in a very particular condition, not a rule of general application.

Sāriputta and Cunda visit

Sāriputta, Mahācunda, and Channa are all staying on Vulture’s Peak near Rājagaha. Learning that Channa is gravely ill, Sāriputta invites Mahācunda to visit him with him. Channa’s account of his pain matches Anāthapiṇḍika’s almost word for word — the same similes of a sharp point drilling the skull, a tightening leather strap, a butcher’s blade, a body held over coals — but where Anāthapiṇḍika only endures it, Channa states his intention plainly: “Reverend Sāriputta, I shall commit suicide. I don’t wish to live.”

An appeal, and Channa's answer

Sāriputta’s first response is not doctrinal at all but practical and pleading: “Please don’t commit suicide! Venerable Channa, keep going! We want you to keep going.” He offers concrete help — food, medicine, a capable carer — if any of these are lacking. Channa answers that none are lacking. His stated reason is striking and specific: not that his pain has become philosophically meaningless, but that he has served the Buddha with genuine devotion for a long time, and it is proper for a disciple to serve the Teacher out of love. He then states, calmly, what he wants Sāriputta to remember of him: “The mendicant Channa will commit suicide blamelessly.”

Sāriputta's examination

Rather than accept or dispute that claim, Sāriputta tests it. He asks, methodically, across each of the six sense fields — eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind, together with their respective consciousness and the things knowable through them — whether Channa regards any of it as “mine,” as “I am this,” or as “my self.” Channa answers, for each in turn, that he does not. Pressed further — what has he seen, what has he directly known, that grounds this? — Channa answers: “after seeing cessation, after directly knowing cessation in these things I regard them in this way.” This is not a man in denial or in crisis losing his grip on the teaching; it is, by the internal logic of the exchange, a demonstration of settled insight into not-self, offered under the most testing possible circumstance.

Mahācunda's addition. Venerable Mahācunda then gives Channa a chain to hold in mind: for the dependent there is agitation; for the independent, none; from that comes tranquility; from tranquility, no inclination; from no inclination, no coming and going; from no coming and going, no passing away and reappearing; and where there is no passing away and reappearing, there is no this life, no next life, no in-between — “just this is the end of suffering.”

What happens, and the Buddha's verdict

Sāriputta and Mahācunda leave; not long after, Channa ends his life. Sāriputta reports this to the Buddha and asks where Channa has been reborn. The Buddha’s answer redirects the question entirely: “didn’t the mendicant Channa declare his blamelessness to you personally?” Sāriputta mentions, almost as a stray fact, that Channa had friendly, hospitable families in a certain village — perhaps wondering whether some attachment lingered there. The Buddha is explicit about what blame would actually require: “When someone lays down this body and takes up another body, I call them ‘blameworthy.’ But the mendicant Channa did no such thing.” Because there was no clinging left to fuel a further rebirth, there was, in the Buddha’s terms, no one left to be reborn and blamed. “The mendicant Channa committed suicide blamelessly.”

The interpretive nuance, restated

What makes this discourse difficult — and why it has been debated for centuries — is exactly this: the verdict of blamelessness is tied to a specific, verified condition (the complete absence of clinging at the moment of death), not to the general circumstance of unbearable terminal pain, and not to the act of ending one’s life considered on its own. The text gives no reason to think the Buddha would have called any severely ill person's suicide blameless by default; the entire weight of the discourse rests on Sāriputta's careful, prior verification of Channa's state of mind, which is precisely why that examination occupies the center of the sutta rather than being a formality on the way to Channa's death.

Key terms

sattham āharissāmi — “I shall use the knife,” rendered here as “I shall commit suicide”: Channa’s stated intention, made plainly and without euphemism.
anupavajja — “blameless”: the term Channa applies to himself in advance, and which the Buddha later confirms — but only after explaining precisely what would have made him blameworthy instead.
“this is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self” — the formula of non-identification Sāriputta tests Channa on across all six sense fields, the standard diagnostic for insight into not-self used throughout the canon.
nissita & anissita — “dependent” and “independent”: the opening terms of Mahācunda’s chain, describing whether the mind still leans on something to be agitated about.
“lays down this body and takes up another” — the Buddha’s own definition of what blameworthiness would require: continued rebirth driven by clinging. Its absence, not the manner of death, is what the verdict turns on.

The text

The discourse in full: Sāriputta and Mahācunda’s visit to the gravely ill Channa, his stated intention, Sāriputta’s examination of his understanding, Mahācunda’s teaching, and the Buddha’s verdict afterward. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral). Readers who would prefer not to encounter a first-person description of physical pain or a description of suicide may wish to skip to the reading guide’s summary above instead.

Sāriputta and Cunda visit

§1–4So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground.

Now at that time the venerables Sāriputta, Mahācunda, and Channa were staying on the Vulture’s Peak Mountain.

Now at that time Venerable Channa was sick, suffering, gravely ill. Then in the late afternoon, Venerable Sāriputta came out of retreat, went to Venerable Mahācunda and said to him, “Come, Reverend Cunda, let’s go to see Venerable Channa and ask about his illness.” “Yes, reverend,” replied Mahācunda.

And then Sāriputta and Mahācunda went to see Channa and exchanged greetings with him. When the greetings and polite conversation were over, they sat down to one side. Then Sāriputta said to Channa, “I hope you’re keeping well, Reverend Channa; I hope you’re all right. I hope that your pain is fading, not growing, that its fading is evident, not its growing.”

An appeal, and Channa's answer

§5–7“Reverend Sāriputta, I’m not keeping well, I’m not getting by. The pain is terrible and growing, not fading; its growing is evident, not its fading. The winds piercing my head are so severe, it feels like a strong man drilling into my head with a sharp point. The pain in my head is so severe, it feels like a strong man tightening a strong leather strap around my head. The winds slicing my belly are so severe, like a deft butcher or their apprentice were slicing open a cow’s belly with a sharp meat cleaver. The burning in my body is so severe, it feels like two strong men grabbing a weaker man by the arms to burn and scorch him on a pit of glowing coals. I’m not keeping well, I’m not getting by. The pain is terrible and growing, not fading; its growing is evident, not its fading. Reverend Sāriputta, I shall commit suicide. I don’t wish to live.”

“Please don’t commit suicide! Venerable Channa, keep going! We want you to keep going. If you don’t have any suitable food, we’ll find it for you. If you don’t have suitable medicine, we’ll find it for you. If you don’t have a capable carer, we’ll find one for you. Please don’t commit suicide! Venerable Channa, keep going! We want you to keep going.”

“Reverend Sāriputta, it’s not that I don’t have suitable food, or suitable medicine, or a capable carer. Moreover, for a long time now I have served the Teacher with love, not without love. For it is proper for a disciple to serve the Teacher with love, not without love. You should remember this: ‘The mendicant Channa will commit suicide blamelessly.’”

Sāriputta's examination

§8–10“I’d like to ask you about a certain point, if you’d take the time to answer.” “Ask, Reverend Sāriputta. When I’ve heard it I’ll know.”

“Reverend Channa, do you regard the eye, eye consciousness, and things knowable by eye consciousness in this way: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’? Do you regard the ear… nose… tongue… body… mind, mind consciousness, and things knowable by mind consciousness in this way: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “Reverend Sāriputta, I regard the eye, eye consciousness, and things knowable by eye consciousness in this way: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’ I regard the ear… nose… tongue… body… mind, mind consciousness, and things knowable by mind consciousness in this way: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self’.”

“Reverend Channa, what have you seen, what have you directly known in these things that you regard them in this way: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self’?” “Reverend Sāriputta, after seeing cessation, after directly knowing cessation in these things I regard them in this way: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self’.”

When he said this, Venerable Mahācunda said to Venerable Channa: “So, Reverend Channa, you should regularly apply your mind to this instruction of the Buddha: ‘For the dependent there is agitation. For the independent there’s no agitation. When there’s no agitation there is tranquility. When there is tranquility there’s no inclination. When there’s no inclination there’s no coming and going. When there’s no coming and going there’s no passing away and reappearing. When there’s no passing away and reappearing there’s no this life or the next or between the two. Just this is the end of suffering.’” And when the venerables Sāriputta and Mahācunda had given Venerable Channa this advice they rose from their seat and left.

What happens, and the Buddha's verdict

§11–13Not long after those venerables had left, Venerable Channa committed suicide.

Then Sāriputta went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and said to him, “Sir, Venerable Channa has committed suicide. Where has he been reborn in his next life?” “Sāriputta, didn’t the mendicant Channa declare his blamelessness to you personally?” “Sir, there is a Vajjian village named Pubbajira. There Channa had families who were friendly, intimate, and hospitable.” “The mendicant Channa did indeed have such families. But this is not enough for me to call someone ‘blameworthy’. When someone lays down this body and takes up another body, I call them ‘blameworthy’. But the mendicant Channa did no such thing. You should remember this: ‘The mendicant Channa committed suicide blamelessly.’” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Sāriputta approved what the Buddha said.

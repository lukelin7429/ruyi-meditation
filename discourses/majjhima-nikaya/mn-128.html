Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 128

Corruptions

Upakkilesasutta

Setting
Kosambī, then the road to Bhagu’s hut, then the Eastern Bamboo Park where Anuruddha, Nandiya, and Kimbila are staying
Speakers
The Buddha, an unnamed defiant mendicant, Bhagu, a park keeper, and Anuruddha
Form
A quarrel the Buddha cannot resolve, a visit to harmonious friends, and a unique first-person account of the Buddha’s own struggle with eleven subtle corruptions of concentration
Length
~18 minutes to read
Northern parallel
The opening shares content with MN 31 and the Vinaya account of the Kosambī schism; no single Āgama match for the full discourse is noted in the available commentary
Difficulty
★★★★★ — the most detailed first-person map of meditative obstacles anywhere in the Majjhima

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-twenty-eighth

Most suttas show the Buddha teaching from a position of settled attainment. This one is different: pressed by three friends who admit their meditation keeps mysteriously stalling, the Buddha responds by opening up his own pre-awakening struggle in unusual detail — eleven distinct ways his concentration used to collapse, discovered and overcome one at a time, each with its own diagnosis and its own vivid image. It is one of the only places in the canon where the path to awakening is described not as a triumphant ascent but as a long process of trial, error, and precise self-correction.

It begins, fittingly, with a failure — a bitter monastic quarrel the Buddha himself cannot resolve, no matter how many times he intervenes.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Asked why their meditation keeps producing light and visionary experience that then mysteriously vanishes, the Buddha answers by tracing his own pre-awakening struggle against eleven distinct “corruptions of the mind” — doubt, distraction, drowsiness, terror, excitement, discomfort, over- and under-exertion, longing, distraction by variety, and over-fixation on the visions themselves — each identified, understood, and deliberately released before deep, stable immersion became possible.

A quarrel the Buddha cannot stop

The setting is the notorious schism at Kosambī, referenced elsewhere in the canon and recorded in detail in the Vinaya. The mendicants there are “fighting, quarreling, and disputing, continually wounding each other with barbed words.” The Buddha goes to them directly and says, simply, “Enough, mendicants! Stop fighting.” A mendicant refuses him — not once, but three times — with words that are almost breathtaking in their defiance: “Wait, sir! Let the Buddha, the Lord of the Dhamma, remain passive, dwelling in blissful meditation in this life. We will be known for this fighting.” Having tried three times and failed, the Buddha does something rare: he simply leaves, reciting verses on his way out that would become some of the most famous in the tradition, shared with the Dhammapada:

The verses at the door. “For never is hatred laid to rest by hate, it’s laid to rest by love: this is an ancient teaching… If you find no alert companion, no attentive friend to live happily together, then, like a king who flees his conquered realm, wander alone like a tusker in the wilds. It’s better to wander alone, there’s no fellowship with fools.”

It is a striking moment: even the Buddha, faced with a dispute he cannot mend, chooses withdrawal over forcing a resolution — and openly says so.

Finding harmonious company

The Buddha walks on alone, first briefly visiting a fellow Sakyan, Bhagu, then arriving at the Eastern Bamboo Park, where Anuruddha, Nandiya, and Kimbila are staying. A park keeper, not recognizing him, tries to turn him away: “Don’t come into this park, ascetic. There are three gentlemen staying here whose nature is to desire only the self. Don’t disturb them.” Anuruddha overhears and rushes to correct the mistake — “Don’t keep the Buddha out! Our Teacher, the Blessed One, has arrived!” — and the three friends come out together to receive him with full hospitality.

What harmony actually looks like

The Buddha asks whether they are living “in harmony, appreciating each other, without quarreling, blending like milk and water” — a pointed question, arriving as it does straight from a community that could not manage this at all. Anuruddha’s answer is concrete and practical: he reflects on his good fortune to have such companions, treats them with kindness in both public and private, and deliberately “sets aside his own ideas” to go along with theirs — “though we’re different in body, we’re one in mind.” Asked further whether they live diligently, they describe an orderly communal routine: whoever returns first from almsround prepares the seats and water; whoever returns last deals with any leftover food without waste; chores are shared, lifted together when one person can’t manage alone, and “we don’t break into speech for that reason” — and every five nights, they sit together until dawn discussing the teaching.

A reluctant admission

Pressed further — had they, living this well, achieved “any superhuman distinction in knowledge and vision worthy of the noble ones”? — the three friends answer with real honesty rather than false modesty or overclaiming: “While meditating diligent, keen, and resolute, we perceive both light and vision of forms. But before long the light and the vision of forms vanish. We haven’t worked out the basis of that.” This is early meditative experience — radiance and visionary imagery arising in deep concentration, a precursor to the clairvoyant power Anuruddha would later become famous for — but incomplete and, as yet, not understood.

The Buddha’s own confession

Rather than simply explaining the phenomenon in the abstract, the Buddha answers with something unusual: a first-person account of his own identical struggle, before his awakening. “I too perceived light and vision of forms. But before long my light and vision of forms vanished.” What follows is a patient, one-by-one diagnosis of eleven “corruptions of the mind” (upakkilesa) — each discovered only after the last was overcome, since a new obstacle would surface the moment the previous one was resolved:

CorruptionWhat it is / its simile
Doubtuncertainty disturbing an unconsolidated immersion
Loss of focusthe mind drifting, attending imprecisely
Dullness and drowsinessa natural lapse of energy over long practice
Terrorlike a traveler ambushed by killers springing out from both sides of the road
Elationlike suddenly finding five entrances to a treasure trove at once
Discomfortordinary bodily strain from sitting for long periods
Excessive energylike gripping a quail too tightly in the hand — it dies
Overly lax energylike gripping a quail too loosely — it flies away
Longingyearning for higher realization, wholesome in itself, yet still a disturbance here
Perception of diversitydistraction by the variety of sense-impressions or visionary forms
Excessive concentration on formsbecoming fascinated by the visions themselves, losing the thread of the meditation

Each is described with careful, almost clinical precision — the same three-part structure recurring throughout: recalling what arose (past tense), inferring the causal principle (“X arose, and because of that my immersion fell away; when immersion falls away, the light and vision vanish”), and resolving never to let it recur (future tense). Once all eleven were understood as corruptions and given up, the Buddha says simply, “I gave them up.”

Two puzzles beyond the eleven

Even after clearing all eleven corruptions, two subtler patterns remained. Sometimes he perceived light but no forms, or forms but no light, for a whole day and night at a stretch — until he realized this depended on which “basis” he was focusing on: attending to the basis of light produced light without forms, and vice versa. And sometimes his light and vision were limited, sometimes limitless — which he traced directly to the scope of his immersion itself: “when my immersion is limited, my vision is limited… when my immersion is limitless, my vision is limitless.” This closing insight connects directly back to MN 127’s teaching on the “expansive release of the heart” — the breadth of one’s cultivated attention determines the breadth of what one perceives.

Immersion in three ways, and the fruit

Having given up all the corruptions, the Buddha resolved to develop immersion three further ways — with the mind both placed and sustained on its object, with only sustaining and no placing, and with neither — each combined in turn with rapture, without rapture, with pleasure, and with equanimity: a systematic, exhaustive mapping of every combination available to a settled mind. Out of this thoroughness came the final knowledge: “My freedom is unshakable; this is my last rebirth; now there’ll be no more future lives.” The discourse that began with a quarrel no one could settle ends with the most settled mind imaginable.

Key terms

upakkilesa — “corruption”: a subtle mental flaw that undermines meditation on the very brink of absorption, distinct from the coarser five hindrances.
obhāsa, dassana rūpānaṁ — “light” and “vision of forms”: the radiance and visionary imagery that can arise in deep concentration, a precursor to the power of clairvoyance (dibbacakkhu) Anuruddha would later be declared foremost in.
nimitta (as “basis”) — in this early usage, not a visual “sign” but the causal ground being investigated: “we haven’t worked out the nimitta [basis, cause] of that.”
the quail similes — gripped too tight, it dies; held too loose, it flies away: a precise image for the balance of energy required in meditation, neither forcing nor slacking.
“different in body, one in mind” — Anuruddha’s description of communal harmony, achieved by deliberately setting aside one’s own preferences for a companion’s.
the Bṛhadāraṇyaka echo — the commentary notes this discourse’s structure of a reluctant sage pressed to reveal an inner “light” parallels a famous dialogue in the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad, where King Janaka presses the sage Yājñavalkya on the true light of a person.

The text

The discourse in full: the Kosambī quarrel and the Buddha’s verses, the visits to Bhagu and to Anuruddha’s community, the discussion of harmony and diligence, and the Buddha’s complete account of the eleven corruptions and their resolution. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A quarrel the Buddha cannot stop

§1–6So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Kosambī, in Ghosita’s Monastery. The mendicants there were fighting, quarreling, and disputing, continually wounding each other with barbed words. The Buddha went to them and said, “Enough, mendicants! Stop fighting, quarreling, and disputing.” A mendicant replied: “Wait, sir! Let the Buddha, the Lord of the Dhamma, remain passive, dwelling in blissful meditation in this life. We will be known for this fighting, quarreling, arguing, and disputing.” A second and a third time the Buddha said the same, and a third time that mendicant answered the same.

Then the Buddha went for alms, and on his return, having set his lodgings in order, recited these verses while standing: “Many voices shout at once, yet no-one thinks that they’re the fool… ‘They abused me, they hit me! They beat me, they robbed me!’ For those bearing such a grudge, hatred is never laid to rest… For never is hatred laid to rest by hate, it’s laid to rest by love: this is an ancient teaching. When others do not understand, let us, who do understand this, restrain ourselves in this regard; for that is how conflicts are laid to rest. Breakers of bones and takers of life, thieves of cattle, horses, wealth, those who plunder the nation: even they can come together, so why can’t you? If you find an alert companion, an attentive friend to live happily together, then, overcoming all adversities, wander with them, joyful and mindful. If you find no alert companion, no attentive friend to live happily together, then, like a king who flees his conquered realm, wander alone like a tusker in the wilds. It’s better to wander alone, there’s no fellowship with fools. Wander alone and do no wrong, at ease like a tusker in the wilds.”

Finding harmonious company

§7–10After speaking these verses, the Buddha went to the village of the child salt-miners, where Venerable Bhagu was staying, and gave him an encouraging Dhamma talk. He then set out for the Park of the East-Raftered Hall, where Anuruddha, Nandiya, and Kimbila were staying. The park keeper, seeing him approach, said: “Don’t come into this park, ascetic. There are three gentlemen staying here whose nature is to desire only the self. Don’t disturb them.” Anuruddha, overhearing, said: “Don’t keep the Buddha out, good park keeper! Our Teacher, the Blessed One, has arrived.” He then called Nandiya and Kimbila, and the three came out together, one receiving the Buddha’s bowl and robe, one spreading a seat, one setting out water for his feet.

What harmony actually looks like

§11–14“I hope you’re living in harmony, appreciating each other, without quarreling, blending like milk and water, and regarding each other with kindly eyes?” “Indeed, sir, we live in harmony.” “But how?” Anuruddha answered: “I think: ‘I’m fortunate, so very fortunate, to live together with spiritual companions such as these.’ I consistently treat these venerables with kindness by way of body, speech, and mind, both in public and in private. I think: ‘Why don’t I set aside my own ideas and just go along with these venerables’ ideas?’ And that’s what I do. Though we’re different in body, sir, we’re one in mind, it seems to me.” Nandiya and Kimbila spoke likewise.

“Good, good! But I hope you’re living diligently, keen, and resolute?” “Indeed, sir. Whoever returns first from almsround prepares the seats, and puts out the drinking water and the rubbish bin. If there’s remainder, whoever returns last eats it if they like, or disposes of it without harm to living creatures. Then they put everything away and sweep the refectory. If anyone sees a water-pot empty, they fill it, lending a hand to each other if needed — but we don’t break into speech for that reason. And every five days we sit together for the whole night and discuss the teachings. That’s how we live diligently.”

A reluctant admission

§15“Good, good! But as you live diligently like this, have you achieved any superhuman distinction in knowledge and vision worthy of the noble ones, a comfortable meditation?” “Well, sir, while meditating diligent, keen, and resolute, we perceive both light and vision of forms. But before long the light and the vision of forms vanish. We haven’t worked out the basis of that.”

The Buddha’s own confession: eleven corruptions

§16–26“Well, you should work out the basis of that. Before my awakening, I too perceived light and vision of forms, and before long they vanished. It occurred to me: ‘Doubt arose in me, and because of that my immersion fell away. When immersion falls away, the light and vision of forms vanish. I’ll make sure that doubt will not arise in me again.’

Meditating again, the light and vision vanished once more. It occurred to me: ‘Loss of focus arose in me… I’ll make sure neither doubt nor loss of focus will arise again.’ Then in turn: ‘Dullness and drowsiness arose…’ Then: ‘Terror arose in me — suppose a person was traveling along a road, and killers were to spring out at them from both sides; they’d feel terrified because of that. In the same way, terror arose in me.’ Then: ‘Elation arose — suppose a person was looking for the entrance to a treasure trove, and all at once came across five entrances; they’d feel excited.’ Then: ‘Discomfort arose.’ Then: ‘Excessive energy arose — suppose a person was to grip a quail too tightly in their hands; it would die right there.’ Then: ‘Overly lax energy arose — suppose a person was to grip a quail too loosely; it would fly out of their hands.’ Then: ‘Longing arose.’ Then: ‘Perceptions of diversity arose.’ And finally: ‘Excessive concentration on forms arose.’

When I understood that doubt, loss of focus, dullness and drowsiness, terror, elation, discomfort, excessive energy, overly lax energy, longing, perception of diversity, and excessive concentration on forms are corruptions of the mind, I gave them up.

Two puzzles beyond the eleven

§28–29Still, meditating diligently, I sometimes perceived light but did not see forms, or saw forms but did not perceive light — for a whole night, a whole day, or even both. I thought: ‘What is the reason for this?’ It occurred to me: ‘When I don’t focus on the basis of the forms, but focus on the basis of the light, I perceive light and do not see forms; and the reverse likewise.’

I also sometimes perceived limited light and saw limited forms, or perceived limitless light and saw limitless forms. I thought: ‘What is the reason for this?’ It occurred to me: ‘When my immersion is limited, my vision is limited; when my immersion is limitless, my vision is limitless.’

Immersion in three ways, and the fruit

§30–32After understanding and giving up all these corruptions of the mind, I thought: ‘Now let me develop immersion in three ways.’ I developed immersion while placing the mind and keeping it connected; without placing the mind, merely keeping it connected; without placing the mind or keeping it connected; with rapture; without rapture; with pleasure; with equanimity. When I had developed immersion in these ways, the knowledge and vision arose in me: ‘My freedom is unshakable; this is my last rebirth; now there’ll be no more future lives.’” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Anuruddha approved what the Buddha said.

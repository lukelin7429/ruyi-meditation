Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 64

The Longer Discourse with Māluṅkya

Mahāmāluṅkyasutta

Setting
Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speakers
The Buddha, with Māluṅkyaputta and Ānanda
Form
A correction opening into a path — the Buddha exposes a misunderstanding of the five lower fetters with the simile of an infant, then lays out the meditative practice that actually cuts them.
Length
~13 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 205 (Madhyama-āgama 205, “The Five Lower Fetters”), with a Sanskrit fragment
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — the distinction between an active fetter and its latent tendency, and the absorption-based path that cuts them, both reward careful study.

Why this discourse, sixty-fourth

The companion to MN 63. Where the shorter discourse set the great speculative questions aside, the longer one gets down to work: it names the five lower fetters that bind beings to the round of rebirth, corrects a subtle but common misunderstanding of what a fetter even is, and lays out the precise meditative path by which the fetters are cut.

The correction comes first, and it is sharp. When Māluṅkyaputta dutifully lists the five fetters, the Buddha asks who taught him to remember them that way — and warns that outsiders could ridicule the answer “using the simile of the infant.” A tiny baby has no concept of ‘self’ or ‘teachings’ or ‘sensual pleasure,’ so the active fetters cannot even arise in it — “yet the underlying tendency still lies within.” A fetter is not merely the manifest state that flares up in the moment; it is the latent disposition beneath. To be free of a fetter is not to have it quiet for now, as the baby does, but to have uprooted the tendency itself.

The rest of the discourse answers the obvious question: how? The Buddha makes clear that the fetters cannot be cut by wishing — “it is not possible to know or see or give up the five lower fetters without relying on a path and a practice,” just as one cannot reach a tree’s heartwood without first cutting through the bark. Then he gives the path: enter an absorption; turn insight upon everything in it, seeing it as impermanent, suffering, and not-self; and incline the mind toward the deathless. Done this way, the practice yields either full awakening or, falling just short, non-return. MN 64 is one of the canon’s most complete maps of how serenity and insight together dismantle the bonds of the heart.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The five lower fetters are not abandoned merely by their momentary absence — even a baby, who cannot form them, still carries their latent tendency — but only by the path of insight grounded in serenity: entering each absorption, seeing all its phenomena as impermanent and not-self, and turning the mind to the deathless, which issues in full awakening or, failing that, non-return.

The five lower fetters

The discourse turns on the pañca orambhāgiya saṃyojana — the five “lower fetters” that bind beings to the lower, sensual realm of existence:

FetterWhat it binds
Substantialist view (sakkāya-diṭṭhi)the deep assumption of a real, solid self among the aggregates
Doubt (vicikicchā)unsettled uncertainty about the path and its truth
Misapprehension of precepts & observances (sīlabbata-parāmāsa)the belief that rules and rituals, by themselves, purify
Sensual desire (kāmacchanda)craving for the pleasures of the senses
Ill will (byāpāda)aversion and hostility toward beings

The first three are cut at stream-entry; the last two, the coarse pulls of desire and aversion, are fully cut only at non-return. Together, when all five are gone, a person can never again be reborn in the sensual world — hence “lower” fetters.

The infant: fetter versus underlying tendency

Māluṅkyaputta lists the five correctly — and is nonetheless corrected, because his way of remembering them misses something. The Buddha invokes “the simile of the infant.” A little baby lying on its back has no concept of ‘substantial reality,’ so the view “I am a self” cannot arise in it; it has no concept of ‘teachings,’ so doubt cannot arise; no concept of ‘sensual pleasure,’ so sensual desire cannot arise. By a naive definition, then, the baby is free of the fetters. But — and this is the whole point — “the underlying tendency still lies within them.” The capacity, the latent disposition, is fully present, waiting only for the concepts to develop before it flowers into the active fetter. A fetter therefore has two layers: the manifest defilement that arises in a given moment, and the underlying tendency (anusaya) that is its dormant root. Liberation is not the temporary quiet of the surface but the uprooting of the root.

Mired versus free

The Buddha then contrasts two people. The untrained ordinary person, who has not learned from the noble ones, has a heart “overcome and mired” in each fetter and “does not truly understand the escape” from it; in them the fetter is “entrenched, not eliminated.” The trained noble disciple has a heart not overcome, “truly understands the escape,” and in them the fetter “along with any underlying tendency to it, is given up.” The difference is not that the noble disciple never feels a flicker of desire or doubt, but that they know the way out and have dug up the root, so the defilement no longer has a permanent home. Knowing the escape is itself part of the cure.

The path is necessary

Two similes drive home that the fetters cannot be wished away. First, the heartwood: just as “it’s not possible to cut out the heartwood of a great tree without first cutting through the bark and the softwood,” so it is not possible to give up the fetters without relying on the path and practice — but with that path, it is possible. Second, the Ganges in flood: a feeble person who tries to swim the brimming river by arm-power alone cannot reach the far shore, while a strong person can. So when the Dhamma is taught for the ending of self-view, the practitioner whose mind “leaps forth, gains confidence, settles down, and becomes decided” is the strong swimmer who can cross; the one whose mind hangs back is the feeble one who cannot. The path works — but it asks for a mind that commits to it.

The path itself: insight grounded in serenity

Now the method, and it is precise. Taking each meditative attainment in turn — the first absorption, then the second, third, and fourth, then the formless dimensions of infinite space, infinite consciousness, and nothingness — the practitioner does two things. First, within that state, they turn insight on everything present: “they contemplate the phenomena there — the form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness — as impermanent, as suffering, as diseased, as a boil, as a dart, as gloom, as an affliction, as alien, as breaking apart, as empty, as not-self.” Eleven angles of vision, all dismantling the sense that there is anything here worth clinging to as a self. Second, having loosened the grip, they “turn the mind away from those things and apply it to the deathless element.” The sublime calm of absorption is not the destination; it is the platform from which one sees through even the calm itself, and then leans toward what lies beyond all of it.

The deathless element

What the mind turns toward is named in one of the canon’s most beautiful formulas: “This is peaceful; this is sublime — the stilling of all activities, the letting go of all attachments, the ending of craving, fading away, cessation, extinguishment.” This is nibbāna, the “deathless element” (amata-dhātu) — not another state to be produced and clung to, but the peace that appears when producing and clinging stop. Abiding in this contemplation, the practitioner either “attains the ending of defilements” here and now — full awakening — or, if a subtle attachment to the meditation itself remains, “with the ending of the five lower fetters they are reborn spontaneously” as a non-returner, “extinguished there, not liable to return.” Either way the lower fetters are gone for good.

Two liberations, one path

Finally Ānanda asks a precise question: if this is the single path, why are some practitioners “released in heart” (ceto-vimutti) and others “released by wisdom” (paññā-vimutti)? The Buddha’s answer is brief and deep: “It is the disparity of their faculties.” The same path produces somewhat different fruits in different people because their spiritual faculties — faith, energy, mindfulness, concentration, wisdom — are differently balanced and developed. One practice, walked by many temperaments, yields liberations of slightly different flavor. The path is one; the people who walk it are not identical, and the teaching makes room for that.

Key terms

pañca orambhāgiya saṃyojana — the five “lower fetters”: substantialist view, doubt, misapprehension of precepts and observances, sensual desire, and ill will. When all are gone, one is a non-returner.
anusaya — underlying tendency; the latent, dormant root of a defilement. The infant has no active fetters but the full anusaya. Liberation uproots the tendency, not merely the surface flare-up.
sakkāya-diṭṭhi — substantialist or identity view: the assumption of a solid, real self among the five aggregates. The first fetter, cut at stream-entry.
anāgāmī — the non-returner; one in whom all five lower fetters are gone. Reborn spontaneously in a refined realm, they attain nibbāna there and never return to the sensual world.
amata-dhātu — the “deathless element”: nibbāna, toward which the mind is turned after insight — “the stilling of all activities… fading away, cessation, extinguishment.”
ceto-vimutti / paññā-vimutti — release of heart / release by wisdom. Two forms of the one liberation, differing “due to the disparity of faculties” among practitioners.

The text

The full discourse. The infant simile and the path with the first absorption are given in full; the “mired / free” passage and the higher attainments are condensed, as in the source. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The fetters — and a correction

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove. There the Buddha said to the mendicants, “Mendicants, do you remember the five lower fetters taught by me?” Venerable Māluṅkyaputta replied, “Sir, I remember them: substantialist view, doubt, misapprehension of precepts and observances, sensual desire, and ill will.”

§3“Who on earth do you remember being taught the five lower fetters in that way? Wouldn’t the wanderers of other religions fault you using the simile of the infant? For a little baby doesn’t even have a concept of ‘substantial reality’, so how could substantialist view possibly arise in them? Yet the underlying tendency to substantialist view still lies within them. A little baby doesn’t even have a concept of ‘teachings’… of ‘precepts’… of ‘sensual pleasures’… of ‘sentient beings’, so how could doubt, misapprehension, sensual desire, or ill will arise in them? Yet the underlying tendency to each still lies within them. Wouldn’t the wanderers of other religions fault you using the simile of the infant?”

§4Then Venerable Ānanda said, “Now is the time, Blessed One! May the Buddha teach the five lower fetters. The mendicants will listen and remember it.” “Well then, Ānanda, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.”

Mired, and free

§5–6“Ānanda, take an unlearned ordinary person who has not seen the noble ones. Their heart is overcome and mired in substantialist view, and they don’t truly understand the escape from it. That substantialist view is entrenched in them, not eliminated: it is a lower fetter. (The same is said of doubt, misapprehension of precepts and observances, sensual desire, and ill will.) But a learned noble disciple, who has seen the noble ones, has a heart not overcome and mired in substantialist view, and truly understands the escape from it. That substantialist view, along with any underlying tendency to it, is given up in them. (The same is said of the other four.)”

The path is necessary

§6–8“There is a path and a practice for giving up the five lower fetters. It’s not possible to know or see or give up the five lower fetters without relying on that path and that practice. Suppose there was a large tree standing with heartwood. It’s not possible to cut out the heartwood without having cut through the bark and the softwood. In the same way… But with that path, it is possible — just as one can cut out the heartwood after cutting through the bark and softwood.

Suppose the river Ganges was full to the brim. A feeble person thinks, ‘By swimming with my arms I’ll cross to the far shore’ — but they can’t. So it is when the Dhamma is taught for the cessation of substantial reality and someone’s mind does not leap forth, gain confidence, settle down, and become decided: they are like that feeble person. But a strong person thinks the same and is able to cross. So it is when someone’s mind, hearing the Dhamma, does leap forth, gain confidence, settle down, and become decided: they are like that strong person.”

The path itself

§9“And what is the path and the practice for giving up the five lower fetters? It’s when a mendicant — due to the seclusion from attachments, the giving up of unskillful qualities, and the complete settling of physical discomforts — quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unskillful qualities, enters and remains in the first absorption. They contemplate the phenomena there — included in form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness — as impermanent, as suffering, as diseased, as a boil, as a dart, as gloom, as an affliction, as alien, as breaking apart, as empty, as not-self. They turn their mind away from those things and apply it to the element free of death: ‘This is peaceful; this is sublime — that is, the stilling of all activities, the letting go of all attachments, the ending of craving, fading away, cessation, extinguishment.’ Abiding in that they attain the ending of defilements. If they don’t attain the ending of defilements, with the ending of the five lower fetters they’re reborn spontaneously, because of their passion and love for that meditation. They are extinguished there, and are not liable to return from that world. This is the path and the practice for giving up the five lower fetters.”

The same is then said of the second, third, and fourth absorptions, and of the dimensions of infinite space, infinite consciousness, and nothingness. In each, the practitioner contemplates whatever phenomena are present — the aggregates included there — as impermanent, suffering, and not-self (the eleven contemplations), then turns the mind to the deathless element. Each is “the path and the practice for giving up the five lower fetters,” yielding the ending of defilements, or non-return.

Two liberations

§16“Sir, if this is the path and the practice for giving up the five lower fetters, how then are some mendicants here released in heart while others are released by wisdom?” “In that case, Ānanda, I say it is the disparity of their faculties.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Ānanda approved what the Buddha said.

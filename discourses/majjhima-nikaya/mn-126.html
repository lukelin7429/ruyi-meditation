Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 126

With Bhūmija

Bhūmijasutta

A four-cornered puzzle about wishing, a careful guess confirmed in full, and four similes on method versus intention
Length
~10 minutes to read

Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — a clean logical argument, repeated four times with escalating vividness

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-twenty-sixth

This is the second of two discourses featuring Prince Jayasena, and it reads almost as a matched pair with MN 125: the same prince, the same nervous mendicant offering a guess about what the Buddha would say, even the identical closing exchange about similes that arrive unbidden. But where MN 125 built toward warm encouragement for a discouraged young novice, this discourse ends on a quieter, more ambiguous note — and its content is a genuinely useful piece of reasoning about a confusion that still shows up in how people think about practice today: does wanting a result, or not wanting it, or something in between, actually change whether you get it?

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Whether the spiritual life bears fruit has nothing to do with whether the practitioner wishes for a result, doesn’t wish, both, or neither — it depends entirely on whether the method itself, the eightfold path, is rightly or wrongly practiced, exactly as pressing sand will never yield oil no matter how badly one wants oil, while pressing sesame will yield it whether one wants it or not.

A puzzle at the prince’s table

Venerable Bhūmija is on almsround at the home of Prince Jayasena — the same prince who, in the previous discourse, flatly dismissed a novice’s teaching. Jayasena poses a puzzle circulating among “some ascetics and brahmins”: a fourfold claim that leading the spiritual life cannot win its fruit whether you make a wish for it, don’t make a wish, both make and don’t make a wish, or neither make nor don’t make a wish — a tetralemma designed to make the whole project of spiritual striving look futile no matter how you approach it. “What does the worthy Bhūmija’s Teacher say about this?”

A careful guess

Like Aciravata before him, Bhūmija is honest about the limits of his knowledge: “I haven’t heard and learned this in the presence of the Buddha. But it’s possible that he might explain it like this” — and then offers a genuinely sharp reframing. The fourfold wish-structure is a red herring, he suggests: what actually matters is whether the spiritual life is led rationally (yoniso) or irrationally (ayoniso). Led irrationally, it wins no fruit regardless of wishing, in any of the four permutations. Led rationally, it wins the fruit regardless of wishing, in any of the four permutations. Jayasena is impressed — “he clearly stands head and shoulders above all the various other ascetics and brahmins” — and serves Bhūmija food from his own dish. Yet the commentary notes something the text leaves quietly unstated: Jayasena’s praise lands on the Teacher, not on Bhūmija himself, and despite his evident interest, no conversion follows. He remains, as he was, a curious and generous prince — nothing more.

Confirmed in full

After the meal, Bhūmija goes straight to the Buddha, reports the whole conversation, and asks anxiously whether he “repeated what the Buddha has said” without misrepresenting him. The Buddha’s answer is unreserved: “Indeed, Bhūmija… your explanation was in line with the teaching, and there are no legitimate grounds for rebuttal or criticism.” He then spells out exactly what “irrational” and “rational” mean in this context: ascetics and brahmins with wrong view, purpose, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, and immersion cannot win the fruit no matter how they wish; those with right view through right immersion — the noble eightfold path — will win it no matter how they wish, or don’t.

Four similes: sand, horn, water, green wood

The Buddha makes the logic unmistakable with four paired images, each showing a hopeless method and its working counterpart — and in every case, wishing changes nothing:

Seeking…Hopeless method (irrational)Working method (rational)
Oilheaping sand in a bucket, sprinkling with water, pressing itheaping sesame flour in a bucket, sprinkling with water, pressing it
Milkpulling the horn of a newly-calved cowpulling the udder of a newly-calved cow
Butterpouring water into a pot and churning itpouring curds into a pot and churning them
Firedrilling a green, sappy log with a drill-stickdrilling a dried-up, withered log with a drill-stick

No amount of wishing extracts oil from sand, and no lack of wishing prevents oil from sesame. The eightfold path works the same way: it is a method, causally connected to its result the way sesame is connected to oil, not a wish-granting exercise whose success depends on the practitioner’s inner stance toward wanting or not wanting the outcome.

An echo, and a quieter ending

The discourse closes exactly as MN 125 did: the Buddha remarks that these similes, had Bhūmija known them, would likely have won Jayasena’s confidence — and Bhūmija, in nearly identical words to Aciravata, wonders how such similes could occur to him, “since they were neither supernaturally inspired, nor learned before in the past.” The repetition invites a direct comparison between the two discourses: both give a nervous junior mendicant a spontaneous, tailor-made teaching tool from the Buddha. But MN 125 ended with explicit reassurance that reached the discouraged novice directly. Here, the loose thread is Jayasena himself — impressed, generous, evidently interested, and yet, as far as this discourse tells us, never actually moved to walk the path the similes so clearly illuminate. Understanding the method perfectly is not, itself, the same as choosing to use it.

Key terms

āsa — “wish” or “hope”: the variable Jayasena’s puzzle treats as decisive, and which the Buddha shows is actually irrelevant to whether the spiritual life bears fruit.
yoniso / ayoniso — “rational” and “irrational,” literally “by way of the womb” or origin: whether a practice is causally, appropriately connected to the result it seeks — the real variable that determines success.
the eightfold path, wrong and right — the concrete content of “irrational” and “rational” practice: wrong view through wrong immersion versus right view through right immersion.
the four similes — oil, milk, butter, and fire, each demonstrating that method determines outcome regardless of the practitioner’s wish for that outcome.
“no legitimate grounds for rebuttal” — the Buddha’s explicit confirmation that Bhūmija’s spontaneous guess, though never directly taught to him, matched the teaching exactly.

The text

The discourse in full: the puzzle at Jayasena’s home, Bhūmija’s careful answer, his report to the Buddha, and the Buddha’s confirmation and four similes, given complete for oil and abridged (as the source text itself abbreviates) for milk, butter, and fire, which repeat the identical structure. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A puzzle at the prince’s table

§1–6So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove. Venerable Bhūmija robed up and went to the home of Prince Jayasena. Jayasena approached and said: “Worthy Bhūmija, there are some ascetics and brahmins who have this doctrine: ‘If you make a wish and lead the spiritual life, you can’t win the fruit. If you don’t make a wish… if you both make and don’t make a wish… if you neither make nor don’t make a wish and lead the spiritual life, you can’t win the fruit.’ What does the worthy Bhūmija’s Teacher say about this?”

“Prince, I haven’t heard and learned this in the presence of the Buddha. But it’s possible that he might explain it like this: ‘If you lead the spiritual life irrationally, you can’t win the fruit, regardless of whether you make a wish, don’t make a wish, both do and do not, or neither do nor don’t. But if you lead the spiritual life rationally, you can win the fruit, regardless of whether you make a wish, don’t make a wish, both do and do not, or neither do nor don’t.’ I haven’t heard this in the presence of the Buddha, but it’s possible he might explain it like that.” “If that’s what your teacher says, worthy Bhūmija, he clearly stands head and shoulders above all the various other ascetics and brahmins.” Then Prince Jayasena served Venerable Bhūmija from his own dish.

Confirmed in full

§7–9After the meal, on his return from almsround, Bhūmija went to the Buddha, told him all that had happened, and added: “Answering this way, I trust that I repeated what the Buddha has said, and didn’t misrepresent him with an untruth. I trust my explanation was in line with the teaching, and that there are no legitimate grounds for rebuttal or criticism.” “Indeed, Bhūmija, in answering this way you repeated what I’ve said, and didn’t misrepresent me with an untruth. Your explanation was in line with the teaching, and there are no legitimate grounds for rebuttal or criticism.

There are some ascetics and brahmins who have wrong view, wrong purpose, wrong speech, wrong action, wrong livelihood, wrong effort, wrong mindfulness, and wrong immersion. If they lead the spiritual life, they can’t win the fruit, regardless of whether they make a wish, don’t make a wish, both do and do not, or neither do nor don’t. Why is that? Because that’s an irrational way to win the fruit.

Four similes

§10–13Suppose there was a person in need of oil. While wandering in search of oil, they tried heaping sand in a bucket, sprinkling it thoroughly with water, and pressing it out. But by doing this, they couldn’t extract any oil, regardless of whether they made a wish or not. Why is that? Because that’s an irrational way to extract oil. And so it is for any ascetics and brahmins who have wrong view… wrong immersion: leading the spiritual life, they can’t win the fruit, whatever their wish, because that’s an irrational way to win the fruit.

Suppose there was a person in need of milk, who tried pulling the horn of a newly-calved cow. But by doing this, they couldn’t get any milk, whatever their wish, because that’s an irrational way to get milk — and so it is for wrong view leading nowhere.

Suppose there was a person in need of butter, who tried pouring water into a pot and churning it with a stick. But by doing this, they couldn’t produce any butter, whatever their wish, because that’s an irrational way to produce butter — and so it is for wrong view leading nowhere.

Suppose there was a person in need of fire, who tried drilling a green, sappy log with a drill-stick. But by doing this, they couldn’t start a fire, whatever their wish, because that’s an irrational way to start a fire — and so it is for wrong view leading nowhere.

§14–18There are some ascetics and brahmins who have right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion. If they lead the spiritual life, they can win the fruit, regardless of whether they make a wish, don’t make a wish, both do and do not, or neither do nor don’t. Why is that? Because that’s a rational way to win the fruit.

Suppose there was a person in need of oil, who heaped sesame flour in a bucket, sprinkled it with water, and pressed it out. By doing this, they could extract oil, whatever their wish, because that’s a rational way to extract oil — and so it is for right view leading to the fruit.

Suppose there was a person in need of milk, who pulled the udder of a newly-calved cow. By doing this, they could get milk, whatever their wish, because that’s a rational way to get milk — and so it is for right view leading to the fruit.

Suppose there was a person in need of butter, who poured curds into a pot and churned them. By doing this, they could produce butter, whatever their wish, because that’s a rational way to produce butter — and so it is for right view leading to the fruit.

Suppose there was a person in need of fire, who drilled a dried-up, withered log with a drill-stick. By doing this, they could start a fire, whatever their wish, because that’s a rational way to start a fire — and so it is for right view leading to the fruit.

An echo

§19Bhūmija, it would be no wonder if, had these four similes occurred to you, Prince Jayasena would have gained confidence in you and shown his confidence.” “But sir, how could these four similes have occurred to me as they did to the Buddha, since they were neither supernaturally inspired, nor learned before in the past?” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Bhūmija approved what the Buddha said.

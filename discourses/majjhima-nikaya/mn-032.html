Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 32

The Greater Discourse at Gosiṅga

Mahāgosiṅgasutta

Setting
The sal forest park at Gosiṅga, on a bright moonlit night with the sal trees in full blossom
Speakers
Sāriputta (moderator), Ānanda, Revata, Anuruddha, Mahākassapa, Mahāmoggallāna, and the Buddha
Form
A round-table conversation — each senior monk describes the ideal practitioner who would adorn this forest; the Buddha gives the final word
Length
~10 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 184 (Madhyama-āgama 184)
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — no technical argument; pure narrative. The discourse is a gallery of spiritual ideals, each answer a self-portrait. The power lies in the contrast between the monks’ varied answers and the Buddha’s single, radical simplicity.

Why this discourse, thirty-second

On a bright moonlit night with the sal trees in full blossom, Sāriputta asks five senior monks a single aesthetic question: “What kind of mendicant would grace this park?” Each monk answers from the heart — and each answer is a mirror of his own mastery. The Buddha validates every answer, then gives his own: a monk who sits down after the meal and resolves not to break the posture until the mind is freed.

The discourse is one of the canon’s most elegant: no argumentation, no doctrine, no correction. Just six accomplished practitioners revealing what they most value — and the Buddha reframing all of it with a single image of uncompromising resolve.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Each great practitioner’s ideal reflects his own mastery — learning, retreat, clairvoyance, asceticism, Dhamma discussion, mental command — and all are valid; but the Buddha’s own ideal is starker than any of them: a monk who sits down after the meal and will not rise until freed.

The six answers and their mirrors

The discourse’s genius is that each monk’s answer is a self-portrait. The Buddha explicitly confirms this by saying each “answered in the right way for him” — meaning each answer reflects the speaker’s own realized strength:

MonkIdeal practitionerWhat it reveals
ĀnandaVery learned, teaching the four assemblies with well-rounded wordsĀnanda is the “guardian of the Dhamma,” famous for remembering everything the Buddha taught. His ideal is the scholar-teacher.
RevataEnjoys retreat, committed to inner serenity, frequents empty hutsRevata is the tradition’s foremost practitioner of seclusion. His ideal is the silent contemplative.
AnuruddhaSurveys a thousandfold galaxy with the divine eyeAnuruddha is famous for his clairvoyance. His ideal is the visionary with cosmic perception.
MahākassapaWilderness-dweller, rag-robed, of few wishes — accomplished in ethics, immersion, wisdom, and freedom, and praises these thingsMahākassapa is the foremost ascetic, the model of contentment with little. His ideal is the austere master who both practices and advocates the ascetic life.
MahāmoggallānaTwo mendicants who discuss the Dhamma without faltering, their conversation flowing onMoggallāna is described by the Buddha as “a Dhamma speaker.” His ideal is the living dialogue — two practitioners sharpening each other.
SāriputtaA mendicant who masters the mind and is not mastered by it — entering whatever meditation or attainment they want, whenever they wantSāriputta has total command of meditative states. His ideal is sovereign mental control — the mind as a wardrobe, each attainment a garment chosen at will.

The Buddha’s answer

The Buddha validates all six answers: “You’ve all spoken well in your own way.” Then he gives his own. And it is startlingly simple: a mendicant who, after the meal, sits down cross-legged, sets the body straight, establishes mindfulness, and resolves: “I will not break this sitting posture until my mind is freed from the defilements by not grasping!”

The Buddha’s answer does not describe an ability, a quality, or a way of life. It describes a moment of resolve. No learning, no cosmic vision, no mastery of states — just a person who sits down and will not get up until they are free. It is the most radical answer in the room: not because it contradicts the others, but because it strips away everything except the essential act.

The narrative frame: a moonlit gathering

The setting is unusually vivid. Sāriputta’s question opens with: “The sal forest park at Gosiṅga is lovely, the night is bright, the sal trees are in full blossom, and heavenly scents seem to float on the air.” This is repeated by each subsequent speaker. The repetition is not formulaic filler — it creates atmosphere. This is a gathering of friends on a beautiful night, sharing what they most care about. The discourse’s tone is intimate and celebratory, not didactic.

Relation to MN 31

MN 31 (The Shorter Discourse at Gosiṅga) and MN 32 share a setting but almost nothing else. MN 31 is about three monks living in harmony; MN 32 is about six senior monks in conversation. MN 31 asks “how do you live together?”; MN 32 asks “what do you most admire?” The two discourses complement each other: MN 31 shows the practice of communal life; MN 32 shows the diversity of realized perspectives within a single community.

Three questions Western students often ask

“Is the Buddha saying the other monks are wrong?” No. He says “Good, good” to each one and explicitly confirms that each answered “in the right way for him.” The monks’ answers are genuine expressions of realized qualities. The Buddha is not correcting but completing: his answer adds the dimension of resolve that underlies all the others. Learning, retreat, vision, asceticism, dialogue, and mastery are all real — but they culminate in a moment when someone sits down and refuses to rise until freed.

“Why does Sāriputta’s answer sound the most impressive?” Sāriputta’s answer — mastering the mind rather than being mastered by it, entering any state at will — sounds like the summit. And it is, in one sense: Sāriputta is the chief disciple. But the Buddha’s answer is different in kind, not degree. Sāriputta describes capacity; the Buddha describes intention. You might have total command of all meditative states and still not resolve to use them for liberation. The Buddha’s point: the decisive factor is not what you can do but what you are determined to do.

“Is this discourse about meditation or about community?” Both. The monks are alone in their contemplative achievements, but they share them freely in conversation. Moggallāna’s answer — two mendicants discussing the Dhamma without faltering — is itself a description of what is happening in the discourse. MN 32 enacts its own teaching: a community of practitioners, each with distinct strengths, enriching each other through honest dialogue.

Key terms

sobheyya — would adorn; would grace. The aesthetic framing of Sāriputta’s question. Not “what is the best practitioner?” but “what kind of practitioner would beautify this place?” The question invites subjective, heartfelt answers rather than doctrinal ones.
bahussuta — very learned; one who has heard much. Ānanda’s ideal. The term denotes not academic knowledge but comprehensive familiarity with the teachings as an oral tradition — hearing, retaining, rehearsing, and being able to teach.
paṭisallāna — retreat; seclusion. Revata’s ideal. Not mere isolation but the active practice of withdrawing into inner serenity — frequenting empty huts as a deliberate discipline.
dibbacakkhu — the divine eye; clairvoyance. Anuruddha’s ideal. The ability to perceive a thousandfold galaxy — a sign of mastery over the higher knowledges.
dhutaṅga — ascetic practices. Mahākassapa’s ideal. Voluntary austerities — wilderness dwelling, rag robes, almsfood only — undertaken not as penance but as simplification and contentment.
dhammīkathā — Dhamma discussion. Moggallāna’s ideal. Two practitioners questioning and answering each other without faltering — a living, flowing exchange about the teachings.
cetovasippatta — mastery of the mind. Sāriputta’s ideal. The capacity to enter any meditative state at will — the mind as instrument, not master.
anādāna — not grasping. The key word in the Buddha’s answer: the mind is freed “by not grasping” (anādānena). Liberation is not an addition but a release — the cessation of clinging.

The text

MN 32 is a narrative discourse structured as a round-table conversation. Each monk’s answer is given once in the moonlit gathering and then summarized when Sāriputta reports to the Buddha. The repeated summaries are condensed here. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The moonlit gathering

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the sal forest park at Gosiṅga, together with several well-known senior disciples, such as the venerables Sāriputta, Mahāmoggallāna, Mahākassapa, Anuruddha, Revata, Ānanda, and others.

§2Then in the late afternoon, Venerable Mahāmoggallāna came out of retreat, went to Venerable Mahākassapa, and said, “Come, Reverend Kassapa, let’s go to Venerable Sāriputta to hear the teaching.” “Yes, reverend,” Mahākassapa replied. Then, together with Venerable Anuruddha, they went to Sāriputta to hear the teaching.

Seeing them, Venerable Ānanda went to Venerable Revata, told him what was happening, and invited him also.

Sāriputta’s question

§3Sāriputta saw them coming from a distance and said to Ānanda, “Come, Venerable Ānanda. Welcome to Ānanda, the Buddha’s attendant, who is so close to the Buddha. Ānanda, the sal forest park at Gosiṅga is lovely, the night is bright, the sal trees are in full blossom, and heavenly scents seem to float on the air. What kind of mendicant would grace this park?”

Ānanda: the learned teacher

§4“Reverend Sāriputta, it’s a mendicant who is very learned, remembering and keeping what they’ve learned. These teachings are good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, meaningful and well-phrased, describing a spiritual practice that’s entirely full and pure. They are very learned in such teachings, remembering them, rehearsing them, mentally scrutinizing them, and penetrating them theoretically. And they teach the four assemblies in order to uproot the underlying tendencies with well-rounded and coherent words and phrases. That’s the kind of mendicant who would grace this park.”

Revata: the solitary contemplative

§5When he had spoken, Sāriputta said to Revata, “Reverend Revata, Ānanda has answered according to his own inspiration. And now we ask you the same question.”

“Reverend Sāriputta, it’s a mendicant who enjoys retreat and loves retreat. They’re committed to inner serenity of the heart, they don’t neglect absorption, they’re endowed with discernment, and they frequent empty huts. That’s the kind of mendicant who would grace this park.”

Anuruddha: the cosmic seer

§6“Reverend Sāriputta, it’s a mendicant who surveys a thousandfold galaxy with clairvoyance that is purified and surpasses the human, just as a person with clear eyes could survey a thousand orbits from the upper floor of a royal longhouse. That’s the kind of mendicant who would grace this park.”

Mahākassapa: the austere master

§7“Reverend Sāriputta, it’s a mendicant who lives in the wilderness, eats only almsfood, wears rag robes, and owns just three robes; and they praise these things. They are of few wishes, content, secluded, aloof, and energetic; and they praise these things. They are accomplished in ethics, immersion, wisdom, freedom, and the knowledge and vision of freedom; and they praise these things. That’s the kind of mendicant who would grace this park.”

Mahāmoggallāna: the Dhamma dialogue

§8“Reverend Sāriputta, it’s when two mendicants engage in discussion about the teaching. They question each other and answer each other’s questions without faltering, and their discussion on the teaching flows on. That’s the kind of mendicant who would grace this park.”

Sāriputta: the sovereign mind

§9Then Mahāmoggallāna said to Sāriputta, “Each of us has spoken from our heart. And now we ask you: Sāriputta, the sal forest park at Gosiṅga is lovely, the night is bright, the sal trees are in full blossom, and heavenly scents seem to float on the air. What kind of mendicant would grace this park?”

“Reverend Moggallāna, it’s when a mendicant masters their mind and is not mastered by it. In the morning, they abide in whatever meditation or attainment they want. At midday, and in the evening, they abide in whatever meditation or attainment they want.

Suppose that a ruler or their chief minister had a chest full of garments of different colors. In the morning, they’d don whatever pair of garments they wanted. At midday, and in the evening, they’d don whatever pair of garments they wanted. In the same way, a mendicant masters their mind and is not mastered by it. That’s the kind of mendicant who would grace this park.”

Before the Buddha

§10Then Sāriputta said to those venerables, “Each of us has spoken from the heart. Come, reverends, let’s go to the Buddha, and inform him about this. As he answers, so we’ll remember it.” “Yes, reverend,” they replied.

Then those venerables went to the Buddha, bowed, and sat down to one side. Venerable Sāriputta reported the entire conversation.

Sāriputta repeats each monk’s answer to the Buddha. After each summary, the Buddha responds: “Good, good, Sāriputta! [Monk’s name] answered in the right way for him.” He confirms that Ānanda is indeed very learned, Revata enjoys retreat, Anuruddha surveys the galaxy with clairvoyance, Kassapa lives the ascetic life and praises it, and Moggallāna is a Dhamma speaker.

§11Then Moggallāna reported Sāriputta’s own answer to the Buddha. The Buddha said, “Good, good, Moggallāna! Sāriputta answered in the right way for him. For Sāriputta masters his mind and is not mastered by it.”

The Buddha’s answer

§12When he had spoken, Sāriputta asked the Buddha, “Sir, who has spoken well?”

“You’ve all spoken well in your own way. However, listen to me also as to what kind of mendicant would grace this sal forest park at Gosiṅga.

It’s a mendicant who, after the meal, returns from almsround, sits down cross-legged, sets their body straight, and brings mindfulness to the present, thinking: ‘I will not break this sitting posture until my mind is freed from the defilements by not grasping!’

That’s the kind of mendicant who would grace this park.”

That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, those venerables approved what the Buddha said.

Self-check quiz

Ten questions. Click an answer to see immediate feedback. No score is recorded — this is for your own checking.

Question 1 of 10
Who initiates the question “What kind of mendicant would grace this park?”
Correct: C. Sāriputta poses the aesthetic question to Ānanda first, then passes it to each monk in turn. The question’s framing — “grace” (sobheyya) rather than “best” — invites personal, heartfelt answers rather than doctrinal ones.
Question 2 of 10
What is Ānanda’s answer?
Correct: A. Ānanda’s ideal reflects his own role as the “guardian of the Dhamma” — the monk who remembered and transmitted the Buddha’s teachings. His answer values learning, retention, and skilled communication.
Question 3 of 10
What is Mahākassapa’s ideal?
Correct: D. Mahākassapa’s answer is the most detailed and the most personal: the ascetic life in full. The repeated “and they praise these things” is distinctive — Kassapa values not only practice but advocacy. He doesn’t merely live austerely; he champions austerity as an ideal.
Question 4 of 10
What is Mahāmoggallāna’s answer?
Correct: B. Moggallāna’s answer is unique in describing a pair, not a solitary individual. The Buddha confirms it by calling Moggallāna “a Dhamma speaker.” The image of two practitioners in flowing dialogue is itself enacted in the discourse: Moggallāna’s ideal is what the monks are doing right now.
Question 5 of 10
What simile does Sāriputta use to illustrate mental mastery?
Correct: A. Sāriputta’s garment-chest simile captures his ideal of sovereign mental freedom: the mind is not a prison but a wardrobe. Any meditative state is available at any time, chosen at will — the practitioner is master, not servant, of their own inner life.
Question 6 of 10
How does the Buddha respond to the monks’ individual answers?
Correct: C. The Buddha validates every answer as a genuine expression of that monk’s realized quality. There is no correction, no ranking. Each answer is true — and true to the speaker. The diversity of valid answers is itself the teaching: the path expresses itself differently in different practitioners.
Question 7 of 10
What is the Buddha’s own answer?
Correct: D. The Buddha’s answer is radically simple. It does not describe an ability, a quality, or a way of life but a moment of resolve. The decisive factor is not what you can do but what you are determined to do — sit down and refuse to rise until freed.
Question 8 of 10
How does the Buddha’s answer differ from Sāriputta’s in kind?
Correct: B. This is the discourse’s deepest point. Sāriputta describes mastery — sovereign command over states. The Buddha describes determination — an irrevocable commitment to liberation in this very sitting. You could have Sāriputta’s capacity and still lack the Buddha’s resolve. The Buddha adds the missing dimension: will.
Question 9 of 10
Why does each monk’s answer mirror their own spiritual strength?
Correct: A. Sāriputta’s question is genius because it is open-ended and aesthetic, not doctrinal. “What would grace this park?” has no single right answer — so each monk reveals what they most deeply value. The result is a gallery of spiritual portraits, each authentic, each complementary.
Question 10 of 10
What is the relationship between MN 31 and MN 32?
Correct: C. MN 31 features Anuruddha, Nandiya, and Kimbila (communal life and progressive attainment); MN 32 features Sāriputta, Ānanda, Revata, Anuruddha, Mahākassapa, and Mahāmoggallāna (a moonlit conversation about spiritual ideals). They complement each other: practice as daily cooperation (MN 31) and practice as diverse realization (MN 32).

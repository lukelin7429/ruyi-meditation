Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 91

With Brahmāyu

Brahmāyusutta

Setting
The land of the Videhans; the city of Mithilā, in the Maghadeva Mango Grove
Speakers
The Buddha, the aged brahmin Brahmāyu, and his pupil Uttara
Form
A famous teacher’s investigation — the marks of a great man verified, the Buddha’s deportment observed for seven months, and the old brahmin’s own awakening.
Length
~20 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 161 (Madhyama-āgama 161)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — long; its treasure is the portrait of awakening made visible in ordinary conduct.

Why this discourse, ninety-first

A brahmin a hundred and twenty years old, the most learned man in his city, hears of a teacher said to be perfectly awakened — and decides to check. He sends his best pupil to verify whether this Gotama bears the “thirty-two marks of a great man,” the bodily signs his ancient hymns say mark out one destined to be either a world-ruler or a Buddha. The pupil finds the marks — but stays seven months more, shadowing the Buddha, and returns with something far more telling than any mark: a portrait of awakening made visible in how a person walks, eats, sits, and speaks.

The discourse turns on two ways of recognising greatness. The first is the brahmin’s inherited science of marks — the auspicious bodily signs (a wheel on each sole, a golden complexion, the proportions of a banyan, a voice like a divine bird) by which the wise of the day claimed to read a man’s destiny. The Buddha is shown to bear all thirty-two, including two normally hidden, which he reveals by his powers. But the marks, for all their fame, are the lesser evidence. The greater is what the pupil Uttara discovers when he simply watches.

For seven months Uttara follows the Buddha “like a shadow,” and the account he brings back is one of the canon’s most beautiful set-pieces: the unhurried, composed, gracious deportment of a fully awakened being, observed in the smallest acts of daily life. How he places his foot; how he sits without fidgeting; how he eats — tasting the food but without greed for the taste, reflecting on its true purpose; how his voice carries, clear and resonant, only as far as the assembly. “Such is the worthy Gotama,” Uttara concludes, “such he is — and more than that.” The marks may make a great man; but only the conduct shows the awakened one.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Buddha bears the thirty-two marks of a great man — but his awakening shows far more in his flawless, unhurried deportment, observed for seven months: every movement composed, his eating mindful and free of greed, his voice clear and measured; and when the old brahmin Brahmāyu finally meets him, hears the “brahmin” and the “awakened” redefined by insight rather than birth, and receives the graduated teaching, the vision of the Dhamma arises in him and he becomes a non-returner.

The marks of a great man

The frame rests on an old belief: that a “great man” (mahāpurisa) is born bearing thirty-two auspicious bodily marks, and that such a one has only two possible destinies — if he stays in the world, he becomes a “wheel-turning monarch” (cakkavatti), a just ruler of the whole earth “without rod or sword”; if he goes forth, he becomes a fully awakened Buddha. Brahmāyu, a master of this lore, sends Uttara to look. Uttara finds all the marks but two — the sheathed genitals and the unusually large tongue — which the Buddha, knowing his doubt, reveals by psychic power and by extending his tongue to touch his ears, nostrils, and forehead. The marks themselves (a thousand-spoked wheel on each sole, golden skin too fine for dust to cling to, a body proportioned like a banyan tree, a voice like the cuckoo’s) belong to the symbolic iconography of the age, and would later shape how the Buddha was depicted in art. But the discourse treats them as a starting point, not the real evidence. They establish that here is, at least, a “great man”; what kind of great man — ruler or Buddha — the marks cannot tell. For that, one must look at the life.

Awakening made visible: the seven-month watch

This is the discourse’s heart and its lasting gift. Convinced of the marks, Uttara decides to “follow him and observe his deportment,” and shadows the Buddha for seven months. What he reports is not doctrine but presence — the way a fully awakened mind shows itself in the body, moment by moment:

  • Walking. He steps first with the right foot, lifting it neither too far nor too near, neither too fast nor too slow; only the lower body moves; he turns his whole body to look, gazing a plough’s length ahead — composed, effortless, unhurried.
  • Sitting. He settles without leaning on his hand or dropping heavily; he does not fidget, fold his limbs nervously, or prop his chin; “even sitting in a crowd, he still practises seclusion.”
  • Eating. He takes a measured amount, chews each portion fully, “tasting the food but without greed for the taste,” and eats reflecting on its real purpose — “not for fun, indulgence, or adornment, but only to sustain this body… and support spiritual practice.”
  • Speaking. His voice has eight qualities — clear, comprehensible, charming, audible, lucid, undistorted, deep, and resonant — and reaches exactly as far as the assembly and no further; afterward, his hearers leave “looking back at him alone, and not forgetting their lesson.”

The point of this minute attention is profound. The Buddha’s grace is not etiquette or performance; it is the natural overflow of a mind emptied of greed, restlessness, and self-concern. A person who wants nothing does not snatch or fidget; a person at peace does not hurry; a person without vanity does not perform. So the awakened mind writes itself on the body, in a thousand small composures, for anyone patient enough to watch. This is recognition by fruit, not by sign: Uttara could have read the marks in an afternoon, but it took him seven months of watching to see the awakening — because awakening is shown not in how one is built, but in how one lives.

Eating, and the reflection on food

The treatment of eating deserves a closer look, because it contains a teaching every practitioner can use. The Buddha eats “experiencing the taste, but without experiencing greed for the taste” — the model of mindful consumption: fully present to the flavour, yet not driven by craving for it. And he eats with a fixed reflection, the classic contemplation on food: it is taken “not for fun, indulgence, adornment, or decoration, but only to sustain this body, to avoid harm, and to support the spiritual life… so that I shall end old discomfort and not give rise to new.” Food is medicine, not entertainment; the body a vehicle to be maintained, not a pleasure to be indulged. In this single daily act — the most ordinary thing a person does — the whole orientation of the awakened life is visible: enjoyment without grasping, use without indulgence, every necessity turned toward freedom.

The brahmin and the awakened, redefined

When Brahmāyu at last meets the Buddha, he asks — in verse — the deep questions behind his life’s learning: how does one truly become a brahmin, a master of the threefold knowledge, a sage, an awakened one? These are his own honorifics, the titles of Vedic mastery; and the Buddha quietly empties and refills them. The true master of the “three knowledges” is not one who has memorised three Vedas, but one who knows his past lives, sees beings reborn by their deeds, and has reached the end of rebirth. The true brahmin, sage, awakened one is defined by purity of mind, the ending of greed, the completion of the spiritual journey, having “gone beyond all things.” It is the same move the Buddha makes throughout the Brāhmaṇa chapter: taking the cherished vocabulary of birth and ritual and re-grounding it in attainment. A brahmin is not born; a brahmin is awakened.

The old master’s awakening

Brahmāyu, a hundred and twenty and famous across his land, kisses the Buddha’s feet and names himself — to the astonishment of the crowd, who cannot believe so eminent a man would so humble himself. The Buddha gently has him return to his seat, then gives him the graduated teaching: generosity, ethics, and heaven; the drawbacks of sensual pleasure and the benefit of renunciation; and, when his mind is “ready, pliable, rid of hindrances,” the four noble truths. “Just as a clean cloth rid of stains would properly absorb dye,” the vision of the Dhamma arises in him on the spot: “Everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease.” He goes for refuge, and hosts the Buddha for seven days. Soon after, he dies — and the Buddha declares that Brahmāyu, who “practised in line with the teaching and did not trouble me,” ended the five lower fetters and became a non-returner, bound for a final extinguishment from which he will not come back. The most learned brahmin of his age, at the very end of a long life, set down his birth and his Vedas and became a noble disciple — recognising the awakened one not by the marks on his body, but by the freedom in his every step.

Key terms

mahāpurisa-lakkhaṇa — the “thirty-two marks of a great man,” auspicious bodily signs by which the wise of the day read destiny: a great man becomes either a wheel-turning monarch or a Buddha.
cakkavatti — a “wheel-turning monarch,” a just ruler of the whole earth “without rod or sword.” The worldly destiny of a great man; the Buddha is its spiritual counterpart.
iriyāpatha — “deportment,” the four postures of walking, standing, sitting, lying. Uttara’s seven-month watch shows awakening written in the Buddha’s composed, unhurried bearing.
reflection on food — eating “not for fun… but only to sustain this body… and support the spiritual life”; tasting without greed for the taste. The whole awakened orientation visible in one daily act.
brahmin redefined — the true master of the “three knowledges,” sage, and awakened one is defined not by birth or Veda but by purity of mind, the ending of greed, and the end of rebirth. A brahmin is awakened, not born.
anāgāmī — a “non-returner.” Brahmāyu, at a hundred and twenty, awakens to the Dhamma-eye and, ending the five lower fetters, is reborn to a final extinguishment from which he will not return.

The text

The full discourse, substantially abridged — the thirty-two marks and the long deportment portrait are summarized here, with the redefinitions and the awakening given more fully. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The brahmin sends his pupil

§1–7So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was wandering in the land of the Videhans. The brahmin Brahmāyu of Mithilā — a hundred and twenty years old, a master of the Vedas and of the marks of a great man — heard of the Buddha’s reputation, and sent his pupil Uttara to verify it. “A great man bearing the thirty-two marks has only two destinies: a wheel-turning monarch, or a Buddha. Go and see.” Uttara examined the Buddha and saw all the marks but two — the sheathed genitals and the large tongue — which the Buddha, knowing his doubt, revealed by his power, extending his tongue to touch his ears, nostrils, and forehead.

Seven months’ watching

§8–22Convinced of the marks, Uttara thought, “Let me follow him and observe his deportment,” and shadowed the Buddha “like a shadow for seven months.” Returning, he reported to Brahmāyu. He described the Buddha’s every movement: how he walks — stepping first with the right foot, neither too far nor too near, only the lower body moving, gazing a plough’s length ahead; how he sits without fidgeting, “even in a crowd still practising seclusion”; how he eats — a measured amount, chewing fully, “tasting the food but without greed for the taste,” reflecting that food is taken “not for fun or indulgence, but only to sustain this body and support the spiritual life”; how his voice has eight qualities — clear, charming, deep, resonant — reaching just as far as the assembly. “He wishes only for the welfare of himself, of others, and of the whole world.” “Such is the worthy Gotama, sir — such he is, and more than that.” At this Brahmāyu rose, raised his palms, and paid homage three times.

Brahmāyu meets the Buddha

§24–31When the Buddha came to Mithilā, Brahmāyu went to see him, examined the marks, and asked in verse about the two he could not see; the Buddha revealed them. Then the Buddha said: “The thirty-two marks are all on my body; doubt not, brahmin. I have known what should be known, developed what should be developed, given up what should be given up — and so, brahmin, I am a Buddha. Ask whatever you desire.”

The brahmin and the awakened, redefined

§32–33Brahmāyu asked: “How does one become a brahmin, a master of the three knowledges, a sage, an awakened one?” The Buddha replied in verse: “One who knows their past lives, sees heaven and the places of loss, and has attained the end of rebirth — such a sage has perfect insight. Knowing their mind is pure, freed from greed, having given up birth and death and completed the journey, gone beyond all things — such a one is declared to be awakened.”

The old master awakens

§34–39Brahmāyu kissed the Buddha’s feet and named himself, to the wonder of the crowd. The Buddha had him return to his seat and taught him step by step — giving, ethics, heaven; the drawbacks of sensuality and the benefit of renunciation; and then the four noble truths. “Just as a clean cloth would absorb dye,” the vision of the Dhamma arose in him: “Everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease.” He went for refuge and hosted the Buddha for seven days. Soon after the Buddha left, Brahmāyu died. Asked his destiny, the Buddha said: “Brahmāyu was astute; he practised in line with the teaching and did not trouble me. With the ending of the five lower fetters, he has been reborn spontaneously, to be extinguished there, not liable to return from that world.”

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 124

With Bakkula

Bakkulasutta

Setting
Near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove — the Buddha does not appear at all in this discourse
Speakers
Venerable Bakkula, questioned by his old lay friend, the naked ascetic Kassapa
Form
An interrogation about a lifetime of extreme austerity, ending in ordination, awakening, and a death announced in advance
Length
~10 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A counterpart survives in the Chinese Madhyama Āgama (MĀ 34), though missing many of the austerity items found here
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — vivid and narrative, but its real interest is what it reveals about how the canon itself grew

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-twenty-fourth

Something is quietly unusual about this discourse before a single word of dialogue begins: the Buddha never appears in it. There is no “at one time the Buddha was staying near…” — only Venerable Bakkula, alone, extraordinarily old, in the Bamboo Grove. Scholars reading the text closely — and even the ancient commentary itself — conclude this discourse was composed a century after the Buddha’s death, likely connected to the Second Council’s dispute over how strict monastic discipline should be. What survives is less a teaching from the Buddha than a portrait: an extreme ascetic ideal, preserved inside the canon by the faction that wanted the Saṅgha held to it.

Read this way, the discourse becomes doubly interesting — both for the striking, austere life it describes, and as a rare window into how the Buddhist scriptures themselves kept growing, and whose voices shaped what got remembered as “incredible and amazing.”

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Questioned by an old friend about a lifetime of monastic discipline, the ninety-year-old Venerable Bakkula recounts eighty years free of even a single sensual thought and an inventory of austerities far beyond ordinary practice, awakens within eight days of ordination, and dies as he lived — on his own terms, announced in advance, seated among his community.

A question reframed

The naked ascetic Kassapa, an old friend of Bakkula’s from lay life, asks the blunt question first: in eighty years since going forth, how many times has Bakkula had sex? Bakkula refuses the premise of the question outright — “you shouldn’t ask me such a question” — and redirects it to something sharper: “Rather, you should ask me this: ‘in these eighty years, how many times have sensual perceptions ever arisen in you?’” The distinction matters. Not committing an act is one thing; never once having the desire arise in the mind at all is a claim of an entirely different order. Bakkula’s answer: none. Not once, in eighty years, has he recalled a sensual perception, a perception of ill will, or a perception of cruelty arising — nor the corresponding thoughts.

An inventory of extreme renunciation

From this claim about the mind, the conversation moves into a long, itemized catalogue of things Bakkula says he never once did in eighty years — conduct that is not forbidden by the monastic code, but that exceeds it by a wide margin:

CategoryNever once, in 80 years
Robesaccepting a robe from a householder, cutting or sewing a robe, dyeing a robe, or searching for robe material even for fellow mendicants
Foodaccepting a meal invitation, wishing to be invited, sitting or eating inside a lay house
Women & nunsnoticing the details of a woman’s appearance, teaching a woman even a four-line verse, or going to the nuns’ quarters at all
Studentsgiving anyone the going-forth or full ordination, taking on a dependent, or being attended by a novice
Comfort & healthbathing with bath powder, seeking a massage, being ill “even for as long as it takes to pull a cow’s udder,” accepting medicine, using a backrest, or sleeping on a proper bed
Retreatbeginning the rainy-season residence within a village

The commentary makes an important, slightly unsettling observation about this list: if every mendicant lived this way, the Saṅgha could not survive. No one would train new members; no one would accept the robes and support that sustain a monastic community. Bakkula represents not the Buddhist mainstream but one faction’s ideal — taken to a limit almost no institution could actually run on.

Seven days a debtor

One detail stands out from the catalogue of restraint: Bakkula’s own account of his awakening. “For seven days I ate the nation’s almsfood as a debtor,” he says. “Then on the eighth day I became enlightened.” The image is precise: to eat food offered in faith while still unawakened is, in this view, to incur a kind of debt — a debt only repaid once the practice bears its fruit. Bakkula’s debt, remarkably, was cleared within a single week of ordination.

Kassapa’s conversion, and a death announced

Moved by what he has heard, Kassapa asks to be ordained under Bakkula on the spot — and, “living alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute,” soon becomes an arahant himself. Some time later, Bakkula takes up a latchkey, the master key used to open the monastery’s inner dwellings, and goes from room to room: “Come forth, venerables, come forth! Today will be my full extinguishment.” He then dies — not alone in a hut, not unexpectedly, but seated in the very middle of the assembled Saṅgha, having personally summoned his community to witness it.

What the text reveals about itself

The repeated refrain running through the discourse — “this too we remember as an incredible and amazing quality of Venerable Bakkula” — is, according to the commentary’s own careful analysis, very likely a later addition, appended by the Elders of the Second Council rather than part of an original telling. The evidence is precise: the refrains are in the plural voice (implying a community speaking, not a single narrator), they lack the grammatical marker of a direct quotation, they continue even after the conversation with Kassapa has already concluded, and — tellingly — the same refrains appear in the Chinese parallel too, meaning they were added before the Pali and Chinese versions diverged, but still well after the Buddha’s own lifetime.

This matters beyond mere textual trivia. It shows precisely how far back comparing different versions of a sutta can actually take us: only to the point where the traditions split, which here was itself already a century or more removed from the events described. And it fits a known historical dispute — the Second Council’s division between “rigorist” and “laxist” factions over how strictly the Vinaya should be kept, a controversy that turned in the rigorists’ favor. Bakkula’s extraordinary self-denial, exceeding even the famously austere Mahākassapa, reads as that rigorist party’s chosen icon. Notably, Bakkula’s own verses preserved elsewhere in the canon, in the Theragāthā, are simple and gentle, without any of this severity — a reminder that even revered figures can be reshaped by later generations into vessels for their own convictions.

Key terms

purāṇagihisahāya — “an old friend from lay life”: a rare phrase marking Kassapa as someone who knew Bakkula before either renounced the household life.
saññā vs. vitakka — “perception” and “thought”: Bakkula’s redirected question distinguishes never committing an act from never once having even the perception or thought of it arise — a claim about the root of desire, not merely its expression.
avāpuraṇa — the “latchkey,” a universal key for lifting the bolt on monastery doors from outside; Bakkula uses it to personally summon the community to witness his death.
rigorists vs. laxists — the two factions at the Second Council, a century after the Buddha, disputing how strictly monastic discipline should be kept; Bakkula’s extreme austerity is read as the rigorist faction’s chosen ideal.
“this too we remember…” — the discourse’s repeated refrain, identified by the commentary itself as a likely later addition — evidence, preserved within the text, of the Second Council generation shaping how Bakkula would be remembered.
Thag 3.3 — Bakkula’s own verses in the Theragāthā, notably simple and free of the severity found in this discourse’s portrait of him.

The text

The discourse in full: Kassapa’s reframed question, the six things that never arose, the long inventory of austerities (grouped by category, as the source itself abbreviates with ellipses), the seven-day debt, and the closing scenes of Kassapa’s ordination and Bakkula’s announced death. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A question reframed

§1–3So I have heard. At one time Venerable Bakkula was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove. Then the naked ascetic Kassapa, an old friend of Bakkula’s in lay life, approached him and asked: “Reverend Bakkula, how long has it been since you went forth?” “It has been eighty years, reverend.” “But in these eighty years, how many times have you had sex?” “You shouldn’t ask me such a question. Rather, you should ask me this: ‘But in these eighty years, how many times have sensual perceptions ever arisen in you?’” “But in these eighty years, how many times have sensual perceptions ever arisen in you?” “In these eighty years, I don’t recall that any sensual perception has ever arisen in me.” This we remember as an incredible and amazing quality of Venerable Bakkula.

Six things that never arose

§4–8“In these eighty years, I don’t recall that any perception of ill will or cruelty has ever arisen in me.” This too we remember as an incredible and amazing quality of Venerable Bakkula. “In these eighty years, I don’t recall that any thought of sensuality, ill will, or cruelty has ever arisen in me.” This too we remember as an incredible and amazing quality of Venerable Bakkula.

An inventory of extreme renunciation

§9–37“In these eighty years, I don’t recall accepting a robe from a householder, cutting a robe with a knife, sewing a robe with a needle, dyeing a robe, sewing a robe during the robe-making ceremony, or wandering to look for robe material for my companions in the spiritual life when they are making robes. I don’t recall accepting an invitation, having such a thought as ‘if only someone would invite me,’ sitting down inside a house, or eating inside a house. I don’t recall getting caught up in the features by way of the details of a lady’s appearance, teaching a lady even so much as a four-line verse, going to the nuns’ quarters, or teaching the nuns, trainee nuns, or novice nuns. I don’t recall giving the going forth, giving the ordination, giving dependence, or being looked after by a novice. I don’t recall bathing in the sauna, bathing with bath powder, looking for a massage from my companions in the spiritual life, being ill even for as long as it takes to pull a cow’s udder, being presented with medicine even as much as a bit of yellow myrobalan, leaning on a leaning-board, or preparing a cot.” This too we remember as an incredible and amazing quality of Venerable Bakkula. “In these eighty years, I don’t recall commencing the rainy season residence within a village.” This too we remember as an incredible and amazing quality of Venerable Bakkula.

Seven days a debtor

§38“Reverend, for seven days I ate the nation’s almsfood as a debtor. Then on the eighth day I became enlightened.” This too we remember as an incredible and amazing quality of Venerable Bakkula.

Kassapa’s conversion, and a death announced

§39–41“Reverend Bakkula, may I receive the going forth, the ordination in this teaching and training?” And the naked ascetic Kassapa received the going forth, the ordination. Not long after his ordination, Venerable Kassapa, living alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute, soon realized the supreme end of the spiritual path in this very life. He understood: “Rebirth is ended; the spiritual journey has been completed; what had to be done has been done; there is nothing further for this place.” And Venerable Kassapa became one of the perfected.

Then some time later, Venerable Bakkula, taking a latchkey, went from dwelling to dwelling, saying: “Come forth, venerables, come forth! Today will be my full extinguishment.” This too we remember as an incredible and amazing quality of Venerable Bakkula. And Venerable Bakkula became fully extinguished while seated right in the middle of the Saṅgha. This too we remember as an incredible and amazing quality of Venerable Bakkula.

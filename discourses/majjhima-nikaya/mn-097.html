Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 97

With Dhanañjāni

Dhanañjānisutta

Setting
Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove; later at Dhanañjāni’s home
Speakers
Venerable Sāriputta and the brahmin Dhanañjāni, with the Buddha at the close
Form
Two meetings — a reproach in health, a guidance in dying — and a gentle correction by the Buddha.
Length
~16 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A counterpart is preserved in the Chinese Madhyama Āgama (MĀ 27)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — a vivid ethical story with a subtle ending.

Why this discourse, ninety-seventh

A corrupt official excuses his dishonesty by all the people he must provide for. Venerable Sāriputta answers with a question that cuts the excuse out from under him: when the wardens of hell drag you away, can any of those you sinned “for” save you? They cannot — and there are honest livelihoods that would have fed them all. Later, at the man’s deathbed, the same Sāriputta gives him the gift of a good rebirth — and the Buddha quietly notes that he could have given more.

Dhanañjāni is a tax-collector at the gate of Rājagaha who “robs the householders in the name of the king, and the king in the name of the householders.” His faithful wife has died; he has married a faithless one and slid into negligence. When Sāriputta finally meets him and asks if he is living diligently, Dhanañjāni pours out the standard defence of a worldly life: he must support parents, wives, children, servants; make offerings to friends, guests, ancestors, gods, and king; and keep his own body strong. Surely all this excuses a little corruption.

The discourse falls into two scenes, years apart. In the first, Sāriputta dismantles the duty-excuse with relentless logic. In the second, summoned to the dying man’s bedside, he meets Dhanañjāni’s deepest aspiration — the brahmin longing for the world of Brahmā, Divinity — and teaches the four boundless states as the path there, so that Dhanañjāni dies and is reborn in that realm. Then comes the sutta’s quiet sting: the Buddha observes that Sāriputta “left while there was still more to be done.”

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

No obligation — to parents, family, servants, guests, gods, or king — can excuse wrongdoing, for when the consequences come no one you sinned “for” can take your place and there are always honest livelihoods that harm no one; and when a person is dying, you meet them at the aspiration they actually hold, though Sāriputta’s stopping at the Divine realm shows that even great compassion can aim too low.

Scene one: the duty-excuse demolished

Dhanañjāni’s defence is one every age recognises: I do wrong, but I do it for those who depend on me. The list is long and respectable — parents, wife and children, workers, friends, guests, ancestors, the gods, the king, even the duty to keep his own body healthy. Each name lends his corruption a borrowed dignity. Sāriputta does not deny the duties. He asks one thing of each in turn:

The plea that cannot be made. “Suppose someone behaves unjustly for the sake of their parents, and the wardens of hell drag them off. Could they escape by pleading, ‘I did it for my parents’? Could the parents save them by pleading, ‘It was done for us’?” — “No, Sāriputta. Even as they wailed, they would be cast down into hell.”

The point is the moral solitude of action. Kamma is not transferable: I alone carry the result of what I do, no matter whose name I did it in. The beneficiaries cannot share the debt, and pointing to them cannot cancel it. Then Sāriputta closes the trap from the other side: there is no need for the wrongdoing in the first place. “Others have livelihoods both profitable and legitimate. By these it is possible to provide for your parents, avoid bad deeds, and practise goodness.” The excuse fails twice over — it would not save him if it were true, and it is not even true that he had no honest alternative.

The principle, generalised

Sāriputta then asks, of each duty: who is the better person — one who acts unjustly for their sake, or one who acts justly? Dhanañjāni must answer every time: the just one, “for principled conduct is better than unprincipled conduct.” By the end he has, with his own mouth, conceded that none of his obligations licenses a single dishonest act — and that an honest life could have met them all. The web of relationships he had used as a shield is shown to be, instead, a web of beings he was harming himself for. He approves, agrees, and leaves — the teaching planted, though its fruit will only show much later.

Scene two: the deathbed, and the ladder of realms

Years on, Dhanañjāni is gravely ill, his pain rendered in four of the canon’s most physical similes — a spike drilled into the skull, a leather strap wrenched tight around the head, a butcher’s cleaver opening the belly, a man scorched over glowing coals. He sends for Sāriputta. Rather than begin from doctrine, Sāriputta walks him up a ladder, asking at each rung which is better: hell or the animal realm? — animal; animal or ghost? — ghost; ghost or human? — human; human, then up through all six heavens of the sense-world. The method lets the dying man’s own values do the climbing — and at the top, when Sāriputta names the world of Brahmā, Divinity, beyond all the sense-gods, Dhanañjāni lights up: “Worthy Sāriputta speaks of the realm of Divinity!” he cries, twice. The brahmin’s heart is set on union with the Divine.

The four boundless states as the path to Divinity

Reading this, Sāriputta thinks, “These brahmins are devoted to Divinity; let me teach him the path to its company.” The path he gives is the four boundless states (brahmavihāra — literally “dwellings of Brahmā”): spreading a heart full of love, then compassion, then rejoicing, then equanimity — “to one direction, and a second, and a third, and a fourth… above, below, across, everywhere — abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will.” It is a perfectly chosen instruction: the way to dwell with Brahmā is to dwell as Brahmā dwells, the heart made boundless and benevolent. Dhanañjāni receives it, sends his homage to the Buddha, and after Sāriputta leaves, dies — and is reborn in the realm of Divinity, exactly as he longed.

“While there was still more to be done”

The discourse could end in triumph — a corrupt man brought, in his last hour, to a heavenly rebirth. Instead the Buddha gives it a searching final turn. Twice it is noted that Sāriputta “established Dhanañjāni in the inferior realm of Divinity, then got up and left while there was still more to be done.” The Buddha asks him why, and accepts his reason — the brahmin’s devotion to Brahmā — but the phrase lingers. Rebirth in the Brahmā world, however exalted, is still within the round; it is not liberation. Sāriputta met Dhanañjāni at his aspiration, which was a real kindness; but he could, perhaps, have pointed beyond it, toward the deathless. The sutta leaves the tension open. It is at once a lesson in skilful means — meet a person where they are — and a quiet caution that compassion guided by another’s limited longing may aim lower than the goal truly worth giving. Even the foremost in wisdom is shown, with great tenderness, stopping one rung short.

Key terms

the duty-excuse — Dhanañjāni’s defence that his obligations to family, guests, gods, and king justify his corruption. The respectable cover for wrongdoing in every age.
kamma is not transferable — “could anyone save them by pleading the act was done for their sake? No.” I alone bear the result of what I do, in whoever’s name I did it. The moral solitude of action.
livelihoods profitable and legitimate — the second blade: there was never any need for wrongdoing, since an honest living could have met every duty. The excuse is not even true.
the ladder of realms — Sāriputta’s deathbed method: asking which of two states is better, rung by rung, letting the dying man’s own values climb from hell up to the heavens.
brahmavihāra — the four “dwellings of Brahmā”: boundless love, compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity, spread to all directions. The path to company with Brahmā is to dwell as Brahmā dwells.
“more left to be done” — the Buddha’s gentle note that the Brahmā realm, though exalted, is still within the round, not liberation. Skilful means met the man’s longing — but stopped one rung short of the deathless.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats the long list of duties and the rungs of the ladder. The duty-excuse, the deathbed climb, and the closing exchange with the Buddha are given closely. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

News of a negligent brahmin

§1–3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha in the Bamboo Grove. Venerable Sāriputta, wandering in the Southern Hills, learned from a visiting monk that the brahmin Dhanañjāni, a tax-collector at the city gate, was living negligently — “he robs the householders in the name of the king, and the king in the name of the householders; his wife of faith has died, and he has taken a faithless wife.” “Bad news,” said Sāriputta. “Perhaps one day I shall meet him.” In time he returned to Rājagaha.

The duty-excuse

§4–5On almsround Sāriputta came to Dhanañjāni, who offered him milk; Sāriputta, having eaten, asked him to come to the tree where he would meditate. There Sāriputta asked, “I hope you are living diligently, Dhanañjāni?” “How can I, worthy Sāriputta? I must provide for my parents, my wives and children, my servants and workers; make offerings to friends, kin, guests, ancestors, deities, and king; and this body too must be kept strong.”

The plea that cannot be made

§6–15“What do you think, Dhanañjāni? Suppose someone behaved unjustly for the sake of their parents, and the wardens of hell dragged them off. Could they escape by pleading, ‘I acted for my parents’? Or could the parents save them by pleading it was done for their sake?” “No, worthy Sāriputta. Even as they wailed, they would be cast into hell.” The same is asked of wives and children, servants, friends, kin, guests, ancestors, deities, king, and of keeping the body strong — and each time the answer is the same: no plea, by the doer or the beneficiary, holds back the wardens of hell.

The better person, and the honest way

§16–26“Who is better, Dhanañjāni: one who acts unjustly for their parents’ sake, or one who acts justly?” “The one who acts justly — for principled conduct is better than unprincipled conduct.” “And there are livelihoods both profitable and legitimate, by which one can provide for one’s parents, avoid bad deeds, and practise goodness.” So too for every other duty in turn. Having approved and agreed with Sāriputta, Dhanañjāni rose and left.

The deathbed, and the climb

§27–31Some time later Dhanañjāni fell gravely ill — the winds drilling his head like a spike, gripping it like a tightened strap, slicing his belly like a butcher’s cleaver, his whole body scorched as over glowing coals. He sent for Sāriputta, who came and asked which was better: hell or the animal realm? — the animal realm; and so on, up through the ghost realm, human life, and all six heavens of the sense-world. “And which is better: the gods who control others’ creations, or the realm of Divinity?” “Worthy Sāriputta speaks of the realm of Divinity! Worthy Sāriputta speaks of the realm of Divinity!”

The path to Divinity

§32–35Sāriputta thought, “These brahmins are devoted to Divinity; let me teach him the path to its company.” “A mendicant meditates spreading a heart full of love to one direction, and a second, and a third, and a fourth — above, below, across, everywhere — abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will. This is a path to company with Divinity.” So too with compassion, with rejoicing, and with equanimity.

“While there was still more to be done”

§36–38Dhanañjāni asked that his homage be carried to the Buddha. Then Sāriputta, “having established Dhanañjāni in the inferior realm of Divinity, got up and left while there was still more to be done.” Soon after, Dhanañjāni passed away and was reborn in the realm of Divinity. The Buddha told the monks of this, and asked Sāriputta why he had stopped there. “Sir, I thought: these brahmins are devoted to Divinity; let me teach him the path to its company.” “And, Sāriputta, the brahmin Dhanañjāni has passed away and been reborn in the realm of Divinity.”

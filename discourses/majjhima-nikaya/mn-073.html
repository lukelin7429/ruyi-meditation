Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 73

The Longer Discourse with Vacchagotta

Mahāvacchagottasutta

Setting
Near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground
Speakers
The Buddha and the wanderer Vacchagotta, who at last sets metaphysics aside and asks how to live — and goes forth
Form
The climax of the trilogy — a concise teaching on the skillful and unskillful, a survey of the whole community’s attainments, an ordination, and a path of practice ending in awakening.
Length
~14 minutes to read
Northern parallel
SĀ 964 (Saṁyukta-āgama 964 — the Chinese tells the trilogy as one discourse)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — accessible and warm; the doctrinal core is the completeness of the fourfold assembly and the pairing of serenity with discernment.

Why this discourse, seventy-third

Two discourses of hard questions have passed, and Vacchagotta is finally done with them. “For a long time I have had discussions with the worthy Gotama,” he says — and then asks, at last, the question that leads somewhere: not “is the cosmos eternal?” but “please teach me what is skillful and what is unskillful.” From that turn the whole discourse flows: a teaching he can live by, the discovery that countless others have walked it to the end, his ordination, and his own awakening.

This is the third and final episode of the Vacchagotta trilogy (MN 71, 72, 73), and it completes a quiet character study. We have watched curiosity (MN 71) deepen into faith through bafflement and the fire simile (MN 72); now faith ripens into the resolve to practise. The change is marked by the question itself. Speculation asks what is true in the abstract; the path asks what is wholesome in action. Vacchagotta has stopped trying to place the Buddha on a map of opinions and started asking how to become free.

The Buddha answers briefly — greed, hate, and delusion are unskillful; their opposites skillful; and craving cut at the root is arahantship. But Vacchagotta wants to know something more: is this a teaching that works, and for whom? So he asks, category by category, whether monks, nuns, and laypeople of every kind actually reach its fruits. The answer — “not one hundred… but many more than that” — convinces him that the path is whole, like the Ganges leaning toward the sea. He asks to go forth, offers four years’ probation where four months would do, and not long after, in solitude, becomes one of the perfected.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Greed, hate, and delusion (with the ten unwholesome courses of action) are unskillful, and their opposites skillful; craving cut at the root is awakening — and because the whole community, from monks to sensual-enjoying laywomen, has reached the fruits of this path, it is complete, and inclines toward extinguishment like the Ganges toward the sea; convinced, Vacchagotta goes forth and, developing serenity and discernment, becomes an arahant.

The turn from speculation to practice

Everything hinges on Vacchagotta’s opening request. After two discourses of metaphysical questions that led only to thickets, he asks for something he can use: “teach me in brief what is skillful and what is unskillful.” This is the pivot of the whole trilogy. The questions of MN 71–72 were about the world — its extent, its eternity, the fate of a realized one. This question is about conduct — how to act, here and now. The Buddha’s patient refusal to be drawn into speculation has finally borne fruit: the seeker has redirected his energy from theory to transformation.

The skillful and unskillful, at three depths

The Buddha gives the teaching at three levels, ascending from root to fruit:

  • The three roots. Greed is unskillful, contentment skillful; hate unskillful, love skillful; delusion unskillful, understanding skillful. These are the springs from which all action flows.
  • The ten courses of action. The roots express themselves in deeds: killing, stealing, sexual misconduct; false, divisive, harsh, and nonsensical speech; covetousness, ill will, and wrong view — unskillful; and refraining from each, together with contentment, kindness, and right view — skillful.
  • The cutting of craving. Beyond avoiding the unskillful lies the goal: “when a mendicant has given up craving so it is cut off at the root, made like a palm stump… that mendicant is perfected.”

It is a complete miniature of the path — intention, action, and liberation — given in the space of a few sentences to a man who had spent years asking the wrong questions.

Does it actually work? The whole assembly

Vacchagotta’s next move is shrewd and practical. A teaching is not proved by its founder alone; he wants to know whether others reach its fruits. So he asks, setting aside the Buddha each time, whether there is “even a single” disciple of each kind who has succeeded — and works through the entire fourfold assembly:

DiscipleAttainment asked about
Monksarahants — the ending of defilements in this life
Nunsarahants — the ending of defilements in this life
Celibate laymen (white-clothed)non-returners — the five lower fetters ended
Laymen enjoying pleasuresstream-enterers — beyond doubt, self-assured in the teaching
Celibate laywomen (white-clothed)non-returners — the five lower fetters ended
Laywomen enjoying pleasuresstream-enterers — beyond doubt, self-assured in the teaching

To every question the answer is the same: “not one hundred… or five hundred, but many more than that.” Two things stand out. First, the parity: nuns and laywomen are named with exactly the same attainments as monks and laymen — the path is open to all. Second, the realism: even laypeople “enjoying sensual pleasures” reach stream-entry, and celibate laypeople reach non-return; one need not be a monastic to make genuine progress, though the highest fruit of arahantship is, in this discourse, named only of the renunciants.

Why a complete path needs a whole community

Vacchagotta draws the inference explicitly: if only the Buddha had succeeded, “this spiritual path would be incomplete in that respect”; because every class of follower has succeeded, “this spiritual path is complete.” It is a striking criterion. A teaching’s validity is shown not by the brilliance of its teacher but by the breadth of those it carries to freedom — men and women, monastic and lay. Then comes his own simile, his first sign of full conviction: as the Ganges “slants, slopes, and inclines towards the ocean, and keeps pressing into it,” so the Buddha’s whole assembly “slants, slopes, and inclines towards extinguishment.” A community is known by what it leans toward.

The four-year offer

Convinced, Vacchagotta goes for refuge and asks to be ordained. Here a small rule appears: one formerly ordained in another sect must spend four months on probation before full ordination. Vacchagotta’s answer measures the distance he has travelled since MN 71: “If four months are required, I’ll spend four years.” The eager over-offer is the mark of a man no longer testing the teaching but throwing himself into it. (The Buddha adds, “I recognize individual differences in this matter” — the rule is not mechanical.) He is ordained.

Serenity and discernment — and what they open

A fortnight later Vacchagotta returns: “I’ve reached as far as the knowledge of a trainee can go. Teach me further.” The Buddha’s instruction is famously economical: “develop two things — serenity and discernment” (samatha and vipassanā). These two, developed together, “lead to the penetration of many elements,” and from them every higher attainment comes “within range”: the psychic powers, the divine ear, the knowledge of others’ minds, the recollection of past lives, the divine eye, and — the only one that matters for freedom — “the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom… due to the ending of defilements.” The whole edifice of meditative knowledge rests on two pillars: a mind made calm, and a mind made clear.

The arahant’s message

The ending is among the tenderest in the canon. Vacchagotta withdraws — “alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute” — and “soon” reaches the goal: “Rebirth is ended… there is nothing further for this place.” He becomes an arahant. Then, seeing monks on their way to the Buddha, he sends a message: “In my name, bow at the Buddha’s feet and say: ‘I have served the Blessed One! I have served the Holy One!’” — the grateful cry of a former sceptic who has arrived. The Buddha’s reply closes the trilogy with quiet warmth: “I had already encompassed his mind and known that he has the three knowledges… and the deities told me too.” The teacher’s long patience with a difficult questioner is repaid in full; the man who once wanted only to argue is now, himself, free.

Key terms

kusala / akusala — “skillful / unskillful.” The category Vacchagotta finally asks about: not what is metaphysically true, but what is wholesome to do. The whole path turns on this distinction.
the three roots — greed, hate, and delusion (lobha, dosa, moha), the springs of all unskillful action; their opposites — contentment, love, and understanding — the springs of the skillful.
dasa kammapatha — the ten courses of action: three of body, four of speech, three of mind. The roots made concrete in deeds; avoiding them, the skillful path of action.
anāgāmī / sotāpanna — non-returner and stream-enterer. Named here of laypeople: the celibate reach non-return (five lower fetters gone), and even those enjoying pleasures reach stream-entry (beyond doubt, self-assured).
samatha & vipassanā — serenity and discernment, the two things Vacchagotta is told to develop. Together they “penetrate many elements” and bring every higher knowledge within range.
parisā — the fourfold assembly: monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen. The discourse’s test of a complete path is that all four reach its fruits — the community leaning toward Nibbāna like the Ganges to the sea.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The teaching on the skillful and unskillful, the refuge and ordination, and the closing message are given in full; the six categories of disciple, and the long lists of higher knowledges, are given in their pattern and condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The right question, at last

§1–3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground. Then the wanderer Vacchagotta went up to the Buddha, exchanged greetings, sat down to one side, and said: “For a long time I have had discussions with the worthy Gotama. Please teach me in brief what is skillful and what is unskillful.” “Vaccha, I can teach you what is skillful and unskillful in brief or in detail. Still, let me do so in brief. Listen and apply your mind well.”

The skillful and the unskillful

§4–6“Greed is unskillful, contentment is skillful. Hate is unskillful, love is skillful. Delusion is unskillful, understanding is skillful. So there are these three unskillful things and three that are skillful. Killing living creatures, stealing, and sexual misconduct; speech that’s false, backbiting, harsh, or nonsensical; covetousness, ill will, and wrong view: these things are unskillful. Refraining from them — with contentment, kind-heartedness, and right view: these things are skillful. And when a mendicant has given up craving so it is cut off at the root, made like a palm stump, unable to arise in the future, that mendicant is perfected — they’ve ended the defilements, done what had to be done, and are rightly freed through enlightenment.”

Does the path bear fruit for others?

§7–8“Leaving aside the worthy Gotama, is there even a single monk disciple who has realized the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom in this very life?” “There are not just one hundred such monks, Vaccha, or two, three, four, or five hundred, but many more than that.” “And among the nuns?” “Not just one hundred, Vaccha… but many more than that.”

Vacchagotta works through the whole fourfold assembly, and the answer is always “many more than that.” Besides the monks and nuns who are arahants, there are celibate white-clothed laymen who, with the five lower fetters ended, are non-returners; laymen enjoying sensual pleasures who have gone beyond doubt and live self-assured in the teaching (stream-enterers); and likewise celibate laywomen who are non-returners, and laywomen enjoying pleasures who are stream-enterers. Of each kind there are “not one hundred… but many more than that.”

A complete path, like the Ganges

§13–14“If the worthy Gotama alone had succeeded in this teaching, and no monks, nuns, or laypeople, then this spiritual path would be incomplete in that respect. But because all of them — monks, nuns, celibate and sensual-enjoying laymen and laywomen alike — have succeeded in it, this spiritual path is complete. Just as the Ganges slants, slopes, and inclines towards the ocean, and keeps pressing into it, in the same way the worthy Gotama’s assembly — with both laypeople and renunciates — slants, slopes, and inclines towards extinguishment.”

The refuge and the four-year offer

§15–16“Excellent, worthy Gotama! I go for refuge to the worthy Gotama, to the teaching, and to the mendicant Saṅgha. May I receive the going forth, the ordination in the worthy Gotama’s presence?” “Vaccha, one formerly ordained in another sect must spend four months on probation; when four months have passed, if the mendicants are satisfied, they give the ordination. Yet I recognize individual differences in this matter.” “Sir, if four months are required, I’ll spend four years on probation. When four years have passed, if the mendicants are satisfied, let them ordain me.” And the wanderer Vacchagotta received the going forth in the Buddha’s presence.

Develop serenity and discernment

§17–24A fortnight after his ordination, Venerable Vacchagotta went to the Buddha and said, “Sir, I’ve reached as far as possible with the knowledge and understanding of a trainee. Please teach me further.” “Well then, Vaccha, develop two things further: serenity and discernment. When you have developed these two, they’ll lead to the penetration of many elements.” The Buddha then names what comes within range: the many kinds of psychic power; the divine ear, hearing sounds human and heavenly; the knowledge of others’ minds; the recollection of many past lives, with features and details; the divine eye, seeing beings pass away and be reborn according to their deeds; and the realization of the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom, through the ending of defilements.

Vacchagotta becomes one of the perfected

§25–26Then Venerable Vacchagotta approved what the Buddha said, rose, bowed, and respectfully circled him before leaving. And living alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute, he soon realized the supreme end of the spiritual path in this very life. He understood: “Rebirth is ended; the spiritual journey has been completed; what had to be done has been done; there is nothing further for this place.” And Venerable Vacchagotta became one of the perfected.

“I have served the Blessed One!”

§27–28Now at that time several mendicants were going to see the Buddha. Vacchagotta saw them in the distance and said, “Venerables, where are you going?” “To see the Buddha.” “Then, in my name, bow with your heads at the Buddha’s feet and say: ‘Sir, the mendicant Vacchagotta bows at your feet and says, “I have served the Blessed One! I have served the Holy One!”’” They delivered the message. The Buddha said, “I had already encompassed Vacchagotta’s mind and understood that he has the three knowledges, and is very mighty and powerful. And deities also told me about this.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said.

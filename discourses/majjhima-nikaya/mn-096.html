Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 96

With Esukārī

Esukārīsutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speakers
The Buddha and the brahmin Esukārī
Form
A point-by-point reply to two caste doctrines — who may serve whom, and what wealth each class is owed.
Length
~13 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A counterpart is preserved in the Chinese Madhyama Āgama (MĀ 150)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — a clear, image-rich refutation of caste.

Why this discourse, ninety-sixth

A brahmin lays out the social order as his tradition fixes it: a ladder of who may serve whom, and a schedule of what kind of wealth each of the four classes is entitled to. The Buddha does not quarrel over the rungs. He asks one disarming question — who gave the brahmins the authority to assign these to everyone? — and then replaces the whole scheme with a single human measure: not birth, but whether a person grows better or worse.

Esukārī comes with two brahmin doctrines, each in four parts. The first is a code of service: a brahmin may be served by all four classes, an aristocrat by three, a peasant by two, a menial only by a menial. The second is a code of wealth: each class has its proper livelihood — alms for the brahmin, the bow for the aristocrat, farming for the peasant, the scythe for the menial — and to step outside it is theft. Together they form a closed world in which a person’s worth, work, and even who may wait on them are decided at birth.

The Buddha dismantles both with the same two moves. First, the doctrines have no warrant: “Did the whole world authorize the brahmins to prescribe this?” — No. They are like a butcher forcing meat on a beggar and demanding payment. Second, he substitutes an ethical criterion for the social one. You should serve whoever makes you grow — in faith, virtue, learning, generosity, wisdom; and a person’s real wealth is not a birth-assigned trade but “the noble, transcendent teaching,” open to anyone of any class who will walk it.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

No one authorized the brahmins to fix who may serve whom or what each class may own; the only worthwhile measure is whether serving someone makes you grow in virtue, and a person’s true wealth is not a birth-assigned trade but the noble teaching — for caste, like a fire named after its fuel, is only a label, while the flame burns the same in all.

“Who authorized this?”

The Buddha’s first response to each doctrine is identical and devastating: “Did the whole world authorize the brahmins to prescribe this?” Esukārī must admit: no. The brahmins have simply declared a hierarchy and assigned everyone a place in it, without the consent of those they assign. The image is sharp — “as if they were to force a chop on a poor, penniless person, telling them, Eat this meat and pay for it.” A rule imposed on people who never agreed to it, and which they are then made to bear the cost of, has no real authority — only the weight of those strong enough to impose it. Before any argument about the content of caste, the Buddha questions its legitimacy.

The measure of service: who makes you grow

Against the caste-ladder of service, the Buddha sets a single ethical test. “I don’t say you should serve everyone, nor that you should serve no one. I say: do not serve someone if serving them makes you worse; do serve someone if serving them makes you better.” And “better” is then defined precisely: growth in faith, ethics, learning, generosity, and wisdom. Ask any of the four classes which they would choose — service that improves them or service that degrades them — and all answer the same. The question of whom to serve is removed from the register of rank entirely and placed in the register of moral growth. Birth, beauty, and wealth, the Buddha adds, make a person neither better nor worse — the well-born and the lowly alike may keep or break the precepts; it is conduct, not class, that decides.

True wealth: the noble teaching

Against the caste-schedule of wealth, the Buddha makes a striking substitution: “I declare that a person’s own wealth is the noble, transcendent teaching” (ariya lokuttara dhamma). The brahmin scheme ties each person’s livelihood, and so their value, to the family they happened to be born into. The Buddha grants that people are labelled by birth — born among aristocrats you are called an aristocrat, among menials a menial — but insists the label is merely that. Their true riches are the Dhamma, which any of them can possess.

The fire named after its fuel

Here comes the discourse’s signature image. Caste, the Buddha says, works like fire:

A fire is named only for what it burns. “A fire that burns dependent on logs is reckoned a log fire; one that burns on twigs, a twig fire; on grass, a grass fire; on cow-dung, a cow-dung fire.” The name comes from the fuel — but the fire itself, its flame and heat, is one and the same.

“Log fire” and “grass fire” are real distinctions of fuel, not of fire. So with “brahmin” and “menial”: they name the family one was born into, nothing about the worth of the person. The fire of human capacity — for virtue, for love, for awakening — burns identically in all.

Three proofs that the flame is one

The Buddha drives the point home with three tests, and Esukārī concedes each:

  • Loving-kindness — can only a brahmin “develop a heart of love, free of enmity”? No; all four classes equally can.
  • Washing clean — can only a brahmin take bathing cleanser to the river and rinse off dirt? No; all four can.
  • Kindling fire — if high-born men light a fire with fine teak and sandalwood, and low-born men with wood from a dog’s trough or a dustbin, does only the “noble” fire have flame and radiance? No — “all fire has flames, color, and radiance, and is usable as fire.”

The third test turns the fire-image back on the doctrine: even fire kindled from the most despised wood is fully, radiantly fire. Whatever the social origin — the “wood” — a person who goes forth and lives by the Realized One’s training “succeeds in the system of the skillful teaching,” the flame undimmed. Esukārī, who came to defend the hierarchy, ends by going for refuge for life.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats the four classes in turn. The two brahmin doctrines, the “forced meat” image, the fire-named-after-fuel simile, and the three tests are given closely. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The four kinds of service

§1–3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove. The brahmin Esukārī came, exchanged greetings, and said: “Worthy Gotama, the brahmins prescribe four kinds of service. A brahmin may be served by all four classes; an aristocrat by aristocrat, peasant, and menial; a peasant by peasant and menial; a menial only by a menial — for who else would serve a menial? What do you say to this?”

“Who authorized it?”

§4–5“But brahmin, did the whole world authorize the brahmins to prescribe these four kinds of service?” “No, worthy Gotama.” “It is as if they were to force a chop on a poor, penniless person, saying, ‘Eat this meat and pay for it.’ Just so, the brahmins prescribe this without anyone’s consent. Brahmin, I do not say you should serve everyone, nor that you should serve no one. I say: do not serve someone if serving them makes you worse, not better; do serve someone if serving them makes you better, not worse.”

Birth does not make you better or worse

§6–9“Ask any class — aristocrat, brahmin, peasant, menial — ‘whom should you serve, one in whose service you get worse, or better?’ and each, answering rightly, says: ‘one in whose service I get better.’ Brahmin, I do not say that eminent family, beauty, or wealth makes a person better or worse. For some of eminent family kill, steal, and lie, are covetous and of wrong view; others of eminent family do the opposite. The same holds for the beautiful and the wealthy. So I say: serve someone if serving them makes you grow in faith, ethics, learning, generosity, and wisdom; do not, if it does not.”

The four kinds of wealth, and the fire

§10–12Then Esukārī said: “The brahmins also prescribe four kinds of wealth: for the brahmin, living on alms; for the aristocrat, the bow and quiver; for the peasant, farming and herding; for the menial, the scythe and flail. To scorn one’s own assigned wealth is to fail in one’s duty, like a guard who steals. What do you say?” “Did the whole world authorize this?” “No, worthy Gotama.” “Again it is forced meat. I declare that a person’s own true wealth is the noble, transcendent teaching. People are merely reckoned by the family they are born into — born among aristocrats, called an aristocrat; among brahmins, a brahmin; and so on. It is like fire, reckoned by what it burns: a log fire, a twig fire, a grass fire, a cow-dung fire. The name comes from the fuel — but a person’s true wealth is the teaching.”

The flame is one in every class

§13–16“Suppose someone of any class — aristocrat, brahmin, peasant, or menial — goes forth and, relying on the Realized One’s training, refrains from killing, stealing, and the rest, and holds right view: they succeed in the system of the skillful teaching. Tell me, brahmin: can only a brahmin develop a heart of love free of enmity, and not the others?” “No — all four classes can.” “Can only a brahmin take cleanser to the river and wash off dirt?” “No — all four can.” “And if high-born men kindle a fire with fine teak and sandalwood, and low-born men with wood from a dog’s trough or a dustbin — does only the high-born fire have flame and radiance?” “No, worthy Gotama. All fire has flame, color, and radiance, and is usable as fire.” “Just so, anyone of any class who goes forth and lives by the training succeeds in the skillful teaching.”

Refuge

§17“Excellent, worthy Gotama! Excellent! From this day forth, may the worthy Gotama remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.”

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 85

With Prince Bodhi

Bodhirājakumārasutta

Setting
The land of the Bhaggas at Crocodile’s Bellow, in Bhesakaḷā’s Wood; the prince’s new “Pink Lotus” longhouse
Speakers
The Buddha and Prince Bodhi, with his attendant Sañjikāputta
Form
A claim met by autobiography — the prince says happiness comes only through pain, and the Buddha answers with the story of his own awakening, then names the five factors that bring it within reach.
Length
~22 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No single Āgama counterpart; the awakening narrative parallels MN 26 and the Vinaya accounts
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — long, but its frame and its closing teaching are its fresh contribution.

Why this discourse, eighty-fifth

“Pleasure is not gained through pleasure,” Prince Bodhi tells the Buddha; “pleasure is gained through pain.” It is the ascetic’s creed: the goal must be bought with suffering. The Buddha answers not with a counter-slogan but with the whole story of his own search — how he once believed exactly that, tortured his body almost to death in the name of it, and found awakening only when he let it go. The discourse is the Buddha’s spiritual autobiography, wrapped around a single correction: the path to the highest happiness is not pain, but a deeper, blameless joy.

The frame is its own small teaching. Prince Bodhi has built a splendid new longhouse, “Pink Lotus,” never yet used, and invites the Buddha to inaugurate it — spreading white cloth across the stairs for the Buddha to walk upon. Three times he asks the Buddha to ascend on the cloth; three times the Buddha is silent, until Ānanda tells the prince to fold it away: “The Realized One will not step on white cloth; he has sympathy for future generations.” A teacher who refuses a small luxury offered in devotion, lest he set a precedent of indulgence for the monks who will come after — it is the same care for the future that runs through the whole discourse.

Then comes the prince’s thesis, and the Buddha’s long reply: the going-forth, the two teachers whose attainments fell short, the three fire-stick similes, the grim austerities that brought him to the edge of death and no further, the saving memory of a childhood meditation, and the awakening itself — the same great narrative told in MN 26. Here it serves a precise point: asceticism failed; the prince’s creed of pain was the very thing the Buddha had to abandon. The discourse ends by turning to the listener’s own prospects: the five factors that, with the Buddha as teacher, can bring awakening in seven years — or in a single day.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The belief that the highest happiness must be won through pain is false — the Buddha’s own brutal austerities brought him no awakening at all, which came instead through the blameless bliss of meditation; and a disciple who has five factors — faith, health, honesty, energy, and wisdom — can reach the goal under the Buddha’s guidance with astonishing speed.

The white cloth, and care for the future

The opening gesture is easy to pass over but quietly important. Prince Bodhi, with every honour, spreads white cloth on the stairs of his new hall for the Buddha to tread — a mark of the highest reverence. The Buddha refuses, silently, three times, and Ānanda explains: he “will not step on white cloth… he has sympathy for future generations.” The Buddha declines a devotion freely and lovingly offered — not because it is wrong in itself, but because of what it would establish. If the Teacher walks on fine cloth, the monks who come after will expect to, and a precedent of luxury will have entered the Saṅgha. His refusal is an act of compassion aimed centuries ahead, at people not yet born. It sets the key for the whole discourse, which is, throughout, about what truly leads to the goal — and luxury, like its opposite, austerity, is not it.

“Pleasure through pain” — and why it is wrong

Prince Bodhi’s thesis — na sukhena sukhaṁ, dukkhena kho sukhaṁ adhigantabbaṁ, “happiness is not won through happiness, but through pain” — was the common wisdom of the ascetic culture: the spiritual prize is bought with self-mortification, and the more you suffer, the closer you draw. The Buddha’s reply is devastating precisely because it is autobiographical. “I too once thought that.” And then he shows where it led him: to austerities so extreme that deities thought he had died, to a body so wasted that his spine could be felt through his belly, his scalp shrivelled, his hair rotted and fell out — and to the flat conclusion, “by this severe, gruelling work I have not achieved any superhuman distinction. Could there be another path?” The creed of pain was not a shortcut to awakening; it was a dead end he had to walk all the way to before he could turn back. His whole story is the refutation of the prince’s opening line.

The story of the search (in brief)

The autobiography that follows is the great quest narrative, told also in MN 26 and MN 36. In brief: the young Gotama goes forth despite his weeping parents; masters the formless attainments under Āḷāra Kālāma (the dimension of nothingness) and Uddaka Rāmaputta (neither-perception-nor-non-perception), but finds each leads “only as far as” that rebirth, not to Nibbāna; settles at Uruvelā; and there is struck by three similes of the fire-sticks — you cannot kindle fire from a wet green log in water, nor from a green log on dry land, but only from a dry log on dry land. So too, only one withdrawn from sensuality in both body and mind can kindle the fire of awakening. Then the austerities: the “breathless” meditation with its winds tearing at his ears, head, and belly; the near-fatal fasting. All of it fails.

The turning point: a child’s blameless joy

The hinge of the whole story — and the direct answer to the prince — is a memory. Brought to the limit of self-torture and still no nearer the goal, the Bodhisatta recalls a day in childhood when, sitting in the cool shade of a rose-apple tree, he had naturally entered the first absorption, “the rapture and bliss born of seclusion.” And the thought arises: “That is the path to awakening.” He examines the fear behind his asceticism: “Why am I afraid of that pleasure? It has nothing to do with sensual pleasures or unskillful qualities.” There is a happiness that is wholesome, blameless, born not of indulgence but of a quiet, collected mind — and this, not pain, is the road. He eats solid food again (and the five companions desert him in disgust), regains his strength, and through the four absorptions attains the three knowledges and full awakening. The prince said happiness comes through pain; the Buddha’s life says the opposite — awakening came when he stopped torturing himself and trusted a pure, meditative joy.

The five factors that bring it within reach

Prince Bodhi’s closing question is practical: with the Buddha as teacher, how long until a monk reaches the goal? The Buddha answers with a simile from the prince’s own world. Bodhi is skilled with the elephant-goad; could a pupil learn that art from him if the pupil were faithless, sickly, dishonest, lazy, or stupid? “Not even with one such fault, let alone all five.” And if the pupil had faith, health, honesty, energy, and wit? “Even with one, let alone all five.” Just so, says the Buddha, there are five factors that support striving (padhāniyaṅga):

FactorWhat it is
Faithconfidence in the Realized One’s awakening
Healthrarely ill, with good digestion — a body “fit for meditation”
Honestyno deceit; revealing oneself truly to teacher and companions
Energystaunch, unflagging effort to abandon the unskilful and grow the skilful
Wisdomthe penetrative insight into arising and passing away

With these five and the Buddha as guide, a disciple “could realize the supreme goal in seven years — or six, or one; in seven months, or one; or even… instructed at dusk, achieve distinction by dawn; instructed at dawn, achieve it by dusk.” The timeframe collapses from years to a single night. The point is liberating and sobering at once: awakening is not a matter of how long one suffers, but of bringing these five honest, ordinary capacities to the practice. The path is fast for the well-equipped — and the equipment is character, not endurance of pain.

The triple refuge

The discourse ends with a charming exchange. When the prince marvels at the teaching, his attendant Sañjikāputta needles him: “Yet you do not go for refuge to the Buddha.” Bodhi corrects him: he has gone for refuge not once but three times — first while still in his mother’s womb, when his pregnant mother went for refuge on his behalf; again as an infant, carried on his nursemaid’s hip; and now, a third time, as a grown man choosing it for himself. It is a small portrait of how faith can be planted before one can speak, carried through childhood, and finally made one’s own — the seed handed down, then consciously claimed. A discourse that began with the Buddha’s care for future generations closes with one such inheritor, taking up for himself the refuge his family had kept for him.

Key terms

“pleasure through pain” — Prince Bodhi’s thesis (dukkhena sukhaṁ adhigantabbaṁ), the ascetic creed that the highest happiness must be bought with suffering. The Buddha’s whole story refutes it.
the three fire-sticks — the similes of the wet log in water, the green log on dry land, and the dry log on dry land. Only one withdrawn from sensuality in both body and mind can kindle awakening.
blameless bliss — the joy of the first absorption, “born of seclusion,” recalled from childhood: “Why am I afraid of that pleasure? It has nothing to do with sensual pleasures or unskillful qualities.” That is the path.
padhāniyaṅga — the five “factors that support striving”: faith, health, honesty, energy, and wisdom. With these and the Buddha as teacher, the goal comes swiftly.
“dusk to dawn” — the astonishing upper limit: a disciple so equipped, “instructed at dusk, could achieve distinction by dawn.” Awakening is not a matter of how long one suffers but of how one is equipped.
care for future generations — why the Buddha refuses to tread the white cloth: not to set a precedent of luxury for the Saṅgha to come. A compassion aimed at people not yet born.

The text

The full discourse, substantially abridged — the long awakening narrative (shared with MN 26 and MN 36) is summarized here, with the distinctive frame and the closing teaching given in full. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The white cloth

§1–8So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the land of the Bhaggas at Crocodile’s Bellow, in Bhesakaḷā’s Wood. Prince Bodhi, having built a new longhouse called Pink Lotus, invited the Buddha for a meal and spread white cloth down the staircase. Three times he begged, “Let the Blessed One ascend on the cloth, for my lasting welfare!” Three times the Buddha was silent. Then he glanced at Ānanda, who said, “Fold up the cloth, prince. The Buddha will not step on white cloth; the Realized One has sympathy for future generations.” So the cloth was folded, and the Buddha went up and was served.

The prince’s thesis

§9–10After the meal, Prince Bodhi said, “Sir, this is what I think: ‘Pleasure is not gained through pleasure; pleasure is gained through pain.’” “Prince, before my awakening I too thought, ‘Pleasure is not gained through pleasure; pleasure is gained through pain.’”

The Buddha then tells the full story of his quest — the same narrative as MN 26. He goes forth despite his weeping parents; masters the formless attainments under Āḷāra Kālāma and Uddaka Rāmaputta but finds them leading “only as far as” those rebirths, not to awakening; and settles at Uruvelā. There the three fire-stick similes strike him: as fire cannot be kindled from a wet or green log, only from a dry one, so only one withdrawn from sensuality in both body and mind can kindle awakening.

The austerities, and their failure

§20–30“Then I practised the breathless meditation, until winds tore at my ears, my head, my belly, and a fierce burning gripped me; deities thought I had died. I fasted until my limbs were like withered reeds, my spine a string of beads, my belly’s skin clinging to my backbone, my hair rotting and falling out. Yet I thought: ‘No ascetic, past or present, has suffered more than this — yet I have gained no distinction worthy of the noble ones by this gruelling work. Could there be another path to awakening?’”

The blameless joy — the real path

§31–33“Then I recalled sitting as a child in the cool shade of a rose-apple tree, entering the first absorption, the rapture and bliss born of seclusion — and I thought, ‘That is the path to awakening!’ ‘Why am I afraid of that pleasure, which has nothing to do with sensual pleasures or unskillful qualities?’ I am not afraid of it. But I could not reach it with a body so wasted. So I ate solid food again.” The group of five mendicants, disgusted, deserted him — “Gotama has become indulgent.” Then through the four absorptions he attained, in the three watches of the night, the three knowledges: the recollection of past lives, the divine eye, and the ending of the defilements. “Rebirth is ended.” He hesitated to teach so deep a truth; Brahmā Sahampati entreated him; and seeing beings “with little dust in their eyes,” like lotuses at different heights in a pond, he resolved to teach — first the five mendicants, whom he persuaded and led to awakening.

How long to the goal? The five factors

§55–59“Sir,” asked Prince Bodhi, “with the Buddha as trainer, how long until a monk reaches the goal?” “Prince, you are skilled with the elephant-goad. Could a pupil learn that art from you if he were faithless, sickly, dishonest, lazy, or stupid?” “Not even with one such fault, sir, let alone all five.” “And if he had faith, health, honesty, energy, and wit?” “Even with one, let alone all five.” “Just so, prince, there are five factors that support striving: faith in the Realized One’s awakening; good health and digestion; honesty, free of deceit; roused, unflagging energy; and the wisdom of arising and passing away. A monk with these five, with the Realized One as trainer, could reach the supreme goal in seven years — or six, or one; in seven months, or one; or even, instructed at dusk, achieve distinction by dawn; instructed at dawn, achieve it by dusk.”

The triple refuge

§60–61“Oh, the Buddha! How well explained — that one instructed at dusk might achieve distinction by dawn!” said the prince. His attendant Sañjikāputta said, “Yet the worthy Bodhi does not go for refuge.” “Do not say that! I have heard from my mother that while I was in her womb she went for refuge on my behalf; and as an infant on my nursemaid’s hip, she went for refuge for me; and now, a third time, I go for refuge to the Buddha, to the teaching, and to the Saṅgha. From this day forth, may the Buddha remember me as a lay follower gone for refuge for life.”

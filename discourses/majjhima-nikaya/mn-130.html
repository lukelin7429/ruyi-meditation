Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 130

Messengers of the Gods

Devadūtasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, addressed to the mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha, narrating a vision of King Yama and the newly dead
Form
A courtroom interrogation before the lord of the dead, followed by an extended tour of hell
Length
~20 minutes to read
Northern parallel
Unusually well attested — at least six separate Chinese parallels survive, more than almost any other discourse in this collection
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — narratively direct, but historically one of the most consequential suttas in Buddhist literature

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-thirtieth

According to the Sri Lankan chronicles, this exact discourse was used by the elder Mahinda to convert an entire island, and by the elder Mahādeva to convert an entire region of India — each time, the chronicle says, resulting in mass conversions on the spot. Scholars studying the comparative history of Indian religion have identified this as likely the earliest complete account of the “hell complex” — the whole cluster of ideas about many named hells, kamma-determined punishment, and hell-wardens — shared across later Buddhist, Hindu, and Jain literature. Several of the specific hells named here reappear, centuries later, as some of the canonical 28 hells of the Hindu Purāṇas. Few suttas in the Majjhima can claim this kind of direct historical footprint.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Everyone alive encounters five unmistakable warnings — a newborn, an elderly person, a sick person, a punished criminal, a corpse — each one silently saying “this too could be you, so act well while you can”; those who ignore all five are handed, entirely on their own account and by no one else’s doing, to a hell whose torments this discourse describes in more sustained detail than any other in the Majjhima Nikāya.

The divine eye, explained by a simile

The discourse opens by describing the power behind everything that follows: “Suppose there were two houses with doors. A person with clear eyes standing in between them would see humans entering and leaving.” In just the same way, the Buddha says, his clairvoyance lets him watch beings pass away and be reborn, “inferior and superior, beautiful and ugly,” and understand exactly how their deeds determined where they went. What follows is presented as something seen, not imagined.

Presented to King Yama

Hell’s wardens bring a newly dead person before King Yama — the ancient lord of the dead, a figure shared across Vedic, Buddhist, and Jain cosmology — accusing them of failing to respect their parents, ascetics, or elders. Yama does not simply sentence them. He interrogates them, one by one, about five “messengers of the gods” (devadūta) that had appeared to them, unmistakably, during their own lifetime.

Five messengers, one refrain

MessengerWhat was seenThe unheeded thought
Firsta little baby collapsed in its own urine and feces“I, too, am liable to be born”
Seconda person of eighty, ninety, or a hundred, bent double, trembling, toothless, grey and blotchy“I, too, am liable to grow old”
Thirda person gravely sick, collapsed, being carried by others“I, too, am liable to become sick”
Fourtha criminal publicly and elaborately tortured by the king’s men“if wrongdoing is punished even in this life, what must happen in the next?”
Fiftha corpse, one to three days dead, bloated and festering“I, too, am liable to die”

Every time, Yama asks first whether the person “saw the messenger” in its deeper sense — and every time, they claim they saw nothing. Pressed further, they admit they did see the literal thing — the baby, the elder, the sick person, the criminal, the corpse — but that it never occurred to them, “being sensible and mature,” to draw the obvious conclusion for their own life. “I couldn’t, sir. I was negligent,” they say each time. And each time, Yama closes with the same unyielding words:

The verdict, five times repeated. “That bad deed wasn’t done by your mother, father, brother, or sister. It wasn’t done by friends and colleagues, by relatives and kin, by ascetics and brahmins, or by the deities. That bad deed was done by you alone, and you alone will experience the result.”

After the fifth round, Yama simply falls silent. His role was never to punish — only to make undeniable, through direct questioning, what the person had every opportunity to see clearly while alive and chose not to.

The torments, expanded

The hell-wardens take over from here, and the description of their work is the most extensive in the Majjhima Nikāya — opening with the same escalating sequence found in the previous discourse (crucifixion with red-hot stakes, hacking with axes and hatchets, the burning chariot, the mountain of coals, the red-hot copper pot) and the same verse describing the Great Hell’s iron walls and hundred-league heat, but continuing much further. Flames crash from wall to wall in every direction at once. Every so often, one of the four gates opens, and the tormented run for it with everything they have — skin, flesh, and sinews burning as they go, bones smoking — only to have the gate slam shut just before they reach it. This happens, gate after gate, an unimaginable number of times — until, once, someone finally makes it through.

What waits outside is worse. Immediately adjacent lies the Dung Hell, where needle-mouthed creatures bore through skin and flesh to devour the marrow; beyond that, the Hell of Burning Chaff; beyond that, the league-high Red Silk-Cotton Forest, its thorns sixteen inches long; beyond that, the Sword-Leaf Forest, where wind-blown leaves slice off hands, feet, ears, and nose; beyond that, the Acid River, sweeping the tormented helplessly up and downstream. Pulled out at last with a hook and asked, “What do you want?”, the answer — hunger, or thirst — is met with a red-hot copper ball forced down the throat, or molten copper poured in, burning a path through the entire body before they are simply thrown back into the Great Hell to begin again.

An ancient and widely shared vision

This relentless catalogue turns out to have real historical weight. Scholars tracing the concept of hell through Indian literature find only vague, scattered hints in the earliest Vedic texts — a “bottomless darkness,” an “endless abyss” — with nothing resembling a fully developed system. This discourse, by contrast, is a complete architecture: named hells, wardens, kamma-calibrated punishment, beings who cannot die until their bad deed is exhausted. Strikingly, several of the hells named here — the Needle-Mouth hell, the Red Silk-Cotton Forest, the Sword-Leaf Forest, the Acid River, the molten-metal drink — reappear, largely unchanged, among the 28 hells catalogued much later in the Hindu Purāṇas. This sutta may be the single earliest surviving full account of a vision of hell that Buddhism, Hinduism, and Jainism would go on to share and elaborate for the next two thousand years.

Even Yama longs for freedom

Amid all this, the discourse offers one unexpectedly tender moment. King Yama himself, having witnessed endless suffering pass before him, is said to think privately: “Oh, I hope I may be reborn as a human being! And that a Realized One — a fully awakened Buddha — arises in the world! And that I may pay homage to the Buddha, and hear his teaching!” Even the judge of the dead, in this vision, wants out.

“I say this because I have seen it myself”

Before closing, the Buddha makes an unusually direct epistemic claim: “I don’t say this because I’ve heard it from some other ascetic or brahmin. I only say it because I’ve known, seen, and realized it for myself.” The entire vision — Yama, the messengers, the hells — is presented not as inherited myth but as the direct report of the Buddha’s own clairvoyant perception, the same power the opening simile of the two houses was meant to establish.

The discourse closes in verse: those who ignore the gods’ messengers “sorrow for a long time when they go to that wretched place,” while “the good and true… never neglect the teaching of the Noble One” — seeing the danger in grasping, they are “freed with the ending of birth and death,” having “gone beyond all threats and perils, and risen above all suffering.”

Key terms

devadūta — “messenger of the gods”: an ordinary sight — birth, old age, sickness, punishment, death — that silently carries an urgent message most people never act on.
King Yama — the ancient lord of the dead, shared across Vedic, Buddhist, and Jain cosmology; here a questioner and witness rather than a punisher.
“you alone will experience the result” — Yama’s repeated closing verdict: kamma is entirely non-transferable, belonging to no one but the one who acted.
niraya — “hell”; here elaborated with named regions (the Great Hell, the Dung Hell, the Red Silk-Cotton Forest, the Sword-Leaf Forest, the Acid River) that scholars connect to the later 28-hell system of the Hindu Purāṇas.
dibbacakkhu — the “divine eye” or clairvoyance, introduced here with the simile of watching people pass between two houses, and the epistemic basis the Buddha claims for the entire vision.

The text

The discourse condensed but faithful to its content: the divine-eye simile in full; the interrogation given in full for the first messenger and summarized for the remaining four, which repeat the identical three-part structure; the torments given as a representative selection through the Great Hell, followed by the extended tour of the adjacent hells; and Yama’s reflection and the closing verses in full. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The divine eye, explained by a simile

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, and addressed the mendicants: “Suppose there were two houses with doors. A person with clear eyes standing in between them would see humans entering and leaving a house and wandering to and fro. In the same way, with clairvoyance that is purified and superhuman, I see sentient beings passing away and being reborn — inferior and superior, beautiful and ugly, in a good place or a bad place — and I understand how they pass on according to their deeds.

Presented to King Yama

§3The wardens of hell take them by the arms and present them to King Yama, saying: ‘Your Majesty, this person did not pay due respect to their mother and father, ascetics and brahmins, or honor the elders in the family. May Your Majesty punish them!’ King Yama pursues, presses, and grills them about the first messenger of the gods.

Five messengers, one refrain

§3–8‘My friend, did you not see the first messenger of the gods that appeared among human beings?’ ‘I saw nothing, sir.’ ‘My friend, did you not see among human beings a little baby collapsed in their own urine and feces?’ ‘I saw that, sir.’ ‘My friend, did it not occur to you — being sensible and mature — “I, too, am liable to be born. I’m not exempt from rebirth. I’d better do good by way of body, speech, and mind”?’ ‘I couldn’t, sir. I was negligent.’ ‘My friend, because you were negligent, you didn’t do good. They’ll definitely punish you to fit your negligence. That bad deed wasn’t done by your mother, father, brother, or sister… by ascetics and brahmins, or by the deities. That bad deed was done by you alone, and you alone will experience the result.’

Then King Yama grills them about the second messenger — an elderly woman or man, eighty, ninety, or a hundred, bent double, trembling, toothless, grey, wrinkled — asking whether it did not occur to them, ‘I, too, am liable to grow old.’ The same admission of negligence, the same verdict follows.

Then the third messenger — a person gravely sick, collapsed in their own urine and feces, being carried by others — and the thought that should have followed: ‘I, too, am liable to become sick.’ The same admission, the same verdict.

Then the fourth messenger — the sight of a criminal being subjected to whipping, mutilation, and the many named tortures of royal punishment — and the thought: ‘if someone who does bad deeds receives such punishment in this very life, what must happen to them in the next?’ The same admission, the same verdict.

Then the fifth messenger — a corpse, dead one, two, or three days, bloated, livid, and festering — and the thought: ‘I, too, am liable to die.’ The same admission, the same verdict. After grilling them about the fifth messenger of the gods, King Yama falls silent.

The torments, expanded

§10–19The wardens of hell punish them with the five-fold crucifixion, driving red-hot stakes through the hands, feet, and chest; they hack them with axes and hatchets; harness them to a burning chariot; make them climb a mountain of blazing coals; sear them in a red-hot copper pot — and they don’t die until that bad deed is eliminated. At last they are tossed into the Great Hell, “four its corners, four its doors… surrounded by an iron wall, of iron is its roof… the heat forever radiates a hundred leagues around.” There, flames surge from wall to wall — east to west, north to south, top to bottom.

After a very long period, one of the four gates of the Great Hell is opened. They run for it as fast as they can, their skin, flesh, and sinews burning, even their bones smoking — but just as they nearly reach it, the gate is slammed shut. This happens for the eastern gate, the western, the northern, the southern, over and over across immense stretches of time — until, at last, on one such opening, they make it out.

Immediately adjacent to the Great Hell is the vast Dung Hell, where needle-mouthed creatures bore through skin and flesh to devour the marrow. Beyond that, the Hell of Burning Chaff. Beyond that, the Red Silk-Cotton Forest, a league high, thorns sixteen inches long, which they are made to climb up and down. Beyond that, the Sword-Leaf Forest, where wind-blown leaves cut off hands, feet, ears, and nose. Beyond that, the Acid River, sweeping them helplessly up and down its length. Pulled out with a hook and asked, ‘My friend, what do you want?’, they answer ‘I’m hungry’ or ‘I’m thirsty’ — and the wardens force a red-hot copper ball or molten copper down their throat, burning a path through their entire body before they are tossed back into the Great Hell.

Even Yama longs for freedom

§28–29Once upon a time, King Yama thought: ‘Those who do such bad deeds in the world receive these many different punishments. Oh, I hope I may be reborn as a human being! And that a Realized One — a perfected one, a fully awakened Buddha — arises in the world! And that I may pay homage to the Buddha! Then the Buddha can teach me Dhamma, so that I may understand his teaching.’ Now, I don’t say this because I’ve heard it from some other ascetic or brahmin. I only say it because I’ve known, seen, and realized it for myself.”

Closing verses

§30“When warned by the gods’ messengers, those sons of Manu who are negligent sorrow for a long time when they go to that wretched place. When warned by the gods’ messengers, the good and true persons here never neglect the teaching of the Noble One. Seeing the danger in grasping, the origin of birth and death, the unattached are freed with the ending of birth and death. Happy, they’ve come to a safe place, quenched in this very life. They’ve gone beyond all threats and perils, and risen above all suffering.”

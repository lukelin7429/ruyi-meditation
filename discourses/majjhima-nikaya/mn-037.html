Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 37

The Shorter Discourse on the Ending of Craving

Cūḷataṇhāsaṅkhayasutta

Setting
Sāvatthī, in the Eastern Park, at Migāramātupāsāda (Migāra’s Mother’s Mansion)
Speakers
The Buddha, Sakka (ruler of the gods), and Mahāmoggallāna
Form
A compact teaching followed by a verification episode — Sakka asks the Buddha a single question about liberation; Moggallāna then flies to the heaven of the Thirty-Three to test whether Sakka truly understood
Length
~8 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 210 (Madhyama-āgama 210)
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — the doctrinal core is condensed and profound; the narrative frame with Moggallāna in heaven is vivid and dramatic. The teaching itself requires familiarity with “not clinging” (anādāna) as a path to liberation.

Why this discourse, thirty-seventh

Sakka, ruler of the gods, comes to the Buddha with a single question: “In brief, how is a monk freed through the ending of craving?” The Buddha’s answer is strikingly concise: when a monk has heard that nothing is worth clinging to, he directly knows everything, and through directly knowing everything, his mind is freed by not clinging. Sakka is delighted, bows, and vanishes.

But the story does not end there. Moggallāna, suspicious that Sakka may have been merely polite, flies to the heaven of the Thirty-Three to test him. Sakka dodges the question three times, showing off his divine palace instead. Moggallāna then shakes the entire celestial mansion with his big toe — and only then does Sakka, hair standing on end, repeat the Buddha’s teaching correctly. The discourse is a parable about the difference between hearing a teaching and truly absorbing it.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A monk who has heard that nothing in all experience is worth clinging to directly knows all phenomena, and through that direct knowledge his mind is freed from craving without remainder.

The structure of the discourse

SceneWhat happens
1. Sakka’s questionSakka appears before the Buddha and asks: “In brief, sir, how is a monk freed through the ending of craving?”
2. The Buddha’s answerWhen a monk has heard that nothing is worth clinging to, he directly knows everything. Having directly known everything, he fully understands everything. Having fully understood any feeling, he observes without attachment. Through this, he is freed; with freedom, he knows he is freed. This is liberation through the ending of craving, in brief.
3. Sakka departsSakka is delighted, bows at the Buddha’s feet, circumambulates him, and vanishes right there.
4. Moggallāna’s suspicionMoggallāna wonders whether Sakka truly understood or simply agreed out of politeness. He resolves to find out.
5. The visit to heavenMoggallāna appears in the heaven of the Thirty-Three. Sakka is enjoying divine music in his palace Vejayanta with a retinue of goddesses. When Moggallāna asks the question, Sakka waves it off three times, instead showing off his palace and its splendors.
6. The earthquakeMoggallāna shakes the entire Vejayanta palace with his big toe. Sakka and the gods are terrified, hair standing on end. Sakka then recites the teaching correctly.
7. Report to the BuddhaMoggallāna returns and tells the Buddha. The Buddha confirms that Sakka had already understood during the original conversation.

The core teaching: nothing worth clinging to

The Buddha’s answer to Sakka is one of the most compressed formulations of liberation in the canon. It has a clear chain of logic: (1) a monk hears that nothing is worth clinging to; (2) he directly knows all phenomena; (3) he fully understands all phenomena; (4) whatever feeling he experiences, he observes it seeing impermanence, dispassion, cessation, and relinquishment; (5) not clinging to anything, he is freed. The entire path — from hearing to liberation — is sketched in five steps. The compression is deliberate: Sakka asked for the teaching “in brief.”

Moggallāna’s verification: hearing versus absorbing

The Moggallāna episode is the discourse’s real teaching. Sakka heard the Buddha’s answer and seemed to understand. But when Moggallāna tests him in heaven, Sakka is distracted by his own pleasures — divine music, a glittering palace, a retinue of goddesses. He cannot bring himself to engage with the question. Only when Moggallāna shakes the palace and terrifies him does Sakka snap to attention and recite the teaching.

The message: understanding a teaching intellectually and living it are different things. Sakka understood the words. But surrounded by divine pleasures, he could not recall them unprompted. The earthquake is a metaphor for the shock that makes a teaching real — the moment when intellectual understanding becomes urgent, felt knowledge.

Three questions Western students often ask

“Is Sakka a real being or a symbol?” In the Pāli canon, Sakka is presented as a real being — the ruler of the Tāvatiṃsa heaven, the Indian equivalent of Indra. But whether one reads him literally or as a literary device, the teaching works the same way: even the most powerful being in the cosmos, surrounded by every pleasure, struggles to hold the Dhamma in mind when distracted. If the king of the gods can be distracted, so can you.

“Why does Moggallāna shake the palace?” The text says he presses the palace with his big toe, and the entire structure trembles. This is a display of iddhi (psychic power), which Moggallāna is famous for. The narrative purpose is clear: polite questioning failed three times. The shaking of the palace is the attention-getting equivalent of a teacher slamming a desk. It works: Sakka, hair standing on end, suddenly takes the question seriously.

“Does the Buddha criticize Sakka?” No. When Moggallāna reports back, the Buddha says Sakka had already understood the teaching during their conversation. The gap is not in Sakka’s comprehension but in his attention: he understood the teaching, but the pleasures of heaven made it hard to recall on demand. The discourse does not condemn Sakka — it illuminates a universal human tendency: the difficulty of keeping a profound insight alive amid comfortable circumstances.

Key terms

taṇhāsaṅkhaya — the ending of craving; the destruction of thirst. The discourse’s title. Craving (taṇhā) is the second noble truth — the origin of suffering. Its complete ending is liberation.
sabbaṃ dhammaṃ nālaṃ abhinivesāya — nothing (no dhamma) is worth clinging to. The seed-statement of the entire teaching. A monk who has truly heard and absorbed this principle has the foundation for liberation.
abhijānāti — directly knows. The first cognitive step after hearing: not conceptual knowledge but direct, experiential apprehension of all phenomena.
parijānāti — fully understands; comprehends completely. Beyond direct knowledge: a thorough, penetrative understanding that leaves nothing hidden.
Sakka devānam inda — Sakka, lord of the gods. The ruler of the Tāvatiṃsa heaven. In the canon he is a devoted lay follower of the Buddha but still bound by sensual attachments — a celestial being who respects the Dhamma but has not transcended worldly pleasure.
Vejayanta — Sakka’s celestial palace. The name means “victory.” Moggallāna shakes it with his toe as a demonstration of spiritual power surpassing divine luxury.
iddhi — psychic power; supernormal ability. Moggallāna is the foremost monk in iddhi. His use of it here is not showy but pedagogical: it is the only thing that can cut through Sakka’s divine distraction.

The text

MN 37 is a short discourse with a compact doctrinal core and a vivid narrative frame. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Sakka’s question

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī, in the Eastern Park, at Migāramātupāsāda.

§2Then Sakka, lord of the gods, went to the Buddha, bowed, stood to one side, and said:

“In brief, sir, how is a monk freed through the ending of craving — one who has reached the ultimate end, the ultimate sanctuary from the yoke, the ultimate spiritual life, the ultimate goal, one who is best among gods and humans?”

The Buddha’s answer

§3“Here, lord of the gods, a monk has heard: ‘Nothing is worth clinging to.’ When a monk has heard that nothing is worth clinging to, he directly knows everything. Having directly known everything, he fully understands everything. Having fully understood everything, whatever feeling he experiences — pleasant, painful, or neutral — he observes impermanence, dispassion, cessation, and relinquishment in those feelings.

Observing in this way, he does not cling to anything in the world. Not clinging, he is not agitated. Not being agitated, he personally attains nibbāna. He understands: ‘Birth is ended, the spiritual life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is no more coming back to any state of being.’

In brief, lord of the gods, that is how a monk is freed through the ending of craving.”

§4Then Sakka, lord of the gods, having been delighted and rejoiced in the Buddha’s words, bowed at the Buddha’s feet, circumambulated him, and vanished right there.

Moggallāna goes to heaven

§5Now at that time Venerable Mahāmoggallāna was sitting not far from the Buddha. He thought: “Did that spirit truly understand the meaning of what the Buddha said, or did he not? Why don’t I find out?”

§6Then Mahāmoggallāna, as easily as a strong person would extend or contract their arm, vanished from the Eastern Park and appeared in the heaven of the Thirty-Three.

At that time Sakka was in the Vejayanta palace, supplied and provided with the five kinds of heavenly music, enjoying himself. When he saw Moggallāna coming, he dismissed the music and went up to him, saying: “Come, Venerable Moggallāna! Welcome! It has been a long time since you found the opportunity to come here. Sit down — this seat is ready.”

§7Moggallāna sat down and said: “Sakka, how did the Buddha explain the liberation through the ending of craving to you? It would be good if I could hear it too.”

When this was said, Sakka said to Moggallāna: “Reverend Moggallāna, I am so busy, with so much to do. There are things I must attend to, and also things the gods of the Thirty-Three need done. Besides, what has been well heard, well learned, well attended to, and well grasped is not quickly forgotten. Reverend, once upon a time a battle took place between the gods and the demons …”

Sakka deflects three times, each time changing the subject — to a war between gods and demons, to the splendors of his palace Vejayanta, to the enjoyment of divine life. Each time Moggallāna re-asks the question. Each time Sakka evades.

The earthquake

§8Then Moggallāna thought: “This spirit is being negligent. I should stir him up.” Then, with his big toe, Moggallāna pressed the Vejayanta palace, and it shook and trembled and quaked.

Sakka and the gods of the Thirty-Three were stunned and amazed, with hair standing on end: “How incredible! How amazing! The ascetic has such psychic power, such might, that he can shake a divine palace with his big toe!”

§9When Moggallāna knew that Sakka was stirred up and alarmed, he asked him again: “Sakka, how did the Buddha explain the liberation through the ending of craving to you in brief?”

“Reverend Moggallāna, the Buddha told me this: ‘A monk has heard that nothing is worth clinging to. When a monk has heard that nothing is worth clinging to, he directly knows everything. Having directly known everything, he fully understands everything. Having fully understood everything, whatever feeling he experiences — pleasant, painful, or neutral — he observes impermanence, dispassion, cessation, and relinquishment in those feelings. Observing in this way, he does not cling to anything in the world. Not clinging, he is not agitated. Not being agitated, he personally attains nibbāna.’

That, Reverend Moggallāna, is how the Buddha explained the liberation through the ending of craving to me in brief.”

The report

§10Then Moggallāna, having been satisfied by Sakka’s answer, vanished from the heaven of the Thirty-Three and appeared in the Eastern Park. He went to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and told the Buddha everything.

The Buddha said: “Moggallāna, Sakka could also have told you about the occasion when he was freed. For when I had taught him, Sakka was freed by the ending of craving — having penetrated that very teaching with the breakthrough to stream-entry.”

That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Moggallāna approved what the Buddha said.

Self-check quiz

Ten questions. Click an answer to see immediate feedback. No score is recorded — this is for your own checking.

Question 1 of 10
Where does Sakka meet the Buddha in this discourse?
Correct: C. The setting is the Eastern Park, at the mansion donated by Visākhā (Migāra’s Mother). This is one of two main monasteries in Sāvatthī; the other is Jeta’s Grove. Sakka descends from heaven to visit the Buddha here.
Question 2 of 10
What does Sakka ask the Buddha?
Correct: A. Sakka’s question is precise and asks for the answer “in brief.” This request for brevity shapes the Buddha’s response: the entire path from hearing to liberation is compressed into a few sentences.
Question 3 of 10
What is the seed-statement of the Buddha’s teaching in this discourse?
Correct: B. The teaching begins with hearing a principle — “nothing is worth clinging to” (sabbaṃ dhammaṃ nālaṃ abhinivesāya) — and then traces the path from hearing to direct knowledge to full understanding to the observation of feelings without attachment, culminating in liberation.
Question 4 of 10
What does the monk observe in feelings on the way to liberation?
Correct: D. The four contemplations in feelings are: impermanence (anicca), dispassion (virāga), cessation (nirodha), and relinquishment (paṭinissagga). This is a progressive deepening: first seeing change, then disenchantment, then the end of clinging, then letting go entirely.
Question 5 of 10
Why does Moggallāna go to the heaven of the Thirty-Three?
Correct: A. Moggallāna’s doubt is practical: Sakka seemed delighted and bowed and vanished, but did he truly absorb the teaching or was he being deferential? Moggallāna resolves to find out — a model of healthy skepticism even about apparently sincere responses.
Question 6 of 10
What does Sakka do when Moggallāna first asks him to repeat the teaching?
Correct: C. Sakka evades three times. He is not hostile — he is distracted. The divine pleasures surrounding him make it hard to focus on the Dhamma. This is the discourse’s central insight: understanding a teaching and being able to access it amid pleasant distractions are different capacities.
Question 7 of 10
How does Moggallāna get Sakka’s attention?
Correct: B. Moggallāna uses his famous psychic power (iddhi) to shake the palace. The image is deliberately comic and serious at once: a monk’s big toe outweighs all divine splendor. Only when shaken does Sakka recover the teaching. The earthquake is a metaphor for the shock needed to make knowledge urgent.
Question 8 of 10
After the palace shakes, can Sakka repeat the teaching?
Correct: D. Once Sakka is shaken out of his distraction, he recites the teaching accurately. He had understood it all along — the knowledge was there, but buried under the weight of celestial pleasure. The earthquake did not give him new information; it gave him access to what he already knew.
Question 9 of 10
What does the Buddha say about Sakka when Moggallāna reports back?
Correct: A. The Buddha confirms that Sakka’s understanding was genuine — he had even attained stream-entry. The problem was never comprehension but recall under distraction. The discourse does not condemn Sakka; it illustrates the universal difficulty of keeping insight alive amid comfortable circumstances.
Question 10 of 10
What is the deeper lesson of the Moggallāna episode?
Correct: C. The discourse’s real teaching is not the doctrinal formula but the drama around it: Sakka understood the Dhamma but could not retrieve it while surrounded by divine music and celestial beauty. The message for practitioners: your understanding is only as strong as your ability to access it when the world is pleasant. Comfort, not crisis, is the greater test.

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 52

The Man from the City of Aṭṭhaka

Aṭṭhakanāgarasutta

Setting
Near Vesālī, in the little village of Beluva; the householder Dasama comes from Paṭaliputta to find Ānanda
Speaker
Venerable Ānanda — answering the householder Dasama of Aṭṭhakanagara
Form
Question and answer — a layman asks for “one thing” that leads to liberation, and Ānanda gives eleven, each a meditative attainment turned into a “door to the deathless.”
Length
~11 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 217 (Madhyama-āgama 217, “The City of Eight”) and T 92; the same discourse also appears at AN 11.16
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the structure is clear and repetitive, but the central move — turning insight upon a meditative attainment itself — is subtle and important.

Why this discourse, fifty-second

A wealthy householder comes a long way to ask Venerable Ānanda a single, pointed question: is there one thing taught by the Buddha by which a diligent practitioner reaches full liberation? Ānanda answers that there is — and then, generously, gives him eleven of them. Dasama calls them eleven “doors to freedom from death.”

The eleven are not eleven different goals but eleven launch-points to the same goal: the four absorptions, the four boundless states of love, compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity, and the first three formless dimensions. What makes each of them a door is not the attainment itself — however deep and blissful — but what the meditator does with it. In every case the instruction is the same: having entered the state, reflect that “even this is produced by choices and intentions,” and therefore “impermanent and liable to cessation.” That single turn of insight, applied to the most refined experience available, is what opens the door.

MN 52 is therefore one of the clearest short statements of how serenity and insight work together. Deep meditation, by itself, leads to peace and to fortunate rebirth, but not to freedom — one can become attached to bliss as surely as to anything else. Freedom comes when the calm, bright, steady mind of absorption is used as a vantage point from which to see that the attainment too is conditioned and must be let go. The discourse also quietly maps the two outcomes of this practice: full awakening here and now, or, falling just short, non-return — rebirth in a refined realm from which one is “not liable to return.”

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Each of eleven meditative attainments — the four absorptions, the four boundless states, and the first three formless dimensions — becomes a “door to the deathless” when the meditator sees that even this refined state is “produced by choices and intentions,” impermanent and to be released; abiding in that insight, they reach either full liberation now or non-return.

A layman’s question to Ānanda

Dasama is a householder, a wealthy merchant of Aṭṭhakanagara — “the City of Eight” — who is in Paṭaliputta on business. He makes a point of seeking out Ānanda, and his question is precise and serious: is there “one thing” (eka dhamma) rightly taught by the Buddha by which “a diligent, keen, and resolute mendicant’s mind is freed, their defilements are ended, and they arrive at the supreme sanctuary from the yoke”? It is the question of a layperson who is not merely seeking blessings but wants to know the actual mechanism of liberation. That Ānanda — the disciple who heard and remembered all the Buddha’s discourses — is the one who answers gives the teaching its authority.

The eleven doors

GroupThe attainments
The four absorptions1st, 2nd, 3rd, and 4th jhāna — the deepening of rapture, stillness, and equanimity
The four boundless statesLove, compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity, radiated “to the whole world — abundant, expansive, limitless”
Three formless dimensionsInfinite space, infinite consciousness, and nothingness

Four plus four plus three makes eleven. These are not a ladder to be climbed in order; each is, on its own, a complete “door.” A practitioner who can enter even the first absorption already has, in that one state, everything needed for liberation — provided they take the next step.

The one move that opens every door

The heart of the discourse is a single reflection, repeated identically eleven times. Having entered an attainment, the meditator does not simply rest in it. They turn and look at it: “Even this… is produced by choices and intentions” (abhisaṅkhata, abhisañcetayita). And then: “But whatever is produced by choices and intentions is impermanent and liable to cessation.” This is the pivot from serenity (samatha) to insight (vipassanā). The very state that the mind would most like to cling to — because it is so peaceful, so sublime — is recognized as constructed, conditioned, and therefore not a refuge. Seeing this, the mind lets go even of its highest experience, and in that letting-go “attains the ending of defilements.”

The genius of the method is its use of the attainment’s own clarity. A mind in absorption is “purified, bright, flawless, pliable, steady”; it is the sharpest instrument a person possesses. Rather than being a dead end of blissful absorption, that very sharpness is turned back upon the absorption itself to see its true nature. Calm becomes the platform for the insight that calm alone could never reach.

The two outcomes

Ānanda is careful to name what happens for two kinds of practitioner. If the insight is complete, they “attain the ending of defilements” — full awakening, arahantship, here and now. But if it is not quite complete — if, “because of their passion and love for that meditation,” a subtle attachment remains — then with “the ending of the five lower fetters” they become a non-returner: reborn spontaneously in a refined realm, they are “extinguished there, and are not liable to return from that world.” Even the lesser outcome is irreversible. The honesty of this is striking: the same practice may carry one person all the way and another almost all the way, and the discourse refuses to pretend the difference away. A residual love even for meditation itself is enough to leave the last fetters uncut.

Why eleven, and not thirteen

The attentive reader may notice that two further attainments are missing: the fourth formless dimension (neither perception nor non-perception) and the cessation of perception and feeling. They are not doors in this scheme for a structural reason. The method requires a mind clear and active enough to reflect — to turn and examine the state as “produced by choices.” In the two highest attainments perception has become so attenuated, or has ceased altogether, that this reflective insight cannot be exercised while in them. The base of nothingness is the last attainment refined enough to be sublime yet clear enough still to be seen through. So the doors number eleven precisely.

Dasama’s joy

The householder’s response is one of the warmest in the canon. He compares himself to a man searching for the single entrance to a treasure trove who suddenly finds eleven; or to someone in a burning house who discovers eleven doors, through any one of which he could escape to safety. He asked for one thing and was given eleven independent paths to the deathless. His gratitude overflows into a great act of generosity: he feeds the whole Saṅgha of two cities with his own hands, clothes every monk, gives Ānanda a set of three robes, and builds him a dwelling. “Those of other religions seek a fee for the tutor,” he says — “why shouldn’t I make an offering?” The teaching that liberates is, in his eyes, beyond any price — and so he gives without being asked.

Key terms

eka dhamma — “one thing.” The form of Dasama’s question: is there a single practice that suffices for liberation? Ānanda’s answer is that there are eleven such things, each one complete in itself.
amata-dvāra — “door to the deathless.” Dasama’s image for the eleven attainments: doors of a burning house, through any one of which one may flee to the safety of the unconditioned.
abhisaṅkhata · abhisañcetayita — “produced by choices and intentions; volitionally constructed.” The key recognition: even a sublime meditative state is something the mind has made, and so is conditioned and impermanent.
samatha & vipassanā — serenity and insight. MN 52 shows their union exactly: the calm of absorption becomes the platform from which the impermanence of the absorption itself is seen.
yogakkhema — “sanctuary from the yoke”; security from bondage. The goal named in the question — the supreme rest beyond the yokes of sensuality, existence, views, and ignorance.
anāgāmī — the non-returner; one who, having ended the five lower fetters, is reborn spontaneously in a refined realm and attains nibbāna there, “not liable to return.” The discourse’s lesser of two outcomes.

The text

The full discourse, spoken by Venerable Ānanda. The identical reflection and two outcomes are given in full for the first absorption, the boundless state of love, and the dimension of nothingness; the intervening attainments are abbreviated with “…” as in the source. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The question

§1So I have heard. At one time Venerable Ānanda was staying near Vesālī in the little village of Beluva. Now at that time the householder Dasama, a wealthy citizen, had arrived at Paṭaliputta on some business.

§2He went to the Chicken Monastery, approached a certain mendicant, bowed, sat down to one side, and said to him, “Sir, where is Venerable Ānanda now staying? For I want to see him.” “Householder, Venerable Ānanda is staying near Vesālī in the little village of Beluva.”

§3Then the householder Dasama, having concluded his business there, went to the little village of Beluva in Vesālī to see Ānanda. He bowed, sat down to one side, and said to Ānanda: “Honorable Ānanda, is there one thing that has been rightly explained by the Blessed One—who knows and sees, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha—practicing which a diligent, keen, and resolute mendicant’s mind is freed, their defilements are ended, and they arrive at the supreme sanctuary from the yoke?” “There is, householder.” “And what is that one thing?”

The four absorptions

§4“Householder, it’s when a mendicant, quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unskillful qualities, enters and remains in the first absorption, which has the rapture and bliss born of seclusion, while placing the mind and keeping it connected. Then they reflect: ‘Even this first absorption is produced by choices and intentions.’ They understand: ‘But whatever is produced by choices and intentions is impermanent and liable to cessation.’ Abiding in that they attain the ending of defilements. If they don’t attain the ending of defilements, with the ending of the five lower fetters they’re reborn spontaneously, because of their passion and love for that meditation. They are extinguished there, and are not liable to return from that world. This is one thing that has been rightly explained by the Blessed One… practicing which a diligent, keen, and resolute mendicant’s mind is freed… and they arrive at the supreme sanctuary from the yoke.

§5–7Furthermore, as the placing of the mind and keeping it connected are stilled, they enter and remain in the second absorption … the third absorption … the fourth absorption …”

The same is said of the second, third, and fourth absorptions: in each, the meditator reflects that the attainment is “produced by choices and intentions,” therefore “impermanent and liable to cessation” — and either attains the ending of defilements, or, falling short, becomes a non-returner.

The four boundless states

§8“Furthermore, a mendicant meditates spreading a heart full of love to one direction, and to the second, and to the third, and to the fourth. In the same way above, below, across, everywhere, all around, they spread a heart full of love to the whole world—abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will. Then they reflect: ‘Even this heart’s release by love is produced by choices and intentions.’ They understand: ‘But whatever is produced by choices and intentions is impermanent and liable to cessation.’ …”

§9–11Furthermore, a mendicant meditates spreading a heart full of compassion … rejoicing … equanimity …”

The same is said of compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity: each boundless state, radiated to the whole world, is then seen as “produced by choices and intentions,” impermanent — and so becomes a door to the ending of defilements, or to non-return.

The formless dimensions

§12“Furthermore, householder, a mendicant, going totally beyond perceptions of form, with the disappearance of perceptions of impingement, not focusing on perceptions of diversity, aware that ‘space is infinite’, enters and remains in the dimension of infinite space. Then they reflect: ‘Even this attainment of the dimension of infinite space is produced by choices and intentions.’ They understand: ‘But whatever is produced by choices and intentions is impermanent and liable to cessation.’ …

§13Furthermore, a mendicant, going totally beyond the dimension of infinite space, aware that ‘consciousness is infinite’, enters and remains in the dimension of infinite consciousness. …

§14Furthermore, a mendicant, going totally beyond the dimension of infinite consciousness, aware that ‘there is nothing at all’, enters and remains in the dimension of nothingness. Then they reflect: ‘Even this attainment of the dimension of nothingness is produced by choices and intentions.’ They understand: ‘But whatever is produced by choices and intentions is impermanent and liable to cessation.’ Abiding in that they attain the ending of defilements. If they don’t attain the ending of defilements, with the ending of the five lower fetters they’re reborn spontaneously because of their passion and love for that meditation. They are extinguished there, and are not liable to return from that world. This too is one thing that has been rightly explained by the Blessed One… practicing which a diligent, keen, and resolute mendicant’s mind is freed, their defilements are ended, and they arrive at the supreme sanctuary from the yoke.”

The eleven doors

§15When he said this, the householder Dasama said to Venerable Ānanda, “Honorable Ānanda, suppose a person was looking for the entrance to a treasure trove. And all at once they’d come across eleven entrances! In the same way, I was searching for the door to freedom from death. And all at once I found eleven doors to freedom from death for cultivation. Suppose a person had a house with eleven doors. If the house caught fire they’d be able to flee to safety through any one of those doors. In the same way, I’m able to flee to safety through any one of these eleven doors to freedom from death. Sir, those of other religions seek a fee for the tutor. Why shouldn’t I make an offering to Venerable Ānanda?”

§16Then the householder Dasama, having assembled the Saṅgha from Vesālī and Paṭaliputta, served and satisfied them with his own hands with delicious fresh and cooked foods. He clothed each and every mendicant in a pair of garments, with a set of three robes for Ānanda. And he had a dwelling worth five hundred built for Ānanda.

Question 1 of 10
Who teaches this discourse, and to whom?
Correct: C. The discourse is spoken by Ānanda — the disciple who heard and remembered all the Buddha’s teachings — in answer to a serious question from Dasama, a wealthy merchant of Aṭṭhakanagara, “the City of Eight.”
Question 2 of 10
What does Dasama ask for?
Correct: A. His question is about the actual mechanism of liberation: is there a single practice (eka dhamma) sufficient to end the defilements and reach freedom? It is the question of a layperson seeking not blessings but the real path.
Question 3 of 10
How many “doors to the deathless” does Ānanda actually give?
Correct: D. Asked for one, Ānanda gives eleven: 4 jhānas + 4 boundless states (love, compassion, rejoicing, equanimity) + 3 formless dimensions (infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness). Each is a complete door in itself.
Question 4 of 10
What single reflection turns each attainment into a “door”?
Correct: B. The pivot from serenity to insight: the meditator recognizes that even a sublime state is something the mind has constructed (abhisaṅkhata), and therefore conditioned and impermanent. Seeing this, the mind releases even its highest experience.
Question 5 of 10
Why is deep meditation, by itself, not enough for liberation?
Correct: C. Absorption brings peace and fortunate rebirth but not freedom — one can cling to sublime bliss as to anything else. Liberation requires using the bright, steady mind of absorption to see that the absorption too is conditioned, and releasing it.
Question 6 of 10
What are the two possible outcomes of this practice?
Correct: A. If the insight is complete, the practitioner attains arahantship here and now. If a subtle attachment remains — “passion and love for that meditation” — then with the ending of the five lower fetters they become a non-returner.
Question 7 of 10
What is a “non-returner” (anāgāmī), as described here?
Correct: D. The non-returner has cut the five lower fetters but not the last subtle attachments. They are “reborn spontaneously” in a refined realm and “extinguished there” — this outcome too is irreversible; they never return to this world.
Question 8 of 10
Why does the list stop at the dimension of nothingness, rather than including the two highest attainments?
Correct: B. Each door requires turning insight upon the state as “produced by choices.” In neither-perception-nor-non-perception and in cessation, perception is too faint or wholly stopped to support that reflection. The base of nothingness is the last attainment sublime yet still clear enough to be seen through — so the doors number exactly eleven.
Question 9 of 10
What images does Dasama use to express his joy at the answer?
Correct: C. He searched for one door to the deathless and found eleven — like stumbling on eleven entrances to a treasure, or a burning house with eleven exits, through any one of which he could flee to safety. He asked for one thing and received eleven independent paths.
Question 10 of 10
How does Dasama respond to receiving the teaching?
Correct: A. “Those of other religions seek a fee for the tutor — why shouldn’t I make an offering?” The teaching that liberates is beyond price, so Dasama gives freely and without being asked: a feast for the whole Saṅgha, garments for all, robes and a dwelling for Ānanda.

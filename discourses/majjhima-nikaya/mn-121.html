Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 121

The Shorter Discourse on Emptiness

Cūḷasuññatasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Migāra’s mother’s stilt longhouse, Eastern Monastery
Speakers
The Buddha and Venerable Ānanda
Form
A graduated meditation of relative emptiness, ascending through perception after perception to a final liberating insight
Length
~12 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A close counterpart survives in the Chinese Madhyama Āgama (MĀ 190, “Lesser Discourse on Emptiness”)
Difficulty
★★★★★ — one of the most quietly radical suttas in the canon, and the root of “emptiness” as a Buddhist term

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-twenty-first

This discourse opens the Suññatavagga, the “chapter on emptiness” — and it opens with Ānanda double-checking his own memory. He recalls the Buddha once saying, almost in passing, “these days I usually practice the meditation on emptiness.” It is a striking admission: not one technique among many, but the Buddha’s own habitual dwelling. Asked to explain, the Buddha does something remarkable — he defines emptiness not as a metaphysical claim about reality, but as a precise, practical, and gradually deepening way of paying attention.

The word suññatā would go on to become one of the most consequential terms in the entire history of Buddhist thought, the centerpiece of later Mahāyāna philosophy. Here, in one of its earliest and clearest settings, it means something almost disarmingly modest: something is empty of something else, the way a room can be empty of furniture while still full of air and light. What makes this discourse extraordinary is where that simple idea, followed all the way through, actually leads.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Emptiness is not the absence of everything but a relative and precise perception — something is empty of the grosser thing no longer attended to, while what remains is recognized as genuinely present — and by ascending through progressively subtler perceptions and finally seeing that even the subtlest is itself conditioned and impermanent, the mind is freed.

A memory double-checked

Ānanda comes to the Buddha with something he half-remembers: at a town called Nagaraka (rendered here as “Townsville”), he once heard the Buddha say, “these days I usually practice the meditation on emptiness.” Rather than simply asserting his memory, Ānanda asks the Buddha to confirm it — “I trust I properly heard, learned, applied the mind, and remembered that?” — a small but telling scene of how oral teachings were verified. The Buddha confirms it, and adds that it remains true now as before. What follows is his explanation of exactly what that practice is.

Emptiness is relative, not absolute

Rather than a definition, the Buddha offers a scene both of them can see. They are sitting, at that very moment, in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother:

The longhouse. “This stilt longhouse is empty of elephants, cows, horses, and mares; of gold and silver; and of gatherings of men and women. There is only this that is not emptiness, namely the oneness dependent on the mendicant Saṅgha.”

The longhouse isn’t empty in some absolute sense — it’s full of monks. It is empty specifically of livestock, treasure, and crowds of laypeople, while what remains (the community actually present) is recognized as genuinely there. This is the whole method in miniature: emptiness is always emptiness of something specific, and what is not empty is acknowledged just as clearly as what is. As the refrain puts it at every stage of the meditation to come: “they regard it as empty of what is not there, but as to what remains they understand that it is present.”

A ladder of ever-quieter perceptions

From this one image, the Buddha builds an entire graduated practice, each stage ignoring the perception before it and settling into something subtler:

StageIgnoring…Settles into…
1the perception of village and peoplethe perception of wilderness
2the perception of people and wildernessthe perception of earth — an inner image stretched smooth “as a bull’s hide is rid of folds when pegged out by a hundred stakes,” ignoring every hill, ravine, and thorn
3the perception of wilderness and earththe dimension of infinite space
4the perception of earth and infinite spacethe dimension of infinite consciousness
5infinite space and infinite consciousnessthe dimension of nothingness
6infinite consciousness and nothingnessthe dimension of neither perception nor non-perception
7nothingness and neither-perception-nor-non-perceptionthe signless immersion of the heart

This traces a meditator’s path from the ordinary business of village life, through solitude, through the earth-image that grounds the form absorptions, up through all four formless attainments — and then one step further, to a seventh state beyond even “neither perception nor non-perception,” where the mind no longer “follows after signs” at all. At every stage the same two recognitions recur: there is less “stress” here than at the coarser stage before, though never none; and this field of perception is empty of what came before, while what remains — the current, subtler perception — is genuinely present. “That’s how emptiness manifests in them: genuine, undistorted, and pure.”

The turn: seeing through, not just settling in

Here the discourse makes its decisive move, and it is easy to read past it. The signless immersion of the heart is extraordinarily refined — but the Buddha does not treat it as the destination. Instead: “They understand: ‘Even this signless immersion of the heart is produced by choices and intentions.’ They understand: ‘But whatever is produced by choices and intentions is impermanent and liable to cessation.’” The meditator turns insight on the very state of deep serenity they have just reached, seeing that even it is saṅkhata — conditioned, constructed, and therefore not exempt from impermanence. “Knowing and seeing like this, their mind is freed from the defilements of sensuality, desire to be reborn, and ignorance.”

This is the hinge the whole sutta turns on. Every stage before this one was serenity (samatha) — each perception genuinely calmer and less stressful than the last, but still, in the end, just another conditioned state to rest in. Liberation doesn’t come from finding the most refined resting place available. It comes from insight (vipassanā) applied even to that resting place itself — from realizing that no matter how subtle the state, if it was constructed by intention, it can end.

What remains, even for the free

Awakening does not erase the body. Even now, the text says, “there is only this modicum of stress, namely that related to the six sense fields dependent on this body and conditioned by life” — the ordinary vulnerability of having eyes, ears, and a nervous system while still alive. This is not a flaw in the teaching but a precise piece of honesty: the commentary notes that the Buddha himself, late in life, used this very “signless” meditation to ease the pain of old age, remaining lucid and functional even as bodily pain continued. Freedom from the three defilements does not require, or produce, freedom from having a body until death.

Pure, ultimate, supreme emptiness

With the defilements ended, this final emptiness is called “genuine, undistorted, pure, and supreme” — the only point in the early texts where something this close to Nibbāna itself is described directly as empty. The Buddha closes with a claim of universality: “whatever ascetics and brahmins enter and remain in the pure, ultimate, supreme emptiness — whether in the past, future, or present — all of them enter and remain in this same” emptiness. There is only one summit, however many paths lead to it. “So, Ānanda,” he concludes, “you should train like this: ‘We will enter and remain in the pure, ultimate, supreme emptiness.’”

Key terms

suññatā — “emptiness”: not a metaphysical property but a relative state — something is empty of something specific, while what remains is recognized as genuinely present.
the longhouse simile — empty of livestock, treasure, and crowds, not empty of the mendicant Saṅgha actually present: the model for every stage of the meditation that follows.
darathā — “stress”: each stage of the graduated meditation carries less of it than the one before, though never none, until liberation itself.
animitta cetosamādhi — the “signless immersion of the heart”: a state where consciousness no longer “follows after signs” created by greed, hate, and delusion; the seventh and final stage of the ascending perceptions.
saṅkhata — “produced by choices and intentions,” hence conditioned and impermanent: the insight that even the signless immersion is this way is what actually liberates the mind, not the immersion itself.
visuddhaṁ paramānuttaraṁ suññataṁ — “pure, ultimate, supreme emptiness”: the discourse’s name for full liberation, and among the closest the early texts come to describing Nibbāna itself as empty.

The text

The discourse in full: Ānanda’s question, the longhouse simile, all seven stages of the graduated meditation on emptiness, the liberating insight, and the closing declaration. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A memory double-checked

§1–3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother, in the Eastern Monastery. Then in the late afternoon, Venerable Ānanda came out of retreat, went to the Buddha, and said: “Sir, this one time the Buddha was staying in the land of the Sakyans, at a town named Townsville. There I heard and learned this in the Buddha’s presence: ‘Ānanda, these days I usually practice the meditation on emptiness.’ I trust I properly heard, learned, applied the mind, and remembered that from the Buddha?” “Indeed, Ānanda, you properly heard, learned, applied the mind, and remembered that. Now, as before, I usually practice the meditation on emptiness.

The longhouse

§4Consider this stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother. It’s empty of elephants, cows, horses, and mares; of gold and silver; and of gatherings of men and women. There is only this that is not emptiness, namely, the oneness dependent on the mendicant Saṅgha. In the same way, a mendicant — ignoring the perception of the village and the perception of people — focuses on the oneness dependent on the perception of wilderness. Their mind leaps forth, gains confidence, settles down, and becomes decided in that perception of wilderness. They understand: ‘Here there is no stress due to the perception of village or the perception of people. There is only this modicum of stress, namely the oneness dependent on the perception of wilderness.’ They understand: ‘This field of perception is empty of the perception of the village. It is empty of the perception of people. There is only this that is not emptiness, namely the oneness dependent on the perception of wilderness.’ And so they regard it as empty of what is not there, but as to what remains they understand that it is present. That’s how emptiness manifests in them — genuine, undistorted, and pure.

Seven ascending stages

§5Furthermore, a mendicant — ignoring the perception of people and the perception of wilderness — focuses on the oneness dependent on the perception of earth. As a bull’s hide is rid of folds when fully stretched out by a hundred pegs, so too, ignoring the hilly terrain, inaccessible riverlands, stumps and thorns, and rugged mountains, they focus on the oneness dependent on the perception of earth. Their mind leaps forth, gains confidence, settles down, and becomes decided in that perception of earth. They understand: ‘Here there is no stress due to the perception of people or the perception of wilderness. There is only this modicum of stress, namely the oneness dependent on the perception of earth.’ This field of perception is empty of the perception of people and of wilderness; there is only this that is not emptiness, the oneness dependent on the perception of earth. That’s how emptiness manifests in them — genuine, undistorted, and pure.

§6–9Furthermore, a mendicant — ignoring the perception of wilderness and of earth — focuses on the oneness dependent on the perception of the dimension of infinite space. Furthermore, ignoring the perception of earth and of infinite space, they focus on the perception of the dimension of infinite consciousness. Furthermore, ignoring infinite space and infinite consciousness, they focus on the perception of the dimension of nothingness. Furthermore, ignoring infinite consciousness and nothingness, they focus on the perception of the dimension of neither perception nor non-perception. At each stage, their mind leaps forth, gains confidence, settles down, and becomes decided; they understand that the stress of the coarser perception is absent, that only the subtler modicum of stress remains, and that this field of perception is empty of what came before while what remains is genuinely present. That’s how emptiness manifests in them, each time — genuine, undistorted, and pure.

The turn

§10–11Furthermore, a mendicant — ignoring the perception of the dimension of nothingness and the perception of the dimension of neither perception nor non-perception — focuses on the oneness dependent on the signless immersion of the heart. Their mind leaps forth, gains confidence, settles down, and becomes decided in that signless immersion of the heart. They understand: ‘Here there is no stress due to the perception of the dimension of nothingness or of neither perception nor non-perception. There is only this modicum of stress, namely that related to the six sense fields dependent on this body and conditioned by life.’

They understand: ‘Even this signless immersion of the heart is produced by choices and intentions.’ They understand: ‘But whatever is produced by choices and intentions is impermanent and liable to cessation.’ Knowing and seeing like this, their mind is freed from the defilements of sensuality, desire to be reborn, and ignorance. When they’re freed, they know they’re freed. They understand: ‘Rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey has been completed, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this place.’

Pure, ultimate, supreme emptiness

§12–13They understand: ‘Here there is no stress due to the defilements of sensuality, desire to be reborn, or ignorance. There is only this modicum of stress, namely that related to the six sense fields dependent on this body and conditioned by life.’ They understand: ‘This field of perception is empty of the defilements of sensuality, desire to be reborn, and ignorance. There is only this that is not emptiness, namely that related to the six sense fields dependent on this body and conditioned by life.’ And so they regard it as empty of what is not there, but as to what remains they understand that it is present. That’s how emptiness manifests in them — genuine, undistorted, pure, and supreme.

Whatever ascetics and brahmins enter and remain in the pure, ultimate, supreme emptiness — whether in the past, future, or present — all of them enter and remain in this same pure, ultimate, supreme emptiness. So, Ānanda, you should train like this: ‘We will enter and remain in the pure, ultimate, supreme emptiness.’ That’s how you should train.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Ānanda approved what the Buddha said.

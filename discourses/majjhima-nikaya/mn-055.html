Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 55

With Jīvaka

Jīvakasutta

Setting
Rājagaha, in the Mango Grove of Jīvaka Komārabhacca, the royal physician
Speakers
The Buddha and Jīvaka, doctor to King Bimbisāra and to the Saṅgha
Form
Question and answer on a contested point — whether the Buddha eats meat from animals killed for him — resolved through the threefold rule, the ethic of intention, and the five grounds of demerit.
Length
~9 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No Chinese Āgama parallel; a Sanskrit fragment survives
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the text is short and concrete, but the topic of meat-eating invites care: the discourse is precise about what it does and does not permit.

Why this discourse, fifty-fifth

Jīvaka, the famous physician who tended both King Bimbisāra and the Buddha, brings a charge he has heard repeated: that people slaughter animals especially for the ascetic Gotama, and that he eats their meat knowing it was killed on his behalf — making him, in effect, party to the killing. The Buddha’s reply is one of the canon’s clearest statements on food, harm, and the ethics of intention.

The heart of the answer is the “threefold rule”: meat may not be eaten if one has seen, heard, or suspects that the animal was killed specifically for oneself; and it may be eaten only when none of these three applies. The criterion is not the substance on the plate but one’s relationship to the act of killing. A mendicant who lives on alms takes what is freely offered and has no part in any creature’s death; but the moment an animal is killed for them, the food becomes unallowable.

Around this rule the Buddha sets two further points. First, the manner of eating: a monk who dwells in boundless love and eats his almsfood “untied, uninfatuated, unattached,” without craving for more, intends harm to no one and so eats “blameless food.” Second, the weight of responsibility falls squarely where the harm is done: the one who kills an animal for a monk “brims with much wickedness” on five distinct counts. The discourse thus locates moral fault precisely — in the intention to kill and the act of killing — rather than in the passive receiving of food that already exists.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Meat may not be eaten if one has seen, heard, or suspects an animal was killed specifically for oneself; an alms-living monk who dwells in love and eats what is freely offered, without craving, harms no one and eats blameless food — while the one who kills an animal “for” a monk accrues grave demerit on five counts.

The physician’s question

Jīvaka Komārabhacca is no ordinary questioner. He was the most celebrated doctor of his age — physician to King Bimbisāra and to the Buddha himself — and the Mango Grove where this takes place was his own gift to the Saṅgha. He raises the charge carefully and fairly: “I have heard this… The ascetic Gotama knowingly eats meat prepared on his behalf. Do those who say this represent you accurately, or misrepresent you?” It is the question of a thoughtful supporter who wants to know whether a troubling rumor is true. The Buddha answers that those who say it “misrepresent me with what is false and untrue” — and then explains exactly why.

The threefold rule

The core of the reply is a clean, memorable rule, stated in two directions:

Meat may NOT be eatenMeat MAY be eaten
If it is seen, heard, or suspected that the animal was killed specifically for youIf it is not seen, not heard, and not suspected that the animal was killed for you

This is the rule the tradition calls tikoṭiparisuddha — meat “pure in three respects.” What it turns on is the eater’s connection to the killing. A mendicant who wanders for alms receives whatever is placed in the bowl; he has not chosen the menu, commissioned a slaughter, or had any creature killed for him. Such food is allowable. But if he sees the animal being killed for him, hears that it was, or has good reason to suspect it, the food becomes off-limits — because now his eating would be entangled with a death caused on his account.

What the discourse does — and does not — say

It is worth being precise, because this discourse is often quoted in debates about vegetarianism. MN 55 does not declare meat-eating ideal, nor does it praise it; it addresses the specific situation of a mendicant who lives by alms and cannot control what donors offer. Its concern is non-complicity in killing, not a general endorsement of a meat diet. Elsewhere in the canon the Buddha declines to make vegetarianism a binding rule for the Saṅgha — precisely because alms-receivers must accept what they are given — while many later Buddhist traditions, reading the spirit of compassion further, have embraced vegetarianism as an expression of it. MN 55 sits at the root of that long conversation: it fixes the minimum principle — never be the reason an animal dies — on which the wider ethic is built.

Blameless food: the monk who dwells in love

Before naming the rule’s mechanics, the Buddha paints a picture of how such a monk eats. He dwells “spreading a heart full of love… to the whole world — abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will.” Invited to a meal and served fine food, it never occurs to him, “How good! I hope they serve me like this again!” He eats “untied, uninfatuated, unattached, seeing the drawback, and understanding the escape.” At that moment, the Buddha asks, is he intending to hurt himself, others, or both? No. He is eating “blameless food.” The point is that blame, in eating, attaches to craving and to harmful intention — and a heart established in love and non-attachment has neither. The same is then said of compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity.

The Buddha truly abides in love

Jīvaka responds with a striking testimony. He has heard the saying, “The Divinity abides in love” — the highest god, Brahmā, is the very emblem of boundless love — “but now I have seen the Buddha with my own eyes, and it is the Buddha who truly abides in love.” The Buddha accepts the comparison, with a precise correction: in him, any greed, hate, or delusion “that might give rise to ill will” has been “cut off at the root, made like a palm stump, obliterated, unable to arise in the future.” His love is not a mood or a practice he sustains but the permanent condition of a mind from which the very roots of cruelty have been destroyed.

The five grounds of demerit

Finally the Buddha locates responsibility exactly. Anyone who slaughters a creature specifically for the Buddha or a disciple “brims with much wickedness” on five counts:

  1. Saying, “Go, fetch that living creature”;
  2. The creature’s pain and fear as it is led along by a collar;
  3. Saying, “Go, slaughter that living creature”;
  4. The creature’s pain and fear as it is slaughtered;
  5. Serving the Buddha or a disciple with food that is thereby unallowable.

Every one of the five attaches to the act of killing and the intention behind it — the command, the suffering caused, the deed, and finally the offering of unallowable food. None of them attaches to the innocent receiving of already-existing alms. This is the ethic of intention (cetanā) made concrete: karma follows the will to harm, not the mere physical fact that a meal contains meat. The person who needs to examine their conscience is the one who arranged the death — not the monk who, unknowing, was handed the bowl.

Jīvaka is delighted: “The mendicants indeed eat allowable food… blameless food,” and goes for refuge as a lay follower for life. The doctor who could heal bodies recognizes a deeper kind of health — a way of living that, even in something as ordinary as a meal, leaves no wound anywhere.

Key terms

Jīvaka Komārabhacca — the renowned physician of Rājagaha, doctor to King Bimbisāra and to the Buddha. He donated the Mango Grove (Jīvakāmbavana) where this discourse takes place.
tikoṭiparisuddha — “pure in three respects.” The rule for allowable meat: not seen, not heard, not suspected to have been killed for oneself.
diṭṭha · suta · parisaṅkita — seen, heard, suspected. The three conditions that make meat unallowable — each a way the eater would be connected to a killing done on their account.
kappiya / akappiya — allowable / unallowable. Food killed specifically for a monk becomes akappiya; freely offered alms, with no such connection, remain kappiya.
cetanā — intention; volition. The Buddha’s ethics locate karma in cetanā — here, in the will to kill — not in the physical substance of a meal.
anavajja-bhojana — blameless food. Food eaten without craving and without harmful intention, by one whose heart is established in love — “untied, uninfatuated, unattached.”

The text

The full discourse. The passage on the monk dwelling in love is given in full; the parallel passages for compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity are abbreviated as in the source. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The charge

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha in the Mango Grove of Jīvaka Komārabhacca.

§2Then Jīvaka went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and said to the Buddha:

§3“Sir, I have heard this: ‘They slaughter living creatures specially for the ascetic Gotama. The ascetic Gotama knowingly eats meat prepared on his behalf: this is a deed he caused.’ I trust that those who say this repeat what the Buddha has said, and do not misrepresent him with an untruth? Is their explanation in line with the teaching? Are there any legitimate grounds for rebuttal and criticism?”

§4“Jīvaka, those who say this do not repeat what I have said. They misrepresent me with what is false and untrue.

The threefold rule

§5In three cases I say that meat may not be eaten: it’s seen, heard, or suspected. These are three cases in which meat may not be eaten. In three cases I say that meat may be eaten: it’s not seen, heard, or suspected. These are three cases in which meat may be eaten.

Blameless food, eaten in love

§6Take the case of a mendicant living supported by a town or village. They meditate spreading a heart full of love to one direction, and to the second, and to the third, and to the fourth. In the same way above, below, across, everywhere, all around, they spread a heart full of love to the whole world—abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will. A householder or their child approaches and invites them for the next day’s meal. The mendicant accepts if they want. When the night has passed, they robe up in the morning, take their bowl and robe, and approach that householder’s home, where they sit on the seat spread out. That householder or their child serves them with delicious almsfood. It never occurs to them, ‘It’s so good that this householder serves me with delicious almsfood! I hope they serve me with such delicious almsfood in the future!’ They don’t think that. They eat that almsfood untied, uninfatuated, unattached, seeing the drawback, and understanding the escape. What do you think, Jīvaka? At that time is that mendicant intending to hurt themselves, hurt others, or hurt both?” “No, sir.” “Aren’t they eating blameless food at that time?”

§7“Yes, sir. Sir, I have heard that ‘The Divinity abides in love.’ Now, I’ve seen the Buddha with my own eyes, and it is the Buddha who truly abides in love.” “Any greed, hate, or delusion that might give rise to ill will has been given up by the Realized One, cut off at the root, made like a palm stump, obliterated, and is unable to arise in the future. If that’s what you were referring to, I grant it.” “That’s exactly what I was referring to.”

The same is said of the other three boundless states. A mendicant who dwells spreading a heart full of compassion … rejoicing … equanimity to the whole world, and who eats the offered almsfood without craving, intends harm to no one and eats blameless food. Jīvaka responds: “I have heard that ‘The Divinity abides in equanimity’ — but it is the Buddha who truly abides in equanimity.”

§11“Any greed, hate, or delusion that might give rise to cruelty, discontent, or aversion has been given up by the Realized One, cut off at the root, made like a palm stump, obliterated, and is unable to arise in the future. If that’s what you were referring to, I grant it.” “That’s exactly what I was referring to.”

The five grounds of demerit

§12“Jīvaka, anyone who slaughters a living creature specially for the Realized One or the Realized One’s disciple brims with much wickedness for five reasons. When they say: ‘Go, fetch that living creature,’ this is the first reason. When that living creature experiences pain and sadness as it’s led along by a collar, this is the second reason. When they say: ‘Go, slaughter that living creature,’ this is the third reason. When that living creature experiences pain and sadness as it’s being slaughtered, this is the fourth reason. When they serve the Realized One or the Realized One’s disciple with unallowable food, this is the fifth reason. Anyone who slaughters a living creature specially for the Realized One or the Realized One’s disciple brims with much wickedness for these five reasons.”

Jīvaka goes for refuge

§13When he had spoken, Jīvaka said to the Buddha: “It’s incredible, sir, it’s amazing! The mendicants indeed eat allowable food. The mendicants indeed eat blameless food. Excellent, sir! Excellent! … From this day forth, may the Buddha remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.”

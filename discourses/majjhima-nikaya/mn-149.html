Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 149

The Great Discourse on the Six Sense Fields

Mahāsaḷāyatanikasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery, addressed to the resident community
Speakers
The Buddha alone, addressing the mendicants directly — no interlocutor or dialogue
Form
A tightly parallel doctrinal exposition: two opposite causal chains, run identically across all six sense fields
Length
~12 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No specific Āgama match is confidently identified in the available comparative scholarship; the Saḷāyatanavagga as a whole is less fully attested in the surviving Chinese Madhyama-āgama than earlier chapters of the Majjhima
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — dense and technical, built from repeating parts, but each part is exact and repays close, patient reading

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-forty-ninth

No brahmin arrives with a puzzle here, no lay follower asks for reassurance. The Buddha simply addresses the mendicants and lays out, with the precision of an engineer, exactly what happens — every time, for every one of the six sense fields — when a person does not understand ordinary experience, and exactly what happens instead when they do. It is one of the most systematically constructed discourses in the whole Majjhima Nikāya: the same twelve-term chain, run in two opposite directions, applied six times over. The “great” (mahā) in its title marks it out from its shorter neighbor two discourses back, MN 148’s Chachakkasutta, which counts the same six sense fields by sixes; this discourse instead traces, in full, where the whole process leads — all the way to the seven factors of awakening and the ending of craving itself.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Whether a person is heading toward bondage or toward freedom is decided, moment by moment, entirely by whether they truly understand the eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind, the objects each one meets, and the feeling that arises from that meeting — not by anything more exotic than that.

A discourse with no dialogue

Unlike most of the suttas around it in this closing chapter, the Mahāsaḷāyatanikasutta has no brahmin questioner, no personal crisis, no named interlocutor at all. The Buddha, staying at Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery outside Sāvatthī, simply announces to the assembled mendicants that he will teach “the great discourse on what relates to the six sense fields,” and proceeds to do exactly that — a pure, self-contained piece of doctrinal architecture, addressed to people already familiar with the vocabulary and ready to follow a long chain of reasoning without a story to carry them through it.

Six fields, four elements each

The “six sense fields” (saḷāyatana) are the eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind. For each one, the discourse tracks exactly the same four elements: the sense organ itself, its object (sights, sounds, smells, flavors, touches, or ideas), the consciousness that knows that object through that organ, and the contact where organ, object, and consciousness meet — from which arises a felt experience, pleasant, painful, or neutral. Nothing in the sutta asks whether this basic structure of experience can be avoided. It cannot. What can differ, entirely, is whether a person understands it.

Not understanding: the chain toward bondage

The first half of the discourse states, for the eye and then in turn for the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind, what follows from not truly knowing and seeing the sense organ, its object, its consciousness, its contact, and the feeling that arises from that contact: a person is “aroused” by these things. Being aroused like this — the text is specific — means living “fettered, confused, concentrating on gratification.” And from that single condition, three things grow at once: the five grasping aggregates (form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness, the raw material of a sense of self) accumulate for the future; craving, “mixed up with relishing and greed, taking pleasure there wherever it alights,” grows, carrying the person on toward a future life; and physical and mental stress, torment, and fever all grow with it. The final line of each pass through this chain is blunt: such a person experiences physical and mental suffering. Not as a punishment handed down from elsewhere — as the direct, mechanical result of not understanding what is, in each moment, right in front of them.

Understanding: the same chain, reversed

The second half of the discourse takes the identical structure and runs it the other way. Truly knowing and seeing the eye, its objects, its consciousness, its contact, and the feeling that arises — a person is simply not aroused by these things. Living unaroused like this means living “unfettered, unconfused, concentrating on drawbacks.” And the same three consequences now run in reverse: the five grasping aggregates disperse rather than accumulate; craving is given up rather than growing; stress, torment, and fever are given up rather than growing. The person experiences physical and mental pleasure — not as a reward, but as what is simply left when the conditions for suffering are no longer being manufactured moment to moment.

The turn on which everything hinges. “When you don’t truly know and see the eye… you’re aroused by these things,” against “When you do truly know and see the eye… you’re not aroused by these things.” Every consequence in the discourse, for good or ill, follows from this one difference.

From right view to the seven awakening factors

Having established the “understanding” chain for the eye, the discourse does something the “not understanding” chain never does: it keeps going. The view of a person who lives this way is right view; their intention, effort, mindfulness, and immersion are right intention, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion — and since their bodily and verbal conduct and livelihood were already purified beforehand, the entire noble eightfold path is now fully developed in them. And the path does not stand alone: once it is developed, the text says, the four kinds of mindfulness meditation, the four right efforts, the four bases of psychic power, the five faculties, the five powers, and the seven awakening factors are all fully developed together with it — the standard list of thirty-seven qualities the tradition calls the “wings to awakening.” Two further qualities, serenity and discernment, proceed together in this person, “in conjunction,” each supporting the other.

Four tasks, four objects

The discourse closes its exposition (first for the eye, and again — written out in full, exactly the same way — for the mind, after the ear, nose, tongue, and body are covered by abbreviation) with a compact fourfold scheme that recurs throughout the canon as a summary of the entire path:

The taskIts object
completely understood by direct knowledgethe five grasping aggregates — form, feeling, perception, choices, consciousness
given up by direct knowledgeignorance and craving for continued existence
developed by direct knowledgeserenity and discernment
realized by direct knowledgeknowledge and freedom

This is, in effect, the Four Noble Truths recast as four tasks rather than four facts: the aggregates (suffering) are to be understood, craving (the origin of suffering) is to be given up, the path (serenity and discernment together) is to be developed, and freedom (cessation) is to be realized. The sutta’s entire argument about the six sense fields turns out to be one long, careful demonstration of exactly where, in ordinary experience, this fourfold work actually gets done.

Why it matters that this is repeated six times, not stated once

It would be easy to read the repetition across eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind as padding. It is not. The discourse’s implicit claim is that there is no sense field exempt from this analysis and none where a shortcut is available — understanding cannot be achieved for the eye and merely assumed for the mind. Each of the six fields has to be known in exactly the same way, which is part of why the text is willing to spell the whole pattern out twice in full (for the eye at the start, and for the mind at the end) rather than trusting the reader to fill in the abbreviated middle four purely by analogy.

Key terms

saḷāyatana — the “six sense fields”: eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind, each paired with its own class of object and giving the closing chapter of the Majjhima Nikāya (MN 143–152) its name.
sārajjati — “aroused,” the pivotal verb marking the difference between the two chains: aroused by what is not understood, not aroused by what is.
pañcupādānakkhandhā — the five grasping aggregates (form, feeling, perception, choices, consciousness), said to accumulate under ignorance and disperse under understanding.
bodhipakkhiyā dhammā — the “qualities that side with awakening”: the four kinds of mindfulness, four right efforts, four bases of psychic power, five faculties, five powers, seven awakening factors, and the noble eightfold path itself — thirty-seven items in seven groups, all said here to mature together.
the four tasks — understanding the aggregates, giving up ignorance and craving, developing serenity and discernment, and realizing knowledge and freedom: a compact restatement of the Four Noble Truths as things to be done.

The text

The discourse in full. Where the source text abbreviates the ear, nose, tongue, and body with an ellipsis (since each repeats the eye and mind sections identically, substituting only the organ and object named), that abbreviation is kept below as in the original; the two fully spelled-out passages — for the eye, and again for the mind, closing the sutta — are given complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

An announcement, not a question

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. There the Buddha addressed the mendicants, “Mendicants!” “Venerable sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this: “Mendicants, I shall teach you the great discourse on what relates to the six sense fields. Listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

Not understanding: the chain toward bondage

§3–8“Mendicants, when you don’t truly know and see the eye, sights, eye consciousness, eye contact, and what is felt as pleasant, painful, or neutral that arises dependent on eye contact, you’re aroused by these things. Someone who lives aroused like this—fettered, confused, concentrating on gratification—accumulates the five grasping aggregates for themselves in the future. And their craving—which leads to future lives, mixed up with relishing and greed, taking pleasure there wherever it alights—grows. Their physical and mental stress, torment, and fever grow. And they experience physical and mental suffering.

When you don’t truly know and see the ear… nose… tongue… body… mind, ideas, mind consciousness, mind contact, and what is felt as pleasant, painful, or neutral that arises dependent on mind contact, you’re aroused by these things. Someone who lives aroused like this—fettered, confused, concentrating on gratification—accumulates the five grasping aggregates for themselves in the future. And their craving—which leads to future lives, mixed up with relishing and greed, taking pleasure there wherever it alights—grows. Their physical and mental stress, torment, and fever grow. And they experience physical and mental suffering.

Understanding: the same chain, reversed

§9–11When you do truly know and see the eye, sights, eye consciousness, eye contact, and what is felt as pleasant, painful, or neutral that arises dependent on eye contact, you’re not aroused by these things. Someone who lives unaroused like this—unfettered, unconfused, concentrating on drawbacks—disperses the five grasping aggregates for themselves in the future. And their craving—which leads to future lives, mixed up with relishing and greed, taking pleasure there wherever it alights—is given up. Their physical and mental stress, torment, and fever are given up. And they experience physical and mental pleasure.

The view of such a person is right view. Their intention is right intention, their effort is right effort, their mindfulness is right mindfulness, and their immersion is right immersion. And their actions of body and speech, and their livelihood, have already been fully purified before. So this noble eightfold path is fully developed. When the noble eightfold path is developed, the following are fully developed: the four kinds of mindfulness meditation, the four right efforts, the four bases of psychic power, the five faculties, the five powers, and the seven awakening factors. And these two qualities proceed in conjunction: serenity and discernment. They completely understand by direct knowledge those things that should be completely understood by direct knowledge. They give up by direct knowledge those things that should be given up by direct knowledge. They develop by direct knowledge those things that should be developed by direct knowledge. They realize by direct knowledge those things that should be realized by direct knowledge.

And what are the things that should be completely understood by direct knowledge? You should say: ‘The five grasping aggregates.’ That is: form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness. These are the things that should be completely understood by direct knowledge. And what are the things that should be given up by direct knowledge? Ignorance and craving for continued existence. These are the things that should be given up by direct knowledge. And what are the things that should be developed by direct knowledge? Serenity and discernment. These are the things that should be developed by direct knowledge. And what are the things that should be realized by direct knowledge? Knowledge and freedom. These are the things that should be realized by direct knowledge.

The pattern completed, for the mind

§12–18When you truly know and see the ear… nose… tongue… body… mind, ideas, mind consciousness, mind contact, and what is felt as pleasant, painful, or neutral that arises dependent on mind contact, you are not aroused by these things.

§19–26Someone who lives unaroused like this—unfettered, unconfused, concentrating on drawbacks—disperses the five grasping aggregates for themselves in the future. And their craving—which leads to future lives, mixed up with relishing and greed, taking pleasure there wherever it alights—is given up. Their physical and mental stress, torment, and fever are given up. And they experience physical and mental pleasure.

The view of such a person is right view. Their intention is right intention, their effort is right effort, their mindfulness is right mindfulness, and their immersion is right immersion. And their actions of body and speech, and their livelihood, have already been fully purified before. So this noble eightfold path is fully developed. When the noble eightfold path is developed, the following are fully developed: the four kinds of mindfulness meditation, the four right efforts, the four bases of psychic power, the five faculties, the five powers, and the seven awakening factors. And these two qualities proceed in conjunction: serenity and discernment. They completely understand by direct knowledge those things that should be completely understood by direct knowledge. They give up by direct knowledge those things that should be given up by direct knowledge. They develop by direct knowledge those things that should be developed by direct knowledge. They realize by direct knowledge those things that should be realized by direct knowledge.

What are the things that should be completely understood by direct knowledge? The five grasping aggregates—that is: form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness. These are the things that should be completely understood by direct knowledge. What are the things that should be given up by direct knowledge? Ignorance and craving for continued existence. These are the things that should be given up by direct knowledge. What are the things that should be developed by direct knowledge? Serenity and discernment. These are the things that should be developed by direct knowledge. What are the things that should be realized by direct knowledge? Knowledge and freedom. These are the things that should be realized by direct knowledge.”

That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said.

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 99

With Subha

Subhasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speakers
The Buddha and the young brahmin Subha, son of Todeyya
Form
A series of brahmin claims met by analysis, vivid simile, and a final path of practice.
Length
~18 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No full counterpart is known in the Chinese Āgamas
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — a wide-ranging dialogue ending in the boundless heart.

Why this discourse, ninety-ninth

A confident young brahmin arrives with the settled opinions of his class: that only householders, not renunciants, succeed; that the busy life is the fruitful one; that five fixed things make merit. The Buddha takes each claim apart, not by flat denial but by analysis — and when the student, stung, repeats his teacher’s sneer that no human can truly see, the Buddha answers with the unforgettable image of a man blind from birth declaring that colours do not exist.

Subha is the son of Todeyya, a wealthy brahmin of Sāvatthī, and a pupil of the famous Pokkharasāti. He comes courteously but armed with positions, and the discourse unfolds as a kind of examination — the brahmin states a doctrine, the Buddha tests it. The method itself is the first lesson: the Buddha “speaks after analysing the question, not one-sidedly” (he is, by his own description here, a vibhajja-vādin, a “speaker by distinction”). The question is never “layperson or renunciant?” but “right practice or wrong?”

As the conversation goes on it gathers nearly every theme of the whole brahmin chapter: the file of blind men from MN 95, the supports for a boundless heart, and — at the close, just as in MN 97 — the path to the realm of Brahmā through the four boundless states. Where Sāriputta taught that path to a dying man, here the Buddha teaches it to a living young seeker, with the ringing image of a master horn-blower whose note carries to every quarter. Subha goes for refuge; on the road home he meets the brahmin Jānussoṇi, who, hearing his report, bows toward the Buddha three times.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

It is right practice, not lay or renunciate status, that brings success; the brahmins’ merit-claims rest on no one who ever realised them — a file of blind men, and a teacher denying truth like a man born blind denying colour exist; and the five things they prize are in fact supports for the one thing that matters, a boundless heart, which is itself the path to the Divine.

Answering by analysis, not by sides

Subha’s first two claims are flat generalisations: only laypeople succeed in the teaching; and the busy lay life, with its “many obligations and undertakings,” is therefore the more fruitful. To both, the Buddha refuses a yes or no. “I speak after analysing the question, not one-sidedly.” Success belongs not to a class but to practice: wrong practice fails for layperson and renunciant alike, right practice succeeds for both. And busyness guarantees nothing — he sorts work into a small grid:

Farming and trade. Work of many duties (like farming) may fail and yield little, or succeed and yield much; work of few duties (like trade) likewise. The lay life is like farming — high effort, high stakes; the renunciate life is like trade — few duties, but when it succeeds, “very fruitful.”

The amount of activity is not the measure; the rightness of the practice and whether it succeeds is. This analytical refusal of one-sided answers (vibhajja-vāda) is itself a hallmark of how the Buddha teaches.

The five merit-things, and the file of blind men

Subha then lists the five things the brahmins prescribe for making merit: truth, fervour, chastity, recitation (of the Vedas), and generosity. The Buddha applies the same test he gave in MN 95: has any brahmin — living, or a teacher back seven generations, or even the ancient seers who composed the hymns — ever declared the fruit of these five “after realising it with their own insight”? No, to each. So the prescription is again “a queue of blind men, each holding the one in front: the first does not see, the middle does not see, the last does not see.” The five may be good, but no one in the chain has actually seen their result.

The man blind from birth

Here Subha loses his composure. Stung, he throws back his teacher Pokkharasāti’s scornful dogma: how could any human being possibly possess “a superhuman distinction in knowledge and vision”? It is impossible — mere empty words. The Buddha’s reply is patient and lethal. Does Pokkharasāti read the minds of all ascetics, to know none of them sees? — “He does not even know the mind of his own maid Puṇṇikā.” Then the great simile:

The man blind from birth. One who has never seen says: “There is no such thing as dark or bright, blue, yellow, red; no moon or sun; and no one who sees them. I do not know it or see it, therefore it does not exist.” Would he be right? — No. To deny what others see, on the strength of one’s own blindness, is simply false.

Pokkharasāti, the Buddha says, is exactly such a man — “blind and sightless,” veiled by the five hindrances (sensual desire, ill will, dullness, restlessness, doubt) and lost in the five sense-pleasures, “blind to their drawbacks and not knowing the escape.” It is not that higher knowledge is impossible; it is that he, in that condition, cannot have it. The denial of a possibility one has not pursued is the verdict of the blind on the seeing.

Two kinds of fire, two kinds of joy

Why can a mind clogged with sense-pleasure not see? Because its very happiness is the coarse kind. The Buddha asks: which fire is finer — one fed by grass and logs, or one that needs no fuel? The fuelled fire, of course… unless a fire could burn without fuel, which would be finer still.

  • Rapture dependent on the five sense-pleasures is like fire that must be fed with grass and logs — tied to its objects, restless, consuming.
  • Rapture apart from sensuality — the joy of the first and second absorptions, “born of seclusion” — is like a fire needing no fuel: finer, brighter, free.

The meditator’s happiness is not a duller version of the senses’ but a cleaner and higher one — and only a heart that has tasted it can see past the grass-and-log fire it once thought was the only warmth there was.

The five reframed: equipment for a boundless heart

Now the Buddha returns the brahmins’ own five things to them, transfigured. Which is most fruitful? — generosity; and giving “out of sympathy,” he draws out, makes compassion a sixth ground of merit. Where are the five found — among laypeople or renunciants? — mostly renunciants, who have the leisure for them. And then the key move: these five — truth, fervour, chastity, recitation, generosity — are not ends in themselves but “equipment of the mind” (cittassa parikkhārā) for “developing a mind free of enmity and ill will.” To be able to think “I am truthful” or “I am generous” gives joy in the teaching, and that joy is the soil in which the boundless heart grows. The whole brahmin merit-system is gently re-pointed toward loving-kindness.

The path to the Divine, taught to the living

Finally Subha asks for what he most wants: the path to “company with Divinity.” The Buddha answers without a moment’s hesitation — like a man born and raised in a village asked the road home — for “the Realized One is never slow when questioned about the realm of Divinity.” The path is the four boundless states: spreading a heart of love, then compassion, then rejoicing, then equanimity, “to one direction and a second and a third and a fourth… abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will.” And a new image for its power:

The horn-blower. “Suppose there were a powerful horn-blower: they would easily make themselves heard in all four quarters.” So when the heart’s release by love is fully developed, “any limited deeds do not remain or persist there” — the boundless overwhelms the petty, as the great horn fills every direction.

Where Sāriputta gave a dying brahmin this same path in MN 97, the Buddha now gives it to a young man at the start of his life. Subha takes refuge and departs — busy, as ever — and on the road his report so moves the brahmin Jānussoṇi that he steps down from his white chariot and bows toward the Buddha three times. (Subha will return in MN 135, asking why beings differ so widely — and receive the great teaching on action and its fruits.)

Key terms

vibhajja-vāda — “speaking by analysis.” The Buddha answers Subha’s sweeping claims “after analysing the question, not one-sidedly” — the issue is never lay versus renunciate, but right versus wrong practice.
the file of blind men — the brahmins prescribe five merit-things, yet no one — living, ancient, or in between — ever realised their fruit with their own insight. A chain of transmission with no eyes in it (as in MN 95).
the man blind from birth — one who, never having seen, declares colours and the sun do not exist. The image for a teacher who denies higher knowledge on the strength of his own blindness.
the five hindrances — sensual desire, ill will, dullness and drowsiness, restlessness and remorse, doubt. They “veil, shroud, and engulf” the mind, making the higher vision impossible for one in their grip.
fire without fuel — the joy of the absorptions, “apart from sensual pleasures,” compared to a finer fire that needs no grass or logs — cleaner and higher than the rapture tied to the senses.
cittassa parikkhārā — “equipment of the mind.” The brahmins’ five merit-things, re-pointed: not ends in themselves but supports for developing a heart free of enmity — the boundless states.
the horn-blower — the image of the boundless heart’s reach: as a strong horn-blower is heard in every quarter, so the developed heart’s-release by love overwhelms all “limited deeds.”

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The analytical answers, the file of blind men, the man born blind, the two fires, the reframing of the five, and the path to Divinity are given closely. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Layperson or renunciate?

§1–7So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove. The student Subha, Todeyya’s son, came and said: “Worthy Gotama, the brahmins say laypeople succeed in the skillful teaching, not renunciates. What do you say?” “On this I speak after analysing, not one-sidedly. I praise neither layperson nor renunciate who practises wrongly; I praise both who practise rightly. By right practice both succeed.” “The brahmins say the busy lay life, with many duties, is very fruitful, and the renunciate life, with few, is not.” “Again I analyse. Work of many duties, like farming, may fail and yield little, or succeed and yield much; work of few duties, like trade, the same. The lay life is like farming; the renunciate life is like trade — and when it succeeds, very fruitful.”

The five merit-things, and the blind men

§8–9“The brahmins prescribe five things for making merit: truth, fervour, chastity, recitation, and generosity. What do you say?” “Is there a single brahmin — or teacher back seven generations, or ancient seer who composed the hymns — who says, ‘I declare the fruit of these five after realising it with my own insight’?” “No, worthy Gotama.” “Then it is a queue of blind men, each holding the one in front: the first does not see, the middle does not see, the last does not see.”

The man blind from birth

§10–13Angered, Subha retorted with his teacher’s view: “Pokkharasāti says no human can have a superhuman distinction in knowledge and vision — it is empty words.” “Does Pokkharasāti know the minds of all ascetics?” “He does not even know the mind of his maid Puṇṇikā.” “Suppose a man blind from birth said: ‘There is no dark or bright, no blue, yellow, red, no moon or sun, and no one who sees them — I do not know it, so it does not exist.’ Would he be right?” “No — there are such things, and those who see them.” “Just so, Pokkharasāti is blind and sightless; it is impossible for him to know the superhuman.”

The hindrances, and two fires

§15–17“There are five hindrances — sensual desire, ill will, dullness and drowsiness, restlessness and remorse, doubt — and Pokkharasāti is veiled and engulfed by them, and tied to the five sense-pleasures, blind to their drawbacks. Tell me: which fire has finer flame — one fed by grass and logs, or one that needs no fuel?” “If a fire could burn without fuel, that would be finer.” “Rapture dependent on the five sense-pleasures is like the fuelled fire; rapture apart from sensuality — the first and second absorptions, born of seclusion — is like the fire that needs no fuel.”

The five reframed

§18–21“Which of the five do the brahmins say is most fruitful?” “Generosity.” And since they give “out of sympathy,” the Buddha drew out compassion as a sixth ground of merit. “Where are the five found — among laypeople or renunciates?” “Mostly renunciates, who have the leisure.” “Then I say these five are equipment of the mind for developing a heart free of enmity and ill will. One who is truthful — or fervent, chaste, learned, generous — finds joy in the teaching; and that joy is a support for the boundless heart.”

The path to Divinity

§22–27“Worthy Gotama, I hear you know the path to company with Divinity.” “If a man born and raised in a village were asked the road home, would he hesitate? Still less does the Realized One hesitate about the realm of Divinity. The path is this: a mendicant spreads a heart full of love to one direction, and a second, third, and fourth — above, below, across, everywhere — abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will. When this is developed, any limited deeds do not remain there — as a powerful horn-blower is easily heard in all four quarters. So too with compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity. This is a path to company with Divinity.”

Refuge, and Jānussoṇi’s homage

§28–31“Excellent, worthy Gotama! I go for refuge to you, the teaching, and the Saṅgha. Now I must go — I have much to do.” On the road, the brahmin Jānussoṇi, driving his all-white chariot, asked Subha where he had been. Hearing his praise of the Buddha, Jānussoṇi stepped down, raised his cupped palms toward the Buddha, and exclaimed three times: “King Pasenadi is lucky, so very lucky, that the Realized One lives in his realm!”

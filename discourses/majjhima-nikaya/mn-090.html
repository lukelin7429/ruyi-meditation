Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 90

At Kaṇṇakatthala

Kaṇṇakatthalasutta

Setting
Near Ujuññā, in the deer park at Kaṇṇakatthala
Speakers
The Buddha and King Pasenadi of Kosala, with General Viḍūḍabha and Venerable Ānanda
Form
A king’s four questions answered — on omniscience, caste and liberation, and the gods — with a deft intervention by Ānanda.
Length
~12 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 212 (Madhyama-āgama 212)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — a varied question-and-answer; its centre is that liberation is one, whatever one’s birth.

Why this discourse, ninetieth

King Pasenadi comes to the Buddha with four questions he has been turning over — a rumour about omniscience he wants checked, a query about whether caste matters for the life to come, and two about the gods. The answers, given plainly and tested with homely similes, gather several of the canon’s key themes into one conversation: knowledge is real but not simultaneous, liberation is the same for every class of person, and the difference that matters is never birth but effort and character.

The first question clears up a distortion. The king has heard that the Buddha said “no ascetic or brahmin will ever claim to know and see everything.” The Buddha says that is a misrepresentation — what he actually said was that no one will ever “know all and see all simultaneously.” One small word, dropped in the retelling, had turned a careful claim into a crude one. The Buddha’s point (as in MN 71) is not that he is ignorant, but that omniscience is not a permanent floodlight switched eternally on; knowledge is available to him as he turns to it, not all at once.

Then the heart of the discourse: does it matter, for the life to come, whether one is an aristocrat, a brahmin, a peasant, or a menial? The Buddha’s answer is one of the canon’s clearest statements of spiritual equality. In worldly precedence the classes differ; but when it comes to liberation, what counts is the five factors that support striving — faith, health, honesty, energy, and wisdom — and these belong to no caste. Anyone of any class who has them and strives rightly reaches the very same freedom, as fires kindled from different kinds of wood all burn with one identical flame.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

No one knows all and sees all at once; caste makes no difference to liberation, which is one and the same for every class of person who has the five factors of striving — faith, health, honesty, energy, and wisdom — and strives rightly, as fires of different woods all burn alike; and the gods who return to this world are those still “afflicted,” while the unafflicted do not return.

The word that was dropped

The first exchange is a small lesson in how teachings get garbled. The rumour ran that the Buddha denied anyone could ever be all-knowing; the Buddha’s actual statement denied only that anyone knows all simultaneously. The careful king does not take the rumour at face value — he asks the Buddha directly, then even tracks down who introduced the distorted version at court (a small comedy follows, as the brahmin Sañjaya and General Viḍūḍabha each blame the other). The substance matters: the Buddha is not claiming ignorance, nor disowning deep knowledge; he is refusing the crude idol of a mind permanently saturated with every fact. His knowing is functional and on-demand, turned to what is needed — the same correction he gave Vacchagotta in MN 71. A teaching loses its precision the moment a qualifying word is lost; the wise check the source.

Caste in this life and the next

When the king asks whether the four classes differ, the Buddha first answers about this world: yes, two — aristocrats and brahmins — are conventionally accorded precedence in matters of etiquette and deference. But the king sharpens his question: “I am not asking about this life, but about the life to come.” And here the Buddha’s answer changes entirely. For the spiritual goal, he does not speak of caste at all; he speaks of the five factors that support striving: faith in the Buddha’s awakening, good health, honesty (no deceit), roused energy, and the wisdom of arising and passing away. These are what decide a person’s spiritual prospects — and they are no respecter of birth. “If members of any of the four classes had these five factors, that would be for their lasting welfare.” The worldly hierarchy that ranks aristocrats and brahmins above the rest simply does not carry over to the path: there, the only ranking is by qualities anyone can cultivate.

The trained and the untrained

The king presses: if all four classes had the five factors, would there be any difference among them? The Buddha’s answer is exact: the only difference would be in “the disparity of their efforts in meditation.” He illustrates with trained and untrained animals — a pair of elephants, horses, or oxen well tamed will do the work of the tamed and reach the level of the tamed; an untrained pair will not, however fine their breed. So among people who all possess the five factors, what then distinguishes them is not their class but how hard and well they actually practise. Birth drops out entirely; training is everything. The factors give one the capacity; the effort realises it.

Fires of different woods — one flame

Finally the king asks the decisive question: if members of all four classes had the five factors and practised rightly, would there be any difference between them? The Buddha’s answer could not be clearer: “In that case, I say there is no difference between the freedom of one and the freedom of the other.” And the simile is unforgettable. Take fire kindled from teak, from sāl, from mango, from cluster-fig — is there any difference in the flame, the colour, the light? None. “In the same way, when fire has been churned by energy and produced by effort, there is no difference between the freedom of one and the freedom of the other.” The wood is the caste; the fire is liberation. However different the origins, the flame they yield is one and the same. There is not a brahmin’s liberation and a menial’s liberation, a noble freedom and a humble one — there is only freedom, identical in all, kindled by the same effort from whatever the starting material. It is among the most radical statements of equality in religious literature, and it is given to a king.

The gods who return, and Ānanda’s intervention

The king then turns to the gods. Do they “survive” — do they come back to this world? The Buddha distinguishes by inner state: gods who are “afflicted” (still carrying ill will and unrest) return; gods who are “unafflicted” do not. General Viḍūḍabha, the king’s son, asks whether the afflicted gods could topple the unafflicted from their place — and here Venerable Ānanda steps in, thinking, “Viḍūḍabha is the king’s son, and I am the Buddha’s son; now is the time for one son to confer with another.” By a neat chain of questions Ānanda shows: King Pasenadi can expel an ascetic within his own realm, but not outside it — and he cannot even see the gods of the Thirty-Three, let alone expel them. Just so, the afflicted gods cannot even perceive the unafflicted, so the idea of toppling them is empty. The point beneath the byplay is real: beings of a coarser, more troubled state cannot reach or disturb those of a purer one; the peaceful dwell, in effect, beyond the reach of the afflicted. The same answer is then given for a divinity (Brahmā): the afflicted Brahmā returns, the unafflicted does not.

A king satisfied

At the end Pasenadi sums up his own visit with a kind of receipt: “I asked about omniscience, about purification in the four classes, about the gods, and about divinities, and the Buddha answered in each case. I endorse and accept this, and am satisfied.” It is the contented close of a thoughtful man who came with real questions and got real answers. Read alongside the previous discourse (MN 89), it completes a pair of portraits of King Pasenadi in his old age — there, his reasoned devotion; here, his careful curiosity — a layman who does not merely revere the Buddha but actually thinks with him. And the central gift he carries away is the one most worth keeping: that the door to the highest freedom is open to everyone alike, barred by no birth, and that what passes through it is, in the end, the same fire in all.

Key terms

“all at once” — the dropped qualifier. The Buddha denied that anyone knows all and sees all simultaneously, not that deep knowledge is impossible. His knowing is functional and on-demand — cf. MN 71.
padhāniyaṅga — the five “factors that support striving”: faith, health, honesty, energy, and wisdom. What decides one’s spiritual prospects — and no respecter of birth (also in MN 85).
the trained and the untrained — among people who all have the five factors, what distinguishes them is “the disparity of their efforts” — like tamed versus untamed animals. Training, not breed, decides.
fires of different woods — teak, sāl, mango, fig: the flame is identical in all. So liberation is one and the same, “no difference between the freedom of one and the freedom of the other,” whatever one’s caste.
afflicted / unafflicted — gods (and a Brahmā) still carrying ill will return to this world; those without do not. The peaceful dwell beyond the reach — even the sight — of the troubled.
“one son to another” — Ānanda’s framing as he answers the king’s son Viḍūḍabha: he is “the Buddha’s son.” The king delights in the elder’s deft reply — “a joy he is, and a joy he seems!”

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The four questions, the similes, and Ānanda’s intervention are given in full. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The sisters’ greeting, and the rumour of omniscience

§1–8So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Ujuññā, in the deer park at Kaṇṇakatthala. King Pasenadi came to visit, conveying the respects of the sisters Somā and Sakulā. Then he said, “Sir, I have heard that you say: ‘No ascetic or brahmin will ever claim to know and see everything without exception — that is not possible.’ Is this rightly reported?” “Those who say this misrepresent me, great king. What I said is: ‘No ascetic or brahmin will ever know all and see all simultaneously — that is not possible.’” “That appears reasonable, sir.”

Caste, this life and the next

§9–10“Sir, there are these four classes — aristocrats, brahmins, peasants, and menials. Is there any difference between them?” “In worldly precedence, two are deemed preeminent — aristocrats and brahmins — in bowing, rising, and etiquette.” “Sir, I am not asking about this life, but about the life to come.” “Great king, there are five factors that support striving: faith in the Realized One’s awakening; good health and digestion; honesty, free of deceit; roused, unflagging energy; and the wisdom of arising and passing away. If members of any of the four classes had these five factors, that would be for their lasting welfare and happiness.”

The trained, and the one flame

§11–12“And if all four classes had the five factors — would there be a difference?” “Then I say it is the disparity of their efforts. As a pair of well-tamed elephants, horses, or oxen do the work of the tamed, while an untrained pair cannot, so what is to be attained by one of faith, health, integrity, energy, and wisdom cannot be attained by the faithless, sickly, deceitful, lazy, and witless.” “And if all four had the five factors and practised rightly — would there be a difference?” “In that case, I say there is no difference between the freedom of one and the freedom of the other. As fire kindled from teak, sāl, mango, or cluster-fig is identical in flame, colour, and light, so when fire has been churned by energy and produced by effort, there is no difference between the freedom of one and the freedom of the other.”

The gods who return

§13–15“Sir, do gods survive?” “What are you asking?” “Whether they come back to this world.” “Gods who are afflicted come back; those unafflicted do not.” General Viḍūḍabha asked, “Can the afflicted gods topple the unafflicted from their place?” Then Ānanda, thinking “one son should confer with another,” replied: “General, King Pasenadi can expel an ascetic within his realm, but not beyond it — and he cannot even see the gods of the Thirty-Three, let alone expel them. Just so, the afflicted gods cannot even see the unafflicted, so how could they topple them?” “What is this venerable’s name?” asked the king. “Ānanda, great king.” “A joy he is, and a joy he seems! And sir, does a divinity survive?” “An afflicted Brahmā comes back to this world; an unafflicted one does not.”

A king satisfied

§17–18“Sir, I asked you about omniscience, about purification in the four classes, about the gods, and about divinities, and you answered in each case. I endorse and accept this, and am satisfied. Well, now, sir, I must go; I have much to do.” “Please, great king, go at your convenience.” And the king rose, bowed, and departed.

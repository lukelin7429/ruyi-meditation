Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 72

With Vacchagotta on Fire

Aggivacchagottasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speakers
The Buddha and the wanderer Vacchagotta, now pressing the great metaphysical questions
Form
A philosophical dialogue resolved by a simile — the Buddha refuses the ten speculative views, denies holding any theory at all, and meets Vacchagotta’s confusion with the image of a fire that goes out.
Length
~12 minutes to read
Northern parallels
SĀ 962 and SĀ² 196 (two Saṁyukta-āgama recensions)
Difficulty
★★★★★ — among the most subtle in the canon; the rejection of the ten views and the fire simile reward slow, careful reading.

Why this discourse, seventy-second

Is the universe eternal? Does an awakened one exist after death, or not, or both, or neither? Vacchagotta lays ten of the age’s great questions before the Buddha, and to every one receives the same reply: “That’s not my view.” What follows is one of the most penetrating exchanges in all the discourses — and the source of the most famous image in early Buddhism: the fire that, when its fuel is gone, cannot be said to have gone anywhere at all.

Vacchagotta returns from MN 71 with a little faith and a great deal of curiosity. He wants the Buddha placed on the map of contemporary opinion: surely a teacher of such standing holds some position on whether the cosmos is eternal, whether soul and body are one, whether a realized one survives death. Ten times he offers a view; ten times the Buddha declines it. These are the famous “undeclared points” (avyākata) — questions the Buddha consistently set aside, not because he lacked an answer, but because every available answer was a trap.

The Buddha then says something more radical than any refusal: a realized one has no theories at all. He has not traded one speculative view for a better one; he has set down the whole enterprise of view-making, because he has seen — directly — how the five aggregates arise and cease. When Vacchagotta, baffled, asks where such a liberated person is reborn, the Buddha answers with the fire: a flame burns dependent on its fuel, and when the fuel is exhausted, to ask “which way did it go?” is to mistake the very nature of what a fire is. So too with one freed from the aggregates — “deep, immeasurable, hard to fathom, like the ocean.”

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Buddha holds none of the ten speculative views — each is a thicket that breeds suffering and leads nowhere — because a realized one has set down theorising altogether, having seen how the aggregates arise and cease; and just as an extinguished fire cannot be said to have gone east or west, so a liberated one, freed from being reckoned in terms of the aggregates, cannot be caught by “reborn,” “not reborn,” “both,” or “neither.”

The ten undeclared points

Vacchagotta’s ten questions are the standard set of speculative views the Buddha left unanswered. They fall into three clusters:

ClusterThe views offered
The cosmoseternal · not eternal · finite · infinite
Soul and bodythe soul and the body are the same · the soul is one thing, the body another
A realized one after deathstill exists · no longer exists · both exists and not · neither exists nor not

Each is offered in the dogmatist’s form: “This is the only truth, anything else is futile.” That little tail matters — it is the demand that one absolutize a position and brand all others false. To all ten the Buddha says only: “That’s not my view, Vaccha.”

Why he sets them all aside

Vacchagotta presses: what drawback do you see, that you avoid every one of these? The answer is unsparing. Each conviction is “a thicket of views, a desert of views, a twist of views, a dodge of views, a fetter of views” — tangled, barren, evasive, binding. They are “beset with suffering, distress, anguish, and fever,” and — the decisive point — “they don’t lead to disillusionment, dispassion, cessation, peace, insight, awakening, and extinguishment.” The objection is not merely that the questions are unanswerable; it is that holding any answer actively entangles the mind and does nothing for liberation. The Buddha’s silence is not evasion but surgery — he declines to hand a sick man a more elegant disease.

A realized one has no theories

Vacchagotta, reasonably, asks: “But do you have any conviction at all?” The reply turns the whole conversation: “A realized one has done away with convictions.” This is the heart of the discourse. The Buddha has not replaced bad views with a correct view of the same kind; he has stepped out of the business of speculative views (diṭṭhigata, “a position one resorts to”) entirely. What he has instead is not a theory but a seeing: “Such is form, such is its origin, such is its ending” — and so for feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness. His “right view” is right precisely because it is grounded in what has been seen, not constructed in thought. From that seeing comes freedom — “the ending… of all conceiving, all churning, and all I-making, mine-making, and underlying tendency to conceit.”

The four that “do not apply”

Now the crux. Where, asks Vacchagotta, is a mind freed like this reborn? “‘They’re reborn’ doesn’t apply, Vaccha.” Not reborn? Doesn’t apply. Both? Neither? None of the four applies. This is not a fifth, secret answer being withheld. The point is that the question is malformed. “Reborn / not reborn / both / neither” all assume there is a fixed entity — a self, a thing — whose fate after death we are tracking. But that assumption is exactly what liberation has dissolved. To ask the question is like asking what colour the number seven is: the terms simply do not fit the case. Vacchagotta is honest enough to admit he is lost: “I’ve fallen into confusion, and lost even the clarity I had before.”

The fire

Here the Buddha does what a good teacher does when a pupil is out of his depth: he reaches for something ordinary. A fire burns in front of you — you can see it. What does it burn in dependence on? Grass and logs. Now suppose it goes out. In which direction did it go — east, south, west, north? “It doesn’t apply,” Vaccha answers at once. “The fire depended on fuel; when the fuel runs out and none is added, it is reckoned ‘quenched.’” He has solved the riddle himself. A fire is not a thing that travels; it is a process that burns while it is fed and ceases when it is not. “Where did it go?” mistakes the process for an object. The vocabulary is quietly loaded: the fire burns “in dependence on” (paṭicca) its “fuel” (upādāna — the very word for “grasping”), and to go out is nibbāyati, the verb at the root of Nibbāna. The simile is the doctrine.

What the simile says — and guards against

Applied: “Any form by which a realized one might be described has been given up, cut off at the root, made like a palm stump… A realized one is freed from reckoning in terms of form” — and likewise feeling, perception, choices, consciousness. Freed from the five things by which a person is measured and named, the realized one is “deep, immeasurable, hard to fathom, like the ocean,” and the four propositions about post-mortem fate find nothing to grip. Crucially, the fire image cuts both ways. It is not annihilation: the flame was never a thing that got destroyed, so its ceasing is not the snuffing-out of some entity. Nor is it survival: nothing persists to be tracked “east or west.” The Buddha walks the razor between the eternalist’s “he still exists” and the annihilationist’s “he is destroyed,” and the fire shows why both miss: each presupposes the self that liberation has already let go.

Vacchagotta’s turning

The dialogue ends not in further argument but in an image of Vacchagotta’s own — a sign that something has landed. The Buddha’s teaching, he says, is like a great sal tree that has shed every loose branch, twig, and strip of bark, until only the pure heartwood remains. From doubt he moves to devotion and goes for refuge. (The detail sits a little oddly — he is already a wanderer, and the very next discourse, MN 73, narrates his going-forth afresh — but the trajectory is plain: across the trilogy, curiosity ripens into faith, and faith into the resolve to practise.) The progression is itself a teaching: the deepest points are not grasped by being told, but grow clear only when one stops demanding a position and begins to look.

Key terms

avyākata — “undeclared,” the set of speculative questions the Buddha consistently set aside (here ten; elsewhere fourteen). Not unanswerable trivia but views whose very holding entangles the mind.
diṭṭhigata — a “view one resorts to,” a speculative position or theory. A realized one has “done away with” these — not by adopting a better theory, but by grounding sight in what is actually seen (diṭṭha).
papañca / maññita — conceptual proliferation and “conceiving”: the mind’s spinning of positions and self-references. Liberation is the ending of all conceiving, “churning,” and I-making.
upādāna — “fuel” and also “grasping.” In the simile the fire burns by upādāna (fuel); in the teaching, existence is sustained by upādāna (clinging). The pun is the point.
nibbāyati — “to be extinguished,” the verb behind Nibbāna. A fire “nibbāna-s” when its fuel is spent; awakening is the going-out of the fires of greed, hate, and delusion.
tathāgata — “realized one.” Here it covers any arahant, not only the Buddha; the question is what can be said of anyone so freed. Such a one is “freed from reckoning,” immeasurable as the ocean.

The text

The full discourse. The ten questions and the four “doesn’t apply” replies are given in their pattern; the aggregate-by-aggregate repetition of the fire’s application is given once and condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The ten questions

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. Then the wanderer Vacchagotta went up to the Buddha, exchanged greetings, sat down to one side, and said:

§3–12“Worthy Gotama, is this your view: ‘The cosmos is eternal. This is the only truth, anything else is futile’?” “That’s not my view, Vaccha.” “Then is this your view: ‘The cosmos is not eternal’?” “That’s not my view, Vaccha.”

Vacchagotta runs through all ten in turn, and to each the Buddha replies, “That’s not my view.” Is the cosmos eternal, or not eternal; finite, or infinite? Are the soul and the body the same, or different? Does a realized one, after death, still exist; or no longer exist; or both; or neither? Ten views are offered, each in the form “this alone is true, all else is futile” — and the Buddha owns none of them.

The drawback in every view

§13–14“Worthy Gotama, seeing what drawback do you avoid all these convictions?” “Each of these ten convictions is a thicket of views, a desert of views, a twist of views, a dodge of views, a fetter of views. They’re beset with suffering, distress, anguish, and fever. They don’t lead to disillusionment, dispassion, cessation, peace, insight, awakening, and extinguishment. Seeing this drawback I avoid all these convictions.”

A realized one has done away with convictions

§15“But does the worthy Gotama have any convictions at all?” “A realized one has done away with convictions. For a realized one has seen: ‘Such is form, such is the origin of form, such is the ending of form; such is feeling… perception… choices… consciousness, such is its origin, such is its ending.’ That’s why a realized one is freed with the ending, fading away, cessation, giving up, and letting go of all conceiving, all churning, and all I-making, mine-making, and underlying tendency to conceit, I say.”

Where is the liberated one reborn?

§16–17“But worthy Gotama, when a mendicant’s mind is freed like this, where are they reborn?” “‘They’re reborn’ doesn’t apply, Vaccha.” “Then are they not reborn?” “‘They’re not reborn’ doesn’t apply.” “Both reborn and not reborn?” “Doesn’t apply.” “Neither reborn nor not reborn?” “Doesn’t apply, Vaccha.” “Here I’ve fallen into confusion, worthy Gotama, and lost even the clarity I had from our earlier talks.”

The fire

§18–19“No wonder you’re confused, Vaccha. This principle is deep, hard to see, hard to understand, peaceful, sublime, beyond the scope of logic, subtle, comprehensible to the astute. Let me ask you something in return. Suppose a fire was burning in front of you. Would you know, ‘This fire is burning in front of me’?” “Yes.” “And if asked, ‘What does it burn in dependence on?’” “‘It burns in dependence on grass and logs as fuel.’” “And if it went out, would you know it?” “Yes.” “And if asked, ‘In which direction did the fire go — east, south, west, or north?’” “It doesn’t apply, worthy Gotama. The fire burned in dependence on its fuel of grass and logs. When that runs out and no more is added, it is reckoned to have been quenched for lack of fuel.”

Like the ocean

§20“In the same way, Vaccha, any form by which a realized one might be described has been given up, cut off at the root, made like a palm stump, obliterated, unable to arise in the future. A realized one is freed from reckoning in terms of form. They’re deep, immeasurable, and hard to fathom, like the ocean. ‘They’re reborn,’ ‘they’re not reborn,’ ‘both,’ ‘neither’ — none of these apply.” The same is said of feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness: each, as a basis for describing a realized one, has been cut off at the root, and the realized one is freed from being reckoned by it — deep and immeasurable as the ocean.

The sal tree, and the refuge

§21–22“Worthy Gotama, suppose there was a great sal tree near a village. Being impermanent, in time it would shed its branches and foliage, bark and shoots and softwood, until it stood pure, consolidated in the heartwood alone. In the same way, the worthy Gotama’s dispensation is rid of branches and foliage, bark and shoots and softwood — pure, consolidated in the core. Excellent, worthy Gotama! From this day forth, may the worthy Gotama remember me as one gone for refuge for life.”

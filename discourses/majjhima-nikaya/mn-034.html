Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 34

The Shorter Discourse on the Cowherd

Cūḷagopālakasutta

Setting
Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speaker
The Buddha, addressing the monks
Form
A narrative parable — two cowherds try to cross the Ganges; the foolish one fails, the wise one succeeds by ordering the crossing carefully. The Buddha maps the crossing to how beings cross the flood of saṃsāra.
Length
~8 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 186 (Madhyama-āgama 186)
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — a vivid narrative requiring no technical background. The parable speaks for itself; the application maps naturally to the four stages of awakening.

Why this discourse, thirty-fourth

Two Magadhan cowherds try to cross their herds over the Ganges during the monsoon. The foolish one drives his cattle in at a random spot — the herd panics mid-stream and is destroyed. The wise one surveys the river, finds the right ford, and sends the herd across in careful order: the great bulls first, then the strong trained cattle, then the young heifers, and finally the calves — even the newborn, following its mother’s lowing, crosses safely.

The parable maps to the Buddhist community crossing the flood of saṃsāra. Arahants go first and break the current of Māra; non-returners, once-returners, and stream-enterers follow; and even Dhamma-followers and faith-followers — the newest practitioners — cross safely by following the way opened by those ahead of them. The discourse is one of the canon’s most elegant images of spiritual community: the strong do not merely save themselves but open a path for the weak.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Those who have gone furthest in the practice — the arahants — cross the flood of saṃsāra first and break the current of Māra; each subsequent group crosses in the path they have opened, until even the newest practitioners cross safely by following the way ahead of them.

The five groups at the crossing

The Buddha maps the cowherd’s ordered crossing to five groups within the Buddhist community. The correspondence is precise:

The great bulls — fathers and leaders of the herdArahants — those who have destroyed the taintsThey are the strongest; they break the current and create a wake for others to follow. Their crossing is complete — they will not return.
The strong trained cattleNon-returners (anāgāmī)Strong enough to cross without being swept back; they will not return to the sense-desire realm.
The young heifers and steersOnce-returners (sakadāgāmī)Still young in the practice but capable; they will return to this world at most one more time.
The calvesStream-enterers (sotāpanna)The youngest who can swim on their own; they have entered the stream and will reach the far shore within seven lives.
The newborn calf, following its mother’s lowingDhamma-followers and faith-followersThe newest practitioners, not yet stream-enterers, who follow the teaching or their faith. Even they cross safely — carried by the current opened ahead and pulled by the call of the Dhamma.

The image of the newborn calf

The most moving detail in the discourse is the newborn calf. It is too weak to swim on its own. But it follows the sound of its mother’s lowing — and the current has already been broken by the bulls. The combination is enough: it crosses safely. The Buddha maps this to faith-followers and Dhamma-followers, practitioners who have not yet entered the stream but are heading in the right direction. The image says: you do not need to be strong enough on your own. If the community is ordered rightly and the teaching is alive, even the weakest can cross.

The foolish cowherd’s mistake

The foolish cowherd’s error is not that he tried to cross the Ganges — it is that he crossed at the wrong place and without order. He drove the cattle in all at once, at a random spot, during the monsoon. The result: the cattle in the middle panicked, the current overwhelmed them, and they came to ruin. The Buddha does not spell out the application in detail, but the implication is clear: a community without order, without the strong going first and the weak following, will be destroyed by the flood. Spiritual practice without structure is not humility — it is negligence.

Relation to MN 33

MN 33 (The Greater Discourse on the Cowherd) and MN 34 form a pair, but they are very different in method. MN 33 is systematic: a point-by-point analogy mapping eleven cowherd skills to eleven monastic qualities. MN 34 is narrative: a story about a river crossing that becomes a parable of liberation. MN 33 describes the individual practitioner’s qualities; MN 34 describes the community’s order. Together they give a complete picture: the individual must have the right qualities (MN 33), and the community must be rightly ordered (MN 34).

Three questions Western students often ask

“Is this a hierarchy?” Yes, but not a hierarchy of authority — a hierarchy of capacity. The bulls go first not because they deserve to but because they are the only ones strong enough to break the current. The ordering is functional, not social. An arahant does not command the stream-enterer; an arahant simply crosses first, and the crossing itself makes the river safer for everyone behind. Hierarchy here is not about power but about the physics of crossing a flood.

“What happens to the foolish cowherd’s cattle?” They are destroyed mid-stream. The discourse uses the phrase “came to calamity and disaster.” The Buddha does not soften the image. The point is stark: without right order, the flood wins. The Ganges during the monsoon is not forgiving, and neither is saṃsāra.

“Can this discourse be read as a teaching about leadership?” Absolutely. The wise cowherd is a model of responsible leadership: he surveys the situation, finds the right crossing, and sends the strongest first to protect the weakest. He does not drive the herd in and hope for the best. The application to monastic communities — and to any community — is direct: the experienced go first, not for privilege but for the safety of those who follow.

Key terms

ogha — flood; torrent. The metaphor for saṃsāra — the cycle of birth and death that must be crossed. The Ganges during the monsoon is the literal image; the flood of defilements is the meaning.
tittha — ford; crossing place. The same word used in MN 33 for “knowing the ford.” Here it is the right place to cross the river — the place where the current is manageable and the far bank is reachable. Spiritually: the right approach to the practice.
arahant / khīṇāsava — one who has destroyed the taints; a fully awakened being. The “great bulls” who cross first. Their crossing is irreversible — they will not return.
anāgāmī — non-returner. The third stage of awakening. They will not return to the sense-desire realm but will attain final liberation in a higher realm. The “strong trained cattle.”
sakadāgāmī — once-returner. The second stage of awakening. They will return to this world at most one more time. The “young heifers and steers.”
sotāpanna — stream-enterer. The first stage of awakening. They have entered the stream leading to liberation and will reach the far shore within seven lives. The “calves.”
dhammānusārī — Dhamma-follower. One who follows the teaching through understanding but has not yet attained stream-entry. Together with the faith-follower, these are the “newborn calves.”
saddhānusārī — faith-follower. One who follows through confidence and trust in the teaching, not yet a stream-enterer. The newest calf, following its mother’s voice.
Mārassa dheyya — Māra’s domain; the realm of the Tempter. The river that must be crossed. The discourse says that all five groups cross “the stream of Māra” — meaning they pass beyond the reach of death and defilement.

The text

MN 34 is a short narrative discourse built around a single vivid parable. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The foolish cowherd

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery.

§2There the Buddha addressed the monks: “Monks!” “Venerable sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

“Once upon a time, monks, a foolish, incompetent, unskilled Magadhan cowherd, in the last month of the rainy season, at harvest time, without inspecting the near bank or the far bank of the river Ganges, drove his cattle across to the north bank in the land of the Suvidehans at a place where there was no ford.

§3Then the cattle, bunching together in the middle of the stream of the Ganges, came to calamity and disaster. Why is that? Because that foolish, incompetent, unskilled Magadhan cowherd, in the last month of the rainy season, at harvest time, without inspecting the near bank or the far bank, drove his cattle across at a place where there was no ford.

§4In the same way, monks, certain ascetics and brahmins who are unskilled in this world and the other world, unskilled in Māra’s realm and what is outside Māra’s realm, unskilled in Death’s realm and what is outside Death’s realm — those who think it worthwhile to listen to them and place faith in them: that will be for their lasting harm and suffering.”

The wise cowherd

§5“Once upon a time, monks, a wise, competent, skilled Magadhan cowherd, in the last month of the rainy season, at harvest time, having inspected the near bank and the far bank of the river Ganges, drove his cattle across to the north bank in the land of the Suvidehans at a place where there was a ford.

§6He first drove across the bulls who were the fathers and leaders of the herd. They cut across the stream of the Ganges and got safely across.

He next drove across the strong trained cattle. They too cut across the stream and got safely across.

He next drove across the young heifers and steers. They too cut across the stream and got safely across.

He next drove across the young calves. They too cut across the stream and got safely across.

§7At that time, there was a tender calf, just born, who was driven on by its mother’s lowing. Even it cut across the stream of the Ganges and got safely across. Why is that? Because that wise, competent, skilled Magadhan cowherd, in the last month of the rainy season, at harvest time, having inspected the near bank and the far bank, drove his cattle across at a place where there was a ford.

The application: crossing Māra’s stream

§8In the same way, monks, those ascetics and brahmins who are skilled in this world and the other world, skilled in Māra’s realm and what is outside Māra’s realm, skilled in Death’s realm and what is outside Death’s realm — those who think it worthwhile to listen to them and place faith in them: that will be for their lasting welfare and happiness.

§9Just like the bulls who were the fathers and leaders of the herd, who cut across the stream of the Ganges and got safely across: in the same way, those monks who are arahants, who have destroyed the taints, who have lived the spiritual life, done what had to be done, laid down the burden, achieved their own true goal, utterly destroyed the fetter of continued existence, and are freed by right knowledge — they have cut across the stream of Māra and got safely across.

§10Just like the strong trained cattle: in the same way, those monks who, with the ending of the five lower fetters, are reborn spontaneously and will become fully extinguished there, not liable to return from that world — they too have cut across the stream of Māra and got safely across.

§11Just like the young heifers and steers: in the same way, those monks who, with the ending of three fetters and the weakening of greed, hatred, and delusion, are once-returners, coming back to this world once more before making an end of suffering — they too have cut across the stream of Māra and got safely across.

§12Just like the young calves: in the same way, those monks who, with the ending of three fetters, are stream-enterers, not liable to be reborn in the lower realms, fixed in destiny, with awakening as their destination — they too have cut across the stream of Māra and got safely across.

§13Just like the tender calf, just born, driven on by its mother’s lowing: in the same way, those monks who are Dhamma-followers or faith-followers — they too are crossing the stream of Māra and will get safely across.”

Note the shift in tense. For the four noble stages (arahant through stream-enterer), the Buddha says they “have cut across” — past tense, accomplished. For the Dhamma-followers and faith-followers, he says they “are crossing” and “will get safely across” — present and future. They are in the river, not yet on the far bank, but they will arrive.

§14That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the monks approved what the Buddha said.

Self-check quiz

Ten questions. Click an answer to see immediate feedback. No score is recorded — this is for your own checking.

Question 1 of 10
What is the foolish cowherd’s central mistake?
Correct: D. The foolish cowherd fails on every count: wrong time (monsoon), wrong place (no ford), no preparation (didn’t inspect the banks). The result is that the cattle bunch up in the middle of the stream and come to calamity. The error is not recklessness but incompetence — he simply did not do the work of preparation.
Question 2 of 10
Who crosses the Ganges first in the wise cowherd’s ordering?
Correct: A. The bulls go first because they are the strongest and can break the current. Their crossing makes the river safer for every group that follows. The ordering is functional: strength goes first not for privilege but for the protection of the weak.
Question 3 of 10
What do the great bulls correspond to in the Buddha’s application?
Correct: C. The arahants are the bulls — the strongest practitioners who cross first and break the current of Māra. Their liberation is complete and irreversible. By crossing first, they demonstrate that the far shore is reachable and make the crossing safer for those behind.
Question 4 of 10
What do the young calves correspond to?
Correct: B. The calves are the stream-enterers — the youngest who can swim on their own. They have entered the stream of the Dhamma and are guaranteed to reach the far shore within seven lives at most. They are still young in the practice but no longer in danger of falling back into the lower realms.
Question 5 of 10
What is remarkable about the newborn calf in the parable?
Correct: D. The newborn calf is too weak to swim on its own, but two forces carry it: the current broken by the bulls, and the sound of its mother’s voice pulling it forward. The image is of the newest practitioner — a Dhamma-follower or faith-follower — who is carried by the community’s practice and the call of the teaching.
Question 6 of 10
What are Dhamma-followers and faith-followers?
Correct: A. Dhammānusārī (Dhamma-followers) and saddhānusārī (faith-followers) are practitioners on the verge of stream-entry. One follows through intellectual understanding of the Dhamma; the other follows through faith and confidence. Both are in the river — not yet on the far bank, but heading there.
Question 7 of 10
How does the tense shift between the four noble stages and the Dhamma-followers / faith-followers?
Correct: C. This is a subtle but crucial detail. The arahants, non-returners, once-returners, and stream-enterers have already crossed — their liberation is accomplished or assured. The Dhamma-followers and faith-followers are still in the water, but the Buddha guarantees they will arrive. The tense shift marks the difference between attainment and trajectory.
Question 8 of 10
What does the “stream of Māra” represent?
Correct: B. Māra’s stream is saṃsāra itself — the flood of birth, aging, death, and the defilements that drive the cycle. To cross it is to attain liberation. The Ganges in monsoon is the physical image; Māra’s domain is the spiritual reality.
Question 9 of 10
What is the wise cowherd’s key preparation before the crossing?
Correct: A. The wise cowherd does what the foolish one did not: he surveys both banks and finds the right ford. The preparation is practical wisdom — not heroism, not luck, but careful assessment. The spiritual parallel: a skilled teacher surveys the situation (this world and the other, Māra’s realm and beyond) before leading others.
Question 10 of 10
How does MN 34 complement MN 33?
Correct: C. MN 33 is about the practitioner’s personal toolkit — eleven qualities needed for growth. MN 34 is about the community’s structure — how different levels of attainment support each other during the crossing of saṃsāra. Individual competence (MN 33) and communal order (MN 34) are both necessary.

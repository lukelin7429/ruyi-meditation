Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 53

A Trainee

Sekhasutta

Setting
Kapilavatthu, in the Banyan Tree Monastery; the teaching is given in the Sakyans’ newly built town hall
Speaker
Venerable Ānanda, at the Buddha’s request, to Mahānāma and the Sakyans
Form
A structured teaching — the complete curriculum of the “practicing trainee,” assembling the factors of conduct that ripen, like a hen’s eggs, into the three knowledges.
Length
~13 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No full Āgama parallel; the component formulas (sense-restraint, the seven qualities, the three knowledges) recur across many discourses, e.g. MN 27, MN 107, DN 3
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — a clear, systematic map. Its value is in the architecture: how the many factors of conduct fit together and mature into knowledge.

Why this discourse, fifty-third

One of the Buddha’s standard epithets is “accomplished in knowledge and conduct” (vijjā-caraṇa-sampanna). MN 53 is the discourse that unpacks what those two words contain — not for a Buddha, but for an ordinary disciple still in training. It is the definitive map of the sekha-paṭipadā, the trainee’s path.

The framing is humanly memorable. The Buddha has come to inaugurate a new town hall for his own people, the Sakyans of Kapilavatthu, and has taught late into the night. Then, his back sore, he asks Ānanda to take over and lies down to rest in the lion’s posture. So the great systematic statement of the trainee’s path comes from Ānanda — the disciple of perfect memory — while the Buddha listens, and at the end rises to say simply, “Good, good, Ānanda.”

What Ānanda lays out is an architecture in two storeys. The lower storey is conduct: ethics, guarding the senses, moderation in eating, wakefulness, then seven inner qualities — faith, conscience, prudence, learning, energy, mindfulness, wisdom — and finally the four absorptions. The upper storey is knowledge: the three great realizations of past lives, the passing-away and rebirth of beings, and the ending of the defilements. And the hinge between them is one of the canon’s gentlest similes: a hen who simply sits on her eggs, keeping them warm, need never wish for her chicks to hatch — if she has done the warming, they will break out by themselves. So too, the disciple who builds the conduct need not anxiously crave the result; knowledge ripens of its own accord.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The disciple in training is one accomplished in conduct — ethics, sense-restraint, moderation in eating, wakefulness, seven good qualities, and the four absorptions — and this conduct, like a hen properly warming her eggs, ripens of itself into knowledge: the three “breakings-out” of recollection of past lives, the divine eye, and liberation.

The setting, and why Ānanda speaks

The Sakyans have built a new assembly hall and ask the Buddha to be the first to use it, “for the lasting welfare and happiness” of the town. He comes, sits against the central pillar, and teaches deep into the night. Then he does something quietly human: “Ānanda, speak about the practicing trainee… My back is sore, I’ll stretch it.” He lies down in the lion’s posture — the same posture the disciple is taught to sleep in — and Ānanda delivers the discourse while the Buddha rests. At the end the Buddha rises and approves it entirely. The teaching is thus doubly authoritative: spoken by the disciple who remembered everything, and ratified by the Teacher himself.

Conduct, storey one: the foundation

Ānanda opens with four practices that prepare body and mind — the disciplines of daily life before formal meditation even begins:

  • Ethics (sīla) — restrained within the training rules, “seeing danger in the slightest fault.”
  • Guarding the sense doors — not getting caught in the “features and details” of what is seen, heard, smelled, tasted, touched, or thought.
  • Moderation in eating — reflecting that food is taken “not for fun, indulgence, adornment… but only to sustain this body… and to support spiritual practice.”
  • Dedication to wakefulness — meditating through the day and the watches of the night, sleeping only in the middle watch.

The seven good qualities

On that foundation stand seven inner strengths — the satta saddhammā, the “seven good qualities” of a person of integrity:

QualityWhat it is
FaithConfidence in the Realized One’s awakening.
Conscience (hiri)Inner shame at bad conduct of body, speech, and mind.
Prudence (ottappa)A wise fear of the consequences of wrongdoing.
LearningMuch heard and remembered, scrutinized and understood.
EnergyStrong, staunch effort to drop the unskillful and develop the skillful.
MindfulnessAble to recall and hold in mind what was long ago said and done.
WisdomThe penetrating insight into “arising and passing away” that leads to the end of suffering.

The pair of hiri and ottappa — conscience and prudence — the Buddha elsewhere calls the “guardians of the world,” the two qualities that keep human life from collapsing into chaos. Here they sit, unglamorously, near the start of the inner list: before concentration, before wisdom, a person needs the simple decency that recoils from harm.

Conduct, storey two: the four absorptions

The factors of conduct culminate in the four jhānas — here described as “blissful meditations in this life that belong to the higher mind,” which the accomplished trainee can enter “when they want, without trouble or difficulty.” Together, these fifteen factors — the four preliminary disciplines, the seven qualities, and the four absorptions — make up the whole of what the tradition calls caraṇa, “conduct.” They are the entire ground a trainee stands on.

The hen and her eggs

Then comes the discourse’s signature image. A disciple who has assembled all of this is like a hen with eight, ten, or twelve eggs, who has “properly sat on them to keep them warm.” She does not need to wish, “may my chicks hatch safely!” — if she has done the incubating, the chicks will break out of the shell by themselves. The simile makes a precise point about effort and result. The trainee’s job is the warming — the patient, daily building of conduct. Awakening is the hatching, and it cannot be forced by craving for it; it ripens naturally when the conditions are complete. Diligence in the cause, not anxiety about the fruit, is what the path asks.

Knowledge: the three breakings-out

“Relying on this supreme purity of mindfulness and equanimity” — the fruit of the fourth absorption — the trainee’s knowledge unfolds in three stages, each explicitly called a “breaking out, like a chick from an eggshell”:

  1. Recollection of past lives — remembering countless former existences with their details.
  2. The divine eye — seeing beings pass away and be reborn according to their deeds.
  3. The ending of the defilements — realizing “the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom in this very life.”

These three are the vijjā, “knowledge,” that completes the disciple. Conduct (caraṇa) and knowledge (vijjā) together make one who is “accomplished in knowledge and conduct” — the very phrase that names the Buddha. MN 53 shows that the same architecture describes the path of every disciple who follows him.

Knowledge and conduct surpass birth

Ānanda closes by recalling a verse of the divinity Sanaṅkumāra, which the Buddha himself approved: “The aristocrat is best among people who take clan as the standard; but one accomplished in knowledge and conduct is best among gods and humans.” Spoken to the Sakyans — a proud warrior-aristocrat clan, the Buddha’s own people — the point lands precisely. Birth and lineage may settle rank in the eyes of those who measure by clan; but the only excellence that counts in the largest reckoning, across gods and humans alike, is the excellence built by training. What you are born is given; what you become through conduct and knowledge is earned.

Key terms

sekha — a trainee; one who has entered the path (from stream-entry onward) but has not yet completed it. The sekha-paṭipadā, the “trainee’s practice,” is the subject of this discourse.
caraṇa — conduct; the fifteen factors that form the ground of the path: ethics, sense-restraint, moderation in eating, wakefulness, the seven good qualities, and the four absorptions.
vijjā — knowledge; the three realizations that crown the path — recollection of past lives, the divine eye, and the ending of the defilements. With caraṇa it forms vijjā-caraṇa-sampanna, “accomplished in knowledge and conduct.”
satta saddhammā — the seven good qualities: faith, conscience, prudence, learning, energy, mindfulness, and wisdom. The inner strengths of one in training.
hiri & ottappa — conscience and prudence; inner shame at wrongdoing and wise fear of its results. The Buddha calls them the “guardians of the world” — the decency that protects human life.
jāgariyānuyoga — dedication to wakefulness; the practice of meditating through the day and the first and last watches of the night, resting only in the middle watch.

The text

The full discourse, spoken by Venerable Ānanda at the Buddha’s request. The repeated sense-faculties and the four absorptions are abbreviated with “…” as in the source. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The new town hall

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the land of the Sakyans, near Kapilavatthu in the Banyan Tree Monastery.

§2Now at that time a new town hall had recently been constructed for the Sakyans of Kapilavatthu. It had not yet been occupied by an ascetic or brahmin or any human at all. Then the Sakyans of Kapilavatthu went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and said to him: “Sir, a new town hall has recently been constructed for the Sakyans of Kapilavatthu… May the Buddha be the first to use it, and only then will the Sakyans use it. That would be for the lasting welfare and happiness of the Sakyans of Kapilavatthu.”

§3The Buddha consented with silence. Then, knowing that the Buddha had consented, the Sakyans went to the new town hall, where they spread carpets all over, prepared seats, set up a water jar, and placed an oil lamp. Then they went back to the Buddha and told him, saying, “Please, sir, come at your convenience.”

§4Then the Buddha robed up and, taking his bowl and robe, went to the new town hall together with the Saṅgha of mendicants. Having washed his feet he entered the town hall and sat against the central column facing east. The Saṅgha sat against the west wall facing east, with the Buddha in front of them; and the Sakyans sat against the east wall facing west, with the Buddha in front of them.

§5The Buddha spent much of the night educating, encouraging, firing up, and inspiring the Sakyans with a Dhamma talk. Then he addressed Venerable Ānanda, “Ānanda, speak about the practicing trainee to the Sakyans of Kapilavatthu as you feel inspired. My back is sore, I’ll stretch it.” “Yes, sir,” Ānanda replied. And then the Buddha spread out his outer robe folded in four and laid down in the lion’s posture—on the right side, placing one foot on top of the other—mindful and aware, and focused on the time of getting up.

The trainee’s conduct

§6Then Ānanda addressed Mahānāma the Sakyan: “Mahānāma, a noble disciple is accomplished in ethics, guards the sense doors, eats in moderation, and is dedicated to wakefulness. They have seven good qualities, and they get the four absorptions—blissful meditations in this life that belong to the higher mind—when they want, without trouble or difficulty.

§7And how is a noble disciple accomplished in ethics? It’s when a noble disciple is ethical, restrained in the monastic code, conducting themselves well and resorting for alms in suitable places. Seeing danger in the slightest fault, they keep the rules they’ve undertaken. That’s how a noble disciple is ethical.

§8And how does a noble disciple guard the sense doors? When a noble disciple sees a sight with their eyes, they don’t get caught up in the features and details. If the faculty of sight were left unrestrained, bad unskillful qualities of covetousness and displeasure would become overwhelming. For this reason, they practice restraint, protecting the faculty of sight. When they hear a sound… smell an odor… taste a flavor… feel a touch… know an idea with their mind, they don’t get caught up in the features and details… That’s how a noble disciple guards the sense doors.

§9And how does a noble disciple eat in moderation? It’s when a noble disciple reflects rationally on the food that they eat: ‘Not for fun, indulgence, adornment, or decoration, but only to sustain this body, to avoid harm, and to support spiritual practice. In this way, I shall put an end to old discomfort and not give rise to new discomfort, and I will have the means to keep going, blamelessness, and a comfortable abiding.’ That’s how a noble disciple eats in moderation.

§10And how is a noble disciple dedicated to wakefulness? It’s when a noble disciple practices walking and sitting meditation by day, purifying their mind from obstacles. In the first watch of the night, they continue to practice walking and sitting meditation. In the middle watch, they lie down in the lion’s posture—on the right side, placing one foot on top of the other—mindful and aware, and focused on the time of getting up. In the last watch, they get up and continue to practice walking and sitting meditation. That’s how a noble disciple is dedicated to wakefulness.

The seven good qualities

§11And how does a noble disciple have seven good qualities? It’s when a noble disciple has faith in the Realized One’s awakening: ‘That Blessed One is perfected, a fully awakened Buddha, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, holy, knower of the world, supreme guide for those fit for training, teacher of gods and humans, awakened, blessed.’

§12–13They have a conscience. They’re conscientious about bad conduct by way of body, speech, and mind… They exercise prudence. They’re prudent when it comes to bad conduct by way of body, speech, and mind, and prudent when it comes to acquiring any bad, unskillful qualities.

§14–15They’re very learned, remembering and keeping what they’ve learned… remembering these teachings, rehearsing them, mentally scrutinizing them, and penetrating them theoretically. They live with energy roused up for giving up unskillful qualities and embracing skillful qualities, strong, staunchly vigorous, not slacking off.

§16–17They’re mindful. They have utmost mindfulness and alertness, and can remember and recall what was said and done long ago. They’re wise. They have the wisdom of arising and passing away which is noble, penetrative, and leads to the complete ending of suffering. That’s how a noble disciple has seven good qualities.

The four absorptions

§18And how does a noble disciple get the four absorptions—blissful meditations in this life that belong to the higher mind—when they want, without trouble or difficulty? It’s when a noble disciple, quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unskillful qualities, enters and remains in the first absorption… the second absorption… the third absorption… the fourth absorption. That’s how a noble disciple gets the four absorptions when they want, without trouble or difficulty.

The hen and her eggs

§19When a noble disciple is accomplished in ethics, guards the sense doors, eats in moderation, and is dedicated to wakefulness; and they have seven good qualities, and they get the four absorptions when they want—they are called a noble disciple who is a practicing trainee. Their eggs are unspoiled, and they are capable of breaking out of their shell, becoming awakened, and achieving the supreme sanctuary from the yoke. Suppose there was a chicken with eight or ten or twelve eggs, and she properly sat on them to keep them warm and incubated. Even if that chicken doesn’t wish, ‘If only my chicks could break out of the eggshell with their claws and beak and hatch safely!’ still they can break out and hatch safely. In the same way, when a noble disciple is practicing all these things they are called a practicing trainee, capable of breaking out, becoming awakened, and achieving the supreme sanctuary from the yoke.

The three breakings-out

§20Relying on this supreme purity of mindfulness and equanimity, that noble disciple recollects their many kinds of past lives—one, two, ten, a hundred thousand rebirths, through many eons of the world contracting and expanding… with features and details. This is their first breaking out, like a chick from an eggshell.

§21Relying on this supreme purity of mindfulness and equanimity, that noble disciple, with clairvoyance that is purified and superhuman, sees sentient beings passing away and being reborn—inferior and superior, beautiful and ugly, in a good place or a bad place—and understands how sentient beings pass on according to their deeds. This is their second breaking out, like a chick from an eggshell.

§22Relying on this supreme purity of mindfulness and equanimity, that noble disciple realizes the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom in this very life. And they live having realized it with their own insight due to the ending of defilements. This is their third breaking out, like a chick from an eggshell.

Knowledge and conduct

§23–24A noble disciple’s conduct includes the following: being accomplished in ethics, guarding the sense doors, moderation in eating, being dedicated to wakefulness, having seven good qualities, and getting the four absorptions when they want. A noble disciple’s knowledge includes the following: recollecting their past lives, clairvoyance that is purified and superhuman, and realizing the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom in this very life due to the ending of defilements.

§25This noble disciple is said to be ‘accomplished in knowledge’, and also ‘accomplished in conduct’, and also ‘accomplished in knowledge and conduct’. And the divinity Sanaṅkumāra also spoke this verse:

‘The aristocrat is best among people
who take clan as the standard.
But one accomplished in knowledge and conduct
is best among gods and humans.’

“And that verse was well sung by the Divinity Sanaṅkumāra, not poorly sung; well spoken, not poorly spoken, beneficial, not harmful, and it was approved by the Buddha.”

§26Then the Buddha got up and said to Venerable Ānanda, “Good, good, Ānanda! It’s good that you spoke to the Sakyans of Kapilavatthu about the practicing trainee.” This is what Venerable Ānanda said, and the teacher approved. Satisfied, the Sakyans of Kapilavatthu approved what Venerable Ānanda said.

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 76

With Sandaka

Sandakasutta

Setting
Near Kosambī, in Ghosita’s Monastery; the talk at the cave of the wavy-leaf fig tree
Speakers
Venerable Ānanda and the wanderer Sandaka, who leads an assembly of some five hundred
Form
A teaching delivered not by the Buddha but by Ānanda — a clear-eyed survey of four doctrines that make the holy life pointless, four teachers who cannot be trusted, and the one path that can.
Length
~16 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No counterpart survives in the Chinese Āgamas (Sanskrit fragments are known)
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — a doxographical survey; rewarding once the four-plus-four structure is in view.

Why this discourse, seventy-sixth

How do you tell a spiritual path worth giving your life to from one that leads nowhere? Venerable Ānanda answers the question with a kind of consumer’s guide to the teachers of the age: four doctrines that quietly cancel the very point of the holy life, and four teachers whose confident claims rest on nothing you could lean on. Only against that backdrop does he describe the path that actually delivers.

The frame is gently human. Ānanda and some monks go on a late-afternoon walk to see a cave; nearby, the wanderer Sandaka and his five hundred are deep in “a colossal racket” of idle gossip. Spotting Ānanda — whose people “like the quiet, are educated to be quiet, and praise the quiet” — Sandaka hushes his crowd to lure the visitor over, then asks him to teach. What Ānanda gives is one of the canon’s clearest pieces of comparative religion, and a rare case of a major doctrinal discourse spoken by a disciple rather than the Buddha himself.

The structure is two sets of four. First, the four ways that negate the spiritual life — the doctrines of the famous heterodox teachers, each of which, if true, would make austerity and practice a waste of effort. Then the four kinds of unreliable spiritual life — teachers whose teaching might happen to be right but offers no firm ground: the false omniscient, the mere traditionalist, the pure reasoner, and the evasive muddler. Against these eight, the reliable path stands out by contrast — and Sandaka is convinced. What he then does, and does not do, gives the discourse its quietly unforgettable ending.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A spiritual path is worthless if its doctrine cancels the point of practice (the four life-negating views) or if it rests on no firm ground (the four unreliable kinds); the path worth living is the one under which a person actually attains the absorptions, the higher knowledges, and the ending of the defilements — verifiable, and shared by “many more than” a few.

Four doctrines that cancel the holy life

The first set names the teachings of the well-known heterodox masters of the Buddha’s day. Ānanda’s test for each is brilliantly simple: if this doctrine were true, the indulgent householder and the austere ascetic would end up in exactly the same place — so why bother practising at all? Each doctrine “negates the spiritual life” (abrahmacariyavāsa) by sawing off the branch the practitioner sits on.

The doctrineWhy it cancels practice
Materialism — no deeds bear fruit, no afterlife; a person is just four elements, annihilated at death (Ajita Kesakambalī)If foolish and wise alike are simply destroyed, the ascetic gains nothing the pleasure-seeker doesn’t.
Amoralism — no wrong in killing, no merit in giving; action has no moral result (Pūraṇa Kassapa)If nothing one does has moral weight, restraint and generosity are pointless.
No-causation — beings are defiled and purified “without cause,” with no power or effort (Makkhali Gosāla)If purification owes nothing to effort, there is nothing to strive for.
Seven eternal bodies — earth, water, fire, air, pleasure, pain, soul are fixed; a sword just “passes between” them; all wander a set course, then end suffering automatically (Pakudha Kaccāyana)If liberation comes after a fixed span no matter what, practice neither helps nor hinders.

The reflective person, seeing this, “leaves disappointed.” Notice the form of the critique: Ānanda does not argue that these doctrines are false. He argues that they are self-defeating for anyone who would teach them as a spiritual path — a teacher who preaches that effort is futile cannot coherently demand the effort of going forth.

Four teachers you cannot lean on

The second set is subtler. These teachings are “unreliable” (anassāsika, “offering no consolation, nothing to rest on”) — not necessarily false, but built on a foundation that might give way:

The teacherThe unreliability
The false omniscientClaims to know all things at every moment — yet enters empty houses, gets no alms, is bitten by dogs, asks the way to a village. The claim is refuted by his own fumbling.
The traditionalistTakes oral transmission as truth — but “some of it is well learned, some poorly; some true, some not.” A relayed truth is only as good as its chain.
The pure reasonerTeaches what he has worked out by logic — but “some is well reasoned, some poorly; some true, some not.” Clever inference can be elegantly wrong.
The evasive muddlerToo dull to commit, he wriggles: “I don’t say it’s so, nor otherwise, nor not so…” — the “endless flip-flops” that answer nothing.

The point is not that tradition or reason are worthless — both have their place — but that a path resting solely on hearsay, or solely on speculation, or on bluff or evasion, gives the practitioner no way to know he is not being led astray. He cannot verify it for himself.

The path that can be relied on

By contrast, the reliable holy life is the gradual training, and its mark is that the practitioner reaches verifiable attainments under it. A Realized One arises; one goes forth, abandons the five hindrances, and enters the four absorptions; then the mind, “purified, bright, flawless,” is turned to the recollection of past lives, the divine eye seeing beings reborn by their deeds, and the knowledge of the ending of the defilements through the four noble truths — until “rebirth is ended.” At each stage Ānanda repeats the refrain: under such a teacher, “a sensible person would lead the spiritual life… and would complete the system of the skillful teaching.” What makes this path trustworthy is not a louder claim but that it actually carries people to the goal — and the goal is something they know for themselves.

What an arahant cannot do — and the amputee’s simile

Sandaka presses with two fine questions. Can a perfected one still indulge in sensual pleasures? No: an arahant “cannot transgress in five respects” — cannot deliberately kill, steal, have sex, lie, or hoard goods for enjoyment as a layperson would. Liberation is not a license; it is the impossibility of those very acts. And is the arahant’s knowledge “I am freed” present every waking and sleeping moment? No — and here Ānanda quietly skewers the false-omniscience claim from earlier. A man whose hands and feet have been amputated is not constantly aware of the loss “while walking, standing, sleeping, and waking”; he knows it “only when he checks.” So too the arahant knows his freedom when he attends to it — a far more honest picture than the teacher who claims unbroken, all-hours omniscience and then asks a stranger for directions.

The mark of a true teaching — and Sandaka’s half-step

Sandaka asks how many have been emancipated in this teaching: “not one hundred… but many more than that.” What amazes him is the spirit of the answer — “there’s no glorifying one’s own teaching and putting down the teaching of others,” and yet the results are abundant; whereas the Ājīvakas “glorify themselves and put others down” and can name only three men ever liberated. A teaching confident enough not to boast, that simply produces freed people by the hundred, is its own advertisement. Convinced, Sandaka turns to his five hundred followers and sends them off: “Go, good fellows. The spiritual life is to be lived under the ascetic Gotama.” But he himself stays behind — “It’s not easy for me to give up the possessions, honour, and popularity I have now.” It is one of the most poignant endings in the Majjhima: a man clear-sighted enough to send everyone he leads toward the truth, and too attached to his own standing to follow them. He sees the white cloth, and keeps the rag.

Key terms

abrahmacariyavāsa — “ways that negate the spiritual life.” Doctrines under which living the holy life makes no sense — because, if true, the practitioner and the indulgent end up identical.
anassāsika — “unreliable, offering no consolation.” A path that may or may not be true but rests on no foundation one can lean on or verify.
the six teachers — the heterodox masters of the age; four of their doctrines are surveyed here (Ajita’s materialism, Pūraṇa’s amoralism, Gosāla’s no-cause, Pakudha’s seven bodies), set out in full at DN 2.
anussava / takka — “oral transmission” and “logic.” Two of the unreliable foundations: a teaching resting only on inherited report, or only on clever reasoning, can be partly true and partly false.
amarāvikkhepa — “endless flip-flopping” (“eel-wriggling”): the evasive non-answer of a teacher too dull to commit — “I don’t say it’s so, nor otherwise…”
the five things an arahant cannot do — deliberately kill, steal, have sex, lie, or hoard for enjoyment. Liberation shows not as license but as the impossibility of these acts.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The frame, the four negating doctrines, the four unreliable kinds, and the closing exchange are given in full; the long lists within the fourth doctrine and the stages of the reliable path are condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The visit

§1–6So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Kosambī, in Ghosita’s Monastery. Now the wanderer Sandaka was residing at the cave of the wavy-leaf fig tree with around five hundred wanderers. One afternoon Venerable Ānanda and several monks came that way. Seeing them, Sandaka hushed his noisy crowd — “Be quiet; the ascetic Ānanda likes the quiet” — and welcomed him. Ānanda asked what they had been discussing; Sandaka set it aside and asked Ānanda to teach. “Sandaka,” he began, “the Buddha has explained four ways that negate the spiritual life, and four kinds of unreliable spiritual life. A sensible person would not practise such paths, and if they did, they would not succeed.”

The four that negate the holy life

§7–9“Take a teacher who holds: ‘There is no fruit of good or bad deeds, no afterlife; a person is just the four elements, annihilated at death; giving is a doctrine of fools.’ A sensible person reflects: ‘If this is true, then I — living at home with my children, enjoying every comfort — will have exactly the same destiny as this naked, hair-tearing ascetic. What do I know or see that I should lead the spiritual life under him? This negates the spiritual life.’ And they leave disappointed. This is the first.”

Ānanda gives three more, each with the same reflection. The second: a teacher who holds that “nothing wrong is done” by killing or stealing, and no merit by giving — amoralism. The third: a teacher who holds that beings are defiled and purified “without cause or reason,” with no power or effort — determinism. The fourth: a teacher who holds that earth, water, fire, air, pleasure, pain, and the soul are seven fixed, unmade substances — “a sword just passes between them,” no one kills or is killed — and that all beings wander a fixed course “like a ball of string unrolling” before automatically ending suffering. In each case the verdict is the same: if it were true, the ascetic gains nothing the householder lacks — so it negates the holy life.

The four that are unreliable

§21–25“And the four unreliable kinds, Sandaka. Take a teacher who claims to know and see everything, constantly, while walking, standing, sleeping, and waking — yet he enters an empty house, gets no alms, is bitten by a dog, meets a wild elephant, and asks a stranger the name of a village and the way to it. Asked why, he says, ‘I had to.’ A sensible person reflects: ‘He claims all this, yet answers like that. This spiritual life is unreliable’ — and leaves. That is the first. Take another who takes oral transmission as truth — but of that ‘some is well learned, some poorly; some true, some not.’ Unreliable. That is the second.”

The third and fourth follow the same form. A teacher who relies on logic and inquiry, teaching what he has reasoned out — but “some is well reasoned, some poorly; some true, some not.” And a teacher too dull and stupid to commit, who meets every question with “endless flip-flops”: “I don’t say it’s like this, nor like that, nor otherwise, nor not so.” Each, the reflective person sees, gives no firm ground — “this spiritual life is unreliable” — and they leave disappointed.

The path that can be relied on

§35–50“But, Sandaka, when a Realized One arises in the world… a person goes forth, gives up the five hindrances, and enters and remains in the first, second, third, and fourth absorptions. Under a teacher with whom one reaches such a high distinction, a sensible person would lead the spiritual life, and would complete it. Then, with the mind purified and imperturbable, they recollect their past lives; they see with the divine eye beings passing away and being reborn according to their deeds; and they understand the four noble truths and the ending of the defilements. Their mind is freed — ‘Rebirth is ended… there is nothing further for this place.’ Under such a teacher, Sandaka, a sensible person would lead the spiritual life and complete the system of the skillful teaching.”

The arahant, and the amputee’s simile

§51–52“Could a perfected one still enjoy sensual pleasures?” “No, Sandaka. One with defilements ended cannot deliberately kill a living creature, steal, have sex, tell a deliberate lie, or hoard goods for enjoyment as a layperson would.” “And is the knowledge ‘my defilements are ended’ present to him constantly, while walking, standing, sleeping, and waking?” “Let me give a simile. Suppose a person’s hands and feet were amputated. Are they aware of it constantly, or only when they check?” “Only when they check, worthy Ānanda.” “In the same way, the perfected one knows their defilements are ended not constantly, but when they attend to it.”

Many freed — and Sandaka stays behind

§53–54“And how many are emancipated in this teaching?” “Not one hundred, Sandaka, or two or five hundred, but many more than that.” “Incredible! There is no glorifying one’s own teaching and putting down others’, and yet so many are freed — while the Ājīvakas glorify themselves, disparage others, and recognise only three ever emancipated.” Then Sandaka said to his assembly, “Go, good fellows. The spiritual life is to be lived under the ascetic Gotama. It’s not easy for me to give up the possessions, honour, and popularity I have now.” And so Sandaka sent his own followers to lead the spiritual life under the Buddha.

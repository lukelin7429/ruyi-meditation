Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 120

Rebirth by Choice

Saṅkhārupapattisutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, addressed to the mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha alone; the mendicants only reply “yes, sir”
Form
A systematic ascent through the Buddhist cosmos, one aspiration at a time, ending in the aspiration to no rebirth at all
Length
~12 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No single Āgama match is noted, though related material correlating absorption levels with rebirth realms survives in the Chinese Madhyama Āgama (MĀ 168) and the Dharmaskandha
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — one repeating formula, but a full map of Buddhist cosmology to hold in view

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-twentieth

This is the closing discourse of the Anupadavagga, the tenth chapter of the Majjhima Nikāya’s middle fifty — and it closes the chapter with something almost startling: the Buddha teaching mendicants how to choose their own rebirth. Elsewhere in the canon, the very suggestion horrifies the Saṅgha, or is diagnosed as a symptom of unresolved desire. Here the Buddha takes it seriously, walking through an entire cosmology, realm by realm, showing exactly what state of mind delivers exactly what destination.

But the discourse is not really an endorsement of heaven-seeking. It is a ladder, and the point of a ladder is to keep climbing. Realm by realm, wish by wish, the aspirations grow more refined — wealth, then the sensual heavens, then the radiant Brahmā worlds, then the formless dimensions beyond form itself — until the final rung reveals what all the others were pointing toward: to aspire, instead, to nothing at all. Not the worst rebirth, but no rebirth.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A mendicant grounded in faith, ethics, learning, generosity, and wisdom can consciously aim their next rebirth at any realm they wish — wealthy households, the sensual heavens, the radiant Brahmā worlds, or the formless dimensions — simply by settling the mind on that aspiration and developing it; but the highest use of this power is to aim, finally, at no rebirth at all.

A more conciliatory teaching

It helps to know what this discourse is arguing against. Elsewhere, wanting a particular rebirth is treated with real suspicion: at MN 16, practicing out of desire for a favorable rebirth is called an “emotional shackle”; at AN 3.18, the mendicants are said to be horrified merely at the suggestion of practicing to be reborn as a god; AN 7.50 goes so far as to trace such aspirations back to unresolved sexual desire. Against that backdrop, this sutta is unusually conciliatory. Rather than condemning the wish for a good rebirth outright, it takes the wish seriously, refines it stage by stage, and lets the meditator discover for themselves — by actually walking the ladder — that every rung, however sublime, is still a rung, and that the summit lies past the top of the ladder altogether.

One formula, aimed at will

Every realm in the discourse is reached by the identical procedure, applied to a mendicant already established in five qualities: faith, ethics, learning, generosity, and wisdom. The aspiration alone is not enough — it only works for someone who already has the ethical and spiritual groundwork to realize it. On that foundation:

The formula. They think: “If only, when my body breaks up, after death, I would be reborn in the company of —” (naming the desired destination). “They settle on that thought, stabilize it, and develop it. Those choices (saṅkhāra) and meditations of theirs, developed and cultivated like this, lead to rebirth there. This is the path and the practice that leads to rebirth there.”

The word translated “choice” here is saṅkhāra — the same term at work in dependent origination and the five aggregates, the morally charged volition that shapes kammic results whether or not we notice it happening. What this discourse describes is someone who understands how kamma works turning that same mechanism to conscious use, aiming it deliberately rather than letting it operate blindly.

The ladder, realm by realm

The sutta then ascends through the entire Buddhist cosmos, one wish at a time:

TierRealms named
Human privilegewell-to-do aristocrats, brahmins, householders
Six sense-sphere heavensthe Four Great Kings, the Thirty-Three, the gods of Yama, the joyful gods (Tusita), the gods who love to create, the gods who control what others create
Brahmā realms, by cosmic scopethe Divinity of a thousand-, two-, three-, four-, five-, ten-, and hundred-thousandfold galaxies — each Brahmā’s field of awareness literally larger than the last
Radiance & beauty realmsthe radiant gods (limited, limitless, streaming radiance); the gods of beauty (limited, limitless, universal beauty)
The highest form realmsthe gods of abundant fruit, and the five Pure Abodes — Aviha, Atappa, Sudassa, Sudassī, Akaniṭṭha — reserved in the tradition for non-returners
The formless dimensionsinfinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness, and neither perception nor non-perception

The Brahmā tier comes with its own escalating similes: the Divinity of a thousandfold galaxy is compared to a person examining a single gallnut held in the hand; further up, the Divinity of ten thousand worlds is like a flawless, eight-faceted beryl gem glowing on a cream rug; the Divinity of a hundred thousand worlds is like a pendant of legendary Jambu River gold, its lustre unmatched among even the gods. Each image is more precious and more radiant than the last — a deliberate crescendo tracking the widening field of each Brahmā’s meditative awareness, not merely their status.

More than a wish

It would be easy to read “they think… they settle on that thought” as mere daydreaming, but the commentarial tradition insists otherwise. Rebirth in the Brahmā realms elsewhere in the canon is said to require the actual development of absorption (jhāna), not simply an ethical life plus a wish. The phrase translated “develop the mind” (cittaṁ bhāveti) is used elsewhere for exactly this kind of deep meditative cultivation — as when the ancient teacher Sunetta, “free of desire,” developed the mind of love and was reborn in the Brahmā realm. Read this way, the practice described here is closer to a recollection meditation: recalling that the deities themselves attained their state through faith, ethics, learning, generosity, and wisdom — the very qualities the meditator already shares with them — and letting the joy of that recognition deepen into genuine samādhi.

The final and highest aspiration

Having climbed every rung of the cosmos, the discourse ends by stepping off the ladder entirely. The same mendicant, grounded in the same five qualities, can instead think: “If only I might realize the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom in this very life.” And when they do — when the defilements have ended and freedom is realized by their own insight — the Buddha adds a single, quiet, decisive line: “that mendicant is not reborn anywhere.” After a sutta spent naming ever more magnificent addresses across the universe, the highest aspiration turns out to need no address at all.

Key terms

saṅkhāra — here, “choice”: the same morally charged volition at work in dependent origination and the five aggregates, here turned to conscious, deliberate use rather than operating unnoticed.
faith, ethics, learning, generosity, wisdom (saddhā, sīla, suta, cāga, paññā) — the five qualities that must already be established before any aspiration for rebirth can succeed; the same five the deities themselves are said to have relied on.
upapatti — “rebirth,” one of several Pali terms for the concept, alongside jāti and punabbhava.
cittaṁ bhāveti — “develop the mind”: a phrase implying deep meditative cultivation, not casual wishing — the actual mechanism, the tradition holds, behind rebirth in the higher realms.
the Pure Abodes (suddhāvāsa) — Aviha, Atappa, Sudassa, Sudassī, and Akaniṭṭha: the five highest form-realm heavens, traditionally reserved for non-returners who will attain full awakening there.
“not reborn anywhere” — the discourse’s final destination: after naming every realm in the cosmos, the highest aspiration is revealed to be the aspiration for no further rebirth at all.

The text

The discourse in full for the first few realms, which establish the exact wording of the formula; the remaining realms abridged to their names alone since each repeats the identical pattern (as the source text itself abbreviates with ellipses). The Brahmā similes and the closing passage on freedom are given complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The formula

§1–3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, and addressed the mendicants: “I shall teach you rebirth by choice. Listen and apply your mind well.” “Yes, sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

“Take a mendicant who has faith, ethics, learning, generosity, and wisdom. They think: ‘If only, when my body breaks up, after death, I would be reborn in the company of well-to-do aristocrats!’ They settle on that thought, stabilize it, and develop it. Those choices and meditations of theirs, developed and cultivated like this, lead to rebirth there. This is the path and the practice that leads to rebirth there.”

Human privilege and the six sense-sphere heavens

§4–11The same is said, in turn, of rebirth among well-to-do brahmins, and well-to-do householders. Further, having heard that the gods of the Four Great Kings, the gods of the Thirty-Three, the gods of Yama, the joyful gods, the gods who love to create, and the gods who control what is created by others are “long-lived, beautiful, and very happy,” a mendicant may aspire, settle the mind, and develop it toward rebirth among any of them — and this is the path and the practice that leads to rebirth there.

The Brahmā realms

§12Furthermore, having heard that ‘the Divinity of a thousand is long-lived, beautiful, and very happy’ — and knowing that the Divinity of a thousand meditates focused on pervading a thousandfold galaxy and the beings reborn there, as a person with clear eyes might pick up a single gallnut in their hand and examine it — a mendicant may think: ‘If only I would be reborn in the company of the Divinity of a thousand!’ They settle on that thought, stabilize it, and develop it, and this is the path and the practice that leads to rebirth there.

§13–16The same is said of the Divinity of two, three, four, and five thousand — the Divinity of five thousand meditating focused on pervading a five-thousandfold galaxy, as a person might pick up five gallnuts and examine them.

§17Furthermore, the Divinity of ten thousand meditates focused on pervading a ten-thousandfold galaxy and its beings — like a beryl gem, naturally lustrous, eight-faceted, well-worked, which shines and glows and radiates when placed on a cream rug. A mendicant who aspires to this, settling and developing the thought, follows the path that leads to rebirth there.

§18Furthermore, the Divinity of a hundred thousand meditates focused on pervading a hundred-thousandfold galaxy and its beings — like a pendant of Black Plum River gold, fashioned by a deft smith and well wrought in the forge, which shines and glows and radiates when placed on a cream rug. A mendicant who aspires to this, settling and developing the thought, follows the path that leads to rebirth there.

Radiance, beauty, and the highest form realms

§19–30In the same way, having heard of their long life, beauty, and happiness, a mendicant may aspire to rebirth among the radiant gods, the gods of limited radiance, limitless radiance, or streaming radiance; the gods of limited beauty, limitless beauty, or universal beauty; the gods of abundant fruit; or the five Pure Abodes — Aviha, Atappa, the gods fair to see, the fair seeing gods, and the gods of Akaniṭṭha. For each, settling the thought and developing it is the path and the practice that leads to rebirth there.

The formless dimensions

§31–36Furthermore, a mendicant may aspire in the same way to the gods of the dimension of infinite space, the dimension of infinite consciousness, the dimension of nothingness, or the dimension of neither perception nor non-perception — each reached by the identical formula of hearing, aspiring, settling the mind, and developing it.

The final aspiration

§37Furthermore, take a mendicant who has faith, ethics, learning, generosity, and wisdom. They think: ‘If only I might realize the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom in this very life, and live having realized it with my own insight due to the ending of defilements.’ They realize the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom in this very life, and live having realized it with their own insight due to the ending of defilements. And, mendicants, that mendicant is not reborn anywhere.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said.

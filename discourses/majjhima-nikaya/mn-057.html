Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 57

The Dog-Duty Ascetic

Kukkuravatikasutta

Setting
The land of the Koliyans, at their town of Haliddavasana
Speakers
The Buddha, Puṇṇa the Koliyan (a cow votary), and the naked ascetic Seniya (a dog votary)
Form
A question reluctantly answered, opening into a teaching — the Buddha exposes the futility of imitative observance, then lays out the four kinds of deeds and their results.
Length
~11 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No full Āgama parallel; the four kinds of deeds are paralleled at AN 4.233–234 and in the Saṅgītiparyāya
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — a vivid, accessible story carrying a precise doctrinal core: how deeds shape rebirth, and the fourfold map that ends in the deeds that end all deeds.

Why this discourse, fifty-seventh

Two ascetics come to the Buddha who have spent years imitating animals: Seniya behaves like a dog — even eating food off the ground — and Puṇṇa behaves like a cow. Each wants to know where his practice will lead him after death. The Buddha three times asks not to be made to answer; when pressed, he gives a reply that is both merciless and merciful.

The mercilessness is logical: one who perfects the “dog observance” — cultivating “a dog’s ethics, a dog’s mentality, a dog’s behavior” fully and without break — is reborn among dogs. You become what you cultivate. And worse: if he holds the view that such an observance will make him a god, that is wrong view, and wrong view leads to hell. So the practice, taken to its end, leads to a dog’s birth; and taken with its usual hope, to a far lower fall. The same is said of the cow observance.

The mercy is what follows the tears. When both men weep — not at the verdict but at the years they have given to a dead end — the Buddha opens the door they have been missing. He sets out the four kinds of deeds: dark, bright, mixed, and the fourth that is “neither dark nor bright” — the intention to give up the other three, “which leads to the ending of deeds.” Seniya the dog-votary asks to ordain, accepts a long probation gladly, and not long after becomes one of the perfected. The man who lived as a dog reaches the goal the moment he is rightly pointed at it.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

One who fully develops the “dog observance” or “cow observance” is reborn among dogs or cows — and if he believes it leads to heaven, that wrong view leads to hell; the Buddha replaces this dead end with the four kinds of deeds (dark, bright, mixed, and neither), the last being the intention to abandon the first three, which leads to the ending of deeds.

Two animal-ascetics and a question the Buddha won’t answer

The animal observances were real ascetic practices of the time: a person took on the full behavior of a dog or a cow, believing such self-abasement was purifying and would lead to a heavenly birth. Seniya the dog-votary even “sits down curled up like a dog” in the Buddha’s presence. When Puṇṇa asks where Seniya’s practice will lead, the Buddha refuses three times: “Enough, Puṇṇa, let it be. Don’t ask me that.” The reluctance is itself a kindness — the Buddha knows the answer will wound. Only when pressed a third time does he speak, prefacing it, “Clearly I’m not getting through to you… Nevertheless, I will answer.”

You become what you cultivate

The core principle is stated with stark clarity. One who develops the dog observance “fully and uninterruptedly” — a dog’s ethics, a dog’s mentality, a dog’s behavior — is, at death, “reborn in the company of dogs.” This is not a punishment imposed from outside; it is the natural ripening of what was practiced. Cultivate the character of a dog single-mindedly for years, and the mind takes that shape; rebirth follows the shape of the mind. The teaching is a vivid case of a universal law: we are formed by what we repeatedly do and intend. The grim joke is that the practice “succeeds” precisely by failing — its best outcome is exactly the rebirth no one would choose.

Why wrong view makes it worse

There is a second, sharper edge. The animal-ascetic does not merely imitate a dog; he believes that “by this precept or observance or fervent austerity or spiritual practice, may I become one of the gods!” That belief — that empty ritual self-mortification purifies and elevates — is wrong view (micchā-diṭṭhi). And one who acts on wrong view, the Buddha says, is reborn in “one of two places: hell or the animal realm.” So the observance has two outcomes, and both are bad: succeed at the practice and you are reborn an animal; cling to the false hope behind it and you fall to hell. This is the canon’s pointed critique of sīlabbata-parāmāsa — the misapprehension that rites and observances, by themselves, can purify. They cannot; only the transformation of the heart can.

The tears, and the turn

Both men weep — and each is careful to say why: “I’m not crying because of what the Buddha said,” but because of the long years already spent. The moment is poignant: it is the grief of realizing a sincere effort has been wholly misdirected. But Puṇṇa turns the grief into a request: “I am confident the Buddha can teach me so that I can give up this cow observance, and Seniya can give up the dog observance.” Disillusionment becomes the opening for the real teaching. The Buddha does not gloat over the dead end; he immediately offers the living path.

The four kinds of deeds

That path is a complete map of action and result — the four kinds of deeds (kamma) the Buddha “realized with his own insight”:

DeedResult & rebirth
Dark, with dark resultsHurtful choices of body, speech, and mind → a hurtful world, “exclusively painful” feelings — like the beings in hell.
Bright, with bright resultsPleasing choices → a pleasing world, feelings of “perfect happiness” — like the gods of universal beauty.
Dark-and-bright, with mixed resultsBoth hurtful and pleasing choices → a mixed world of pleasure and pain — like humans, some gods, some lower beings.
Neither dark nor brightThe intention to give up all three of the above — the deed that “leads to the ending of deeds.”

Running through the first three is a refrain that states the law of karma at its plainest: “This is how a being is born from a being. Your deeds determine your rebirth, and when you’re reborn, contacts strike you. This is why I say that sentient beings are heirs to their deeds.” We inherit exactly what we have done.

The deeds that end deeds

The fourth kind is the hinge of the whole teaching, and it is easy to misread. It is not a special category of “neutral” action floating free of consequence. It is defined precisely as “the intention to give up” the other three — the volition of the path itself, which dismantles the very machinery of action-and-result. While dark, bright, and mixed deeds all keep the wheel of rebirth turning (even bright deeds only buy a finer turn of it), the fourth steps off the wheel. This is the supramundane factor — the wholesome intention that aims not at a better rebirth but at the end of rebirth altogether. For the two ascetics, it reframes everything: the goal was never to earn a good destination by self-abasement, but to cultivate the one kind of deed that leads beyond all destinations.

Seniya’s ordination and awakening

Both men go for refuge. Seniya, the former dog-votary, asks to ordain. The Buddha mentions the customary four-month probation for those who come from other sects — and Seniya, undaunted, offers four years. He is ordained, and “not long after,” living “alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute,” he becomes one of the perfected: “Rebirth is ended… there is nothing further for this place.” The contrast could not be sharper. The man who spent years cultivating the mind of a dog, once rightly directed, reaches the highest human attainment with startling speed. The years were not the problem; the direction was. Sincerity misaimed leads to a dog’s birth; the same sincerity, rightly aimed, leads all the way home.

Key terms

kukkuravatika / govatika — the dog-votary / the cow-votary: one who undertakes the full “observance” (vata) of behaving like a dog or a cow, believing such austerity purifies and leads to heaven.
sīlabbata-parāmāsa — the misapprehension of rites and observances: the wrong view that ritual or self-mortification, by itself, can purify. One of the fetters; this discourse is its starkest illustration.
micchā-diṭṭhi — wrong view. Here, the belief that an animal observance leads to godhood. The Buddha: one who acts on wrong view is reborn “in hell or the animal realm.”
kaṇha / sukka kamma — dark / bright deeds. The four kinds: dark (hurtful), bright (pleasing), dark-and-bright (mixed), and neither-dark-nor-bright — the last leading to the ending of deeds.
kammakkhaya — the ending of deeds. The aim of the fourth kind of deed: not a better rebirth but the dismantling of the whole machinery of action-and-rebirth.
kammadāyāda — “heir to one’s deeds.” The refrain of the teaching: “sentient beings are heirs to their deeds” — we inherit exactly what we have done.

The text

The full discourse. The parallel treatment of the dog observance and the cow observance is given in full; the four kinds of deeds follow. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Two ascetics, one question

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the land of the Koliyans, where they have a town named Haliddavasana. Then Puṇṇa the Koliyan, a cow votary, and the naked ascetic Seniya, a dog votary, went to see the Buddha. Puṇṇa bowed and sat down to one side, while Seniya exchanged greetings before sitting down to one side curled up like a dog. Puṇṇa said to the Buddha, “Sir, this naked ascetic Seniya the dog votary does a hard thing: he eats food placed on the ground. For a long time he has undertaken that observance to behave like a dog. Where will he be reborn in his next life?” “Enough, Puṇṇa, let it be. Don’t ask me that.” A second time, and a third time Puṇṇa asked the same. “Clearly, Puṇṇa, I’m not getting through to you when I say, ‘Enough, let it be.’ Nevertheless, I will answer you.

You become what you cultivate

§3Take someone who develops the dog observance fully and uninterruptedly. They develop a dog’s ethics, a dog’s mentality, and a dog’s behavior fully and uninterruptedly. When their body breaks up, after death, they’re reborn in the company of dogs. But if they have such a view: ‘By this precept or observance or fervent austerity or spiritual practice, may I become one of the gods!’—this is their wrong view. An individual with wrong view is reborn in one of two places, I say: hell or the animal realm. So if the dog observance succeeds it leads to rebirth in the company of dogs, but if it fails it leads to hell.”

§4When he said this, Seniya cried and burst out in tears. The Buddha said to Puṇṇa, “This is what I didn’t get through to you when I said, ‘Enough, let it be.’” Seniya said, “Sir, I’m not crying because of what the Buddha said. But, sir, for a long time I have undertaken this observance to behave like a dog. Sir, this Puṇṇa is a cow votary. For a long time he has undertaken that observance to behave like a cow. Where will he be reborn in his next life?” “Enough, Seniya, let it be. Don’t ask me that.” A second and a third time Seniya asked the same. “Clearly, Seniya, I’m not getting through to you… Nevertheless, I will answer you.

§5Take someone who develops the cow observance fully and uninterruptedly. They develop a cow’s ethics, a cow’s mentality, and a cow’s behavior fully and uninterruptedly. When their body breaks up, after death, they’re reborn in the company of cows. But if they have such a view: ‘By this observance may I become one of the gods!’—this is their wrong view. An individual with wrong view is reborn in one of two places: hell or the animal realm. So if the cow observance succeeds it leads to rebirth in the company of cows, but if it fails it leads to hell.”

§6When he said this, Puṇṇa cried and burst out in tears… “Sir, I’m not crying because of what the Buddha said. But for a long time I have undertaken this observance to behave like a cow. I am quite confident that the Buddha is capable of teaching me so that I can give up this cow observance, and the naked ascetic Seniya can give up that dog observance.” “Well then, Puṇṇa, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, sir,” he replied.

The four kinds of deeds

§7“Puṇṇa, I declare these four kinds of deeds, having realized them with my own insight. What four? There are dark deeds with dark results; bright deeds with bright results; dark and bright deeds with dark and bright results; and neither dark nor bright deeds with neither dark nor bright results, which lead to the ending of deeds.

§8And what are dark deeds with dark results? It’s when someone makes hurtful choices by way of body, speech, and mind. Having made these choices, they’re reborn in a hurtful world, where hurtful contacts strike them. Touched by hurtful contacts, they experience hurtful feelings that are exclusively painful—like the beings in hell. This is how a being is born from a being. For your deeds determine your rebirth, and when you’re reborn contacts strike you. This is why I say that sentient beings are heirs to their deeds. These are called dark deeds with dark results.

§9And what are bright deeds with bright results? It’s when someone makes pleasing choices by way of body, speech, and mind. Having made these choices, they are reborn in a pleasing world, where pleasing contacts strike them. Touched by pleasing contacts, they experience pleasing feelings of perfect happiness—like the gods of universal beauty. This is how a being is born from a being… sentient beings are heirs to their deeds. These are called bright deeds with bright results.

§10And what are dark and bright deeds with dark and bright results? It’s when someone makes both hurtful and pleasing choices by way of body, speech, and mind. Having made these choices, they are reborn in a world that is both hurtful and pleasing, where hurtful and pleasing contacts strike them. Touched by both, they experience feelings that are a mixture of pleasure and pain—like humans, some gods, and some beings in the underworld… sentient beings are heirs to their deeds. These are called dark and bright deeds with dark and bright results.

§11And what are neither dark nor bright deeds with neither dark nor bright results, which lead to the ending of deeds? It’s the intention to give up dark deeds with dark results, bright deeds with bright results, and both dark and bright deeds with both dark and bright results. These are called neither dark nor bright deeds with neither dark nor bright results, which lead to the ending of deeds. These are the four kinds of deeds that I declare, having realized them with my own insight.”

Refuge, and Seniya’s awakening

§12–13When he had spoken, Puṇṇa the Koliyan said, “Excellent, sir! Excellent! … From this day forth, may the Buddha remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.” And Seniya the naked ascetic said, “Excellent, sir! Excellent! … I go for refuge to the Buddha, to the teaching, and to the mendicant Saṅgha. Sir, may I receive the going forth, the ordination in the Buddha’s presence?”

§14“Seniya, if someone formerly ordained in another sect wishes to take the going forth and the ordination in this teaching and training, they must spend four months on probation. When four months have passed, if the mendicants are satisfied, they’ll give the going forth and the ordination into monkhood. However, I recognize individual differences in this matter.” “Sir, if four months’ probation are required, I’ll spend four years on probation. When four years have passed, if the mendicants are satisfied, let them give me the ordination.”

§15And the naked ascetic Seniya received the going forth and the ordination in the Buddha’s presence. Not long after his ordination, Venerable Seniya, living alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute, soon realized the supreme end of the spiritual path in this very life. He lived having achieved with his own insight the goal for which gentlemen rightly go forth from the lay life to homelessness. He understood: “Rebirth is ended; the spiritual journey has been completed; what had to be done has been done; there is nothing further for this place.” And Venerable Seniya became one of the perfected.

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 100

With Saṅgārava

Saṅgāravasutta

Setting
Caṇḍalakappa in Kosala, in the mango grove of the Todeyya brahmins
Speakers
The Buddha and the young brahmin Saṅgārava, framed by the laywoman Dhanañjānī
Form
A challenge answered by the whole story of the awakening — teachers outgrown, extremes abandoned, the middle way found.
Length
~20 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No full counterpart survives in the Chinese Āgamas; the awakening narrative it shares with MN 26 and MN 36 has Āgama parallels
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — the canonical autobiography of the awakening; the capstone of the chapter.

Why this discourse, one-hundredth

A young scholar sneers at a devout woman for honouring “that shaveling.” She tells him: you would never say such a thing if you understood the Buddha’s ethics and wisdom. So he goes to find out — and asks the one question that cuts to the root: how can you claim to teach what you have realised for yourself, when others only pass on what they were told? The answer is the whole story of the awakening, told in the Buddha’s own voice.

The frame is small and human. The brahmin lady Dhanañjānī, a devoted follower, stumbles and three times exclaims homage to the Buddha. Her young kinsman Saṅgārava — sixteen, freshly accomplished in the three Vedas, sure of himself — is appalled, and abuses the “fake ascetic.” Her reply is quietly confident: knowledge would silence his contempt. When the Buddha comes, Saṅgārava goes to test him, and asks how he stands among those who claim “perfection and consummation of insight in this very life.”

The Buddha’s reply sorts all such claimants into three, and places himself in the third — not those who repeat an oral tradition (the Vedic brahmins), nor those who reason “by mere faith” (the logicians), but those who, “having directly known for themselves a principle in teachings not learned before from another,” speak from what they have seen. To show what that means, he tells everything: the going-forth, the two great meditation teachers he matched and outgrew, the three firewood similes, the years of self-torture taken to the very edge of death, and the sudden memory of a child’s natural absorption that opened the middle way to the three knowledges. This is the canonical autobiography — and a fitting capstone to the Brahmin chapter and to the Middle Fifty of the Majjhima.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Asked how he can claim to teach what he has realised for himself, the Buddha tells his whole story — the two teachers whose attainments he matched and saw were not freedom, the firewood that will not catch while it is green and wet with craving, the self-torture pushed to the edge of death and abandoned as useless, and the child’s untaught absorption recalled as “the path to awakening” — showing that he speaks not from tradition or reasoning but from what he came to know and see.

The young scoffer, and the question that matters

Saṅgārava enters the chapter as its last sceptic — a brother to the young Kāpaṭika of MN 95: clever, Veda-proud, and openly contemptuous. The laywoman’s answer to his abuse is the chapter’s recurring note: contempt for the holy life is the verdict of ignorance, and would dissolve in knowledge. When he finally faces the Buddha, his question is sharp and fair: many teachers claim to have reached the summit of the spiritual life — on what basis do you? It is, at bottom, the same question the whole chapter has circled: how is a true claim to knowledge distinguished from an inherited or merely reasoned one?

Three kinds of claimant

The Buddha’s framework is precise. Among those who claim mastery of the spiritual life, there are three kinds:

KindBasis of the claim
Oral transmittersThey claim it “through oral transmission” — the brahmins of the three Vedas, repeating what was handed down.
ReasonersThey claim it “solely by mere faith” — those who rely on logic and inquiry, building systems by thought.
Those who know for themselvesThey claim it “having directly known a principle in teachings not learned before from another.” — “I am one of those.”

The distinction echoes the warning of MN 95 against the five unreliable grounds: tradition and reasoning may carry truth or falsehood indifferently, because neither rests on seeing. The Buddha claims only the third footing — and the rest of the discourse is offered as evidence, a life laid open to show what “knowing for oneself” cost and meant.

The two teachers matched and outgrown

He goes forth — his parents weeping — and studies under the two foremost meditation masters of the age. Under Āḷāra Kālāma he reaches the dimension of nothingness; under Uddaka Rāmaputta, the dimension of neither perception nor non-perception — the highest formless attainments then known. In each case he does not merely memorise the doctrine but realises the state itself, so fully that the teacher offers to share leadership of the whole community with his own pupil. And in each case he walks away, with the same clear verdict:

Not far enough. “This teaching does not lead to disillusionment, dispassion, cessation, peace, insight, awakening, extinguishment. It leads only as far as rebirth in that dimension.” Even the loftiest meditative peace, if it leaves the defilements intact, is a refined resting-place — not the end of suffering.

It is a striking refusal: offered the summit of the existing path and the honour of teaching it, he sets both down because they do not reach what he is seeking. The seeker’s integrity is measured by what he will not settle for.

The three firewood similes

Alone now at Uruvelā, by a clear river, three images come to him unbidden — the discourse’s signature teaching, found also in MN 36. A man wants to kindle fire with a drill-stick:

  • Against a green, sappy log lying in water — no fire; he only tires himself. So with ascetics not withdrawn from sensuality in body and still wet with craving within.
  • Against a green, sappy log on dry land — still no fire, for the wood is green. So with ascetics withdrawn in body from sensual pleasures, but who have not stilled the craving within.
  • Against a dry, withered log on dry land — fire catches. So with ascetics withdrawn in body and inwardly free of the “desire, affection, thirst, and passion for sensual pleasures” — they are capable of awakening.

The point is decisive and easily missed: outward renunciation is not enough. Physical withdrawal from pleasures, however severe, kindles nothing while the inner wood is still sappy with longing. What makes a person capable of awakening is not the rigour of the body’s austerity but the drying-out of craving in the heart — and this insight is precisely what turns him from the path of self-torture he is about to attempt.

Self-torture to the edge of death

And attempt it he does, fully, so that no one could say he had not gone as far as the path of pain can go. He crushes “mind with mind” until sweat pours; he practises the breathless meditation until winds drill his skull, wrench his head like a tightened strap, slice his belly like a butcher’s cleaver, and scorch his body like coals — the very similes of agony that MN 97 used for a dying man. Deities watching think he has died. He starves himself until his spine can be felt through his belly, his eyes sink like water at the bottom of a well, his hair falls out at the root. Then the sober reckoning:

This is as far as it goes. “Whatever ascetics have suffered painful, sharp feelings through overexertion — past, future, or present — this is the most; no one has done more. Yet by this grueling work I have not reached any superhuman distinction. Could there be another path to awakening?”

The child’s jhāna, and the middle way

The answer rises from memory. He recalls sitting as a boy in the cool shade of a rose-apple tree while his father worked, and entering naturally, untaught, the first absorption — “the rapture and bliss born of seclusion.” And the recognition: “That is the path to awakening.” Why had he feared that pleasure? It “has nothing to do with sensual pleasures or unskillful qualities” — it is a clean joy, the very fire of the dry log. But it cannot be reached in a body wrecked by starvation; so he eats solid food, and the five companions, disgusted at his “indulgence,” abandon him. Strengthened, he enters the four absorptions in turn, and in the three watches of the night attains the three knowledges: recollection of his own past lives; the divine eye, seeing beings pass and re-arise “according to their deeds”; and the ending of the defilements through the Four Noble Truths — “rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey completed.” The middle way, between the green log of indulgence and the futile drilling of self-torture, is the one that catches fire.

The closing question: do the gods survive?

Saṅgārava, moved, asks one last, almost abrupt question: “Do the gods survive?” The Buddha answers obliquely — “I have understood about gods on good grounds” — and when pressed, explains: whether one says plainly “gods survive” or says “I have understood it on good grounds,” a sensible person draws the same conclusion, for “it is agreed by the eminent in the world that gods survive.” The exchange is a small lesson in careful speech — affirming on the strength of knowledge rather than mere assertion — and perhaps a gentle test of whether the young brahmin is listening for words or for meaning. Either way, the contempt with which Saṅgārava began is wholly gone: “as if righting the overturned, or lighting a lamp in the dark,” he goes for refuge for life — the chapter’s last scoffer become its last convert, and the hundredth discourse closed.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The frame, the three kinds of claimant, the two teachers, the firewood similes, the self-torture, the turning, and the awakening are given closely; the long catalogues of emaciation and the absorptions are condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The scoffer and the laywoman

§1–5So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was wandering in Kosala. The brahmin lady Dhanañjānī of Caṇḍalakappa, devoted to the Buddha, tripped and three times exclaimed: “Homage to the Blessed One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha!” The young brahmin Saṅgārava, sixteen and master of the three Vedas, heard her and scolded: “A disgrace — she praises that shaveling fake ascetic!” “Dear boy, you do not understand his ethics and wisdom; if you did, you would never abuse him.” “Then tell me when he comes.” When the Buddha arrived at the Todeyya mango grove, she sent Saṅgārava to him.

Three kinds of claimant

§6–8“Worthy Gotama, some ascetics and brahmins claim to have attained perfection and consummation of insight in this very life. Where do you stand among them?” “There is a disparity among such claimants. Some are oral transmitters, claiming it through tradition — the brahmins of the three Vedas. Some claim it solely by mere faith — those who rely on logic and inquiry. And some, having directly known for themselves a principle in teachings not learned before from another, make the claim. I am one of those — and here is how you may understand it.”

The two teachers

§9–12“Before my awakening, while still a seeker, I thought: ‘Home life is cramped; let me go forth.’ So, while young, with black hair, though my parents wept, I went forth. I came to Āḷāra Kālāma, mastered his teaching, and realised for myself the dimension of nothingness — and he set me as his equal, offering to lead the community together. But I thought: ‘This does not lead to disillusionment, dispassion, peace, awakening, extinguishment — only to rebirth in nothingness.’ So I left. Then I came to Uddaka, son of Rāma, and realised the dimension of neither perception nor non-perception — and he set me in the place of a teacher. But this too led only to rebirth in that dimension. Dissatisfied, I left.”

The three firewood similes

§13–16“Travelling on, I came to a delightful grove by a clear river near Uruvelā, and sat down to strive. Then three similes came to me, unlearned before. Suppose a man wished to make fire with a drill-stick. If he drills against a green, sappy log lying in water — can he make fire? ‘No, worthy Gotama; he only tires himself.’ So with those not withdrawn from sensuality, still wet with craving within. And against a green, sappy log on dry land? ‘Still no — the wood is green.’ So with those withdrawn in body, but not within. And against a dry, withered log on dry land? ‘Yes — the wood is dry.’ So with those withdrawn in body and freed of craving within: they are capable of awakening.”

Self-torture to the edge of death

§17–27“Then I clenched my teeth and crushed mind with mind till sweat poured. I practised the breathless meditation, and winds drilled my head, gripped it like a tightened strap, sliced my belly like a cleaver, scorched me like coals; deities thought I had died. I starved until my limbs were like withered reeds, my spine could be felt through my belly, my eyes sank like water in a well, my hair fell out. Then I thought: ‘Whatever painful feelings ascetics have suffered, this is the utmost; no one has done more. Yet I have reached no superhuman distinction. Could there be another path to awakening?’”

The child’s jhāna, and the awakening

§28–41“I recalled: as a boy, in the cool shade of a rose-apple tree, I had entered the first absorption, the rapture and bliss born of seclusion. ‘That is the path to awakening!’ Why fear that pleasure, which has nothing to do with sensuality? But it cannot be reached in a starved body. So I took solid food — and the five companions left me, disgusted at my ‘indulgence.’ Strengthened, I entered the first, second, third, and fourth absorptions. With mind purified and steady, in the first watch I recollected my many past lives; in the middle watch, with the divine eye, I saw beings passing away and being reborn according to their deeds; in the last watch I understood suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path, and my mind was freed from the defilements. ‘Rebirth is ended; the spiritual journey has been completed; what had to be done has been done.’”

Do the gods survive?

§42–43“Your striving was indeed that of a true person, a perfected and fully awakened Buddha. But worthy Gotama, do the gods survive?” “I have understood about gods on good grounds.” “Then why answer so — is it not hollow?” “Whether one says ‘gods survive’ or ‘I have understood it on good grounds,’ a sensible person concludes that gods survive; it is agreed by the eminent in the world.” “Excellent, worthy Gotama! As if lighting a lamp in the dark… I go for refuge to the worthy Gotama, to the teaching, and to the Saṅgha. From this day forth, may he remember me as a lay follower gone for refuge for life.”

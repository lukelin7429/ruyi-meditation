Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 65

With Bhaddāli

Bhaddālisutta

Setting
Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery
Speakers
The Buddha and Venerable Bhaddāli, who refuses a training rule
Form
A failure, a confession, and a cluster of teachings — on the growth that comes from admitting a fault, on obedience and its fruits, on discerning discipline, on why rules multiply, and the simile of the thoroughbred colt.
Length
~17 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 194 (Madhyama-āgama 194, “Bhaddāli”), with a Sanskrit fragment
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — a vivid human story carrying several distinct teachings; the discussions of discipline and of why rules increase are unusually candid.

Why this discourse, sixty-fifth

When the Buddha asks the monks to take up eating a single meal a day, one of them, Bhaddāli, simply refuses — openly, in front of the assembly. Then, unable to face the Teacher, he avoids him for the whole three-month rains retreat. Only when fellow monks gently warn him does he come and confess. From this small, very human episode the Buddha draws an unusually rich cluster of teachings.

The first is about confession itself. The Buddha does not minimize the fault — he calls it “foolish, stupid, and unskillful” — and presses Bhaddāli to see how exposed his refusal left him. But then he accepts it, with a principle that runs through the whole tradition: “It is growth in the training of the Noble One to recognize a mistake for what it is, deal with it properly, and commit to restraint in the future.” A fault honestly owned is not a disgrace but a step forward.

From there the discourse opens out. The Buddha shows why fulfilling the training is what lets meditation bear fruit, and why the disobedient monk, even in solitude, finds himself “reprimanded by the Teacher, by wise companions, by deities, and by himself.” He explains the discernment behind monastic discipline — firm with the obstinate, gentle with the weak-but-faithful, “like protecting a person’s one good eye.” He answers, with striking candour, why the rules have grown more numerous over time even as awakened monks have grown fewer. And he ends with the simile of a fine colt patiently trained by stages — the image of how a practitioner, too, is gradually made into “the supreme field of merit for the world.”

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Bhaddāli’s refusal of the one-meal rule and his eventual confession become the occasion for the Buddha to teach that recognizing and amending a fault is itself growth in the noble training; that fulfilling the training is what makes meditation bear fruit; that discipline is applied wisely — firm with the obstinate, gentle with the weak-but-faithful; that rules multiply as the Saṅgha grows and meets new corruptions; and that a practitioner, like a thoroughbred patiently trained, becomes the world’s supreme field of merit.

The refusal

The trigger is modest: the Buddha recommends eating “in one sitting per day,” reporting that he himself finds it makes him “healthy and well, nimble, strong, and living comfortably.” Bhaddāli balks — “I’m not going to try… I might feel remorse and regret.” The Buddha offers a gentle compromise (eat part at the meal, bring the rest back), and Bhaddāli refuses even that. So as the rule is formally adopted by the whole Saṅgha, Bhaddāli alone publicly declines to keep it. The pettiness of the occasion is the point: this is not a heroic conscientious objection but a small, self-indulgent obstinacy — and it costs him dearly.

Three months in hiding, and the confession

The consequence is isolation. For the entire three-month retreat, Bhaddāli “would not see the Buddha face to face, as happens when someone doesn’t fulfill the training.” His refusal has cut him off from the very source of the teaching. Only when other monks — sewing a robe for the Buddha before his departure — tell him kindly, “Learn your lesson; don’t make it hard for yourself later,” does he go and confess: “I made a mistake, sir. It was foolish, stupid, and unskillful of me… Please accept my mistake, so I will restrain myself in future.”

“Vacuous, hollow, mistaken”

Before accepting the confession, the Buddha makes Bhaddāli feel the full weight of what he did — not to humiliate him, but so the lesson lands. He poses a test: would any truly accomplished disciple — one “freed both ways,” or “freed by wisdom,” or any of the seven kinds of noble person — refuse if the Teacher asked them to “be a bridge for me to cross over the mud”? No; such a one would lay themselves down at once. “And were you at that time any of those seven?” No. “Weren’t you, then, vacuous, hollow, and mistaken?” The sting is precise: Bhaddāli refused as if from some position of attainment, when in fact he had none. His obstinacy had no ground to stand on at all.

Why confession is growth

Then the turn. “Since you have recognized your mistake for what it is, and have dealt with it properly, I accept it. For it is growth in the training of the Noble One to recognize a mistake for what it is, deal with it properly, and commit to restraint in the future.” This is one of the most important principles of the spiritual life as the Buddha taught it. The goal is not a record of never having erred — an impossibility — but the living capacity to see a fault clearly, own it without evasion, make it right, and resolve to do better. A community held together this way is not one of the faultless, but one of the honest. The fault is not the failure; concealment and denial are.

Fulfilling the training bears fruit

The Buddha then shows what hangs on obedience to the training — not as mere submission, but as the condition for the path to work. The monk who does not fulfill the training may go off to a forest hut hoping for “a superhuman distinction in knowledge and vision,” but while there he is “reprimanded by the Teacher, by sensible companions, by deities, and by himself” — and so attains nothing. The one who does fulfill the training, going to the same solitude, is reprimanded by no one, and so “realizes a superhuman distinction”: the four absorptions, and then the three knowledges — recollection of past lives, the divine eye, and the ending of the defilements — issuing in full liberation. The refrain hammers the point: this is “what happens when someone fulfills the training according to the Teacher’s instructions.” A divided, self-excusing heart cannot settle deep enough to see.

Discipline with discernment: the one eye

Bhaddāli asks why some monks are “repeatedly pressured” in disciplinary matters while others are not. The Buddha’s answer is a small masterclass in wise governance. A frequent offender who, when admonished, “dodges the issue, displays annoyance, hate, and bitterness,” and won’t say “I’ll do what pleases the Saṅgha” — him the community examines firmly, so the matter is not quickly settled. One who responds well — falls in line, makes amends — has his matter settled quickly. But a monk who “gets by with mere faith and fondness,” who has little but his trust and love for the Buddha, is handled with great gentleness: “Suppose we pressure him — no, let him not lose what little faith and love he has!” The image is tender: “like protecting a person’s one good eye.” Discipline is not uniform severity; it is calibrated to what each person can bear, fierce where firmness will help and soft where it would only break.

Why the rules multiplied

Bhaddāli then asks a question of remarkable candour: why were there once “fewer training rules but more enlightened mendicants,” and now “more training rules and fewer enlightened”? The Buddha’s answer is sociological, not mystical. The Teacher “does not lay down rules until certain defiling influences appear in the Saṅgha” — and these appear only once the community “has attained a great size, an abundance of material support and fame, learning, and seniority.” Rules proliferate not because the Dhamma has weakened but because a large, prosperous, prominent institution generates problems a small band of earnest renunciants never had. It is an honest acknowledgment that growth and success bring their own corruptions, and that the multiplying of regulations is a response to them, not the cause of fewer awakenings.

The thoroughbred colt, and the ten qualities

The discourse closes with a simile — one the Buddha notes Bhaddāli had failed to absorb the first time, “because he doesn’t pay attention or actively listen.” A skilled trainer takes a fine colt and accustoms it by stages: first the bit, then the harness, then the paces of procession and gallop and charge. At each new stage the colt “still resorts to some twists, ducks, and dodges” — but “with regular and gradual practice its bad behaviour is extinguished,” until at last it is rewarded with a grooming, “worthy of a king.” So too the practitioner is trained gradually, the resistance worn away step by step. A mendicant perfected in this way — possessing the ten qualities of the adept (the eightfold path, plus right knowledge and right freedom) — is “worthy of offerings… the supreme field of merit for the world.” The colt that once twisted against the bit becomes the king’s own mount; the monk who once balked at a meal-rule can, through patient training, become the world’s most fruitful ground for giving. Bhaddāli’s story, told to Bhaddāli, holds out exactly that hope.

Key terms

eka-bhatta — eating “in one sitting” / a single meal a day. The training the Buddha recommends for health and ease, and which Bhaddāli refuses.
accaya-desanā — the confession or acknowledgment of a fault. “It is growth in the training of the Noble One to recognize a mistake… and commit to restraint in the future.”
the seven noble persons — freed-both-ways, freed-by-wisdom, the direct witness, attained-to-view, freed-by-faith, the follower-of-teachings, and the follower-by-faith. None would ever refuse the Teacher — unlike Bhaddāli, who was “none of these.”
saddhāmattaka · pemamattaka — “mere faith and fondness”: the beginner who has little but trust and love for the Buddha. Such a one is handled gently — “like protecting a person’s one good eye.”
āsava-ṭhāniyā dhammā — “things that are a basis for defilement”: the corruptions that appear as the Saṅgha grows large and prosperous, prompting the Teacher to lay down rules.
the ten qualities — the “tenfold path” of the adept: right view, purpose, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, immersion, plus right knowledge and right freedom. One who has them is “the supreme field of merit for the world.”

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The refusal, the confession, and the bridge test are given in full; the meditative attainments, the discipline cases, and the closing simile are condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The refusal

§2–4So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove. There he said to the mendicants, “Mendicants, I eat my food in one sitting per day. Doing so, I find that I’m healthy and well, nimble, strong, and living comfortably. You too should eat in one sitting per day.” Venerable Bhaddāli said, “Sir, I’m not going to try to eat in one sitting per day. For when eating once a day I might feel remorse and regret.” “Well then, Bhaddāli, eat one part where you’re invited, and bring the rest back to eat.” “Sir, I’m not going to try that either…” So as the rule was being laid down and the Saṅgha was undertaking it, Bhaddāli announced he would not keep it. And for the whole of that three months he would not see the Buddha face to face, as happens when someone doesn’t fulfill the training.

The confession

§5–7At that time several mendicants were making a robe for the Buddha, expecting that when it was finished and the rains had passed he would set out wandering. Bhaddāli came to them, and they said, “Come on, Bhaddāli, learn your lesson. Don’t make it hard for yourself later.” “Yes, reverends,” he replied, and went to the Buddha: “I made a mistake, sir. It was foolish, stupid, and unskillful of me that… I announced I would not keep the rule. Please, sir, accept my mistake for what it is, so I will restrain myself in future.”

§8–9“Indeed, Bhaddāli, you made a mistake… And you didn’t realize this situation: ‘The Buddha is staying in Sāvatthī, and he’ll know me as the mendicant Bhaddāli who doesn’t fulfill the training.’ You didn’t realize that the monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen residing there — and even the ascetics of other religions — would all know you as ‘Bhaddāli, a senior disciple of Gotama, who doesn’t fulfill the training.’”

“Vacuous, hollow, mistaken”

§11–13“What do you think, Bhaddāli? Suppose I said to a mendicant who is freed both ways, ‘Please, mendicant, be a bridge for me to cross over the mud.’ Would they cross over themselves, or struggle out of it, or just say no?” “No, sir.” “And if I said the same to one freed by wisdom, a direct witness, one attained to view, one freed by faith, a follower of teachings, or a follower by faith?” “No, sir — they would not refuse.” “And were you, at that time, any of these seven?” “No, sir.” “Weren’t you, then, vacuous, hollow, and mistaken?” “Yes, sir. I made a mistake… please accept it, so I will restrain myself in future.” “Since you have recognized your mistake for what it is, and dealt with it properly, I accept it. For it is growth in the training of the Noble One to recognize a mistake for what it is, deal with it properly, and commit to restraint in the future.”

Fulfilling the training bears fruit

§14–15“Bhaddāli, a mendicant who does not fulfill the training may go to a secluded lodging hoping to ‘realize a superhuman distinction in knowledge and vision worthy of the noble ones.’ But while living withdrawn, they are reprimanded by the Teacher, by sensible companions, by deities, and by themselves — and so they realize no such distinction. Why? Because that’s how it is when someone doesn’t fulfill the training. But a mendicant who does fulfill the training, going to the same seclusion, is reprimanded by none of these — and so they realize a superhuman distinction.”

The Buddha then describes that distinction in full — the first, second, third, and fourth absorptions, and then the three knowledges: recollection of past lives, the divine eye seeing beings reborn by their deeds, and the ending of the defilements, with the knowledge “Rebirth is ended… there is nothing further for this place.” After each, the refrain: “Because that’s what happens when someone fulfills the training according to the Teacher’s instructions.”

Discipline with discernment

§22–26“Sir, why do they punish some monk, repeatedly pressuring him, but not another?” “Take a monk who is a frequent offender. When admonished, he dodges the issue, displays annoyance, hate, and bitterness, doesn’t fall in line, and won’t say ‘I’ll do what pleases the Saṅgha.’ Of such a one the monks say, ‘Let this matter not be quickly settled’ — and examine him firmly. But a monk who, admonished, responds well — falls in line, makes amends, says ‘I’ll do what pleases the Saṅgha’ — of him they say, ‘Let this be quickly settled.’”

§27–28“But take a monk who gets by with mere faith and fondness. Of him the monks say, ‘Suppose we pressure him — no, let him not lose what little faith and fondness he has!’ Suppose there was a person with one eye. Their friends and relatives would protect that one eye: ‘Let them not lose the one eye that they have!’ In the same way, some monk gets by with mere faith and fondness — ‘let him not lose what little faith and love he has!’ This is why they pressure some monks and not others.”

Why the rules multiplied

§29–31“Sir, why were there once fewer training rules but more enlightened mendicants, and now more rules but fewer enlightened?” “That’s how it is, Bhaddāli. When beings decline and the true teaching is disappearing, there are more rules and fewer enlightened. The Teacher does not lay down rules until certain defiling influences appear in the Saṅgha. And they don’t appear until the Saṅgha has attained a great size, an abundance of material support and fame, learning, and seniority. When it has attained these, such defiling influences appear, and the Teacher lays down rules to protect against them.”

The thoroughbred colt

§32–33“There were only a few of you present when I taught the simile of the thoroughbred colt. Do you remember it?” “No, sir.” “That is because, for a long time, you have not paid attention or actively listened. Still, I shall teach it. Suppose a deft trainer obtained a fine thoroughbred. First he gets it used to the bit. Because it has not done this before, it still resorts to some twists, ducks, and dodges — but with regular and gradual practice its bad behaviour is extinguished. Then the harness… then the paces of procession, circling, prancing, galloping, and charging… each time it twists and dodges, and each time, with gradual practice, the resistance is extinguished. At last he rewards it with a grooming. Such a thoroughbred is worthy of a king.”

The ten qualities

§34“In the same way, a mendicant with ten qualities is worthy of offerings dedicated to the gods, worthy of hospitality and religious donations, worthy of veneration with cupped palms, and is the supreme field of merit for the world. What ten? It’s when a mendicant has an adept’s right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right immersion, right knowledge, and right freedom. A mendicant with these ten qualities is the supreme field of merit for the world.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Bhaddāli approved what the Buddha said.

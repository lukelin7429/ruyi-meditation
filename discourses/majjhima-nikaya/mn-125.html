Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 125

The Level of the Tamed

Dantabhūmisutta

Setting
Near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, following an encounter reported by the novice Aciravata
Speakers
The Buddha and the novice Aciravata (Aggivessana); Prince Jayasena appears only in the reported exchange
Form
Pastoral encouragement for a discouraged young novice, built into an extended allegory of taming a wild elephant
Length
~16 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A counterpart survives in the Chinese Madhyama Āgama (MĀ 198), in a more streamlined form
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the Gradual Training already familiar from other suttas, retold through one sustained, vivid image

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-twenty-fifth

Before any doctrine, there is a small, human scene: a teenage novice, alone in his forest hut, is drawn into conversation by a prince, hesitantly agrees to teach him, and is flatly told his teaching is impossible. He gets up, defeated, and goes to report the failure to the Buddha. What follows is not a rebuke but an act of care — the Buddha explains exactly why the encounter was doomed from the start, hands the young man two similes invented on the spot, and then builds, image by patient image, the single most sustained animal-training allegory in the Majjhima Nikāya: the taming of a wild elephant, mapped step by step onto the entire path to awakening.

It closes, fittingly, by circling back to comfort the novice who started it all — a reminder that in this teaching, seniority is no measure of what someone can still become.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A prince immersed in sensual pleasure cannot grasp what only renunciation reveals — not from any fault in the teacher — and the Buddha illustrates why, then builds a full picture of the path as the patient taming of a wild elephant: led out of the forest, tethered to the post of mindfulness, tested under fire, and finally “reckoned a factor of kingship,” just as a mendicant who ends the defilements is reckoned to have reached “the level of the tamed,” whatever their age or seniority.

A teenager questioned by a prince

The novice Aciravata — addressed by the clan name Aggivessana — is staying alone in a wilderness hut when Prince Jayasena, out walking, strikes up a conversation and asks him to teach the Dhamma. Aciravata hesitates: “I’m not competent to do so, Prince… you might not understand the meaning, which would be wearying and troublesome for me.” The commentary notes what makes this so uncomfortable: novices were under twenty, so this is, in effect, a reigning prince pressing a teenager for instruction. Jayasena insists twice; Aciravata finally agrees and teaches what he has learned. The prince’s response is blunt: “It is impossible, worthy Aggivessana, it cannot happen that a mendicant who meditates diligently, keenly, and resolutely can experience unification of mind” — and he simply gets up and leaves.

Not your fault

Aciravata goes straight to the Buddha and reports the whole exchange. The Buddha’s first words are pure reassurance: “How could it possibly be otherwise, Aggivessana?” Jayasena, he explains, “dwells in the midst of sensual pleasures, enjoying them, consumed by thoughts of them, burning with fever for them, and eagerly seeking more” — and it is simply impossible for someone in that state to know, see, or realize what only renunciation can reveal. The failure was never the young novice’s to own.

Two similes, made on the spot

To make the point vivid, the Buddha offers Aciravata two images — and then, remarkably, tells him where they came from. The first is the discourse’s namesake: a pair of well-tamed animals can “perform the tasks of the tamed and reach the level of the tamed”; an untamed pair cannot, however capable they might otherwise be. The second is set in terrain any listener in Rājagaha, ringed by mountains, would recognize at once:

The mountain. Two friends climb a mountain together; one stops at the foot, the other continues to the peak. From the peak, the friend reports seeing “delightful parks, woods, meadows, and lotus ponds” — and the one below insists this is impossible. So the friend at the peak comes down, takes the other by the arm, and leads them up. Once there, seeing the same view, the second friend understands: “It was because I was obstructed by this big mountain that I didn’t see what could be seen.” But bigger than that mountain, the Buddha says, is the mass of ignorance veiling Prince Jayasena.

When Aciravata marvels that such similes could occur to him, the Buddha gives an answer worth pausing on: they were “neither supernaturally inspired, nor learned before in the past” — that is, invented freshly, in the moment, for this particular young man’s particular need. It is a quiet demonstration of the Buddha’s own teaching creativity, offered as encouragement rather than doctrine.

Taming the wild elephant

The Buddha then develops the taming simile into the discourse’s centerpiece: a full, sequential account of how a king’s elephant tracker and trainer bring a wild bull elephant into royal service.

  1. Coming into the open. A tame royal elephant is sent into the forest to find a wild one and lead it out — for “a wild bull elephant clings to the elephant wood,” and only comes into the open led by a tame companion.
  2. Tethered to the post. The trainer digs a great post into the ground and tethers the wild elephant to it by the neck, to “subdue its wild behaviors, its wild memories and thoughts, and its wild stress, weariness, and fever,” speaking to it gently until it listens and accepts food — “now the wild elephant will survive!”
  3. Escalating tasks. Pick up, put down; forward, back; stand, sit — each command given and, in time, obeyed.
  4. The task called imperturbability. A plank is fastened to its trunk, lancers surround it, and the trainer stands before it with a long lance. Struck with spears, swords, arrows, and axes, deafened by drums and horns, the elephant “doesn’t budge” — and is at last “worthy of a king, fit to serve a king, reckoned a factor of kingship.”

The allegory mapped, step by step

Every stage of the elephant’s training is then given its exact counterpart in a mendicant’s path. A Realized One arises and teaches; a householder hears, reflects that “life at home is cramped and dirty, life gone forth is wide open,” and goes forth — “it’s only then that a noble disciple comes out into the open,” the text says, echoing the wild elephant’s emergence from the forest, “for gods and humans cling to the five kinds of sensual stimulation” exactly as the elephant clings to the wood. The Realized One then guides the new mendicant through ethical conduct and sense restraint (told in full, the text notes, exactly as in MN 107) — and here the sutta makes its central equation explicit: the four establishments of mindfulness are “tethers for the mind,” the exact counterpart of the training-post, subduing “behaviors tied to domestic life, memories and thoughts tied to domestic life, stress, weariness, and fever tied to domestic life.” From there, the absorptions follow, and from a mind “purified, bright, flawless, rid of corruptions, pliable, workable, steady, and imperturbable,” the mendicant extends toward the three knowledges: recollection of past lives, seeing how beings pass on according to their deeds, and finally the knowledge that ends the defilements of sensuality, desire to be reborn, and ignorance — arahantship.

(A small scholarly puzzle sits inside this section: the Pali text here places the four establishments of mindfulness where a description of the first absorption would normally appear, a departure not found in the Chinese parallel, which most translators — and the commentary’s own analysis — treat as a likely textual variant rather than a deliberate substitution.)

The final test, and a full circle

The last stage of taming — standing unmoved under a hail of spears and the thunder of war-drums — has its match too: such a mendicant “endures cold, heat, hunger, and thirst… rude and unwelcome criticism… physical pain, sharp, severe, acute… life-threatening,” and is, like the trained elephant, “rid of all defects… the supreme field of merit for the world.” The discourse closes with a final refrain: a royal elephant that dies without training, whatever its age, “is reckoned a royal bull elephant who passed away untamed”; a mendicant who dies without ending the defilements, whether senior, middle, or junior, is likewise reckoned “untamed.” But a mendicant of any seniority — senior, middle, or junior — who ends the defilements is reckoned to have reached the level of the tamed. Spoken to a discouraged teenage novice who had just been told his practice was impossible, that closing word is not incidental.

Key terms

dantabhūmi — “the level of the tamed”: the discourse’s title, drawn from the opening simile of tamed versus untamed animals and developed into the full elephant-training allegory.
“coming into the open” — the wild elephant’s emergence from the forest, led by a tame companion, mirrored by a noble disciple’s going-forth from a lay life clung to “like a wild elephant clings to the elephant wood.”
the training-post — the great post the wild elephant is tethered to, explicitly equated with the four establishments of mindfulness as “tethers for the mind.”
“the task called imperturbability” — the elephant’s final trial, standing unmoved under spears and war-drums; matched by a mendicant’s endurance of cold, hunger, criticism, and severe pain without wavering.
the three knowledges — recollection of past lives, knowledge of beings’ death and rebirth according to their deeds, and knowledge of the ending of the defilements: the culmination reached from a mind made “pliable, workable, steady, and imperturbable” through the absorptions.
“senior, middle, or junior” — the closing refrain’s insistence that seniority does not determine who reaches “the level of the tamed”: a direct word of comfort to the young novice Aciravata.

The text

The discourse in full: the encounter with Prince Jayasena, Aciravata’s report to the Buddha, the two spontaneous similes, and the full elephant-training allegory mapped onto the Gradual Training. The ethics-and-sense-restraint section, which the source text itself abbreviates by cross-reference to MN 107, is given in summary. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A teenager questioned by a prince

§1–5So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove. Now at that time the novice Aciravata was staying in a wilderness hut. As Prince Jayasena was going for a walk, he approached Aciravata and, after greetings, said: “Worthy Aggivessana, I have heard that a mendicant who meditates diligently, keenly, and resolutely can experience unification of mind.” “That’s so true, Prince!” “Please teach me the Dhamma as you have learned and memorized it.” “I’m not competent to do so, Prince. If I were to teach you, you might not understand the meaning, which would be wearying and troublesome for me.” The prince insisted, and Aciravata agreed. But when he had taught the Dhamma, Jayasena replied: “It is impossible, worthy Aggivessana, it cannot happen that a mendicant who meditates diligently, keenly, and resolutely can experience unification of mind.” And having declared this impossible, he got up and left.

Not your fault

§6–7Not long after, Aciravata went to the Buddha and told him everything. The Buddha said: “How could it possibly be otherwise, Aggivessana? Prince Jayasena dwells in the midst of sensual pleasures, enjoying them, consumed by thoughts of them, burning with fever for them, and eagerly seeking more. It’s simply impossible for him to know or see or realize what can only be known, seen, and realized by renunciation.

Two similes

§8–10Suppose there was a pair of elephants, horses, or oxen who were well tamed and trained, and a pair who were not. Wouldn’t the tamed pair perform the tasks of the tamed and reach the level of the tamed, unlike the untamed pair? In the same way, it’s impossible for Jayasena, immersed in sensual pleasures, to realize what only renunciation reveals.

Suppose there was a big mountain not far from a town. Two friends set out, helping each other climb; one remained at the foot, the other reached the peak. The one at the peak reported seeing delightful parks, woods, meadows, and lotus ponds; the one below insisted this was impossible. So the friend at the peak came down, took the other by the arm, and led them up. Once there, seeing the same view, the second friend understood: ‘It was because I was obstructed by this big mountain that I didn’t see what could be seen.’ But bigger than that mountain is the mass of ignorance veiling Prince Jayasena. It would be no wonder if these two similes had occurred to you and won his confidence.” “But sir, how could these similes have occurred to me as they did to the Buddha, since they were neither supernaturally inspired, nor learned before in the past?”

Taming the wild elephant

§12“Suppose an anointed king addressed his elephant tracker: ‘Mount the royal bull elephant, enter the elephant wood, and when you see a wild bull elephant, tether it by the neck to the royal elephant.’ The royal elephant leads the wild elephant into the open — for a wild bull elephant clings to the elephant wood, and only then does it come into the open. The king then addresses his elephant trainer: ‘Tame the wild bull elephant. Subdue its wild behaviors, its wild memories and thoughts, and its wild stress, weariness, and fever. Make it happy to be within a village, and instill behaviors congenial to humans.’ The trainer digs a large post into the earth and tethers the elephant to it by the neck. He speaks to it in a way that’s mellow, pleasing, endearing, and agreeable; the elephant wants to listen, and listens. So the trainer rewards it with grass, fodder, and water. When the elephant accepts these, the trainer knows: ‘Now the wild elephant will survive!’ He sets it further tasks — pick up, put down; forward, back; stand, sit — and when the elephant follows each instruction, he sets the task called imperturbability. A plank is fastened to its trunk; lancers surround it; the trainer stands before it with a long lance. Struck by spears, swords, arrows, and axes, deafened by drums, kettledrums, horns, and tom-toms, the elephant doesn’t budge its feet, quarters, head, ears, tusks, tail, or trunk. Rid of all crooks and flaws, and purged of defects, it is worthy of a king, fit to serve a king, and reckoned a factor of kingship.

The allegory mapped

§13–23In the same way, a Realized One arises in the world and proclaims a teaching good in the beginning, middle, and end. A householder hears it, gains faith, and reflects: ‘Life at home is cramped and dirty, life gone forth is wide open… why don’t I go forth from the lay life to homelessness?’ And it’s only then that a noble disciple comes out into the open, for gods and humans cling to the five kinds of sensual stimulation. The Realized One guides them further: ‘Be ethical and restrained in the monastic code… guard your sense doors…’ (told in full as in MN 107, the Discourse with Moggallāna the Accountant). They give up the five hindrances, corruptions of the heart that weaken wisdom, and meditate observing an aspect of the body, feelings, mind, and principles — keen, aware, and mindful. It’s like the elephant tethered to the post: in the same way, a noble disciple has these four kinds of mindfulness meditation as tethers for the mind, to subdue behaviors, memories and thoughts, and stress tied to domestic life, to discover the system, and to realize extinguishment.

§24–29The Realized One guides them further: ‘Meditate observing body, feelings, mind, and principles, but don’t think thoughts connected with sensual pleasures.’ As the placing of the mind and keeping it connected are stilled, they enter the second, third, and fourth absorptions. When their mind has become immersed in samādhi like this — purified, bright, flawless, rid of corruptions, pliable, workable, steady, and imperturbable — they extend it toward recollection of past lives; toward knowledge of the death and rebirth of sentient beings according to their deeds; and toward knowledge of the ending of the defilements. Knowing and seeing like this, their mind is freed from the defilements of sensuality, desire to be reborn, and ignorance. They understand: ‘Rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey has been completed, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this place.’

The final test, and a full circle

§30–32Such a mendicant endures cold, heat, hunger, and thirst, the touch of flies and mosquitoes, rude and unwelcome criticism, and physical pain — sharp, severe, acute, life-threatening. Rid of all greed, hate, and delusion, and purged of defects, they are worthy of offerings, worthy of hospitality, and the supreme field of merit for the world.

If a royal bull elephant — whether elderly, middle aged, or youthful — passes away untamed and untrained, it is reckoned a royal bull elephant who passed away untamed. In the same way, if a mendicant — whether senior, middle, or junior — passes away without having ended the defilements, they are reckoned a mendicant who passed away untamed. But if a royal bull elephant, whether elderly, middle aged, or youthful, passes away tamed and trained, it is reckoned tamed. In the same way, if a mendicant — whether senior, middle, or junior — passes away having ended the defilements, they are reckoned a mendicant who passed away tamed.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the novice Aciravata approved what the Buddha said.

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 29

The Greater Simile of the Heartwood

Mahāsāropamasutta

Setting
Rājagaha, on Vulture Peak — shortly after Devadatta’s departure from the Saṅgha
Speaker
The Buddha, addressing the mendicants
Form
A single extended simile — a person seeking heartwood in a great tree — mapped onto five levels of spiritual attainment: gains and honor, virtue, concentration, knowledge and vision, and the unshakeable liberation of mind
Length
~10 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 121 (Madhyama-āgama 121, “The Simile of Heartwood”)
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — structurally simple and immediately clear. The simile is intuitive: don’t mistake peripheral attainments for the final goal. Power lies in the hierarchy it establishes and the historical occasion that prompted it.

Why this discourse, twenty-ninth

MN 29 was prompted by Devadatta’s departure. The Buddha does not name Devadatta, but the historical context is explicit: “Here, mendicants, a certain person goes forth from the lay life to homelessness … having achieved gains, honor, and fame, they are pleased with those gains and their intention is fulfilled.” The discourse is a direct response to the danger of mistaking intermediate attainments for the goal.

The simile is one of the canon’s most memorable: a person needs heartwood. They go to a great tree standing with heartwood. But they might cut away the branches and leaves and carry those off, thinking they have the heartwood. Or they might take the bark. Or the sapwood. Or the inner sapwood. Only the person who recognizes the heartwood itself and takes that away has succeeded. The discourse maps this onto the spiritual life: gains and honor are branches; virtue is bark; concentration is sapwood; knowledge and vision is inner sapwood; the unshakeable liberation of mind is the heartwood.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The spiritual life has a specific goal — the unshakeable liberation of mind — and every intermediate attainment (gains, virtue, concentration, knowledge and vision), however genuine, is not that goal, just as branches, bark, sapwood, and inner sapwood are not heartwood.

The occasion: Devadatta’s departure

The discourse opens with an explicit narrative frame: “At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, on Vulture Peak, not long after Devadatta had left.” Devadatta was the Buddha’s cousin and a member of the Saṅgha who eventually caused a schism, motivated (according to the tradition) by desire for leadership and fame. The Buddha uses this occasion — without naming Devadatta directly in the main teaching — to address a structural danger: a practitioner who achieves something real on the path and then stops, mistaking the intermediate for the final.

The discourse does not say Devadatta lacked attainments. It says he stopped at the wrong level. He achieved gains, honor, and fame — and his intention was fulfilled by those. He mistook the branches for the heartwood. The teaching is not about failure to achieve; it is about failure to recognize what one has achieved relative to the actual goal.

The five levels of the tree

The simile maps five parts of a tree onto five levels of spiritual attainment:

Part of the treeSpiritual attainmentThe danger
Branches and leavesGains, honor, and fameBeing pleased, thinking one’s intention is fulfilled; praising oneself and disparaging others
BarkAccomplishment of virtueBeing pleased with one’s virtue, thinking the goal is reached; becoming arrogant
SapwoodAccomplishment of concentrationBeing pleased with one’s immersion; stopping at samādhi as if it were the destination
Inner sapwoodKnowledge and vision (ñāṇadassana)Being pleased with psychic experiences or insight-knowledge; mistaking these for liberation
HeartwoodThe unshakeable liberation of mind (akuppā cetovimutti)No danger — this is the goal itself

The hierarchy is not one of rejection but of ordering. The discourse does not say virtue is worthless — it says virtue is not the goal. Concentration is not worthless — it is not the goal. Even knowledge and vision, which includes the kind of supernormal knowledge described in MN 27, is not the goal. Each level is genuinely part of the tree; each is genuinely closer to the heartwood. But only the heartwood is the heartwood.

The structural pattern

For each of the first four levels, the discourse follows an identical five-part pattern:

  1. A person goes forth and achieves the attainment.
  2. They are pleased with it and their intention is fulfilled.
  3. They praise themselves and disparage others on that basis.
  4. They become negligent — they stop practicing further.
  5. These attainments are called “branches,” “bark,” “sapwood,” or “inner sapwood” — not the heartwood.

The pattern makes the danger structural, not personal. It is not that only bad practitioners stop at virtue or concentration. It is that any practitioner who is “pleased” and thinks the “intention is fulfilled” at an intermediate stage has mistaken part of the tree for the whole. The discourse is identifying a psychological mechanism: satisfaction leads to complacency, which leads to stopping.

The heartwood: akuppā cetovimutti

The unshakeable liberation of mind is the heartwood. The discourse does not describe it in detail here — for that, one must look to the graduated training (MN 27) or the destruction of the defilements. What MN 29 contributes is the claim that this, and only this, is the purpose of the spiritual life. Everything else — however valuable — is instrumental.

The word akuppā (unshakeable) is significant. It distinguishes final liberation from temporary meditative attainments. One can enter deep concentration and emerge from it; that concentration is not “unshakeable” because it ceases. The liberation described here does not cease. It is not a state one enters and leaves — it is an irreversible transformation.

The discourse’s final statement

The discourse ends with one of the Buddha’s clearest statements of teleology: “So this spiritual life does not have gains, honor, and fame as its benefit, or accomplishment of virtue as its benefit, or accomplishment of concentration as its benefit, or knowledge and vision as its benefit. Rather, it is the unshakeable liberation of mind that is the goal of this spiritual life, its heartwood, its culmination.”

This is a rare moment of explicit purpose-statement. The Buddha is not merely describing a hierarchy; he is declaring the telos of the entire path. The implication: if you don’t know what you’re aiming for, you cannot know when you’ve arrived — and you will stop too early.

Three questions Western students often ask

“If virtue, concentration, and knowledge are not the goal, why practice them?” Because they are the route to the goal. You cannot reach heartwood without passing through bark and sapwood. The discourse does not say “skip these stages”; it says “do not stop at these stages.” Each level is necessary but not sufficient. The danger is not in achieving virtue or concentration — it is in being satisfied with them as final. A tree without bark has no heartwood; but bark alone is not what you came for.

“Is this a criticism of Devadatta specifically?” The occasion is Devadatta’s departure, and the first level (gains, honor, fame) fits his story. But the teaching is structural, not biographical. It applies to any practitioner at any level. The person who stops at virtue (common among devout lay practitioners) is making the same structural error as the person who stops at gains — just at a deeper level of the tree. The discourse universalizes the lesson from a specific historical event.

“What is the difference between ‘knowledge and vision’ and ‘liberation’? Aren’t they the same thing?” No. “Knowledge and vision” (ñāṇadassana) includes psychic powers, the divine eye, and insight into impermanence — but it does not necessarily include the destruction of the defilements. One can see clearly (insight) without being freed (liberation). The tradition records practitioners with knowledge and vision who still had residual defilements. Liberation means the defilements are destroyed and do not return. Knowledge shows you the situation; liberation transforms it irreversibly.

Key terms

sāra — heartwood; essence; core. The central metaphor: the hardest, most durable part of the tree, which is also the part that cannot be reached without passing through the outer layers. Used figuratively for the ultimate goal of the spiritual life.
akuppā cetovimutti — the unshakeable liberation of mind. The heartwood of the path. “Unshakeable” (akuppā) means irreversible — unlike temporary meditative attainments, this liberation does not cease or recede. It is the destruction of the defilements.
lābhasakkhārasiloka — gains, honor, and fame. The outermost layer — branches and leaves. The most superficial attainment: being known, being respected, receiving material support. Not inherently wrong, but catastrophically inadequate as a goal.
sīlasampadā — accomplishment of virtue. The bark of the tree. Genuine ethical conduct — non-harm, truthfulness, restraint. Necessary for the path but not the path’s destination.
samādhisampadā — accomplishment of concentration. The sapwood. Meditative immersion, including the absorptions (jhāna). Deep and valuable, but still instrumental.
ñāṇadassana — knowledge and vision. The inner sapwood. Includes supernormal knowledge, the divine eye, and direct insight into impermanence and conditionality. Very close to the heartwood, but distinct from irreversible liberation.
pabbajjā — going forth. The act of leaving the household life for homelessness. The beginning of the search for heartwood — the moment one enters the forest with a specific purpose.
pamatta — negligent; heedless. The state that follows satisfaction at an intermediate level. When one is “pleased” and thinks one’s purpose is fulfilled, negligence follows — and with it, stagnation.
Devadatta — the Buddha’s cousin and a mendicant who caused a schism in the Saṅgha. The occasion for this discourse, though not named in the main teaching. His story exemplifies the danger of stopping at the level of gains and fame.

The text

MN 29 is a compact discourse with a single sustained simile applied to five levels. The pattern repeats with minimal variation; repetitive language is condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The occasion

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, on Vulture Peak, not long after Devadatta had left.

§2There, referring to Devadatta, the Buddha addressed the mendicants:

“Mendicants, a certain person here goes forth from the lay life to homelessness because of faith. They think, ‘I am swamped by rebirth, old age, and death; by sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress. I am swamped by suffering, mired in suffering. Hopefully I can find an end to this entire mass of suffering.’

Branches and leaves: gains, honor, and fame

§3Having gone forth in this way, they achieve gains, honor, and fame. They are pleased with those gains, honor, and fame, and their intention is fulfilled. Because of those gains they praise themselves and disparage others: ‘I have gains, honor, and fame; but those other mendicants are obscure and unknown.’

They don’t stir up enthusiasm or make an effort to realize those other things that are superior and sublime beyond gains, honor, and fame. They become negligent and fall into decline.

Suppose a person who needs heartwood, who is seeking heartwood, who goes searching for heartwood, came to a great tree standing with heartwood. Passing over the heartwood, the inner sapwood, the sapwood, and the bark, they would cut off the branches and leaves and carry them away, thinking they were heartwood. A person with good eyesight would see them and say, ‘This person didn’t know the heartwood, inner sapwood, sapwood, bark, or branches and leaves. That’s why they cut off just branches and leaves, thinking they were heartwood. Whatever they need to do with heartwood, their purpose won’t be served.’

Bark: accomplishment of virtue

§4Here, a certain person goes forth from the lay life to homelessness because of faith. They achieve gains, honor, and fame. They are not pleased with those gains, and their intention is not fulfilled. They don’t praise themselves or disparage others on that account. They stir up enthusiasm and make an effort to realize other things that are superior and sublime beyond gains. They are not negligent and do not fall into decline.

They achieve accomplishment of virtue. They are pleased with that accomplishment of virtue, and their intention is fulfilled. Because of that virtue they praise themselves and disparage others: ‘I have good virtue; I have good character. But those other mendicants have bad virtue; they have bad character.’

They don’t stir up enthusiasm or make an effort to realize those other things that are superior and sublime beyond accomplishment of virtue. They become negligent and fall into decline.

Suppose a person who needs heartwood came to a great tree. Passing over the heartwood, the inner sapwood, and the sapwood, they would strip off the bark and carry it away, thinking it was heartwood. A person with good eyesight would see them and say, ‘This person didn’t know the heartwood. Whatever they need to do with heartwood, their purpose won’t be served.’

Sapwood: accomplishment of concentration

§5Here, a certain person goes forth … They are not pleased with accomplishment of virtue, and their intention is not fulfilled. They stir up enthusiasm and make an effort to realize other things superior and sublime beyond virtue.

They achieve accomplishment of concentration. They are pleased with that accomplishment of concentration, and their intention is fulfilled. Because of that concentration they praise themselves and disparage others: ‘I have immersion; I am collected. But those other mendicants have no immersion; they are not collected.’

They don’t stir up enthusiasm or make an effort to realize those other things that are superior and sublime beyond accomplishment of concentration. They become negligent and fall into decline.

Suppose a person who needs heartwood came to a great tree. Passing over the heartwood and the inner sapwood, they would cut off the sapwood and carry it away, thinking it was heartwood. A person with good eyesight would see them and say, ‘This person didn’t know the heartwood. Whatever they need to do with heartwood, their purpose won’t be served.’

Inner sapwood: knowledge and vision

§6Here, a certain person goes forth … They are not pleased with accomplishment of concentration, and their intention is not fulfilled. They stir up enthusiasm and make an effort to realize other things superior and sublime beyond concentration.

They achieve knowledge and vision. They are pleased with that knowledge and vision, and their intention is fulfilled. Because of that knowledge and vision they praise themselves and disparage others: ‘I live knowing and seeing. But those other mendicants live without knowing and seeing.’

They don’t stir up enthusiasm or make an effort to realize those other things that are superior and sublime beyond knowledge and vision. They become negligent and fall into decline.

Suppose a person who needs heartwood came to a great tree. Passing over the heartwood, they would cut off the inner sapwood and carry it away, thinking it was heartwood. A person with good eyesight would see them and say, ‘This person didn’t know the heartwood. Whatever they need to do with heartwood, their purpose won’t be served.’

Heartwood: the unshakeable liberation of mind

§7Here, a certain person goes forth from the lay life to homelessness because of faith. They achieve gains, honor, and fame. They are not pleased with those gains, and their intention is not fulfilled. They achieve accomplishment of virtue. They are not pleased with that accomplishment of virtue, and their intention is not fulfilled. They achieve accomplishment of concentration. They are not pleased with that accomplishment of concentration, and their intention is not fulfilled. They achieve knowledge and vision. They are not pleased with that knowledge and vision, and their intention is not fulfilled.

They stir up enthusiasm and make an effort to realize other things superior and sublime beyond knowledge and vision. They are not negligent and do not fall into decline.

They achieve the unshakeable liberation of mind. It is not possible that they will fall into decline from that unshakeable liberation of mind. There is no condition for that.

Suppose a person who needs heartwood came to a great tree standing with heartwood. They would cut off just the heartwood, knowing it was heartwood. Whatever they need to do with heartwood, their purpose will be served.

The purpose of this spiritual life

§8So this spiritual life, mendicants, does not have gains, honor, and fame as its benefit, or accomplishment of virtue as its benefit, or accomplishment of concentration as its benefit, or knowledge and vision as its benefit. Rather, it is the unshakeable liberation of mind that is the goal of this spiritual life, its heartwood, its culmination.”

That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved of what the Buddha said.

Self-check quiz

Ten questions. Click an answer to see immediate feedback. No score is recorded — this is for your own checking.

Question 1 of 10
What historical event prompted the Buddha to give this discourse?
Correct: B. The discourse opens with an explicit reference to Devadatta’s departure. This provides the occasion but not the limit of the teaching: the simile applies to anyone who stops at an intermediate attainment, not only to those motivated by fame.
Question 2 of 10
In the heartwood simile, what does the heartwood represent?
Correct: D. The heartwood is the unshakeable liberation of mind. The word akuppā (unshakeable) distinguishes it from temporary attainments: unlike concentration or knowledge, this liberation does not cease. It is the explicit goal, heartwood, and culmination of the spiritual life.
Question 3 of 10
What do “branches and leaves” represent in the simile?
Correct: A. Gains, honor, and fame are the outermost layer — the most visible but least substantial part of the tree. The historical context (Devadatta’s desire for leadership and fame) makes this the most pointed level, but the structural argument applies equally to all four non-heartwood levels.
Question 4 of 10
What is the correct order from outermost to innermost in the simile?
Correct: C. The order maps onto: gains/fame (branches) → virtue (bark) → concentration (sapwood) → knowledge and vision (inner sapwood) → unshakeable liberation (heartwood). Each layer is genuinely closer to the core but none of the first four is the core itself.
Question 5 of 10
What psychological pattern does the discourse identify at each intermediate level?
Correct: B. The discourse identifies a structural mechanism: satisfaction → complacency → self-praise and comparison → negligence → stagnation. This pattern is identical at every level, showing that the danger is not specific to any one attainment but inherent in treating any intermediate achievement as final.
Question 6 of 10
Does the discourse say that virtue, concentration, and knowledge are worthless?
Correct: D. The simile preserves the value of each layer. You cannot reach heartwood without passing through bark and sapwood — they are part of the tree’s structure. The discourse warns against mistaking these layers for the heartwood, not against having them. Each is necessary but not sufficient.
Question 7 of 10
What does akuppā (unshakeable) mean in the context of liberation?
Correct: A. The discourse explicitly states: “It is not possible that they will fall into decline from that unshakeable liberation of mind. There is no condition for that.” This distinguishes final liberation from all temporary attainments, including deep concentration (which one enters and exits) and knowledge (which one can have without being freed).
Question 8 of 10
What is the difference between “knowledge and vision” (inner sapwood) and “liberation” (heartwood)?
Correct: C. One can see clearly without being freed. The tradition records practitioners with extraordinary knowledge and vision who still had residual defilements. Liberation is not a higher degree of seeing — it is an irreversible transformation in which the defilements are destroyed. Knowledge shows the situation; liberation transforms it permanently.
Question 9 of 10
The discourse says the person who takes the wrong part of the tree “didn’t know the heartwood.” What does this imply about the practitioner?
Correct: B. The simile’s epistemological point: if you don’t know what heartwood looks like, you will mistake something else for it. The practitioner’s error is not laziness or immorality — it is confusion about the goal. They genuinely think their purpose is fulfilled because they don’t know what fulfillment actually is.
Question 10 of 10
How does the discourse end?
Correct: D. The discourse ends with an explicit purpose-statement: a rare moment where the Buddha declares the telos of the entire path in a single sentence. The spiritual life has one goal — the unshakeable liberation of mind — and everything else, however valuable, is instrumental to that goal.

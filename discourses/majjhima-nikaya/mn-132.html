Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 132

Ānanda and One Fine Night

Ānandabhaddekarattasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, in the assembly hall
Speakers
Venerable Ānanda, teaching the assembly on his own initiative; the Buddha, testing and confirming
Form
A disciple teaches unprompted, is questioned, and is found to have gotten it exactly right
Length
~5 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No specific Āgama match is noted in the available commentary
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — the content repeats MN 131 exactly; the interest is entirely in the frame

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-thirty-second

This short discourse contains no new teaching at all — every word of its central verse and analysis is identical to MN 131. What it contains instead is a small, revealing scene: Ānanda, entirely on his own initiative, teaching this exact verse to a hall full of mendicants, word-perfect, without having been asked to. The Buddha, overhearing only that someone was giving this talk, walks in and asks Ānanda to repeat it — not as a correction, but as a check. What Ānanda recites back is precise. This is a discourse about a disciple who has genuinely absorbed the teaching, not merely memorized it, being tested and found sound.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Ānanda teaches the “one fine night” verse and its analysis to the assembled mendicants entirely on his own initiative; when the Buddha, overhearing that someone was giving this talk, asks him to repeat it exactly, Ānanda recites the identical verse and analysis word for word — and the Buddha simply says, “Good, good, Ānanda,” before teaching it once more himself.

A talk overheard

The scene opens mid-lesson: Ānanda is already “educating, encouraging, firing up, and inspiring the mendicants in the assembly hall with a Dhamma talk on the topic of the summary recital and the analysis of the one who has one fine night.” The Buddha, coming out of retreat in the late afternoon, walks in and asks simply, “Who was inspiring the mendicants…?” He didn’t assign this talk, and doesn’t yet know its content — he has only heard that it happened.

The question, and the exact answer

Rather than assuming anything, the Buddha asks Ānanda directly: “But in what way were you inspiring the mendicants?” Ānanda’s reply is not a paraphrase or a summary — he recites the entire verse, “don’t run back to the past, don’t anticipate the future…” through to its closing line, and then works through the complete analysis: what it means to run to the past (mustering delight in each of the five aggregates as they were), what it means to anticipate the future (the same delight, aimed forward), and the full twenty-fold account of what it means to falter, or not falter, amid the present. It matches MN 131 precisely, segment for segment.

“Good, good, Ānanda”

The Buddha’s response is unqualified: “Good, good, Ānanda. It’s good that you were inspiring the mendicants with a talk on the summary recital and the analysis of the one who has one fine night.” There is no correction, no addition, no hedge. And then, notably, the Buddha does not simply leave it there — he “repeated the verses and analysis once more” himself, teaching the identical material a second time to the same gathering. The repetition is not redundant; it is a form of endorsement, placing the Buddha’s own authority visibly behind exactly what Ānanda had already taught.

A different kind of check

This discourse forms an instructive pair with MN 126, where the novice Bhūmija, having improvised an answer for Prince Jayasena, rushes anxiously to the Buddha afterward to ask whether he had “misrepresented” the teaching. Here, the dynamic runs the other way: Ānanda teaches confidently, without seeking approval first, and it is the Buddha who initiates the check, out of ordinary curiosity about what was being taught in his absence — not out of doubt. Both patterns end the same way, with full confirmation, but they show two different postures a disciple can hold toward a teacher’s authority: anxious verification and confident initiative, both, in the end, validated.

Key terms

bhaddekaratta — “one fine night”: see MN 131 for the full verse and its line-by-line analysis, repeated here without alteration.
“educating, encouraging, firing up, and inspiring” — the standard formula for a skillful Dhamma talk, used here of Ānanda’s own unprompted teaching to the assembly.
“Good, good” (sādhu sādhu) — the Buddha’s formula of full, unqualified approval, given here without any correction or addition.
the doubled teaching — the Buddha’s decision to repeat the verse and analysis himself immediately after Ānanda’s recitation, lending his own authority visibly to what a disciple had already taught correctly.

The text

The discourse in full: the Buddha overhearing Ānanda’s talk, his question, and Ānanda’s complete recitation of the verse and its analysis — identical to MN 131 — followed by the Buddha’s approval and his own repetition of the same material. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A talk overheard

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove. Now at that time Venerable Ānanda was educating, encouraging, firing up, and inspiring the mendicants in the assembly hall with a Dhamma talk on the topic of the summary recital and the analysis of the one who has one fine night. Then in the late afternoon, the Buddha came out of retreat, went to the assembly hall, sat down, and asked: “Who was inspiring the mendicants with a talk on the summary recital and the analysis of the one who has one fine night?” “It was Venerable Ānanda, sir.” The Buddha said to Ānanda: “But in what way were you inspiring the mendicants?”

The exact answer

§3“I was doing so in this way, sir,” replied Ānanda. “Don’t run back to the past, don’t anticipate the future. What’s past is left behind, the future has not arrived; and any present phenomenon you clearly discern in every case. The unfaltering, the unshakable: having known that, foster it. Today’s the day to keenly work — who knows, tomorrow may bring death! For there is no bargain to be struck with Death and his mighty horde. One who keenly meditates like this, tireless all night and day: that’s who has one fine night — so declares the peaceful sage.

And how do you run back to the past? You muster delight there, thinking: ‘I had such form in the past… such feeling… perception… choices… consciousness in the past.’ That’s how you run back to the past. And how do you not run back to the past? You don’t muster delight there in the same way.

And how do you anticipate the future? You muster delight there, thinking: ‘May I have such form in the future… such feeling… perception… choices… consciousness in the future.’ And how do you not anticipate the future? You don’t muster delight there in the same way.

And how do you falter amid presently arisen phenomena? It’s when an unlearned ordinary person, untrained in the teaching of the noble ones, regards form as self, self as having form, form in self, or self in form — and feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness in the same fourfold way. And how do you not falter? It’s when a learned noble disciple, trained in that teaching, doesn’t regard any of the five aggregates in any of those four ways. That’s how you don’t falter amid presently arisen phenomena.

‘Don’t run back to the past, don’t anticipate the future… that’s who has one fine night — so declares the peaceful sage.’ That’s how I was inspiring the mendicants with a talk on the summary recital and the analysis of the one who has one fine night.”

“Good, good, Ānanda”

§11–19“Good, good, Ānanda. It’s good that you were inspiring the mendicants with a talk on the summary recital and the analysis of the one who has one fine night.” And the Buddha repeated the verses and analysis once more. That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Ānanda approved what the Buddha said.

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 69

With Goliyāni

Goliyānisutta

Setting
Near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground
Speakers
Venerable Sāriputta, instructing the assembled mendicants; occasioned by Goliyāni (Gulissāni), a wilderness monk of boorish behaviour; with a closing question from Venerable Mahāmoggallāna
Form
A code of conduct — Sāriputta lays out, point by point, what a forest-dwelling monk who comes to live in the community ought to observe, lest his solitude count for nothing.
Length
~10 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 26 (Madhyama-āgama 26)
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — accessible; a practical checklist, but the sting is in the closing twist — it binds every monk, the village-dweller most of all.

Why this discourse, sixty-ninth

A monk lives alone in the forest, austere and self-reliant. Does that solitude make him exemplary? Not by itself, says Venerable Sāriputta. When such a monk comes down to live among the community, his conduct is on display — and if it is coarse, his wilderness life becomes a reproach rather than a credit. This discourse is the plain, exacting list of what the forest monk owes his companions.

The occasion is a particular man: Goliyāni — whom the translator renders by the variant Gulissāni — “a wilderness mendicant of boorish behaviour” who had come down to the midst of the Saṅgha on some business. He is known to the tradition for nothing else; he survives only as the negative example that prompted a teaching. Seeing how he carried himself, Sāriputta does not rebuke him to his face but addresses the mendicants generally, drawing out of the moment a complete picture of how a forest monk should behave when he rejoins the community.

The form is a refrain. Each duty is stated, then guarded by the same warning: if the wilderness monk neglects it, “there’ll be some who say, What’s the point of this wilderness venerable’s staying alone and autonomous in the wilderness, since he…?” The forest is supposed to produce something; if the monk who lives there cannot sit without crowding his elders, cannot answer a question on the teaching, cannot guard his senses or his tongue, then his celebrated solitude is exposed as empty. Sāriputta’s list runs from the small courtesies of communal life up to the highest meditative attainments — and at the end, Mahāmoggallāna asks the question that turns the whole teaching outward.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A wilderness monk who comes to live among the community must conduct himself well — in courtesy, restraint, learning, and meditation alike — or his solitude is worth nothing; and Mahāmoggallāna adds the sting: these duties bind not only forest monks but all monks, the village-dweller most of all.

Why a boor in the forest is worse than a boor anywhere

The premise is unspoken but everywhere present: the wilderness monk has a reputation. To live “alone and autonomous in the wilderness” (seri, “self-moving,” living at one’s own pleasure like a deer in open country) is, in the eyes of the laity and the order, a mark of seriousness. The forest is austere; it has no audience, no comforts, no one to defer to. A monk who chooses it is presumed to have chosen the hard path for the right reasons. That presumption is precisely what makes coarse behaviour, when he returns to the Saṅgha, so jarring. Each failing draws the same retort — “What’s the point of his living in the wilderness, since he…?” The expectation is the standard he is measured against. Goliyāni had earned the title and not the substance.

The small courtesies of communal life

Sāriputta begins not with meditation but with manners. The forest monk should respect and revere his spiritual companions; should be careful where he sits — “so that I don’t intrude on the senior monks and I don’t block the junior monks from a seat”; should know even the supplementary regulations (the minor rules of communal etiquette); should not enter the village too early or return too late; should not visit families before or after the meal; should not be restless and fickle, nor scurrilous and loose-tongued; and should be easy to admonish, with good friends. The list is striking for how ordinary it is. A man fresh from months of solitude might think such trifles beneath him — but it is exactly here, in the friction of shared space, that the quality of his solitude shows. Real practice makes a person easier to live with, not harder.

The disciplines of the path

From manners the list climbs to training. The forest monk should guard the sense doors, eat in moderation, be committed to wakefulness, energetic, mindful, possessed of immersion (samādhi), and wise. These are the familiar rungs of the gradual training — sense-restraint, moderation in eating, and dedication to wakefulness form a standard triad, the groundwork on which mindfulness and concentration are built. The point of naming them here is continuity: the same disciplines that the forest is supposed to foster are the ones the community will look for. Solitude is not a holiday from the training; it is meant to be the training intensified.

What he should be able to answer

The list culminates in three demands that concern not only practice but understanding. The forest monk should make an effort in the teaching and training (abhidhamma and abhivinaya — here meaning discussion “about” the teaching and the discipline, before those words came to name separate bodies of text); he should meditate to realise the peaceful formless liberations; and he should meditate to realise the superhuman state. The recurring fear is being “stumped” — for people will ask the forest monk such questions, assuming his solitude has borne fruit. To be unable to answer is to be exposed. The last item is pointed: the “superhuman state” is named in the singular, and the warning is the sharpest of all — that he “doesn’t know the goal for which he went forth.” That goal is liberation itself; the Chinese parallel makes it explicit as the ending of the defilements.

The turn: not only forest monks

Then the discourse pivots. Mahāmoggallāna asks: “Should these things be undertaken and followed only by wilderness monks, or by those who live within a village as well?” Sāriputta’s answer is the whole point in one stroke: “by wilderness monks, and still more by those who live within a village.” The list was never really about the forest. The forest monk is held to a high standard because solitude is presumed to produce it — but the village monk, surrounded by families, food, conversation, and temptation, needs every one of these disciplines more, not less. The reader who has spent the discourse picturing some other, more austere monk is gently turned around: the standard is yours. Austerity of setting earns no exemption from conduct; the closer you live to temptation, the higher the bar.

Sāriputta as teacher

It is worth noticing who is speaking. This is not the Buddha but his chief disciple, “foremost in wisdom,” teaching in his own right — and the discourse is a small portrait of how he does it. He does not shame Goliyāni publicly; he uses the occasion to instruct everyone. He builds from the smallest courtesy to the highest attainment without a break, treating manners and liberation as one continuous fabric of the holy life. And he leaves room for a colleague’s question to complete the teaching, rather than closing it himself. The Majjhima preserves several such discourses by Sāriputta; they show the early Saṅgha as a community that could teach itself, the wisdom of the Buddha carried forward in disciples who had made it their own.

Key terms

āraññika — a wilderness or forest monk; one who keeps the ascetic practice of dwelling in remote places. The whole discourse measures such a monk against the reputation his way of life carries.
seri — “autonomous,” literally “self-moving” — living at one’s own pleasure, like a deer roaming free. The prized independence of the forest life, and exactly what makes coarse conduct, on return, ring hollow.
ābhisamācārika — the “supplementary regulations”: the minor rules of communal etiquette and good conduct, as distinct from the rules fundamental to the holy life. The forest monk is expected to know even these.
suvaco — “easy to admonish”: open to correction, the opposite of stubborn. Paired here with “good friends” (kalyāṇamitta) — the company one keeps and the spirit in which one takes feedback.
abhidhamma, abhivinaya — discussion “about” the teaching and “about” the training. In the suttas these are living conversations, not yet the separate analytical texts the words later came to name. The forest monk should not be stumped by them.
uttarimanussadhamma — the “superhuman state.” Usually plural (absorptions, higher powers, stages of realisation), but singular here, denoting the one goal for which a monk goes forth: liberation, the ending of the defilements.

The text

The full discourse. Each duty in Sāriputta’s list is guarded by the same refrain — “there’ll be some who say, ‘What’s the point…?’” The opening, the first duties, and the closing exchange are given in full; the long middle list is given once in the pattern and then condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral), who renders the name by the variant Gulissāni.

The occasion

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground. Now at that time a wilderness mendicant of boorish behaviour named Gulissāni had come down to the midst of the Saṅgha on some business. There Venerable Sāriputta spoke to the mendicants about Gulissāni:

Courtesy among companions

§3–4“Reverends, a wilderness monk who has come to stay in the Saṅgha should have respect and reverence for his spiritual companions. If he doesn’t, there’ll be some who say: ‘What’s the point of this wilderness venerable’s staying alone and autonomous in the wilderness, since he has no respect and reverence for his spiritual companions?’ That’s why he should have respect and reverence for his spiritual companions. He should be careful where he sits, thinking: ‘I shall sit so that I don’t intrude on the senior monks and I don’t block the junior monks from a seat.’ … He should know even the supplementary regulations — for if he doesn’t, there’ll be some who say: ‘What’s the point of his living in the wilderness, since he doesn’t even know the supplementary regulations?’”

Conduct in the village

§5–6“He shouldn’t enter the village too early or return too late in the day. If he does, there’ll be some who say: ‘What’s the point of his living in the wilderness, since he enters the village too early or returns too late?’ And he shouldn’t visit families before or after the meal. If he does, there’ll be some who say: ‘This wilderness venerable, staying alone and autonomous, must be used to wandering about at the wrong time, since he behaves like this when he’s come to the Saṅgha.’”

Temperament and tongue

§7–9“He shouldn’t be restless and fickle — or some will say he must be used to it, since he behaves so on coming to the Saṅgha. He shouldn’t be scurrilous and loose-tongued. And he should be easy to admonish, with good friends; for if he is hard to admonish, with bad friends, there’ll be some who say: ‘What’s the point of his living alone in the wilderness, since he’s hard to admonish, with bad friends?’”

Sāriputta carries the same refrain up through the disciplines of the path. A wilderness monk should guard the sense doors; should eat in moderation; should be committed to wakefulness, energetic, mindful, possessed of immersion, and wise. In each case, if he is not, there’ll be some who say, “What’s the point of this wilderness venerable’s staying alone and autonomous in the wilderness, since he…?” — and so, for each, “that’s why a wilderness monk should” cultivate it.

What he should be able to answer

§17–19“A wilderness monk should make an effort in regards to the teaching and training. There are those who will question him about the teaching and training; if he is stumped, there’ll be some who say: ‘What’s the point of his living in the wilderness, since he is stumped by a question about the teaching and training?’ That’s why he should make an effort to learn it. He should practise meditation to realise the peaceful liberations that are formless, transcending form — for people will ask him about the formless liberations, and if he is stumped, there’ll be some who say the same. And he should practise meditation to realise the superhuman state. There are those who will question him about the superhuman state; if he is stumped, there’ll be some who say: ‘What’s the point of this wilderness venerable’s staying alone and autonomous in the wilderness, since he doesn’t know the goal for which he went forth?’ That’s why a wilderness monk should practise meditation to realise the superhuman state.”

Not only forest monks

§19When Venerable Sāriputta said this, Venerable Mahāmoggallāna said to him, “Reverend Sāriputta, should these things be undertaken and followed only by wilderness monks, or by those who live within a village as well?” “Reverend Moggallāna, these things should be undertaken and followed by wilderness monks, and still more by those who live within a village.”

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 49

On the Invitation of Brahmā

Brahmanimantanikasutta

Setting
Told at Sāvatthī; the events recounted take place in the realm of Brahmā, reached from the Subhaga Forest near Ukkaṭṭhā
Speakers
The Buddha, Baka the Brahmā (a high god), and Māra the Wicked
Form
A first-person story the Buddha tells the monks — he visits a god who believes his heaven eternal and himself supreme, exposes the conceit, displays knowledge beyond the god’s, and unmasks Māra speaking through the divine assembly.
Length
~16 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 78 (Madhyama-āgama 78, “Brahmā Invites the Buddha”); the figure of Baka also appears at SN 6.4
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — challenging. A dramatic narrative carrying some of the deepest material in the canon: the critique of the creator-god, the analysis of conceiving (“I am earth”), and the cryptic verse on non-manifesting consciousness.

Why this discourse, forty-ninth

This is the discourse in which the Buddha confronts God. Baka, the highest divinity of his world-system, is convinced that his heaven is permanent, that he is its almighty maker, and that there is no escape beyond it. The Buddha appears in his realm to tell him, gently and then devastatingly, that he is wrong — and that standing invisibly behind his throne is Māra, the lord of death and delusion.

The drama works on two levels. On the surface it is a contest of power and knowledge between the ascetic Gotama and a god: who knows more, who can vanish from whom. Beneath that it is the Buddhist critique of theism in its sharpest form. Baka’s error is not that he is evil but that he has mistaken a very long life for an eternal one, and a very high place for the highest. Having forgotten his own past and being unable to see the realms above him, he has crowned himself creator of a cosmos he does not even fully perceive.

The deepest turn comes when the Buddha explains how he is free where Baka is bound. He does not merely know more realms; he relates to all of them differently. Directly knowing earth as earth — and knowing what lies outside the entire field of experience — he does not identify with any of it: “I have not become earth… I do not think ‘earth is mine.’” And he points, in a famously cryptic verse, to “consciousness where no form appears, infinite, luminous all-round” — that which no god, and no “all,” can contain. The name of the discourse comes from its two poles: the silencing of Māra, and the invitation of Brahmā.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Buddha visits Baka Brahmā, who believes his heaven eternal and himself the supreme creator; the Buddha exposes this as ignorance, demonstrates knowledge of realms Baka cannot see, shows that he identifies with none of it, points to what transcends all phenomena, and unmasks Māra hiding behind the god’s authority.

A note on names

Bhikkhu Sujato translates brahmā as “Divinity” and brahmaloka as “the realm of Divinity,” so the text below reads “Baka the Divinity.” This is the god the tradition calls Baka Brahmā — not the creator of monotheism but a being who, through immense merit, has been reborn at the summit of the cosmos and mistaken his exalted, long-lived position for eternity and supremacy. “Māra the Wicked” is Māra pāpimā, the personification of death, temptation, and the pull of continued existence.

The structure of the discourse

SectionWhat happens
1. Baka’s conceitBaka Brahmā holds the view that his realm is permanent, eternal, complete, with no escape beyond it. The Buddha reads his mind and appears before him.
2. The first correctionThe Buddha declares Baka “lost in ignorance”: he calls the impermanent permanent, and denies the escape that does exist.
3. Māra intervenesMāra possesses a member of the divine assembly and warns the Buddha not to challenge the god, praising Brahmā as the almighty Maker and Creator. The Buddha names him: “I know you, Wicked One.”
4. The contest of knowledgeBaka boasts of his power. The Buddha grants its extent — a thousand world-systems — then names three higher realms Baka cannot see, including the one Baka himself fell from and forgot.
5. Non-identificationThe heart of the teaching: directly knowing earth, water… up to “the all,” the Buddha has not become any of it, nor thinks “this is mine.”
6. The vanishing contestBaka tries to disappear from the Buddha and cannot. The Buddha disappears from Baka — heard but not seen — and speaks a verse on the danger in all existence.
7. Māra’s last ployMāra urges the Buddha to keep his realization to himself and not teach. The Buddha refuses, affirming that he alone is truly awakened, the defilements cut off “like a palm stump.”

Baka’s error: mistaking the high for the highest

Baka’s view is the eternalist conceit (sassata-diṭṭhi) lifted to the top of the universe. He says of his realm: “This is permanent… this is where there’s no being born, growing old, dying… and there’s no other escape beyond this.” Every clause is false in the same way. His heaven is sublime and astonishingly long-lived — but it is still within saṃsāra, still impermanent, still a place one is born into and falls from. Baka has simply lived so long that he has forgotten arriving, and so high that he cannot see above. His divinity is real; his eternity is a memory failure.

Māra behind the throne

The discourse’s most arresting claim is structural: when the supreme god’s authority is invoked to silence the truth, it is Māra who speaks. Twice Māra possesses a member of the divine assembly — first to defend Baka’s eternalism, then to discourage the Buddha from teaching. The Buddha says plainly that “the Divinity, the Divinity’s assembly, and the retinue of Divinity have all fallen into your hands; they’re under your sway.” The point is sharp: a religion of cosmic permanence, however exalted, serves death’s purposes, because it binds beings to continued existence. The defender of God and the lord of death turn out to be the same voice.

The heart of it: “I have not become earth”

The pivotal passage is easy to skim and worth slowing down for. Of earth, water, fire, air… and finally of “the all,” the Buddha says: directly knowing it as it is, “I have not become earth, I have not become in earth, I have not become as earth, I have not become one who thinks ‘earth is mine’, I have not affirmed earth.” This is the analysis of conceiving (maññanā) that opens the entire Majjhima collection in MN 1. Baka is bound because he identifies with his realm — he is Brahmā, the realm is his, he affirms it. The Buddha knows every layer of experience just as intimately, but takes none of it as self, as ground, or as possession. That non-identification, not superior cosmic real estate, is what makes him free.

“Consciousness, non-manifesting, infinite, luminous all-round”

When Baka scoffs that the Buddha’s “beyond all” may prove “vacuous and hollow,” the Buddha answers with one of the most discussed verses in the canon: “Consciousness where no form appears, infinite, luminous all-round — that is what does not fall within the scope of experience characterized by earth, water… and the all.” The Pāli viññāṇaṃ anidassanaṃ is not a hidden eternal soul or a cosmic mind — that would simply be Baka’s error in subtler dress. It points, rather, to the mode of knowing of one who has stopped identifying: an awareness not established on any object, not “landing” on earth or god or all, and therefore unbounded. It names freedom from the inside, without making freedom into one more thing to grasp.

Why Māra fears the teaching

The closing movement reveals Māra’s real fear. He does not mind the Buddha being enlightened in private; he begs him only not to teach — “remain passive, dwelling in blissful meditation… this is better left unsaid.” The Buddha sees through it: “You think, ‘Those whom the ascetic Gotama teaches will go beyond my reach.’” A private awakening leaves Māra’s kingdom intact; a teaching that frees others empties it. The discourse ends with the Buddha’s unshakable affirmation that he is the fully awakened one — the defilements “cut off at the root, made like a palm stump, unable to arise in the future” — and that this remains true whether or not he ever opens his mouth.

Key terms

Baka brahmā — “Baka the Divinity”: a high god who, through vast merit, reached the summit of the cosmos and mistook his long, exalted life for eternity. He is sincere, not malevolent — which is what makes his delusion instructive.
Māra pāpimā — “Māra the Wicked”: the personification of death, temptation, and the gravitational pull of continued existence. Here he speaks through the divine assembly, defending cosmic permanence and discouraging the teaching.
sassata-diṭṭhi — eternalism; the view that some realm or self is permanent and unchanging. Baka’s conceit is this view enthroned at the top of the universe: his heaven declared eternal, with “no escape beyond.”
maññanā — conceiving; the mind’s habit of taking experience as “I,” “in me,” “mine.” The Buddha’s freedom is described precisely as not conceiving earth, gods, or “the all” in any of these ways — the teaching that opens MN 1.
viññāṇaṃ anidassanaṃ — non-manifesting consciousness; “consciousness where no form appears, infinite, luminous all-round.” A pointer to the unbounded knowing of one who no longer establishes awareness on any object — not a hidden eternal self.
tālāvatthukata — “made like a palm stump”: a stock image for defilements cut off at the root, incapable of regrowth, “like a palm tree with its crown cut off.” The Buddha’s closing proof that his awakening is final.

The text

The full discourse, given here complete. Sujato renders brahmā as “Divinity” (the god the tradition calls Baka Brahmā). The long repeated list — earth, water, fire, air, creatures, gods, the Progenitor, the Divinity, three god-realms, the Vanquisher, and “the all” — is abbreviated with “…” as in the source. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Baka’s misconception

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. There the Buddha addressed the mendicants, “Mendicants!” “Venerable sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

§2“This one time, mendicants, I was staying near Ukkaṭṭhā, in the Subhaga Forest at the root of a magnificent sal tree. Now at that time Baka the Divinity had the following harmful misconception: ‘This is permanent, this is everlasting, this is eternal, this is whole, this is not liable to pass away. For this is where there’s no being born, growing old, dying, passing away, or being reborn. And there’s no other escape beyond this.’

§3Then I knew what Baka the Divinity was thinking. As easily as a strong person would extend or contract their arm, I vanished from the Subhaga Forest and reappeared in that realm of divinity. Baka saw me coming off in the distance and said, ‘Come, good fellow! Welcome, good fellow! It’s been a long time since you took the opportunity to come here. For this is permanent, this is everlasting, this is eternal, this is complete, this is not liable to pass away. For this is where there’s no being born, growing old, dying, passing away, or being reborn. And there’s no other escape beyond this.’

§4When he had spoken, I said to him, ‘Oh lord, Baka the Divinity is lost in ignorance! Oh lord, Baka the Divinity is lost in ignorance! Because what is actually impermanent, not lasting, transient, incomplete, and liable to pass away, he says is permanent, everlasting, eternal, complete, and not liable to pass away. And where there is being born, growing old, dying, passing away, and being reborn, he says that there’s no being born, growing old, dying, passing away, or being reborn. And although there is another escape beyond this, he says that there’s no other escape beyond this.’

Māra speaks through the assembly

§5Then Māra the Wicked took possession of a member of the retinue of Divinity and said this to me, ‘Mendicant, mendicant! Don’t attack this one! Don’t attack this one! For this is the Divinity, the Great Divinity, the Vanquisher, the Unvanquished, the Universal Seer, the Wielder of Power, God Almighty, the Maker, the Creator, the First, the Begetter, the Controller, the Father of those who have been born and those yet to be born. There have been ascetics and brahmins before you, mendicant, who criticized and loathed earth, water, fire, air, creatures, gods, the Progenitor, and the Divinity. When their bodies broke up and their breath was cut off they were established in a lower realm. There have been ascetics and brahmins before you, mendicant, who praised and approved earth, water, fire, air, creatures, gods, the Progenitor, and the Divinity. When their bodies broke up and their breath was cut off they were established in a higher realm. So, mendicant, I tell you this: please, good fellow, do exactly what the Divinity says. Don’t go beyond the word of the Divinity. If you do, then you’ll end up like a person who, when approached by Lady Luck, would fend her off with a staff; or who, as they are falling over a cliff, would push away the ground with their hands and feet. Please, good fellow, do exactly what the Divinity says. Don’t go beyond the word of the Divinity. Do you not see the assembly of the Divinity gathered here?’ And so Māra the Wicked presented the assembly of the Divinity to me.

§6When he had spoken, I said to Māra, ‘I know you, Wicked One. Do not think, “He does not know me.” You are Māra the Wicked. And the Divinity, the Divinity’s assembly, and the retinue of Divinity have all fallen into your hands; they’re under your sway. And you think, “Maybe this one, too, has fallen into my hands; maybe he’s under my sway!” But I haven’t fallen into your hands; I’m not under your sway.’

Baka defends his view

§7When I had spoken, Baka the Divinity said to me, ‘But, good fellow, what I say is permanent, everlasting, eternal, complete, and not liable to pass away is in fact permanent… And where I say there’s no being born, growing old, dying, passing away, or being reborn there is in fact no being born… And when I say there’s no other escape beyond this there is in fact no other escape beyond this. There have been ascetics and brahmins in the world before you, mendicant, whose deeds of fervent mortification lasted as long as your entire life. When there was another escape beyond this they knew it, and when there was no other escape beyond this, they knew it. So, mendicant, I tell you this: you will never find another escape beyond this, and you will eventually get weary and frustrated. If you attach to earth, you will lie close to me, in my domain, subject to my will, and expendable. If you attach to water… fire… air… creatures… gods… the Progenitor… the Divinity, you will lie close to me, in my domain, subject to my will, and expendable.’

§8‘Divinity, I too know that if I attach to earth, I will lie close to you, in your domain, subject to your will, and expendable. If I attach to water… fire… air… creatures… gods… the Progenitor… the Divinity, I will lie close to you, in your domain, subject to your will, and expendable. And in addition, Divinity, I understand your range and your light: “That’s how powerful is Baka the Divinity, how illustrious and mighty.”’ ‘But in what way do you understand my range and my light?’

The contest of knowledge

§9‘A galaxy extends a thousand times as far as the moon and sun revolve and the shining ones light up the quarters. And there you wield your power. You know the high and low, the passionate and dispassionate, and the coming and going of sentient beings from this realm to another. That’s how I understand your range and your light.

§10But there are three other realms that you don’t know or see, but which I know and see. There is the realm named after the gods of streaming radiance. You passed away from there and were reborn here. You’ve dwelt here so long that you’ve forgotten about that, so you don’t know it or see it. But I know it and see it. So Divinity, I am not your equal in knowledge, let alone your inferior. Rather, I know more than you. There is the realm named after the gods of universal beauty… There is the realm named after the gods of abundant fruit, which you don’t know or see. But I know it and see it. So Divinity, I am not your equal in knowledge, let alone your inferior. Rather, I know more than you.

“I have not become earth”

§11Since directly knowing earth as earth, and since directly knowing that which does not fall within the scope of experience characterized by earth, I have not become earth, I have not become in earth, I have not become as earth, I have not become one who thinks ‘earth is mine’, I have not affirmed earth. So Divinity, I am not your equal in knowledge, let alone your inferior. Rather, I know more than you.’

The same formula is repeated in full for water, fire, air, creatures, gods, the Progenitor, the Divinity, the gods of streaming radiance, the gods of universal beauty, the gods of abundant fruit, and the Vanquisher — each time: “directly knowing it… I have not become it, not become in it, not become as it, not one who thinks ‘it is mine’, I have not affirmed it.”

§23‘Since directly knowing all as all, and since directly knowing that which does not fall within the scope of experience characterized by all, I have not become all, I have not become in all, I have not become as all, I have not become one who thinks ‘all is mine’, I have not affirmed all. So Divinity, I am not your equal in knowledge, let alone your inferior. Rather, I know more than you.’

§24‘Well, good fellow, if you have directly known that which does not fall within the scope of experience characterized by all, may that not be vacuous and hollow for you!’

§25‘Consciousness where no form appears, infinite, luminous all-round. That is what does not fall within the scope of experience characterized by earth, water, fire, air, creatures, gods, the Progenitor, the Divinity, the gods of streaming radiance, the gods of universal beauty, the gods of abundant fruit, the Vanquisher, and the all.’

The vanishing contest

§26‘Come then, good fellow, see me vanish from you!’ ‘Come on then, Divinity, vanish from me—if you can.’ Then Baka the Divinity said, ‘I will vanish from the ascetic Gotama! I will vanish from the ascetic Gotama!’ But he was unable to vanish from me. So I said to him, ‘Come then, Divinity, see me vanish from you!’ ‘Come on then, good fellow, vanish from me—if you can.’ Then I used my psychic power to will that my voice would extend so that Divinity, his assembly, and his retinue would hear me, but they would not see me. And while vanished I spoke this verse:

‘Seeing the danger in existence—
that life in any existence will cease to be—
I didn’t affirm any kind of existence,
and didn’t grasp at relishing.’

§28Then the Divinity, his assembly, and his retinue, their minds full of wonder and amazement, thought, ‘Oh lord, how incredible, how amazing! The ascetic Gotama has such psychic power and might! We’ve never before seen or heard of any other ascetic or brahmin with psychic power and might like the ascetic Gotama, who has gone forth from the Sakyan clan. Though people enjoy continued existence, loving it so much, he has extracted it, root and all.’

Māra’s final attempt

§29Then Māra the Wicked took possession of a member of the retinue of Divinity and said this to me, ‘If such is your understanding, good fellow, do not present it to your disciples or those gone forth! Do not teach this Dhamma to your disciples or those gone forth! Do not wish this for your disciples or those gone forth! There have been ascetics and brahmins before you, mendicant, who claimed to be perfected ones, fully awakened Buddhas. They presented, taught, and wished this for their disciples and those gone forth. When their bodies broke up and their breath was cut off they were established in a lower realm. But there have also been other ascetics and brahmins before you, mendicant, who claimed to be perfected ones, fully awakened Buddhas. They did not present, teach, or wish this for their disciples and those gone forth. When their bodies broke up and their breath was cut off they were established in a higher realm. So, mendicant, I tell you this: please, good fellow, remain passive, dwelling in blissful meditation in this life, for this is better left unsaid. Good fellow, do not instruct others.’

§30When he had spoken, I said to Māra, ‘I know you, Wicked One. Do not think, “He doesn’t know me.” You are Māra the Wicked. You don’t speak to me like this out of sympathy, but with no sympathy. For you think, “Those who the ascetic Gotama teaches will go beyond my reach.” Those who formerly claimed to be fully awakened Buddhas were not in fact fully awakened Buddhas. But I am. The Realized One remains such whether or not he teaches disciples. The Realized One remains as such whether or not he presents the teaching to disciples. Why is that? Because the Realized One has given up the defilements that are corrupting, leading to future lives, hurtful, resulting in suffering and future rebirth, old age, and death. He has cut them off at the root, made them like a palm stump, obliterated them so they are unable to arise in the future. Just as a palm tree with its crown cut off is incapable of further growth, the Realized One has given up the defilements… He has cut them off at the root, made them like a palm stump, obliterated them so they are unable to arise in the future.’”

§31And so, because of the silencing of Māra, and because of the invitation of the Divinity, the name of this discussion is “On the Invitation of Divinity”.

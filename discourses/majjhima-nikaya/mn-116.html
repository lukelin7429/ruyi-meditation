Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 116

At Isigili

Isigilisutta

Setting
Near Rājagaha, on Isigili Mountain, addressed to the mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha alone; the mendicants only answer “yes, sir”
Form
A frame story about five mountains, a name’s legend, and a roll call of 103 Independent Buddhas
Length
~7 minutes to read
Northern parallel
EĀ 38.7 shares only the opening frame, then diverges — just three names match
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — no argument to follow, but a puzzle-box of forgotten names

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-sixteenth

There is no debate here, no simile unpacked step by step, no meditator questioned about their attainment. Standing on a mountain outside Rājagaha, the Buddha simply points at the hills around him, tells a story about a name, and then does something the suttas almost never do: he recites, one after another, the names of a hundred and three people — awakened beings who lived, taught no one, and vanished so completely that only their names survived.

Read as doctrine, Isigilisutta teaches almost nothing new. Read as an act of memory, it is one of the most startling discourses in the canon: a glimpse of a Buddhist deep time stretching back through countless ages, populated by awakened beings who came and went with no Dhamma, no Saṅgha, and no one left to remember them — until, on this one afternoon, the Buddha remembers.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

On Isigili Mountain the Buddha tells the legend behind its name — “seer-swallower,” for the 500 Independent Buddhas who once entered it and were never seen again — and then recites the names of 103 of these forgotten awakened beings, an act of remembrance for those who attained without a teacher and left no teaching behind.

Five mountains, one that kept its name

The discourse opens with the Buddha pointing, one by one, at the hills that ring Rājagaha: Vebhāra, Paṇḍava, Vepulla, Vulture’s Peak. Each time he asks the mendicants, “Do you see that mountain?” — and each time, after they say yes, he adds the same quiet aside: it used to have a different label and description. Names change; even mountains are renamed as ages pass and the people who named them are forgotten.

Then he points to the mountain they are standing on. “Do you see Isigili?” This one, he says, has “exactly the same label and description” it always had. Four mountains lost their names to time. One did not — and the reason why is the story he tells next.

The mountain that swallows seers

Isigili, “seer-swallower.” Long ago, 500 Independent Buddhas dwelt on this mountain for a long time. People would see them walking in, but never saw them come out again. So they said to one another: “This mountain swallows these seers!” — and the name stuck: Isigili, “seer” (isi) + “swallows” (gilati).

It is a folk etymology and, the commentarial tradition suggests, a deliberate pun — the more mundane derivation is simply isi + giri, “seers’ mountain,” since Pali r regularly becomes l in the Magadhan dialect the Buddha spoke. But the legend the Buddha tells is the more vivid one: a mountain that seers walked into and never walked out of, because inside it, unrecorded, unnoticed, they attained final Nibbāna.

What is an Independent Buddha?

The Pali term is paccekabuddha, sometimes rendered “Solitary Buddha” or “Silent Buddha.” The concept fills a gap in Buddhist cosmology: the vast stretches of time when no sammāsambuddha — a fully Self-Awakened Buddha like Gotama, who discovers the path and teaches it to the world — is alive. In those ages, the tradition holds, certain beings can still wake up entirely on their own, unaided by any teacher. They are Buddhas in the sense that matters most — fully awakened, free of all suffering — but they differ from a sammāsambuddha in their relation to others.

“Independent” is the operative word, not “solitary” in the sense of living alone: this sutta has 500 of them dwelling on one mountain at the same time. What they don’t do is found a fourfold community of monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen bound by a shared teaching, the way Gotama did. They practice and awaken each on their own thread, independently of one another and of any Dhamma passed down. The tradition sometimes attributes verses to them — a number are preserved elsewhere in the canon — but no path, no Vinaya, no institution survives them. When they die, nothing is left to remember them by except, perhaps, a name.

A roll call of 103 names

Having told the story, the Buddha does the only thing that can still be done for beings like this: he says their names aloud. “I shall declare the names of the Independent Buddhas,” he tells the mendicants, “I shall extol the names… I shall teach the names… listen and apply your mind well.” Thirteen names follow immediately in prose — Ariṭṭha, Upariṭṭha, Tagarasikhī, and ten more. Then, switching to verse, he continues for some ninety further names, packed two, three, even four to a line, until the count (by the traditional reckoning) reaches 103.

Even ancient translators struggled with this list. As the commentary itself admits, it is often impossible to tell where a proper name ends and a descriptive epithet begins — is “Sudassana” a person, or just someone “good-looking”? Many names are common Indian words for virtues (Aparājita, “unconquered”; Anāsava, “undefiled”) or places (Gandhāra, Kosala, Sumbha) that could belong to any sage from that region or with that quality. The list is less a genealogy than an incantation — a naming so thorough it stands in for a biography no one preserved.

Echoes older than the Buddha

One detail makes the list more than a curiosity: as many as eight of these names match the names of Jain tīrthaṅkaras — the twenty-four great teacher-founders of Jainism across the ages — and several more resemble other revered Jain sages. Ariṭṭha and Nemi, side by side in the list, are elsewhere compounded into Ariṣṭanemi, the 22nd tīrthaṅkara’s name. Usabha (“bull”) matches the first tīrthaṅkara, Ṛṣabha. The sutta’s own title character — a sage named Isigiri — even turns up in a Jain scripture, the Isibhāsiyāiṁ.

Buddhism and Jainism arose in the same region of North India within a generation of each other, and both, it seems, drew on a shared, older memory of wandering ascetics and awakened sages — a common inheritance each tradition then folded into its own account of the deep past. Isigili is a rare place where that shared root shows through.

A chant of honor

The discourse closes with a verse of tribute — “honor these great seers who have slipped all chains, fully quenched, limitless” — and in Sri Lanka it is still recited today as a paritta, a protection chant. Its parallel in the Chinese Ekottarika Āgama preserves only the opening scene before diverging into an unrelated teaching, and shares just three names with this version (Ariṭṭha, Upariṭṭha, Sudassana) — a sign of how early, and how differently, this list of the forgotten was already being remembered across the Buddhist world.

Key terms

samaññā ca paññatti — “label and description”: what the Buddha says four of the five mountains have lost over time. Even geography is impermanent; only Isigili kept its name.
Isigili — “seer-swallower” — the legend: 500 Independent Buddhas entered this mountain and were never seen leaving. A folk etymology (isi + gilati) layered over the plainer “seers’ mountain” (isi + giri).
paccekabuddha — “Independent Buddha”: one who awakens fully, unaided by a teacher, in an age with no living sammāsambuddha — but does not found a fourfold community or leave a path for others to follow.
103 names — the traditional count of Independent Buddhas named here, 13 in prose and roughly 90 more in verse; ambiguous throughout between proper names and descriptive epithets.
tīrthaṅkara — the 24 great teacher-sages of Jainism. As many as eight names in this list overlap with tīrthaṅkaras, pointing to a shared well of ancient North Indian ascetic memory older than either tradition.
paritta — a protection chant. This sutta is still recited as one in Sri Lanka today, its closing verse a tribute to the “great seers… fully quenched, limitless.”

The text

The full discourse, given complete — opening frame, mountain legend, and the entire roll call of names in both prose and verse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Five mountains

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, on the Isigili Mountain. There the Buddha addressed the mendicants, “Mendicants!” “Venerable sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

§2“Mendicants, do you see that Mount Vebhāra?” “Yes, sir.” “It used to have a different label and description. Do you see that Mount Paṇḍava?” “Yes, sir.” “It too used to have a different label and description. Do you see that Mount Vepulla?” “Yes, sir.” “It too used to have a different label and description. Do you see that Mount Vulture’s Peak?” “Yes, sir.” “It too used to have a different label and description. Do you see that Mount Isigili?” “Yes, sir.”

The seer-swallower

§3“It used to have exactly the same label and description. Once upon a time, five hundred Independent Buddhas dwelt for a long time on this Isigili. They were seen entering the mountain, but after entering were seen no more. When people noticed this they said: ‘That mountain swallows these seers!’ That’s how it came to be known as Isigili, the ‘seer-swallower.’ I shall declare the names of the Independent Buddhas; I shall extol the names of the Independent Buddhas; I shall teach the names of the Independent Buddhas. Listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

The names, in prose

§4“The independent Buddhas who dwelt for a long time on this Isigili were named Ariṭṭha, Upariṭṭha, Tagarasikhī, Yasassin, Sudassana, Piyadassin, Gandhāra, Piṇḍola, Upāsabha, Nīta, Tatha, Sutavā, and Bhāvitatta.

The names, in verse

§5Those saintly beings, untroubled, with no need for hope, who each achieved awakening independently; hear me extol their names, the supreme men, free of thorns. Ariṭṭha, Upariṭṭha, Tagarasikhī, Yasassin, Sudassana, and Piyadassin the awakened; Gandhāra, Piṇḍola, and Upāsabha, Nīta, Tatha, Sutavā, and Bhāvitatta.

§6Sumbha, Subha, Methula, and Aṭṭhama, then Assumegha, Anīgha, and Sudāṭha, Independent Buddhas, enders of the leash to existence; and Hiṅgū and Hiṅga the mighty. Two sages named Jāli, and Aṭṭhaka; then the awakened one Kosala, then Subāhu; Upanemi, Nemi, and Santacitta, right and true, stainless and astute. Kāḷa and Upakāḷa, Vijita and Jita, Aṅga and Paṅga, and Guttijita too; Passin gave up attachment, suffering’s root, while Aparājita defeated Māra’s power. Satthar, Pavattar, Sarabhaṅga, Lomahaṁsa, Uccaṅgamāya, Asita, Anāsava, Manomaya, and Bandhumā the cutter of conceit, then Adhimutta, and Ketumā the immaculate. Ketumbharāga, Mātaṅga, and Ariya, then Accuta, Accutagāma, and Byāmaka, Sumaṅgala, Dabbila, Supatiṭṭhita, Asayha, Khemābhirata, and Sorata. Durannaya, Saṅgha, and also Ujjaya, another sage, Sayha of peerless effort. There are twelve Ānandas, Nandas, and Upanandas, and Bhāradvāja, bearing his final body. Bodhi, Mahānāma, and also Uttara, Kesī, Sikhī, Sundara, and Bhāradvāja, Tissa and Upatissa, who’ve both cut the bonds to rebirth, Upasīdarī and Sīdarī, who’ve both cut off craving. Maṅgala was awakened, free of greed, Usabha cut the net, the root of suffering, Upanīta who attained the state of peace, Uposatha, Sundara, and Saccanāma. Jeta, Jayanta, Paduma, and Uppala; Padumuttara, Rakkhita, and Pabbata, Mānatthaddha, beautiful and free of greed, and the Buddha Kaṇha, his mind well freed.

Closing tribute

§7These and other mighty ones awakened independently, enders of the leash to existence—honor these great seers who have slipped all chains, fully quenched, limitless.”

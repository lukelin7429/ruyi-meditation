Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 95

With Caṅkī

Caṅkīsutta

Setting
The brahmin village of Opāsāda in Kosala, in the Godswood of sal trees
Speakers
The Buddha and the young brahmin Kāpaṭika Bhāradvāja, framed by the brahmin Caṅkī
Form
A dialogue on truth — how to guard it honestly, awaken to it, and finally arrive at it.
Length
~18 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No full counterpart survives in the Chinese Āgamas; the “file of blind men” image is distinctive to the Pāli
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — the heart of the Buddhist attitude toward truth and dogma.

Why this discourse, ninety-fifth

A sixteen-year-old prodigy, sent forward by his elders, throws down the brahmins’ great claim: that the ancient hymns hold “the only truth, and anything else is futile.” The Buddha does not argue the content. He asks one quiet question — does anyone actually know it to be so? — and from the answer unfolds the most careful account in the canon of how an honest person should hold, test, and finally reach the truth.

The discourse opens with a long, almost comic prelude on status. The eminent brahmin Caṅkī decides to call on the Buddha; five hundred visiting brahmins protest that a man of his pedigree should not go to him — he should be visited. Caṅkī answers by reciting the Buddha’s qualities one by one until the protocol reverses itself: by their own measure of worth, it is fitting that he go. The whole frame is a study in how reputation and birth are weighed — and a preparation for the deeper question of how anything is known at all.

At its core the sutta is the Buddha’s answer to dogmatism, the twin of the famous Kālāma instruction. Confronted with an inherited certainty, he distinguishes three things that are usually run together: preserving the truth (saying honestly “this is my faith” without leaping to “this alone is true”), awakening to the truth (the long, testing path from scrutinising a teacher to seeing for oneself), and arriving at the truth (the repeated cultivation that completes it). It is a model of intellectual honesty that has lost none of its force.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

No brahmin, no teacher back seven generations, not even the ancient seers who composed the hymns, ever knew the inherited claim “this alone is true” to be so — they are a file of blind men, each holding the one in front — and so an honest person preserves the truth by saying only “such is my faith,” awakens to it by testing a teacher and seeing for oneself, and arrives at it by cultivating what one has seen.

The reversal of protocol

Half the discourse is taken up with whether the Buddha should visit Caṅkī or Caṅkī the Buddha — a question of honour that the ancient audience took seriously. The visiting brahmins list Caṅkī’s claims to precedence: pure birth back seven generations, wealth, mastery of the three Vedas, beauty, virtue, eloquence, royal patronage. Caṅkī answers point for point, but turns each toward the Buddha — who left greater wealth, went forth in the prime of youth though his parents wept, is beautiful, virtuous, eloquent, sought by people from distant lands, honoured by kings and by Caṅkī’s own teacher. The effect is to dismantle the metric of birth from inside: by the very standards the brahmins prize, the renunciant outranks them all. It is the same demolition of caste-pride that runs through MN 84 and MN 93, here turned into an etiquette lesson.

One question that empties the claim

The young Kāpaṭika states the brahmin creed in its strongest form: by lineage and canonical authority, “this is the only truth, anything else is futile.” The Buddha does not dispute the Vedas. He asks instead whether anyone — a living brahmin, a teacher, a teacher’s teacher back seven generations, or even the ancient seers Aṭṭhaka, Vāmaka, Vāmadeva and the rest who first sang the hymns — ever stood up and said, “I know this, I see this.” To each, Kāpaṭika must answer: no. The conclusion lands as an image that has outlived its setting:

The file of blind men. “Suppose there was a queue of blind men, each holding the one in front: the first does not see, the middle does not see, and the last does not see.” A claim passed hand to hand by people who never saw its truth is exactly this — a chain of confident transmission with no eyes anywhere along it.

The point is not that the hymns are false, but that belief in them rests on nothing seen. Faith handed down is not knowledge, however old or unbroken the line.

Five things that cut two ways

Kāpaṭika retreats from faith to “oral transmission” — but the Buddha names five grounds people lean on, and shows every one of them is unreliable as a stamp of truth:

  • Faith (saddhā), endorsement (ruci, personal approval), oral transmission (anussava), reasoned thought (ākāra-parivitakka), and acceptance of a view after deliberation (diṭṭhi-nijjhānakkhanti).

“Even something you have full faith in may be vacuous, hollow, and false; and something you have no faith in may be true and real.” The same holds for each of the five. None of them — not even careful reasoning or a well-pondered view — guarantees that what it endorses is true. So “a sensible person who is preserving truth” does not, on any of these grounds, jump to “this alone is true, all else is futile.” This is the Buddha’s standing warning against turning any second-hand ground — including reason itself — into a dogma.

Three stages: preserve, awaken, arrive

Out of this the Buddha builds a graded answer to Kāpaṭika’s real question — then how does one relate to truth at all?

StageWhat it means
Preserving truth
saccānurakkhanā		The honest holding of a conviction: “Such is my faith,” “such is the view I have accepted” — without escalating it to “this alone is true.” You guard the truth by not overclaiming.
Awakening to truth
saccānubodha		The active path to seeing: scrutinise a teacher for greed, hate, and delusion; finding none, place faith, approach, listen, remember, examine, strive — and “directly realise the ultimate truth, seeing it with penetrating wisdom.”
Arriving at truth
saccānuppatti		The completion: “by the cultivation, development, and making much of these very same things.” Awakening once is not enough; the truth is fully reached only by repeated practice.

The middle stage is the striking one. How does a seeker tell a true teacher from a false one, before they can judge the teaching for themselves? Not by credentials, but by character: a long, patient scrutiny of whether the teacher’s conduct shows any greed, hate, or delusion that might tempt them to claim what they do not know. Only when the teacher is found clean does faith arise — and even then it is a faith that goes on to test, listen, and finally see for itself. Faith here is not the end of inquiry but its responsible beginning.

The ladder of conditions

Finally the Buddha runs the whole sequence backwards as a chain of supports, each resting on the one before: arriving at truth depends on striving, which depends on weighing the teachings, on zeal, on enthusiasm, on accepting them after deliberation, on examining their meaning, on remembering them, on hearing them, on active listening, on paying homage, on approaching a teacher — and that, at the root, on faith. So faith returns, but transformed: not the inherited faith that says “this alone is true,” but the working faith that moves a person to go, to listen, and to begin the path that ends in seeing for oneself. Kāpaṭika, who began by defending a creed he had never tested, ends won over — confessing that he had once sneered at ascetics as “shavelings, black spawn,” and now takes refuge for life.

Key terms

“this is the only truth, anything else is futile” — idam-eva saccaṁ mogham-aññaṁ. The formula of dogmatic certainty the Buddha repeatedly refuses — not because the content is false, but because no one preserving truth can honestly assert it.
the file of blind men — a claim transmitted by people none of whom ever saw it to be true: the first, the middle, and the last all blind. Old and unbroken transmission is not knowledge.
saccānurakkhanā — the preservation of truth: holding a conviction honestly as “such is my faith,” without inflating it into a categorical claim on others.
saccānubodha — the awakening to truth: testing a teacher’s character for greed, hate, and delusion, then placing faith, listening, striving, and at last seeing the truth directly with wisdom.
saccānuppatti — the arrival at truth: the cultivation and repeated development of what one has awakened to. A single insight is not the destination.
the five grounds — faith, endorsement, oral transmission, reasoned thought, and a view accepted after deliberation: each can hold something false as easily as something true, so none can stamp a thing as “the only truth.”

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The long mutual praise of the protocol scene is condensed; the heart of the dialogue — the blind men, the five grounds, and the three stages of truth — is given closely. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Caṅkī decides to call on the Buddha

§1–9So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was wandering in Kosala with a large Saṅgha when he reached the brahmin village of Opāsāda, and stayed in the Godswood of sal trees to the north. The brahmin Caṅkī, hearing the Buddha’s good reputation, set out to see him. Five hundred visiting brahmins objected: “It is not fitting for you to go to the ascetic Gotama — you of pure descent back seven generations, rich, master of the three Vedas, beautiful, virtuous, eloquent, honoured by King Pasenadi. It is fitting that he come to you.” Caṅkī replied: “Listen, sirs, to why it is fitting for me to go to him. He too is of pure descent; he left abundant gold and went forth in the prime of youth though his parents wept; he is beautiful, virtuous, eloquent; people come from distant lands to question him; kings and the brahmin Pokkharasāti have gone to him for refuge. The possession of even one of these qualities makes it fitting for me to go to him, not he to me. Come, gentlemen — let us all go.”

The young brahmin’s challenge

§10–12Caṅkī went with a large group and sat to one side. In the assembly sat the student Kāpaṭika, sixteen years old, a master of the three Vedas. When the Buddha looked his way he asked: “Worthy Gotama, regarding the ancient hymnal of the brahmins, handed down by lineage and canonical authority, the brahmins reach the categorical conclusion: This is the only truth, anything else is futile. What do you say to that?”

The file of blind men

§13“Tell me, Bhāradvāja: is there a single brahmin who says, ‘I know this, I see this: this is the only truth’?” “No, worthy Gotama.” “A single teacher, or teacher’s teacher, back seven generations, who says it?” “No.” “And the ancient seers of the brahmins — Aṭṭhaka, Vāmaka, Vāmadeva, and the rest, who composed and sang the hymns — did even they say, ‘We know this, we see this’?” “No, worthy Gotama.” “So not one brahmin, nor teacher, nor ancient seer ever knew or saw it. Suppose there were a queue of blind men, each holding the one in front: the first does not see, the middle does not see, the last does not see. Just so, Bhāradvāja, the brahmins’ statement turns out to be a queue of blind men. Does not their faith then turn out to be baseless?”

Five grounds, two ways

§14“The brahmins honour this not by faith alone, but by oral transmission.” “First you fell back on faith, now on transmission. There are five things that can turn out in two ways: faith, endorsement, oral transmission, reasoned thought, and a view accepted after deliberation. Something you fully believe may be hollow and false; something you do not believe may be true and real. The same with all five. For a sensible person preserving truth, none of these is enough to conclude: this is the only truth, anything else is futile.”

Preserving the truth

§15“Then how, worthy Gotama, does one preserve truth?” “If a person has faith, they preserve truth by saying, ‘Such is my faith’ — without concluding, ‘this alone is true.’ The same if they have a belief, a transmission, a reasoned reflection, or a view accepted after contemplation: they say only, ‘such is the view I accept,’ and do not leap to the categorical claim. That is the preservation of truth — but it is not yet the awakening to truth.”

Awakening to the truth

§16–20“Then how is there awakening to truth?” “Take a mendicant living near a village. A householder scrutinises them for three things: qualities of greed, of hate, of delusion — any of which might lead them to claim they know what they do not, or to push others toward lasting harm. Finding none — finding instead conduct free of greed, free of hate, free of delusion, and a teaching ‘deep, peaceful, subtle, beyond mere logic, that no greedy or deluded person could teach’ — they place faith in that teacher. With faith they approach, pay homage, listen, hear and remember the teaching, examine its meaning, accept it, grow enthusiastic, apply zeal, weigh it up, and strive; and striving, they realise the ultimate truth and see it with penetrating wisdom. That is the awakening to truth — but not yet its final attainment.”

Arriving at the truth, and what supports it

§21–33“And the attainment of truth?” “By the cultivation, development, and making much of these very same things, there is the attainment of truth.” “And what is most helpful for it?” “Striving. Without striving you will not arrive.” And striving rests on weighing the teachings, which rests on zeal, on enthusiasm, on acceptance after deliberation, on examining their meaning, on remembering them, on hearing them, on active listening, on paying homage, on approaching a teacher — and that, in the end, on faith. “If faith does not arise, you will not approach; because you have faith, you approach. So faith is helpful for approaching a teacher.”

The student won over

§34–35“Whatever I asked the worthy Gotama, he has answered, and I accept it gladly. I used to think, ‘Who are these shavelings, fake ascetics, black spawn, beside those who understand the teaching?’ The Buddha has inspired in me fondness and respect for ascetics. Excellent, worthy Gotama! From this day forth, may he remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.”

Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 62

The Longer Advice to Rāhula

Mahārāhulovādasutta

Setting
Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery; the teaching begins on the road and concludes that evening
Speakers
The Buddha and his son Rāhula, now an older monk; with a prompt from Venerable Sāriputta
Form
A graduated meditation curriculum — from seeing all things as not-self, through the elements and the antidote meditations, to the full sixteen steps of mindful breathing.
Length
~14 minutes to read
Northern parallel
EĀ 17.1 (Ekottarika-āgama)
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — rich and comprehensive. Several complete meditation methods are packed into one discourse; the sixteen-step breath at the end repays slow reading.

Why this discourse, sixty-second

The longer of the Buddha’s teachings to his son is a whole meditation curriculum compressed into a single afternoon. It begins on the road into Sāvatthī, where the Buddha, walking for alms with Rāhula behind him, turns and delivers one of the most piercing instructions in the canon: see all form — and all feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness — as “not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.”

So struck is Rāhula that he abandons the alms-round and sits straight down under a tree to meditate. Venerable Sāriputta, passing by, urges him to develop mindfulness of breathing. But when Rāhula brings the question to the Buddha that evening, his father does not go straight to the breath. He builds toward it. First he resolves the body into the impersonal elements — earth, water, fire, air, space. Then he teaches the mind to become like those elements: like the earth, which is not disgusted when filth is thrown on it. Then come six antidote meditations, each dissolving a specific poison of the heart. Only then, at the summit, does he unfold the sixteen steps of mindful breathing.

The arc is deliberate. Insight into not-self opens the work; equanimity steadies it; the antidotes purify the specific defilements; and the breath gathers everything into one continuous practice that runs “even as the final breaths cease.” MN 62 is, in effect, the Buddha handing his son a map of the entire inner path — concrete enough to begin today, deep enough to carry to the last breath.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

See all form — indeed all five aggregates — as “not mine, not I, not my self”; resolve the body into the impersonal elements; make the mind like earth, water, fire, wind, and space, unmoved by what is thrown at it; cultivate love, compassion, rejoicing, equanimity, the perception of ugliness, and of impermanence, each against its defilement; and develop the sixteen steps of mindful breathing, so that one is mindful even at the last breath.

A word on the road

The discourse opens in motion. Walking into Sāvatthī for alms with Rāhula following behind, the Buddha looks back and says: “You should truly see any kind of form at all — past, future, or present; internal or external; gross or subtle; far or near — all form, with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’” When Rāhula asks, “Only form?” the Buddha extends it to all five aggregates: feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness too. This threefold formula — not mine, not I, not my self — is the heart of the teaching on not-self, dissolving the three ways the mind tries to claim experience: as a possession, as an identity, and as a hidden owner behind it.

Sāriputta’s prompt, the Buddha’s longer answer

Rāhula is so moved that he turns back from alms and sits to meditate. Venerable Sāriputta, seeing him, recommends mindfulness of breathing. But when Rāhula puts the question to his father that evening, the Buddha does not begin with the breath at all. The longer answer is the gift of MN 62: rather than handing Rāhula a single technique, he lays out the supporting practices that make breath-meditation deep — the elements, equanimity, and the antidotes — before arriving at the breath itself. It is a lesson in how a complete practice is built, not just performed.

The body as elements

First the Buddha resolves the body into its elements (dhātu). The earth element is everything hard and solid — “head hair, body hair, nails, teeth, skin, flesh, sinews, bones…” The water element is everything fluid — “bile, phlegm, pus, blood, sweat, tears…” The fire element is what warms, ages, and digests. The air element is the winds, including the in-and-out breaths. The space element is the body’s cavities and openings. For each, the key move: “the interior earth element and the exterior earth element are just the earth element” — there is no difference in kind between the hardness of my bones and the hardness of a stone. Seeing this, “you grow disillusioned with the element, detaching the mind from it.” The body, examined closely, is not a self but a temporary gathering of the same elements found everywhere in nature.

“Meditate like the earth”

Then a turn from analysis to attitude — and one of the most beautiful passages in the discourses. “Rāhula, meditate like the earth. For when you meditate like the earth, pleasant and unpleasant contacts will not occupy your mind. Suppose they were to toss clean and unclean things on the earth — feces, urine, spit, pus, and blood. The earth isn’t horrified, repelled, or disgusted because of this.” The same is said of water (which washes clean and filthy alike), fire (which burns both without recoiling), wind (which blows on both), and space (which “is not established anywhere”). The teaching is not passivity but imperturbability: a mind that, like the great elements, simply receives what comes — praise and insult, comfort and pain — without being thrown into reactivity. The elements do not take any of it personally; nor need the practiced mind.

The six antidote meditations

Next the Buddha names six meditations, each paired precisely with the defilement it dissolves:

Meditate on……and this is given up
Love (mettā)ill will
Compassion (karuṇā)cruelty
Rejoicing (muditā)discontent
Equanimity (upekkhā)aversion (resentment)
Ugliness (asubha)lust
Impermanence (anicca)the conceit ‘I am’

The first four are the “divine abidings”; the last two reach deeper — the perception of the body’s unattractiveness to undercut lust, and the perception of impermanence to dissolve the subtlest fetter of all, the conceit “I am” (asmi-māna). This is meditation as targeted medicine: for each specific poison of the heart, a specific cure.

Mindfulness of breathing: the sixteen steps

Only now does the Buddha reach the breath — and gives it in full, the complete sixteen-step practice arranged in four sets of four (the four “tetrads”):

  1. Body — knowing the breath long, knowing it short, experiencing the whole body, stilling the bodily process.
  2. Feeling — breathing in experiencing rapture, bliss, the mental process (feeling), and stilling it.
  3. Mind — experiencing the mind, gladdening it, immersing it in samādhi, and freeing it.
  4. Principles — observing impermanence, fading away, cessation, and letting go.

The sixteen move from the coarsest object to the subtlest — from the physical rhythm of breathing, through the feelings and states of mind it gives rise to, to the direct contemplation of impermanence and release. It is a single thread on which the whole path can be strung, the breath always present as the anchor.

Mindful to the last breath

The discourse ends with a quietly profound promise. When mindfulness of breathing is developed in this way, the Buddha says, it is “very fruitful” — and “even as the final breaths cease, they are known, not unknown.” The one who has truly cultivated the breath dies mindfully, aware of the very last out-breath as it goes. There is no more fitting gift for a father to give a son: a practice that begins with the next breath and holds, unbroken, all the way to the final one.

Key terms

“n’etaṃ mama, n’eso’ham asmi, na me so attā” — “this is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.” The threefold formula of not-self, applied to all five aggregates — dissolving experience as possession, identity, and owner.
dhātu — element. The body resolved into earth (solid), water (fluid), fire (heat), air (movement, the breath), and space (cavities) — each “just the element,” the same within and without, and not a self.
paṭhavī-samaṃ bhāvanaṃ — “meditation like the earth.” Cultivating a mind that, like earth, water, fire, wind, and space, receives pleasant and unpleasant alike without reactivity.
brahmavihāra — the divine abidings: love, compassion, rejoicing, equanimity — the first four of the six antidotes, removing ill will, cruelty, discontent, and aversion.
asmi-māna — the conceit ‘I am’: the subtlest sense of self. The perception of impermanence (anicca-saññā) is named as its specific antidote.
ānāpānasati — mindfulness of breathing, in sixteen steps across four tetrads (body, feeling, mind, principles). The culminating practice — “very fruitful,” carrying one mindfully to the final breath.

The text

The full discourse. The element analysis is given in full for earth; the others are condensed. The “meditate like the elements” section gives earth in full; the antidotes and the sixteen steps of breathing are given complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A word on the road

§1–3So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove. Then the Buddha robed up in the morning and entered Sāvatthī for alms, with Venerable Rāhula following behind. Then the Buddha looked back at Rāhula and said, “Rāhula, you should truly see any kind of form at all — past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near: all form — with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’” “Only form, Blessed One?” “Form, Rāhula, as well as feeling and perception and choices and consciousness.”

§4–7Then Rāhula thought, “Who would go to the village for alms today after being advised directly by the Buddha?” Turning back, he sat down cross-legged at the root of a tree. Venerable Sāriputta saw him and said, “Rāhula, develop mindfulness of breathing. When developed and cultivated it’s very fruitful and beneficial.” Then in the late afternoon Rāhula came out of retreat, went to the Buddha, and asked, “Sir, how is mindfulness of breathing developed and cultivated to be very fruitful and beneficial?”

The body as elements

§8“Rāhula, the interior earth element is anything internal, personal, that’s hard, solid, and appropriated: head hair, body hair, nails, teeth, skin, flesh, sinews, bones, bone marrow, kidneys, heart, liver, diaphragm, spleen, lungs, intestines, mesentery, undigested food, feces; or anything else internal that’s hard and solid. The interior earth element and the exterior earth element are just the earth element. This should be truly seen with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’ When you truly see with right understanding, you grow disillusioned with the earth element, detaching the mind from it.”

The same is said of the water, fire, air, and space elements. Water is the fluids — bile, phlegm, pus, blood, sweat, tears, saliva, urine. Fire is what warms, ages, and digests. Air is the winds — including the in-and-out breaths. Space is the body’s openings and cavities. In each case: “the interior and exterior element are just that element; see it as ‘not mine, not I, not my self’; seeing thus, grow disillusioned and detach the mind from it.”

Meditate like the elements

§13“Rāhula, meditate like the earth. For when you meditate like the earth, pleasant and unpleasant contacts will not occupy your mind. Suppose they were to toss both clean and unclean things on the earth — feces, urine, spit, pus, and blood. The earth isn’t horrified, repelled, or disgusted because of this. In the same way, meditate like the earth.”

The same is said of water, fire, wind, and space. Water washes both clean and filthy things without disgust; fire burns both; wind blows on both; and space “is not established anywhere.” “Meditate like water… like fire… like wind… like space — for then pleasant and unpleasant contacts will not occupy your mind.”

The six antidote meditations

§18–23“Meditate on love. For when you meditate on love, any ill will will be given up. Meditate on compassion. For when you meditate on compassion, any cruelty will be given up. Meditate on rejoicing. For when you meditate on rejoicing, any discontent will be given up. Meditate on equanimity. For when you meditate on equanimity, any aversion will be given up. Meditate on ugliness. For when you meditate on ugliness, any lust will be given up. Meditate on impermanence. For when you meditate on impermanence, any conceit ‘I am’ will be given up.”

Mindfulness of breathing: the sixteen steps

§24–25“Develop mindfulness of breathing. When developed and cultivated it’s very fruitful and beneficial. And how? It’s when a mendicant — gone to a wilderness, the root of a tree, or an empty hut — sits down cross-legged, sets the body straight, and brings mindfulness to the present. Just mindful, they breathe in; mindful, they breathe out.

§26(Body) Breathing in heavily they know, ‘I’m breathing in heavily’; breathing out heavily they know it. Breathing in lightly they know it; breathing out lightly they know it. They practice: ‘I’ll breathe in experiencing the whole body’… ‘I’ll breathe in stilling the physical process’ … and out.

§27(Feeling) They practice: ‘I’ll breathe in experiencing rapture’… ‘experiencing bliss’… ‘experiencing the mental process’… ‘stilling the mental process’ … and out.

§28(Mind) They practice: ‘I’ll breathe in experiencing the mind’… ‘gladdening the mind’… ‘immersing the mind in samādhi’… ‘freeing the mind’ … and out.

§29(Principles) They practice: ‘I’ll breathe in observing impermanence’… ‘observing fading away’… ‘observing cessation’… ‘observing letting go’ … and out.

Mindful to the last breath

§30“Mindfulness of breathing, when developed and cultivated in this way, is very fruitful and beneficial. When mindfulness of breathing is developed and cultivated in this way, even as the final breaths cease they are known, not unknown.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Venerable Rāhula approved what the Buddha said.

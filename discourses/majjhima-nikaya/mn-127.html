Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 127

With Anuruddha

Anuruddhasutta

Setting
The home of the chamberlain Pañcakaṅga, near Sāvatthī
Speakers
Venerable Anuruddha, the chamberlain Pañcakaṅga, and Venerable Sabhiya Kaccāna
Form
A doctrinal question opening into meditation theory, cosmology, and a candid personal revelation
Length
~12 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No specific Āgama match is noted in the available commentary
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — technical meditation distinctions and cosmology, carried by a genuinely surprising conversation

Why this discourse, one-hundred-and-twenty-seventh

Anuruddha was declared, in the tradition, foremost among the Buddha’s disciples in the “divine eye” — the clairvoyant knowledge of how beings pass on according to their deeds. This discourse is where that reputation becomes concrete. What begins as an ordinary lay questioner’s confusion over two similar-sounding meditation terms ends with Anuruddha describing, in specific and confident detail, the internal life of a class of luminous gods — and then, pressed by a sharp-eyed fellow monk, admitting flatly that he knows this because he has personally spent time in their company.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The “limitless release of the heart” (the four boundless meditations of love, compassion, joy, and equanimity) and the “expansive release of the heart” (a distinct practice of gradually widening one’s field of pervasion from a tree root to the whole known world) are genuinely different practices with different results, and the specific quality of a meditator’s absorption — its breadth and its purity — directly shapes the specific kind of luminous rebirth that follows.

A chamberlain’s question

Pañcakaṅga, a royal chamberlain with “many duties and much work to do for the king,” invites Anuruddha and the Saṅgha for an early meal. Afterward, he raises something that has been troubling him: various senior mendicants have told him to develop, in one case, “the limitless release of the heart,” and in another, “the expansive release of the heart.” Are these simply two names for the same thing, or genuinely different practices? Anuruddha, in good teacherly form, first asks Pañcakaṅga what he thinks. Pañcakaṅga guesses they’re the same, just phrased differently — and is told, gently but plainly, that he’s wrong.

Two releases of the heart

Limitless releaseExpansive release
What it isthe four brahmavihāras: spreading love, compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity in all directions, “abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will”a distinct meditation focused on pervading an ever-widening physical extent
The progressionall four directions, above, below, across — the whole world at oncea single tree root, then two or three, a village district, two or three districts, a kingdom, two or three kingdoms, “this land surrounded by ocean”

The two are related — the commentary notes that “expansive” (mahaggata) appears right alongside “limitless” in the standard description of the divine meditations, which likely prompted Pañcakaṅga’s confusion in the first place — but they are not the same technique. The expansive practice, gradually enlarging the field of attention until it reaches the edge of the known world, may itself be a path that eventually opens into the truly limitless, “like the great ocean.”

Four kinds of luminous rebirth

Anuruddha then connects meditation directly to cosmology. Someone who meditates on “limited radiance” is reborn among the gods of limited radiance; on “limitless radiance,” among the gods of limitless radiance; and — more surprisingly — on “corrupted radiance” or “pure radiance,” among the corresponding gods. The commentary notes this last pair cuts against the usual assumption that absorption is simply pure by definition; evidently, in this account, even a genuine attainment of radiant meditation can be more or less refined.

Lamps in a house

To explain how such different beings can belong to a single class of god, Anuruddha offers one of the most striking images anywhere in the Nikāyas:

Gathered and dispersed. When these deities gather together as one, a difference in their color is evident, but not in their radiance — “like when several oil lamps are brought into one house: you can detect a difference in their flames, but not in their radiance.” But when they go their separate ways, a difference in both color and radiance becomes evident — like taking those lamps back out of the house one by one.

Individual light sources blend into a single field of illumination when massed together, and only reveal their separate character once isolated — a genuinely careful piece of phenomenological observation, offered here as cosmology.

Clinging without knowing it

Anuruddha adds a quiet but important qualification: it isn’t that these radiant deities consciously believe “what we have is permanent, lasting, and eternal.” Rather, “wherever those deities cling, that’s where they take pleasure” — exactly, he says, like flies riding along on a carrying-pole, who don’t think their position is permanent, but simply enjoy wherever they happen to have landed. Craving for continued existence, the simile suggests, does not require a conscious belief in permanence to operate.

Sabhiya Kaccāna’s questions

A fellow mendicant, Sabhiya Kaccāna, has been listening, and presses further. Why do some gods within a single order have limited radiance while others have limitless radiance? Anuruddha answers with his own Socratic method, walking Kaccāna back through the expansive-release progression: is pervading two or three tree roots more expansive than pervading one? Two or three kingdoms more than one? By the time they reach “this land surrounded by ocean,” the answer is obvious — the breadth of the meditator’s cultivated pervasion determines the breadth of the resulting radiance.

And why do some have corrupted radiance while others have pure? Here Anuruddha reaches for a simile: an oil lamp burning with impure oil and an impure wick burns dimly; one burning with pure oil and a pure wick burns clean and bright. In the same way, a meditator whose physical discomfort, dullness, drowsiness, restlessness, and remorse are not fully settled “practices absorption dimly, as it were,” and is reborn among the corrupted-radiance gods; one whose mind is fully settled practices without dimming, and is reborn among the pure-radiance gods. The quality of one’s meditation, down to how thoroughly the remaining hindrances have actually cleared, shows up directly in the resulting rebirth.

“You must have lived among them”

Impressed, Kaccāna makes a pointed observation: Anuruddha never says “so I have heard” or “it ought to be like this” — the hedging language of secondhand report — but states plainly, “these deities are like this, those deities are like that.” “Clearly,” Kaccāna presses, “Venerable Anuruddha has previously lived together with those deities, conversed, and engaged in discussion.” Anuruddha’s reply is candid, though visibly reluctant: “Your words are clearly invasive and intrusive, Reverend Kaccāna. Nevertheless, I will answer you. For a long time I have previously lived together with those deities, conversed, and engaged in discussion.” The commentary notes a real awkwardness here: declaring one’s own meditative attainments to a layperson is a minor monastic offense, and Pañcakaṅga, a layman, is sitting right there listening — a situation the Vinaya doesn’t clearly address, since Anuruddha is technically answering a fellow monastic’s question. Kaccāna closes by turning to Pañcakaṅga directly: “You’re fortunate, householder, so very fortunate, to have given up your uncertainty and gotten the chance to listen to this exposition of the teaching.”

Key terms

appamāṇā cetovimutti — the “limitless release of the heart”: the four boundless meditations (brahmavihāra) of love, compassion, joy, and equanimity, spread in all directions without limit.
mahaggatā cetovimutti — the “expansive release of the heart,” literally “grown great”: a distinct practice of gradually widening one’s field of pervasion from a single tree root to the entire known world.
the four radiances — limited, limitless, corrupted, and pure radiance: meditation subjects whose breadth and purity determine rebirth among four corresponding classes of luminous gods.
the lamps in the house — gathered deities show a difference in color but not radiance, like lamps massed in one room; dispersed, both color and radiance become distinct, like the lamps taken back out one by one.
the flies on the carrying-pole — illustrating that clinging to a state of being does not require a conscious belief that it is permanent; beings simply take pleasure in wherever they have landed.
“so I have heard” vs. direct report — the standard hedge marking secondhand testimony, notably absent from Anuruddha’s speech here, which Kaccāna reads correctly as a sign of firsthand experience.

The text

The discourse in full: the meal invitation, Pañcakaṅga’s question and the two releases of the heart, the four kinds of radiant rebirth and their similes, and the exchange with Sabhiya Kaccāna. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A chamberlain’s question

§1–4So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove. The chamberlain Pañcakaṅga sent word inviting Venerable Anuruddha and the Saṅgha to a meal, asking him to come early on account of his many duties for the king. Anuruddha consented, and the next morning ate at Pañcakaṅga’s home. Afterward, Pañcakaṅga sat to one side and said: “Sir, some senior mendicants have said to me, ‘Householder, develop the limitless release of heart.’ Others have said, ‘Householder, develop the expansive release of heart.’ Now, do these things differ in both meaning and phrasing? Or do they mean the same thing, and differ only in the phrasing?”

“Well then, householder, let me know what you think about this. Afterwards, it’s a sure bet that you’ll get it.” “Sir, this is what I think. The limitless release of the heart and the expansive release of the heart mean the same thing, and differ only in the phrasing.” “The limitless release of the heart and the expansive release of the heart differ in both meaning and phrasing. This is a way to understand how these things differ.

Two releases of the heart

§7–8And what is the limitless release of the heart? It’s when a mendicant meditates spreading a heart full of love to one direction, and to the second, third, and fourth. In the same way above, below, across, everywhere, all around, they spread a heart full of love to the whole world — abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will. So too with a heart full of compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity. This is called the limitless release of the heart.

And what is the expansive release of the heart? It’s when a mendicant meditates focused on pervading the extent of a single tree root as expansive. Also, a mendicant meditates focused on pervading the extent of two or three tree roots… a single village district… two or three village districts… a single kingdom… two or three kingdoms… this land surrounded by ocean. This too is called the expansive release of the heart. This is a way to understand how these things differ in both meaning and phrasing.

Four kinds of luminous rebirth

§9–12Householder, there are these four kinds of rebirth in a future life. Take someone who meditates focused on pervading ‘limited radiance’: when their body breaks up, they’re reborn among the gods of limited radiance. Take someone who meditates on ‘limitless radiance’: they’re reborn among the gods of limitless radiance. Take someone who meditates on ‘corrupted radiance’: they’re reborn among the gods of corrupted radiance. Take someone who meditates on ‘pure radiance’: they’re reborn among the gods of pure radiance. These are the four kinds of rebirth in a future life.

There comes a time, householder, when these deities gather together as one. When they do, a difference in their color is evident, but not in their radiance — like when several oil lamps are brought into one house, you can detect a difference in their flames, but not in their radiance. There comes a time when those deities go their separate ways. When they do, a difference both in color and in radiance is evident — like when those lamps are taken out of the house one by one, you can detect a difference in both.

It’s not that those deities think, ‘What we have is permanent, lasting, and eternal.’ Rather, wherever those deities cling, that’s where they take pleasure — like flies being carried along on a carrying-pole or basket. It’s not that they think, ‘What we have is permanent’; rather, wherever those flies cling, that’s where they take pleasure.”

Sabhiya Kaccāna’s questions

§13–16Venerable Sabhiya Kaccāna, who had been listening, said: “Good, Honorable Anuruddha! I have a further question. Do all the radiant deities have limited radiance, or do some have limitless radiance?” “Some have limited, some have limitless.” “What is the reason why, reborn in a single order of gods, some have limited radiance while some have limitless?” “I’ll ask you in return: which is more expansive — pervading the extent of a single tree root, or two or three?” “Two or three.” And so on, up through village districts, kingdoms, and finally “this land surrounded by ocean” — each time the more expansive pervasion is the answer. “This is the reason why some deities have limited radiance, and some limitless.”

“Good, Honorable Anuruddha! Do all the radiant deities have corrupted radiance, or do some have pure radiance?” “Some have corrupted, some have pure.” “What is the reason?” “I shall give you a simile, for by means of a simile some sensible people understand the meaning of what is said. Suppose an oil lamp was burning with impure oil and an impure wick: because of the impurity it burns dimly. In the same way, a mendicant who meditates on ‘corrupted radiance’ has not completely settled their physical discomfort, not completely eradicated dullness and drowsiness, not completely eliminated restlessness and remorse — so they practice absorption dimly, as it were, and are reborn among the gods of corrupted radiance. But suppose an oil lamp was burning with pure oil and a pure wick: it doesn’t burn dimly. In the same way, a mendicant who has completely settled these things doesn’t practice absorption dimly, and is reborn among the gods of pure radiance. This is the reason.”

“You must have lived among them”

§17–18“Good, Honorable Anuruddha! You don’t say, ‘So I have heard’ or ‘It ought to be like this.’ Rather, you say: ‘These deities are like this, those deities are like that.’ Sir, it occurs to me: clearly, Venerable Anuruddha has previously lived together with those deities, conversed, and engaged in discussion.” “Your words are clearly invasive and intrusive, Reverend Kaccāna. Nevertheless, I will answer you. For a long time I have previously lived together with those deities, conversed, and engaged in discussion.”

Venerable Sabhiya Kaccāna said to Pañcakaṅga the chamberlain: “You’re fortunate, householder, so very fortunate, to have given up your state of uncertainty, and to have got the chance to listen to this exposition of the teaching.”

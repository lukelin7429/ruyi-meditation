Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 79

The Shorter Discourse with Sakuludāyī

Cūḷasakuludāyisutta

Setting
Near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove; the Buddha again visits the wanderers’ park at the peacocks’ feeding ground
Speakers
The Buddha and the wanderer Sakuludāyī (“Udāyī”)
Form
A dismantling and a redirection — a grand but empty doctrine is exposed, “perfect happiness” is relocated from mortification to meditation, and even that is shown not to be the real goal.
Length
~13 minutes to read
Northern parallel
MĀ 208 (Madhyama-āgama 208)
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — the similes are vivid, but the layered redefinition of “perfect happiness” needs care.

Why this discourse, seventy-ninth

What is the highest thing a spiritual path can offer? Sakuludāyī’s tradition has a grand-sounding answer — “the ultimate splendour” — that turns out, on a single question, to be a word with nothing behind it. From there the Buddha walks him through a careful relocation of the goal: away from empty superlatives, away from painful austerity, toward meditation — and then past even that, to the finer things for which the holy life is really lived.

The discourse opens where MN 77 left off: the Buddha visiting Sakuludāyī’s park. Sakuludāyī raises a complaint about a rival — the Jain Nātaputta, who claimed to be all-knowing and all-seeing “constantly, while walking, standing, sleeping, and waking,” yet when asked a hard question about the past, “dodged the issue… and displayed annoyance, hate, and bitterness.” The contrast with the Buddha’s composure is what draws Sakuludāyī to him. But when the Buddha offers to teach — setting aside the metaphysics of “the first beginning and the final end” in favour of dependent origination — Sakuludāyī cannot follow at all. So the Buddha meets him where he is, and examines his own tradition instead.

What follows is a small masterclass in cross-examination. The doctrine of “ultimate splendour” collapses under the simple demand to describe it; the promise of a “world of perfect happiness” reached by mortification collapses once one notices that mortification hurts. The Buddha then offers the real article — a genuine happiness reached through the absorptions — only to add that even this is not why his monks practise. There are finer things still. The teaching is generous and complete; its ending, however, is one of the quietly saddest in the canon.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Grand spiritual claims that cannot be described — an “ultimate splendour” dimmer than a firefly — have no substance; a “perfect happiness” cannot be reached by a practice of mixed pleasure and pain, but only through the absorptions, realized fully in the fourth; and yet even that is not the goal, for the finer things — the higher knowledges and the ending of the defilements — are what the holy life is truly for.

Setting aside the unanswerable

Sakuludāyī admires the Buddha because, unlike the Jain who lost his temper when cornered, the Buddha could in principle discuss the deepest questions — the “first beginning” (knowable by recollecting past lives) and the “final end” (knowable by the divine eye). But the Buddha’s response is telling: “Leave aside the first beginning and the final end. I shall teach you the Dhamma: when this exists, that comes to be; when this ceases, that ceases.” He redirects from cosmological speculation to dependent origination — the lawful arising and ceasing of experience, here and now. It is the same instinct as the parable of the poisoned arrow (MN 63): the urgent work is not to map the universe’s edges but to understand how suffering arises and stops. Sakuludāyī, however, is out of his depth — he can’t recall even this life’s details, and dependent origination is “even more unclear.” So the Buddha changes tack and turns to ground Sakuludāyī thinks he knows.

The “ultimate splendour” with nothing inside

Sakuludāyī’s tradition proclaims “the ultimate splendour” (paramavaṇṇa). Asked what it is, he can only circle: “the splendour compared to which no other is finer” — a definition that defines nothing. The Buddha’s simile is devastating in its ordinariness: it is like a man who declares, “I love the most beautiful woman in the land!” — but cannot say her name, her family, her height, her complexion, or where she lives, because he has never seen her. A passion for someone wholly unknown has “no demonstrable basis.” A doctrine that cannot describe what it exalts is in exactly that position: enthusiasm wrapped around a blank.

The ladder of splendours

Pressed, Sakuludāyī finally offers content: the ultimate splendour is like a flawless beryl gem glowing on a cream rug — “the splendour of the self that is free of disease after death.” So the Buddha sets the claimed “ultimate” against a rising ladder of brightnesses:

StepSplendour
the claima beryl gem on a cream rug
brighter than thata firefly in the dark
brighter stillan oil lamp → a great bonfire
brighter stillthe morning star → the full moon → the midday autumn sun
beyond all thesegods on whom sun and moon make no impression

The point lands hard: the very thing Sakuludāyī calls “the splendour than which none is finer” is, by his own admission, dimmer than a firefly — the faintest light on the ladder. “Yet of a splendour inferior to a firefly you say, ‘This is the ultimate’ — and you cannot even describe it.” Sakuludāyī concedes: “Pressed and grilled on our own tradition, we turned out to be vacuous, hollow, and mistaken.”

“Perfect happiness” cannot grow from pain

The Buddha next takes up a second claim: that there is a “world of perfect happiness” (ekantasukha, unalloyed bliss) and a practice to reach it. Sakuludāyī’s practice is keeping precepts — not killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying — topped with “some kind of mortification.” The Buddha probes gently: at the moment you keep a precept, or perform an austerity, is the self perfectly happy, or is it a mix of pleasure and pain? Mixed, Sakuludāyī admits each time. “And can a perfectly happy world be realized by a practice of mixed pleasure and pain?” It cannot. You do not arrive at unalloyed bliss along a road paved with discomfort. The means must share the nature of the end.

The real road: the absorptions

So the Buddha supplies the genuine article. The grounded practice for a world of perfect happiness is the jhānas: the first, second, and third absorptions are “the practice for realizing” it — and the fourth absorption, with its “giving up of pleasure and pain,” is where such a world is actually realized, for “there are deities reborn in a perfectly happy world” whose state the meditator now touches. Here is happiness that is itself peaceful, reached by a road that is itself peaceful — the opposite of mortification. When Sakuludāyī’s followers hear this, they cry out in dismay: “We’re lost, and so are our traditions! We know nothing higher than this!”

And yet — not even this is the goal

Then the discourse’s real pivot. “Surely,” says Sakuludāyī, “the monks practise under you for the sake of this perfectly happy world?” “No, Udāyī.” A blissful rebirth, even the highest, is still within the round; it is not freedom. “There are other things finer” — and the Buddha lays out the familiar arc: a Buddha arises, one abandons the five hindrances and enters the four absorptions, then turns the purified mind to the recollection of past lives, the divine eye, and the ending of the defilements, until “rebirth is ended.” These are the finer things. The whole conversation has been a ladder of its own: from an empty word, to a happiness that hurts to reach, to a real meditative bliss — and finally past all happiness-as-goal to liberation itself.

So near, and held back

Sakuludāyī is won over. “Excellent, sir!” he says, and asks to go for refuge and be ordained. But his own assembly stops him: “Don’t live as a pupil — you have been a tutor! You’ll end up like a water-jar turned into a mere ladle.” And so, the text flatly records, “his own assembly prevented him.” It is the third such ending in this run of discourses — after Sandaka, who stayed for his reputation, and so unlike Vacchagotta, Māgaṇḍiya, and Dīghanakha, who went forth and were freed. Here the obstacle is not even the seeker’s own attachment but his followers’: their investment in his status as their teacher keeps him from becoming a student of the truth. Sometimes what stands between a person and liberation is not their doubt but the crowd that needs them to stay where they are.

Key terms

pubbanta / aparanta — “the first beginning” and “the final end”: speculation about the cosmos’s origin and destiny, which the Buddha sets aside in favour of dependent origination.
idappaccayatā — “specific conditionality,” dependent origination: “when this exists, that comes to be; when this ceases, that ceases.” The lawful here-and-now that the Buddha teaches in place of metaphysics.
paramavaṇṇa — “ultimate splendour,” Sakuludāyī’s grand but undefined ideal — exposed by the unseen-lady simile and the ladder of brightnesses as dimmer than a firefly.
ekantasukha — “a world of perfect / unalloyed happiness.” Unreachable by a practice of mixed pleasure and pain (mortification); reached only through the absorptions.
the means must match the end — the principle behind the critique of mortification: you cannot arrive at unalloyed bliss along a road of discomfort. Peaceful happiness comes by a peaceful path.
paṇītatara — the “finer things”: not even a blissful rebirth is the goal, but the higher knowledges and the ending of the defilements — freedom, for which the holy life is truly lived.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The exposure of “ultimate splendour,” the relocation of perfect happiness, and the finer things are given in full; the ladder of splendours and the gradual training are condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Leave aside the beginning and the end

§1–7So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was near Rājagaha in the Bamboo Grove, and visited the wanderer Sakuludāyī. Sakuludāyī told how the Jain Nātaputta had claimed total, constant omniscience, yet dodged a question about the past with annoyance and bitterness — “which reminded me of you, sir.” The Buddha replied: “One who recollects past lives could discuss the first beginning; one with the divine eye, the final end. But leave aside the first beginning and the final end. I shall teach you the Dhamma: when this exists, that comes to be; when this ceases, that ceases.”

“The ultimate splendour”

§8–10“Sir, I cannot even recall this life’s details, let alone past lives; dependent origination is even more unclear to me. Perhaps I might do better on my own tradition.” “And what is your tradition?” “That ‘this is the ultimate splendour.’” “But what is that splendour?” “The splendour compared to which no other is finer.” “You could say that endlessly, yet never describe it. Suppose a man said, ‘I desire the finest lady in the land!’ — but could not say her name, clan, height, complexion, or village, because he had never seen her. Would his claim not turn out to have no basis?” “Clearly so, sir.” “Just so with your ‘ultimate splendour’ that you cannot describe.”

The ladder of brightnesses

§11–19“Sir, it is like a flawless beryl gem glowing on a cream rug — such is the splendour of the self that is free of disease after death.” The Buddha then leads him up a ladder: which is brighter — the gem, or a firefly? The firefly. The firefly, or an oil lamp? The lamp. The lamp, or a bonfire? The bonfire. The bonfire, or the morning star? The star. The star, or the full moon? The moon. The moon, or the midday autumn sun? The sun. “And beyond even these I know gods on whom sun and moon make no impression — yet I do not call any of these ‘the ultimate.’ But of a splendour dimmer than a firefly, you say, ‘This is the ultimate’ — and cannot describe it.” “The Blessed One has cut short the discussion! Pressed and grilled on our own tradition, we turned out vacuous, hollow, and mistaken.”

Happiness cannot grow from pain

§20–23“Is there a world of perfect happiness, Udāyī, and a practice to realize it?” “Our tradition says yes: giving up killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, and lying, and undertaking some mortification.” “When you keep such a precept, or perform an austerity — is the self perfectly happy then, or does it have both pleasure and pain?” “Both pleasure and pain.” “And can a perfectly happy world be realized by a practice of mixed pleasure and pain?” “The Blessed One has cut short the discussion! Again we turned out hollow. But sir — is there such a world, and a practice for it?”

The real practice, and beyond it

§24–28“There is, Udāyī. The first, second, and third absorptions are the practice for realizing a world of perfect happiness.” “But surely that is the realization?” “Not yet — those are the practice. It is in the fourth absorption, with the giving up of pleasure and pain, that such a world is realized; for there are deities reborn in a world of perfect happiness, and the meditator now touches their state.” “Then surely the monks practise under you for the sake of that?” “No, Udāyī. There are finer things.” And the Buddha described them: a Buddha arises; one abandons the five hindrances and enters the four absorptions; then, with a purified mind, recollects past lives, sees beings reborn by their deeds with the divine eye, and realizes the ending of the defilements — “rebirth is ended.” These are the finer things for which the monks lead the spiritual life.

So near, and held back

§45–46“Excellent, sir! I go for refuge to the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha. May I receive the going forth?” But Sakuludāyī’s assembly said to him, “Worthy Udāyī, don’t live as a pupil under the ascetic Gotama — you have been a tutor! You’ll end up like a water-jar turned into a mere ladle.” And so his own assembly prevented the wanderer Sakuludāyī from leading the spiritual life under the Buddha.

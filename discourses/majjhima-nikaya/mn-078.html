Majjhima Nikāya · Discourse 78

With Uggāhamāna Samaṇamuṇḍika

Samaṇamuṇḍikasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove; the chamberlain Pañcakaṅga visits the wanderer Uggāhamāna at Mallikā’s debating-hall park
Speakers
The Buddha, the chamberlain Pañcakaṅga, and (reported) the wanderer Uggāhamāna Samaṇamuṇḍika’s son
Form
A refutation and a replacement — the Buddha dismantles a too-easy definition of the “perfect ascetic” with a single image, then gives the real, tenfold measure.
Length
~10 minutes to read
Northern parallel
No counterpart survives in the Chinese Āgamas
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — accessible, with one subtle turn: how even skillful conduct is finally let go.

Why this discourse, seventy-eighth

What makes someone a perfect, “invincible” ascetic — one who has reached the very top of the spiritual life? A wanderer offers a tidy answer: do no evil in body, speech, thought, or livelihood. The Buddha demolishes it with a single, unanswerable image — then a helpless baby is your perfect ascetic — and replaces it with a far deeper measure that reaches all the way to liberation.

The chamberlain Pañcakaṅga, a lay follower of the Buddha, finds it too early to visit the Buddha or the senior monks, so he calls instead on the wanderer Uggāhamāna at Queen Mallikā’s park — a hall built for philosophical debate. Uggāhamāna makes his claim: a person of four qualities is “an invincible ascetic, accomplished in the skilful, attained to the highest attainment.” The four are purely negative — no bad deed, no bad word, no bad thought, no bad livelihood. Pañcakaṅga, to his credit, “neither approves nor rejects” it; he takes it straight to the Buddha to learn what it really means. His suspended judgement is itself a small lesson in how a thoughtful student handles a confident-sounding doctrine.

The Buddha’s reply turns on the difference between innocence and attainment. Mere not-doing of evil — especially the not-doing that comes from incapacity — is no achievement at all. A baby on its back does no evil deed, speaks no evil word, plots no evil scheme, runs no crooked trade. By Uggāhamāna’s definition, the baby has reached the summit. Clearly something is missing: the absence of wrong is only the floor of the path, not its peak. What the peak actually requires, the Buddha then lays out in full.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Merely doing no evil in deed, speech, thought, and livelihood does not make a perfect ascetic — that would crown a helpless baby; the real measure is a tenfold accomplishment that understands where unskilful and skilful conduct and thought arise and cease, and culminates in the adept’s right knowledge and right freedom — with even skilful conduct, in the end, transcended rather than clung to.

The baby that refutes the definition

The Buddha’s rebuttal is one of the most economical in the canon. Uggāhamāna’s four qualities are all negations — no bad deed, word, thought, livelihood. But a “little baby lying on its back” satisfies every one: it has no concept of “body,” so does no bad deed “aside from just wriggling”; no concept of “speech,” so speaks no bad word “aside from just crying”; no concept of “thought,” so thinks no bad thought “aside from just whimpering”; no concept of “livelihood,” so earns no wrong living “aside from its mother’s breast.” If Uggāhamāna is right, the baby is the highest of ascetics. The Buddha’s verdict is precise: such a person has not reached the summit but “the same level as a little baby.” Innocence by incapacity is not virtue; it is merely the absence of opportunity. Real attainment must be something positive and understood, not just a blank where wrongdoing might have been.

The ten things to be understood

So the Buddha raises the bar from four to ten — and the first move is to insist that a true ascetic must understand the workings of conduct and thought, not merely abstain. Four domains are each analysed in the same fourfold way — what it is, where it stems from, where it ceases, and the practice leading to its cessation:

DomainStems fromCeases in / as
Unskilful conduct (bad deeds, words, livelihood)a mind of greed, hate, delusiongiving up bad conduct, taking up good conduct & right livelihood
Skilful conduct (good deeds, words, pure livelihood)a mind free of greed, hate, delusionbeing ethical “but not determined by it” — the freedom where even good conduct ceases
Unskilful thought (sensuality, malice, cruelty)perception of sensuality, malice, crueltythe first absorption
Skilful thought (renunciation, goodwill, harmlessness)perception of renunciation, goodwill, harmlessnessthe second absorption

The practice that carries each to its cessation is, in every case, the four right efforts — rousing energy to prevent and abandon the unskilful and to arouse and mature the skilful. Notice the architecture: conduct (sīla) is traced to the mind; thought (saṅkappa) is traced to perception; and each is quieted at a definite stage of meditation. The ascetic is not a person who happens not to misbehave, but one who sees the whole machinery of skilful and unskilful states and knows how to still it.

The subtle turn: even goodness is let go

The discourse’s deepest point is easy to miss. When the Buddha asks where skilful conduct ceases, the answer is startling: in a mendicant who “behaves ethically, but is not determined by ethical behaviour” (na ca sīlamaya) — one who “truly understands the freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom where these skilful behaviours cease without remainder.” Virtue is indispensable, but it is a raft, not the far shore. The fully accomplished one is perfectly ethical and yet does not identify with his ethics, does not make “being good” into a self to be defended. Good conduct flows from a freed heart without being clung to as an achievement. This is the difference between the moralist, who is made of his morality, and the liberated person, whose goodness is effortless because there is no longer anyone inside insisting on it.

The ten qualities of the invincible ascetic

Finally the Buddha names the ten qualities that actually define the “invincible ascetic, attained to the highest attainment.” They are the factors of the adept (the arahant — one beyond training): the eight factors of the noble path — right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right immersion — plus two more: right knowledge and right freedom. The eightfold path is what the trainee walks; the tenfold path is what the arahant has become, the two final factors being the knowing and the liberation that crown the rest. Against Uggāhamāna’s four empty negations, this is a portrait full to the brim: not the absence of wrong, but the presence of the entire path, completed and fulfilled in freedom.

Key terms

samaṇo akuppo — the “invincible” or unshakeable ascetic, “attained to the highest attainment.” The title Uggāhamāna claims for a person of four negations, and the Buddha redefines as the tenfold adept.
innocence vs. attainment — the crux of the baby simile: not-doing evil by sheer incapacity is no achievement. The summit requires positive understanding and development, not merely a blank where wrongdoing might have been.
sīla / saṅkappa — conduct and thought. Conduct is traced to the mind (with or without greed, hate, delusion); thought to perception (of sensuality or of renunciation, and so on).
na ca sīlamaya — “not determined by ethical behaviour.” The accomplished one is perfectly ethical yet does not identify with his virtue. Even skilful conduct is transcended, not clung to.
the four right efforts — the practice that carries each domain to its cessation: rousing energy to prevent and abandon the unskilful, and to arouse and mature the skilful.
the tenfold path — the eight factors of the noble path plus right knowledge and right freedom: the factors of the adept (asekha, the arahant), the path not merely walked but fulfilled.

The text

The full discourse, abridged where the source repeats. The frame, the baby simile, and the ten qualities are given in full; the fourfold analysis of conduct and thought is given in its pattern and condensed. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Uggāhamāna’s claim

§1–6So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove. Finding it too early to see the Buddha or the senior monks, the chamberlain Pañcakaṅga visited the wanderer Uggāhamāna at Mallikā’s debating-hall park, who hushed his noisy assembly to receive him. Uggāhamāna said: “Householder, when a person has four qualities I describe them as an invincible ascetic — accomplished in the skilful, attained to the highest attainment: they do no bad deed with the body, speak no bad words, think no bad thoughts, and earn no living by bad livelihood.” Pañcakaṅga neither approved nor rejected this, but rose, thinking, “I will learn the meaning of this from the Buddha himself,” and went to him and reported it all.

Then a baby is your perfect ascetic

§8“Chamberlain, if what Uggāhamāna says is true, then a little baby lying on its back is an invincible ascetic, attained to the highest attainment! For a baby has no concept of ‘a body,’ so how could it do a bad deed, aside from just wriggling? It has no concept of ‘speech,’ so how could it speak badly, aside from just crying? No concept of ‘thought,’ so how could it think badly, aside from just whimpering? No concept of ‘livelihood,’ so how could it earn a wrong living, aside from its mother’s breast? When a person has these four qualities I describe them not as an invincible ascetic, but as having reached the same level as a little baby.”

Ten things to understand

§9–13“When a person has ten qualities, chamberlain, I describe them as an invincible ascetic. The following must be understood: ‘These are unskilful behaviours; they stem from this; here they cease; this is the practice for their cessation’ — and the same for skilful behaviours, for unskilful thoughts, and for skilful thoughts.” The Buddha then analyses each: unskilful conduct stems from a mind of greed, hate, and delusion, and ceases when one gives it up for good conduct and right livelihood; skilful conduct stems from a mind free of greed, hate, and delusion, and ceases in one who “behaves ethically but is not determined by ethics,” understanding the freedom where even good conduct ceases without remainder; unskilful thoughts (sensuality, malice, cruelty) stem from perception and cease in the first absorption; skilful thoughts (renunciation, goodwill, harmlessness) stem from perception and cease in the second absorption. In every case the practice is the four right efforts.

The ten qualities

§14“And with what ten qualities, chamberlain, do I describe a person as an invincible ascetic, attained to the highest attainment? It is when a mendicant has an adept’s right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right immersion, right knowledge, and right freedom. When a person possesses these ten qualities, I describe them as an invincible ascetic — accomplished in the skilful, excelling in the skilful, attained to the highest attainment.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Pañcakaṅga the chamberlain approved what the Buddha said.

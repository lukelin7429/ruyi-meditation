Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 35.28

Burning

Ādittasutta

Setting
Near Gayā, on Gayā Head, together with a thousand mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha alone, addressing the assembled mendicants
Form
A single sustained image — burning — applied systematically to all six senses, closing with a report of mass awakening
Length
~3 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Saṃyukta-āgama (T99), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a single formula repeated across the six senses, plain in structure though total in its claim

Why this discourse

Few images in the early texts have traveled further outside Buddhist circles than this one — popularly known simply as “The Fire Sermon,” borrowed as a title and closing image by T. S. Eliot in The Waste Land. Its actual content is a single, relentless claim: everything a person experiences through eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind is burning, right now, with the fires of greed, hatred, and delusion. Tradition holds this was delivered to an audience for whom fire was not an abstract metaphor but a religious practice — a large group of ascetics who had spent years tending sacred fires before encountering the Buddha. Whatever the audience, the sutta rewards attention for how completely it applies its claim: not one sense door is exempted, and not one kind of feeling — pleasant, painful, or neutral — escapes the diagnosis.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

“All is burning” — every sense faculty, every object of sense, every act of sense-consciousness, every contact, and every feeling that arises from that contact, all of it alight with the fires of greed, hatred, and delusion, and with the further burning of rebirth, old age, death, and every form of sorrow that follows from them.

A fitting audience for a fire sermon

The setting is brief but pointed: the Buddha is staying at Gayā Head together with a thousand mendicants. Tradition — recorded in the account of the Buddha’s early ministry — identifies this thousand as the recently converted followers of the three Kassapa brothers, ascetics who had spent years as fire-worshippers, tending the sacred flame at the center of their religious life before their conversion. Whether or not a reader holds that identification as certain, it is easy to see why the tradition finds it fitting: a teacher choosing to speak about universal burning to an audience of former fire-tenders is either a remarkable coincidence or a deliberate, resonant choice of image for precisely the people in front of him, turning a practice they knew intimately into a diagnosis of the mind itself.

Defining “the all”

The discourse opens with the broadest possible claim — “all is burning” — and then immediately narrows it to a precise, closed list. “The all” (sabba) is not a vague gesture at everything in the universe; it is a technical term, defined exactingly elsewhere in this same section of the canon (the Saḷāyatana Saṃyutta) as exactly the twelve sense bases: the six internal sense faculties — eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mind — and their six corresponding external objects — sights, sounds, smells, tastes, touches, and mental phenomena. Anything outside that scheme, the earlier definition insists, simply cannot be spoken of meaningfully. So when this discourse declares “all is burning,” it is making the most complete claim available within that scheme: there is no experience a person could have — through any sense door, of any object, resulting in any feeling — that falls outside what is now said to be alight.

The formula, six times over

For each sense door the text repeats an identical five-part structure: the sense faculty is burning, its object is burning, the consciousness that arises from the two meeting is burning, the contact between them is burning, and whatever feeling — pleasant, painful, or neutral — arises dependent on that contact is also burning. The pattern is given in full for the eye and again for the mind, with the four sense doors in between (ear, nose, tongue, body) compressed by the standard elision the early texts use for exactly this kind of mechanical repetition — a courtesy to the reciter, not a sign that anything is actually missing from the teaching.

The five-part burning, for any sense door. The faculty is burning. Its object is burning. The resulting consciousness is burning. The contact is burning. The feeling — pleasant, painful, or neutral — that arises dependent on that contact is burning too.

Burning with what, exactly

The sutta does not leave “burning” as an unexplained flourish; it names the fires directly, twice, once for the eye and once (identically) for the mind: “the fires of greed, hate, and delusion.” These three — rāga, dosa, moha — recur throughout the canon as the three root causes of unwholesome action, and this discourse is among the sources that cements them as a fixed set, later widely known simply as “the three fires” or “the three poisons.” But the text adds a second burning alongside the first: “burning with rebirth, old age, and death, with sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress.” The first burning names the fuel — the mental defilements that keep the fire lit; the second names the result — the entire shape of unsatisfactory existence that follows from feeding those fires moment by moment.

From burning to freedom

The turn in the discourse is compact but complete. “Seeing this” — seeing that every sense door, every object, and every resulting feeling is on fire — a learned noble disciple “grows disillusioned” with all of it, sense door by sense door. Disillusionment (nibbidā) is not despair or aversion in the ordinary sense; it is a clear-eyed loss of appetite for something once seen to be burning. From disillusionment, “desire fades away”; from the fading of desire, one is freed; and having been freed, one knows directly that one is freed. The discourse closes this chain with the standard formula for full liberation: “Rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey has been completed, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this place.” The same six-sense-door structure that diagnosed the problem in exhaustive detail now carries, with equal completeness, the account of its cure.

An extraordinary close

The final lines report something the canon rarely claims on this scale: “while this discourse was being spoken, the minds of the thousand mendicants were freed from defilements by not grasping.” If the traditional identification of the audience is accepted, this describes an entire congregation of newly ordained monks — formerly devoted fire-ascetics — becoming fully liberated arahants together, in the time it took to hear a single sermon centered on the very element, fire, that had organized their prior spiritual life. Whatever weight a reader gives to the traditional setting, the sutta itself is unambiguous about the scale of the result it reports: not one listener awakening, but a thousand, together, at once.

Key terms

āditta — “burning, on fire”: the discourse’s central and only image, applied without exception to every sense faculty, object, consciousness, contact, and feeling.
sabba — “the all”: a fixed technical term, defined exactingly elsewhere in the Saḷāyatana Saṃyutta as precisely the twelve sense bases — the six sense faculties and their six objects — and nothing beyond that scheme.
rāga, dosa, moha — greed, hatred, and delusion, “the three fires” this discourse names as what all six sense doors are burning with; later widely known as the three unwholesome roots or three poisons.
nibbindati — “grows disillusioned”: the clear-eyed, appetite-losing response of a learned noble disciple upon seeing that everything sensed is alight, the first link in the chain leading to freedom.
anupādāya āsavehi cittāni vimucciṃsu — “their minds were freed from defilements by not grasping”: the closing formula reporting that the entire audience of a thousand mendicants attained full liberation while the discourse was being spoken.

The text

The discourse in full, given complete: the declaration that all is burning, the five-part formula applied across the six sense doors, the turn toward disillusionment and freedom, and the closing report of mass awakening. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

All is burning

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Gayā on Gayā Head together with a thousand mendicants. There the Buddha addressed the mendicants: “Mendicants, all is burning. And what is the all that is burning?

§2The eye is burning. Sights are burning. Eye consciousness is burning. Eye contact is burning. The painful, pleasant, or neutral feeling that arises dependent on eye contact is also burning. Burning with what? Burning with the fires of greed, hate, and delusion. Burning with rebirth, old age, and death, with sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress.

§3The ear … nose … tongue … body … The mind is burning. Ideas are burning. Mind consciousness is burning. Mind contact is burning. The painful, pleasant, or neutral feeling that arises dependent on mind contact is also burning. Burning with what? Burning with the fires of greed, hate, and delusion. Burning with rebirth, old age, and death, with sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress, I say.

Disillusionment and freedom

§4Seeing this, a learned noble disciple grows disillusioned with the eye, sights, eye consciousness, and eye contact. And they grow disillusioned with the painful, pleasant, or neutral feeling that arises dependent on eye contact. They grow disillusioned with the ear … nose … tongue … body … mind … painful, pleasant, or neutral feeling that arises dependent on mind contact. Being disillusioned, desire fades away. When desire fades away they’re freed. When they’re freed, they know they’re freed.

§5They understand: ‘Rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey has been completed, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this place.’”

A mass awakening

§6That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said. And while this discourse was being spoken, the minds of the thousand mendicants were freed from defilements by not grasping.

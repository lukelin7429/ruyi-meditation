Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 1.1

Crossing the Flood

Oghataraṇasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, late at night
Speakers
The Buddha and an unnamed, radiant deity
Form
A brief riddle-like exchange in prose, closed with a short verse of praise from the deity
Length
~2 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Saṃyukta-āgama (T99), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — extremely short, but its compressed, paradox-like phrasing takes real unpacking

Why this discourse

This tiny exchange happens to open the entire Saṃyutta Nikāya, but it is included here for its content, not its position — a deity’s question about “crossing the flood” draws out one of the most compact teachings in the whole canon on the difference between passive stagnation and frantic effort. The Buddha’s answer is a genuine riddle at first hearing: he crossed “neither standing nor swimming.” Only once the two failure modes are named — sinking when still, and being swept away when struggling — does the shape of the teaching come into focus. It rewards close, patient reading far out of proportion to its length.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Asked how he crossed “the flood” — the canonical image for the pull of craving and repeated rebirth — the Buddha answers that he crossed it “neither standing nor swimming,” because standing still means sinking and struggling forward means being swept away, so the way across avoids both extremes at once.

A visitor in the night

The scene is set with the spare, formulaic economy typical of the Saṃyutta’s shortest suttas: the Buddha is staying at Jeta’s Grove near Sāvatthī, and late at night a deity — unnamed, described only as “glorious,” lighting up the entire grove with its own radiance — approaches, bows, and asks a single question: “Good fellow, how did you cross the flood?” The flood (ogha) is a standing metaphor throughout the early texts for the four floods of sensual desire, continued existence, wrong views, and ignorance — the currents that keep beings cycling through repeated birth and death. A deity opening with this question, rather than something more ordinary, signals immediately that this is not small talk; it is the question every serious practitioner is ultimately trying to answer for themselves.

An answer built to puzzle

The Buddha’s reply is deliberately compressed: “Neither standing nor swimming, respectable sir, I crossed the flood.” Read on its own, this sounds like nonsense — how does one cross water by neither standing nor swimming? The deity asks exactly the question a careful reader would ask, and the Buddha supplies the missing logic:

The two failures. “When I stood, I sank under. When I swam, I was swept away. That’s how I crossed the flood neither standing nor swimming.”

“Standing” here means coming to a complete halt — giving up all forward movement, settling into passivity, complacency, or simple inertia. In the flood of craving and becoming, standing still does not mean safety; it means sinking, because the current does not pause simply because effort has stopped. “Swimming,” by contrast, is thrashing, straining effort without proper grounding — the frantic over-exertion of someone who tries to force their way across by sheer struggle. That, too, fails, because ungrounded striving gets swept away by the very current it is fighting. The Buddha’s crossing avoids both: neither the stillness of giving up nor the agitation of forcing, but a way of proceeding that is grounded without being static, and moving without being frantic.

Why the paradox matters beyond wordplay

It would be easy to file this away as a clever verbal trick, but the two failure modes it names map onto two of the most common ways spiritual effort actually goes wrong. “Standing” describes the practitioner who mistakes calm or equanimity for the goal itself and stops applying any energy at all, drifting into passivity that masquerades as peace. “Swimming” describes the opposite error: treating awakening as something to be seized by sheer willpower, over-efforting until the mind becomes agitated and destabilized rather than settled. The Buddha’s image suggests that liberating effort is neither of these — it is engaged without being frantic, and settled without being inert, a balance the tradition elsewhere describes through the image of tuning a lute string neither too tight nor too loose.

The deity’s verse, and a further question

Delighted, the deity responds not in prose but in verse, the register the early texts consistently reserve for a listener’s spontaneous, heightened appreciation: “After a long time I see a brahmin fully quenched. Neither standing nor swimming, he’s crossed over clinging to the world.” Calling the Buddha a “brahmin” here is honorific rather than a caste claim — a title of highest respect the early texts frequently redirect toward anyone who has genuinely reached the goal, regardless of birth. The text notes simply and pointedly that “the teacher approved” of what the deity said — a brief but significant confirmation that the deity has understood correctly. Having received this silent nod of approval, the deity bows, circles the Buddha respectfully keeping him on the right (the standard gesture of reverence and farewell throughout the canon), and vanishes.

An opening note for an enormous collection

As the very first sutta of the Saṃyutta Nikāya, this discourse sets a tone for what follows: brief, often riddling exchanges between the Buddha and various interlocutors — deities, brahmins, wanderers, kings — each turning on a single sharp point rather than an extended argument. Nothing here depends on that placement, but it is worth noticing: a collection of many thousands of suttas across fifty-six thematic groupings opens not with an elaborate discourse but with the shortest possible statement of a teaching about avoiding two opposite errors — a fitting doorway into a book whose organizing habit, throughout, is compression rather than expansion.

Key terms

ogha — “flood”: a standard metaphor in the early texts for the four floods (sensual desire, continued existence, views, and ignorance) that sweep beings through repeated rebirth; “crossing” the flood means reaching liberation.
appatiṭṭhaṁ, anāyūhaṁ — “without standing still, without struggling”: the Buddha’s summary phrase for the two extremes his crossing avoided, rendered here as “neither standing nor swimming.”
devatā — “deity”: one of the countless minor divine beings of Buddhist cosmology who appear throughout the Saṃyutta’s early chapters, often posing sharp questions or offering verses of praise after an exchange with the Buddha.
“the teacher approved” — a brief formulaic confirmation, used here and elsewhere in the canon, that a listener’s verse or statement of understanding has been correctly grasped.

The text

The discourse in full: a very short sutta, given complete. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A deity’s question

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery. Then, late at night, a glorious deity, lighting up the entire Jeta’s Grove, went up to the Buddha, bowed, stood to one side, and said to him, “Good fellow, how did you cross the flood?”

“Neither standing nor swimming, respectable sir, I crossed the flood.”

“But in what way did you cross the flood neither standing nor swimming?”

“When I stood, I sank under. When I swam, I was swept away. That’s how I crossed the flood neither standing nor swimming.”

The deity’s verse

§2“After a long time I see
a brahmin fully quenched.
Neither standing nor swimming,
he’s crossed over clinging to the world.”

§3This is what that deity said, and the teacher approved. Then that deity, knowing that the teacher approved, bowed and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on his right, before vanishing right there.

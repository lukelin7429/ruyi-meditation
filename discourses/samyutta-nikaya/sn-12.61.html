Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 12.61

Unlearned

Assutavāsutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, at Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery, addressed to the assembled mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha alone
Form
A comparative observation about body and mind, illustrated with a simile, then connected directly to dependent origination
Length
~5 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Saṃyukta-āgama (T99), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the argument and simile are vivid and accessible, though the closing application to dependent origination assumes some prior familiarity with the chain

Why this discourse

Most teaching on not-self starts with the body, since a body that visibly ages, sickens, and dies is the easiest thing to let go of. This discourse makes a sharper, more uncomfortable claim: it is actually the mind — the part of us most people are least willing to give up, the seat of “me” — that changes far more constantly and violently than the body ever does, and precisely because of that constant change, it is far harder to see through. The body’s slow decline is visible to anyone; the mind’s moment-to-moment churn is so continuous it becomes invisible, mistaken instead for a single, stable thing worth calling “myself.” The image the Buddha uses to make this vivid — a monkey swinging branch to branch through a forest — is one of the most memorable in the entire canon.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

An ordinary, unlearned person can reasonably grow disenchanted with the body, since its slow rise and decline over years is plain to see, but that same person struggles to grow disenchanted with what is called “mind,” “sentience,” or “consciousness,” precisely because it changes so continuously, moment to moment, that its instability is harder to notice than the body’s — and it is exactly this continuous change, seen clearly through dependent origination, that a well-trained disciple learns to recognize instead of mistaking it for a stable self.

An easier target: the body

The Buddha opens with something close to a concession: an unlearned ordinary person — someone without training in the Dhamma — “might become disillusioned, dispassionate, and freed from this body made up of the four principal states.” The reason given is simple and observational: the body “is seen to accumulate and disperse, to be taken up and laid to rest.” Its changes are the kind anyone can track — growth in childhood, illness, aging, eventual death — and so even without any particular training, disillusionment with the body is at least within reach.

A harder target: “mind”

The same unlearned person, however, is described as “unable to become disillusioned, dispassionate, or freed” from “that which is called ‘mind’ and also ‘sentience’ and also ‘consciousness’.” The reason is not that the mind is somehow more stable or trustworthy than the body — quite the opposite — but that “for a long time they’ve been attached to it, thought of it as their own, and mistaken it: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self.’” The very familiarity and intimacy of the mind, the sense that it is the one thing that has been “me” all along, is exactly what makes it so hard to see through. What feels most inward and most constant is, on this account, actually the least stable of the two.

A deliberately provocative reversal

The Buddha then makes the comparison explicit, and states it in a way clearly designed to unsettle: an unlearned person “would be better off taking this body… to be their self, rather than the mind.” His reasoning is direct — the body can last a year, two years, on up to a hundred years or more, a duration long enough that identifying with it, however mistaken, at least tracks something that holds roughly steady across a human lifetime. “Mind,” “sentience,” and “consciousness,” by contrast, “arises as one thing and ceases as another all day and all night” — a claim about a rate of change far faster than anything the body undergoes, happening continuously and imperceptibly rather than over years or decades.

The monkey in the forest

This claim about the mind’s instability is then made vivid with one of the most memorable similes in the canon: “It’s like a monkey moving through the forest. It grabs hold of one branch, lets it go, and grabs another; then it lets that go and grabs yet another.” The mind, moment to moment, is described as doing exactly this — taking hold of one thought, feeling, or object of attention, releasing it, and immediately grasping the next, in an unbroken sequence that never actually rests on any single branch for long. What feels, from the inside, like one continuous stream called “my mind” turns out, on this account, to be closer to an unbroken series of momentary graspings, each different from the last.

Turning the observation into practice

Having established that the mind is less stable than the body, not more, the Buddha describes what a trained disciple does with that fact: “a learned noble disciple carefully and rationally applies the mind to dependent origination itself,” using the general principle “when this exists, this comes to be… when this doesn’t exist, this doesn’t come to be,” and then the full twelve-fold chain, forward and in reverse. Rather than trying to locate a stable self somewhere inside the mind’s rapid changes, the disciple investigates the actual causal process producing those changes — consciousness and feeling conditioned by contact, and so on through the chain. Seeing this clearly is what produces genuine disillusionment: not with the body alone, but with “form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness” together, leading to the fading of desire, freedom, and the closing declaration familiar from other awakening accounts: “Rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey has been completed, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this place.”

Why this complements body-focused teachings

A great deal of not-self teaching in the canon works through the body, since its impermanence is the easiest kind to observe directly. This discourse deliberately targets the harder, more subtle case: the sense of a continuous inner witness or experiencer, the thing most people would point to first if asked what they “really” are. By showing that this inner sense is, if anything, less stable than the body it is usually contrasted with, the discourse closes off a common retreat — the idea that even if the body isn’t a fixed self, surely the mind that observes the body must be.

Key terms

assutavā puthujjana — the “unlearned ordinary person,” the discourse’s subject: someone without training in the Dhamma, capable of some disenchantment with the body but not with the mind.
citta, mano, viññāṇa — “mind,” “sentience,” and “consciousness”: three overlapping terms the discourse treats as naming the same changeable phenomenon under different labels.
the monkey simile — a mind grabbing one branch (one momentary object), releasing it, and grasping another, without pause, illustrating the mind’s constant arising and ceasing.
idappaccayatā — “specific conditionality,” the general principle stated just before the twelve-fold chain here: “when this exists, this comes to be… when this doesn’t exist, this doesn’t come to be.”
the five aggregates — form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness; the disciple in this discourse grows disenchanted with all five together, not with the mind in isolation.

The text

The discourse in full: the comparison between body and mind, the monkey simile, and the trained disciple’s investigation of dependent origination with respect to the mind. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

An easier target: the body

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. “Mendicants, an unlearned ordinary person might become disillusioned, dispassionate, and freed from this body made up of the four principal states. Why is that? This body made up of the four principal states is seen to accumulate and disperse, to be taken up and laid to rest. That’s why an unlearned ordinary person might become disillusioned, dispassionate, and freed from it.

A harder target: “mind”

§2But an unlearned ordinary person is unable to become disillusioned, dispassionate, or freed from that which is called ‘mind’ and also ‘sentience’ and also ‘consciousness’. Why is that? Because for a long time they’ve been attached to it, thought of it as their own, and mistaken it: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self.’ That’s why an unlearned ordinary person is unable to become disillusioned, dispassionate, or freed from it.

A deliberately provocative reversal

§3But an unlearned ordinary person would be better off taking this body made up of the four principal states to be their self, rather than the mind. Why is that? This body made up of the four principal states is seen to last for a year, or for two, three, four, five, ten, twenty, thirty, forty, fifty, or a hundred years, or even longer.

The monkey in the forest

§4But that which is called ‘mind’ and also ‘sentience’ and also ‘consciousness’ arises as one thing and ceases as another all day and all night. It’s like a monkey moving through the forest. It grabs hold of one branch, lets it go, and grabs another; then it lets that go and grabs yet another. In the same way, that which is called ‘mind’ and also ‘sentience’ and also ‘consciousness’ arises as one thing and ceases as another all day and all night.

The disciple’s investigation

§5In this case, a learned noble disciple carefully and rationally applies the mind to dependent origination itself: ‘When this exists, this comes to be; due to the arising of this, this arises. When this doesn’t exist, this doesn’t come to be; due to the cessation of this, this ceases. That is: ignorance is a requirement for choices. Choices are a requirement for consciousness. … That is how this entire mass of suffering originates. When ignorance fades away and ceases with no residue left behind, choices cease. When choices cease, consciousness ceases. … That is how this entire mass of suffering ceases.’

§6Seeing this, a learned noble disciple grows disillusioned with form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness. Being disillusioned, desire fades away. When desire fades away they’re freed. When they’re freed, they know they’re freed. They understand: ‘Rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey has been completed, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this place.’”

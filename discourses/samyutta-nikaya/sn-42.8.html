Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 42.8

A Horn Blower

Saṅkhadhamasutta

Setting
Near Nāḷandā, in Pāvārika’s mango grove
Speakers
The Buddha and Asibandhaka’s son, a village headman and disciple of the Jains
Form
A logical refutation of a rival teacher’s doctrine, followed by a contrast between despair and reform, closed with a simile of a horn blower illustrating four boundless meditations
Length
~7 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Saṃyukta-āgama (T99), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the frequency-based refutation takes careful tracking, and it opens onto the wider practice of the four immeasurables

Why this discourse

This discourse belongs to the Gāmaṇi-saṃyutta, a short section of the collection addressed to village headmen — local chiefs, several with striking former occupations, who bring the Buddha questions shaped by their prior training. Here the headman is a follower of the Jains, reporting a doctrine of his own teacher that sounds severe and airtight: certain acts always lead to hell, full stop, because a person is “led on by what they frequently live by.” The Buddha does not simply reject this; he shows, step by careful step, that the claim contradicts itself once frequency is actually measured. From there the discourse opens into something larger and more constructive — what actually happens to a person who has done wrong, depending on whether they are handed a verdict of doom or the tools to reform, closing with an image, the powerful horn blower heard in every direction, that gives the sutta its name.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A doctrine claiming that anyone who has ever killed, stolen, misbehaved sexually, or lied is bound for hell contradicts itself once actual frequency is measured — and the real difference between a person who is crushed by an unworkable verdict and one who reforms lies not in what they once did, but in whether they are left believing the deed cannot be gotten past.

A headman's report

Asibandhaka’s son, a village chief and a disciple of the Jains, approaches the Buddha at Nāḷandā, and the Buddha opens by asking him directly how his own teacher instructs his followers. The chief reports it plainly: “Everyone who kills a living creature, steals, commits sexual misconduct, or lies goes to a place of loss, to hell. You’re led on by what you frequently live by.” It is a doctrine that sounds strict and complete — certain deeds simply settle a person’s fate, without qualification. The Buddha’s response is not to dismiss this outright but to test it on its own stated terms, using the very principle of “being led by what you frequently live by” that the teaching itself proposes.

An internal contradiction, exposed by counting

The Buddha’s method here is exact and almost mathematical. Take a person who kills living creatures: comparing the periods of a full day and night, are the occasions of actually killing more frequent, or the occasions of not killing? The chief, tracking the case honestly, answers that the occasions of killing are the rarer of the two — far more of any person’s time, even a killer’s, is not spent killing. But if the doctrine is right that a person is “led on by what they frequently live by,” and non-killing is what this person does more frequently, then by the teaching’s own logic they ought to be led toward a good rebirth, not hell. The same test, repeated for stealing and sexual misconduct and then given in full for lying, produces the identical result each time: whatever the specific unwholesome deed, a person’s ordinary conduct across any stretch of time is overwhelmingly not that deed. The doctrine, applied consistently, would mean “no-one would go to a place of loss, to hell” — which directly contradicts what the doctrine itself asserts. It is refuted not by appeal to a different authority, but by simply being followed through to where it actually leads.

The refutation, stated plainly. “‘You’re led on by what you usually live by’: if this were true, then, according to what the Jain ascetic of the Ñātika clan says, no-one would go to a place of loss, to hell.”

Two disciples, two very different outcomes

Having dismantled the doctrine’s logic, the Buddha turns to something more consequential than winning an argument: what this kind of teaching actually does to a person who has, in fact, done something wrong. A disciple devoted to a teacher who holds the absolutist view — that everyone who has ever killed, stolen, misbehaved, or lied is bound for hell — and who has, in fact, done any of these things, is left with only one conclusion available: “I too am going to a place of loss, to hell.” Unless that disciple abandons the view itself, the text says starkly, “they will be placed in hell as if delivered there” — the doctrine becomes self-fulfilling not because the universe enforces it, but because the person, believing there is no way back, stops trying to change course at all.

The Buddha then describes a disciple of a very different kind of teacher — one, described through the standard formula of praise for a fully awakened Buddha, who criticizes and denounces these same unwholesome deeds directly: “Stop killing living creatures!… Stop lying!” A disciple of this teacher who has, in fact, killed “to a certain extent” reflects rather differently: “That’s not right, it’s not good, and I feel remorseful because of it. But I can’t undo what I have done.” This reflection — honest, remorseful, and clear-eyed about what cannot be changed — is exactly what allows the person to give up the deed going forward: “That’s how to give up this bad deed and get past it.” The same pattern repeats for stealing, sexual misconduct, and lying, and extends further to backbiting, harsh speech, nonsense talk, covetousness, ill will, and wrong view — ten courses of unwholesome conduct given up one by one, replaced with their wholesome opposites.

The horn blower

The discourse’s closing image gives it its title. Once that noble disciple is “rid of desire, rid of ill will, unconfused, aware, and mindful,” they meditate spreading a heart full of love in every direction — “abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will” — and then compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity in the same boundless way. The simile arrives identically each time: “Suppose there was a powerful horn blower. They’d easily make themselves heard in the four quarters.” In the same way, once this boundless heart has been developed and cultivated, “any limited deeds they’ve done don’t remain or persist there.” A horn sounded with real force carries in every direction without being confined to one; a heart genuinely opened without limit, the simile suggests, is no longer the kind of container in which a single finite past deed can occupy the whole space and determine everything that follows. This is not a claim that past unwholesome action simply vanishes without residue, but that it is put in proportion — small and limited, set against something now made vast.

Which “horn blower” sutta this actually is

It is worth being direct about what this discourse is not. Readers who know the Gāmaṇi-saṃyutta by reputation may expect a headman with a military background asking whether soldiers who die in battle are reborn in a heavenly realm — that is a real and memorable discourse in this same section of the collection, but it is a different sutta. Here, the headman is a disciple of the Jains rather than a former warrior, and the question at stake is the logical soundness of an absolutist doctrine of karmic doom, not the fate of the battlefield dead. The title “A Horn Blower” refers specifically to the simile of the four boundless meditations that closes this discourse, not to any military signaling.

A convert's grief made explicit

The chief’s response, though brief, closes the discourse on a note of genuine relief rather than mere formal agreement: “Excellent, sir! Excellent!… From this day forth, may the Buddha remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.” Having spent time as a disciple of a teaching that offered him, on its own logic, no way back from any past wrongdoing, he is offered instead an account in which honest remorse, a clear decision to stop, and the cultivation of a genuinely boundless heart are enough to move forward — a considerably more workable proposition than the one he had been living under.

Key terms

gāmaṇi — “headman” or “chief”: the local, often influential figures addressed throughout this section of the collection, several of whom bring questions shaped by earlier occupations or the doctrines of teachers they had followed.
Nigaṇṭha Nāṭaputta — the historical Jain teacher (called here “the Jain ascetic of the Ñātika clan”) whose doctrine of absolute, habit-determined rebirth the headman reports and the Buddha refutes.
“led on by what you usually live by” — the specific principle under scrutiny; the Buddha shows that, measured honestly against actual frequency of conduct, this principle contradicts the very doctrine it was meant to support.
vippaṭisāra — remorse: the honest, non-self-punishing recognition “that’s not right, it’s not good” that, in this discourse, is precisely what allows a disciple to give up a bad deed and move forward, rather than being trapped by it.
appamāṇa cetovimutti — the “heart’s release” made boundless (appamāṇa), through love, compassion, rejoicing, and equanimity spread without limit; the simile of the horn blower illustrates how this boundlessness places a limited past deed in proportion rather than erasing it.

The text

The discourse in full: the headman's report of his teacher's doctrine, the Buddha's frequency-based refutation, the contrast between a disciple trapped by despair and one who reforms through remorse, the ten courses of unwholesome conduct given up, and the closing simile of the horn blower across all four boundless meditations. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A headman's report

§1At one time the Buddha was staying near Nāḷandā in Pāvārika’s mango grove. Then Asibandhaka’s son the chief, who was a disciple of the Jains, went up to the Buddha, and sat down to one side. The Buddha said to him, “Chief, how does the Jain ascetic of the Ñātika clan teach his disciples?” “Sir, this is how the Jain Ñātika teaches his disciples: ‘Everyone who kills a living creature, steals, commits sexual misconduct, or lies goes to a place of loss, to hell. You’re led on by what you frequently live by.’ This is how the Jain Ñātika teaches his disciples.” “‘You’re led on by what you usually live by’: if this were true, then, according to what the Jain Ñātika says, no-one would go to a place of loss, to hell.

The frequency test

§2What do you think, chief? Take an individual who kills living creatures. If we compare periods of time during the day and night, which is more frequent: the occasions when they’re killing or when they’re not killing?” “The occasions when they’re killing are less frequent, while the occasions when they’re not killing are more frequent.” “‘You’re led on by what you usually live by’: if this were true, then, according to what the Jain ascetic of the Ñātika clan says, no-one would go to a place of loss, to hell.

§3What do you think, chief? Take an individual who steals … Take an individual who commits sexual misconduct … Take an individual who lies. If we compare periods of time during the day and night, which is more frequent: the occasions when they’re lying or when they’re not lying?” “The occasions when they’re lying are less frequent, while the occasions when they’re not lying are more frequent.” “‘You’re led on by what you usually live by’: if this were true, then, according to what the Jain Ñātika says, no-one would go to a place of loss, to hell.

A disciple trapped by despair

§4Take some teacher who has this doctrine and view: ‘Everyone who kills a living creature, steals, commits sexual misconduct, or lies goes to a place of loss, to hell.’ And there’s a disciple who is devoted to that teacher. They think: ‘My teacher has this doctrine and view: “Everyone who kills a living creature, steals, commits sexual misconduct, or lies goes to a place of loss, to hell.” But I’ve killed living creatures … stolen … committed sexual misconduct … or lied.’ They get the view: ‘I too am going to a place of loss, to hell.’ Unless they give up that speech and that thought, and let go of that view, they will be placed in hell as if delivered there.

A disciple who reforms through remorse

§5But consider when a Realized One arises in the world, perfected, a fully awakened Buddha, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, holy, knower of the world, supreme guide for those fit for training, teacher of gods and humans, awakened, blessed. In many ways he criticizes and denounces killing living creatures, saying: ‘Stop killing living creatures!’ He criticizes and denounces stealing … sexual misconduct … lying, saying: ‘Stop lying!’ And there’s a disciple who is devoted to that teacher. Then they reflect: ‘In many ways the Buddha criticizes and denounces killing living creatures, saying: “Stop killing living creatures!” But I have killed living creatures to a certain extent. That’s not right, it’s not good, and I feel remorseful because of it. But I can’t undo what I have done.’ Reflecting like this, they give up killing living creatures, and in future they don’t kill living creatures. That’s how to give up this bad deed and get past it.

§6‘In many ways the Buddha criticizes and denounces stealing …’ ‘In many ways the Buddha criticizes and denounces sexual misconduct …’ ‘In many ways the Buddha criticizes and denounces lying, saying: “Stop lying!” But I have lied to a certain extent. That’s not right, it’s not good, and I feel remorseful because of it. But I can’t undo what I have done.’ Reflecting like this, they give up lying, and in future they refrain from lying. That’s how to give up this bad deed and get past it.

Ten things given up

§7They give up killing living creatures. They give up stealing. They give up sexual misconduct. They give up lying. They give up backbiting. They give up harsh speech. They give up talking nonsense. They give up covetousness. They give up ill will and malevolence. They give up wrong view and have right view.

The horn blower

§8That noble disciple is rid of desire, rid of ill will, unconfused, aware, and mindful. They meditate spreading a heart full of love to one direction, and to the second, and to the third, and to the fourth. In the same way above, below, across, everywhere, all around, they spread a heart full of love to the whole world — abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will. Suppose there was a powerful horn blower. They’d easily make themselves heard in the four quarters. In the same way, when the heart’s release by love has been developed and cultivated like this, any limited deeds they’ve done don’t remain or persist there.

§9Then that noble disciple is rid of desire, rid of ill will, unconfused, aware, and mindful. They meditate spreading a heart full of compassion … They meditate spreading a heart full of rejoicing … They meditate spreading a heart full of equanimity to one direction, and to the second, and to the third, and to the fourth. In the same way above, below, across, everywhere, all around, they spread a heart full of equanimity to the whole world — abundant, expansive, limitless, free of enmity and ill will. Suppose there was a powerful horn blower. They’d easily make themselves heard in the four quarters. In the same way, when the heart’s release by equanimity has been developed and cultivated like this, any limited deeds they’ve done don’t remain or persist there.”

The headman's response

§10When he said this, Asibandhaka’s son the chief said to the Buddha, “Excellent, sir! Excellent! … From this day forth, may the Buddha remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.”

Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 56.11

Rolling Forth the Wheel of Dhamma

Dhammacakkappavattanasutta

Setting
Near Varanasi, in the deer park at Isipatana
Speakers
The Buddha, addressing the group of five ascetics who had previously practiced austerities with him
Form
The Buddha's first formal discourse: the two extremes, the middle way, and the four noble truths analyzed in three turnings and twelve aspects
Length
~9 minutes to read
Northern parallel
Exceptionally well attested across the tradition — versions of the first sermon survive in Chinese, Tibetan, Sanskrit, and Gāndhārī sources, reflecting its unique standing as the opening proclamation of the entire teaching
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — every phrase is load-bearing; short in length but exceptionally dense and precisely worded

Why this discourse

Every teaching the Buddha gave over forty-five years can, in one sense, be read as commentary on this one. Delivered to five former companions who had abandoned him in disappointment, at a deer park near the city now called Varanasi, this is the discourse the tradition remembers as the first turning of the wheel of Dhamma — the moment a private awakening became a public teaching. It names the two extremes to be avoided, describes the middle way that avoids them, and states the four noble truths in the precise, three-fold analysis the Buddha says he needed to complete before he could rightly claim full awakening at all. Few passages in any religious tradition carry this much structural weight in so short a space, and few reward as much care in the reading.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Avoiding both indulgence in sensual pleasure and indulgence in self-mortification, the Buddha describes a middle way — the noble eightfold path — and states the four noble truths of suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the practice leading to its cessation, each fully understood in three stages, the completion of which is what qualified him, in his own account, to declare his awakening to the world.

Setting: five companions, one disappointment

The discourse opens simply: "At one time the Buddha was staying near Varanasi, in the deer park at Isipatana. There the Buddha addressed the group of five mendicants." Those five had practiced extreme austerities alongside the future Buddha for years, and had left him when he abandoned that path in favor of ordinary food, judging him to have given up the struggle. This is their reunion, and it is worth remembering that the Buddha is speaking to an audience that has reason to be skeptical, not automatically receptive — which makes his opening move, addressing self-mortification directly and by name as one of two mistaken extremes, a direct answer to exactly the disagreement that had separated them.

Two extremes, named plainly

"Mendicants, these two extremes should not be cultivated by one gone forth. What two? Indulgence in sensual pleasures, which is low, crude, ordinary, ignoble, and pointless. And indulgence in self-mortification, which is painful, ignoble, and pointless." Neither extreme is rejected on grounds of difficulty or unfamiliarity; both are rejected as simply unproductive — the first because it is beneath what a serious seeker should pursue, the second because pain by itself, however severe, does not by itself produce insight. This is a genuinely original position relative to the surrounding culture of the Buddha's day, which largely assumed that greater asceticism meant greater spiritual attainment; the Buddha's own six years of extreme self-mortification, abandoned just before his awakening, stand behind this judgment as lived experience rather than abstract theory.

The middle way, named precisely

"Avoiding these two extremes, the Realized One understood the middle way of practice, which gives vision and knowledge, and leads to peace, direct knowledge, awakening, and extinguishment." And what is it? "It is simply this noble eightfold path, that is: right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion." The middle way, notably, is not vague moderation between two poles but a specific eight-factor program, named factor by factor in exactly the same list analyzed in detail in SN 45.8, also in this collection. The claim is precise: this particular path, and not some undefined balance point, is what avoids both named extremes.

The first noble truth: suffering

The Buddha then states the four truths, each introduced with the same formula, "Now this is the noble truth of…". Suffering is enumerated concretely before it is summarized abstractly: "Rebirth is suffering; old age is suffering; illness is suffering; death is suffering; being coupled with the disliked is suffering; separation from the liked is suffering; not getting what you wish for is suffering." Only after this list of recognizable, ordinary difficulties does the discourse offer its compressed formula: "In brief, the five grasping aggregates are suffering." The order matters — the truth is anchored first in lived experience anyone can recognize, and only then generalized into the technical language (the five aggregates subject to grasping: form, feeling, perception, volitional formations, and consciousness) that the rest of the tradition will use as shorthand for the same point.

The second noble truth: origin

The cause of suffering is named as "the craving that leads to future lives, mixed up with relishing and greed, taking pleasure there wherever it alights" — and then specified into three kinds: "craving for sensual pleasures, craving to continue existence, and craving for nonexistence." The third of these, craving for nonexistence (vibhava-taṇhā), is easy to skip past but doctrinally important: craving is not simply the desire to acquire or to continue existing but can equally take the form of wanting to be annihilated, to escape or end experience altogether — a craving the tradition treats as no less a cause of continued suffering than the wish to go on existing.

The third noble truth: cessation

Cessation is defined as nothing added to the picture, only craving's removal: "the fading away and cessation of that very same craving with no residue left behind; giving it away, letting it go, releasing it, and not clinging to it." The truth of cessation names no new state to be attained on top of the first two truths; it is what remains, or rather what is no longer obstructed, once the specific cause identified in the second truth is uprooted.

The fourth noble truth: the practice

The path leading to that cessation is, once again, "simply this noble eightfold path" — the same list given earlier as the middle way. The eightfold path therefore appears twice in this single short discourse: once as the answer to how a renunciant should live between two failed extremes, and once as the fourth of the four truths, the practical, causal answer to how suffering's cessation is actually brought about. The two framings are not separate teachings but the same content viewed from two angles.

Three turnings, twelve aspects

Having stated all four truths, the discourse does something else many readers pass over too quickly: for each truth, it registers three distinct realizations, using an almost incantatory repeated formula. For the first truth: "'This is the noble truth of suffering' … 'This noble truth of suffering should be completely understood' … 'This noble truth of suffering has been completely understood.'" The same three-part structure is then applied to each of the remaining three truths, with the appropriate verb substituted for what that particular truth calls for:

TruthTurning 1: recognizedTurning 2: the taskTurning 3: task done
Suffering"This is suffering"should be completely understoodhas been completely understood
Origin"This is the origin"should be given uphas been given up
Cessation"This is the cessation"should be realizedhas been realized
Practice"This is the practice"should be developedhas been developed

Four truths, three turnings each, gives the traditional count of twelve aspects (dvādasākāra) of knowledge. The structure matters because it specifies a different, precise task appropriate to each truth — suffering is to be understood, not eliminated; its origin is to be given up; cessation is to be realized, since it is already latently present rather than manufactured; and the path is to be developed, since it is a practice cultivated over time rather than a fact simply grasped. Collapsing all four into a single undifferentiated verb ("believe," say, or "know") loses exactly this precision.

The discourse is explicit that this entire twelvefold structure is what awakening required: "As long as my true knowledge and vision about these four noble truths was not fully purified in these three rounds and twelve aspects, I didn't announce my supreme perfect awakening… But when my true knowledge and vision… was fully purified in these three rounds and twelve aspects, I announced my supreme perfect awakening." Full awakening, on this account, is not simply grasping the content of the four truths in the abstract but having completed the specific task each truth calls for, and knowing with certainty that this completion has actually occurred.

The earth shakes, and Koṇḍañña understands

What follows is recounted with unusual cosmic drama for so terse a text: as the Buddha finishes speaking, "the stainless, immaculate vision of the Dhamma arose in Venerable Koṇḍañña: 'Everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease.'" This brief formula — recognizing impermanence as a universal feature of conditioned things — is treated across the canon as the hallmark realization of stream-entry, the first stage of awakening. Koṇḍañña is thereby marked as the first person after the Buddha himself to attain any level of awakening, and the news is said to travel outward instantly: earth gods cry out that the wheel of Dhamma has been rolled forth and cannot be rolled back "by any ascetic or brahmin or god or Māra or divinity or by anyone in the world," and the cry is taken up in turn by each successive tier of gods, all the way up to the Brahmā realms, while "this ten-thousandfold galaxy shook and rocked and trembled" and "an immeasurable, magnificent light appeared in the world, surpassing the glory of the gods." The Buddha's own reaction is understated by comparison, and famous precisely for that understatement: "Koṇḍañña has really understood! Koṇḍañña has really understood!" — the exclamation from which Koṇḍañña's lasting epithet, Aññāsi-Koṇḍañña, "Koṇḍañña who understood," derives.

Reading the whole arc together

It is worth holding the whole discourse in view at once: a rejection of two failed extremes born of the Buddha's own tested experience; a positive alternative named with total precision, the eightfold path; four truths that diagnose the condition, its cause, its resolution, and the route to that resolution; a threefold analysis of exactly what completing that diagnosis actually requires; and, immediately, a living demonstration that the teaching works — not left as a claim to be tested only in some distant future, but confirmed within the same conversation, in the same clearing, in the person of Koṇḍañña. Very few founding documents of any tradition build their case with this much structural discipline while still ending in something as human and specific as one man's dawning understanding.

Key terms

majjhimā paṭipadā — the "middle way," identified in this discourse specifically with the noble eightfold path, avoiding indulgence in sensual pleasure and in self-mortification.
cattāri ariyasaccāni — the four noble truths: suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the practice leading to its cessation, given here in their classic, compressed formulas.
taṇhā — craving, the second truth's cause of suffering, analyzed into three kinds: for sensual pleasures, for continued existence, and for non-existence (annihilation).
tiparivaṭṭaṃ dvādasākāraṃ — "three turnings, twelve aspects": the threefold analysis (this is the truth; it calls for a specific task; that task is done) applied to each of the four truths, whose completion the Buddha says qualified him to declare full awakening.
dhammacakkhu — the "vision of the Dhamma," the realization that arises in Koṇḍañña ("everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease"), marking stream-entry, the first stage of awakening.
Aññāsi-Koṇḍañña — "Koṇḍañña who understood," the epithet given to Koṇḍañña from the Buddha's own exclamation at the close of this discourse.

The text

The discourse in full: the two extremes, the middle way, the four noble truths in their three-turnings analysis, and the account of Koṇḍañña's awakening and the cosmic response that follows. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Setting

§1At one time the Buddha was staying near Varanasi, in the deer park at Isipatana. There the Buddha addressed the group of five mendicants:

Two extremes, and the middle way

§2"Mendicants, these two extremes should not be cultivated by one gone forth. What two? Indulgence in sensual pleasures, which is low, crude, ordinary, ignoble, and pointless. And indulgence in self-mortification, which is painful, ignoble, and pointless. Avoiding these two extremes, the Realized One understood the middle way of practice, which gives vision and knowledge, and leads to peace, direct knowledge, awakening, and extinguishment.

§3And what is that middle way of practice? It is simply this noble eightfold path, that is: right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion. This is that middle way of practice, which gives vision and knowledge, and leads to peace, direct knowledge, awakening, and extinguishment.

The four noble truths

§4Now this is the noble truth of suffering. Rebirth is suffering; old age is suffering; illness is suffering; death is suffering; being coupled with the disliked is suffering; separation from the liked is suffering; not getting what you wish for is suffering. In brief, the five grasping aggregates are suffering.

Now this is the noble truth of the origin of suffering. It's the craving that leads to future lives, mixed up with relishing and greed, taking pleasure there wherever it alights. That is, craving for sensual pleasures, craving to continue existence, and craving for nonexistence.

Now this is the noble truth of the cessation of suffering. It's the fading away and cessation of that very same craving with no residue left behind; giving it away, letting it go, releasing it, and not clinging to it.

Now this is the noble truth of the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering. It is simply this noble eightfold path, that is: right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion.

Three turnings, twelve aspects

§5–8'This is the noble truth of suffering.' Such was the vision, knowledge, wisdom, realization, and light that arose in me regarding teachings not learned before from another. 'This noble truth of suffering should be completely understood.' Such was the vision that arose in me… 'This noble truth of suffering has been completely understood.' Such was the vision that arose in me…

'This is the noble truth of the origin of suffering.' Such was the vision that arose in me… 'This noble truth of the origin of suffering should be given up.' Such was the vision that arose in me… 'This noble truth of the origin of suffering has been given up.' Such was the vision that arose in me…

'This is the noble truth of the cessation of suffering.' Such was the vision that arose in me… 'This noble truth of the cessation of suffering should be realized.' Such was the vision that arose in me… 'This noble truth of the cessation of suffering has been realized.' Such was the vision that arose in me…

'This is the noble truth of the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering.' Such was the vision that arose in me… 'This noble truth of the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering should be developed.' Such was the vision that arose in me… 'This noble truth of the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering has been developed.' Such was the vision, knowledge, wisdom, realization, and light that arose in me regarding teachings not learned before from another.

The qualification for awakening

§9–10As long as my true knowledge and vision about these four noble truths was not fully purified in these three rounds and twelve aspects, I didn't announce my supreme perfect awakening in this world with its gods, Māras, and Divinities, this population with its ascetics and brahmins, its gods and humans. But when my true knowledge and vision about these four noble truths was fully purified in these three rounds and twelve aspects, I announced my supreme perfect awakening in this world with its gods, Māras, and Divinities, this population with its ascetics and brahmins, its gods and humans. Knowledge and vision arose in me: 'My freedom is unshakable; this is my last rebirth; now there'll be no more future lives.'"

That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the group of five mendicants approved what the Buddha said.

Koṇḍañña's awakening, and the wheel rolled forth

§11And while this discourse was being spoken, the stainless, immaculate vision of the Dhamma arose in Venerable Koṇḍañña: "Everything that is liable to arise is liable to cease."

§12–13And when the Buddha rolled forth the Wheel of Dhamma, the earth gods raised the cry: "Near Varanasi, in the deer park at Isipatana, the Buddha has rolled forth the supreme Wheel of Dhamma. And that wheel cannot be rolled back by any ascetic or brahmin or god or Māra or divinity or by anyone in the world." Hearing the cry of the earth gods, the gods of the four great kings… the gods of the thirty-three… the gods of Yama… the joyful gods… the gods who love to create… the gods who control what is created by others… the gods of the Divinity's host raised the cry: "Near Varanasi, in the deer park at Isipatana, the Buddha has rolled forth the supreme Wheel of Dhamma. And that wheel cannot be rolled back by any ascetic or brahmin or god or Māra or divinity or by anyone in the world."

And so at that moment, that second, that hour, the cry soared up to the realm of divinity. And this ten-thousandfold galaxy shook and rocked and trembled. And an immeasurable, magnificent light appeared in the world, surpassing the glory of the gods.

"Koṇḍañña has really understood!"

§14Then the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment: "Koṇḍañña has really understood! Koṇḍañña has really understood!" And that's how Venerable Koṇḍañña came to be known as "Koṇḍañña Who Understood".

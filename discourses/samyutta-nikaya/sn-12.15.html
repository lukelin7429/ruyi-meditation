Why this discourse

Almost every metaphysical debate about the self collapses into one of two moves: insisting that something — a soul, a world, a self — really and simply exists, or insisting, in reaction, that it simply does not. This short exchange between the Buddha and the monk KaccƒÅna refuses both moves at once, and is candid about what makes that refusal possible: not a clever verbal trick, but a description of experience that never needs the word “exists” or “doesn’t exist” in the first place. Few passages in the canon compress so much philosophical weight into so few lines, and few are cited as often, by later interpreters across very different traditions, as the definitive statement of what dependent origination is actually for.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Asked how “right view” is defined, the Buddha answers that the world generally leans on one of two extremes — existence or non-existence — but that someone who sees the world’s origin and cessation clearly has no use for either extreme, and he locates his own teaching, dependent origination, precisely at this middle point between them.

A monk’s direct question

The setup is minimal: Venerable KaccƒÅnagotta approaches the Buddha and asks a single, pointed question — “They speak of this thing called ‘right view’. How is right view defined?” There is no narrative frame, no other characters, no scene-setting beyond the bare fact of the question. This spareness is itself part of the discourse’s character: everything that matters happens in the answer.

Two extremes the world leans on

The Buddha begins with a diagnosis rather than a definition: “This world mostly relies on the duality of existence and non-existence.” That is, when people try to make sense of themselves and the world, they tend to gravitate to one of two poles — treating the world, or a self within it, as something that simply and straightforwardly is (a position the tradition elsewhere calls eternalism), or reacting against that by insisting it simply is not, that nothing really persists or matters (annihilationism). Both are described as extremes, and the Buddha declines to adjudicate between them by picking one. Instead, he offers a way of seeing that makes the choice between them beside the point.

Right view: neither extreme

The core of the answer turns on two matching clauses: “for one who truly sees the origin of the world with right understanding, the concept of non-existence regarding the world does not occur” — and, symmetrically, “for one who truly sees the cessation of the world with right understanding, the concept of existence regarding the world does not occur.” Seeing how something arises, dependent on conditions, makes it impossible to say it simply doesn’t exist at all — something is plainly happening. And seeing how that same thing ceases, once its conditions cease, makes it equally impossible to say it exists in the fixed, independent way “existence” usually implies. Watching a process arise and pass rules out both a flat denial and a flat affirmation.

What actually holds the world in place

The Buddha then diagnoses why the extremes have such a grip: “this world is for the most part shackled by getting involved, grasping, and insisting” — not by abstract philosophical error alone, but by an active, ongoing habit of mental fixation on “my self.” Someone who does not get caught up in that particular grasping and fixation is described as simply seeing that “what arises is just suffering arising, and what ceases is just suffering ceasing” — without doubt or uncertainty, and, notably, with knowledge that is “independent of others.” This last phrase matters: the right view being described here is not a doctrine accepted on someone else’s authority, including the Buddha’s own, but something confirmed directly, by seeing the arising and ceasing of experience for oneself.

The middle teaching, stated as a formula

Having answered KaccƒÅna’s question, the Buddha names the two extremes explicitly — “‘All exists’: this is one extreme. ‘All does not exist’: this is the second extreme” — and states that avoiding both, “the Realized One teaches by the middle way,” before reciting the familiar twelve-fold chain of dependent origination, forward and in reverse, as that middle teaching itself. This is one of the clearest places in the canon where dependent origination is explicitly framed not primarily as an account of causation for its own sake, but as the specific alternative to two failed metaphysical positions: it says neither that things simply are nor that they simply are not, only that they arise given conditions and cease when those conditions cease.

Why this reframing matters

It is easy to encounter the twelve-fold chain purely as a list to be memorized, or as a technical account of rebirth mechanics. This discourse insists on a different first use for it: as a way of talking about the world that never has to commit to either “it exists” or “it doesn’t exist” in an unqualified sense, because both of those claims already assume a kind of fixed, independent thing that dependent origination denies from the outset. Later interpreters across the tradition, including entire schools of philosophical commentary, return to this short exchange again and again as the canonical statement of the middle way in its properly metaphysical, and not merely ethical, sense.

The text

The discourse in full: KaccƒÅna’s question, the Buddha’s answer defining right view against the two extremes, and the closing statement of the middle teaching as dependent origination itself. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A monk’s question

§1At SƒÅvatthƒ´. Then Venerable KaccƒÅnagotta went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and said to him: “Sir, they speak of this thing called ‘right view’. How is right view defined?”

Right view: neither extreme

§2“KaccƒÅna, this world mostly relies on the duality of existence and non-existence. But for one who truly sees the origin of the world with right understanding, the concept of non-existence regarding the world does not occur. And for one who truly sees the cessation of the world with right understanding, the concept of existence regarding the world does not occur. This world is for the most part shackled by getting involved, grasping, and insisting. But if—for all that getting involved and grasping, mental fixation, and insistence and underlying tendency—they do not get attracted, grasp, and fixate on the thought, ‘my self’, they’ll have no doubt or uncertainty that what arises is just suffering arising, and what ceases is just suffering ceasing. Their knowledge of this is independent of others. This is how right view is defined.

The middle teaching

§3‘All exists’: this is one extreme. ‘All does not exist’: this is the second extreme. Avoiding these two extremes, the Realized One teaches by the middle way: ‘Ignorance is a requirement for choices. Choices are a requirement for consciousness. … That is how this entire mass of suffering originates. When ignorance fades away and ceases with no residue left behind, choices cease. When choices cease, consciousness ceases. … That is how this entire mass of suffering ceases.’”

Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 45.8

Analysis

Vibhaṅgasutta

Setting

Speakers

Form

Length
~6 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Saṃyukta-āgama (T99), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — plain, systematic definitions, though right immersion's four-absorption formula rewards a slower reading

Why this discourse

The noble eightfold path is invoked constantly across the canon — as the fourth noble truth, as the summary of the entire practice, as the answer to nearly every question about what a Buddhist should actually do. What is invoked far less often is a full, careful definition of each of its eight factors, stated once and for all in the Buddha's own words. This is that discourse: a plain, unhurried walk through right view, right thought, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion, each given its own precise formula. Paired with SN 12.2, which performs the identical service for dependent origination, these two short suttas function as the closest thing the early texts offer to a technical glossary for the two frameworks invoked more often than any others in the entire Pāli Canon.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Asked, in effect, "what exactly is the noble eightfold path?", the Buddha answers with eight short, exact definitions — one for each factor — that function throughout the rest of the canon as the default meaning of those eight terms whenever they appear elsewhere without further explanation.

A promise, then the analysis

The discourse opens with the Buddha announcing exactly what he is about to do: "I will teach and analyze for you the noble eightfold path." There is no narrative setting beyond this, no interlocutor asking a question, no simile framing the teaching — just the path named, and then, factor by factor, defined. This directness is itself notable: elsewhere the eightfold path is assumed, listed, or invoked as a known quantity; here alone it is unpacked term by term, as if the Buddha is deliberately supplying the dictionary entry that every other reference to the path elsewhere in the canon quietly relies on.

The first four: view, thought, speech, action

Right view is defined with striking economy: simply "knowing about suffering, the origin of suffering, the cessation of suffering, and the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering" — that is, understanding the four noble truths. Right view, on this definition, is not a set of philosophical opinions to hold but a correct grasp of the diagnostic framework the rest of the practice depends on.

Right thought (or right purpose, in some translations) is the intention behind action, stated as three orientations: "the purpose of renunciation, good will, and harmlessness" — that is, thinking in ways free of sensual craving, ill will, and cruelty.

Right speech and right action are both defined negatively, as abstentions: right speech is "refraining from speech that's false, backbiting, harsh, or nonsensical"; right action is "refraining from killing living creatures, stealing, and unchastity." Both definitions map directly onto four of the five precepts observed by lay followers, showing how the eightfold path and basic lay ethics are, at this level, the same conduct described from two angles.

Livelihood and the three efforts

Right livelihood receives the briefest definition of all eight: "a noble disciple gives up wrong livelihood and earns a living by right livelihood" — a formula that states the principle without listing which occupations count as wrong, a specification supplied elsewhere in the canon (trading in weapons, living beings, meat, alcohol, and poison, among others).

Right effort is given in full as the four right efforts already familiar from AN 4.13, on this site: generating enthusiasm, trying, exerting the mind, and striving so that (1) unskillful qualities not yet arisen don't arise, (2) unskillful qualities already arisen are given up, (3) skillful qualities not yet arisen do arise, and (4) skillful qualities already arisen are maintained, increased, and brought to fulfillment. Effort, on this analysis, is not raw exertion in general but exertion aimed with precision at exactly these four points along the boundary between what is and isn't yet present in the mind.

Mindfulness and immersion: the two meditative factors

Right mindfulness is defined as the four foundations of mindfulness: meditating by observing an aspect of the body, of feelings, of the mind, and of principles — each time "keen, aware, and mindful, rid of covetousness and displeasure for the world." This is the same fourfold structure elaborated at length in the Majjhima and Dīgha satipaṭṭhāna discourses; here it appears in its compressed, formulaic form, exactly as it is meant to be recognized whenever invoked elsewhere.

Right immersion is defined as the four jhānas in their standard description — from the first absorption, "secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unskillful qualities," with rapture and bliss born of seclusion, through the stilling of applied and sustained thought in the second, the fading of rapture in the third, and the purification of equanimity and mindfulness beyond pleasure and pain in the fourth. Concentration, on this account, is not a vague settledness of mind but this specific, four-stage progression.

A glossary, not a sermon

What makes this discourse distinctive is exactly what makes it easy to overlook: it argues for nothing, defends nothing against a rival view, and offers no simile or narrative frame. It simply supplies, once, in ordinary declarative sentences, the precise content behind eight words that get repeated constantly throughout the rest of the tradition. Read alongside SN 12.2's identical treatment of dependent origination's twelve links, this sutta and its companion together give the field a matched pair of technical glossaries behind its two most load-bearing frameworks — the analysis of how suffering arises and continues, and the analysis of the path that ends it.

Key terms

vibhaṅga — "analysis" or "exposition": the discourse's title, and its method — not argument or narrative but a plain factor-by-factor definition.
ariyo aṭṭhaṅgiko maggo — the noble eightfold path, here defined factor by factor: right view, thought, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, and immersion.
sammā-saṅkappa — right thought (or right purpose/intention), defined here as the intention of renunciation, good will, and harmlessness.
sammappadhāna — the four right efforts (preventing, giving up, developing, and maintaining), given here as the full definition of right effort; treated at length in AN 4.13.
satipaṭṭhāna — the four foundations of mindfulness (body, feelings, mind, principles), given here as the compressed formula defining right mindfulness.
jhāna — the four absorptions, given here in their standard formula as the definition of right immersion.

The text

The discourse in full: the Buddha's announcement, and all eight factors defined in sequence, each in its exact canonical formula. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The path named

§1At Sāvatthī. "Mendicants, I will teach and analyze for you the noble eightfold path. Listen and apply your mind well, I will speak." "Yes, sir," they replied. The Buddha said this:

§2"And what is the noble eightfold path? It is right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion.

Right view and right purpose

§3And what is right view? Knowing about suffering, the origin of suffering, the cessation of suffering, and the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering. This is called right view.

§4And what is right purpose? It is the purpose of renunciation, good will, and harmlessness. This is called right purpose.

Right speech and right action

§5And what is right speech? Refraining from speech that's false, backbiting, harsh, or nonsensical. This is called right speech.

§6And what is right action? Refraining from killing living creatures, stealing, and unchastity. This is called right action.

Right livelihood

§7And what is right livelihood? It's when a noble disciple gives up wrong livelihood and earns a living by right livelihood. This is called right livelihood.

Right effort

§8And what is right effort? It's when a mendicant generates enthusiasm, tries, makes an effort, exerts the mind, and strives so that bad, unskillful qualities don't arise. They generate enthusiasm, try, make an effort, exert the mind, and strive so that bad, unskillful qualities that have arisen are given up. They generate enthusiasm, try, make an effort, exert the mind, and strive so that skillful qualities that have not arisen do arise. They generate enthusiasm, try, make an effort, exert the mind, and strive so that skillful qualities that have arisen remain, are not lost, but increase, mature, and are fulfilled by development. This is called right effort.

Right mindfulness

§9And what is right mindfulness? It's when a mendicant meditates by observing an aspect of the body—keen, aware, and mindful, rid of covetousness and displeasure for the world. They meditate observing an aspect of feelings—keen, aware, and mindful, rid of covetousness and displeasure for the world. They meditate observing an aspect of the mind—keen, aware, and mindful, rid of covetousness and displeasure for the world. They meditate observing an aspect of principles—keen, aware, and mindful, rid of covetousness and displeasure for the world. This is called right mindfulness.

Right immersion

§10And what is right immersion? It's when a mendicant, quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unskillful qualities, enters and remains in the first absorption, which has the rapture and bliss born of seclusion, while placing the mind and keeping it connected. As the placing of the mind and keeping it connected are stilled, they enter and remain in the second absorption, which has the rapture and bliss born of immersion, with internal clarity and mind at one, without placing the mind and keeping it connected. And with the fading away of rapture, they enter and remain in the third absorption, where they meditate with equanimity, mindful and aware, personally experiencing the bliss of which the noble ones declare, 'Equanimous and mindful, one meditates in bliss.' With the giving up of pleasure and pain and the disappearance of former happiness and sadness, they enter and remain in the fourth absorption, without pleasure or pain, with pure equanimity and mindfulness. This is called right immersion."

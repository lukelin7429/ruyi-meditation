Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 56.31

In a Rosewood Forest

Siṁsapāsutta

Setting
Near Kosambī, in a rosewood (siṃsapā) grove
Speakers
The Buddha, addressing the assembled mendicants
Form
A single physical gesture — a handful of leaves held up against the forest canopy — used to explain the deliberate scope of everything the Buddha taught
Length
~3 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Saṃyukta-āgama (T99), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★☆☆☆☆ — brief, direct, and one of the more immediately memorable images in the entire canon

Why this discourse

This is the last of the twenty discourses gathered in this curated selection from the Saṃyutta Nikāya, and it closes on an image rather than a doctrine: a handful of rosewood leaves, held up against the whole canopy of a forest. The Buddha uses it to make a single, disarming admission — that what he taught is a small, deliberately chosen fraction of what he directly knew, offered not as a complete account of reality but strictly because it serves one purpose, the ending of suffering. After nineteen discourses ranging from the precise mechanics of dependent origination to the first sermon's full statement of the four noble truths, it is a fitting note to end on: even the most systematic teaching in this collection was never presented as the whole of what could be said, only as what actually helps.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Holding up a small handful of leaves against the leaves of the whole forest canopy, the Buddha says that what he has directly known but not taught vastly outweighs what he has taught, because he has deliberately restricted his teaching to what is beneficial and leads to liberation — namely the four noble truths — leaving unstated much else he could truthfully have said but which would not have served that purpose.

A gesture, not an argument

The setting is spare: "the Buddha was staying near Kosambī in a rosewood grove," and "the Buddha picked up a few rosewood leaves in his hand" before putting a simple question to the mendicants around him: "Which is more: the few leaves in my hand, or those in the grove above?" The answer is obvious and immediate — "the few leaves in your hand are a tiny amount. There are far more leaves in the grove above" — and it is exactly this obviousness the Buddha is relying on. Unlike many discourses that build an argument step by step, this one simply holds up a comparison so visually stark that no argument is needed, and then states the point directly: "In the same way, there is much more that I have directly known but have not explained to you. What I have explained is a tiny amount."

Not ignorance, but restraint

The claim is not that the Buddha's knowledge was limited to what he taught — if anything, the image insists on the opposite, that what he knew vastly exceeds what he said. The question the discourse actually addresses is why so much of that knowledge went unspoken, and the Buddha answers without hesitation: "Because it's not beneficial or relevant to the fundamentals of the spiritual life. It doesn't lead to disillusionment, dispassion, cessation, peace, insight, awakening, and extinguishment. That's why I haven't explained it." This is a striking editorial principle for any teacher to state so plainly: possessing true information is not, by itself, a reason to share it. What governs the choice is whether the information serves the one stated purpose of the whole undertaking.

What made the cut

What actually made it into "the leaves in the hand" is stated with the same compression used throughout this teaching, restated here in its most familiar form: "'This is suffering' … 'This is the origin of suffering' … 'This is the cessation of suffering' … 'This is the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering.'" The same four noble truths given in full, with their three-fold analysis, at the opening of this curated selection's account of the first sermon (SN 56.11) reappear here at its close, in their barest possible form — a deliberate bookend, whether or not the discourse's placement in the canon was designed with that symmetry in mind. And the reason given for teaching exactly these four and nothing more restates the same principle: "it's beneficial and relevant to the fundamentals of the spiritual life. It leads to disillusionment, dispassion, cessation, peace, insight, awakening, and extinguishment."

What was deliberately left out

Elsewhere in the canon this same reticence surfaces around specific questions the Buddha declined to answer directly — about the ultimate origin or spatial and temporal extent of the universe, and a handful of other speculative points collected under the heading of the "undeclared" (avyākata) questions. This discourse does not list those questions by name, but its logic explains why they go unanswered elsewhere: not because the Buddha lacked a view, and not because the questions are unanswerable in principle, but because settling them, one way or another, would not move a single person closer to the end of suffering. A correct answer to a question that changes nothing practical is, on this view, not worth giving.

A closing note for this curated selection

It would have been possible to end twenty discourses spanning the length and breadth of the Saṃyutta Nikāya on something more triumphant — the shaking earth and celebrating gods of the first sermon, say, or the sweeping abstraction of dependent origination. Instead, the collection closes here, on an image of restraint: a small handful of leaves, held up against a whole forest left otherwise unremarked. It is worth sitting with what that choice implies. Everything gathered across these twenty discourses — the analysis of the path, the four truths in their fullest statement, the aggregates, the six senses, the fire that burns through all experience — was, on the Buddha's own account, never offered as a complete map of everything there is to know. It was offered because it works: because the diagnosis it proposes and the practice it recommends actually address suffering, not because that diagnosis exhausts the world. A tradition capable of saying this about its own founding teachings, at the very moment those teachings are being gathered and presented as a curated whole, is making a quiet but serious claim about what teaching itself is for.

Key terms

siṃsapā — the rosewood tree (Dalbergia sissoo) whose grove gives this discourse its name and its central image.
abhiññāta — "directly known," the term for everything the Buddha says he has personally realized, whether or not he went on to teach it.
atthasaṃhita — "connected with benefit" or "beneficial," the discourse's stated criterion for what actually gets taught: relevance to the fundamentals of the spiritual life.
avyākata — the "undeclared" or "unanswered" questions elsewhere in the canon (on the origin and extent of the universe, and similar points) which this discourse's logic explains without naming.
the four noble truths, restated — suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the practice leading to its cessation, given here as the entire content of "the leaves in the hand," in their most compressed canonical form.

The text

The discourse in full, brief by design: the leaves held up, the comparison drawn, and the reasoning behind what was taught and what was withheld. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A handful of leaves

§1At one time the Buddha was staying near Kosambī in a rosewood grove. Then the Buddha picked up a few rosewood leaves in his hand and addressed the mendicants: "What do you think, mendicants? Which is more: the few leaves in my hand, or those in the grove above?"

"Sir, the few leaves in your hand are a tiny amount. There are far more leaves in the grove above."

What was withheld, and why

§1"In the same way, there is much more that I have directly known but have not explained to you. What I have explained is a tiny amount. And why haven't I explained it? Because it's not beneficial or relevant to the fundamentals of the spiritual life. It doesn't lead to disillusionment, dispassion, cessation, peace, insight, awakening, and extinguishment. That's why I haven't explained it.

What was taught, and why

§2–3And what have I explained? I have explained: 'This is suffering' … 'This is the origin of suffering' … 'This is the cessation of suffering' … 'This is the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering'.

And why have I explained this? Because it's beneficial and relevant to the fundamentals of the spiritual life. It leads to disillusionment, dispassion, cessation, peace, insight, awakening, and extinguishment. That's why I've explained it.

The conclusion

§4That's why you should practice meditation …"

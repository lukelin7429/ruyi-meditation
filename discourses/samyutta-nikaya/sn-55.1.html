Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 55.1

A Wheel-Turning Monarch

Cakkavattisutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, addressed to the assembled mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha alone
Form
A single provocative comparison, weighing the greatest conceivable worldly and heavenly sovereignty against four qualities of stream-entry
Length
~4 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Saṃyukta-āgama (T99), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — short and vivid, its force lying in the comparison rather than in any difficult vocabulary

Why this discourse

This is the opening discourse of the Sotāpatti-saṃyutta, the "connected discourses on stream-entry," and it announces its theme with a deliberately extravagant comparison. On one side: a wheel-turning monarch (cakkavatti), the legendary figure who rules all four continents of the ancient cosmology by righteousness rather than force, and who upon death is reborn among the gods of the Thirty-Three to enjoy heavenly pleasures beyond ordinary imagining. On the other side: four qualities said to define the first stage of awakening, stream-entry — qualities available, at least in principle, to any sincere lay follower. The Buddha states, without hedging, that the second is worth incomparably more than the first. It is one of the canon's most memorable exercises in relative value, precisely because it stages the comparison at the most extreme scale imaginable.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Ruling all four continents of the world and enjoying rebirth among the Thirty-Three Gods is not worth a sixteenth part of possessing the four qualities of a stream-enterer — unwavering confidence in the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha, together with ethical conduct loved by the noble ones — because worldly and heavenly sovereignty, however vast, still leaves a being exposed to future rebirth in the lower realms, while stream-entry closes off that possibility for good.

The largest possible worldly prize

The discourse opens by setting the highest conceivable secular and heavenly bar. A wheel-turning monarch is not merely a powerful king but, in the traditional cosmology, a righteous ruler of literally the entire habitable world — all four continents at once, governing by the appearance of a miraculous wheel rather than by conquest. And the sutta does not stop with earthly rule: this same monarch, "when his body breaks up, after death," is reborn "in a good place, a heavenly realm, in the company of the gods of the thirty-three," entertained in the Garden of Delight, escorted by nymphs, supplied with the five kinds of heavenly sensual pleasure. It is difficult to imagine a more totalizing picture of worldly and heavenly success than the one offered in these two sentences — complete dominion in this life, complete heavenly indulgence in the next.

And yet, the Buddha immediately adds, "as he's lacking four things, he's not exempt from hell, the animal realm, or the ghost realm." Even this pinnacle of fortune carries no protection against a fall into the lower realms somewhere down the line of future rebirths. Vastness of position, however extreme, does not by itself secure a being's future.

Rags, scraps, and four things

Against this the Buddha sets a deliberately unglamorous counter-image: "a noble disciple wears rags and feeds on scraps of almsfood." No throne, no continent, no retinue of nymphs — by ordinary standards, about as modest a life as the text can picture. But this person, "as they have four things," is "exempt from hell, the animal realm, or the ghost realm." The contrast in surface presentation could not be sharper, which is exactly the point: the discourse is testing whether the reader is tracking real security against loss, or merely visible magnificence.

The four factors of stream-entry

The four qualities named are the classic factors of stream-entry (sotāpatti), given here in their standard formulas:

FactorIn brief
Confidence in the Buddha"experiential confidence" that he is perfected, fully awakened, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, teacher of gods and humans
Confidence in the teachingthat it is well explained, apparent in the present life, immediately effective, inviting inspection, to be known for oneself by sensible people
Confidence in the Saṅghathat the community of the Buddha's disciples practices well, directly, systematically, and properly, and is a supreme field of merit
Ethical conduct loved by the noble onesunbroken, impeccable, spotless, unmarred, liberating, praised by sensible people, leading to immersion

Each confidence is called "experiential" — not blind faith adopted on authority, but a confidence formed through one's own direct encounter with the Buddha's teaching and its results. The fourth factor, ethical conduct, is distinct from the first three in kind: where confidence concerns what a person trusts, ethical conduct concerns how a person actually behaves, and the formula's emphasis on conduct that is "unbroken" and "leading to immersion" ties ethics directly back into the path toward deeper meditative attainment rather than treating it as a separate department of life.

Why the comparison isn't close

The Buddha's closing line states the ratio plainly: "gaining these four continents is not worth a sixteenth part of gaining these four things." The reasoning beneath the number, though not spelled out in this brief sutta, follows directly from what has just been said: the wheel-turning monarch's dominion, however total, and his heavenly rebirth, however blissful, are still temporary states within an ongoing cycle of rebirth that can, in time, still lead down into loss. Stream-entry, by contrast, is defined elsewhere in the canon as an irreversible attainment — a "vision of the Dhamma" that permanently forecloses rebirth in the lower realms and guarantees eventual full awakening within a bounded number of further lives (traditionally, at most seven). The comparison is therefore not really between two different sizes of good fortune but between two different kinds of security: one merely very large and still exposed to loss, the other modest in outward appearance and permanently secured.

A provocation, deliberately

It would be easy to read this discourse as devaluing worldly success in general, but its target is more precise: not power or pleasure as such, but the assumption that scale of worldly attainment tracks scale of real benefit. By setting the comparison at the most extreme point the tradition's cosmology allows — total earthly rule plus heavenly indulgence — the sutta forecloses the objection that a more modest example might have tipped the scales differently. Whatever the very best the world and the heavens have to offer might be, the argument runs, it still falls short of a secured trajectory toward liberation, however unglamorous the life of the person who holds it.

Key terms

cakkavatti — "wheel-turning monarch," a righteous universal ruler of all four continents in the traditional cosmology, guided by a miraculous wheel rather than by conquest.
tāvatiṃsa — the heaven of the "Thirty-Three Gods," ruled by Sakka, one of the lower heavenly realms and the destination named for the monarch's rebirth here.
sotāpatti — "stream-entry," the first of the four stages of awakening, marked by the four factors given in this discourse and defined elsewhere as irreversible.
aveccappasāda — "experiential" or "verified" confidence, the term used for the first three factors: confidence grounded in direct encounter, not adopted second-hand.
ariya-kanta sīla — "ethical conduct loved by the noble ones," the fourth factor: conduct that is unbroken, impeccable, and conducive to immersion.

The text

The discourse in full, brief by design: the wheel-turning monarch's fortune, the noble disciple's four qualities, and the closing comparison. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A monarch's fortune, still exposed

§1At Sāvatthī. There the Buddha… said: "Mendicants, suppose a wheel-turning monarch were to rule as sovereign lord over these four continents. And when his body breaks up, after death, he's reborn in a good place, a heavenly realm, in the company of the gods of the thirty-three. There he entertains himself in the Garden of Delight, escorted by a band of nymphs, and supplied and provided with the five kinds of heavenly sensual stimulation. Still, as he's lacking four things, he's not exempt from hell, the animal realm, or the ghost realm. He's not exempt from places of loss, bad places, the underworld.

A disciple's four things

§1Now suppose a noble disciple wears rags and feeds on scraps of almsfood. Still, as they have four things, they're exempt from hell, the animal realm, or the ghost realm. They're exempt from places of loss, bad places, the underworld.

§2What four? It's when a noble disciple has experiential confidence in the Buddha: 'That Blessed One is perfected, a fully awakened Buddha, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, holy, knower of the world, supreme guide for those fit for training, teacher of gods and humans, awakened, blessed.' They have experiential confidence in the teaching: 'The teaching is well explained by the Buddha—apparent in the present life, immediately effective, inviting inspection, relevant, so that sensible people can know it for themselves.' They have experiential confidence in the Saṅgha: 'The Saṅgha of the Buddha's disciples is practicing the way that's good, direct, systematic, and proper. It consists of the four pairs, the eight individual persons. This is the Saṅgha of the Buddha's disciples that is worthy of offerings dedicated to the gods, worthy of hospitality, worthy of a religious donation, worthy of greeting with cupped palms, and is the supreme field of merit for the world.' Furthermore, a noble disciple's ethical conduct is loved by the noble ones, unbroken, impeccable, spotless, and unmarred, liberating, praised by sensible people, not mistaken, and leading to immersion. These are the four qualities that they possess.

The comparison

§2And, mendicants, gaining these four continents is not worth a sixteenth part of gaining these four things."

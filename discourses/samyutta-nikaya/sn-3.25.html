Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 3.25

The Simile of the Mountain

Pabbatūpamasutta

Setting
At Sāvatthī; King Pasenadi visits the Buddha in the middle of a busy working day
Speakers
The Buddha and King Pasenadi of Kosala
Form
A hypothetical scenario posed as a question, the king’s honest answer, the point revealed directly, then restated as verse
Length
~6 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Saṃyukta-āgama (T99), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — a vivid, self-explaining parable with little technical vocabulary

Why this discourse

Few images in the early texts land as hard, or as fast, as this one: an unstoppable mountain of solid rock, advancing from every direction at once, crushing everything in its path, with no wall, no army, and no amount of wealth able to slow it down. The Buddha builds the image patiently through a hypothetical question to a busy, powerful king, lets the king supply the only sane answer himself, and only then reveals what the mountain actually is. Addressed to political power at the height of its confidence, in the middle of a working day devoted to statecraft, it is one of the canon’s most direct confrontations with mortality — and one of its most quotable.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Asked what he would do if an unstoppable mountain of rock were crushing everything as it advanced from all four directions at once, King Pasenadi answers that there would be nothing to do but live by the teaching and practice what is skillful — and the Buddha then reveals that old age and death are exactly that mountain, already advancing on everyone, including the king himself.

A king in the middle of his day

The scene opens on King Pasenadi of Kosala — a recurring figure across the Saṃyutta Nikāya’s third book, which collects a whole series of his encounters with the Buddha — arriving in the middle of the day’s business. Asked where he has come from, he explains candidly that he has been occupied with “the duties of such kings” who are “infatuated with authority, and obsessed with greed for sensual pleasures,” language that reads less as an insult from the Buddha than as the king’s own frank self-description of the pressures of rule: administration, territory, and the constant demands of holding power. It is a deliberately unglamorous opening — not a king at leisure or in crisis, but a king interrupting a perfectly ordinary, busy workday to pay a visit.

The Buddha builds an impossible scenario

Rather than commenting directly on the king’s business, the Buddha poses a hypothetical, methodically, direction by direction: suppose a trustworthy, dependable man arrived from the east reporting a huge mountain, reaching the clouds, advancing and crushing every living creature in its path. Then suppose a second such report arrived from the west, a third from the north, and a fourth from the south — the same mountain, or an identical one, closing in from every side at once, leaving no direction of escape. The Buddha then asks directly: given that human life is rare and precious, and given such an inescapable, universal threat, what would the king do?

The king’s answer. “Sir, what could I do but practice the teachings, practice morality, doing skillful and good actions?”

Pasenadi’s reply is immediate and unhesitating — he does not need the scenario explained twice. Faced with a genuinely inescapable threat, the only sane response, he sees at once, is to spend whatever time remains living well.

The reveal: it was never hypothetical

Having secured this answer, the Buddha delivers the turn the whole exchange has been building toward: “I tell you, great king, I announce to you: old age and death are advancing upon you.” The mountain was never a fantasy scenario at all — it was a description, in disguise, of something already actually happening to the king as they spoke. Pressed with the same question restated directly — given that old age and death are advancing, what will he do? — Pasenadi gives the identical answer, now elaborated at length: he lists, one by one, the tools a king would ordinarily rely on against an approaching threat — battles fought with elephants, cavalry, chariots, and infantry; wise ministers skilled in diplomacy, capable of dividing an enemy through counsel and negotiation; abundant stored wealth, gold both coined and uncoined, that could be used to bribe an approaching enemy into retreat — and states flatly, of each one in turn, that “there is no place, no domain” for it when old age and death are what is advancing. Military force, diplomatic skill, and financial power are precisely the instruments of statecraft the king has spent the day exercising; each is named and set aside as useless here.

The verse restatement

The Buddha confirms the king’s answer — “That’s so true, great king! That’s so true!” — and then restates the entire teaching as verse, sharpening the image further: the mountains are now explicitly “of solid rock,” pressing against the sky, drawing in from all sides, crushing the four quarters, just as old age and death advance on every kind of living creature without exception — “aristocrats, brahmins, peasants, menials, corpse-workers, and scavengers.” The verse deliberately runs down the entire social hierarchy of the day, high to low, insisting that the mountain spares nobody on the basis of rank. It closes by restating the same practical conclusion the king had already reached on his own: an astute person, seeing clearly what is actually good for themselves, places faith in the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha, and lives by that teaching in body, speech, and mind.

Why a king, and why so blunt

This sutta belongs to a cluster of Pasenadi dialogues elsewhere in this same book of the Saṃyutta that consistently use the king as a stand-in for worldly power at its most self-assured — wealthy, armed, advised by capable ministers, ruling a “vast conquered territory.” Addressing the mountain parable to exactly this figure sharpens its point: if even a king’s armies, treasury, and diplomats are powerless against old age and death, the same is true, a fortiori, of everyone with less power than a king. The discourse never asks Pasenadi to renounce his throne; its instruction is simply to spend whatever time royal duties leave over on ethical practice, since that is the one investment the mountain cannot crush.

Key terms

Pasenadi — the king of Kosala, a powerful contemporary and lay supporter of the Buddha, whose many dialogues with him form a distinct cluster within the third book of the Saṃyutta Nikāya.
pabbatūpama — “the simile of the mountain,” the discourse’s name, referring to the central image of an unstoppable mass of rock advancing from all four directions.
“no place, no domain” — the phrase repeated for each worldly power in turn (military battle, diplomacy, wealth) to state that it has no application against old age and death.
the four quarters — the cardinal directions from which the mountain approaches in the verse, emphasizing that the threat is total and inescapable rather than avoidable by fleeing.

The text

The discourse in full: the king’s arrival, the mountain scenario built direction by direction, the reveal, and the closing verses. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A king in the middle of his day

§1At Sāvatthī. King Pasenadi of Kosala sat to one side, and the Buddha said to him, “So, great king, where are you coming from in the middle of the day?” “Sir, there are anointed aristocratic kings who are infatuated with authority, and obsessed with greed for sensual pleasures. They have attained stability in the country, occupying a vast conquered territory. Today I have been busy fulfilling the duties of such kings.”

The mountain from four directions

§2“What do you think, great king? Suppose a trustworthy and dependable man were to come from the east. He’d approach you and say: ‘Please great king, you should know this. I come from the east. There I saw a huge mountain that reached the clouds. And it was coming this way, crushing all creatures. So then, great king, do what you must!’ Then a second trustworthy and dependable man were to come from the west … a third from the north … and a fourth from the south. He’d approach you and say: ‘Please great king, you should know this. I come from the south. There I saw a huge mountain that reached the clouds. And it was coming this way, crushing all creatures. So then, great king, do what you must!’ Should such a dire threat arise—a grim loss of human life, when human birth is so rare—what would you do?”

§3“Sir, what could I do but practice the teachings, practice morality, doing skillful and good actions?”

The reveal

§4“I tell you, great king, I announce to you: old age and death are advancing upon you. Since old age and death are advancing upon you, what would you do?” “Sir, what can I do but practice the teachings, practice morality, doing skillful and good actions? Sir, there are anointed aristocratic kings who are infatuated with authority, and obsessed with greed for sensual pleasures. They have attained stability in the country, occupying a vast conquered territory. Such kings engage in battles of elephants, cavalry, chariots, or infantry. But there is no place, no domain for such battles when old age and death are advancing. In this royal court there are ministers of wise counsel who are capable of dividing an approaching enemy by wise counsel. But there is no place, no domain for such diplomatic battles when old age and death are advancing. In this royal court there is abundant gold, both coined and uncoined, stored above and below ground. Using this wealth we can bribe an approaching enemy. But there is no place, no domain for such monetary battles when old age and death are advancing. When old age and death are advancing, what can I do but practice the teachings, practice morality, doing skillful and good actions?”

§5“That’s so true, great king! That’s so true! When old age and death are advancing, what can you do but practice the teachings, practice morality, doing skillful and good actions?” That is what the Buddha said. Then the Holy One, the Teacher, went on to say:

The verse

§6–7“Suppose there were vast mountains
of solid rock pressing against the sky
drawing in from all sides
and crushing the four quarters.
So too old age and death
advance upon all living creatures—
aristocrats, brahmins, peasants,
menials, corpse-workers, and scavengers.
They spare nothing.
They crush all beneath them.

§8There’s nowhere for elephants to take a stand,
nor chariots nor infantry.
They can’t be defeated
by diplomatic battles or by wealth.

§9–10That’s why an astute person,
seeing what’s good for themselves,
being wise, would place faith
in the Buddha, the teaching, and the Saṅgha.
Whoever lives by the teaching
in body, speech, and mind,
is praised in this life
and departs to rejoice in heaven.”

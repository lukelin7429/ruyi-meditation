Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 12.1

Dependent Origination

Paṭiccasamuppādasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in Jeta’s Grove, addressed to the assembled mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha alone, addressing the mendicant community directly
Form
A formal announcement, followed by the standard twelve-link formula stated forward and then in reverse
Length
~3 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Saṃyukta-āgama (T99), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — the formula itself is short and precise, but each link condenses a great deal that later suttas spend entire discourses unpacking

Why this discourse

This short, formulaic discourse opens the Nidānavagga, the “book of causation” that forms the second of the Saṃyutta Nikāya’s five books, and it states in its cleanest, most standard form the single formula that this entire book exists to explore, qualify, and apply: the twelve links of dependent origination, running from ignorance to old age and death, and then running back the other way to show how the whole chain can end. Almost nothing else in the early texts is referenced, restated, and built upon as often as this exact sequence. Reading it here, in its bare, unadorned form, is the necessary starting point for understanding a huge portion of everything else the Buddha is recorded as teaching.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Buddha lays out dependent origination as a chain of twelve conditions running from ignorance through to old age, death, and sorrow, then immediately restates the same chain in reverse to show that when ignorance fully fades away and ceases, every subsequent link ceases with it, ending the entire mass of suffering.

A formal announcement, not a casual remark

The framing here is more formal than in many suttas: the Buddha does not simply begin speaking mid-thought or in response to a question, but explicitly announces his subject in advance — “Mendicants, I will teach you dependent origination” — and asks for full attention before proceeding, using the same set-piece phrase found whenever something is being marked out as especially important: “Listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” This formality signals that what follows is not one application of a principle among others but the base statement of the principle itself, the version everything else in this section of the canon takes for granted.

The chain, forward

The Buddha then states the twelve links in strict, unbroken sequence, each condition given as a requirement for the next:

The Buddha closes this direction of the formula with a summary line worth pausing on: “That is how this entire mass of suffering originates.” The image of a “mass” (khandha, the same word used for the five aggregates) is deliberate — suffering here is not a single event with a single cause but an accumulated, compounding structure, each link feeding forward into the next, building toward the concrete, unavoidable experiences of old age, death, and grief with which the chain ends.

The chain, reversed

Immediately after stating the forward chain, the Buddha restates the entire sequence in reverse, using the language of cessation rather than conditioning: “When ignorance fades away and ceases with no residue left behind, choices cease. When choices cease, consciousness ceases,” and so on down the same twelve links, in the same order, ending: “When rebirth ceases, old age and death… cease. That is how this entire mass of suffering ceases.” The forward and reverse statements are given equal weight and identical structure — this is not an afterthought appended to the main teaching but the other half of a single, symmetrical formula. Suffering is not merely explained; its ending is shown to follow the same causal logic, link by link, all the way back to the first condition.

Why the order and the symmetry matter

It is easy to read a list of twelve terms and lose the structural point being made. The forward direction insists that suffering is not random, uncaused, or imposed from outside — it arises through a specific, traceable sequence of conditions, several of which (craving and grasping in particular) are things a person actively participates in in the present moment, not simply inherited from the distant past. The reverse direction insists, with equal force, that because suffering is conditioned in this specific, structured way, it can be unconditioned in the same way: there is no step in the chain that is mysteriously exempt from cessation once its own condition ceases. Together, the two directions form the causal skeleton beneath the four noble truths — the forward chain corresponds to the truth of the origin of suffering, and the reverse chain to the truth of its cessation, though neither the four truths nor the eightfold path is separately mentioned in this particular, minimal statement of the formula.

The opening sutta of an entire book

This discourse’s placement is not incidental to how the Saṃyutta Nikāya is organized: it is literally the first sutta of the Nidānavagga, the second of the collection’s five books, whose Pali name means, precisely, “the chapter on causes.” Dozens of suttas later in this same book return to this formula from different angles — analyzing each link individually, applying the chain to particular case studies, addressing objections to it, and exploring what does and does not follow from it — but nearly all of them presuppose that the reader already has the bare formula given here clearly in mind. Reading it as a base statement, rather than as an isolated teaching complete in itself, is the most accurate way to hold it.

Key terms

paṭiccasamuppāda — “dependent origination” or “specific conditionality,” the discourse’s subject and title: the principle that phenomena arise in dependence on specific conditions, rather than uncaused or from a single all-explaining source.
avijjā — “ignorance,” the first link in the chain: not simple factual ignorance but a fundamental not-seeing of things as they really are, particularly the four noble truths.
saṅkhārā — “choices” or intentional formations, the second link: the volitional activities, shaped by ignorance, that in turn condition consciousness.
taṇhā — “craving,” the seventh link, arising from feeling and in turn conditioning grasping; the point in the chain most directly addressed by present-moment practice.
Nidānavagga — “the book of causation,” the second of the Saṃyutta Nikāya’s five books, opened by this discourse and largely organized around variations on this same formula.

The text

The discourse in full: the Buddha’s formal announcement, the twelve-link chain stated forward, and the same chain stated in reverse. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A formal announcement

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery. There the Buddha addressed the mendicants, “Mendicants!” “Venerable sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this: “Mendicants, I will teach you dependent origination. Listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

The chain, forward

§2“And what is dependent origination? Ignorance is a requirement for choices. Choices are a requirement for consciousness. Consciousness is a requirement for name and form. Name and form are requirements for the six sense fields. The six sense fields are requirements for contact. Contact is a requirement for feeling. Feeling is a requirement for craving. Craving is a requirement for grasping. Grasping is a requirement for continued existence. Continued existence is a requirement for rebirth. Rebirth is a requirement for old age and death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress to come to be. That is how this entire mass of suffering originates. This is called dependent origination.

The chain, reversed

§3When ignorance fades away and ceases no residue left behind, choices cease. When choices cease, consciousness ceases. When consciousness ceases, name and form cease. When name and form cease, the six sense fields cease. When the six sense fields cease, contact ceases. When contact ceases, feeling ceases. When feeling ceases, craving ceases. When craving ceases, grasping ceases. When grasping ceases, continued existence ceases. When continued existence ceases, rebirth ceases. When rebirth ceases, old age and death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress cease. That is how this entire mass of suffering ceases.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said.

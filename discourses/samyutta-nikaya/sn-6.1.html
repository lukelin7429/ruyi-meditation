Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 6.1

The Appeal of the Divinity

Āyācanasutta

Setting
At Uruvelā, under the goatherd’s banyan tree by the Nerañjarā River, very shortly after the Buddha’s awakening
Speakers
The Buddha, in private reflection, and the high divinity Brahmā Sahampati
Form
Interior reflection and verse, an appeal delivered in person, and the Buddha’s survey of the world before he answers
Length
~5 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Saṃyukta-āgama (T99), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — narratively vivid, but its central hesitation assumes some familiarity with dependent origination and the idea of nibbāna

Why this discourse

This is the story of the single most consequential change of mind in the entire tradition: newly awakened, sitting alone by a river, the Buddha nearly decides to say nothing to anyone, ever. What he has understood, he reasons, runs so completely against how ordinary people live and think that trying to explain it would only exhaust him for nothing. It takes the intervention of a high divine being, and then the Buddha’s own careful survey of the different capacities scattered across the human population, to reverse that decision. Every teaching that follows anywhere in the canon depends on the outcome of this one quiet, nearly private moment of doubt.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Newly awakened and doubting whether anyone could understand what he had realized, the Buddha initially inclines toward not teaching at all, until the divinity Brahmā Sahampati appeals to him directly on behalf of “beings with little dust in their eyes,” and the Buddha, surveying humanity like a pond of lotuses at every different stage of growth, agrees out of compassion to teach.

A very particular moment

The setting is unusually precise for the Saṃyutta Nikāya, which often gives only a bare location: this happens at Uruvelā, at the root of the goatherd’s banyan tree on the bank of the Nerañjarā River, and the text specifies that it takes place “when he was first awakened” — that is, in the earliest days after the awakening itself, before any teaching has been given to anyone. This is, in other words, one of the very first scenes in the Buddha’s career as Buddha, predating the first sermon, the first students, even the decision to found a community. Everything that later becomes “Buddhism” as an institution and tradition hangs, narratively, on what happens in this single private reflection.

The Buddha’s own doubt

Sitting in retreat, the Buddha reflects on the nature of what he has just realized, and his own description of it is worth reading slowly: “deep, hard to see, hard to understand, peaceful, sublime, beyond the scope of logic, subtle, comprehensible to the astute.” He names two aspects of the realization specifically as especially hard for people to grasp: “specific conditionality, dependent origination” on one hand, and on the other “the stilling of all activities, the letting go of all attachments, the ending of craving, fading away, cessation, extinguishment” — that is, both the causal explanation of how suffering arises, and the account of what its ending actually is. His stated reason for hesitation is not modesty about his own understanding but a diagnosis of his likely audience: “people like clinging, they love it and enjoy it,” and a teaching built around letting go of exactly that clinging will strike such people as alien from the outset. Two brief verses follow, underscoring the same worry — that those “mired in greed and hate” and “besotted by greed” simply cannot see a teaching that runs, as the text puts it, “against the stream.” His conclusion, stated plainly: teaching would be “wearying and troublesome,” and so his mind “inclined to remaining passive, not to teaching the Dhamma.”

An alarmed intervention

The divinity Brahmā Sahampati, perceiving this train of thought, reacts with something close to alarm: “Oh lord! The world will be lost, the world will perish!” He appears before the Buddha at once — the text uses the same simile found throughout the canon for divine speed, “as easily as a strong person would extend or contract their arm” — kneels, and makes a direct, formal appeal: “Sir, let the Blessed One teach the Dhamma! Let the Holy One teach the Dhamma!” His argument is specific and carefully limited: not that everyone will understand, but that “there are beings with little dust in their eyes,” currently “in decline because they haven’t heard the teaching,” who will understand if given the chance. He reinforces the appeal with verse, invoking an image of standing on a high rocky mountain from which “you can see the people all around,” and urging the Buddha, “Rise, hero! Victor in battle, caravan leader, wander the world cleared of debt.”

The survey of the pond

Rather than simply accepting or refusing Sahampati’s appeal on faith, the Buddha responds by actually looking: he “surveyed the world with the eye of a Buddha, out of his compassion for sentient beings,” and what he sees is not a uniform mass of the unready but a genuine range — “beings with little dust in their eyes, and some with much dust in their eyes; with keen faculties and with weak faculties, with good qualities and with bad qualities, easy to teach and hard to teach,” some already alert to the dangers of an unskillful life and its consequences, others not. This is expressed through the discourse’s most famous image:

The lotus pond. “It’s like a pool with blue water lilies, or pink or white lotuses. Some of them sprout and grow in the water without rising above it, thriving underwater. Some of them sprout and grow in the water reaching the water’s surface. And some of them sprout and grow in the water but rise up above the water and stand with no water clinging to them.”

The image supplies exactly the reasoning the earlier hesitation lacked: if all people were equally trapped “underwater,” teaching genuinely would be pointless, exactly as the Buddha first feared. But because beings exist at every stage — some already at the surface, some already standing clear above it, needing only the right words at the right moment — there is real work for a teaching to do. The decision to teach is not optimism about universal readiness; it is a realistic assessment that readiness varies, and that some portion of it is ripe right now.

The reversal, in verse

Having surveyed the field, the Buddha replies to Sahampati directly in verse: “Flung open are the doors to freedom from death! Let those with ears to hear commit to faith.” He explains his earlier hesitation plainly rather than concealing it — “Thinking it would be troublesome, Divinity, I did not teach the sophisticated, sublime Dhamma among mankind” — and the discourse closes with Sahampati, satisfied that his request has been granted, bowing and departing exactly as the deity does at the close of SN 1.1: circling the Buddha with his right side toward him before vanishing.

What later tradition made of this

This scene has served the tradition ever after as the narrative explanation for why there is a Buddhist teaching at all, rather than a private realization that dies with the person who had it. It also supplies the lotus-pond image later used throughout Buddhist art, poetry, and teaching to describe the varying spiritual readiness of different listeners — not a single audience to be addressed identically, but a field of individuals at every different stage, some needing only to be shown what is already almost within their own reach.

Key terms

Brahmā Sahampati — a high divinity of Buddhist cosmology who intervenes at several key turning points across the canon; here he appeals to the newly awakened Buddha to teach.
paṭiccasamuppāda — “specific conditionality, dependent origination”: one of the two aspects of the realization the Buddha names as especially difficult for people to grasp.
apparajakkha — “little dust in their eyes”: the discourse’s image for beings whose habitual clinging is thin enough that a clear teaching can actually reach them.
the lotus pond — the sutta’s central simile: lotuses submerged, at the surface, and risen clear above the water, describing beings at different stages of readiness for awakening.
āyācana — “appeal” or “request,” the discourse’s title, referring to Sahampati’s formal request that the Buddha teach.

The text

The discourse in full: the Buddha’s private hesitation and its verses, Sahampati’s appeal and its verses, the survey of the lotus pond, and the Buddha’s verse of reply. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A private hesitation

§1So I have heard. At one time, when he was first awakened, the Buddha was staying in Uruvelā at the root of the goatherd’s banyan tree on the bank of the Nerañjarā River. Then as he was in private retreat this thought came to his mind, “This principle I have discovered is deep, hard to see, hard to understand, peaceful, sublime, beyond the scope of logic, subtle, comprehensible to the astute. But people like clinging, they love it and enjoy it. It’s hard for them to see this topic; that is, specific conditionality, dependent origination. It’s also hard for them to see this topic; that is, the stilling of all activities, the letting go of all attachments, the ending of craving, fading away, cessation, extinguishment. And if I were to teach this principle, others might not understand me, which would be wearying and troublesome for me.” And then these verses, which were neither supernaturally inspired, nor learned before in the past, occurred to the Buddha:

§2–3“I’ve struggled hard to realize this,
enough with trying to explain it!
Those mired in greed and hate
can’t really understand this teaching.

It goes against the stream, subtle,
deep, obscure, and very fine.
Those besotted by greed cannot see,
for they’re cloaked in a mass of darkness.”

§4And as the Buddha reflected like this, his mind inclined to remaining passive, not to teaching the Dhamma.

The divinity’s alarm and appeal

§5Then, knowing the Buddha’s train of thought, it occurred to the divinity Sahampati, “Oh lord! The world will be lost, the world will perish! For the mind of the Realized One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha, inclines to remaining passive, not to teaching the Dhamma.” Then, as easily as a strong person would extend or contract their arm, he vanished from the realm of divinity and reappeared in front of the Buddha. He arranged his robe over one shoulder, knelt with his right knee on the ground, raised his cupped palms toward the Buddha, and said: “Sir, let the Blessed One teach the Dhamma! Let the Holy One teach the Dhamma! There are beings with little dust in their eyes. They’re in decline because they haven’t heard the teaching. There will be those who understand the teaching!” This is what the divinity Sahampati said. Then he went on to say:

§6–8“Among the Magadhans there appeared in the past
an impure teaching thought up by the stained.
Fling open the door to freedom from death!
Let them hear the teaching the immaculate one discovered.

Standing high on a rocky mountain,
you can see the people all around.
In just the same way, All-seer, so intelligent,
having ascended the Temple of Truth,
rid of sorrow, look upon the people
swamped with sorrow, oppressed by rebirth and old age.

Rise, hero! Victor in battle, caravan leader,
wander the world cleared of debt.
Let the Blessed One teach the Dhamma!
There will be those who understand!”

The survey of the pond

§9Then the Buddha, understanding the Divinity’s invitation, surveyed the world with the eye of a Buddha, out of his compassion for sentient beings. And the Buddha saw sentient beings with little dust in their eyes, and some with much dust in their eyes; with keen faculties and with weak faculties, with good qualities and with bad qualities, easy to teach and hard to teach. And some of them lived seeing the danger in the fault to do with the next world, while others did not. It’s like a pool with blue water lilies, or pink or white lotuses. Some of them sprout and grow in the water without rising above it, thriving underwater. Some of them sprout and grow in the water reaching the water’s surface. And some of them sprout and grow in the water but rise up above the water and stand with no water clinging to them. In the same way, the Buddha saw sentient beings with little dust in their eyes, and some with much dust in their eyes; with keen faculties and with weak faculties, with good qualities and with bad qualities, easy to teach and hard to teach. And some of them lived seeing the danger in the fault to do with the next world, while others did not. When he had seen this he replied in verse to the divinity Sahampati:

§10“Flung open are the doors to freedom from death!
Let those with ears to hear commit to faith.
Thinking it would be troublesome, Divinity,
I did not teach the sophisticated, sublime Dhamma among mankind.”

§11Then the divinity Sahampati, knowing that his request for the Buddha to teach the Dhamma had been granted, bowed and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on his right, before vanishing right there.

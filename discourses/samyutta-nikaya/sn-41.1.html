Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 41.1

The Fetter

Saṁyojanasutta

Setting
Near Macchikāsaṇḍa, in the Wild Mango Grove, after a meal offered by the householder Citta
Speakers
The householder Citta and the senior mendicant Isidatta, answering on behalf of a group of senior mendicants
Form
An overheard doctrinal dispute among mendicants, resolved by a lay follower's precise question and a monk's simile
Length
~4 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Saṃyukta-āgama (T99), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the question sounds like a technicality at first, but the distinction it draws is genuinely subtle and easy to get backwards

Why this discourse

Is the eye itself what binds a person to craving — or is something else doing the binding, using the eye and its objects as its occasion? This discourse opens the Citta-saṃyutta, a short section of the collection built around one of the tradition’s most celebrated lay disciples, and it earns its place through the precision of a single distinction: senior mendicants themselves are divided on the answer, and it takes a householder’s carefully chosen simile — two oxen yoked by a single harness — to settle it cleanly. The discourse rewards close attention because the wrong answer is intuitive and easy to fall into, while the right one has real consequences for how a person actually practices: blaming the senses themselves, versus locating the problem precisely in what arises when desire meets an object.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Neither the eye nor sights — nor any sense faculty and its corresponding object — is itself “the fetter” that binds a person; the fetter is the desire and greed that arises dependent on the two occurring together, exactly as neither of two oxen yoked side by side is the yoke that binds the other.

An overheard disagreement

The scene is unusually detailed for such a short discourse. Several senior mendicants are staying near Macchikāsaṇḍa in the Wild Mango Grove, and after their meal they sit together and fall into a genuine doctrinal disagreement: is “the fetter” the same thing as “the things that tighten fetters,” differing only in wording — or are the two actually different in meaning as well as phrasing? The text records that the group splits, some holding one view and some the other, without resolving it themselves. It is a small, human detail: these are experienced, senior monastics, and they simply disagree, openly, in a way the text does not try to smooth over.

A householder steps in

The householder Citta, visiting Migapathaka on business, hears about the unresolved discussion and comes to the mendicants directly, asking them to settle it for him. What follows is one of this collection’s more striking role reversals: the person answering on behalf of the entire group of senior monastics is Isidatta — and the text specifically notes that Isidatta was ordained more recently than the other mendicants present. Whatever seniority ordinarily confers in status or the expectation of being deferred to, insight into this specific question apparently belonged to the newer monk, and the others let him answer for them.

The black ox and the white ox

Isidatta’s answer sides firmly with one half of the original dispute: the fetter and the things that tighten fetters are “different in both meaning and phrasing.” Rather than simply asserting this, he reaches for a simile precisely because, as he says, “by means of a simile some sensible people understand the meaning of what is said” — a small aside worth noticing for what it implies about how teaching is meant to work in this tradition, matching argument to the way a particular mind actually grasps things. The simile itself is homely and immediate: a black ox and a white ox, yoked together by a single harness. Would it be right to say the black ox is the yoke of the white ox, or the white ox the yoke of the black? Citta’s fellow mendicants answer correctly on their own: no — the yoke is neither ox, but the single harness that joins them.

The simile, applied directly. “The eye is not the fetter of sights, nor are sights the fetter of the eye. The fetter there is the desire and greed that arises from the pair of them.”

Why this distinction actually matters

It would be easy to treat this as a scholastic hair-splitting exercise, but the practical stakes are real. If the eye itself, or sights themselves, were the fetter, then freedom would seem to require eliminating the senses or their objects — an impossible and mistaken goal, since a person cannot simply stop having eyes or stop sights from existing. Locating the fetter instead in “the desire and greed that arises from the pair of them” identifies something that actually can be worked with: not the presence of a sense faculty and its object, which is simply the condition for ordinary experience, but the reactive desire and greed that may or may not arise when they meet. The simile is repeated identically across all six sense fields — ear and sounds, nose and smells, tongue and flavors, body and touches, mind and ideas — making clear this is a general principle about the entire structure of sense experience, not a special case involving sight alone.

A monk who quietly leaves

Citta responds with evident delight, praising the answer and offering Isidatta ongoing material support — robes, alms food, lodging, and medicine. The discourse closes on a small, human note that is easy to miss: uncomfortable, it seems, at having become known and singled out for praise as the one who gave this precise answer, Isidatta gets up from his seat and leaves the area, and does not return. The text does not moralize about this departure, but it sits naturally alongside the discourse’s other quiet reversal of expectation — the least senior monk giving the most decisive answer — as a small study in monastic humility, someone visibly avoiding being singled out and praised for wisdom that was not, in his own view, a matter of personal credit.

Key terms

saṃyojana — “fetter”: what actually binds a being to continued existence; the discourse's whole question is whether this is the sense faculty and object themselves, or the desire and greed arising between them.
saṃyojaniyā dhammā — “things that tighten fetters”: the phrase the senior mendicants initially disagree about, debating whether it differs from “the fetter” in meaning or only in wording.
chandarāga — “desire and greed”: identified here as the actual fetter, arising dependent on a sense faculty and its object occurring together, rather than being either of those two things itself.
Citta the householder — a lay disciple later declared elsewhere by the Buddha to be foremost among lay male disciples in teaching the Dhamma; this discourse opens the short section of the collection built around his exchanges with various mendicants.
Isidatta — the senior mendicant who answers on the group's behalf, notable for having been ordained more recently than the others present, and for leaving quietly afterward rather than accept ongoing recognition.

The text

The discourse in full: the mendicants’ unresolved dispute, Citta’s question, Isidatta’s answer built around the simile of the two yoked oxen, and Citta’s response. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

An unresolved dispute among mendicants

§1At one time several senior mendicants were staying near Macchikāsaṇḍa in the Wild Mango Grove. Now at that time, after the meal, on return from almsround, several senior mendicants sat together in the pavilion and this discussion came up among them: “Reverends, the ‘fetter’ and the ‘things that tighten fetters’: do these things differ in both meaning and phrasing? Or do they mean the same thing, and differ only in the phrasing?” Some senior mendicants answered like this: “Reverends, the ‘fetter’ and the ‘things that tighten fetters’: these things differ in both meaning and phrasing.” But some senior mendicants answered like this: “Reverends, the ‘fetter’ and the ‘things that tighten fetters’ mean the same thing; they differ only in the phrasing.”

Citta the householder arrives

§2Now at that time the householder Citta had arrived at Migapathaka on some business. He heard about what those senior mendicants were discussing. So he went up to them, bowed, sat down to one side, and said to them, “Sirs, I heard that you were discussing whether the ‘fetter’ and the ‘things that tighten fetters’ differ in both meaning and phrasing, or whether they mean the same thing, and differ only in the phrasing.” “That’s right, householder.”

The simile of the two oxen

§3“Sirs, the ‘fetter’ and the ‘things that tighten fetters’: these things differ in both meaning and phrasing. Well then, sirs, I shall give you a simile. For by means of a simile some sensible people understand the meaning of what is said. Suppose there was a black ox and a white ox yoked by a single harness or yoke. Would it be right to say that the black ox is the yoke of the white ox, or the white ox is the yoke of the black ox?” “No, householder. The black ox is not the yoke of the white ox, nor is the white ox the yoke of the black ox. The yoke there is the single harness or yoke that they’re yoked by.”

§4“In the same way, the eye is not the fetter of sights, nor are sights the fetter of the eye. The fetter there is the desire and greed that arises from the pair of them. The ear … nose … tongue … body … mind is not the fetter of ideas, nor are ideas the fetter of the mind. The fetter there is the desire and greed that arises from the pair of them.”

Citta's response

§5“You’re fortunate, householder, so very fortunate, to traverse the Buddha’s deep teachings with the eye of wisdom.”

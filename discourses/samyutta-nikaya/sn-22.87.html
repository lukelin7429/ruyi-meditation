Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 22.87

With Vakkali

Vakkalisutta

Setting
Near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove; then at a potter’s shed, and finally at the Black Rock on the slopes of Isigili
Speakers
The Buddha and the gravely ill Venerable Vakkali, with two unnamed deities and several attending mendicants
Form
A bedside visit and teaching, followed by a narrative of Vakkali’s final hours, his death, and the Buddha’s response
Length
~9 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Saṃyukta-āgama (T99), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★★★☆ — doctrinally familiar but emotionally demanding; its closing account calls for slow, careful, non-glib reading

Why this discourse

A dying monk wants nothing more than to see the Buddha with his own eyes before the end, and what he gets instead is one of the sharpest redirections in the canon: “What use is there in seeing this filthy body? Whoever sees the Dhamma sees me.” That single reversal — from physical presence to understanding — would already make this a landmark text. But the discourse does not stop there. It follows Vakkali through his final hours, including an ending that has been discussed and argued over by scholars for exactly the reason it should be read carefully rather than quickly: what actually happens, what the Buddha says about it afterward, and what that says about how this tradition thinks about a mind at the very end of its capacity to bear pain.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Dying and in severe pain, the mendicant Vakkali longs simply to see the Buddha in person; the Buddha instead redirects him — “whoever sees the Dhamma sees me” — walks him again through the impermanence and not-self of the five aggregates, and, after Vakkali’s death, declares without reservation that he died well and reached full liberation.

A monk too weak to visit

Vakkali is “sick, suffering, gravely ill,” staying in a potter’s shed near Rājagaha. Rather than ask the Buddha to heal him, he sends word through his carers asking the Buddha to visit “out of sympathy.” When the Buddha arrives and asks how he is faring, Vakkali is unflinchingly honest: his pain is “terrible and growing, not fading.” Asked directly whether he has any remorse or self-blame regarding his conduct, he says he has none on that count — his only regret, he admits, is different: “For a long time I’ve wanted to go and see the Buddha, but I was physically too weak.” It is a small, deeply human confession, and it is exactly what the Buddha responds to next.

“What use is there in seeing this filthy body?”

The Buddha’s reply does not console the wish; it reframes it entirely:

The redirection. “Enough, Vakkali! Why would you want to see this rotting body? One who sees the teaching sees me. One who sees me sees the teaching.”

This is not a rebuke of affection so much as a correction of where that affection is pointed. The Buddha as a physical presence — ageing, embodied, eventually to die like anyone else — was never the actual point of contact the teaching offers; what endures, and what can actually be “seen” by anyone regardless of proximity, is the Dhamma itself. For a dying man who can no longer travel to be physically near his teacher, this is not a consolation prize. It is the whole of what was ever really on offer, made explicit at the moment it matters most.

The teaching itself: the five aggregates again

Having reoriented Vakkali’s attention, the Buddha gives him the identical analysis found in the discourse to the five ascetics: is form permanent or impermanent? Impermanent. Suffering or happiness, given that it is impermanent? Suffering. Fit, then, to be regarded as “this is mine, I am this, this is my self”? No. The same three-step sequence runs through feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness, and the same totalizing instruction follows — to see “any kind” of each aggregate, past, future, or present, internal or external, coarse or subtle, inferior or superior, far or near, as not self. Vakkali does not need this explained to him as new information; he already understands it. What the visit does is apply a teaching he has presumably heard many times before to the one moment where its truth is not abstract at all — his own body, failing in front of him.

Moving toward the end

After the Buddha leaves for Vulture’s Peak, Vakkali asks his carers to move him to the Black Rock on the slopes of Isigili — explicitly so as not to die “in an inhabited area.” That night, two radiant deities appear to the Buddha and report that “the mendicant Vakkali is intent on liberation” and will “definitely be well freed.” The next morning the Buddha sends mendicants to relay this to Vakkali directly, along with his own reassurance: “Do not fear, Vakkali, do not fear! Your death will not be a bad one; your passing will not be a bad one.” This message — delivered before anything else happens — matters for how the ending that follows should be read: whatever comes next, the Buddha has already, independently, affirmed that Vakkali’s death carries no blame.

The ending, read carefully

Vakkali asks to be helped off his cot rather than receive the Buddha’s message from a “high seat,” then dictates a final reply for the mendicants to carry back — confirming, aggregate by aggregate, that he has “no doubt” that form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness are impermanent, that what is impermanent is suffering, and that he holds “no desire or greed or fondness” for any of it. Shortly after the mendicants leave, “Venerable Vakkali committed suicide.” The text states this plainly, without commentary at the moment it happens. What the Buddha says afterward is what gives the episode its meaning within the tradition, and it deserves the same care scholars have given a closely related case, the death of the monk Channa (see MN 144, already covered on this site): rather than reading the act in isolation, the discourse frames it through the deities’ report of Vakkali’s liberated intent, the Buddha’s own prior reassurance that this death would not be “a bad one,” and his declaration afterward, discussed below, that Vakkali reached the end fully free. Interpreters differ on how much weight to place on each of these elements, and this reading guide does not claim to resolve that discussion — only to present the sequence in the order the text itself gives it.

“Vakkali is quenched”

The Buddha travels to the Black Rock with several mendicants and sees Vakkali’s body from a distance, described starkly as “lying with aggregates cast off.” A cloud of smoke or darkness is seen moving in every direction, and the Buddha identifies it: “That’s Māra the Wicked searching for Vakkali’s consciousness, wondering: ‘Where is Vakkali’s consciousness established?’” The discourse’s final line answers Māra’s search directly: “since his consciousness is not established, Vakkali is quenched.” “Quenched” here renders parinibbuto — the state of one who has attained final liberation, with nothing left for consciousness to fasten onto anywhere, ever again. It is the discourse’s last word on Vakkali, and it is unambiguous: not merely undisturbed, not merely at peace, but fully and finally free.

Key terms

“whoever sees the Dhamma sees me” — the Buddha’s central redirection to Vakkali, repeated in both directions (seeing the Dhamma / seeing the Buddha), reorienting devotion away from physical proximity.
the five aggregates — form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness; the same impermanence-suffering-not-self analysis given to the five ascetics in SN 22.59 is repeated here for Vakkali directly.
Isigili — a hill near Rājagaha; the Black Rock on its slopes is where Vakkali asks to be moved and where he dies.
apāpakaṃ maraṇaṃ — “a death that is not bad”: the Buddha’s reassurance to Vakkali, relayed through the mendicants before his death, that his passing carries no blame.
parinibbuto — “quenched,” fully and finally liberated; the Buddha’s closing declaration about Vakkali, made after locating the true absence of any place for Māra to find his consciousness.

The text

The discourse in full: Vakkali’s illness and the Buddha’s visit, the redirection and the five-aggregate teaching, the deities’ report, the final message, Vakkali’s death, and the Buddha’s closing declaration. Where the source elides parallel repetitions across the five aggregates, or a mendicant's exact repetition of an already-quoted message, this is reconstructed in full using the wording given earlier in the text; where the source itself compresses a repeated message into a single summarizing sentence (as Bhikkhu Sujato’s translation does at several points below), that same summarizing sentence is kept as given, without further invention. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A dying monk's request

§1At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground. Now at that time Venerable Vakkali was staying in a potter’s shed, and he was sick, suffering, gravely ill. Then he addressed his carers, “Please, reverends, go to the Buddha, and in my name bow with your head to his feet. Say to him: ‘Sir, the mendicant Vakkali is sick, suffering, and gravely ill. He bows with his head to your feet.’ And then say: ‘Sir, please go to the mendicant Vakkali out of sympathy.’” “Yes, reverend,” those monks replied. They did as he asked. The Buddha consented with silence.

The Buddha's visit

§2Then the Buddha robed up and, taking his bowl and robes, went to Venerable Vakkali. Venerable Vakkali saw the Buddha coming off in the distance and stirred in his cot. But the Buddha said to him, “It’s all right, Vakkali, don’t get up. There are some seats spread out, I will sit there.” He sat on the seat spread out and said to Vakkali, “I hope you’re keeping well, Vakkali; I hope you’re all right. And I hope the pain is fading, not growing, that its fading is evident, not its growing.” “Sir, I’m not all right, I’m not getting by. My pain is terrible and growing, not fading; its growing is apparent, not its fading.” “I hope you don’t have any remorse or regret?” “Indeed, sir, I have no little remorse and regret.” “I hope you have no reason to blame yourself when it comes to ethical conduct?” “No sir, I have no reason to blame myself when it comes to ethical conduct.” “In that case, Vakkali, why do you have remorse and regret?” “For a long time I’ve wanted to go and see the Buddha, but I was physically too weak.”

“What use is there in seeing this filthy body?”

§3“Enough, Vakkali! Why would you want to see this rotting body? One who sees the teaching sees me. One who sees me sees the teaching. Seeing the teaching, you see me. Seeing me, you see the teaching.

The five aggregates, once more

§4What do you think, Vakkali? Is form permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.”

“Is feeling permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.”

“Is perception permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.”

“Are choices permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if they’re impermanent, are they suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if they’re impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.”

“Is consciousness permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.”

“So you should truly see any kind of form at all — past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near: all form — with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’ Any kind of feeling at all… any kind of perception at all… any kind of choices at all… any kind of consciousness at all — past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near: all consciousness — should be truly seen with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’

Seeing this, a learned noble disciple grows disillusioned with form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness. Being disillusioned, desire fades away. When desire fades away they’re freed. When they’re freed, they know they’re freed. They understand: ‘Rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey has been completed, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this place.’”

Moving to the Black Rock

§5And then, after giving Venerable Vakkali this advice, the Buddha rose from his seat and went to the Vulture’s Peak Mountain. Then Venerable Vakkali addressed his carers, “Come on, reverends, lift my cot and take me to the Black Rock on the slopes of Isigili. For how on earth could one such as I imagine dying in an inhabited area!” “Yes, reverend,” replied those mendicants, and did as he asked.

Meanwhile, the Buddha spent the rest of that night and day on Vulture’s Peak Mountain. Then, late at night, two glorious deities, lighting up the entire Vulture’s Peak, went up to the Buddha, bowed, and stood to one side. One deity said to him, “Sir, the mendicant Vakkali is intent on liberation!” And another deity said to him, “He’ll definitely be well freed!” This is what those deities said. Then they bowed and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on their right side, before vanishing right there.

“Do not fear, Vakkali”

§6Then, when the night had passed, the Buddha addressed the mendicants: “Come, mendicants, go to the mendicant Vakkali and tell him:

§7‘Vakkali, hear the word of the Buddha and two deities. Late last night, two glorious deities, lighting up the entire Vulture’s Peak, went up to the Buddha, bowed, and stood to one side. One deity said to him, “Sir, the mendicant Vakkali is intent on liberation!” The other deity said to him, “He’ll definitely be well freed!” And the Buddha said, “Do not fear, Vakkali, do not fear! Your death will not be a bad one; your passing will not be a bad one.”’” “Yes, sir,” those monks replied. They went to Vakkali and said to him: “Vakkali, hear the word of the Buddha and two deities.”

A final message

§8Then Vakkali addressed his carers, “Please, reverends, help me off my cot. For how on earth could one such as I presume to listen to the Buddha’s instructions sitting on a high seat?” “Yes, reverend,” replied those mendicants, and helped him off his cot. They repeated what the Buddha had said. Vakkali said, “Well then, reverends, in my name bow with your head at the Buddha’s feet. Say to him: ‘Sir, the mendicant Vakkali is sick, suffering, gravely ill. He bows with his head to your feet.’ And then say: ‘Form is impermanent. I have no doubt of that. I’m certain that what is impermanent is suffering. And I’m certain that I have no desire or greed or fondness for what is impermanent, suffering, and perishable.

Feeling is impermanent. I have no doubt of that. I’m certain that what is impermanent is suffering. And I’m certain that I have no desire or greed or fondness for what is impermanent, suffering, and perishable.

Perception is impermanent. I have no doubt of that. I’m certain that what is impermanent is suffering. And I’m certain that I have no desire or greed or fondness for what is impermanent, suffering, and perishable.

Choices are impermanent. I have no doubt of that. I’m certain that what is impermanent is suffering. And I’m certain that I have no desire or greed or fondness for what is impermanent, suffering, and perishable.

Consciousness is impermanent. I have no doubt of that. I’m certain that what is impermanent is suffering. And I’m certain that I have no desire or greed or fondness for what is impermanent, suffering, and perishable.’”

“Yes, reverend,” those monks replied, and left. And then, not long after those mendicants had left, Venerable Vakkali committed suicide.

“Vakkali is quenched”

§9–10Then those mendicants went up to the Buddha and told him Vakkali’s message. Then the Buddha said to the mendicants, “Come, mendicants, let’s go to the Black Rock on the slopes of Isigili, where Vakkali, the gentleman, committed suicide.” “Yes, sir,” they replied. Then the Buddha together with several mendicants went to the Black Rock on the slopes of Isigili. The Buddha saw Vakkali off in the distance on his cot, lying with aggregates cast off.

§11Now at that time a cloud of black smoke was moving east, west, north, south, above, below, and in-between. The Buddha said to the mendicants, “Mendicants, do you see that cloud of black smoke moving east, west, north, south, above, below, and in-between?” “Yes, sir.” “That’s Māra the Wicked searching for Vakkali’s consciousness, wondering: ‘Where is Vakkali’s consciousness established?’ But since his consciousness is not established, Vakkali is quenched.”

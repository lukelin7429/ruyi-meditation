Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 22.59

The Characteristic of Not-Self

Anattalakkhaṇasutta

Setting
Near Varanasi, in the deer park at Isipatana — the same place as the first sermon
Speakers
The Buddha, addressing the group of five mendicants (pañcavaggiya) directly
Form
A methodical proof applied to each of the five aggregates in turn, followed by a guided series of yes/no questions and a closing formula
Length
~6 minutes to read
Northern parallel
Exceptionally well attested across the tradition, with wide currency in Chinese Āgama material and beyond; this reading guide does not attempt to pin an exact matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the logic is precise and repays close attention, though the repeated structure across five aggregates makes it easy to follow once grasped

Why this discourse

Tradition holds that this is the Buddha’s second formal discourse, delivered at the same deer park at Isipatana to the same five mendicants who had just heard the first sermon on the four noble truths. Where that first teaching laid out the diagnosis and the path, this one goes straight for the deepest habit blocking anyone from acting on it — the assumption that some part of experience is, at bottom, really “me.” The Buddha doesn’t simply assert that this assumption is false; he tests it, aggregate by aggregate, with a plain logical criterion anyone can apply for themselves. The discourse closes with a detail found nowhere else quite like this: while it was being spoken, all five listeners became fully liberated arahants, the first anywhere besides the Buddha himself — making this, by the tradition’s own account, the moment the Buddhist community of awakened beings actually began.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

None of the five aggregates — form, feeling, perception, choices, or consciousness — can be self, because a genuine self could be commanded at will and would never lead to affliction, yet every aggregate does lead to affliction and none obeys the will; seeing this clearly, all five mendicants present are freed on the spot.

The same place, the same audience, a second teaching

The setting is deliberately unchanged: “near Varanasi, in the deer park at Isipatana,” where the Buddha had recently given his first sermon to this same “group of five mendicants” (pañcavaggiya) — the ascetics who had practiced alongside him before his awakening and who, according to the tradition, were the first to hear the Dhamma from him afterward. That first discourse is said to have brought one of the five, Koṇḍañña, to the initial breakthrough of stream-entry. This second discourse is addressed to all five together, and it is aimed at something more final than a first breakthrough: full liberation.

The logical test

The Buddha’s method here is exact and repeatable. For each aggregate in turn, he gives the same argument: “If [this aggregate] were self, it wouldn’t lead to affliction. And you could compel [it]: ‘May my [aggregate] be like this! May it not be like that!’ But because [it] is not-self, it leads to affliction. And you can’t compel [it]…” The test has two independent prongs, and either one alone would be enough: a genuine self should be able to be commanded (form obviously cannot be reshaped by wish alone, nor can feeling, perception, choices, or consciousness), and a genuine self should not be a source of affliction (yet all five aggregates plainly are). Applied to form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness in turn, the same double failure appears every time.

From not-self to impermanent, suffering, and not fit to call “mine”

Having established not-self for each aggregate, the Buddha shifts to a second, closely related line of questioning, this time asking the mendicants directly and letting them answer: “Is form permanent or impermanent?” — “Impermanent, sir.” — “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” — “Suffering, sir.” — “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” — “No, sir.” This three-step chain — impermanent, therefore suffering, therefore not fit to be claimed as self — is repeated for all five aggregates, and it does real work: it connects the abstract argument about compellability to something each mendicant can verify directly in their own experience, since impermanence is not a doctrine to be taken on faith but something observable in any moment of attention.

Every form there has ever been, or will be

The Buddha then widens the frame as far as it can go, instructing the mendicants to see “any kind of form at all” — “past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near” — with the same verdict: “This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.” The same totalizing sweep is applied to feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness. Nothing is left out or treated as a special exception; the point is not to identify one particular troublesome experience and set it aside, but to see that not one instance of any aggregate, in any form, at any time, anywhere, qualifies as self.

The outcome: the first five arahants

Seeing this, “a learned noble disciple grows disillusioned” with all five aggregates; “being disillusioned, desire fades away,” and with the fading of desire comes freedom, along with the knowledge of having been freed — expressed in the standard closing formula, “rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey has been completed, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this place.” The discourse then adds a detail that marks it as historically distinctive even within the canon: “while this discourse was being spoken, the minds of the group of five mendicants were freed from defilements by not grasping.” Not partial insight, but complete liberation, arriving for all five together, in real time, during the teaching itself. By the tradition’s own reckoning, this is the moment the first arahants apart from the Buddha came into being — the point at which the community of awakened disciples, and not merely the teaching, began to exist in the world.

Key terms

anattā — “not-self”: the discourse’s central claim, argued individually for each of the five aggregates using the twin criteria of affliction and compellability.
pañcavaggiya — “the group of five,” the five ascetics who had practiced with the Buddha-to-be before his awakening and became his first audience, here becoming his first arahant disciples.
the five aggregates — form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness (rūpa, vedanā, saññā, saṅkhāra, viññāṇa): the five components analyzed identically, one after another, throughout the discourse.
“this is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self” — the formula applied to every kind of each aggregate, past, future, or present, internal or external, coarse or subtle, inferior or superior, far or near.
anupādāya āsavehi cittāni vimuccimsu — “their minds were freed from defilements by not grasping”: the discourse’s closing line, recording that all five mendicants became arahants while the teaching itself was still being given.

The text

The discourse in full: the not-self argument for each of the five aggregates, the guided impermanence-suffering-not-self questioning, the totalizing “any kind at all” instruction, and the closing account of liberation. Where the source elides the parallel treatment of feeling, perception, and choices as exact repetitions of the pattern given for form and consciousness, it is reconstructed here in full using the same wording. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

Setting: the same five mendicants

§1At one time the Buddha was staying near Varanasi, in the deer park at Isipatana. There the Buddha addressed the group of five mendicants: “Mendicants!” “Venerable sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

None of the five aggregates is self

§2“Mendicants, form is not-self. For if form were self, it wouldn’t lead to affliction. And you could compel form: ‘May my form be like this! May it not be like that!’ But because form is not-self, it leads to affliction. And you can’t compel form: ‘May my form be like this! May it not be like that!’

§3Feeling is not-self. For if feeling were self, it wouldn’t lead to affliction. And you could compel feeling: ‘May my feeling be like this! May it not be like that!’ But because feeling is not-self, it leads to affliction. And you can’t compel feeling: ‘May my feeling be like this! May it not be like that!’

§4Perception is not-self. For if perception were self, it wouldn’t lead to affliction. And you could compel perception: ‘May my perception be like this! May it not be like that!’ But because perception is not-self, it leads to affliction. And you can’t compel perception: ‘May my perception be like this! May it not be like that!’

Choices are not-self. For if choices were self, they wouldn’t lead to affliction. And you could compel choices: ‘May my choices be like this! May they not be like that!’ But because choices are not-self, they lead to affliction. And you can’t compel choices: ‘May my choices be like this! May they not be like that!’

§5Consciousness is not-self. For if consciousness were self, it wouldn’t lead to affliction. And you could compel consciousness: ‘May my consciousness be like this! May it not be like that!’ But because consciousness is not-self, it leads to affliction. And you can’t compel consciousness: ‘May my consciousness be like this! May it not be like that!’

Impermanent, therefore suffering, therefore not fit to call “self”

§6What do you think, mendicants? Is form permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.”

“Is feeling permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.”

“Is perception permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.”

“Are choices permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if they’re impermanent, are they suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if they’re impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.”

“Is consciousness permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, sir.” “But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?” “No, sir.”

Every kind, at every time, everywhere

§7So you should truly see any kind of form at all — past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near: all form — with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’

§8Any kind of feeling at all — past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near: all feeling — should be truly seen with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’

§9Any kind of perception at all — past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near: all perception — should be truly seen with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’

Any kind of choices at all — past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near: all choices — should be truly seen with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’

§10You should truly see any kind of consciousness at all — past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near: all consciousness — with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’

Freedom, on the spot

§11Seeing this, a learned noble disciple grows disillusioned with form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness. Being disillusioned, desire fades away. When desire fades away they’re freed. When they’re freed, they know they’re freed. They understand: ‘Rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey has been completed, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this place.’”

§12That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the group of five mendicants approved what the Buddha said. And while this discourse was being spoken, the minds of the group of five mendicants were freed from defilements by not grasping.

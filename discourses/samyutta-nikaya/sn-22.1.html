Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 22.1

Nakula’s Father

Nakulapitusutta

Setting
In the land of the Bhaggas, at Crocodile’s Bellow, in the deer park at Bhesakaḷā’s Wood
Speakers
The householder Nakula’s father and the Buddha, followed by a second conversation between Nakula’s father and Venerable Sāriputta
Form
A brief personal request answered with one celebrated line, then unpacked at length through the five aggregates in a separate exchange
Length
~7 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Saṃyukta-āgama (T99), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — the opening exchange is simple and direct; Sāriputta’s aggregate analysis asks for careful, unhurried reading

Why this discourse

This is the source of one of the most quietly practical lines in the entire canon — “though my body is ailing, my mind will be healthy” — delivered not as abstract philosophy but as a direct answer to an old man’s plain request for help facing his own decline. What makes the discourse more than a single memorable sentence is what happens next: the same householder walks straight from the Buddha to Venerable Sāriputta and asks him to explain it properly, and Sāriputta obliges with one of the clearest analyses anywhere in the suttas of exactly how a mind stays unshaken while a body does not. Few passages address illness and aging as directly, or offer as workable an account of what “a healthy mind” could actually mean in the middle of physical decline.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

An old, ailing householder asks the Buddha for a teaching to carry into his declining years and receives a single line to train by — “though my body is ailing, my mind will be healthy” — which Venerable Sāriputta then explains precisely: an ordinary, unlearned mind identifies with the five aggregates and so is shaken whenever they change, while a trained mind sees them clearly enough that the body can decline without dragging the mind down with it.

An old man’s honest request

The householder Nakula’s father approaches the Buddha and describes his condition without any softening: “I’m an old man, elderly and senior. I’m advanced in years and have reached the final stage of life. My body is ailing and I’m constantly unwell.” He adds, almost in passing, that he rarely gets to see the mendicants anymore — a small, human detail suggesting that age and illness have narrowed his world along with his health. His request is not for a cure or for comfort; it is for “advice” and “instruction” that will serve his “lasting welfare and happiness,” which is to say, something durable enough to actually carry into whatever time remains.

The line itself

The Buddha’s reply opens with an unusually warm double affirmation — “That’s so true, householder! That’s so true, householder!” — before stating what amounts to a plain fact about embodiment: “this body is ailing, swaddled in its shell,” and anyone claiming to be healthy “even for an hour” while dragging this body around is simply being foolish. The training the Buddha then gives is short enough to memorize on the spot:

The line. “Though my body is ailing, my mind will be healthy.”

Notice what this does and doesn’t claim. It doesn’t promise that the body can be willed into wellness, or that illness is somehow unreal or unimportant. It draws a line between two things ordinarily fused together — physical condition and mental state — and insists that the second doesn’t have to track the first as closely as habit assumes.

A second conversation, and a direct question

Satisfied, Nakula’s father leaves the Buddha and, on his way, stops to see Venerable Sāriputta, who notices at once how clear and bright his features look and asks whether he received a Dhamma talk that day. Nakula’s father calls it being “anointed with the deathless ambrosia of a Dhamma talk” and recounts the whole exchange. Sāriputta then asks the question that gives the rest of the discourse its shape: didn’t it occur to him to ask the Buddha how, exactly, one defines being “ailing in body and ailing in mind” versus being “ailing in body and healthy in mind”? Nakula’s father, disarmingly, says he’d rather hear the explanation from Sāriputta himself — and gets it.

What actually makes the mind “ailing”

Sāriputta’s analysis runs through the five aggregates — form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness — one at a time, using an identical structure for each. An “unlearned ordinary person,” someone untrained in the teaching of the noble ones, “regards form as self, self as having form, form in self, or self in form,” and becomes obsessed with the thought “I am form, form is mine.” Because that aggregate inevitably “decays and perishes,” its owner is left with “sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress” whenever it does. Repeat this for feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness, and the pattern is complete: identification with any changing thing guarantees suffering the moment that thing changes — and every one of the five aggregates changes.

The “learned noble disciple,” by contrast, has trained precisely in not making this identification. The aggregates still decay — nothing here claims otherwise — but because this person never regarded form, feeling, perception, choices, or consciousness as self, self as having them, or self and aggregate as mutually contained, their decay “doesn’t give rise to sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress.” That is the entire difference Sāriputta is describing: not a body that fails to age, but a mind that has stopped staking its own identity on a body, or on any of the other aggregates, that was always going to age.

A distinct sutta from AN 6.16, despite the identical title

Readers of this site may already know a different discourse called “Nakula’s Father” — AN 6.16, set at the very same location, the deer park at Bhesakaḷā’s Wood in the land of the Bhaggas. That is a genuinely separate text, and it is worth being precise about the difference. AN 6.16 is set at Nakula’s father’s sickbed, where his wife, Nakula’s mother, reassures her gravely ill husband by naming and answering six anticipated worries he might carry toward death — concern for her welfare, for their children, and so on — with the Buddha appearing only afterward to confirm her own standing on the path. This discourse, SN 22.1, is a different occasion entirely: Nakula’s father is unwell but not depicted as dying, he approaches the Buddha himself rather than being visited at a sickbed, and the teaching that results — “though my body is ailing, my mind will be healthy,” unpacked through the five aggregates — has nothing to do with reassurance about a spouse’s future. Same householder, same setting, same title in English translation, but two independent discourses addressing two different situations.

Key terms

āturakāyassa me sato cittaṃ anāturaṃ bhavissati — “though my body is ailing, my mind will be healthy”: the Buddha’s core instruction, given directly to Nakula’s father as something to train in.
the five aggregates — form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness (rūpa, vedanā, saññā, saṅkhāra, viññāṇa): the five components Sāriputta analyzes one by one to explain the Buddha’s line in full.
“regards form as self…” — the fourfold pattern of identification (self as form, form as self, form in self, self in form) that defines an unlearned ordinary person’s relationship to each aggregate; its absence defines the learned noble disciple’s.
ailing in body and ailing/healthy in mind — Sāriputta’s own framing of the distinction the whole second half of the discourse exists to explain.
Nakulapitā — “Nakula’s father,” a lay householder who appears elsewhere in the canon (see AN 6.16, a wholly separate discourse) as a long-time, devoted lay disciple together with his wife, Nakula’s mother.

The text

The discourse in full: Nakula’s father’s request and the Buddha’s reply, his retelling of it to Venerable Sāriputta, and Sāriputta’s complete analysis through the five aggregates. Where the source elides Nakula’s father’s retelling of the opening exchange as an exact repetition, it is reconstructed here in full using the same wording given earlier in the text. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

An old man’s request

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the land of the Bhaggas at Crocodile’s Bellow, in the deer park at Bhesakaḷā’s Wood. Then the householder Nakula’s father went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and said to the Buddha:

§2“Sir, I’m an old man, elderly and senior. I’m advanced in years and have reached the final stage of life. My body is ailing and I’m constantly unwell. I hardly ever get to see the esteemed mendicants. May the Buddha please advise me and instruct me. It will be for my lasting welfare and happiness.”

The line itself

§3“That’s so true, householder! That’s so true, householder! For this body is ailing, swaddled in its shell. If anyone dragging around this body claimed to be healthy even for an hour, what is that but foolishness? So you should train like this: ‘Though my body is ailing, my mind will be healthy.’ That’s how you should train.”

A second conversation, with Sāriputta

§4And then the householder Nakula’s father approved and agreed with what the Buddha said. He rose from his seat, bowed, and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on his right. Then he went up to Venerable Sāriputta, bowed, and sat down to one side. Sāriputta said to him: “Householder, your faculties are so very clear, and your complexion is pure and bright. Did you get to hear a Dhamma talk in the Buddha’s presence today?”

§5–6“What else, sir, could it be? Just now the Buddha anointed me with the deathless ambrosia of a Dhamma talk.” “But with what kind of ambrosial Dhamma talk did the Buddha anoint you?” So Nakula’s father told Sāriputta all that had happened, and said: “Sir, I’m an old man, elderly and senior. I’m advanced in years and have reached the final stage of life. My body is ailing and I’m constantly unwell. May the Buddha please advise me and instruct me. It will be for my lasting welfare and happiness. When I said this, sir, the Buddha said to me: ‘That’s so true, householder! That’s so true, householder! For this body is ailing, swaddled in its shell. If anyone dragging around this body claimed to be healthy even for an hour, what is that but foolishness? So you should train like this: “Though my body is ailing, my mind will be healthy.” That’s how you should train.’ That’s the ambrosial Dhamma talk with which I was anointed by the Buddha.”

§7“But didn’t you feel the need to ask the Buddha the further question: ‘Sir, how do you define someone ailing in body and ailing in mind, and someone ailing in body and healthy in mind’?” “Sir, we would travel a long way to learn the meaning of this statement in the presence of Venerable Sāriputta. May Venerable Sāriputta himself please clarify the meaning of this.”

§8“Well then, householder, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, sir,” replied Nakula’s father. Sāriputta said this:

Ailing in body and ailing in mind

§9“And how is one ailing in body and ailing in mind? It’s when an unlearned ordinary person has not seen the noble ones, and is neither skilled nor trained in the qualities of a noble one. They’ve not seen true persons, and are neither skilled nor trained in the qualities of a true person. They regard form as self, self as having form, form in self, or self in form. They’re obsessed with the thought: ‘I am form, form is mine!’ But that form of theirs decays and perishes, which gives rise to sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress.

§10–13They regard feeling as self, self as having feeling, feeling in self, or self in feeling. They’re obsessed with the thought: ‘I am feeling, feeling is mine!’ But that feeling of theirs decays and perishes, which gives rise to sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress. They regard perception as self, self as having perception, perception in self, or self in perception. They’re obsessed with the thought: ‘I am perception, perception is mine!’ But that perception of theirs decays and perishes, which gives rise to sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress. They regard choices as self, self as having choices, choices in self, or self in choices. They’re obsessed with the thought: ‘I am choices, choices are mine!’ But those choices of theirs decay and perish, which gives rise to sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress. They regard consciousness as self, self as having consciousness, consciousness in self, or self in consciousness. They’re obsessed with the thought: ‘I am consciousness, consciousness is mine!’ But that consciousness of theirs decays and perishes, which gives rise to sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress. That’s how one is ailing in body and ailing in mind.

Ailing in body, healthy in mind

§14And how is one ailing in body and healthy in mind? It’s when a learned noble disciple has seen the noble ones, and is skilled and trained in the teaching of the noble ones. They’ve seen true persons, and are skilled and trained in the teaching of the true persons. They don’t regard form as self, self as having form, form in self, or self in form. They’re not obsessed with the thought: ‘I am form, form is mine!’ So when that form of theirs decays and perishes, it doesn’t give rise to sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress.

§15–18They don’t regard feeling as self, self as having feeling, feeling in self, or self in feeling. They’re not obsessed with the thought: ‘I am feeling, feeling is mine!’ So when that feeling of theirs decays and perishes, it doesn’t give rise to sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress. They don’t regard perception as self, self as having perception, perception in self, or self in perception. They’re not obsessed with the thought: ‘I am perception, perception is mine!’ So when that perception of theirs decays and perishes, it doesn’t give rise to sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress. They don’t regard choices as self, self as having choices, choices in self, or self in choices. They’re not obsessed with the thought: ‘I am choices, choices are mine!’ So when those choices of theirs decay and perish, it doesn’t give rise to sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress. They don’t regard consciousness as self, self as having consciousness, consciousness in self, or self in consciousness. They’re not obsessed with the thought: ‘I am consciousness, consciousness is mine!’ So when that consciousness of theirs decays and perishes, it doesn’t give rise to sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress. That’s how an individual is ailing in body and healthy in mind.”

§19That’s what Venerable Sāriputta said. Satisfied, Nakula’s father approved what Sāriputta said.

Saṃyutta Nikāya · Discourse 36.6

An Arrow

Sallasutta

Setting
No location given; addressed directly to the assembled mendicants
Speakers
The Buddha alone
Form
A contrastive analysis in prose, illustrated with the simile of one arrow versus two, closed with a summarizing verse
Length
~4 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A rough counterpart may exist in the Chinese Saṃyukta-āgama (T99), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — the central image is immediate and memorable, though the underlying-tendency analysis behind it repays a second, slower reading

Why this discourse

Pain is unavoidable; suffering, this discourse insists, is partly optional — and the gap between the two is exactly what separates an unlearned ordinary person from a learned noble disciple. Both feel the same physical pain when it arises; there is no difference there. The difference is what happens next, in the small span of time between a painful sensation occurring and a person’s reaction to it. This discourse compresses that difference into an image so vivid it needs no further explanation once heard: being struck by one arrow is bad enough, but reacting to that first arrow with worry, grief, and resistance is like being struck by a second arrow immediately after, so that what began as one wound becomes two. Few images from the early texts have found a more direct afterlife in contemporary conversations about pain, stress, and emotional reactivity.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

An unlearned ordinary person and a learned noble disciple both feel the same painful, pleasant, and neutral bodily sensations, but only the unlearned person compounds physical pain with a second, mental layer of worry, resistance, and lament — like being struck by a second arrow immediately after the first.

A question with no difference — at first

The Buddha opens with what sounds almost like a concession: “An unlearned ordinary person feels pleasant, painful, and neutral feelings. A learned noble disciple also feels pleasant, painful, and neutral feelings.” Bodily sensation itself is not the mark of spiritual attainment or its absence; the noble disciple has not somehow stopped feeling pain. The real question the discourse then poses is sharper and more useful: “What, then, is the difference between a learned noble disciple and an unlearned ordinary person?” Since both feel the same three kinds of feeling, whatever distinguishes them must lie somewhere else — not in the raw sensation, but in what happens to a person once that sensation arises.

The first arrow, and the second

For the unlearned ordinary person, painful physical feeling does not simply pass through and fade; it triggers a cascade — “they sorrow and wail and lament, beating their breast and falling into confusion.” The result, the text says plainly, is that this person experiences two feelings where there might have been one: “physical and mental.” The simile that follows is the discourse’s enduring contribution: “It’s like a person who is struck with an arrow, only to be struck with a second arrow. That person experiences the feeling of two arrows.” The first arrow — the bodily pain itself — is often unavoidable: illness, injury, ordinary physical hardship. The second arrow is self-inflicted, added by the mind’s own reaction to the first, and it is this second wound, not the first, that this discourse holds out real hope of avoiding.

One arrow, or two. The learned noble disciple, touched by painful feeling, “doesn’t sorrow or wail or lament… They experience one feeling: physical, not mental… like a person struck with an arrow, but not struck with a second arrow.”

Three underlying tendencies, traced to their root

The discourse does not stop at the image; it explains, with some precision, what actually happens beneath the surface of the unlearned person’s reaction. Touched by pain, they resist it — and “the underlying tendency for aversion towards painful feeling” is what underlies that resistance. Still seeking relief, they turn toward sensual pleasure, “because an unlearned ordinary person doesn’t understand any escape from painful feeling apart from sensual pleasures” — and this is where “the underlying tendency to greed for pleasant feeling” takes hold. And running underneath both reactions is a third: because this person “doesn’t truly understand feelings’ origin, disappearance, gratification, drawback, and escape,” the “underlying tendency to ignorance” operates with respect to neutral feeling as well. The result is that every kind of feeling, not only the painful kind, gets experienced “attached” — gripped, reacted to, taken personally.

The noble disciple’s alternative is not indifference to pain but a different relationship to it at every one of these same three points: touched by pain, there is no resistance built on an underlying tendency to aversion, because the pain is simply met as it is; there is no reflexive turn to sensual pleasure, because this disciple “understands an escape from painful feeling apart from sensual pleasures”; and because they “truly understand feelings’ origin, disappearance, gratification, drawback, and escape,” no underlying tendency to ignorance operates regarding neutral feeling either. Every kind of feeling is felt “detached” — not suppressed or denied, but no longer fuel for a second arrow.

Not stoic detachment, but clear understanding

It would be easy to misread this teaching as advocating suppression — gritting one’s teeth and simply refusing to feel. The text explicitly rules this out with its opening admission that the noble disciple genuinely feels pleasant, painful, and neutral sensations exactly as anyone does. The difference the discourse identifies is not in the presence or intensity of the first feeling, but in whether a further layer of reactive worry, resistance, and craving gets added on top of it. This is a substantive, testable distinction: the practice implied here is not the elimination of sensation, but the interruption of the automatic chain from sensation to reactive elaboration, made possible by actually understanding where feelings come from, what they offer, what is unsatisfying about them, and how a person can be released from being driven by them.

The closing verses

The discourse closes, as several suttas in this section of the collection do, by restating its prose teaching in verse: “A wise and learned person does not not feel the feeling of pleasure and pain. This is the great difference of skill between the wise one and the ordinary.” The double negative is deliberate — the wise person does feel pleasure and pain, genuinely, but is not disturbed by desirable things nor repelled by the undesirable, having “cleared and ended” both favoring and opposing. The final line describes someone who has “gone beyond rebirth” and “understands rightly” — a fitting close for a teaching that began with the plainest possible observation: everyone feels pain, but not everyone need be struck twice by it.

Key terms

salla — “arrow” or “dart”: the discourse’s central image, giving the sutta its name; the first arrow is unavoidable bodily pain, the second is the mind’s own added reaction to it.
anusaya — “underlying tendency”: a latent disposition (here, toward aversion, greed, or ignorance) that operates beneath a person’s conscious reaction to feeling, and that the noble disciple’s clear understanding leaves no room for.
paṭighānusaya — the underlying tendency toward aversion or resistance, said to underlie the unlearned person’s reaction to painful feeling specifically.
rāgānusaya — the underlying tendency toward greed, said to underlie the unlearned person’s reflexive turn to sensual pleasure as their only known escape from pain.
avijjānusaya — the underlying tendency toward ignorance, said to underlie a failure to truly understand the origin, ending, gratification, drawback, and escape of feeling — including neutral feeling, which is easiest to overlook entirely.

The text

The discourse in full: the opening question, the contrasting portraits of the unlearned ordinary person and the learned noble disciple with the twin arrow similes, the underlying-tendency analysis for each, and the closing verses. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A shared question

§1“Mendicants, an unlearned ordinary person feels pleasant, painful, and neutral feelings. A learned noble disciple also feels pleasant, painful, and neutral feelings. What, then, is the difference between a learned noble disciple and an ordinary unlearned person?” “Our teachings are rooted in the Buddha…”

The unlearned person: two arrows

§2“When an unlearned ordinary person experiences painful physical feelings they sorrow and wail and lament, beating their breast and falling into confusion. They experience two feelings: physical and mental. It’s like a person who is struck with an arrow, only to be struck with a second arrow. That person experiences the feeling of two arrows. In the same way, when an unlearned ordinary person experiences painful physical feelings they sorrow and wail and lament, beating their breast and falling into confusion. They experience two feelings: physical and mental.

§3When they’re touched by painful feeling, they resist it. The underlying tendency for aversion towards painful feeling underlies that. When touched by painful feeling they look forward to enjoying sensual pleasures. Why is that? Because an unlearned ordinary person doesn’t understand any escape from painful feeling apart from sensual pleasures. Since they look forward to enjoying sensual pleasures, the underlying tendency to greed for pleasant feeling underlies that. They don’t truly understand feelings’ origin, disappearance, gratification, drawback, and escape. The underlying tendency to ignorance about neutral feeling underlies that.

§4If they feel a pleasant feeling, they feel it attached. If they feel a painful feeling, they feel it attached. If they feel a neutral feeling, they feel it attached. They’re called an unlearned ordinary person who is attached to rebirth, old age, and death, to sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress. They are attached to suffering, I say.

The learned noble disciple: one arrow

§5When a learned noble disciple experiences painful physical feelings they don’t sorrow or wail or lament, beating their breast and falling into confusion. They experience one feeling: physical, not mental. It’s like a person who is struck with an arrow, but was not struck with a second arrow. That person would experience the feeling of one arrow. In the same way, when a learned noble disciple experiences painful physical feelings they don’t sorrow or wail or lament, beating their breast and falling into confusion. They experience one feeling: physical, not mental.

§6When they’re touched by painful feeling, they don’t resist it. There’s no underlying tendency for aversion towards painful feeling underlying that. When touched by painful feeling they don’t look forward to enjoying sensual pleasures. Why is that? Because a learned noble disciple understands an escape from painful feeling apart from sensual pleasures. Since they don’t look forward to enjoying sensual pleasures, there’s no underlying tendency to greed for pleasant feeling underlying that. They truly understand feelings’ origin, disappearance, gratification, drawback, and escape. There’s no underlying tendency to ignorance about neutral feeling underlying that.

§7If they feel a pleasant feeling, they feel it detached. If they feel a painful feeling, they feel it detached. If they feel a neutral feeling, they feel it detached. They’re called a learned noble disciple who is detached from rebirth, old age, and death, from sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress. They are detached from suffering, I say. This is the difference between a learned noble disciple and an unlearned ordinary person.

Closing verses

§8A wise and learned person does not not feel
the feeling of pleasure and pain.
This is the great difference of skill
between the wise one and the ordinary.

§9A learned person who has appraised the teaching
discerns this world and the next.
Desirable things don’t disturb their mind,
nor are they repelled by the undesirable.

§10Both favoring and opposing
are cleared and ended, and are no more.
Knowing the stainless, sorrowless state,
they who have gone beyond rebirth understand rightly.”

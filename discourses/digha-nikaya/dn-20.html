Dīgha Nikāya · Discourse 20

The Great Assembly

Mahāsamayasutta

Setting
The Great Wood near Kapilavatthu, land of the Sakyans, with five hundred arahant mendicants present
Speakers
The Buddha, addressing the mendicants; four deities of the Pure Abodes speak brief verses at the opening
Form
Mostly verse — an extended catalog-style roll call of deva hosts, framed by a short narrative of Māra's failed intrusion
Length
~10 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart likely exists in the Chinese Dīrgha-āgama (T1), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — short, but its unfamiliar catalog of names rewards a slower, less literal reading

Why this discourse, twentieth

Most discourses in this collection are conversations. This one is closer to a hymn. Word spreads through the cosmos that the Buddha is staying in a forest with five hundred perfected disciples, and the response is a converging of deities on a scale the text can only render as an extended chant of names — earth-gods and sky-gods, the four great kings and their armies, dragons and centaurs and phoenix-birds, the gods of sun and moon and the wandering stars, host after host arriving to pay respects. It is one of the few substantially versified suttas in the whole Mahāvagga, and its strangeness is exactly the point: this is a text trying to gesture at a scale of devotion too large for ordinary narrative to hold.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Deities from throughout the ten-thousand-fold universe system converge on a forest to see the Buddha and his five hundred arahants, prompting the Buddha to recite an extended catalog of the deva hosts present by name and origin — a roll call interrupted only by Māra the Wicked, who arrives with his own army to disrupt the gathering and is instantly recognized and repelled.

An unusual shape for a discourse

Almost every other sutta in this collection is built from dialogue — a question, an answer, a story told to illustrate a point. DN 20 works differently. After a brief prose frame, it becomes almost entirely verse: a long, formal listing of deity groups, introduced and closed with the same refrain, “Rejoicing, they’ve come forth to the meeting of mendicants in the wood.” If you come to this discourse expecting an argument to follow, expect instead something closer to a liturgical chant or a ceremonial welcome, more akin to an ancient epic’s catalog of ships or heroes than to a philosophical dialogue.

Why a list

The occasion is simple: the Buddha is staying in the Great Wood near Kapilavatthu together with five hundred mendicants, all of them arahants, and most of the deities from ten solar systems have gathered to see them. Four especially refined deities of the Pure Abodes arrive first and speak short verses of praise, and their arrival prompts the Buddha to do something he says previous Buddhas have also done when faced with a gathering this large: declare, extol, and teach the names of the heavenly hosts, so that the mendicants present — who begin to perceive these visitors directly, some seeing hundreds, some seeing an uncountable number spread in every direction — understand exactly who has come to see them. The list that follows names earth-spirits from specific mountains and regions, the four great kings of the cardinal directions and their retinues of centaurs, dragons, and spirits, the titans defeated in ancient battles, the gods of water and fire, of the sun, moon, and planets, and finally a great host connected to the Brahmā realms, including the Buddha’s own past-life associate, the divinity Sanaṅkumāra, met already in the previous discourse.

A note on reading a list like this

It is tempting to skim a passage built from unfamiliar proper names — Kuṭeṇḍu, Viṭeṇḍu, the Sadāmattas and Hāragajas — the way one might skim a genealogy in an old chronicle. Resist that impulse here. The catalog is not decoration around the discourse’s real content; for this particular sutta, the catalog is the content, and its very unfamiliarity is doing something specific: it is trying to convey scale. No single named being matters as much as the cumulative effect of hearing, group after group, just how much of the cosmos the text imagines turning out for this occasion. Read slowly, and let the strangeness of the names register rather than trying to memorize any of them individually; the effect the text is built for is closer to standing at the edge of an enormous crowd than to studying a roster.

Māra's brief, failed intrusion

The list is not left to stand entirely alone. Once the deva hosts have all arrived, Māra the Wicked — the tempter-figure the tradition treats as opposition to the path personified — brings his own army, ordering them to surround the gathering, seize everyone present, and bind them with desire so that no one can escape. It is a brief, almost comic failure: the army fled, the text says, and “not a single hair was stirred,” because those gathered were already free of the very passions Māra’s forces depend on to take hold. The whole episode occupies only a handful of verses, but it gives the long catalog a narrative frame and a point: an assembly built entirely around freedom from craving has, by definition, nothing in it left for Māra to seize.

Why this belongs next to DN 19

Read alongside the previous discourse, DN 20 continues a thread rather than starting a new one. DN 19 closed with Pañcasikha vanishing back into the deva world after reporting Sakka’s story; here, that world itself arrives at the Buddha’s doorstep, in person and in overwhelming numbers. Where DN 19 dramatized a single steward’s long, patient effort toward the Brahmā-realm, DN 20 dramatizes the sheer size of the cosmos that effort opens onto — and then, in Māra’s failed raid, reminds the reader that access to all of it still depends on the same freedom from craving that any individual practitioner has to cultivate directly.

Key terms

the Pure Abodes (Suddhāvāsa) — the highest Brahmā realms, reserved for non-returners; the four deities who open this discourse come from there.
the four great kings — Dhataraṭṭha, Virūḷhaka, Virūpakkha, and Vessavaṇa (also called Kuvera), guardian deities of the four cardinal directions, each ruling a different class of being — centaurs, spirits, dragons, and gnomes respectively.
“Rejoicing, they’ve come forth…” — the refrain closing nearly every stanza of the catalog, binding dozens of otherwise unconnected deva groups into a single continuous roll call.
Māra — the personification of death, temptation, and everything opposed to liberation; his failed attempt to bind the assembly with desire closes the discourse.
Sanaṅkumāra — “the Eternal Youth,” the Brahmā deity encountered at length in DN 19, who appears again here among the higher hosts.

The text

This discourse is unusual: after a brief prose opening, it consists almost entirely of an extended catalog-verse listing the deva hosts who converge on the Buddha, followed by a short account of Māra’s failed attempt to disrupt the gathering. The catalog is reproduced here in full, exactly as it appears in the source — it is easy to assume a list of unfamiliar names can be skipped over, but here the list is the discourse; nothing has been abridged. Read it slowly, letting the sheer accumulation of names do its work, rather than trying to track every figure individually. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

The gathering

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the land of the Sakyans, in the Great Wood near Kapilavatthu, together with a large Saṅgha of five hundred mendicants, all of whom were perfected ones. And most of the deities from ten solar systems had gathered to see the Buddha and the Saṅgha of mendicants.

§2–3Then four deities of the Pure Abodes, aware of what was happening, thought: “Why don’t we go to the Buddha and each recite a verse in his presence?” Then, as easily as a strong person would extend or contract their arm, they vanished from the Pure Abodes and reappeared in front of the Buddha. They bowed to the Buddha and stood to one side. Standing to one side, one deity spoke this verse in the Buddha’s presence: “There’s a great congregation in the woods, where heavenly hosts have assembled. We’ve come to this righteous congregation to see the invincible Saṅgha!”

Then another deity spoke this verse: “The mendicants there are immersed in samādhi, they’ve straightened their own minds. Like a charioteer holding the reins, the astute ones protect their senses.” Then another: “They snapped the post and snapped the cross-bar, unstirred, they tore out Indra’s pillar. They live pure and immaculate, the young giants tamed by the Clear-eyed One.” And then another: “Anyone who has gone to the Buddha for refuge won’t go to a plane of loss. After giving up this human body, they swell the hosts of gods.”

1. The Gathering of Deities

§4Then the Buddha said to the mendicants: “Mendicants, most of the deities from ten solar systems have gathered to see the Realized One and the mendicant Saṅgha. Those who were perfected ones, fully awakened Buddhas in the past also had gatherings of deities that were at best like the gathering for me now. Those who will be perfected ones, fully awakened Buddhas in the future will also have gatherings of deities that will be at best like the gathering for me now. I shall declare the names of the heavenly hosts; I shall extol the names of the heavenly hosts; I shall teach the names of the heavenly hosts. Listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

The roll call of the deva hosts

§5“I invoke a paean of praise! Where the earth-gods dwell, there, in mountain caves, resolute and composed, dwell many like lonely lions, who have mastered their fears. Their minds are bright and pure, clear and undisturbed.” The teacher knew that over five hundred were in the wood at Kapilavatthu. Therefore he addressed the disciples who love the teaching: “The heavenly hosts have come forth; mendicants, you should be aware of them.” Those monks grew keen, hearing the Buddha’s instruction.

§6Knowledge manifested in them, seeing those non-human beings. Some saw a hundred, a thousand, even seventy thousand, while some saw a hundred thousand non-human beings. But some saw an endless number spread out in every direction. And all that was known and distinguished by the Clear-eyed One. Therefore he addressed the disciples who love the teaching: “The heavenly hosts have come forth; mendicants, you should be aware of them. I shall extol them for you, with lyrics in proper order.

§7There are seven thousand spirits, earth-gods of Kapilavatthu. They’re powerful and brilliant, so beautiful and glorious. Rejoicing, they’ve come forth to the meeting of mendicants in the wood. From the Himalayas there are six thousand spirits of different colors… From Mount Sātā there are three thousand spirits of different colors… And thus there are sixteen thousand spirits of different colors—they’re powerful and brilliant, so beautiful and glorious. Rejoicing, they’ve come forth to the meeting of mendicants in the wood.

§8From Vessāmitta’s mountain there are five hundred spirits of different colors… And there’s Kumbhīra of Rājagaha, whose home is on Mount Vepulla. Attended by more than a hundred thousand spirits, Kumbhīra of Rājagaha also came to the meeting in the wood.

§9King Dhataraṭṭha rules the eastern quarter, lord of the centaurs, a great king, glorious. And he has many mighty sons, all of them named Indra—powerful and brilliant, so beautiful and glorious. Rejoicing, they’ve come forth to the meeting of mendicants in the wood.

King Virūḷhaka rules the southern quarter, lord of the gnomes, a great king, glorious. And he has many mighty sons, all of them named Indra… King Virūpakkha rules the western quarter, lord of the dragons, a great king, glorious. And he has many mighty sons, all of them named Indra… King Kuvera rules the northern quarter, lord of spirits, a great king, glorious. And he has many mighty sons, all of them named Indra—powerful and brilliant, so beautiful and glorious. Rejoicing, they’ve come forth to the meeting of mendicants in the wood. Dhataraṭṭha in the east, Virūḷhaka to the south, Virūpakkha to the west, and Kuvera in the north. These four great kings, all around in the four quarters, stood there dazzling in the wood at Kapilavatthu.

§10Their deceitful heathens came, so treacherous and crafty—the deceivers Kuṭeṇḍu, Viṭeṇḍu, with Viṭucca and Viṭuṭa. And Candana and Kāmaseṭṭha, Kinnughaṇḍu and Nighaṇḍu, Panāda and Opamañña, and Mātali, the god’s equerry. Cittasena the centaur came too, and the kings Nala and Janesabha. Pañcasikha came too, with Timbaru and Suriyavaccasā. These and other kings there were, the centaurs with their kings. Rejoicing, they’ve come forth to the meeting of mendicants in the wood.

§11Then came the dragons of Nābhasa lake, and those from Vesālī with those from Takkasilā. The Kambalas and Assataras came and those from Payāga with their kin. Those from Yamunā, and the Dhataraṭṭha dragons came, so glorious. And Erāvaṇa the great dragon also came to the meeting in the wood. Those who seize the dragon kings by force—heavenly, twice-born birds with piercing vision—swoop down to the wood from the sky; their name is ‘Rainbow Phoenix’. But the dragon kings remained fearless, for the Buddha kept them safe from the phoenixes. Wooing each other with gentle words, the dragons and phoenixes took the Buddha as refuge.

§12Defeated by Vajirahattha, the titans live in the ocean. They’re brothers of Vāsava, powerful and glorious. There’s the terrifying Kālakañjas, the Dānava and Ghasa titans, Vepacitti and Sucitti, Pahārāda with Namuci, and Bali’s hundred sons, all named after Virocana. Bali’s army armed themselves and went to the auspicious Rāhu, saying: ‘Now is the time, sir, for the meeting of mendicants in the wood.’

§13The gods of water and earth, and fire and wind came there. The gods of Varuṇa and Varuṇa’s offspring, and Soma together with Yasa. A host of the gods of love and compassion came, so glorious. These ten hosts of gods shone in all different colors—powerful and brilliant, so beautiful and glorious. Rejoicing, they’ve come forth to the meeting of mendicants in the wood.

§14The Vishnu and Sahalī gods, and the unequaled pair of twins. The gods living on the moon came, with the Moon before them. The gods living on the sun came, with the Sun before them. And with the stars before them came the languid gods of clouds. And Vāsava came, the greatest of the Vasus, who is Sakka the Able, Purindada the Firstgiver: these ten hosts of gods shone in all different colors—powerful and brilliant, so beautiful and glorious. Rejoicing, they’ve come forth to the meeting of mendicants in the wood.

§15Then came the Sahabhū gods, blazing like a crested flame; and the Ariṭṭhakas and Rojas too, and the gods hued blue as flax. The Varuṇas and Sahadhammas, the Accutas and Anejakas, the Sūleyyas and Ruciras all came, as did the Vāsavanesi gods. These ten hosts of gods shone in all different colors—powerful and brilliant, so beautiful and glorious. Rejoicing, they’ve come forth to the meeting of mendicants in the wood.

§16The Samānas, Mahāsamānas, Mānusas, and Mānusuttamas all came, and the gods depraved by play, and those who are malevolent. Then came the gods of Mercury, and those who live on Mars. The Pāragas and Mahāpāragas came, such glorious gods. These ten hosts of gods shone in all different colors—powerful and brilliant, so beautiful and glorious. Rejoicing, they’ve come forth to the meeting of mendicants in the wood.

§17The gods of Venus, the newborn Sun, and the Dawn came along with those from Saturn. And the gods of the white globe came leading the brilliant gods. The Sadāmattas and Hāragajas, and assorted glorious ones; Pajjuna came thundering, he who rains on all quarters. These ten hosts of gods shone in all different colors—powerful and brilliant, so beautiful and glorious. Rejoicing, they’ve come forth to the meeting of mendicants in the wood.

§18The Khemiyas from the realms of Tusita and Yama, and the glorious Kaṭṭhakas came; the Lambītakas, Lāmaseṭṭhas, those called the Shining, and those Yonder. The gods who love to create came too, and those who control what is created by others. These ten hosts of gods shone in all different colors—powerful and brilliant, so beautiful and glorious. Rejoicing, they’ve come forth to the meeting of mendicants in the wood.

§19–20These sixty hosts of gods shone in all different colors. They came organized by name, these and others likewise, thinking: ‘They who have shed rebirth, the kindly ones, the undefiled ones who have crossed the flood—let us see them, with the dragon who brought them across, who like the Moon has overcome darkness.’

Subrahmā and Paramatta came, with sons of those powerful ones. Sanaṅkumāra and Tissa also came to the meeting in the wood. Of a thousand realms of divinity, the Great Divinity stands forth. He has arisen, brilliant, his formidable body so glorious. Ten Gods Almighty came there, each one of them wielding power, and in the middle of them came Hārita with his following.”

Māra's failed intrusion

§21When they had all come forth—the gods with their Lord, and the divinities—Māra’s army came forth too: see the stupidity of the Dark Lord! “Come, seize them and bind them,” he said, “let them be bound by desire! Surround them on all sides, don’t let any escape!” And so there the great general, the Dark Lord sent forth his army. He struck the ground with his fist to make a horrifying sound like a cloud shedding rain, thundering and flashing. But then turned back, furious, out of control.

§22And all that was known and distinguished by the Clear-eyed One. Thereupon the Teacher addressed the disciples who love the teaching: “Māra’s army has arrived; mendicants, you should be aware of them.” Those monks grew keen, hearing the Buddha’s instruction. The army fled from those free of passion, and not a single hair was stirred! “All triumphant in battle, so fearless and glorious. They rejoice with all the spirits, the disciples renowned among men.”

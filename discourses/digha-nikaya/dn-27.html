Dīgha Nikāya · Discourse 27

What Came First

Aggaññasutta

Setting
Near Sāvatthī, in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother, Eastern Monastery
Speakers
The Buddha, and two brahmin-born novices on probation for ordination, Vāseṭṭha and Bhāradvāja
Form
A reply to caste-pride, delivered as a long, partly satirical origin-myth of the cosmos and of society
Length
~35 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A broad counterpart likely exists in the Chinese Dīrgha-āgama (T1), though this reading guide does not assert a specific matching sutra number
Difficulty
★★★☆☆ — narrative and often playful, but it tracks a long chain of cause and effect that rewards careful reading

Why this discourse

Two brahmin students who have given up caste privilege to become monks are being mocked by their own relatives for it. Rather than mount a direct defense, the Buddha does something more radical: he tells a story — half cosmology, half satire — about where the world, and human society, and caste itself, actually came from. By the end, “brahmin,” “aristocrat,” “peasant,” and “menial” turn out to be nothing more than old job titles that ordinary people gave themselves, for ordinary reasons, at a particular point in a very long story. What actually divides people, the Buddha insists, was never birth. It was conduct — and conduct is available to everyone alike.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

When two ex-brahmin monks are mocked by their own caste for “abandoning the highest class,” the Buddha answers not with a defense of monks in particular, but with a complete origin-myth of the cosmos and society showing that all four classes — aristocrats, brahmins, peasants, and menials — arose from identical, ordinary, historical causes; none was born superior, and only conduct, not birth, actually divides people into better and worse.

An insult, and a strange kind of answer

Vāseṭṭha and Bhāradvāja are brahmins by birth who have left home to train as monks under the Buddha. Their former caste-fellows are furious: brahmins, they insist, are “the best class,” “the light class,” “purified,” the Creator-divinity’s own true-born children — “born from his mouth” — while everyone else, including these two turncoats now living among “shavelings” and “primitives,” is inferior. The Buddha’s response is not to argue that monks are fine people too. He goes underneath the argument entirely: brahmin women, after all, visibly menstruate, become pregnant, give birth, and breastfeed, just like everyone else’s mothers. The claim of miraculous birth “from the mouth of the Divinity” is simply false on its face — and from there the Buddha widens out into an explanation of where the whole system of caste actually came from.

The same virtue and vice in every class

Before turning to cosmology, the Buddha makes an empirical point: killing, theft, sexual misconduct, and lying — along with their wholesome opposites — are all found among aristocrats, brahmins, peasants, and menials alike. Good and bad conduct is “mixed up in all four classes”; no class has a monopoly on either. Since it is a fully awakened person from any of these four classes, having ended defilement entirely, who is rightly called foremost, that ranking runs “by virtue of principle, not against principle” — worth is decided by what a person actually does, not by what family they were born into. The Buddha even cites an example everyone in the audience could verify for themselves: King Pasenadi shows the Buddha the same deference a vassal shows an overlord, not because he thinks himself lesser by birth, but purely out of respect for what the Buddha embodies.

An origin-myth of the cosmos

Then comes the sutta’s most famous material. Over enormous stretches of time the cosmos contracts and then expands; when it expands, beings arise as radiant, mind-made, sexless, self-luminous creatures who feed on joy alone and need nothing else. A savory, cream-like “earth’s nectar” eventually forms on the surface of the primordial waters. One curious being tastes it, likes it, and craving is born — others follow, and as the beings begin literally eating the physical world, their luminosity fades, replaced (for the first time) by the sun, moon, stars, and the ordinary cycle of day and night. Their bodies coarsen. Vanity over relative beauty causes the nectar itself to vanish, replaced in turn by ground-fungus, then flowering pods, each vanishing in the same way, through the same pattern of enjoyment, pride, and loss. The text pauses twice to note that ordinary sayings still used “even today” — “Oh, what a taste!” at something delicious, “Oh, what we’ve lost!” at a misfortune — are, without anyone realizing it, echoes of this ancient, half-forgotten history.

From rice, to sex, to property, to kings

Eventually a different food appears: ripe, untilled rice, which regrows overnight without cultivation. Bodies coarsen further, sexual difference appears, and desire leads to the first sexual acts — at first considered so shameful that couples are pelted with dirt and barred from villages for months (a memory, the text says, behind the old custom in some places of pelting a bride with dirt at her wedding). Eventually one lazy being decides to gather two days’ rice at once rather than daily; others copy him, hoarding grows, and the rice itself changes — no longer self-renewing, it must now be planted, harvested, and defended. Beings divide the land into private plots. One being steals from another’s plot; scolding fails, then striking follows — and “from that day on, stealing was found, and blame, and lying, and the taking up of rods.” Faced with disorder they cannot resolve themselves, the community elects the most impressive among them to judge fairly, banish wrongdoers, and keep the peace, in exchange for a share of the harvest — the origin, the Buddha says, of the very words for “king” and “aristocrat” (mahāsammata, “the great elect”; khattiya, “lord of the fields”; rāja, “one who pleases with principle”).

Brahmins, peasants, and menials — invented the same way

The origin-story doesn’t stop with kings. Others, appalled at the same disorder, choose instead to “set aside bad, unskillful things” and withdraw to meditate in the wilderness — the first “brahmins,” a word the Buddha derives from exactly that renunciation, not from any birthright. When some of these meditators can’t sustain the discipline and settle near villages to compile texts instead, they earn the second brahmin title, “reciter” — literally, “those who no longer meditate,” a title the Buddha notes was once considered second-rate and is now, ironically, considered the most prestigious of all. Others among the same original population take up trades and active family life, becoming the ancestors of the peasant class; the remainder, who live by hunting and menial work, become the menial class. And from any of these four classes, in turn, individuals continue to go forth as renunciates — the origin of the ascetic community itself. Every class, without exception, was invented by ordinary people responding to ordinary problems, at a specific and traceable point in this shared history.

The real dividing line

Having dismantled the mythology of birth entirely, the Buddha closes with what actually does divide people: any aristocrat, brahmin, peasant, menial, or ascetic can act badly by body, speech, and mind and be reborn in a place of loss; any of them can act well and be reborn in a heavenly realm; any of them can develop the qualities that lead to awakening and become “extinguished in this very life.” The chapter closes by having the Buddha quote — and endorse — a verse originally spoken by the divinity Sanaṅkumāra: by the ordinary standard of clan and lineage, the aristocrat is “best among people”; but the person “accomplished in knowledge and conduct is first among gods and humans.” Two rankings exist, in other words — one by birth, one by realization — and only the second one, the Buddha makes unmistakably clear, is the one that actually matters.

Key terms

aggañña — “what came first” or “concerning the beginning”: the discourse’s title, referring to its account of the first origins of the cosmos, society, and caste.
mahāsammata — “the great elect,” the very first title given to the community’s chosen ruler; the mythic root of the word for “aristocrat” and “king” in this sutta’s etymology.
the earth’s nectar — the first physical food to appear once the newly-formed cosmos’s radiant beings begin craving something more than joy; its vanishing, through vanity and greed, begins the whole downward cascade toward ordinary embodied life.
“by virtue of principle, not against principle” — the sutta’s refrain: every social category it describes — king, brahmin, peasant, menial, ascetic — was created for real, traceable, non-arbitrary reasons, never as a birthright.
dhammabhūta / brahmabhūta — “the embodiment of principle” and “the embodiment of divinity,” titles the Buddha claims are properly his own, replacing the brahmins’ claim to be the Divinity’s literal children.

The text

The discourse in full: the brahmins’ insult, the Buddha’s point-by-point dismantling of caste pride, the extended origin-myth of the cosmos and society, and the closing verse on what actually distinguishes “the best.” Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

An insult from the brahmins

§1.1–1.4So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother in the Eastern Monastery. Now at that time Vāseṭṭha and Bhāradvāja were living on probation among the mendicants in hopes of being ordained. Then in the late afternoon, the Buddha came downstairs from the longhouse and was walking mindfully in the open air, beneath the shade of the longhouse.

§2.1–2.7Vāseṭṭha saw him and said to Bhāradvāja, “Reverend Bhāradvāja, the Buddha is walking mindfully in the open air, beneath the shade of the longhouse. Come, reverend, let’s go to the Buddha. Hopefully we’ll get to hear a Dhamma talk from him.” “Yes, reverend,” replied Bhāradvāja. So they went to the Buddha, bowed, and walked beside him.

§3.1–3.12Then the Buddha said to Vāseṭṭha, “Vāseṭṭha, you are both brahmins by birth and family, and have gone forth from the lay life to homelessness from a brahmin family. I hope you don’t have to suffer abuse and insults from the brahmins.” “Actually, sir, the brahmins do insult and abuse us with their typical insults to the fullest extent, holding nothing back.” “But how do the brahmins insult you?” “Sir, the brahmins say: ‘Only brahmins are the best class; other classes are inferior. Only brahmins are the light class; other classes are dark. Only brahmins are purified, not others. Only brahmins are the Divinity’s true-born sons, born from his mouth, born of the Divinity, created by the Divinity, heirs of the Divinity. You’ve both abandoned the best class to join an inferior class, namely these shavelings, fake ascetics, primitives, black spawn from the feet of our kinsman. This is not right, it’s not proper!’ That’s how the brahmins insult us.”

§4.1–4.12“Actually, Vāseṭṭha, the brahmins are forgetting their tradition when they say this to you. For brahmin women are seen menstruating, being pregnant, giving birth, and breast-feeding. Yet even though they’re born from a brahmin womb they say: ‘Only brahmins are the best class; other classes are inferior. Only brahmins are the light class; other classes are dark. Only brahmins are purified, not others. Only brahmins are the Divinity’s true-born sons, born from his mouth, born of the Divinity, created by the Divinity, heirs of the Divinity.’ They misrepresent the brahmins, speak falsely, and brim with much wickedness.

1. Purification in the Four Classes

§5.1–7.10Vāseṭṭha, there are these four classes: aristocrats, brahmins, peasants, and menials. Some aristocrats kill living creatures, steal, and commit sexual misconduct. They use speech that’s false, backbiting, harsh, and nonsensical. And they’re covetous, malicious, with wrong view. These things are unskillful, blameworthy, not to be cultivated, unworthy of the noble ones — and are reckoned as such. They are dark deeds with dark results, criticized by sensible people. Such things are exhibited in some aristocrats. And they are also seen among some brahmins, peasants, and menials.

But some aristocrats refrain from killing living creatures, stealing, and sexual misconduct. They refrain from speech that’s false, backbiting, harsh, and nonsensical. And they’re content, kind-hearted, with right view. These things are skillful, blameless, to be cultivated, worthy of the noble ones — and are reckoned as such. They are bright deeds with bright results, praised by sensible people. Such things are exhibited in some aristocrats. And they are also seen among some brahmins, peasants, and menials.

Both these things occur like this, mixed up in these four classes — the dark and the bright, that which is praised and that which is criticized by sensible people. Yet of this the brahmins say: ‘Only brahmins are the best class … heirs of the Divinity.’ Sensible people don’t grant this. Why is that? Because any mendicant from these four classes who is perfected — with defilements ended, who has completed the spiritual journey, done what had to be done, laid down the burden, achieved their heart’s goal, utterly ended the fetter of continued existence, and is rightly freed through enlightenment — is said to be foremost by virtue of principle, not against principle. For principle, Vāseṭṭha, is the best thing for people in both this life and the next. And here’s a way to understand how this is so.

§8.1–8.10King Pasenadi of Kosala knows that the ascetic Gotama has gone forth from the neighboring clan of the Sakyans. And the Sakyans are his vassals. The Sakyans show deference to King Pasenadi by bowing down, rising up, greeting him with cupped palms, and observing proper etiquette for him. Now, King Pasenadi shows the same kind of deference to the Realized One. But he doesn’t think: ‘The ascetic Gotama is well-born, I am ill-born. He is powerful, I am weak. He is handsome, I am ugly. He is illustrious, I am insignificant.’ Rather, in showing such deference to the Realized One he is only honoring, respecting, and venerating principle. And here’s another way to understand how principle is the best thing for people in both this life and the next.

§9.1–9.6Vāseṭṭha, you have different births, names, clans, and families, yet have gone forth from the lay life to homelessness. When they ask you what you are, you claim to be ascetics who follow the Sakyan. But only when someone has faith in the Realized One — settled, rooted, and planted deep, strong, not to be shifted by any ascetic or brahmin or god or Māra or divinity or by anyone in the world — is it appropriate for them to say: ‘I am the Buddha’s true-born son, born from his mouth, born of principle, created by principle, heir to principle.’ Why is that? For these are terms for the Realized One: ‘the embodiment of principle,’ and ‘the embodiment of divinity,’ and ‘the manifestation of principle,’ and ‘the manifestation of divinity.’

2. The Earth’s Nectar Appears

§10.1–10.6There comes a time when, Vāseṭṭha, after a very long period has passed, this cosmos contracts. As the cosmos contracts, sentient beings are mostly headed for the realm of streaming radiance. There they are mind-made, feeding on rapture, self-luminous, wandering in midair, steadily glorious, and they remain like that for a very long time. There comes a time when, after a very long period has passed, this cosmos expands. As the cosmos expands, sentient beings mostly pass away from that host of radiant deities and come back to this realm. Here they are mind-made, feeding on rapture, self-luminous, wandering in midair, steadily glorious, and they remain like that for a very long time.

§11.1–11.13But the single mass of water at that time was utterly dark. The moon and sun were not found, nor were stars and constellations, day and night, months and fortnights, years and seasons, or male and female. Beings were simply known as ‘beings.’ After a very long period had passed, the earth’s nectar curdled in the water. It appeared just like the curd on top of hot milk-rice as it cools. It was beautiful, fragrant, and delicious, like ghee or butter. And it was as sweet as pure dwarf-bee honey. Now, one of those beings was reckless. Thinking, ‘Oh my, what might this be?’ they tasted the earth’s nectar with their finger. They enjoyed it, and craving was born in them. And other beings, following that being’s example, tasted the earth’s nectar with their fingers. They too enjoyed it, and craving was born in them.

3. The Moon and Sun Appear

§12.1–13.12Then those beings started to eat the earth’s nectar, breaking it into lumps. But when they did this, their luminosity vanished. And with the vanishing of their luminosity the moon and sun appeared, stars and constellations appeared, days and nights were distinguished, and so were months and fortnights, and years and seasons. So far had the world evolved once more.

Then those beings eating the earth’s nectar, with that as their food and nourishment, remained for a very long time. But so long as they ate that earth’s nectar, their bodies became more solid and they diverged in appearance; some beautiful, some ugly. And the beautiful beings looked down on the ugly ones: ‘We’re more beautiful, they’re the ugly ones!’ And the vanity of the beautiful ones made the earth’s nectar vanish. They gathered together and bemoaned, ‘Oh, what a taste! Oh, what a taste!’ And even today when people get something tasty they say: ‘Oh, what a taste! Oh, what a taste!’ They’re just remembering an ancient primordial saying, but they don’t understand what it means.

4. Ground-Fungus, and 5. Bursting Pods

§14.1–15.12When the earth’s nectar had vanished, ground-fungus appeared to those beings. It appeared just like a mushroom. It was beautiful, fragrant, and delicious, like ghee or butter, and as sweet as pure dwarf-bee honey. Then those beings started to eat the ground-fungus, and with that as their food and nourishment they remained for a very long time. But so long as they ate that ground-fungus, their bodies became more solid and diverged further in appearance, some beautiful, some ugly — and the vanity of the beautiful ones made the ground-fungus vanish, just as before.

When the ground-fungus had vanished, bursting pods appeared, like the fruit of the kadam tree — beautiful, fragrant, and delicious, as sweet as pure dwarf-bee honey. Those beings started to eat the bursting pods, remaining on that food for a very long time; but again their bodies coarsened, they diverged into beautiful and ugly, and the vanity of the beautiful ones made the bursting pods vanish too. They gathered together and bemoaned, ‘Oh, what we’ve lost! Oh, what we’ve lost — those bursting pods!’ And even today when people experience suffering they say: ‘Oh, what we’ve lost! Oh, what we’ve lost!’ They’re just remembering an ancient primordial saying, but they don’t understand what it means.

6. Ripe Untilled Rice, and 7. Sex Appears

§16.1–16.15When the bursting pods had vanished, ripe untilled rice appeared to those beings. It had no powder or husk, pure and fragrant, with only the rice-grain. What they took for supper at dusk, by daybreak had grown back and ripened; what they took for breakfast at daybreak had grown back and ripened by the evening, leaving no trace showing. Then those beings, eating the ripe untilled rice, remained for a very long time. But so long as they ate that ripe untilled rice, their bodies became more solid and they diverged in appearance — and female characteristics appeared on women, while male characteristics appeared on men.

Women spent too much time gazing at men, and men at women. They became lustful, and their bodies burned with fever. Due to this fever they had sex with each other. Those who saw them having sex pelted them with dirt, clods, or cow-dung, saying, ‘Get lost, filth! Get lost, filth! How on earth can one being do that to another?’ And even today people in some countries, when carrying a bride off, pelt her with dirt, clods, or cow-dung. They’re just remembering an ancient primordial saying, but they don’t understand what it means.

8. Sexual Intercourse

§17.1–17.23What was deemed as unprincipled at that time, these days is deemed as principled. The beings who had sex together weren’t allowed to enter a village or town for one or two months. Ever since they excessively threw themselves into immorality, they started to make buildings to hide their immoral deeds.

Then one of those beings of idle disposition thought, ‘Oh my, why should I be bothered to gather rice at dusk for supper, and at daybreak for breakfast? Why don’t I gather rice for supper and breakfast all at once?’ So that’s what he did. Then another being invited him to gather rice together; he replied there was no need, since he’d already gathered for two meals at once. That being, following the example, gathered rice for two days all at once — then, following further examples, for four days, then eight, each time thinking: ‘This seems fine.’ But when they started to store up rice to eat, the rice grains became wrapped in powder and husk; it didn’t grow back after reaping, leaving a trace showing, and the rice stood in clumps.

9. Dividing the Rice

§18.1–19.12Then those beings gathered together and bemoaned, ‘Oh, how wicked things have appeared among beings! For we used to be mind-made, feeding on rapture, self-luminous … but due to bad, unskillful things among us, the earth’s nectar vanished, then the ground-fungus vanished, then the bursting pods vanished, and now the rice grains have become wrapped in powder and husk, it doesn’t grow back after reaping, traces are left showing, and the rice stands in clumps. We’d better divide up the rice and lay down boundaries.’ So that’s what they did.

Now, one of those beings was reckless. While guarding their own share they took another’s share without it being given, and ate it. They grabbed the one who had done this and said, ‘You have done a bad thing, good being, in that while guarding your own share you took another’s share without it being given, and ate it. Do not do such a thing again.’ ‘Yes, worthy sirs,’ replied that being. But for a second time, and a third time, they did the same thing, and were told not to continue — and then they struck that being, some with fists, others with stones, and still others with rods. From that day on stealing was found, and blame, and lying, and the taking up of rods.

10. The Elected King

§20.1–21.5Then those beings gathered together and bemoaned, ‘Oh, how wicked things have appeared among beings, in that stealing is found, and blaming and lying and the taking up of rods! Why don’t we elect one being who would rightly accuse those who deserve it, blame those who deserve it, and expel those who deserve it? We shall pay them with a share of rice.’ Then those beings approached the being among them who was most attractive, good-looking, lovely, and illustrious, and said, ‘Come, good being, rightly accuse those who deserve it, blame those who deserve it, and banish those who deserve it. We shall pay you with a share of rice.’ ‘Yes, worthy sirs,’ replied that being. They acted accordingly, and were paid with a share of rice.

‘Elected by the populace,’ Vāseṭṭha, is the meaning of ‘popularly elected,’ the first term applied to them. ‘Lord of the fields’ is the meaning of ‘aristocrat,’ the second term applied to them. ‘They please others with principle’ is the meaning of ‘king,’ the third term applied to them. And that, Vāseṭṭha, is how the ancient primordial terms for the circle of aristocrats were created; for those very beings, not others; for those like them, not unlike; by virtue of principle, not against principle. For principle, Vāseṭṭha, is the best thing for people in both this life and the next.

11. The Circle of Brahmins

§22.1–23.7Then some of those same beings thought, ‘Oh, how wicked things have appeared among beings, in that stealing is found, and blaming and lying and the taking up of rods and banishment! Why don’t we set aside bad, unskillful things?’ So that’s what they did. ‘They set aside bad, unskillful things’ is the meaning of ‘brahmin,’ the first term applied to them. They built leaf huts in a wilderness region where they meditated pure and bright, without lighting cooking fires or digging the soil, coming down at daybreak and dusk to seek a meal in the village, town, or royal capital. ‘They meditate’ is the meaning of ‘meditator,’ the second term applied to them.

But some of those beings were unable to keep up with their meditation in the leaf huts in the wilderness. They came down to the neighborhood of a village or town where they dwelt compiling texts instead. ‘Now they don’t meditate’ is the meaning of ‘reciter,’ the third term applied to them. What was deemed as worse at that time, these days is deemed as best. And that, Vāseṭṭha, is how the ancient primordial terms for the circle of brahmins were created; for those very beings, not others; for those like them, not unlike; by virtue of principle, not against principle. For principle, Vāseṭṭha, is the best thing for people in both this life and the next.

12. The Circle of Peasants, and 13. The Circle of Menials

§24.1–25.4Some of those same beings, taking up an active sex life, applied themselves to various jobs. ‘Having taken up an active sex life, they apply themselves to various jobs’ is the meaning of ‘peasant,’ the term applied to them. And that is how the ancient primordial term for the circle of peasants was created; for those very beings, not others; for those like them, not unlike; by virtue of principle, not against principle. For principle, Vāseṭṭha, is the best thing for people in both this life and the next.

The remaining beings lived by venal hunting and minor tasks. ‘They live by venal hunting and minor tasks’ is the meaning of ‘menial,’ the term applied to them. And that is how the ancient primordial term for the circle of menials was created — for those very beings, not others; for those like them, not unlike; by virtue of principle, not against principle. For principle, Vāseṭṭha, is the best thing for people in both this life and the next.

§26.1–26.8There came a time when an aristocrat, brahmin, peasant, or menial, deprecating their own vocation, went forth from the lay life to homelessness, thinking, ‘I will be an ascetic.’ From these four circles, Vāseṭṭha, the circle of ascetics was created; for those very beings, not others; for those like them, not unlike; by virtue of principle, not against principle. For principle, Vāseṭṭha, is the best thing for people in both this life and the next.

14. On Bad Conduct, and 15. The Qualities on the Side of Awakening

§27.1–30.5An aristocrat, brahmin, peasant, menial, or ascetic may do bad things by way of body, speech, and mind. They have wrong view, and they act out of that wrong view. And because of that, when their body breaks up, after death, they’re reborn in a place of loss, a bad place, the underworld, hell.

An aristocrat, brahmin, peasant, menial, or ascetic may do good things by way of body, speech, and mind. They have right view, and they act out of that right view. And because of that, when their body breaks up, after death, they’re reborn in a good place, a heavenly realm.

An aristocrat, brahmin, peasant, menial, or ascetic may do mixed things by way of body, speech, and mind. They have mixed view, and they act out of that mixed view. And because of that, when their body breaks up, after death, they experience both pleasure and pain.

An aristocrat, brahmin, peasant, menial, or ascetic who is restrained in body, speech, and mind, and develops the seven qualities on the side of awakening, becomes extinguished in this very life.

§31.1–32.13Any mendicant from these four classes who is perfected — with defilements ended, who has completed the spiritual journey, done what had to be done, laid down the burden, achieved their heart’s goal, utterly ended the fetter of continued existence, and is rightly freed through enlightenment — is said to be the foremost by virtue of principle, not against principle. For principle, Vāseṭṭha, is the best thing for people in both this life and the next.

The divinity Sanaṅkumāra also spoke this verse: ‘The aristocrat is best among people who take clan as the standard. But one accomplished in knowledge and conduct is first among gods and humans.’ That verse was well sung by the Divinity Sanaṅkumāra, not poorly sung; well spoken, not poorly spoken; beneficial, not harmful, and I agree with it. I also say: The aristocrat is best among people who take clan as the standard. But one accomplished in knowledge and conduct is first among gods and humans.”

That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Vāseṭṭha and Bhāradvāja approved what the Buddha said.

How does the discourse conclude, in the verse attributed to the divinity Sanaṅkumāra and endorsed by the Buddha?
Correct: B. The Buddha explicitly agrees with this verse, closing the sutta on this dual but clearly weighted ranking.

The insult
  • brahmins call themselves "best class"
  • claim: born from the Divinity's mouth
  • Buddha: brahmin women visibly give birth
The cosmic myth
  • radiant, sexless, mind-made beings
  • earth's nectar → ground-fungus → pods → rice
  • craving, vanity, coarsening bodies
  • sex, theft, property, violence
Four classes invented
  • aristocrat ← elected judge
  • brahmin ← meditator, then reciter
  • peasant ← household trade
  • menial ← hunting, minor tasks
The real ranking
  • "by virtue of principle, not against principle"
  • any class can act well or badly
  • knowledge and conduct, not clan, rank first

Dīgha Nikāya · Discourse 32

The Āṭānāṭiya Protection

Āṭānāṭiyasutta

Setting
Near Rājagaha, on Vulture’s Peak Mountain, late at night
Speakers
The Buddha and Great King Vessavaṇa, one of the Four Great Kings, together with a vast retinue of spirits
Form
A protective chant offered by non-human guardians and accepted by the Buddha, mostly in verse, framed by a short prose narrative
Length
~10 minutes to read
Northern parallel
Its closest Chinese parallel survives not within the Dīrgha-āgama collection itself but as an independent canonical text (Taishō no. 1245), reflecting its long life as a standalone protective chant rather than a narrative sutta
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — unusual in content and mostly straightforward to follow, though its long lists of names reward patience

Why this discourse, thirty-second

Of all thirty-four discourses in the Dīgha Nikāya, this is the one still recited today with the least alteration. The Āṭānāṭiya protection is not a philosophical teaching or a narrative about the Buddha’s life; it is a working chant, offered to the Buddha by non-human guardians for the concrete, practical purpose of keeping meditating monks and nuns safe in remote wilderness dwellings. The Buddha’s response is equally practical: he says nothing about its doctrinal content, offers no commentary, and simply accepts it for use. Centuries later, this same chant is still recited as one of the standard paritta texts of Theravāda Buddhism, alongside the Mettā Sutta and the Ratana Sutta — a rare case of an early Buddhist text whose primary use, from its very first appearance in the canon down to temples today, has stayed exactly the same.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

The Four Great Kings, guardian deities of the four directions, offer the Buddha a protective chant — honoring the seven Buddhas of the past by name and invoking the guardians and their retinues — for monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen to recite for safety and peace of mind against hostile, undevoted spirits, and the Buddha accepts it for exactly that use.

A concerned visit, late at night

The setting is unusual for the Dīgha Nikāya: not a debate hall, not a wanderers’ park, but Vulture’s Peak at night, suddenly lit up by the arrival of the Four Great Kings and a vast retinue of spirits, centaurs, gnomes, and dragons. Great King Vessavaṇa, lord of the north and of the spirits generally, explains the problem directly. Confidence in the Buddha is uneven even among non-human beings — some spirits, high, middling, and low, have it, but “mostly the spirits don’t,” precisely because the Buddha teaches the five precepts and many spirits are unwilling to give up killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying, or intoxicants. This matters because the Buddha’s own disciples frequently dwell alone in exactly the kind of remote wilderness lodgings where such undevoted spirits also live. Vessavaṇa’s proposed solution is concrete rather than doctrinal: a protective verse the monastics and lay followers can learn and recite, to build the confidence of friendly spirits and ward off the hostile ones. The Buddha consents in silence, and Vessavaṇa recites it on the spot.

Homage to seven Buddhas

The chant opens with a striking feature: verses of homage not to Gotama alone but to a lineage of seven Buddhas — Vipassī, Sikhī, Vessabhū, Kakusandha, Koṇāgamana, Kassapa, and finally “Aṅgīrasa, the glorious Sakyan,” a poetic epithet for the historical Buddha himself. This roll call of past Buddhas, each given a brief honorific phrase, situates Gotama’s teaching within a much longer procession of awakened teachers reaching back before recorded memory — a theme found in only a handful of other discourses, most notably DN 14’s account of Vipassī in full.

A tour of the four directions

The chant then moves, direction by direction, through each of the Four Great Kings’ domains — east (Dhataraṭṭha, lord of the centaurs), south (Virūḷha, lord of the gnomes), west (Virūpakkha, lord of the dragons), and north (Kuvera/Vessavaṇa, lord of the spirits) — each described with the same repeating refrain: the king’s realm, his many sons all confusingly “named Indra,” and the recurring homage, “We bow to Gotama the victor, accomplished in knowledge and conduct! We bow to Gotama the awakened!” The northern quarter, Kuvera’s own domain, receives by far the most elaborate description — a small mythological travelogue of heavenly cities, lakes, halls, birdsong, and orchards that briefly reads more like cosmological geography than doctrine, evidence of just how seriously this text takes its guardian-deity framing.

What to do if harassed

Having delivered the verses, Vessavaṇa turns to practical instruction. Any monastic or lay follower who has learned the protection well is guaranteed a baseline of safety: a hostile spirit that harasses such a person will be shunned by other non-humans, denied hospitality, and refused entry to the spirits’ own assembly — a social sanction imposed by the Great Kings’ own government of the spirit world, likened directly to how the king of Magadha’s officials treat rebels and bandits who refuse to obey. And if harassment happens anyway, the response is not passive: the harassed person is instructed to cry out by name to a long list of spirits, great spirits, and generals — Indra, Soma, Varuṇa, and dozens more — naming the specific protectors whose help can be invoked on the spot.

A chant with a job to do. Unlike most Dīgha Nikāya discourses, the Āṭānāṭiya protection is not primarily meant to be understood or reflected on doctrinally — it is meant to be memorized and recited, exactly as the Buddha instructs the mendicants at the discourse’s close.

The Buddha's endorsement

After the Four Kings depart, the Buddha does something notable: rather than commenting on or reinterpreting what he has just heard, he simply repeats the entire episode and all its verses to the assembled mendicants the next morning, then instructs them directly: “Mendicants, learn the Āṭānāṭiya protection! Memorize the Āṭānāṭiya protection! Remember the Āṭānāṭiya protection! The Āṭānāṭiya protection is beneficial, and is for the guarding, protection, safety, and comfort of the monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen.” There is no philosophical gloss added, no reservation expressed about its non-human origin. This straightforward acceptance is itself part of what makes the discourse historically significant: it shows the earliest Buddhist community incorporating a protective chant of frankly cosmological content into ordinary practice, with the Buddha’s own authority behind it.

Key terms

paritta — “protection” or “safeguard”: the general term for chants recited for protection and blessing in Theravāda practice; the Āṭānāṭiya protection is one of the tradition’s oldest examples, alongside the Mettā Sutta and Ratana Sutta.
the Four Great Kings — guardian deities of the four cardinal directions: Dhataraṭṭha (east, centaurs), Virūḷha (south, gnomes), Virūpakkha (west, dragons), and Kuvera/Vessavaṇa (north, spirits) — a standard feature of early Buddhist cosmology.
the seven Buddhas — Vipassī, Sikhī, Vessabhū, Kakusandha, Koṇāgamana, Kassapa, and Gotama (here called by the epithet “Aṅgīrasa”): the lineage of past Buddhas honored in the chant’s opening verses.
yakkha — “spirit”: a broad class of powerful non-human beings, some benevolent and devoted to the Buddha’s teaching, others hostile or simply indifferent to it, whose potential harassment of solitary meditators is the discourse’s stated concern.
the cry for help — the specific instruction, given late in the discourse, to call out by name to a long list of spirits and generals if harassed by a hostile non-human, treating the chant as an active remedy rather than only a preventative recitation.

The text

The discourse in full: the Four Kings’ visit, Vessavaṇa’s complete verse chant honoring the seven Buddhas and touring the four directions, the practical instructions for protection, and the Buddha’s closing endorsement to the mendicants. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A concerned visit, late at night

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, on the Vulture’s Peak Mountain. Then, late at night, the four great kings — with large armies of spirits, centaurs, gnomes, and dragons — set guards, troops, and wards at the four quarters and then, lighting up the entire Vulture’s Peak with their beauty, went up to the Buddha, bowed, and sat down to one side. Before sitting down to one side, some spirits bowed, some exchanged greetings and polite conversation, some held up their cupped palms toward the Buddha, some announced their name and clan, while some kept silent.

§2Seated to one side, the Great King Vessavaṇa said to the Buddha, “Sir, some high spirits have confidence in the Buddha, some do not. Some middling spirits have confidence in the Buddha, some do not. Some low spirits have confidence in the Buddha, some do not. But mostly the spirits don’t have confidence in the Buddha. Why is that? Because the Buddha teaches them to refrain from killing living creatures, stealing, lying, sexual misconduct, and consuming intoxicants of beer, wine, and liquor. But mostly they don’t refrain from such things. They don’t like that or approve of it. Sir, there are disciples of the Buddha who frequent remote lodgings in the wilderness and the forest that are quiet and still, far from the madding crowd, remote from human settlements, and fit for retreat. There dwell high spirits who have no confidence in the Buddha’s dispensation. To give them confidence, may the Buddha please learn the Āṭānāṭiya protection for the guarding, protection, safety, and comfort of the monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen.” The Buddha consented with silence.

Homage to seven Buddhas

§3Then, knowing that the Buddha had consented, on that occasion Great King Vessavaṇa recited the Āṭānāṭiya protection.

“Hail Vipassī, the glorious Clear-eyed One!
Hail Sikhī, sympathetic for all beings!
Hail Vessabhū, cleansed and fervent!
Hail Kakusandha, crusher of Māra’s army!
Hail Koṇāgamana, the brahmin who has lived the life!
Hail Kassapa, everywhere free!
Hail Aṅgīrasa, the glorious Sakyan!
He taught this Dhamma that casts aside all suffering.
Those in the world who are quenched, truly discerning, not backbiters; such people being great of heart and intrepid, revere that Gotama; he who is helpful to gods and humans, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, great of heart and intrepid.”

A tour of the four directions

§4“Where rises the sun — Aditi’s child, the great orb — with whose rising night’s umbra also ends, and of whom, when sun has risen, it is said to be the day — there is a deep lake, an ocean of flowing waters. That’s how they understand that lake there, as an ocean of flowing waters. From here that is the eastern quarter, so the people say. That quarter is warded by a great king, glorious, the lord of the centaurs; his name is Dhataraṭṭha. He delights in song and dance, honored by the centaurs. And he has many mighty sons all of one name, so I’ve heard. Eighty, and ten, and one — all of them named Indra. After seeing the Awakened One, the Buddha, Kinsman of the Sun, they revere him from afar, the one great of heart and intrepid. Homage to you, O thoroughbred! Homage to you, supreme among men! You examine us skillfully; the non-humans bow to you. We’ve been asked many a time, ‘Do you bow to Gotama the victor?’ And so we ought to declare: ‘We bow to Gotama the victor, accomplished in knowledge and conduct! We bow to Gotama the awakened!’”

§5“It’s where the departed go, they say, who are backbiters and calumniators, killers and hunters, bandits and cheats. From here that is the southern quarter, so the people say. That quarter is warded by a great king, glorious, the lord of the gnomes; his name is Virūḷha. He delights in song and dance, honored by the gnomes.” The same verses of the many sons, all named Indra, and the same homage — “We bow to Gotama the victor, accomplished in knowledge and conduct! We bow to Gotama the awakened!” — are repeated for this quarter as well.

§6“Where sets the sun — Aditi’s child, the great orb — with whose setting the day also ends; and of whom, when sun has set, it is said to be night’s umbra — there is a deep lake in that place, an ocean of flowing waters. From here that is the western quarter, so the people say. That quarter is warded by a great king, glorious, the lord of the dragons; his name is Virūpakkha. He delights in song and dance, honored by the dragons.” Again the same sons and the same homage close the verse for this quarter.

§7“Where the lovely land north of Kuru is, and the beautiful Mount Meru, humans born there are unselfish, without possessions. They do not sow the seed, nor do they draw the plough. The rice eaten by people ripens in untilled soil, free of powder or husk, pure, fragrant, with only the rice-grain. After cooking in a tandoor oven, they enjoy eating that food.” The verse goes on to describe cattle, carriages, and vehicles drawn about from place to place, palaces and palanquins, and cities well-built in the sky — Āṭānāṭā, Kusināṭā, Parakusināṭā, Nāṭapuriyā, and Parakusiṭanāṭā, with Kapīvanta and Janogha beyond, and the royal capital named Āḷakamandā. “The Great King Kuvera, dear sir, has a capital named Visāṇā, which is why the great king is called ‘Vessavaṇa’.” The verse names further searching, shining beings — Tatolā, Tattalā, Tatotalā, Ojasi, Tejasi, Tatojasi, Sūra, Rājā, Ariṭṭha, and Nemi — and describes a lake named Dharaṇī whence the clouds rain forth, and a hall named Sālavatī where the spirits frequent, with trees ever in fruit and many flocks of birds calling out, peacocks and herons and cuckoos, cocks and kookaburras and woodpeckers, parrots and mynahs and the “little stick-boy’ birds, and Kuvera’s pond of rushes, lovely all the time. “From here that is the northern quarter, so the people say. That quarter is warded by a great king, glorious, the lord of spirits; his name is Kuvera. He delights in song and dance, honored by the spirits.” The verse closes, as with the other three quarters, with the many sons named Indra and the homage: “We bow to Gotama the victor, accomplished in knowledge and conduct! We bow to Gotama the awakened!”

What to do if harassed

§8“This, good fellow, is the Āṭānāṭiya protection for the guarding, protection, safety, and comfort of the monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen. The monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen should learn this Āṭānāṭiya protection well and completely memorize it. If anyone who does so is approached while walking, standing, sitting, or lying down by any non-human being with malicious intent — including males, females, boys, girls, chief ministers, councillors, and servants among the spirits, centaurs, gnomes, and dragons — that non-human will receive no homage or respect in any village or town. And they will receive no ground or dwelling in my capital of Āḷakamandā. Nor will they get to go to the conference of the spirits. In addition, the non-humans would not give or take them in marriage. They’d heap personal abuse on them, drop an empty bowl on their head, and even split their head into seven pieces!

§9For there are, good fellow, non-humans who are fierce, cruel, and violent. They don’t obey the Great Kings or their men or their men’s men. They’re said to be rebelling against the Great Kings. They’re just like the bandits in the king of Magadha’s realm who don’t obey the king, his men, or his men’s men, and are said to be rebelling against the king. If any non-human being with malicious intent — including males, females, boys, girls, ministers, councillors, and servants among the spirits, centaurs, gnomes, and dragons — approaches a monk, nun, layman, or laywoman while walking, standing, sitting, or lying down, one ought to yell, cry, and scream to the spirits, great spirits, generals, great generals: ‘This spirit’s got me! This spirit’s entered me! This spirit’s annoying me! This spirit’s harassing me! This spirit’s hurting me! This spirit’s harming me! This spirit won’t let me go!’

§10To what spirits, great spirits, generals, great generals? ‘Indra, Soma, and Varuṇa, Bhāradvāja, the Progenitor, Candana and Kāmaseṭṭha, Kinnughaṇḍu and Nighaṇḍu, Panāda and Opamañña, and Mātali, the god’s equerry. Cittasena the centaur, and the kings Nala and Janesabha, Sātāgira, Hemavata, Puṇṇaka, Karatiya, and Guḷa; Sivaka and Mucalinda, Vessāmitta, Yugandhara, Gopāla, Suppagedha, Hiri, Netti, and Mandiya; Pañcālacaṇḍa, Āḷavaka, Pajjunna, Sumana, Sumukha, Dadhimukha, Maṇi, Māṇivara, Dīgha, together with Serīsaka.’

§11This, good fellow, is the Āṭānāṭiya protection for the guarding, protection, safety, and comfort of the monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen. Well, now, good fellow, I must go. I have many duties, and much to do.” “Please, Great Kings, go at your convenience.” Then the four great kings rose from their seats, bowed, and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on their right side, before vanishing right there. And before the other spirits present vanished, some bowed and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on their right side, some exchanged greetings and polite conversation, some held up their cupped palms toward the Buddha, some announced their name and clan, while some kept silent. The first recitation section is complete.

The Buddha's endorsement

§12–13Then, when the night had passed, the Buddha told the mendicants all that had happened, repeating all the verses spoken in full. Then he added: “Mendicants, learn the Āṭānāṭiya protection! Memorize the Āṭānāṭiya protection! Remember the Āṭānāṭiya protection! The Āṭānāṭiya protection is beneficial, and is for the guarding, protection, safety, and comfort of the monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said.

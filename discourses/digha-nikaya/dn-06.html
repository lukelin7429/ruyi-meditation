Dīgha Nikāya · Discourse 6

With Mahāli

Mahālisutta

Setting

Speakers

A frame story of visitors waiting for an audience, followed by two doctrinal episodes told in the Buddha’s own words

Why this discourse, sixth

A crowd gathers outside the Buddha’s dwelling because of a rumor about supernatural sightseeing — a monk named Sunakkhatta claims that meditative concentration let him see heavenly forms, though not hear heavenly sounds. It is exactly the kind of claim that could turn a spiritual path into a sideshow. The Buddha’s answer does two things at once: it explains, with almost technical precision, why a meditator might see without hearing or hear without seeing, and then it firmly redirects the whole conversation away from spectacle and toward the four real fruits of practice. The discourse closes with an old, still-unresolved question about the soul and the body — and a demonstration, not just an answer, of why that question eventually stops needing to be asked at all.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Meditative concentration is not cultivated so that a mendicant can see heavenly forms or hear heavenly sounds — those are just predictable side effects of how narrowly or broadly one directs a particular kind of immersion — but so that a mendicant can realize the four progressively deeper fruits of the path, stream-entry through arahantship, by way of the noble eightfold path; and once someone has walked that path all the way to its end, an old philosophical question like “is the soul the same as the body, or different?” simply stops being a question they would think to ask.

A crowd at the peaked-roof hall

The sutta opens with an unusually detailed piece of scene-setting for the Pali canon: a party of brahmin emissaries from Kosala and Magadha, already in Vesālī on business, hear of the Buddha’s reputation and go to see him, only to be told by his attendant Nāgita that it is the wrong time — the Buddha is on retreat. They sit down to wait. Then Oṭṭhaddha the Licchavi arrives with a large retinue of Licchavis and receives the identical answer, and he too sits down to wait. It takes a novice named Sīha, who notices the crowd and takes the initiative to ask the Buddha directly, before a seat is finally spread out and the Buddha comes out to receive everyone. It is a small, human moment before the philosophy begins: even access to a teacher this renowned depended on someone being willing to ask on other people’s behalf.

Sunakkhatta’s half-vision

Oṭṭhaddha, called “Mahāli” throughout the dialogue (apparently a family or clan name used as a form of address, distinct from the disciple named Mahāli who appears elsewhere in the canon), raises what a follower named Sunakkhatta had told him: after nearly three years living in dependence on the Buddha, Sunakkhatta could see pleasant, sensual, heavenly sights through meditation, but could not hear the corresponding heavenly sounds. Do such sounds even exist? The Buddha confirms that they do — Sunakkhatta simply hasn’t developed the concentration needed to hear them. He then lays out the underlying mechanics: a mendicant can develop immersion toward a given quarter of space in one aspect only (to see forms but not hear sounds, or to hear sounds but not see forms) or in both aspects at once, and the result matches exactly what was cultivated. There is nothing mystical left unexplained here — the outcome is simply a function of how the practice was aimed.

Not what the path is for

Oṭṭhaddha then asks the natural follow-up: is this — developing this kind of directional immersion — the reason mendicants live the spiritual life under the Buddha? The Buddha's answer is a flat no, followed by a redirection to what actually matters: the four fruits of the path. Ending three fetters makes one a stream-enterer, assured of eventual awakening. Ending those same three fetters and weakening greed, hate, and delusion makes one a once-returner, who will complete the path after at most one further human life. Ending the five lower fetters entirely produces a non-returner, who will reach final liberation elsewhere and never return to this realm. And realizing the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom, with the ending of all defilements, is arahantship itself. Asked whether there is a path to realize these things, the Buddha answers simply: the noble eightfold path.

An old question revisited

The Buddha then recalls an earlier occasion at Kosambī, when two wanderers — Muṇḍiya and Jāliya, the pupil of the wood-bowl ascetic — asked him directly whether the soul and the body are one and the same thing, or different things. Rather than answering the metaphysical question head-on, the Buddha walks them through the graduated training culminating in the four absorptions and, ultimately, complete liberation. At each stage before the last, he asks whether it would be fitting to assert “same” or “different” of a mendicant who has reached that point, and the wanderers agree that it would be fitting to ask — yet even so, the Buddha declines to assert either position. Only at the final stage, full liberation, does the question itself stop being one it would even be fitting to ask.

Why the question fades rather than gets answered

This is the discourse’s real payload, and it is easy to read past it. The Buddha never says “the soul and body are the same” or “the soul and body are different” at any point in the sequence — his silence on the metaphysics is constant throughout. What changes, stage by stage, is whether the question would even be a meaningful one to pose of the person practicing. Early on, when identity-view and the sense of a fixed self are still operating in the background, the question at least makes grammatical sense, even if the Buddha won’t answer it. By the time someone has directly realized complete liberation, the entire framework that made “same or different” sound like a sensible question to ask has itself dissolved. This is not the Buddha withholding an answer he secretly has — it is the demonstration that direct insight replaces the question with something other than an answer: the recognition that the dichotomy was never well-formed as a question about someone who has fully seen through it. Oṭṭhaddha’s satisfied approval, closing the sutta, suggests he grasped exactly this.

Key terms

samādhi — “immersion” or concentration; the discourse’s opening episode shows that samādhi can be directed narrowly (toward one sense-domain, one quarter of space) with correspondingly narrow results.
the four fruits — stream-enterer, once-returner, non-returner, and arahant: the four progressively complete stages of awakening the Buddha names as the actual reason for practicing, in place of any psychic byproduct.
ariya aṭṭhaṅgika magga — the noble eightfold path: right view, purpose, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, and immersion, given here as the sole practice for realizing the four fruits.
jīvaṁ … sarīraṁ — “the soul … the body”: one of the classic undeclared (avyākata) questions of the canon, revisited here and again, nearly verbatim, in the following discourse, DN 7.
“Mahāli” — the form of address the Buddha uses for Oṭṭhaddha the Licchavi throughout; readers should not confuse this Oṭṭhaddha with other figures named Mahāli elsewhere in the canon.

The text

The discourse in full: the frame story of the waiting crowd, Oṭṭhaddha’s question about Sunakkhatta, the Buddha’s explanation of directional immersion, the four fruits and the eightfold path, and the retold Kosambī conversation with Muṇḍiya and Jāliya. Ellipses (…) mark points where the original text abbreviates a formula spelled out in full elsewhere in the canon (chiefly DN 2). Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A crowd waits at the peaked-roof hall

§1–4So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Vesālī, at the Great Wood, in the hall with the peaked roof. Now at that time several brahmin emissaries from Kosala and Magadha were residing in Vesālī on some business. They heard: “It seems the ascetic Gotama—a Sakyan, gone forth from a Sakyan family—is staying near Vesālī, at the Great Wood, in the hall with the peaked roof. He has this good reputation: ‘That Blessed One is perfected, a fully awakened Buddha, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, holy, knower of the world, supreme guide for those fit for training, teacher of gods and humans, awakened, blessed.’ He has realized with his own insight this world—with its gods, Māras, and divinities, this population with its ascetics and brahmins, gods and humans—and he makes it known to others. He proclaims a teaching that’s good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, meaningful and well-phrased. And he reveals a spiritual practice that’s entirely full and pure. It’s good to see such perfected ones.” Then they went to the hall with the peaked roof in the Great Wood to see the Buddha. Now, at that time Venerable Nāgita was the Buddha’s attendant. The brahmin emissaries went up to him and said, “Worthy Nāgita, where is the worthy Gotama at present? For we want to see him.” “It’s the wrong time to see the Buddha; he is on retreat.” So right there the brahmin emissaries sat down to one side, thinking, “We’ll go only after we’ve seen the worthy Gotama.”

Oṭṭhaddha the Licchavi together with a large assembly of Licchavis also approached Nāgita at the hall with the peaked roof. He bowed, stood to one side, and said to Nāgita, “Worthy Nāgita, where is the Blessed One at present, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha? For we want to see him.” “It’s the wrong time to see the Buddha; he is on retreat.” So right there Oṭṭhaddha also sat down to one side, thinking, “I’ll go only after I’ve seen the Blessed One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha.” Then the novice Sīha approached Nāgita. He bowed, stood to one side, and said to Nāgita, “Honorable Kassapa, these several brahmin emissaries from Kosala and Magadha, and also Oṭṭhaddha the Licchavi together with a large assembly of Licchavis, have come here to see the Buddha. It’d be good if these people got to see the Buddha.” “Well then, Sīha, tell the Buddha yourself.” “Yes, sir,” replied Sīha. He went to the Buddha, bowed, stood to one side, and told him of the people waiting to see him, adding: “Sir, it’d be good if these people got to see the Buddha.” “Well then, Sīha, spread out a seat in the shade of the dwelling.” “Yes, sir,” replied Sīha, and he did so. Then the Buddha came out of his dwelling and sat in the shade of the dwelling on the seat spread out.

Sunakkhatta’s half-vision

§5–11Then the brahmin emissaries went up to the Buddha, and exchanged greetings with him. When the greetings and polite conversation were over, they sat down to one side. Oṭṭhaddha the Licchavi together with a large assembly of Licchavis also went up to the Buddha, bowed, and sat down to one side. Oṭṭhaddha said to the Buddha, “Sir, a few days ago Sunakkhatta the Licchavi came to me and said: ‘Mahāli, soon I will have been living in dependence on the Buddha for three years. I see heavenly sights that are pleasant, sensual, and arousing, but I don’t hear heavenly sounds that are pleasant, sensual, and arousing.’ The heavenly sounds that Sunakkhatta cannot hear: do such sounds really exist or not?” “Such sounds really do exist, but Sunakkhatta cannot hear them.” “What is the cause, sir, what is the reason why Sunakkhatta cannot hear them, even though they really do exist?” “Mahāli, take a mendicant who has developed immersion to the eastern quarter in one aspect: so as to see heavenly sights but not to hear heavenly sounds. When they have developed immersion for that purpose, they see heavenly sights but don’t hear heavenly sounds. Why is that? Because that is how it is for a mendicant who develops immersion in that way. Furthermore, take a mendicant who has developed immersion to the southern quarter in one aspect … western quarter … northern quarter … above, below, across … That is how it is for a mendicant who develops immersion in that way. Take a mendicant who has developed immersion to the eastern quarter in one aspect: so as to hear heavenly sounds but not to see heavenly sights. When they have developed immersion for that purpose, they hear heavenly sounds but don’t see heavenly sights. Why is that? Because that is how it is for a mendicant who develops immersion in that way. Furthermore, take a mendicant who has developed immersion to the southern quarter in one aspect … western quarter … northern quarter … above, below, across … That is how it is for a mendicant who develops immersion in that way. Take a mendicant who has developed immersion to the eastern quarter in both aspects: so as to hear heavenly sounds and see heavenly sights. When they have developed immersion for that purpose, they see heavenly sights and hear heavenly sounds. Why is that? Because that is how it is for a mendicant who develops immersion in that way. Furthermore, take a mendicant who has developed immersion to the southern quarter in both aspects … western quarter … northern quarter … above, below, across … That is how it is for a mendicant who develops immersion in that way. This is the cause, Mahāli, this is the reason why Sunakkhatta cannot hear heavenly sounds that are pleasant, sensual, and arousing, even though they really do exist.”

Not what the path is for

§12–14“Surely the mendicants must lead the spiritual life under the Buddha for the sake of realizing such a development of immersion?” “No, Mahāli, the mendicants don’t lead the spiritual life under me for the sake of realizing such a development of immersion. There are other things that are finer, for the sake of which the mendicants lead the spiritual life under me.” “But sir, what are those finer things?” “Firstly, Mahāli, with the ending of three fetters a mendicant is a stream-enterer, not liable to be reborn in the underworld, assured, destined for awakening. This is one of the finer things for the sake of which the mendicants lead the spiritual life under me. Furthermore, a mendicant—with the ending of three fetters, and the weakening of greed, hate, and delusion—is a once-returner. They come back to this world once only, then make an end of suffering. This too is one of the finer things. Furthermore, with the ending of the five lower fetters, a mendicant is reborn spontaneously and will become extinguished there, not liable to return from that world. This too is one of the finer things. Furthermore, a mendicant has realized the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom in this very life, and lives having realized it with their own insight due to the ending of defilements. This too is one of the finer things. These are the finer things, for the sake of which the mendicants lead the spiritual life under me.” “But, sir, is there a path and a practice for realizing these things?” “There is, Mahāli.” “Well, what is it?” “It is simply this noble eightfold path, that is: right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion. This is the path and the practice for realizing these things.

An old question revisited

§15–19This one time, Mahāli, I was staying near Kosambī, in Ghosita’s Monastery. Then two renunciates—the wanderer Muṇḍiya and Jāliya, the pupil of the wood-bowl ascetic—came and exchanged greetings with me. When the greetings and polite conversation were over, they stood to one side and said to me: ‘Reverend Gotama, are the soul and the body one and the same, or is the soul one thing, the body another?’ ‘Well then, reverends, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.’ ‘Yes, reverend,’ they replied. I said this: ‘Take the case when a Realized One arises in the world, perfected, a fully awakened Buddha … That’s how a mendicant is accomplished in ethics. … They enter and remain in the first absorption. When a mendicant knows and sees like this, would it be appropriate to say of them: “The soul and the body are one and the same” or “The soul is one thing, the body another”?’ ‘It would, reverend.’ ‘But reverends, I know and see like this. Nevertheless, I do not say: “The soul and the body are one and the same” or “The soul and the body are different things”. … They enter and remain in the second absorption … third absorption … fourth absorption. When a mendicant knows and sees like this, would it be appropriate to say of them: “The soul and the body are one and the same” or “The soul is one thing, the body another”?’ ‘It would, reverend.’ ‘But reverends, I know and see like this. Nevertheless, I do not say: “The soul and the body are one and the same” or “The soul is one thing, the body another”. … They project and extend the mind toward knowledge and vision … When a mendicant knows and sees like this, would it be appropriate to say of them: “The soul and the body are one and the same” or “The soul is one thing, the body another”?’ ‘It would, reverend.’ ‘But reverends, I know and see like this. Nevertheless, I do not say: “The soul and the body are one and the same” or “The soul is one thing, the body another”. … They understand: “… there is nothing further for this place.” When a mendicant knows and sees like this, would it be appropriate to say of them: “The soul and the body are one and the same” or “The soul is one thing, the body another”?’ ‘It would not, reverend.’ ‘But reverends, I know and see like this. Nevertheless, I do not say: “The soul and the body are one and the same” or “The soul is one thing, the body another”.’” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, Oṭṭhaddha the Licchavi approved what the Buddha said.

Dīgha Nikāya · Discourse 7

With Jāliya

Jāliyasutta

Setting

Why this discourse, seventh

This is the shortest sutta in the entire Sīlakkhandhavagga — and it exists, essentially, because two more people asked the question that closed the previous discourse. Muṇḍiya and Jāliya want to know whether the soul and the body are the same thing or different things, and the Buddha gives them, almost word for word, the same graduated answer he had earlier described giving to these same two wanderers at this same monastery, as recounted within DN 6. Reading it right after DN 6 is less like encountering new material and more like watching a formula get applied a second time, which is exactly the point: this was a stock answer to a stock question, delivered the same way whenever it came up.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

Asked directly whether the soul is the same as the body or a different thing, the Buddha responds not with a metaphysical ruling but with the graduated path itself — ethics, the four absorptions, and final liberation — showing, stage by stage, that the question stops being one worth asking once someone has directly seen through to the end of it.

The same two wanderers, the same question

DN 6 ends with the Buddha recalling a past conversation at Kosambī with two renunciates, the wanderer Muṇḍiya and Jāliya (a pupil of the so-called wood-bowl ascetic), who had asked him whether the soul and the body are one thing or different things. DN 7 is that very conversation, told this time in its own right rather than as a story within a story: Muṇḍiya and Jāliya approach the Buddha at Ghosita’s Monastery near Kosambī and ask their question directly.

The same answer, almost verbatim

What follows in DN 7 is, sentence for sentence, essentially identical to the account already given secondhand in DN 6: the Buddha walks the questioners through a mendicant’s training in ethics, then the four progressive absorptions, then the final attainment of liberation, pausing after each stage to ask whether it would be “appropriate” to say “the soul and body are the same” or “the soul and body are different” of a mendicant who has reached that point. At every stage but the last, the wanderers agree it would be appropriate to ask — and at every stage, including the last, the Buddha declines to assert either position. Only when a mendicant reaches the final attainment, “there is nothing further for this place,” do the wanderers themselves agree that the question would no longer even be appropriate to pose.

Why the repetition itself is worth noticing

It would be easy to treat DN 7 as redundant, since DN 6 already reports its content. But the duplication is itself informative about how these texts were composed and transmitted: the graduated non-answer to “same or different” was clearly a fixed, memorized unit of teaching, reused whenever the question came up, in whichever conversation it happened to arise. Seeing it appear twice, once embedded as a memory inside another discourse and once as a freestanding encounter, is a rare chance to watch the oral-formulaic character of these texts directly: the same block of teaching, deployed twice, word for word, because it was understood to be the correct and complete response regardless of who was asking.

What the question really was

“Is the soul the same as the body, or different?” belongs to a well-known family of philosophical questions in the wanderers’ milieu of the Buddha’s day, alongside questions about whether the cosmos is eternal or finite, and whether a fully awakened being exists after death. The Buddha’s characteristic response to this whole family of questions, seen again in DN 9, is not to defend a competing metaphysical position but to point out that they presuppose a fixed, nameable thing called “the soul” whose relationship to the body could then be settled one way or the other. Once someone has directly realized the end of clinging through the complete path, that presupposition simply has nothing left to attach to — not because the question was answered, but because the framework that made it feel like a real question no longer applies to what has been seen.

Key terms

jīvaṁ … sarīraṁ — “the soul … the body”: the question this entire sutta exists to address, one of the classic undeclared (avyākata) points of ancient Indian philosophical debate.
Muṇḍiya and Jāliya — the two wanderers who ask the question here in person, having already been mentioned by name in the Buddha’s retelling at the close of DN 6.
the wood-bowl ascetic — Jāliya’s teacher, otherwise unidentified in this text; his “pupil” designation is simply how Jāliya is introduced.
stock formula — a fixed, memorized block of teaching reused across different discourses; this sutta and the end of DN 6 preserve the same one twice, a useful window into how these texts were composed orally.

The text

A question at Ghosita’s Monastery

§1So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Kosambī, in Ghosita’s Monastery. Now at that time two renunciates—the wanderer Muṇḍiya and Jāliya, the pupil of the wood-bowl ascetic—came to the Buddha and exchanged greetings with him. When the greetings and polite conversation were over, they stood to one side and said to the Buddha, “Reverend Gotama, are the soul and the body one and the same, or is the soul one thing, the body another?” “Well then, reverends, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, reverend,” they replied. The graduated answer

§2–5“Take the case when a Realized One arises in the world, perfected, a fully awakened Buddha … That’s how a mendicant is accomplished in ethics. … They enter and remain in the first absorption … When a mendicant knows and sees like this, would it be appropriate to say of them: ‘The soul and the body are one and the same’ or ‘The soul is one thing, the body another’?” “It would, reverend.” “But reverends, I know and see like this. Nevertheless, I do not say: ‘The soul and the body are one and the same’ or ‘The soul is one thing, the body another’. … They enter and remain in the second absorption … third absorption … fourth absorption. When a mendicant knows and sees like this, would it be appropriate to say of them: ‘The soul and the body are one and the same’ or ‘The soul is one thing, the body another’?” “It would, reverend.” “But reverends, I know and see like this. Nevertheless, I do not say: ‘The soul and the body are one and the same’ or ‘The soul is one thing, the body another’. … They project and extend the mind toward knowledge and vision … When a mendicant knows and sees like this, would it be appropriate to say of them: ‘The soul and the body are one and the same’ or ‘The soul is one thing, the body another’?” “It would, reverend.” “But reverends, I know and see like this. Nevertheless, I do not say: ‘The soul and the body are one and the same’ or ‘The soul is one thing, the body another’. … They understand: ‘… there is nothing further for this place.’ When a mendicant knows and sees like this, would it be appropriate to say of them: ‘The soul and the body are one and the same’ or ‘The soul is one thing, the body another’?” “It would not, reverend.” “But reverends, I know and see like this. Nevertheless, I do not say: ‘The soul and the body are one and the same’ or ‘The soul is one thing, the body another’.” That is what the Buddha said. Satisfied, the two renunciates approved what the Buddha said.

Dīgha Nikāya · Discourse 31

Advice to Sigālaka

Siṅgālasutta

Setting
Near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground — encountered by the Buddha on his morning alms round
Speakers
The Buddha and Sigālaka, a householder’s son
Form
An inherited ritual observed in the street, then given an entirely new ethical meaning, in prose interspersed with summarizing verse
Length
~14 minutes to read
Northern parallel
A parallel exists in the Chinese Dīrgha-āgama at DĀ 16 (T1), alongside two further independent Chinese translations (T16, T17) — one of the best-attested lay-ethics texts to survive in Chinese
Difficulty
★★☆☆☆ — concrete and practical, among the most accessible discourses in the whole canon

Why this discourse, thirty-first

This is one of the most quietly important discourses in the entire canon — not because it describes an exalted meditative attainment, but because it is, almost uniquely, addressed from beginning to end to the concerns of an ordinary householder. Sigālaka is not a monk, not a philosopher, not even especially devout; he is a young man performing a family ritual he does not understand. Rather than dismiss the ritual or demand he take up a spiritual path instead, the Buddha does something more interesting: he keeps the ritual’s outward form — six directions, each greeted in turn — and replaces its content entirely, turning a gesture of unexamined piety into the most complete statement anywhere in the early texts of what one ordinary person owes another. Employers and employees, spouses, parents and children, teachers and students, friends, and the lay community’s relationship to renunciants all receive their own precise, reciprocal accounting. Its distinction between four false friends and four true ones remains, in translation after translation, one of the most quoted passages in the whole Pali canon.

Reading guide

The teaching in one sentence

A young man ritually worships six directions of the compass because his father told him to; the Buddha keeps the six directions but replaces the worship with something far more demanding — a complete, reciprocal ethics covering every important relationship in an ordinary life, from parents and children to employers and workers to the lay community’s support of renunciants.

A ritual without its reason

Sigālaka, a householder’s son, rises early each morning, and with wet clothes and wet hair — having presumably just bathed — raises his cupped hands in reverence to the six directions: east, south, west, north, below, and above. He is doing this, he explains when the Buddha finds him at it on his alms round, because his father said so on his deathbed: “My dear, please revere the quarters.” Sigālaka is honoring his father’s last instruction faithfully and precisely — and understanding none of it. The Buddha does not tell him the ritual is worthless. He tells him it is being done wrong: “that’s not how the six directions should be revered in the training of the Noble One.” Asked to explain the correct way, the Buddha delivers what is, in effect, a full course in lay ethics.

Fourteen things left behind

Before naming the six directions, the Buddha specifies a foundation: a noble disciple “covers the six directions” only after giving up four corrupt deeds, avoiding wrongdoing on four grounds, and avoiding six drains on wealth — fourteen things in total. The four corrupt deeds are the familiar core of lay ethical conduct: killing living creatures, stealing, sexual misconduct, and lying. The four grounds are the psychological roots from which any bad deed grows: acting out of favoritism, hostility, stupidity, or cowardice — a useful diagnostic quite apart from any specific rule, since almost any lapse in conduct can be traced back to one of these four.

Six ways a fortune disappears

The six drains on wealth are treated at unusual length and with real social specificity: habitual drinking, roaming the streets at night, frequenting festivals and shows, gambling, keeping bad company, and laziness. For each, the text lists concrete drawbacks rather than abstract condemnation — drinking brings immediate loss of money, promotes quarrels, and invites illness and disrepute; gambling breeds enmity in the winner and grief in the loser, and “no-one wants to marry a gambler”; laziness is diagnosed with a wry, exact precision (“It’s too cold! It’s too hot. It’s too late! It’s too early!”) that reads as freshly recognizable now as it must have twenty-five centuries ago.

Four false friends, four true ones

The discourse’s single most quoted passage distinguishes four kinds of friend who are really enemies in disguise from four kinds of friend who are genuinely good-hearted, each defined by four concrete behavioral tests rather than by any vague sentiment:

Four false friendsHow to recognize them
The takerends up with your possessions; gives little, expects a lot; helps out of fear; associates only for personal advantage
The talkerhospitable about the past and the future, full of meaningless pleasantries, but pleads misfortune the moment something needs doing now
The flattererbacks you whether you do good or bad; praises you to your face and puts you down behind your back
The spenderonly ever your companion in drinking, roaming at night, festivals, and gambling
Four true friendsHow to recognize them
The helperguards you and your property when you are careless; is a refuge in danger; provides double what is needed when something must be done
The one constant in good times and badtells you secrets, keeps yours, never abandons you in trouble, would give their life for your welfare
The counselorkeeps you from wrongdoing, supports you in what is good, teaches what you don’t know, explains the way to a good rebirth
The sympathetic onedoesn’t delight in your misfortune, delights in your success, stops others criticizing you, encourages praise of you

The six directions, reinterpreted

Only now does the Buddha give the ritual its new content. The six directions are recast as six categories of relationship, each carrying duties running both ways — not a hierarchy where only the junior party owes service, but a genuine exchange:

DirectionRelationshipDuties (five each way)
EastParents ↔ childrenChildren support their parents, maintain the family line, and honor the inheritance and its memory; parents in turn keep children from wrongdoing, support them in what is good, train them in a livelihood, arrange a suitable marriage, and pass on the inheritance in time
SouthTeachers ↔ studentsStudents serve their teacher with respect and attentive learning; teachers educate them thoroughly, introduce them to colleagues, and provide protection in every quarter
WestSpouse ↔ spouseA husband honors his wife, remains faithful, and shares authority and property with her; a wife manages the household well, remains faithful, and is deft and tireless in her duties
NorthFriends ↔ friendsGenerosity, kind words, mutual concern, treating each other as equals, and honesty run in both directions
NadirEmployers ↔ workersAn employer assigns work suited to ability, pays fairly, cares for the sick, shares treats, and gives proper time off; workers rise early, finish late, take nothing not given, work well, and uphold a good reputation
ZenithLay supporters ↔ renunciantsLay people act, speak, and think toward ascetics and brahmins with love, keep an open door, and provide material needs; renunciants in turn keep them from wrongdoing, support what is good, teach what they don’t know, and explain the way to a good rebirth
Not a hierarchy of service. Every direction runs both ways. A householder’s duty to a teacher, a spouse, an employee, or a renunciant is matched, item for item, by an equal and specific duty running back — this reciprocity is the discourse’s entire ethical architecture.

A conversion on the spot

Sigālaka’s response uses the same formula found throughout the canon for a listener whose confusion has genuinely lifted, and he goes for refuge as a lay follower “from this day forth.” What began as a puzzled young man performing an inherited rite he could not explain ends as a fully articulated code for living well among other people — arguably the most complete such code the Buddha is recorded giving to any single layperson in one sitting.

Key terms

chalabhidisā — “the six directions”: east, south, west, north, below (nadir), and above (zenith), the ritual object Sigālaka worships and the Buddha reinterprets as six categories of human relationship.
Siṅgālaka — the householder’s son whose sincere but uncomprehending performance of his late father’s instruction becomes the occasion for the entire discourse.
the fourteen things left behind — four corrupt deeds (killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying), four grounds of wrongdoing (favoritism, hostility, stupidity, cowardice), and six drains on wealth (drink, night-roaming, festivals, gambling, bad friends, laziness) — the ethical foundation beneath the six directions.
the four false, four true — the discourse’s most frequently quoted passage: the taker, the talker, the flatterer, and the spender, set against the helper, the constant companion, the counselor, and the sympathetic friend.
reciprocity — the structural principle behind every one of the six directions: each relationship carries duties running both ways, never obligation flowing only from junior to senior.

The text

The discourse in full, including all fourteen preliminary items, the four false and four true friends, all six directions in complete detail, and the closing verses. Translation: Bhikkhu Sujato (CC0, SuttaCentral).

A ritual without its reason

§1–2So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground. Now at that time the householder’s son Sigālaka rose early and left Rājagaha. With his clothes and hair all wet, he raised his cupped palms to revere the various quarters — east, south, west, north, below, and above.

Then the Buddha robed up in the morning and, taking his bowl and robe, entered Rājagaha for alms. He saw Sigālaka revering the various quarters and said to him, “Householder’s son, why are you revering the various quarters in this way?” “Sir, on his deathbed my father said to me: ‘My dear, please revere the quarters.’ Honoring, respecting, and venerating my father’s words, I rose early and left Rājagaha and, with my clothes and hair all wet, raised my cupped palms to revere the various quarters — east, south, west, north, below, and above.”

“Householder’s son, that’s not how the six directions should be revered in the training of the Noble One.” “But sir, how should the six directions be revered in the training of the Noble One? Sir, please teach me this.” “Well then, householder’s son, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, sir,” replied Sigālaka. The Buddha said this:

Four corrupt deeds, four grounds, six drains on wealth

§3–4“Householder’s son, a noble disciple gives up four corrupt deeds, doesn’t do bad deeds on four grounds, and avoids six drains on wealth. When they’ve left these fourteen bad things behind they have the six directions covered. They’re practicing to win in both worlds, and they succeed in this world and the next. When their body breaks up, after death, they’re reborn in a good place, a heavenly realm.

What four corrupt deeds have they given up? Killing living creatures, stealing, sexual misconduct, and lying: these are corrupt deeds. These are the four corrupt deeds they’ve given up.” That is what the Buddha said. Then the Holy One, the Teacher, went on to say: “Killing, stealing, telling lies, and committing adultery: astute people don’t praise these things.”

§5–6“On what four grounds do they not do bad deeds? One does bad deeds prejudiced by favoritism, hostility, stupidity, and cowardice. When a noble disciple is not prejudiced by favoritism, hostility, stupidity, and cowardice, they don’t do bad deeds on these four grounds.” That is what the Buddha said. Then the Holy One, the Teacher, went on to say: “If you act against the teaching out of favoritism, hostility, cowardice, or stupidity, your fame shrinks, like the moon in the waning fortnight. If you don’t act against the teaching out of favoritism, hostility, cowardice, and stupidity, your fame swells, like the moon in the waxing fortnight.”

§7–13“What six drains on wealth do they avoid? Habitually engaging in the following things is a drain on wealth: intoxicants of beer, wine, and liquor; roaming the streets at night; frequenting festivals; gambling; bad friends; laziness.

There are these six drawbacks of habitually consuming intoxicants of beer, wine, and liquor: immediate loss of wealth, promotion of quarrels, susceptibility to illness, disrepute, indecent exposure, and weakened wisdom.

There are these six drawbacks of roaming the streets at night: yourself, your partners and children, and your property are all left unguarded; you’re suspected of bad deeds; untrue rumors spread about you; you’re at the forefront of many things that entail suffering.

There are these six drawbacks of frequenting festivals: you’re always thinking, ‘Where’s the dancing? Where’s the singing? Where’s the music? Where are the stories? Where’s the applause? Where are the kettledrums?’

There are these six drawbacks of habitually gambling: victory breeds enmity; the loser mourns their money; there is immediate loss of wealth; a gambler’s word carries no weight in public assembly; friends and colleagues treat them with contempt; and no-one wants to marry a gambler, for they think, ‘This individual is a gambler — they’re not able to support a partner.’

There are these six drawbacks of bad friends: you become friends and companions with those who are addicts, carousers, drunkards, frauds, swindlers, and thugs.

There are these six drawbacks of habitual laziness: you don’t get your work done because you think, ‘It’s too cold! It’s too hot. It’s too late! It’s too early! I’m too hungry! I’m too full!’ By dwelling on so many excuses for not working, you don’t make any more money, and the money you already have runs out.” That is what the Buddha said. Then the Holy One, the Teacher, went on to say:

“Some are just drinking buddies, some call you their dear, dear friend, but a true friend is one who stands by you in need. Sleeping late, adultery, making enemies, harmfulness, bad friends, and avarice: these six grounds ruin a person. With bad friends, bad comrades, bad behavior and alms-resort, a man falls to ruin in both this world and the next. Dice, women, toddy, song and dance; sleeping by day and roaming at night; bad friends, and avarice: these six grounds ruin a person. They play dice and drink beer, and consort with women loved by others. Associating with the worse, not the better, they diminish like the waning moon. One who is broke and destitute due to toddy, thirsty, drinking in the bar, drowning in debt, will quickly lose their way. When you’re in the habit of sleeping late, seeing night as time to rise, and always getting drunk, you can’t keep up the household life. ‘Too cold, too hot, too late,’ they say. When the young neglect their work like this, riches pass them by. But one who considers heat and cold as no more than blades of grass — he does his duties as a man, and happiness never fails.”

Fake friends

§15–20“Householder’s son, you should recognize these four enemies disguised as friends: the taker, the talker, the flatterer, the spender.

You can recognize a fake friend who’s all take on four grounds: your possessions end up theirs; giving little, they expect a lot; they do their duty out of fear; they associate for their own advantage.

You can recognize a fake friend who’s all talk on four grounds: they’re hospitable in the past, hospitable in the future, and full of meaningless pleasantries; but when something needs doing in the present, they point to their own misfortune.

You can recognize a fake friend who’s a flatterer on four grounds: they support you equally in doing bad and doing good; they praise you to your face, and put you down behind your back.

You can recognize a fake friend who’s a spender on four grounds: they accompany you when drinking, roaming the streets at night, frequenting festivals, and gambling.” That is what the Buddha said. Then the Holy One, the Teacher, went on to say: “One friend is all take, another all talk; one’s just a flatterer, and one’s a comrade who spends. An astute person understands these four enemies for what they are and keeps them at a distance, as they’d shun a risky road.”

Good-hearted friends

§21–26“Householder’s son, you should recognize these four good-hearted friends: the helper, the friend in good times and bad, the counselor, and the one who’s sympathetic.

You can recognize a good-hearted friend who’s a helper on four grounds: they guard you and your property when you’re negligent; they keep you safe in times of danger; when something needs doing, they provide you with twice the money you need.

You can recognize a good-hearted friend who’s the same in good times and bad on four grounds: they tell you secrets and keep yours; they don’t abandon you in times of trouble; they’d even give their life for your welfare.

You can recognize a good-hearted friend who’s a counselor on four grounds: they keep you from doing bad; they support you in doing good; they teach you what you do not know; they explain the path to heaven.

You can recognize a good-hearted friend who’s sympathetic on four grounds: they don’t delight in your misfortune; they delight in your good fortune; they keep others from criticizing you; they encourage praise of you.” The Buddha spoke this matter. Then the Holy One, the Teacher, went on to say:

“A friend who’s a helper, one the same in both pleasure and pain, a friend of good counsel, and one of sympathy; an astute person understands these four friends for what they are and carefully looks after them, like a mother the child at her breast. The astute and virtuous shine like a burning flame. They pick up riches as bees roaming round pick up pollen. And their riches proceed to grow, like a termite mound piling up. In gathering wealth like this, a householder does enough for their family. And they’d hold on to friends by dividing their wealth in four: one portion is to enjoy, two parts invest in work, and the fourth should be kept for times of trouble.”

Covering the six directions

§27“And how, householder’s son, does a noble disciple cover the six directions? These six directions should be recognized: parents as the east, tutors as the south, partner and children as the west, friends and colleagues as the north, bondservants and workers as beneath, and ascetics and brahmins as above.

§28A child should serve their parents as the eastern quarter in five ways, thinking: ‘I will support those who supported me. I’ll do my duty for them. I’ll maintain the family lineage. I’ll take care of the inheritance. When they have passed away, I’ll make an offering on their behalf.’ Parents served by the children in these five ways show sympathy to them in five ways: they keep them from doing bad, support them in doing good, train them in a profession, connect them with a suitable partner, and transfer the inheritance in due time. And that’s how the eastern quarter is covered, kept safe and free of peril.

§29A pupil should serve their tutor as the southern quarter in five ways: by rising for them, by serving them, by listening well, by looking after them, and by carefully learning their profession. Tutors served by their pupils in these five ways show sympathy to them in five ways: they make sure they’re well educated and well drilled in memorization; they clearly explain all the knowledge of the profession; they introduce them to their friends and colleagues; they provide protection in every region. And that’s how the southern quarter is covered, kept safe and free of peril.

§30A husband should serve his wife as the western quarter in five ways: by treating her with honor, by not looking down on her, by not being unfaithful, by relinquishing authority to her, and by presenting her with adornments. A wife served by her husband in these five ways shows sympathy to him in five ways: she’s well-organized in her work, manages the domestic help, is not unfaithful, preserves his earnings, and is deft and tireless in all her duties. And that’s how the western quarter is covered, kept safe and free of peril.

§31A gentleman should serve his friends and colleagues as the northern quarter in five ways: giving, kindly words, taking care, equality, and not using tricky words. Friends and colleagues served by a gentleman in these five ways show sympathy to him in five ways: they guard him and his property when they’re negligent; they keep him safe in times of danger; they don’t abandon him in times of trouble; they honor his descendants. And that’s how the northern quarter is covered, kept safe and free of peril.

§32A master should serve their bondservants and workers as the lower quarter in five ways: by organizing work according to ability, by paying food and wages, by nursing them when sick, by sharing special treats, and by giving time off work. Bondservants and workers served by a master in these five ways show sympathy to him in five ways: they get up first and go to bed last; they don’t steal; they do their work well; and they promote a good reputation. And that’s how the lower quarter is covered, kept safe and free of peril.

§33A gentleman should serve ascetics and brahmins as the upper quarter in five ways: by loving deeds of body, speech, and mind, by not turning them away at the gate, and by providing them with material needs. Ascetics and brahmins served by a gentleman in these five ways show sympathy to him in six ways: they keep him from doing bad; they support him in doing good; they think of him with kindly thoughts; they teach him what he does not know; they clarify what he’s already learned; they explain the path to heaven. And that’s how the upper quarter is covered, kept safe and free of peril.” The Buddha spoke this matter. Then the Holy One, the Teacher, went on to say:

§34“Parents are the east, tutors the south, wives and child the west, friends and colleagues the north, servants and workers below, and ascetics and brahmins above. By honoring these quarters a householder does enough for their family. The astute and the virtuous, the gentle and the articulate, the placid in manner and the amenable: they’re the kind who win glory. The diligent, not lazy, those not disturbed by troubles, those consistent in conduct, the intelligent: they’re the kind who win glory. The inclusive, the makers of friends, the bountiful, those rid of stinginess, those who lead, train, and persuade: they’re the kind who win glory. Giving and kindly words, taking care here, and treating equally in worldly conditions, as they deserve in each case; these ways of being inclusive in the world are like a moving chariot’s linchpin. If there were no such ways of being inclusive, neither mother nor father would be respected and honored for what they’ve done for their children. But since these ways of being inclusive do exist, the astute do regard them well, so they achieve greatness and are praised.”

A conversion on the spot

§35When this was said, Sigālaka the householder’s son said to the Buddha, “Excellent, sir! Excellent! As if he were righting the overturned, or revealing the hidden, or pointing out the path to the lost, or lighting a lamp in the dark so people with clear eyes can see what’s there, the Buddha has made the teaching clear in many ways. I go for refuge to the Buddha, to the teaching, and to the mendicant Saṅgha. From this day forth, may the Buddha remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.”

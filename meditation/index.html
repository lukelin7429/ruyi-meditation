Begin to Meditate · 靜坐入門

Learning to sit, in plain English.

You don't need incense, a robe, or a quiet mind to start. You need a place to sit, a few minutes, and a willingness to begin again each time you drift. This is the whole of it — the posture, the breath, and why it's worth doing — taught the way the Buddha taught it.

For complete beginners Posture The breath Rooted in the suttas
Why sit at all

What meditation is for.

Meditation is not about forcing the mind blank or escaping your life. It is training in attention — learning to see what's happening, clearly and without panic. Everything else grows from that.

A body that settles

Sitting still and watching the breath calms the nervous system — the heart slows, the shoulders drop, the constant bracing eases off.

A mind you can steady

Attention is a muscle. Each time you notice you've wandered and come back, you strengthen it — until you can hold your focus where you choose.

Room around your feelings

You learn to feel anger, worry, or grief without being swept away by them — meeting emotion with a little space instead of a reflex.

Clearer seeing

A quiet, steady mind is the ground the Buddha's whole path rests on — the calm from which real insight into ourselves becomes possible.

The practical basics.

The posture · 坐姿

Seven points, from the ground up.

A good posture is alert and relaxed at once — awake, but not tense. Set it once at the start, then let it hold you. Work up from the base.

Steady as a mountain

  1. Seat · 坐墊

    Sit on a cushion with your hips a little higher than your knees, so your back can stay upright on its own. On a chair, sit forward, both feet flat on the floor.

  2. Legs · 盤腿

    Cross your legs loosely — either ankle in front of the other is fine. No lotus required, and no strain. On a chair, simply keep your feet grounded and parallel.

  3. Back · 脊背

    Straight but not stiff. Picture the spine as a stack of coins, rising naturally. Keep the small curve at the lower back — don't tuck or slump.

  4. Hands · 手

    Rest them in your lap, right hand cupped in the left, thumb-tips lightly touching — or simply lay both palms down on your knees. Let the arms hang loose.

  5. Shoulders & chest · 肩胸

    Roll the shoulders gently back and down to open the chest, then let them soften. Relaxed and broad, never hunched toward the screen you just left.

  6. Head & chin · 頭頸

    Lengthen the back of the neck and tuck the chin a hair, as if the crown were lifted by a thread. The head floats level, balanced on top of the spine.

  7. Eyes, jaw & breath · 眼息

    Lower the eyes softly — half-closed, gaze resting on the floor a few feet ahead, or fully closed if that's steadier. Unclench the jaw, rest the tongue behind the upper teeth, and breathe through the nose.

The breath · 觀息

Now, just watch the breath.

This is the oldest meditation the Buddha taught — ānāpānasati, mindfulness of breathing. The instructions are almost embarrassingly simple. The simplicity is the point.

First

Don't control it

Breathe naturally through the nose. You're not doing a breathing exercise — you're only noticing a breath that's already happening on its own. Long or short, deep or shallow: leave it be.

Then

Pick one spot

Choose a single place to feel it — the cool touch at the tip of the nose, or the rise and fall of the belly. Keep your attention resting there, at that one spot, for the whole sit.

Optionally

Count to ten

To steady a busy mind, count each out-breath: one… two… up to ten, then start again at one. Lose count? Just begin again at one. Losing count is not failing — it's the practice showing you where the mind went.

Always

Come back, gently

Your mind will wander — into plans, memories, the shopping list. The moment you notice, that's a moment of waking up. Without scolding yourself, set the thought down and return to the breath.

The wandering is not the problem. Noticing you wandered, and coming back — that is the meditation.

Your first sit

A ten-minute session.

Set a timer for ten minutes and move through these six stages. After a week it will feel like second nature — you'll drop the map and simply sit.

  1. Settle in 1 min

    Take your posture, point by point. Then three slow, deliberate breaths to arrive — a little longer on each out-breath.

  2. Feel the body 1 min

    Sense the weight of you on the seat, the points of contact, the hands in the lap. Let the body grow heavy and still.

  3. Find the breath 30 sec

    Bring your attention to your one chosen spot. Catch the very next in-breath, and stay with it.

  4. Follow & count 6 min

    Watch each breath in and out, counting to ten and beginning again. Each time the mind slips off, walk it back — as many times as it takes.

  5. Rest open 1 min

    Drop the counting. Just sit, aware, letting breath and body simply be — nothing to do now but be present.

  6. Close with good will 30 sec

    Wish the calm you've found for someone else too. Then open the eyes slowly, and carry a little of the stillness into the rest of your day.

When it gets hard

Common difficulties.

Everyone meets these. They aren't signs you're doing it wrong — they're the very material of the practice.

"My mind won't stop."

It won't — and it doesn't have to. You're not trying to empty the mind or stop thoughts. You're training one small move: noticing you've drifted, and returning. A busy mind simply gives you more chances to practise that move.

I keep getting sleepy.

Drowsiness is common, especially early morning or after meals. Sit up a little taller, lift the gaze and open the eyes wider, take a few brighter breaths — or simply meditate earlier and shorten the sit. Better ten alert minutes than twenty drowsy ones.

I'm too restless to sit still.

Don't fight the restlessness — turn toward it. Name it ("restlessness"), feel where it lives in the body, let it be there. Then come back to the breath. If it's fierce, try slow walking meditation instead: the same attention, applied to each step.

My legs / back hurt.

Meditation is not an endurance test. Adjust your position, add a cushion, or move to a chair — a chair is a completely legitimate way to sit. Sharp pain is a signal to change, not to push through.

How do I know I'm doing it right?

Here's the whole test: if at some point you realised your mind had wandered and you brought it back to the breath — even once — you did it exactly right. That returning is the practice succeeding, not a sign it failed.

Sit once today. That's the whole practice.
